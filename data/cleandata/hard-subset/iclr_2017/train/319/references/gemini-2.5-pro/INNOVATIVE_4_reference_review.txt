### **Summary**

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel and intuitive method for knowledge transfer from a large "teacher" network to a smaller "student" network. The core idea is that instead of transferring knowledge via softened logits (as in Knowledge Distillation) or by forcing the student to mimic the teacher's full intermediate feature maps (as in FitNets), the student can be guided by learning to replicate the teacher's spatial "attention maps". The authors propose two ways to define these maps: a simple and effective activation-based method, which computes statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values) across feature channels, and a more complex gradient-based method, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. The paper provides extensive empirical evidence showing that activation-based AT significantly improves student network performance across a variety of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (ResNets, Wide ResNets). Notably, it demonstrates a significant accuracy boost on ImageNet, a task where prior hint-based knowledge transfer methods have reportedly struggled.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that introduces a conceptually novel, simple, and surprisingly effective approach to knowledge transfer. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The idea of transferring "attention" as an abstract, spatial summary of a teacher's internal representations is a significant and elegant conceptual leap. It provides a new mechanism for knowledge transfer that is distinct from existing methods and is intuitively appealing—it's akin to teaching a student *where* to focus its limited capacity, rather than forcing it to replicate complex features it may be unable to represent.
2.  **Strong and Compelling Empirical Results:** The activation-based method is backed by thorough experiments that demonstrate its value. The consistent gains are impressive, but the result on ImageNet is the standout contribution. Successfully applying a hint-based transfer method to improve a ResNet-18 on ImageNet is a major achievement and provides strong evidence that this is not just an incremental improvement but a genuinely powerful technique.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its central idea. While the term "attention" has been used extensively, its application here as a distillation mechanism is new. The proposed activation-based maps (`F_sum`, `F_max`) are remarkably simple, requiring no extra parameters, yet they effectively capture a distilled form of the teacher's spatial knowledge.

The experimental validation is a key pillar of this work. The authors wisely compare their method not only to a baseline student but also to a more direct competitor: transferring the full activation tensors (FitNets-style). The finding that AT outperforms full-activation transfer is a crucial result, strongly supporting the hypothesis that transferring a *summary* of information is more beneficial than transferring the raw, high-dimensional representation, especially for a capacity-constrained student.

Furthermore, showing that AT can be combined with traditional Knowledge Distillation (KD) to yield further improvements demonstrates its orthogonality and practical utility. The validation on large-scale datasets moves the work beyond a proof-of-concept on CIFAR and establishes its relevance for real-world applications. The successful ImageNet experiment, in particular, addresses a notable gap in the literature and sets a new benchmark for what is achievable with hint-based training.

While the gradient-based attention transfer section is less developed, I see its inclusion as a strength. It showcases a bold, alternative direction for AT that, despite its current technical hurdles (e.g., interaction with batch normalization), opens up interesting avenues for future research. In the context of this reviewer's preference for novel directions, this exploration of a more complex but potentially powerful idea is a positive signal, even if its validation is incomplete.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Further Intuition on Activation Maps:** The activation-based attention maps are justified by their empirical success, which is convincing. However, the paper could be slightly strengthened by a more explicit discussion of the intuition behind them. For example, why is `sum(|A_i|)` a good proxy for attention? A plausible hypothesis is that different feature channels act as detectors for various concepts, and summing their activation strengths at a spatial location aggregates the "evidence" of importance for that location. A brief discussion along these lines would be welcome.
*   **Framing of Gradient-Based AT:** The gradient-based method is a fascinating idea. Given its preliminary nature, it might be beneficial to frame it more explicitly as an initial exploration of a promising future direction. This would manage reader expectations and place more emphasis on the thoroughly-validated activation-based method, which is the paper's main workhorse.
*   **Symmetry Norm:** The introduction of a "symmetry norm" using horizontal flips (Eq. 6) is another novel and interesting idea for regularization that seems quite effective. This contribution is somewhat buried. While it may be out of scope to expand on it significantly, it's worth noting that this idea alone could be of interest to the community.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  The paper's claim that AT is the first hint-based method to work on ImageNet is a powerful one. Could you elaborate on the specific failure modes you observed or are aware of for methods like FitNets or other intermediate-layer matching techniques on ImageNet? Does the training become unstable, or does it simply fail to provide an accuracy benefit over the baseline? This context would further underscore the significance of your results.
2.  In activation-based AT, you l2-normalize the vectorized attention maps before applying the transfer loss. You note that this normalization is "important for the success." Could you provide more insight or even a small ablation showing what happens without normalization? Does it cause the loss to be dominated by specific layers or simply lead to unstable training?
3.  Regarding the gradient-based method's interaction with batch normalization: have you considered potential workarounds, such as freezing the batch norm population statistics during the second backward pass, or using an alternative normalization scheme like LayerNorm or GroupNorm that might be more amenable to double backpropagation?
