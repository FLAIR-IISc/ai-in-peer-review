### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel and intuitive approach for knowledge transfer in deep neural networks. The core idea is to train a smaller "student" network to mimic the spatial attention patterns of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose and explore two distinct methods for defining these attention maps: a simple and effective activation-based method that summarizes hidden activations across channels, and a more complex gradient-based method that captures the sensitivity of the network's output to its input. Through a series of experiments on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet, the paper demonstrates that Attention Transfer significantly improves the performance of student models. Crucially, it shows that transferring these summarized attention maps can be more effective than transferring the full, high-dimensional activation tensors (as in FitNets) and can be successfully combined with traditional knowledge distillation.

### **Recommendation: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The core contribution—using attention as the medium for knowledge transfer—is a bold, novel, and conceptually elegant idea that moves beyond existing methods. While the experimental validation of the gradient-based approach is preliminary, the thorough and compelling results for the activation-based method, particularly on ImageNet, provide strong evidence for the power and practicality of this new direction.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Appeal:** The central idea of teaching a student network *where to look* rather than *what to see* is a significant conceptual departure from prior work like Knowledge Distillation (matching output distributions) or FitNets (matching intermediate feature maps). It offers a more abstract and potentially more potent form of guidance. This is exactly the kind of creative, high-impact idea that the community should be excited about.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support for the Core Idea:** The activation-based attention transfer method is simple, well-motivated, and shown to be effective across a variety of architectures and datasets. The result on ImageNet, where the authors report a 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18, is particularly impressive, especially given their claim that other knowledge transfer methods have struggled on this scale. The ablation showing that AT outperforms full-activation transfer is a critical piece of evidence that validates the central hypothesis: that the compressed attentional information is a more valuable signal for transfer than the full feature representation.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is the novelty of its core proposal. By defining attention as a spatial map derived from intermediate activations, the authors propose a mechanism that distills the teacher's "focus" into a low-dimensional, spatially coherent signal. This is arguably a more fundamental form of knowledge than the raw feature vectors, which may contain idiosyncrasies specific to the teacher's oversized capacity. The finding that transferring these simple attention maps yields better results than transferring the full activation tensors is a powerful validation of this hypothesis and a standout result of the paper.

The empirical evaluation of the activation-based method is comprehensive. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness in various scenarios: between networks of the same and different depths (WRNs on CIFAR), in transfer learning/fine-tuning settings (CUB and Scenes), and on the large-scale ImageNet dataset. The consistency of the improvements across these diverse settings lends significant credibility to the method's general applicability.

While I am less convinced by the gradient-based attention transfer section due to its limited scope (a simple NIN on CIFAR without Batch Norm) and the authors' own admission of unresolved technical challenges (e.g., interaction with BN), I view this not as a fatal flaw but as an honest exploration of a more complex but related idea. Given my reviewing philosophy, I am happy to accept the paper on the strength of the primary activation-based contribution, treating the gradient-based section as a preliminary but interesting secondary exploration. The introduction of the symmetry norm is also a creative idea that could inspire future work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While my decision is a firm accept, the following points could help strengthen the paper for the final version. These are suggestions for improvement, not obstacles to acceptance.

1.  **Strengthening the Gradient-Based Section:** The gradient-based attention transfer section feels underdeveloped compared to the rest of the paper. The authors should either frame this more explicitly as a preliminary investigation and move some details to an appendix, or attempt to address the Batch Normalization issue, even if just speculatively. For instance, they could discuss whether treating BN statistics as fixed during the second backpropagation would be a viable heuristic.
2.  **Deeper Analysis of Attention Maps:** The paper includes some compelling visualizations of attention maps. This analysis could be deepened. For example, it would be insightful to see a comparison of attention maps for the same image from a baseline student, a student trained with AT, and the teacher. This could visually demonstrate how AT successfully corrects the student's focus, particularly on challenging or misclassified examples.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The selection of the loss weight `β` is mentioned briefly. A small ablation study, perhaps on CIFAR-10, showing the effect of `β` on student performance would add practical value and improve the paper's completeness.
4.  **Justification for `p=2`:** The `F_sum^2` function is chosen for most experiments. While Table 2 shows it performs well, a more explicit discussion in the main text about why summing squares might be preferable (e.g., amplifying strong signals while still aggregating evidence, unlike a `max` operator) would strengthen the motivation.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The finding that transferring attention maps is more effective than transferring full activation tensors is fascinating. Do you have a deeper intuition for why this is the case? Could it be that summarizing across the channel dimension acts as a form of regularization, filtering out "teacher-specific noise" in the feature representation and providing a cleaner, more generalizable signal for the student?
2.  Could you elaborate on the practical challenges of integrating Batch Normalization with the second-order derivatives required for gradient-based AT? Is this a fundamental incompatibility, or primarily an engineering challenge within current auto-differentiation frameworks?
3.  The symmetry norm proposed for gradient-based AT is an interesting regularization technique on its own. Have you considered applying a similar symmetry or equivariance constraint directly to the activation-based attention maps during training? It seems this idea could be decoupled from the gradient-based formulation.
