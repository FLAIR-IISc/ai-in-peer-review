### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer mechanism called Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve the performance of a smaller "student" neural network by forcing it to mimic the "attention" patterns of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose that attention can be summarized by spatial maps derived from a network's intermediate layers, indicating which regions of an input image the network focuses on to make its decision.

Two distinct methods for defining these spatial attention maps are presented:
1.  **Activation-based Attention:** These maps are generated by computing summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values, potentially raised to a power `p`) across the channel dimension of a layer's activation tensor. This method is computationally efficient and intuitive, leveraging the magnitude of activations as a proxy for importance.
2.  **Gradient-based Attention:** These maps are defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image, creating a saliency map that highlights input regions most influential to the final output. This method is more computationally intensive, requiring a "double backpropagation" step.

The proposed training objective combines the standard task-specific loss (e.g., cross-entropy) with a distillation loss that minimizes the L2 distance between the normalized attention maps of the student and teacher. The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). Their key findings are that AT significantly improves student network performance, can be combined synergistically with traditional knowledge distillation (KD), and, notably, outperforms a FitNets-style approach of transferring full activation tensors. The authors also claim this is the first successful application of such a knowledge transfer method on the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Intuitive Concept:** The paper presents a compelling and novel approach to knowledge transfer. Shifting the focus from distilling *what* a network predicts (logits in KD) or *what features* it computes (activations in FitNets) to *where* it looks (spatial attention) is a conceptually significant and well-motivated contribution. The simplicity and effectiveness of the activation-based method are particularly strong.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by rigorous experiments across a variety of tasks, datasets, and modern network architectures. The consistent performance improvements, especially the impressive >1% top-1 accuracy gain for ResNet-18 on ImageNet, provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness and scalability. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the superiority of AT over transferring full activations and analyze the impact of different attention mapping functions.

### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strength lies in its clear motivation, simple yet effective methodology, and thorough experimental validation.

**Conceptual Strength:** The analogy to human attention is well-drawn. The idea that a student model should learn not just the correct answer but also the *right way to look for it* is powerful. This provides a different, complementary form of supervision compared to existing methods. The activation-based attention maps, defined as `F_sum(|A|^p)`, are a clever and lightweight way to extract this spatial focus information without adding any trainable parameters to the transfer mechanism itself, unlike the 1x1 convolutions required in FitNets.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental design is excellent.
*   **CIFAR-10 Experiments:** These serve as a strong testbed for ablation studies. Table 1 clearly demonstrates that AT provides a significant boost, and that combining it with KD (AT+KD) yields the best results. The direct comparison in Table 1 against "F-ActT" (full-activation transfer) is crucial, showing that summarizing activations into attention maps is more beneficial than naively regressing the entire feature tensor. This supports the central claim that it's the *spatial focus*, not the full feature representation, that is the most valuable information to transfer.
*   **ImageNet Result:** The successful application of AT to ImageNet is a major result. Many knowledge transfer techniques are only demonstrated on smaller datasets. Achieving a 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 is non-trivial and validates the method's practical utility for large-scale problems. The authors' note that standard KD struggles in this cross-architecture setting further strengthens the impact of their contribution.
*   **Transfer Learning Experiments:** The fine-tuning experiments on CUB-200 and Scenes demonstrate the method's versatility. The result on CUB, where AT significantly outperforms KD, is particularly insightful. It plausibly suggests that for fine-grained recognition, where localizing discriminative parts is key, transferring spatial attention is more critical than transferring the final class distribution.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could further improve its clarity and impact:

*   **Gradient-based AT Discussion:** The gradient-based attention transfer section feels less developed than the activation-based one. The authors rightly point out the difficulties with batch normalization and the high computational cost. It might be beneficial to frame this section more as a preliminary exploration and clearly state its practical limitations (e.g., by providing a runtime comparison with activation-based AT). The current results are on a simplified setting (NIN without BN), which makes it hard to gauge its potential for modern architectures.
*   **Analysis of Attention Mapping Functions:** Table 2 shows that `sum`-based functions outperform `max`-based ones. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the potential reasons for this. One hypothesis could be that `sum` encourages the student to learn a more holistic focus by aggregating evidence from multiple channels, whereas `max` might cause it to focus too narrowly on the signal from a single, dominant feature channel.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions how `β` is set but does not provide a sensitivity analysis. While extensive tuning is not required, a short discussion on how performance varies with different orders of magnitude for `β` would add to the paper's completeness.
*   **Layer Selection for Transfer:** For the ImageNet experiment, losses were added to the last two residual groups. Was there a specific reason for this choice? A brief comment on the trade-off between the number of transfer losses (and thus the strength of the guidance) and training complexity/stability would be helpful.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Regarding the gradient-based attention transfer: Could you elaborate on the computational overhead of the "double backpropagation" step compared to a standard training iteration? Did you experiment with applying the gradient transfer to intermediate layers instead of just the input, and if so, how did that affect performance and complexity?
2.  Your results on CIFAR and CUB show that different attention mapping functions (`F_sum` vs `F_max`) and different knowledge transfer types (AT vs KD) perform differently depending on the task. This suggests a "no free lunch" scenario. Do you have any general guidelines or intuition on which type of attention transfer might be most suitable for different kinds of vision tasks (e.g., classification, fine-grained recognition, localization)?
3.  In Section 4.2.2, you mention that KD struggled to work on ImageNet for the ResNet-18/ResNet-34 pair. This is a very interesting result. Could you provide more details on the hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, `α`) you tried for KD? Is it possible that KD requires much more extensive hyperparameter tuning in this cross-architecture, large-scale setting compared to AT?
