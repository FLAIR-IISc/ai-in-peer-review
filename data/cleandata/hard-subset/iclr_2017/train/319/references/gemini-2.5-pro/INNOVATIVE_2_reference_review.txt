### Review

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper introduces a novel and intuitive form of knowledge transfer, termed "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network by training it to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct methods for defining these spatial attention maps: a simple and efficient activation-based approach (summarizing feature maps across the channel dimension) and a more complex gradient-based approach (using the gradient of the loss with respect to the input). Through a comprehensive set of experiments on CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the paper demonstrates that transferring attention significantly improves student performance. Crucially, it shows that transferring these summarized attention maps can be more effective than transferring the full, detailed activation tensors (as in FitNets) and that the proposed method succeeds on ImageNet where other knowledge transfer techniques have reportedly struggled.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. It introduces a bold, novel, and conceptually elegant idea that re-frames knowledge distillation. Instead of focusing on transferring the final output (logits) or the full internal representations (features), it proposes to transfer the network's spatial focus of computation. This is a significant conceptual contribution, and the strong empirical results, particularly on ImageNet, validate this new direction. While some experimental avenues are not fully explored, the novelty of the core idea and the strength of the primary results are more than sufficient to warrant acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Appeal:** The central contribution of this paper is the idea of using attention as the "knowledge" to be distilled. This is a departure from prior work like Knowledge Distillation (KD) and FitNets. It is a powerful concept because it suggests that teaching a student network *how* to focus its internal representations is a more effective learning signal than simply forcing it to match those representations directly. The observation that transferring a simple summary statistic of activations (the attention map) outperforms transferring the full activation tensor is a striking and non-obvious finding that strongly supports the paper's main thesis.

2.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The authors validate their approach across a diverse range of tasks and modern architectures (NINs, ResNets, Wide ResNets). The consistent improvements on CIFAR are convincing, but the results on larger-scale problems are particularly impressive. The significant performance gains in fine-tuning scenarios (CUB, Scenes) and, most notably, on the challenging ImageNet dataset, demonstrate the method's practical utility. The claim that this is one of the first knowledge transfer methods to show a clear benefit on ImageNet for student/teacher pairs of different architectures is a major result. Even if the ImageNet experiments are not exhaustively tuned, the reported 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 is a powerful proof of concept that opens up new possibilities.

3.  **Exploration of a Bold, Alternative Direction:** The paper introduces not one but two methods for attention transfer. The gradient-based approach, while more computationally intensive and less thoroughly explored experimentally, is a fascinating idea rooted in sensitivity analysis. Its inclusion, along with the proposed symmetry regularization, showcases a creative and multi-faceted exploration of the core concept. I value this kind of bold exploration. The authors are transparent about the limitations of their gradient-based experiments (e.g., the difficulty with batch normalization), which is perfectly acceptable for a paper introducing a new research direction. The initial positive results are sufficient to demonstrate the promise of the idea.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

While I am enthusiastic about this work, the following points could help strengthen the paper further:

*   **Deeper Intuition:** The paper could benefit from more discussion on *why* attention maps are a better transfer medium than full activations. Is it because the summary statistic acts as a noise filter, removing irrelevant channel-specific details? Or does it capture a more abstract and generalizable concept of spatial importance? Expanding on the intuition behind this key finding would be valuable.
*   **Gradient-Based Method:** The experiments for the gradient-based method feel preliminary. While understandable given the technical challenges, it would be beneficial to include a more detailed discussion on the theoretical hurdles (like handling batch norm during double backpropagation) and potential solutions, even if not implemented. This would provide a clearer path for future work.
*   **Careful Phrasing of Claims:** The claim of being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is very strong. I suggest rephrasing this to be slightly more conservative, for example, by highlighting that it demonstrates strong results in a setting where prior methods have been reported to struggle, unless a comprehensive literature review fully substantiates the original claim. The result stands on its own as highly significant regardless.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide more detail on the computational overhead of your methods? The activation-based method seems very efficient, but a quantitative comparison of training time (e.g., wall-clock time per epoch) for the baseline student, AT, KD, and full-activation transfer would be very useful. How much more expensive is the gradient-based AT?
*   In your experiments, you found that AT works well when transferring from a deeper teacher (ResNet-34) to a shallower student (ResNet-18). Have you explored the reverse or other scenarios with significant architectural mismatches? How robust is AT to the choice of teacher-student pairs compared to methods like KD?
*   Have you considered alternative functions `F` for generating activation-based attention maps? For example, a learned projection, such as a 1x1 convolution across channels, could potentially create an even better representation of attention than a fixed statistical summary.
