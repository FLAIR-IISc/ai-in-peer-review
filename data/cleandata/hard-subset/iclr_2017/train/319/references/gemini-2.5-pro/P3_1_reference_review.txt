### **Summary**

This paper proposes "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the spatial attention patterns of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The core idea is that forcing the student to focus on the same salient image regions as the teacher provides a powerful learning signal that improves its performance. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps: (1) activation-based maps, which are derived from summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values of p-th powers) of feature maps across the channel dimension, and (2) gradient-based maps, which represent the sensitivity of the output loss with respect to the input image. The proposed attention transfer loss, implemented as the L2 distance between the teacher and student attention maps, is added to the standard classification loss. The paper provides extensive empirical evidence showing that AT consistently improves student performance across various datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). Notably, the authors demonstrate that activation-based AT outperforms a FitNets-style transfer of full activation tensors and can be successfully combined with knowledge distillation.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Method:** The paper introduces a conceptually simple, intuitive, and novel mechanism for knowledge transfer. Instead of matching logits (Knowledge Distillation) or full intermediate representations (FitNets), AT focuses on a different, arguably more targeted, piece of information: *where* the network is looking. This provides a new and effective way to regularize and guide the training of a student network.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The method is shown to be effective across a wide range of settings, including different network depths and widths, various datasets from small-scale (CIFAR) to large-scale (ImageNet), and different tasks (classification from scratch and fine-tuning). The positive results on ImageNet, a challenging benchmark for knowledge transfer methods, are particularly compelling.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's main strength lies in its well-motivated approach and the rigorous experiments that validate it.

**Methodology:** The distinction between activation-based and gradient-based attention is clear. The activation-based approach is particularly appealing due to its computational efficiency (it only requires a simple forward pass) and its impressive performance. The authors' hypothesis is that these compressed attention maps carry more salient information for transfer than the full, noisy activation tensors. This is convincingly supported by the experiment in Table 1 and Figure 7(b), where AT significantly outperforms full-activation transfer ("F-ActT"). This is a key finding that differentiates the work from prior methods like FitNets and provides insight into *what* information is most useful to transfer.

**Experiments:** The experimental design is excellent.
*   **Architectures:** The use of modern, high-performing architectures like Wide Residual Networks (WRN) and ResNets establishes strong baselines and demonstrates the relevance of the method to the current state-of-the-art.
*   **Baselines:** The paper includes crucial comparisons against not only the baseline student but also Knowledge Distillation (KD) and a FitNets-style approach. The results show that AT is a competitive method on its own and can be complementary to KD, further boosting performance.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The authors perform useful ablations, such as testing various activation-mapping functions (Table 2), which adds credibility to their design choices (e.g., selecting `F_sum^2`). They also analyze the effect of placing the AT loss at different network depths.
*   **ImageNet Results:** The successful application of AT to ImageNet (improving a ResNet-18 with a ResNet-34 teacher) is a significant contribution. As the authors note, many hint-based knowledge transfer methods have not been shown to work effectively in this large-scale setting, making this result particularly noteworthy.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help improve its clarity and impact:

1.  **Terminology of "Attention":** The term "attention" is used here to describe post-hoc saliency or importance maps derived from activations/gradients. This differs from the explicit, learnable attention mechanisms common in NLP and recent vision models (e.g., Transformers). While the connection is intuitive, it could be beneficial to briefly acknowledge this distinction in the introduction to avoid potential confusion and better position the work as transferring "spatial importance" rather than a learned attention distribution.

2.  **Gradient-Based AT Experiments:** The gradient-based method is an interesting concept, but its evaluation is limited to a simplified setting (NIN without batch normalization on CIFAR). The authors rightly point out the challenges with double backpropagation and BN. Given these limitations, this section could be framed more as a preliminary investigation or a proof-of-concept, while emphasizing the activation-based method as the primary, more practical contribution of the paper.

3.  **Hyperparameter `beta`:** The method for setting the loss weight `beta` seems somewhat ad-hoc ("103 divided by number of elements..."). A short discussion on the sensitivity of the model to this hyperparameter would be valuable. Is the performance robust across a range of `beta` values, or does it require careful tuning for each teacher-student pair?

4.  **Strength of Claims:** The claim of being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" might be an overstatement. While hint-based methods are not commonly reported, KD has been applied in some contexts. It would be safer and more accurate to state that ImageNet is a challenging domain where their method has demonstrated clear success, unlike many previous hint-based approaches.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The comparison between `F_sum` and `F_max` in Table 2 is interesting. `F_sum` performs better, which suggests that aggregating information from all feature maps at a spatial location is more beneficial than relying on the single most active feature. Could you provide more intuition on why this might be the case? Does this imply that the *density* of feature responses is a more robust indicator of importance than the *peak* response?

2.  Could you elaborate on the technical challenges of using batch normalization with the gradient-based AT? Is the issue that the second backpropagation would incorrectly update the running mean/variance statistics, or is it a more fundamental problem with calculating the second-order derivatives through the BN operation?

3.  The proposed AT method focuses purely on spatial information. Have you considered transferring other forms of summary statistics from intermediate layers, for instance, statistics that capture channel-wise relationships (e.g., Gram matrices)? How do you think such an approach would compare to the spatially-focused attention transfer?
