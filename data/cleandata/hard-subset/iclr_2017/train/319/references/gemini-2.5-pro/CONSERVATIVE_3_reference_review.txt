### **Summary**

This paper proposes "Attention Transfer" (AT), a method for knowledge transfer from a large teacher network to a smaller student network. The core idea is that a student's performance can be improved by forcing its intermediate "attention maps" to mimic those of the teacher. The authors define attention maps in two ways: (1) activation-based maps, which are spatial summary statistics (e.g., sum of squared activations) of intermediate feature tensors, and (2) gradient-based maps, which are saliency maps derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. The proposed method is evaluated across several classification datasets, including CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, using architectures like Network-in-Network and Residual Networks. The authors claim that AT provides significant improvements, outperforms transferring full activations (as in FitNets), can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD), and is the first knowledge transfer method to show success on ImageNet.

### **Decision: Weak Reject**

My primary reasons for this recommendation are:
1.  The definition of "attention" is heuristic and lacks a rigorous justification. The proposed method is essentially a form of intermediate feature map matching, but the specific choice of summary statistics used is not well-motivated compared to more established knowledge transfer techniques.
2.  The empirical evaluation, particularly on ImageNet, is not sufficiently rigorous. The failure to provide results for a standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) baseline on this key dataset significantly weakens the paper's claims of superiority.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**1. Methodological Soundness and Motivation:**

The paper's central premise hinges on the definition of "attention maps." The activation-based approach defines these maps as functions like `F_sum(A) = sum(|A_i|)` or `F_psum(A) = sum(|A_i|^p)`. While these operations produce spatial maps that may visually correlate with important image regions, calling this "attention" feels like an overstatement. These are simply summary statistics of the activation tensor. The paper does not provide a strong theoretical or principled reason why this particular form of dimensionality reduction of the feature maps should be the most effective information to transfer. Why is the sum of squared activations a better "hint" than, for instance, the channel-wise mean, variance, or other statistical moments? The comparison in Table 2 is limited to a single teacher-student pair and does not constitute a thorough investigation. Established methods like KD have a clearer motivation in matching the output distributions of the two models. This work feels more like an ad-hoc engineering solution that happens to work, rather than a well-founded method.

The gradient-based attention transfer method is even more problematic. The authors themselves note the high computational cost (double backpropagation) and, more critically, the unresolved issue of how to handle modern architectural components like Batch Normalization. The experiments are consequently limited to a simple NIN model on CIFAR without BN. As presented, this part of the contribution feels preliminary and not applicable to the state-of-the-art architectures that the paper otherwise uses.

**2. Rigor of Experimental Validation:**

While the breadth of experiments is commendable, the validation against established baselines is lacking in key areas. The most significant issue is the ImageNet experiment. The authors claim a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for a ResNet-18 student, which is a noteworthy result. However, they also state that they "were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet," suggesting that it hurt convergence. This is a highly surprising outcome, as KD is a canonical baseline for this type of work. The failure to get a standard baseline working raises questions about the implementation or hyperparameter search. Without a properly tuned KD result on ImageNet, the claim that AT is a superior method for large-scale transfer is unsubstantiated. The assertion that AT "is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is not well-supported if other established methods were not properly evaluated.

Furthermore, on the CIFAR datasets (Table 1), AT is often presented in combination with KD (AT+KD). While this combination outperforms the student baseline, it is less clear how AT alone compares to KD alone. A more direct comparison would be necessary to understand the standalone contribution of AT. The paper shows that AT outperforms a FitNets-style transfer of full activations, which is a valuable result, but the comparison against KD remains the most critical one.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Reframing the Contribution:** Consider framing the work less around the ambiguous concept of "attention" and more as a novel and efficient method for creating "hints" for intermediate layer supervision, contrasting it directly with the approach of FitNets. The observation that a compact summary of activations is a better hint than the full activation tensor is an interesting finding and could be the core message.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the loss weight `β` is set based on a heuristic involving the size of the attention map. A sensitivity analysis for this crucial hyperparameter would strengthen the results and provide guidance for practitioners.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The claim that L2 normalization of the attention maps is "important for the success" should be supported by an ablation study showing the performance with and without this step.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on your efforts to implement Knowledge Distillation on the ImageNet ResNet-18/ResNet-34 pair? What range of temperatures and `α` values were explored? Given that KD is a standard technique, its failure on your setup is unexpected and requires a more detailed explanation to rule out implementation or tuning issues.
2.  The gradient-based transfer method seems incompatible with Batch Normalization as presented. Do you have a proposal for how to integrate BN into the double backpropagation framework? Without a solution, the applicability of this method to nearly all modern networks is severely limited.
3.  Have you explored other aggregation functions for the activation-based maps beyond sum/max of powers? For example, using the channel-wise mean or L1-norm? A more thorough exploration could help determine if there is something unique about the `sum(|A|^p)` formulation or if any channel-wise aggregation would suffice.
