### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer mechanism termed "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network's performance by forcing it to mimic the "attention" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors define attention via spatial maps derived from network activations (activation-based AT) or from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input (gradient-based AT). For activation-based AT, several functions are proposed to summarize feature maps into a 2D attention map, such as summing the absolute values of activations across channels. The proposed attention transfer loss is then added to the standard cross-entropy loss. The authors conduct experiments across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, WRN, ResNet), claiming that AT provides significant improvements, outperforms transferring full activation features (a la FitNets), and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). Notably, the authors claim this is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied to the ImageNet dataset.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Clarity:** The paper's methodology is presented with insufficient detail and justification, making the work difficult to reproduce and assess. Critical components, such as hyperparameter selection and the normalization scheme, are described in a hand-wavy manner. The gradient-based attention transfer section, in particular, rests on a fragile experimental setup and sidesteps a crucial technical challenge (its interaction with batch normalization), severely limiting its applicability.

2.  **Incomplete and Unconvincing Experimental Evaluation:** While the breadth of experiments is appreciated, the execution lacks the necessary rigor for a top-tier publication. The use of single-run results for the gradient-based method is inappropriate. The ImageNet experiments are admittedly preliminary and untuned. Furthermore, the failure to reproduce positive results with a standard baseline like Knowledge Distillation on ImageNet casts doubt on the entire experimental setup and undermines the claims of superiority.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on a significant number of minor and moderate flaws that, in aggregate, question the reliability and contribution of this work.

**Methodological Deficiencies:**

*   **Activation-based AT:** The normalization of attention maps (`Q/||Q||_2`) is stated as "important for the success" but is not justified. Why is l2-normalization chosen over l1 or no normalization? How does this interact with the loss weight `β`? The description for setting `β` ("we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements in attention map and batch size") is imprecise and not a reproducible recipe. The choice of `10^3` seems arbitrary.
*   **Gradient-based AT:** This section is highly problematic. The claim that the second backpropagation has "approximately the same cost" as the first is a gross oversimplification of computing Hessian-vector products. The method is only tested in a highly constrained environment (no batch norm, no random crops, weight decay turned off) on a simple network. The authors explicitly state they avoided batch normalization because its behavior in the second backpropagation step is "unclear." This is not a minor detail; it is a failure to address a core component of modern deep learning, making the proposed gradient-based method currently irrelevant for state-of-the-art architectures.

**Experimental Weaknesses:**

*   **Inconsistent Reporting:** The paper reports the median of 5 runs for some experiments (Table 2) but results from a single run for all gradient-based experiments (Table 3), which is unacceptable for stochastic training processes.
*   **Questionable Baselines:** The comparison against full-activation transfer (F-ActT) is not convincing. The authors mention applying several tricks (l2 normalization, decaying `β`) to their method, but it is unclear if the F-ActT baseline received the same careful tuning. The superior performance of AT could simply be an artifact of better implementation and tuning.
*   **Incomplete ImageNet Results:** The authors concede that the ImageNet experiments are preliminary ("did not have time to tune any hyperparameters," "plan to update the paper"). A top-tier conference paper should present completed, not planned, work. The reported 1.1% gain is based on untuned hyperparameters and losses applied to only two of four possible locations.
*   **Unsupported Claims:** The assertion that AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is a very strong claim that appears to be unsubstantiated by a thorough literature search. Moreover, the authors' inability to achieve positive results with standard KD on ImageNet is a significant red flag, suggesting potential issues in their training framework or hyperparameter search that could also affect their own method's results.

**Presentation and Formatting Issues:**

*   The manuscript is riddled with formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography (e.g., `Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E`). This lack of polish is unbecoming of a serious conference submission.
*   The paper uses MATLAB-style notation (e.g., `abs(Ai).∧p`) while the implementation is in Torch, which is an odd and potentially confusing choice.
*   Several figures (Fig. 1, 3, 5) are referenced but not included in the provided text.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite my recommendation, I believe the core idea of using attention as a distillation signal is intuitive and holds promise. To improve the paper, I would suggest the following:
*   Provide a more principled motivation for the design choices in activation-based AT, especially the map normalization and the `β` formulation.
*   For gradient-based AT, either properly address the interaction with batch normalization or frame the method as a preliminary idea with clearly stated limitations.
*   All experiments must be conducted with proper scientific rigor. This means reporting mean and standard deviation over multiple random seeds for all CIFAR experiments and completing the ImageNet experiments with thorough hyperparameter tuning for both the proposed method and all baselines (KD, FitNets).
*   Perform a thorough proofread to correct the numerous formatting and grammatical errors.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more rigorous, reproducible algorithm for setting the hyperparameter `β` in Eq. 2? How sensitive is the final performance to this value and the choice of `10^3` as a scaling factor?
2.  Regarding the comparison to full-activation transfer in Table 1, was the baseline (F-ActT) tuned with the same level of diligence as your AT method, specifically concerning the use of l2-normalization on the feature maps and a decaying loss weight?
3.  Your failure to get standard Knowledge Distillation to work on ImageNet is surprising. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameters you tried? Is it possible that the training instability you observed with KD also affects AT, but is perhaps masked by the untuned nature of the experiment?
