### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel knowledge transfer framework designed to improve the performance of a compact "student" network by leveraging the knowledge of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the "attention maps" of the teacher at intermediate layers. The authors propose and investigate two distinct forms of attention:

1.  **Activation-based Attention:** These maps are generated by computing spatial statistics (e.g., the sum of absolute values or their powers) across the channel dimension of activation tensors. This method is simple, computationally efficient, and requires no additional parameters.
2.  **Gradient-based Attention:** These maps are defined as the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image, representing a sensitivity analysis of the network's output. This method is more computationally intensive, requiring second-order derivatives.

The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). The results demonstrate that activation-based AT consistently improves student network performance, is more effective than transferring full, uncompressed activation tensors (a la FitNets), and can be synergistically combined with traditional knowledge distillation. Notably, the method shows a significant performance boost on the large-scale ImageNet dataset, a challenging domain for previous hint-based transfer methods.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and valuable contribution. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The proposed activation-based attention transfer is a simple, intuitive, and highly effective method for knowledge transfer. The idea of using a compressed, summary representation of intermediate features as the transfer medium, rather than the full feature maps, is a novel insight that is well-justified by the strong empirical results.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors provide compelling and comprehensive evidence supporting their claims. The method is shown to provide consistent gains across multiple datasets and architectures. The ablation studies are well-designed, and the direct comparison against full-activation transfer provides strong support for the central hypothesis. The successful application to ImageNet is particularly significant and demonstrates the scalability and practical utility of the approach.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in the elegance and effectiveness of the activation-based attention transfer method. Unlike hint-based methods like FitNets that transfer entire feature maps and often require auxiliary layers (e.g., 1x1 convolutions) to match dimensions, AT transfers a simple spatial summary. This approach is parameter-free and computationally lightweight. The paper's most crucial experiment (Table 1, supported by Figure 7(b)) convincingly shows that this summarized information is a more potent "hint" for the student than the full activation tensor, leading to significantly better final accuracy. This suggests that forcing the student to focus on *what* spatial locations are important, rather than *how* the teacher represents them, is a more robust form of guidance.

The empirical validation is extensive and rigorous. On CIFAR-10, the authors use modern WRN architectures and demonstrate improvements over strong baselines, including standalone students and students trained with knowledge distillation. They perform useful ablations, examining the effect of different attention mapping functions and the contribution of transfer losses at different network stages. The extension to fine-tuning tasks on CUB and Scenes further validates the method's versatility.

The ImageNet experiment, where a ResNet-18 student is improved by a ResNet-34 teacher, is the paper's most impactful result. The authors' claim that prior hint-based methods have struggled to show benefits on ImageNet is plausible, and their positive result (a 1.1% top-1 accuracy gain) marks a significant step forward for this class of methods. This demonstrates that the approach is not limited to small-scale datasets and can be applied effectively to complex, real-world problems.

While the gradient-based attention transfer section is less developed—with experiments limited to a non-standard setup on CIFAR-10 and acknowledged challenges with modern components like batch normalization—this does not significantly detract from the paper's main contribution. The activation-based method stands on its own as a solid and valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation and model compression.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions for improving the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Motivation:** The framing around human cognitive attention is a useful narrative device, but the connection is somewhat superficial. The proposed "attention maps" are more accurately described as spatial summaries of feature importance. Grounding the motivation more directly in signal processing or information theory—e.g., as a form of regularized or compressed feature transfer—could provide a stronger theoretical footing.
*   **Analysis:** The paper demonstrates *that* AT works better than full-activation transfer, but a deeper analysis of *why* would be insightful. Is it because the summarization acts as a regularizer, preventing the student from overfitting to the teacher's specific feature representations? Or does it filter out "noise" in the teacher's activations? A discussion of these potential mechanisms would strengthen the paper's impact.
*   **Gradient-Based AT:** The gradient-based method feels preliminary compared to the activation-based one. Given its high computational cost and the unresolved issues with batch normalization, it might be more effective to frame this section as an exploration of a promising future direction, thereby focusing the paper more sharply on the well-validated activation-based approach.
*   **Clarification on Claims:** The claim of being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is very strong. While it may be true for hint-based methods, the authors should carefully verify this against the literature or soften the phrasing (e.g., "one of the first," or specifying "hint-based" transfer) to avoid potential overstatement.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  The failure of standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) on your ImageNet experiment is surprising. Could you elaborate on the hyperparameters used (temperature, α) and why you believe it failed where AT succeeded? Is it possible that KD is simply more sensitive to hyperparameters in this cross-architecture setting?
2.  Regarding the computational cost of gradient-based AT: You mention it involves a "second backpropagation." Could you quantify this overhead? For instance, what is the increase in wall-clock training time for the NIN experiment compared to the baseline or to the very efficient activation-based AT?
3.  For the activation-based maps, you primarily use `F^2_sum`. Table 2 on CIFAR suggests the choice of `p` in `F^p_sum` is not highly sensitive. Did you observe a similar lack of sensitivity on the larger-scale datasets like ImageNet, or does this hyperparameter require more careful tuning in those settings?
