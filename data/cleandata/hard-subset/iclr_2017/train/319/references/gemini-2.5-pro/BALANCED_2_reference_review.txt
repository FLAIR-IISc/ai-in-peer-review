### **Review**

#### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for knowledge transfer called Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network by training it to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The authors define attention maps as spatial summaries of a network's intermediate activations, hypothesizing that these maps encode valuable information about *where* a network focuses its computational resources. Two types of attention are proposed: 1) activation-based maps, derived from channel-wise statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values) of feature tensors, and 2) gradient-based maps, derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. Through extensive experiments, the authors demonstrate that activation-based AT consistently improves student network performance across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). Notably, they show that transferring these summarized attention maps is more effective than transferring the full, un-processed activation tensors (as in FitNets) and that AT can be effectively combined with traditional knowledge distillation (KD).

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper introduces a simple, intuitive, and surprisingly effective method for knowledge transfer. The idea of using summarized spatial feature maps as a distillation target is a novel contribution that provides a compelling middle ground between distilling final logits (KD) and transferring full intermediate representations (FitNets).

2.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The claims for activation-based AT are backed by strong and comprehensive experimental results. The method shows consistent gains across multiple datasets, including a significant 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement on ImageNet for ResNet-18. The direct comparison showing AT's superiority over full-activation transfer is a crucial piece of evidence that highlights the value of the proposed summarization.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments**

**Strengths:**

*   **Simplicity and Intuition:** The activation-based AT method is remarkably simple to implement. The proposed mapping functions (e.g., `F_sum`) are parameter-free and computationally cheap, requiring only a forward pass through the frozen teacher network, similar to standard KD. The underlying intuition that a powerful network learns "where to look" and that this knowledge is transferable is compelling.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation (Activation-based AT):** The authors have done an excellent job validating activation-based AT. They test on a diverse set of tasks (general classification, fine-grained classification, scene recognition) and modern architectures (ResNets, WRNs). The ablations are insightful, particularly the comparison between different attention mapping functions (Table 2) and the demonstration that combining attention losses from multiple layers is beneficial.
*   **Significant ImageNet Results:** Achieving a notable improvement on ImageNet is a significant result. The authors claim this is one of the first successful applications of a hint-based knowledge transfer method to ImageNet, which, if accurate, underscores the importance of this work. The fact that AT succeeds where the authors report KD and other methods struggle in their setup adds considerable weight to the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Underdeveloped Gradient-Based Contribution:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer is significantly less developed than the activation-based part. The experiments are limited to a simplified setting on CIFAR-10 (a simple NIN without batch normalization, single experimental runs), and the authors acknowledge unresolved technical challenges (e.g., interaction with batch normalization during double backpropagation). While interesting, this contribution feels preliminary and detracts from the otherwise strong and focused narrative of the paper.
*   **Loose Definition of "Attention":** The term "attention" is used in a slightly non-standard way. In much of the recent literature, "attention" refers to a mechanism that learns to dynamically re-weight features. Here, it refers to a post-hoc static summary of activations. While the resulting maps are visually correlated with salient object parts, clarifying this distinction in terminology could prevent confusion.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   The gradient-based AT section could be strengthened by addressing the batch normalization issue (even a heuristic solution would be valuable) and expanding the experiments. Alternatively, to tighten the paper's focus, this section could be framed as preliminary work and potentially moved to the appendix, allowing more space to elaborate on the successful activation-based results.
*   Providing more intuition on *why* summary statistics (`F_sum`) are more effective for transfer than full activation tensors (`F-ActT`) would be a great addition to the discussion. Does this suggest that the student benefits more from high-level guidance on spatial importance rather than being forced to match the teacher's exact, high-dimensional feature representations?
*   A brief discussion of the computational overhead would be helpful. It appears that activation-based AT adds minimal cost over standard KD (one forward pass of the teacher), which is a significant practical advantage. Explicitly stating this would strengthen the paper's claims of efficiency.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the rebuttal period:

1.  The paper makes the strong claim that AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." While I am not aware of many successful applications of *hint-based* transfer on ImageNet, standard knowledge distillation has been explored. Could you please clarify the scope of this claim? For instance, does it specifically refer to hint-based methods transferring knowledge between networks of different depths?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the loss-weighting hyperparameter `β`? The paper provides a heuristic for setting it, but is this robust across different datasets and model pairs, or does it require careful tuning for optimal performance?
3.  Regarding the gradient-based method and batch normalization: Have you considered a simple approach for the second backpropagation, such as freezing the batch statistics calculated during the initial forward/backward pass? This would avoid updating the running mean/variance with the "double-backpropped" gradients and might provide a workable solution.
