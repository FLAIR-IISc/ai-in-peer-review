### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer from a large, powerful "teacher" network to a smaller "student" network. The core idea is to compel the student to mimic the "attention maps" of the teacher, thereby guiding its learning process. The authors propose two distinct methods for generating these spatial attention maps: one based on summaries of activation tensors from intermediate layers (activation-based AT) and another based on the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image (gradient-based AT). The paper presents experimental results across several datasets, including CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, showing that the proposed activation-based AT method improves the performance of various student architectures (NIN, WRN, ResNet). The authors claim that their method is superior to transferring full activation tensors and is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully demonstrated on ImageNet.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant Flaws in Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript suffers from numerous presentation issues, most critically an unformatted and unreadable reference list. This, combined with informal notation and references to missing figures and tables, falls well below the expected standard for a top-tier conference and impedes a thorough evaluation of the work.
2.  **Weak and Unconvincing Evaluation of Gradient-Based AT:** One of the two primary contributions, gradient-based attention transfer, is evaluated in a highly constrained and non-standard experimental setting (a simple NIN model without batch normalization or standard data augmentation). The authors acknowledge this is due to technical challenges with modern architectures, which severely limits the practical utility and credibility of this proposed method.
3.  **Insufficient and Potentially Inaccurate Literature Review:** The paper claims to be "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This strong claim appears to be incorrect, as other forms of knowledge distillation have been explored in the context of ImageNet. This suggests an incomplete literature survey, which misrepresents the novelty and significance of the contributions.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The manuscript, in its current state, is not ready for publication. While the central idea of activation-based attention transfer is interesting and shows promising results, the paper is undermined by a significant number of minor and major flaws.

*   **Presentation and Formatting:** The reference list is provided as a raw, unstructured text block, making it impossible to look up citations or properly situate the work. Throughout the paper, figures and tables are referenced (e.g., Fig. 1, Fig. 5, Table 1, Table 4) that are not included in the provided text. The use of informal "Matlab notation" (e.g., `abs(Ai).∧p`) is inappropriate for a formal publication. These issues collectively demonstrate a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.

*   **Methodological Weaknesses and Evaluation:**
    *   The entire evaluation of gradient-based AT (Section 4.1.3) is problematic. The experiments are conducted on a "thin NIN network" without batch normalization because "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." This is a critical limitation. Since batch normalization is a ubiquitous component of modern high-performance networks, a method that is incompatible with it is of limited practical value. Furthermore, in their own limited experiments (Table 3), the proposed `grad-based AT` (12.1% error) is outperformed by the `symmetry norm` (11.8%) and is substantially worse than `activation-based AT` (11.2%). This section does not provide convincing evidence for the utility of the gradient-based approach.
    *   The description of key hyperparameters is vague. The loss weight `β` is described as `10^3` divided by the number of elements in the attention map and batch size. This ad-hoc scaling rule is a crucial implementation detail that is not formally defined or justified.
    *   The ImageNet results, while numerically strong (1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18), are presented as preliminary, with the authors stating they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters." This weakens the confidence in the reported results.

*   **Inadequate Positioning in Literature:** The claim that this is the first successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet is a significant overstatement. While transferring intermediate representations in the manner of FitNets may be less common on ImageNet, the foundational technique of knowledge distillation (transferring softened logits), introduced by Hinton et al., has been explored in this context. The failure to correctly position the work relative to existing knowledge distillation literature is a serious flaw.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite the recommendation for rejection, the core concept of activation-based attention transfer shows merit. To improve the paper for a future submission, I suggest the following:

1.  **Focus the Contribution:** The activation-based method is clearly the stronger and better-evaluated contribution. The paper would be more compelling if it focused solely on this method, developing the analysis and experiments more deeply. The gradient-based method should either be fixed to work with standard architectures (including batch norm) and properly evaluated, or removed entirely.
2.  **Conduct a Thorough Literature Review:** A comprehensive review of knowledge distillation and transfer learning, especially on large-scale datasets, is essential. The paper must accurately compare AT to other methods and make precise, defensible claims about its novelty.
3.  **Improve Experimental Rigor:** The ImageNet experiments should be completed with proper hyperparameter tuning. An ablation study on the scaling of `β` would strengthen the methodology. A direct comparison to standard knowledge distillation (Hinton et al.) on ImageNet would be a critical baseline to demonstrate the superiority of transferring attention maps over just output distributions.
4.  **Thoroughly Revise and Proofread:** The manuscript requires a complete overhaul of its formatting. The reference list must be properly generated using BibTeX and the conference style file. All figures and tables must be correctly embedded and captioned. All mathematical notation should be made formal and consistent.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the technical difficulties of performing the second backpropagation step through batch normalization layers? Is this an issue with specific automatic differentiation frameworks, or a more fundamental theoretical problem (e.g., how to treat the running statistics)?
2.  Can you provide a precise formula for the layer-dependent calculation of the hyperparameter `β` and provide an intuition or empirical justification for why this scaling is necessary or beneficial compared to a fixed `β`?
3.  Given that standard knowledge distillation using logits is a simpler method, did you perform a comparison on ImageNet? Your results show KD hurts convergence, which is surprising. Could you detail the hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, `α`) used and speculate on why it failed while AT succeeded?
