### Review

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper introduces a novel and conceptually elegant approach to knowledge transfer called "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is to guide a smaller "student" network during training by encouraging its spatial attention maps to mimic those of a more powerful, pre-trained "teacher" network. The authors propose two methods for defining these attention maps: a simple and effective activation-based method (summarizing feature maps across the channel dimension) and a more complex gradient-based method (using input sensitivity). Through extensive experiments on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, the paper demonstrates that this approach consistently improves the student network's performance. Notably, the authors show that transferring these compressed attention maps is more effective than transferring the full, dense activation tensors (a la FitNets) and that their method succeeds on ImageNet, a task where they claim other knowledge transfer methods have struggled.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear, novel, and impactful core idea. My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Appeal:** The paper reframes the problem of knowledge transfer from distilling *what* a network predicts (logits in Knowledge Distillation) or *how* it represents features (full tensors in FitNets) to *where* it looks. This is a significant conceptual leap that is both intuitive and powerful. It connects the fields of model compression and interpretability in a compelling way. This bold new direction is precisely the kind of work that should be highlighted at ICLR.
2.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The authors back up their novel concept with surprisingly strong and consistent empirical results. The method shows clear benefits across multiple datasets, architectures (NINs, ResNets, WRNs), and tasks (standard classification, fine-grained recognition). The result on ImageNet, where ResNet-18 is improved by 1.1% top-1 accuracy, is particularly impressive and constitutes a major contribution, especially given the reported failures of other distillation methods at this scale.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its central thesis: a compressed, spatially-focused summary of a teacher's knowledge is a more effective learning signal for a student than the full, high-dimensional feature map. The experiments directly support this. The comparison against "F-ActT" (full-activation transfer) in Table 1 is crucial and convincing, showing that AT provides a much larger performance boost. This "less is more" finding is counter-intuitive and opens up interesting new research questions.

The proposed activation-based attention mechanism is simple, computationally efficient, and requires no extra parameters, making it a very practical contribution. The exploration of different aggregation functions (sum vs. max, different powers) is a nice piece of analysis that adds to the paper's completeness.

While the gradient-based attention transfer experiments are more preliminary and have some limitations (e.g., conducted without batch normalization), they serve as a valuable proof-of-concept for an alternative formulation of the core idea. I see this not as a weakness but as an interesting exploration of the design space that adds to the paper's richness. The authors are transparent about the challenges, and even in this limited setting, the results are promising. My enthusiasm for the paper is primarily driven by the activation-based approach, which is thoroughly validated.

The consistent success of AT, especially in the fine-tuning experiments on CUB and Scenes and the large-scale ImageNet experiment, demonstrates its robustness and general applicability. The claim of being the first knowledge transfer method of this type to be successfully applied to ImageNet is bold and, if accurate, significantly raises the paper's impact.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

While I am strongly in favor of acceptance, the following points could further strengthen the paper:

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The explanation for setting the loss weight `β` is somewhat ad-hoc ("we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements..."). While perfect hyperparameter tuning is not required, providing a more principled explanation, or at least an ablation study on the effect of `β`, would improve the paper's methodological rigor.
2.  **Deeper Analysis of Attention Maps:** The paper convincingly shows that AT improves accuracy. It would be even more insightful to include a deeper qualitative or quantitative analysis of *how* the student's attention maps change during training. For example, showing how the Jensen-Shannon divergence or another similarity metric between student and teacher attention maps evolves over training epochs could provide a clearer picture of the transfer process.
3.  **Positioning of Gradient-Based AT:** The gradient-based section feels slightly disconnected from the main, highly successful activation-based narrative due to the different experimental setup. I would suggest framing this section more explicitly as an exploration of a promising but computationally challenging future direction, perhaps clarifying the specific hurdles (like the batch norm issue) that need to be overcome.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The finding that transferring compressed attention maps is superior to transferring full activation tensors is fascinating. What is your primary hypothesis for why this is the case? Is it a form of regularization, forcing the student to focus on learning the most salient spatial information without being overwhelmed by the noise or stylistic details present in the full teacher activations?
2.  You state that you could not find successful applications of FitNets-style methods on ImageNet in the literature. This significantly strengthens the impact of your ImageNet results. Could you please confirm if this is still the case and elaborate on why you believe previous methods may have struggled where yours succeeded?
3.  Regarding the gradient-based method, you mention the challenge of handling batch normalization during the second backpropagation. Do you have any preliminary thoughts on how this could be addressed? For instance, would using the fixed running statistics from the first forward pass during the second backpropagation be a viable approach?
