### 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method called Attention Transfer (AT). The central idea is to improve the performance of a smaller "student" network by compelling it to mimic the spatial attention patterns of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors define two distinct forms of attention maps for this purpose. The first, "activation-based attention," derives spatial maps from intermediate layer activations by applying summary statistics (e.g., the sum of squared values) across the channel dimension. This captures where the network focuses its computational resources spatially. The second, "gradient-based attention," is defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image, representing an input sensitivity map.

The training objective is augmented with a loss term that minimizes the distance between the student's and teacher's attention maps at one or more corresponding layers. The paper provides extensive empirical validation across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). Key claims are that AT consistently improves student network performance, can be effectively combined with existing knowledge distillation techniques for further gains, and is more effective than transferring full, unprocessed activation tensors as proposed in prior work. Notably, the paper reports successful application of activation-based AT on the large-scale ImageNet dataset, a setting where similar hint-based methods have reportedly struggled.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper introduces a conceptually simple and intuitive method for knowledge transfer. The idea of transferring abstracted spatial attention as a "hint" is a novel contribution that is shown to be more effective than transferring raw intermediate feature maps.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple datasets, architectures, and tasks (image classification and fine-tuning). The reported success on ImageNet, in particular, demonstrates the method's scalability and practical value.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strengths lie in its novelty, technical soundness, and rigorous experimental evaluation.

*   **Novelty and Motivation:** The work is well-positioned within the literature on knowledge distillation and model compression. While building upon the teacher-student paradigm, it proposes a new and distinct form of knowledge to be transferred. Instead of matching output distributions (like in Knowledge Distillation) or full intermediate representations (like in FitNets), this work proposes to match a distilled, spatial representation of computation—the attention map. This is well-motivated by the observation that higher-performing networks often exhibit more focused attention maps. The finding that transferring these summary maps is more beneficial than transferring the full activation tensors is a significant and non-obvious result, suggesting that attention maps provide a more potent and less noisy training signal for the student.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed methods are technically sound and clearly formulated. The activation-based transfer method is straightforward to implement, adding minimal computational overhead to the training process. The loss function (Eq. 2), based on the L2 distance between normalized vectorized maps, is a standard and reasonable choice. The gradient-based method, while computationally more intensive due to the required second-order derivatives (double backpropagation), is also formulated correctly based on existing techniques.

*   **Empirical Rigor:** The experimental results strongly support the paper's claims.
    *   **Consistent Improvements:** Across all experiments on CIFAR-10, fine-tuning tasks (CUB, Scenes), and ImageNet, Attention Transfer consistently improves the performance of the student network over its baseline.
    *   **Ablation Studies:** The authors perform useful ablations, such as comparing different activation-based mapping functions (Table 2), demonstrating that each transfer point contributes to the final performance, and showing that AT can be combined with Knowledge Distillation for additive benefits (Table 1).
    *   **Scalability:** The successful application to ImageNet using ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 is a key strength. The reported 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement is significant, especially given the authors' claim that other hint-based methods have not been successfully demonstrated on this scale. This validates AT as a practical tool for training large-scale models.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The gradient-based attention transfer section is less developed than the activation-based section. The experiments are confined to a specific setting (NIN without batch normalization on CIFAR-10). While the authors note the challenges with batch normalization, further discussion on the potential theoretical and practical issues (e.g., how should batch statistics be handled during the second backpropagation?) would strengthen the paper.
*   The selection of the hyperparameter β is described as a heuristic based on the size of the attention map. While this seems to work in practice, providing a sensitivity analysis or a more principled justification for its value would enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the method.
*   The visualizations are helpful. It would be compelling to include a qualitative analysis of a failure case where the baseline student model fails but the AT-trained student succeeds. Showing the corresponding attention maps (teacher, baseline student, AT student) for such an example could provide powerful intuition into *how* AT corrects the student's behavior.

**Questions for Clarification:**
1.  Regarding activation-based transfer, the paper settles on using the sum of squared activations (`F_2sum`). Have you investigated whether the optimal power `p` in `F_psum` is dependent on the layer depth or the nature of the task? For instance, one might hypothesize that a higher `p` (encouraging sparser attention) could be more beneficial in deeper layers or for fine-grained recognition tasks where focus on discriminative parts is crucial.
2.  The paper states that Knowledge Distillation (KD) performed poorly on ImageNet in your experiments. This is an important negative result. Could you elaborate on the failure mode? Did the training diverge, or did it converge to a solution substantially worse than the student trained from scratch? This contrast highlights the strength of AT and is worth detailing.
3.  For the gradient-based method, the attention map is defined using the gradient of the total loss `L(W, x)` with respect to the input `x`. This implies the map is dependent on the entire output distribution. Have you considered defining attention based on the gradient of the logit for the ground-truth class, which might produce a more focused, class-discriminative map?
4.  You mention that ZCA whitening had a negative effect on CIFAR validation accuracy. This is a somewhat surprising finding. Was this effect observed for all models (including baselines), or was it specifically detrimental when training with attention transfer?
