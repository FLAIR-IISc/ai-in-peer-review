### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel method for knowledge transfer between a powerful "teacher" neural network and a smaller "student" network, framed as "Attention Transfer" (AT). The core idea is that a student network can be trained more effectively if it is encouraged to mimic the teacher's "attention" patterns. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps: 1) Activation-based maps, which are derived by summarizing the activation tensors of a convolutional layer across the channel dimension (e.g., by taking the sum of p-th powers of absolute values), and 2) Gradient-based maps, defined as the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. The student network is then trained with an auxiliary loss that minimizes the L2 distance between its normalized attention maps and those of the teacher.

The primary contributions are:
1.  The introduction of a simple, intuitive, and effective hint-based knowledge transfer mechanism using spatial attention maps.
2.  The proposal and evaluation of two distinct attention formulations: a computationally cheap activation-based method and a more complex gradient-based one.
3.  Extensive empirical validation showing that AT provides significant performance improvements across various datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN).
4.  A compelling demonstration that transferring these compressed attention maps is more effective than transferring full activation tensors (a la FitNets) and that AT can be successfully applied to the large-scale ImageNet dataset, a setting where traditional knowledge distillation has reportedly struggled.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper presents a strong contribution to the field of knowledge distillation and model compression. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The proposed activation-based attention transfer is an elegant and simple idea that is remarkably effective. It provides a more targeted form of hint-based learning than prior work like FitNets, by abstracting away the raw feature values and focusing on the spatial distribution of important activations. This approach is computationally efficient and requires no additional parameters, making it highly practical.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evidence:** The authors provide convincing evidence of their method's effectiveness across a wide range of settings. The most significant result is the successful application to ImageNet, where they improve a ResNet-18 model using a ResNet-34 teacher. This is a non-trivial achievement, as knowledge transfer between networks of different depths on such a large scale is notoriously difficult. The ablation studies on CIFAR are also thorough and clearly demonstrate the superiority of AT over full-activation transfer.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and scientifically sound. The central hypothesis—that guiding a student's spatial focus can improve its learning—is intuitive. The operationalization of this idea via activation and gradient maps is direct and well-justified.

The comparison with FitNets is particularly insightful. The finding that transferring a summarized, 2D attention map is more beneficial than regressing on the full, high-dimensional activation tensor (Section 4.1.2) suggests that the *spatial distribution of feature importance* is a more valuable and digestible form of "hint" for the student than the raw feature representations themselves. This is a significant finding for the knowledge distillation community.

The experiments are rigorous and well-designed. The choice of modern architectures like WRNs and ResNets provides strong and relevant baselines. The positive results in both standard training (CIFAR, ImageNet) and transfer learning/fine-tuning (CUB, Scenes) scenarios demonstrate the broad applicability of the method. The ImageNet result, in particular, stands out. While the authors note that they could not get standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) to work well in their ImageNet experiments, their successful application of AT is a valuable data point and potentially opens a new avenue for large-scale model compression.

The gradient-based method, while less practical due to the computational cost of double backpropagation and issues with batch normalization, serves as an interesting conceptual counterpart to the activation-based approach. The introduction of the symmetry-norm regularizer is also a clever idea, showing how the concept of gradient attention can be used beyond student-teacher transfer.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved for even greater impact:

1.  **Deeper Analysis of Attention Maps:** The term "attention" is used to describe a summary statistic of activation magnitudes. While intuitive, it would strengthen the paper to discuss how this definition relates to more explicit, learned attention mechanisms (e.g., self-attention, squeeze-and-excitation). Are these maps capturing something similar? A more nuanced discussion would better position the work within the broader literature on attention.
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The loss weight `β` is a critical hyperparameter. The paper states it is set based on a heuristic involving map size and batch size. A small sensitivity analysis on `β` would be beneficial to understand how robust the method is to this choice. Similarly, the paper mentions decaying `β` when combining AT with KD; more details on this schedule would improve reproducibility.
3.  **Qualitative Analysis:** Figure 6 in the appendix provides a good qualitative example. This could be strengthened by adding quantitative metrics. For instance, do the student's attention maps become demonstrably more similar to the teacher's after AT, as measured by cosine similarity or Pearson correlation? This would provide quantitative proof that the transfer is indeed happening as intended.
4.  **Gradient-based AT on Intermediate Layers:** The gradient-based method is only explored with respect to the input layer. A natural extension would be to compute the gradient of the loss with respect to an intermediate feature map. This would provide layer-specific sensitivity maps, analogous to the activation-based maps, and could be a more powerful signal. This could be mentioned as a direction for future work.

### **Questions for Authors**

1.  Your activation-based attention maps are defined by simple, fixed functions (sum, max of powers). Did you consider a learned projection, for example, using a 1x1 convolution to reduce the channel dimension to 1, to create the attention map? Do you think a learned mapping could be more effective, or is the simplicity of the fixed function a key advantage?
2.  In Section 4.2.2, you report that standard KD struggled on ImageNet for the ResNet-34 -> ResNet-18 pair. Could you elaborate on why you believe this is the case? What is it about AT's spatial guidance that allows it to succeed where the soft-target-based supervision of KD fails in this cross-architecture, large-scale setting?
3.  The results in Table 2 suggest that sum-based functions outperform max-based ones. This implies that aggregating information from all feature channels at a spatial location is better than relying on the single most active channel. Do you have an intuition for why this might be the case? Does this suggest that diverse, multi-channel features at a single location are collectively important?
