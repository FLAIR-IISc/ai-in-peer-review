### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel and effective method for transferring knowledge from a large, powerful "teacher" network to a smaller "student" network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the spatial attention maps of the teacher at intermediate layers. The authors propose two distinct methods for generating these attention maps: an activation-based approach, which computes statistics (e.g., sum of squared activations) across feature channels to create a 2D map, and a gradient-based approach, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. Through extensive experiments on CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the authors demonstrate that AT significantly boosts the performance of student networks across a variety of architectures (NINs, ResNets, Wide ResNets). They show that activation-based AT is more effective than transferring full, uncompressed activation tensors (as in FitNets) and that it can be productively combined with traditional Knowledge Distillation (KD). A key result is a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for a ResNet-18 student on ImageNet, showcasing the method's scalability and practical value.

### **Decision and Justification**
**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, intuitive, and highly effective method for knowledge transfer. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach:** The idea of using spatial attention as the "hint" for knowledge transfer is a novel contribution that sits neatly between transferring full intermediate representations (FitNets) and transferring only the final output distribution (KD). The motivation is clear: distilling the teacher's knowledge into "where to look" is a powerful and compact form of guidance. The proposed activation-based method is elegant in its simplicity and easy to implement.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed method works well in practice. The authors show consistent and significant performance gains across multiple datasets, including the challenging ImageNet benchmark. The experiments are well-designed, including ablations on the attention mapping function, comparisons against relevant baselines like full-activation transfer, and demonstrating synergy with KD. The success on ImageNet is particularly noteworthy, as many hint-based distillation methods have not demonstrated strong results on this scale.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**
The paper's main strength lies in the activation-based attention transfer method. The concept is straightforward: summarize a C x H x W activation tensor into an H x W map and minimize the L2 distance between the normalized student and teacher maps. Despite its simplicity, the results are impressive.

*   **Effectiveness:** On CIFAR-10 (Table 1), AT consistently improves upon strong WRN baselines and, crucially, delivers better performance than a FitNets-style full-activation transfer (F-ActT). This supports the central hypothesis that an abstracted summary of activations can be a more useful form of guidance than the full tensor. The fact that AT and KD are complementary (AT+KD is best) further enhances the method's practical utility.
*   **Scalability:** The ImageNet experiment is the paper's most significant result. Improving a ResNet-18 baseline by 1.1% top-1 accuracy is a non-trivial accomplishment. The authors claim that, to their knowledge, this is one of the first hint-based knowledge transfer methods to be successfully applied to ImageNet. This result alone makes the paper a valuable contribution to the literature on model compression and knowledge distillation.
*   **Clarity and Analysis:** The paper is well-written and the proposed methods are explained clearly. The authors provide good analysis, for example by testing different mapping functions (F^p_sum vs F^p_max) and showing that sum-based statistics are more effective. The visualizations in the appendix help build intuition for how the student's attention maps become more teacher-like after training with AT.

While the activation-based method is a clear success, the gradient-based section is less developed. The experiments are confined to a non-standard setting (NIN without batch normalization), and the results, while interesting, are not as convincing or generalizable. However, this does not detract significantly from the paper's main contribution, which is the highly effective activation-based method.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**
These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to my decision to accept.
*   The gradient-based AT section feels preliminary compared to the activation-based section. The experimental setup is limited, and the authors themselves note implementation challenges with modern architectures (e.g., batch normalization during the second backpropagation). This part could be strengthened with more robust experiments or framed more explicitly as a direction for future work.
*   The paper claims AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This is a very strong claim. It might be safer and more accurate to soften this to "one of the first" or "to our knowledge," as the authors do in the text, to avoid potential overclaiming.
*   While the heuristic for setting the loss weight `β` is provided, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the method's sensitivity to this hyperparameter. Is the performance robust to changes in `β`?
*   The visualizations are a nice addition. To make the point in Figure 4 even stronger, consider adding difference maps (e.g., |Teacher Map - Student Map|) to more clearly show how AT closes the gap.

### **Questions for the Authors**
I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
1.  Your results show that transferring a 2D attention map is more effective than transferring the full 3D activation tensor. This is a fascinating result. What is your primary intuition for this? Is it that abstracting away the specific channel-wise feature representations gives the student more flexibility to learn its own optimal features, while still being guided on *where* to focus spatially?
2.  On ImageNet, you transferred attention from the final two residual groups. On CIFAR, you found that adding transfer losses at each group was beneficial. Did you experiment with adding more transfer points for the ImageNet experiment, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Regarding Knowledge Distillation on ImageNet, you report that it hurt convergence for the ResNet-18/34 pair. This is an important negative result. Do you have a hypothesis for why KD fails in this cross-architecture setting on a large-scale dataset, while your AT method succeeds? This could highlight a fundamental advantage of AT.
4.  Could you elaborate on the specific technical challenge of combining gradient-based AT with batch normalization? What is the core issue with the second backpropagation step in this context?
