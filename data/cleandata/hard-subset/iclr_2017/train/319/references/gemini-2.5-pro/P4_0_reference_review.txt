### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel method for knowledge distillation where a smaller "student" network is trained to improve its performance by mimicking the attention maps of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct ways to define these spatial attention maps. The first, "activation-based attention," is derived by computing summary statistics (e.g., the sum of powered absolute values) across the channel dimension of activation tensors at various layers. The second, "gradient-based attention," is defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image, capturing input sensitivity. The core contribution is a training framework where the student network minimizes a loss that encourages its attention maps to match those of the teacher. Through extensive experiments, the paper demonstrates that this approach yields significant performance improvements for student networks across a variety of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). Notably, the authors show that transferring these compact attention maps can be more effective than transferring full activation tensors and that the method is successfully applied to the large-scale ImageNet dataset, a setting where other hint-based transfer methods have reportedly struggled.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a novel, intuitive, and effective method for knowledge transfer. The concept of using distilled spatial attention maps as the medium for transferring knowledge is a compelling and well-motivated contribution to the field of knowledge distillation.
2.  The claims are substantiated by strong and comprehensive empirical evidence. The method demonstrates consistent performance gains across multiple datasets, tasks, and modern architectures, and critically, it is shown to outperform a strong baseline of transferring full activation tensors. The successful application to ImageNet is a particularly significant result.

### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strengths lie in its novelty, technical soundness, and rigorous empirical validation.

**Novelty and Motivation:** The work is well-positioned within the literature on knowledge distillation and model compression. While methods like Knowledge Distillation (KD) transfer knowledge via output logits and FitNets use intermediate "hints," this paper introduces a new form of hint: a compressed representation of the teacher's internal spatial focus. The motivation is clear and supported by qualitative visualizations (Fig. 4) suggesting that more accurate networks produce more focused and semantically meaningful attention maps. The finding that transferring a summary statistic of activations (attention) is more beneficial than transferring the full activation tensor is a non-obvious and impactful result. It suggests that attention maps provide a more potent, denoised signal for guiding the student's learning process.

**Technical Soundness and Experiments:** The proposed activation-based attention transfer method is simple, well-defined (Section 3.1, Eq. 2), and computationally efficient. The experimental setup is thorough and convincing.
*   **Breadth:** The method is validated on diverse datasets, including the small-scale CIFAR-10, the fine-grained CUB, the distinct domain of MIT Scenes, and the large-scale ImageNet. This demonstrates the general applicability of the approach.
*   **Architectures:** The use of modern, competitive architectures like Wide Residual Networks (WRN) and ResNets establishes strong baselines and shows the relevance of the method to current state-of-the-art models.
*   **Baselines:** The authors provide crucial comparisons against training the student from scratch, using standard Knowledge Distillation (KD), and, most importantly, a FitNets-style full-activation transfer (F-ActT). The results in Table 1 and Figure 7(b) clearly show that AT provides a significant advantage over F-ActT, lending strong support to the central claim of the paper.
*   **ImageNet Results:** The successful application of AT to improve a ResNet-18 on ImageNet using a ResNet-34 teacher is a key result. The authors' claim that this may be the first hint-based knowledge transfer method successfully applied at this scale highlights the significance of the contribution.

The gradient-based method is less developed, but its inclusion is valuable, and the authors are transparent about the implementation challenges that limited its evaluation. The results presented are still promising and suggest interesting directions for future work.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at strengthening the paper further and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Connection to Attention Literature:** The term "attention" here is defined functionally based on activation statistics. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss how this implicit, post-hoc definition of attention relates to explicit attention mechanisms used in other architectures (e.g., self-attention in Transformers, squeeze-and-excitation blocks). This could help frame the contribution more broadly.
*   **Gradient-Based Attention Limitations:** The paper rightly notes the difficulty of using gradient-based AT with batch normalization. A short discussion on potential approaches or the theoretical reasons for this difficulty would be insightful for readers interested in pursuing this direction.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameter β is set using a heuristic based on the attention map size. A brief analysis of the method's sensitivity to this parameter would increase the practical value of the work. Furthermore, the intuition for decaying β when combining AT with KD could be explained in slightly more detail.
*   **Negative Result on KD:** The observation that KD struggles on ImageNet in the authors' setup is interesting. Elaborating on potential reasons for this (e.g., capacity mismatch, optimization difficulties) would further strengthen the motivation for AT as a more robust alternative for large-scale, cross-architecture knowledge transfer.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

To aid my understanding and confirm my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The finding that transferring summarized attention maps is superior to transferring full activation tensors is a key result. What is your primary hypothesis for why this is the case? Does the summarization (`F_sum`, `F_max`) act as a form of regularization, forcing the student to learn the teacher's high-level spatial saliency while ignoring noisy or teacher-specific low-level feature details?
2.  Your primary experiments on ResNets transfer attention after each "group" of residual blocks. Have you experimented with the granularity of transfer? For example, what is the effect of transferring attention only from the final group versus transferring it from an earlier group?
3.  Regarding the gradient-based method, you define attention with respect to the input `x`. Have you considered defining it with respect to an intermediate feature map `A_j`, i.e., `∂L/∂A_j`? This might be a computationally simpler alternative that avoids the complexities of a full backpropagation to the input layer.
4.  Could you provide a brief analysis of the computational overhead of activation-based AT during training compared to a) standard student training, b) KD, and c) a FitNets-style approach? This would help clarify the practical trade-offs.
