### Review

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer from a high-performing teacher network to a smaller student network. The core idea is to compel the student to mimic the teacher's spatial "attention patterns." The authors define two distinct types of attention maps. The first, "activation-based attention," is derived from summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values of activations) computed across the channel dimension of intermediate feature maps. The second, "gradient-based attention," is defined as the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image, serving as a sensitivity map. The proposed AT loss minimizes the L2 distance between the student's and teacher's normalized attention maps. The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, demonstrating that AT consistently improves student network performance, can be combined with knowledge distillation, and may outperform transferring full activation tensors. A particularly notable claim is that AT is the first knowledge transfer method to show success on the ImageNet dataset.

**Decision and Key Reasons:**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant Methodological Opacity and Lack of Rigor:** The paper suffers from several under-explained and poorly justified methodological choices. Key components, such as the normalization within the loss function and the selection of the hyperparameter β, are presented without clear motivation, hindering reproducibility and scientific rigor.
2.  **Pervasive Formatting and Presentational Flaws:** The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of typographical errors, grammatical inconsistencies, unconventional notation, and a severely corrupted reference section. This accumulation of minor flaws collectively undermines the paper's professionalism and clarity to an unacceptable degree for a top-tier conference.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision:**

1.  **Methodological Concerns:**
    *   **Definition of "Attention":** The paper uses the term "attention" loosely. The proposed maps are post-hoc interpretations of network internals (saliency maps), not an integrated mechanism that modulates information flow during the forward pass, as is common in the attention literature (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014). This terminological choice is confusing and potentially misleading.
    *   **Unconventional Notation:** The use of Matlab-style notation (e.g., `A(i, :, :)`, `abs(Ai).∧p`) in Section 3.1 is highly inappropriate for a formal scientific publication. Standard mathematical notation should be used for clarity and professionalism.
    *   **Unjustified Normalization:** In Equation 2, the attention maps `Q` are l2-normalized (`Q/‖Q‖2`) before the distance is computed. The paper states that "normalization of attention maps is important for the success," but provides no ablation study or theoretical justification for why this is the case. Does this prevent trivial solutions? Does it balance the scale of gradients from different layers? This crucial detail is left unexplained.
    *   **Ad-hoc Hyperparameter Setting:** The description of setting `β` in Section 4.1.1 is alarming: "it usually varies about 0.1, as we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements in attention map and batch size for each layer." This is an unprincipled and convoluted heuristic. A hyperparameter should be set via a validation set or have a clear theoretical grounding; this method appears arbitrary and makes the results difficult to interpret and reproduce.
    *   **Weak Gradient-Based Experiments:** The experiments for gradient-based AT (Section 4.1.3) are conducted in a highly constrained and non-standard setting (no batch normalization, no random crops). The authors state this is because it is "unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." This is not a justification for a weak experiment, but rather an admission of an unresolved technical challenge in the proposed method. The results from this section are therefore not generalizable.
    *   **Insufficient Empirical Rigor:** In Table 2, the authors report the "Median of 5 runs." While medians are robust to outliers, reporting the mean and standard deviation is standard practice and provides a more complete picture of performance variance. Furthermore, the claim of success on ImageNet is weakened by the admission that "we also did not have time to tune any hyperparameters."

2.  **Presentational and Formatting Flaws:**
    *   **Corrupted Bibliography:** The reference section is completely broken, containing raw BibTeX-like artifacts (e.g., `Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E, Bahdanau et al\.,`). This is a critical failure of manuscript preparation and is unacceptable for submission.
    *   **Overblown Claims:** The assertion in Section 4.2.2 that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is a very strong claim and is likely inaccurate. Other distillation-style methods have been explored in large-scale settings. This claim requires much stronger evidence and a more thorough literature review.
    *   **Typographical and Grammatical Errors:** The paper is replete with minor errors. Examples include: "w.r.t." used inconsistently, "traning" (Section 4.1.1), "parallelizm" (Appendix A.2), awkward phrasing like "was experimentally showed" (Section 2), and inconsistent question mark placement in the introduction. While individually minor, their high frequency suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   Please refine the terminology. Consider referring to the proposed maps as "activation saliency maps" or "gradient saliency maps" to distinguish them from integrated attention mechanisms.
*   The manuscript requires a complete overhaul of its formatting, especially the bibliography. Every citation and reference must be checked for correctness. A thorough proofreading for grammar and typos is essential.
*   The justification for key methodological choices needs to be strengthened. Please include an analysis or ablation study on the effect of the l2 normalization in the AT loss.
*   The procedure for setting hyperparameter `β` should be replaced with a more standard and principled approach.
*   The claims regarding novelty on ImageNet must be carefully re-evaluated and toned down, supported by a more comprehensive literature review.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed motivation for normalizing the attention maps before computing the L2 distance in Equation 2? What happens to performance if this normalization is removed?
2.  Can you clarify the claim that this is the first successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet? Have you compared against or considered prior work on knowledge distillation for large-scale classification?
3.  Regarding the gradient-based method, instead of avoiding batch normalization, have you considered alternatives, such as freezing the batch statistics during the second backpropagation step?
