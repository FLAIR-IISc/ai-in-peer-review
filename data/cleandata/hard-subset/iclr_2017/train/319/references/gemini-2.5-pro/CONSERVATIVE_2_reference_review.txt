### Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a new knowledge transfer mechanism, termed "attention transfer" (AT), to improve the performance of a smaller "student" network using guidance from a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the "attention" of the teacher. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps for this purpose. The first, "activation-based attention," is derived by computing channel-wise statistics (e.g., sum of squared values) of activation tensors at intermediate layers. The second, "gradient-based attention," is defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image, serving as a sensitivity map. The transfer is achieved by adding a loss term that minimizes the distance between the teacher's and student's attention maps. The authors present experiments across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, claiming that their method provides significant improvements, outperforms a FitNets-style full activation transfer, and is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied to ImageNet.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Incremental Novelty and Insufficient Motivation:** The proposed "attention transfer" is framed as a novel concept, but the activation-based method is essentially a variant of existing hint-based knowledge transfer techniques, such as FitNets. The primary difference is the application of a spatial summary function to the intermediate feature maps before computing the transfer loss. The paper fails to provide a compelling, principled motivation for why this summarization is superior to transferring the full feature map, relying instead on a single empirical comparison.
2.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor and Overstated Claims:** The empirical validation, while broad in scope, lacks the depth and rigor required. The gradient-based method is tested in a highly constrained and outdated setting, limiting its relevance. More critically, the ImageNet results, which form a major part of the claimed contribution, are admittedly based on untuned hyperparameters and an incomplete experimental setup. This preliminary evidence is insufficient to support the strong claim that this is the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," a claim which is likely inaccurate and dismisses the challenges of tuning established baselines like Knowledge Distillation (KD).

### Supporting Arguments

**1. Novelty and Motivation:**

The central idea of using intermediate representations from a teacher to guide a student is well-established (e.g., Romero et al., 2014, "FitNets"). The proposed activation-based AT method modifies this approach by first summarizing the activation tensor `A ∈ R^(C×H×W)` into a 2D map `Q ∈ R^(H×W)`. The paper shows this works better than a FitNets-style baseline ("F-ActT" in Table 1) but offers little insight into *why*. The justification that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" is a circular restatement of the results rather than a scientific explanation. A stronger contribution would require a deeper analysis of this phenomenon. For example, does this summarization act as a regularizer, preventing the student from overfitting to the teacher's specific feature implementations? Or does it force the student to learn a more abstract spatial distribution of important features? Without such analysis, the method appears to be an ad-hoc modification of an existing framework. The use of the term "attention" is also questionable, as these maps are simple statistical summaries of activations or input gradients, not learned, context-dependent attention mechanisms in the vein of Bahdanau et al. (2014).

**2. Experimental Rigor:**

The experimental section contains several weaknesses that undermine the paper's conclusions.

*   **Gradient-Based AT:** This method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 using a thin NIN model without batch normalization or standard data augmentations like random cropping. The authors themselves note the uncertainty of how to handle batch normalization during the required second backpropagation step. Given that batch normalization is a crucial component of virtually all modern architectures, demonstrating a method on a model that explicitly omits it severely curtails the method's claimed applicability and soundness.
*   **ImageNet Results:** The authors claim a significant contribution by successfully applying AT to ImageNet. However, they explicitly state, "we also did not have time to tune any hyperparameters and kept them from finetuning experiments." Furthermore, their baseline comparison to Knowledge Distillation is flawed; they report that KD "hurts convergence" using the same hyperparameters from CIFAR. It is standard practice that hyperparameters do not transfer naïvely across datasets of such different scales and complexities. Dismissing a foundational method like KD based on an untuned experiment is not rigorous. Consequently, the claim that AT is the first successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet is unsubstantiated and likely incorrect. The results, while promising, must be considered preliminary until they are supported by a properly tuned and comprehensive set of experiments, including fair comparisons to established baselines.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The paper would be significantly stronger if it focused exclusively on the more practical and better-developed activation-based AT. The gradient-based method, in its current state, feels underdeveloped and detracts from the main message.
*   To strengthen the motivation, I suggest adding an ablation study that explores the summarization function `F` in more detail. For instance, how does performance change as the function retains more or less channel information? This could provide insight into why summarization is beneficial.
*   The framing of the work should be more carefully situated within the existing knowledge distillation literature. Positioning the method as a specific type of hint-based transfer, with a clear hypothesis about *why* this form of hint is effective, would be more convincing than claiming a new paradigm of "attention transfer."
*   If the authors wish to maintain the ImageNet results, it is imperative to conduct a thorough hyperparameter search for both the proposed method and the KD baseline to ensure a fair and meaningful comparison. The claims regarding the novelty of success on ImageNet should be toned down or supported by a more comprehensive literature review.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Beyond the empirical results, can you provide a more fundamental motivation or theoretical argument for why compressing an intermediate activation tensor into a 2D spatial map is a more effective form of knowledge transfer than using the full tensor?
2.  You state that Knowledge Distillation struggles on ImageNet with your teacher-student pair. Could this failure be a result of using untuned CIFAR hyperparameters? How can you be certain that this is an inherent limitation of KD rather than a consequence of the experimental setup?
3.  Given the significant computational overhead of double backpropagation and the unresolved issues with batch normalization, what do you see as the practical path forward for the gradient-based attention transfer method in modern, large-scale deep learning?
