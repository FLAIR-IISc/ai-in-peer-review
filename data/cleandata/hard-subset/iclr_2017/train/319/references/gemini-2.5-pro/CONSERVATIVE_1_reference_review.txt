**Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes "Attention Transfer" (AT), a new knowledge transfer technique where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The core idea is that transferring spatial attention, which highlights where a network focuses, is a more effective way to distill knowledge than transferring entire feature representations. The authors define two types of attention maps: (1) activation-based maps, derived from summary statistics (e.g., sum of squared activations) across feature channels at a given layer, and (2) gradient-based maps, derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. Through experiments on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, the authors claim that their proposed AT method significantly improves student network performance, outperforms a baseline that transfers full activation maps (similar to FitNets), and can be combined with traditional knowledge distillation (KD) for further gains. Notably, they claim AT is the first knowledge transfer method to show success on the ImageNet dataset.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: **Reject**

My decision is based on two primary concerns:

1.  **Insufficient Methodological Justification:** The proposed definitions of "attention" are heuristic and lack strong theoretical grounding. The connection to cognitive attention is tenuous, and the specific mathematical formulations for the maps are presented without a principled derivation, making them seem arbitrary.
2.  **Weak and Incomplete Empirical Validation Against Baselines:** The experimental comparison to established knowledge transfer methods is unconvincing. The authors' implementation of a FitNets-style baseline appears superficial, and their dismissal of Knowledge Distillation's effectiveness on ImageNet is a very strong claim based on minimal evidence, which is more likely indicative of inadequate hyperparameter tuning than a fundamental flaw in the baseline method.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **On Methodological Soundness:** The paper's core contribution rests on the definition of its attention maps. The activation-based maps, `F_sum(|A|^p)`, are an engineered choice. While the authors show empirically that different functions yield different results, there is no discussion as to *why* summing the p-th power of absolute activations should be a superior form of knowledge to transfer compared to other statistics. It is an interesting engineering finding, but it is not presented with the methodological rigor one would expect for a foundational claim. The gradient-based method is even more problematic. The experiments are confined to a simplified setting (a thin NIN without batch normalization or certain augmentations on CIFAR) precisely because, as the authors admit, it is unclear how to handle core components of modern networks like batch normalization during the required second backpropagation step. This severely limits the demonstrated applicability and soundness of the gradient-based approach, rendering the results on this method preliminary at best.

2.  **On Empirical Comparisons:** The strength of a new knowledge transfer method must be demonstrated through rigorous comparison with existing, established techniques. This paper falls short in this regard.
    *   **Comparison with FitNets:** The paper compares AT against "FitNets-style hints using l2 losses on full activations" (F-ActT). However, FitNets was a significant and well-established baseline. The brief description provided does not give confidence that the authors implemented a faithful, well-tuned version of this baseline. For instance, were the regressor layers and loss weighting schemes from the original paper carefully replicated and optimized? Without these details, the claim that AT provides "much better final accuracy" is not well-supported, as it could be a comparison against a poorly configured strawman.
    *   **Claims Regarding Knowledge Distillation on ImageNet:** The paper makes the bold claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," citing that they "were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet" and that it "hurts convergence." This is an extraordinary claim that requires extraordinary evidence. Dismissing one of the most foundational methods in the field based on a single set of experiments with "the same hyperparameters as on CIFAR" is not scientifically rigorous. It is far more plausible that KD requires different hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, loss weighting) for a complex, large-scale dataset like ImageNet. Attributing the failure to the method itself rather than the experimental setup undermines the credibility of the paper's central claims about its superiority.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While I recommend rejection, I believe the core idea has empirical merit and could be developed into a strong contribution with further work. I offer the following suggestions:

*   **Strengthen the Motivation:** The paper would benefit from a more principled justification for the attention map formulation. Could this be connected to information theory (e.g., maximizing mutual information between teacher and student maps) or by showing that these maps are provably better proxies for network focus than other statistics?
*   **Focus the Paper:** The gradient-based attention transfer section is very preliminary and its limitations detract from the overall paper. I would suggest removing it and focusing entirely on the more promising and practical activation-based method. This would allow more space for the rigorous baseline comparisons that are currently missing.
*   **Conduct Rigorous Baseline Experiments:** To make a convincing case, the paper needs a much more thorough comparison with FitNets and KD. For FitNets, a careful re-implementation with hyperparameter tuning is necessary. For KD on ImageNet, a dedicated hyperparameter search should be conducted to find a setting where it performs well (as one would expect it to) before comparing it with AT. The goal should be to show that AT provides benefits *on top of* or *instead of* a well-tuned, strong baseline, not a poorly tuned one.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The ablation on which layer to transfer from is a good start. It would be strengthened by analyzing the effect of the weighting parameter `β` more systematically. How sensitive is the method to this parameter?

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more formal justification for choosing the sum of p-powered absolute activations as the definition for an attention map, beyond the empirical observation that it works? Are there other formulations you considered and why were they discarded?
2.  Can you provide more specific details on your implementation of the full-activation transfer baseline (F-ActT)? Specifically, how did you handle matching feature dimensions between teacher and student (e.g., regressor layers), and how was the loss weight for this hint term tuned?
3.  Regarding the ImageNet experiments with Knowledge Distillation, what range of temperatures and loss weights (`α`) were explored before concluding that the method "hurts convergence"?
4.  What is the computational overhead of AT compared to standard training and standard KD? The gradient-based method requires a second backpropagation, which seems very expensive. How much does the activation-based method add in terms of training time?
