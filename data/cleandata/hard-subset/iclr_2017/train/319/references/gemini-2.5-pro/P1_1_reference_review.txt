### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge distillation where a high-capacity teacher network transfers knowledge to a smaller student network by teaching it *where to look*. The core idea is to define spatial attention maps for a given layer and train the student network to produce attention maps similar to the teacher's. The authors propose two distinct methods for generating these attention maps:

1.  **Activation-based AT:** Spatial attention maps are generated by computing summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values, sum of p-th powers) across the channel dimension of a layer's activation tensor. This is computationally efficient as it only requires a forward pass.
2.  **Gradient-based AT:** Attention is defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image. This method is more computationally intensive, requiring a "double backpropagation" step to compute the loss gradient.

The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). The key findings are that activation-based AT significantly improves the performance of student models, can be combined with traditional knowledge distillation (KD) for further gains, and, perhaps most interestingly, is more effective than transferring the full, uncompressed activation tensors (a "FitNets" style approach). The authors also demonstrate that their method is the first knowledge transfer technique to show significant gains on the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Idea with Strong Results:** The core idea of using summarized spatial attention as the medium for knowledge transfer is novel, simple to implement (especially the activation-based version), and intuitively appealing. It reframes the concept of "hints" from prior work like FitNets into a more compressed and seemingly more effective form. The paper backs this up with strong, consistent performance improvements across a wide variety of tasks and network architectures.
2.  **Excellent Experimental Rigor and a Key Finding:** The experiments are well-designed and thorough. The authors compare against appropriate baselines, including standard student training, knowledge distillation, and full activation transfer. The finding that transferring a simple summary of activations (the attention map) is *more* effective than transferring the full activation tensor is a significant and non-obvious result that deepens our understanding of knowledge transfer. The successful application to ImageNet, where other methods reportedly struggle, is a major contribution.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's main strength lies in its clear hypothesis and the extensive evidence provided to support it.

The concept of activation-based attention transfer is particularly compelling. By summarizing activations across the channel dimension, the method distills the teacher's spatial focus without forcing the student to replicate the teacher's exact feature representations. This provides guidance while allowing the student network the flexibility to learn its own, lower-capacity feature extractors. The authors' ablation study on CIFAR-10 (Table 1), directly comparing AT to full activation transfer ("F-ActT"), provides clear evidence for this. The fact that AT provides a significant boost while F-ActT offers minimal improvement is a powerful argument for the proposed method.

The breadth of the experiments validates the generality of the approach. Success is shown in multiple scenarios:
*   **Small-scale classification (CIFAR):** Demonstrates the core effectiveness and allows for detailed ablations (e.g., comparing different attention functions in Table 2).
*   **Fine-tuning/Transfer Learning (CUB, Scenes):** Shows the method's utility in a very common and practical use case, significantly closing the gap between a ResNet-18 student and a ResNet-34 teacher.
*   **Large-scale classification (ImageNet):** This is a standout result. Improving a ResNet-18 by 1.1% top-1 accuracy is a non-trivial achievement. The claim that this is the first successful application of knowledge transfer at this scale makes the contribution particularly impactful for the community.

The gradient-based method, while less developed experimentally, is an interesting alternative and the proposed "symmetry norm" is a clever regularization technique in its own right. Though its evaluation is limited, it adds intellectual depth to the paper's exploration of "attention."

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

1.  **Clarity on Gradient-based AT Limitations:** The experiments for gradient-based attention transfer are performed under constrained conditions (no batch norm, no random crops). The authors acknowledge this is due to the complexity of double backpropagation with batch norm. While understandable, this makes the comparison in Table 3 less direct. It would strengthen the paper to either elaborate more on the specific technical challenges with BN or to de-emphasize this section slightly to focus on the much more robust and generalizable activation-based results.
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper gives a rule of thumb for setting the loss weight `β` but does not discuss its sensitivity. A brief analysis or discussion of how performance varies with `β` would add to the paper's practical utility for researchers wishing to adopt this method.
3.  **The "Why" of AT's Superiority:** The paper convincingly shows *that* AT is better than full activation transfer, but could speculate more on *why*. Is it a form of regularization that prevents the student from overfitting to the teacher's specific feature implementations? Does the compression inherent in creating the attention map discard "teacher-specific noise" that is not useful for the smaller student? A deeper discussion of this point in the conclusion would be very insightful.
4.  **Visualizations:** The visualizations are helpful. Figure 6 in the appendix is particularly strong evidence that the student's attention maps qualitatively change to become more like the teacher's. Given its importance, the authors could consider trying to incorporate it into the main body of the paper, perhaps by combining it with another figure.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you quantify the computational overhead of activation-based AT during training, particularly for the ImageNet experiments? My impression is that it is minimal, as the teacher's forward pass is already required for methods like KD, but confirming the wall-clock time difference would be useful.
2.  In the combined AT+KD experiments, you mention decaying `β` during training. Could you elaborate on the schedule used and the motivation for this? Does this prevent the attention loss from interfering with the KD loss in later stages of training?
3.  The finding that `sum`-based attention functions outperformed `max`-based ones is interesting. Do you have an intuition for why encouraging attention at all locations with high-activating features is better than focusing only on the single highest-activating feature at each location?
