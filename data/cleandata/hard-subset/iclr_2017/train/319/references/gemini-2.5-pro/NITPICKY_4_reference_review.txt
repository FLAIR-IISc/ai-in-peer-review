### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel mechanism for knowledge distillation where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the attention maps of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct methods for defining these attention maps: an activation-based approach, which derives spatial maps from statistics of feature activations (e.g., sum of squared activations across channels), and a gradient-based approach, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image as a sensitivity map. The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN), demonstrating that the proposed attention transfer method can significantly improve student network performance. The authors also claim that AT is more effective than transferring full activation tensors (a la FitNets) and is the first knowledge transfer method to show success on the ImageNet dataset.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The paper's central claims regarding its novelty and superiority on large-scale datasets are unsubstantiated and appear to be factually incorrect. The assertion that this is the first successful application of knowledge transfer to ImageNet is a significant overstatement that ignores prior work and undermines the paper's scientific credibility.
2.  The manuscript is in a preliminary and incomplete state, suffering from numerous and severe presentation issues. Multiple critical figures and tables are referenced but missing, the bibliography is completely broken, and several experimental details are either unclear or described as unfinished. These deficiencies make a rigorous evaluation of the work impossible.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My decision for rejection is based on the following detailed points:

*   **Unsupported and Exaggerated Claims:** The paper claims in Section 4.2.2 that "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature" and "we can assume that proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." These statements are demonstrably false. Knowledge distillation and related techniques have been applied to ImageNet in various contexts prior to this work. This significant misrepresentation of the related literature calls into question the authors' diligence and the paper's overall contribution. Furthermore, the experiments supporting the ImageNet results are described as preliminary, using untuned hyperparameters and an incomplete set of transfer losses, with a promise to "update the paper" later. Such work is not ready for publication at a top-tier conference.

*   **Incomplete and Unclear Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evidence is substantially weakened by its presentation and several methodological issues:
    *   **Missing Figures/Tables:** The text references at least seven figures (Fig. 1-7) and three tables (Table 1, 4, 5) that are not included in the manuscript. These are not minor omissions; they are essential for understanding the method (e.g., Fig. 1, 5), visualizing the core concept (Fig. 2, 4, 6), and verifying the primary results (Table 1, 4, 5, Fig. 7). Without these, the paper's claims cannot be properly assessed.
    *   **Constrained Gradient-Based Experiments:** The gradient-based AT method, one of the two main contributions, is only tested on CIFAR-10 under a highly constrained setting (a specific NIN architecture without batch normalization or random cropping). The authors acknowledge difficulties with batch normalization in the second backpropagation step, but this effectively positions the gradient-based method as a fragile curiosity rather than a generally applicable technique. Tellingly, the simpler activation-based method outperforms it even in this tailored setting (Table 3).
    *   **Unclear Hyperparameters:** The procedure for setting the crucial loss weight `β` is opaque. It is described as "10^3 divided by number of elements in attention map and batch size for each layer." This ad-hoc rule is not well-motivated and lacks clarity. Is it applied per-layer? How does it interact with the number of loss terms? A sensitivity analysis is absent.

*   **Severe Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The manuscript is riddled with errors that suggest a lack of care and professionalism.
    *   **Broken References:** The bibliography section is unusable, with entries like `Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E, Bahdanau et al\.,`. This is a critical failure in academic writing.
    *   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** Minor but frequent errors are present throughout the text, such as "traning" instead of "training" (Section 4.1.1), a space before a question mark ("networks ?"), and inconsistent use of abbreviations ("w.r.t.").
    *   **Inconsistent Notation:** Table 2 lists `F 1max`, which is likely a typo for `F_max`. Such inconsistencies impede clarity.

While the core idea of using compressed representations of activations (attention maps) for distillation is interesting, the paper's execution, evaluation, and presentation are far below the standards required for ICLR.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The core concept of activation-based AT is promising. It appears to be simple, effective, and scalable. I would advise focusing on this contribution and strengthening its evaluation.
*   A thorough and accurate literature review is essential. You must retract the claim of being the first on ImageNet and properly situate your work with respect to existing knowledge transfer methods that have been used on this scale.
*   Conduct a fair and rigorous comparison against other knowledge transfer baselines (e.g., the original KD, FitNets) on *all* relevant datasets, including ImageNet. If KD indeed performs poorly with your setup, you need to investigate why, rather than dismissing it.
*   The entire paper requires extensive proofreading and re-formatting. All figures, tables, and references must be correctly included and formatted. The writing should be polished to remove typos and grammatical errors.
*   Provide a more principled motivation or at least an ablation study for the choice of `β` and the normalization of attention maps in the loss function.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you please provide the proper citations or evidence to support your claim that knowledge distillation methods had not been successfully applied to ImageNet prior to your work?
*   Can you clarify the exact formulation for setting the hyperparameter `β`? Is the divisor `(# elements * batch size)`? Is `β` a single scalar for all losses, or is it computed individually for each of the `j∈I` loss terms?
*   In the gradient-based experiments (Table 3), the student NIN architecture used for the activation-based AT comparison was modified ("it doesn’t have ReLU activations before pooling layers"). How does this architectural difference confound the comparison and the conclusion that activation-based AT is superior?
