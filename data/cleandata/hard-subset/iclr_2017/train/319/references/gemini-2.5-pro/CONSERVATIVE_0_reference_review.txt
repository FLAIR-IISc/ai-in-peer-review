### **Review**

#### **Summary**

The paper proposes a novel method for knowledge transfer, termed "Attention Transfer" (AT), designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" network by leveraging knowledge from a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the "attention maps" of the teacher. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based maps, which are spatial summaries of activation tensor magnitudes at intermediate layers, and gradient-based maps, which are saliency maps derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input. The method is evaluated across several datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN), demonstrating consistent performance improvements for the student network. The authors claim that their method is more effective than transferring full activation tensors (a la FitNets) and is the first knowledge transfer method to show success on the ImageNet dataset.

#### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Weak Methodological Justification:** The proposed definitions of "attention," particularly the activation-based maps, are heuristic and lack a strong theoretical or empirical justification. The paper relies on a loose analogy to human attention but does not provide a clear rationale for why summarizing activation magnitudes via `sum(|A|^p)` should constitute a superior form of knowledge transfer compared to other possibilities. The method feels arbitrary and under-motivated.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines on ImageNet:** The paper's most significant claim is a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement on ImageNet. However, this result is presented without a comparison to the most prominent knowledge transfer method, Knowledge Distillation (KD). The authors state they "were not able to achieve positive results with KD," but this anecdotal failure is insufficient. For such a central claim, a rigorous and thoroughly tuned baseline is required. Without it, it's impossible to assess if AT is genuinely superior or if the observed gains are simply a result of a form of regularization that KD could also provide with proper tuning.

#### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The central premise of this work rests on the idea that "attention maps" are a potent and efficient medium for knowledge transfer. However, the paper's definition of these maps is not well-grounded. The activation-based attention map `F_psum(A) = sum(|A_i|^p)` is a simple statistical summary of feature maps. Why is this specific formulation chosen? Why the sum of p-powered absolute values? Would variance, or other moments of the distribution across channels, not be equally plausible proxies for "attention"? The paper does not explore these design choices, making the proposed method appear ad-hoc. The use of the term "attention" itself is questionable, as it overloads a term that has more specific meanings in other contexts (e.g., Bahdanau et al., 2014) and may not accurately describe what is being transferred, which is essentially a map of activation energy.

The experimental validation, while broad, has a critical weakness in its most important experiment. The ImageNet results are promising but are only compared against the baseline student network. The authors' claim to be the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is a very strong one that requires extraordinary evidence. Dismissing Knowledge Distillation based on an inability to tune hyperparameters is not sufficient evidence. Established methods like KD are the primary yardstick against which new knowledge transfer techniques must be measured. Without a direct, carefully tuned comparison on ImageNet, the main claim of the paper's superiority remains unsubstantiated.

Similarly, the comparison against full-activation transfer ("F-ActT") is only performed on CIFAR-10. While the results favor AT, it is unclear if this finding generalizes to larger-scale problems like ImageNet, where transferring full activations might be more challenging but also potentially more informative.

Finally, the gradient-based attention transfer section feels preliminary. The experiments are conducted on a simplified setup (NIN without batch normalization, no random cropping) due to implementation complexity. The results in Table 3 show that this method is outperformed by the authors' own activation-based AT, weakening its contribution. This part of the work seems underdeveloped and detracts from the paper's focus.

#### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The authors should consider renaming "attention transfer" to something more precise, such as "activation summary transfer" or "activation magnitude transfer," to avoid confusion with other attention mechanisms and to more accurately reflect the methodology.
*   An ablation study exploring different statistics for generating the activation-based maps would significantly strengthen the paper. This would help justify the choice of `sum(|A|^p)` and demonstrate that this formulation is indeed a good choice.
*   The paper would be much stronger if the gradient-based transfer section were either removed or significantly expanded with more rigorous experiments that match the setup of the activation-based methods.
*   The visualization in Figure 4 is interesting, but it would be more compelling to show a quantitative analysis correlating the "attention" patterns with network performance, rather than just a qualitative observation.

#### **Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the specific mathematical formulation of the activation-based attention maps? Have you experimented with other channel-wise summary statistics (e.g., variance, L1 norm, etc.), and if so, how did they perform in comparison?
2.  Regarding the ImageNet experiments, could you elaborate on your efforts to tune Knowledge Distillation? Specifically, what range of temperatures and alpha values were explored? Given that KD is a very strong baseline, the claim that it "hurts convergence" requires more substantial evidence.
3.  In your CIFAR experiments comparing AT to full-activation transfer (F-ActT), how did you ensure a fair comparison in hyperparameter tuning, particularly for the loss weighting coefficient `β`? Is it possible that the suboptimal performance of F-ActT was due to its sensitivity to this hyperparameter?
