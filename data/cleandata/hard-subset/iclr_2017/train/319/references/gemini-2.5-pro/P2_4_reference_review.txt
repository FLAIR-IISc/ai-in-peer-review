### Review of "Attention Transfer"

**1. Summary of Claims/Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer mechanism called Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network's performance by training it to mimic the spatial attention patterns of a more powerful "teacher" network. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps: 1) Activation-based maps, derived from summary statistics (e.g., sum of squared activations) across feature channels at intermediate layers, and 2) Gradient-based maps, derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. The proposed loss function encourages the student's normalized attention maps to match the teacher's. The authors present a comprehensive set of experiments across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN), demonstrating that AT consistently improves student performance. Key findings include that AT outperforms transferring full activation tensors (a la FitNets), can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD) for further gains, and, notably, provides significant improvements on ImageNet where the authors claim standard KD is less effective.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Intuitive Method:** The paper proposes a simple, elegant, and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer. The idea of distilling *where* a network focuses its computational resources, rather than just matching its output predictions or intermediate feature representations, is a compelling and novel contribution to the field.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Support:** The claims are backed by extensive experiments across multiple datasets, network architectures, and training paradigms (from-scratch, fine-tuning). The consistent improvements, especially the significant gains shown on ImageNet and the direct comparison showing superiority over full-activation transfer, provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness and generalizability.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's primary strength lies in its central idea. Traditional knowledge distillation focuses on the final output distribution (soft labels), while methods like FitNets transfer knowledge by matching entire intermediate activation tensors. This paper carves out a clever middle ground. By summarizing activations into a 2D spatial map, it captures the crucial spatial "gist" of the teacher's computation without forcing the student to replicate the teacher's exact high-dimensional feature representation. This is well-justified by the empirical result in Table 1 and Figure 7(b), where AT provides significantly better accuracy than full-activation transfer (F-ActT), suggesting that the compressed attention information is a more potent and flexible form of guidance.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
*   On **CIFAR**, the authors demonstrate AT's effectiveness in various teacher-student pairings (same depth, different depth) and show that it combines additively with traditional knowledge distillation. The ablation study on different attention mapping functions (Table 2) is valuable, providing practical guidance for implementation.
*   The exploration of **gradient-based AT** is also a valuable contribution. Although computationally more expensive and tested in a more constrained setting, it offers an alternative view of attention and the proposed "symmetry norm" for enforcing flip invariance is an interesting regularization technique in its own right.
*   The results on **large-scale datasets** are the most impactful. The success in the fine-tuning setting (CUB, Scenes) demonstrates practical utility. The observation that AT works much better than KD on the fine-grained CUB dataset provides strong intuitive support for the method; intermediate spatial attention is likely critical for distinguishing subtle object parts, a signal that is lost in standard KD. The 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement on **ImageNet** for ResNet-18 is a significant result and a powerful proof-of-concept for the method's scalability.

Finally, the paper is well-written and clear. The methodology is explained precisely, and the provision of code enhances reproducibility.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could further improve it:

*   **Overly Strong Claim on ImageNet KD:** The statement in Section 4.2.2, "...we can assume that proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," is likely an overstatement. While standard KD can be tricky between heterogeneous architectures, various forms of knowledge transfer and distillation have been explored in the context of ImageNet. This claim should be softened to something like, "Our method demonstrates strong performance on ImageNet, succeeding in a cross-architecture setting where we found standard KD with our hyperparameters to be unstable." This would be more accurate without diminishing the significance of the result.
*   **Details on Gradient-Based AT Limitations:** The experiments for gradient-based AT are limited to a NIN model without batch normalization due to the complexities of the second backpropagation step. It would be beneficial to expand on these challenges. A brief discussion on the theoretical and practical difficulties of handling BN statistics during the second-order differentiation would add depth and provide a clearer direction for future work.
*   **Hyperparameter β:** The explanation for setting `β` is somewhat ad-hoc ("we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements..."). A more formal justification or a brief sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper's rigor. For instance, explaining its role as a relative weighting of the cross-entropy and attention losses and showing that performance is robust across a reasonable range of values would be helpful.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The visualizations are good. To make them more impactful, it would be powerful to include a figure in the main paper showing a specific example where the baseline student fails but the AT-trained student succeeds, and then visually compare the student's attention map (before and after AT) to the teacher's map to illustrate *how* the transferred attention led to the correct classification.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the claim regarding the novelty of applying knowledge transfer to ImageNet? Were your difficulties with KD specific to the cross-architecture transfer (ResNet-34 to ResNet-18), or did you find it challenging even in same-architecture settings?
2.  You state that normalizing the vectorized attention maps (`Q/||Q||_2`) is "important for the success" of the training. Could you provide more intuition or an ablation study on this? Does the training diverge or simply result in lower performance without this normalization?
3.  What is the practical training time overhead of activation-based AT? Since the teacher's forward pass is already required for methods like KD, is the main cost simply the computation of the summary statistic and the additional backpropagation through the L2 loss term?
