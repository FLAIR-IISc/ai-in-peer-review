### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel method for knowledge distillation where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The central hypothesis is that transferring information about *where* a network focuses its computational resources is a more effective and efficient form of knowledge transfer than matching entire feature activations. The authors propose and evaluate two distinct definitions of attention: (1) activation-based attention, derived by summarizing activation tensors across the channel dimension (e.g., using sum of squares), and (2) gradient-based attention, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image as a sensitivity map. Through extensive experiments on CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, the authors demonstrate that activation-based AT consistently improves student network performance, can be combined with traditional knowledge distillation, and provides a greater benefit than transferring full activation tensors (a la FitNets). Notably, they report a successful application of this knowledge transfer technique on the challenging ImageNet dataset, a domain where other distillation methods have reportedly struggled.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The core idea of using compressed spatial attention maps as the medium for knowledge transfer is novel, intuitive, and well-motivated. The paper's primary contribution, activation-based attention transfer, is supported by a strong and comprehensive set of experiments that convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness across multiple datasets, tasks, and modern architectures.

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  **Novel and Effective Distillation Mechanism:** The paper successfully bridges two distinct areas of research: network visualization/saliency and knowledge distillation. Instead of forcing a student to replicate the teacher's high-dimensional and potentially idiosyncratic feature space (as in FitNets), AT distills a more abstract and arguably more fundamental piece of information: the spatial allocation of focus. The direct comparison showing AT outperforms full-activation transfer is a key result that validates this central hypothesis.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The authors provide compelling evidence for activation-based AT. The consistent improvements on CIFAR-10 across various WRN configurations are significant. More importantly, the method's success in the transfer learning setting (CUB, Scenes) and on the large-scale ImageNet dataset demonstrates its practical utility and scalability. The claimed success on ImageNet is particularly noteworthy, as this is a known challenge for many distillation techniques.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strength lies in the simplicity and effectiveness of the activation-based AT method. The formulation of the attention map `F(A)` and the corresponding loss function is straightforward, computationally inexpensive (adding minimal overhead to standard training), and easy to implement. The authors perform valuable ablation studies, for instance, comparing different aggregation functions (`F_sum`, `F_max`) and showing that placing transfer losses at multiple network stages is beneficial.

The positioning with respect to related work is clear. The authors correctly identify the connection to FitNets but clearly articulate their key difference: distilling a low-dimensional summary (the attention map) versus the full high-dimensional activation tensor. This makes their contribution distinct and well-contextualized.

While the gradient-based AT section is less developed, the activation-based AT contribution is substantial enough on its own to merit publication at ICLR. The experiments in Section 4.1.1 and 4.2 are thorough and provide a solid foundation for the paper's claims. The consistent performance gains, especially when combined with knowledge distillation (AT+KD), suggest this is a valuable tool for model compression and performance enhancement.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

1.  **Underdeveloped Gradient-Based Method:** The gradient-based attention transfer section feels preliminary. The experiments are limited to a single, non-standard network configuration (NIN without BN or random crops) on CIFAR-10. The authors acknowledge the technical challenge of handling Batch Normalization during the required second backpropagation pass. This is a major limitation that prevents its application to modern, state-of-the-art architectures. I suggest the authors either significantly strengthen this section by addressing the BN issue or reframe it more explicitly as a preliminary exploration and a direction for future work. The current presentation gives it almost equal weight to the activation-based method, which is not justified by the experimental support.

2.  **Analysis of Attention Maps:** The paper provides some qualitative visualizations of the attention maps, which are helpful. However, a deeper analysis could provide more insight. For example, why do sum-based functions (`F_sum`) consistently outperform max-based ones (`F_max`)? This might suggest that the consensus of multiple feature channels is more important for attention than the single most active channel. A brief discussion on this could enhance the paper's theoretical grounding.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the loss weight `β` is set based on a heuristic involving the size of the attention map. It would be beneficial to include a brief sensitivity analysis. How much does performance change with different orders of magnitude for `β`? Showing that the method is robust to this choice would strengthen the results.

4.  **Clarity on Knowledge Distillation (KD) on ImageNet:** The claim that KD struggles on ImageNet is a strong one and is based on the authors' own negative results and an absence of literature. It would be fairer to state that *their implementation* of KD with their chosen hyperparameters did not yield improvements, while acknowledging that successful application may be possible with extensive tuning. This would slightly soften the claim while still highlighting the comparative ease and success of their own method.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Regarding the gradient-based AT, could you elaborate on the specific technical challenge with Batch Normalization during the second backpropagation? Is the issue related to whether the batch statistics should be updated, or is it a more fundamental incompatibility with the gradient computation in existing autodiff frameworks?

2.  What is the computational overhead of activation-based AT compared to baseline training and standard KD? A quantitative measure (e.g., % increase in training time per epoch) would be helpful to substantiate the claim that the cost is minimal.

3.  In the fine-tuning experiments (Section 4.2.1), you freeze the first group of residual blocks. Was this done for the baseline ResNet-18 as well, or only for the student network during AT?

4.  Could you provide more intuition on why transferring a 2D spatial summary is more effective than transferring the full 3D activation tensor? Does this imply that the student network has more freedom to learn its own feature representations, as long as it focuses on the right spatial locations?
