### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer mechanism, termed "Attention Transfer" (AT), designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" network by leveraging knowledge from a larger "teacher" network. The core idea is that instead of transferring knowledge via logits (as in knowledge distillation) or full intermediate feature maps (as in FitNets), the student can learn by mimicking the teacher's spatial "attention." The authors define two distinct forms of attention maps: (1) activation-based maps, which summarize the statistics (e.g., sum or max of powered absolute values) of activation tensors across the channel dimension, and (2) gradient-based maps, which capture the sensitivity of the network's output to its input. The paper presents a framework for incorporating these attention maps into the training loss. Empirically, the authors demonstrate that this approach yields performance improvements across a variety of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). Notably, they claim that activation-based AT outperforms transferring full activations and is the first knowledge transfer method to show success on the ImageNet dataset.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Methodological Rigor and Justification:** The proposed methods, particularly the activation-based attention transfer, rely on critical design choices and hyperparameters (e.g., the scaling factor β, the choice of attention function) that are poorly justified and appear ad-hoc. This lack of principled motivation undermines the robustness and generalizability of the proposed technique.
2.  **Incomplete and Unconvincing Experimental Evaluation:** While the paper presents a breadth of experiments, the execution lacks depth and rigor. The gradient-based attention transfer is tested on a highly constrained and non-standard setup, limiting its significance. The key ImageNet results are presented as preliminary, with the authors admitting to a lack of hyperparameter tuning and incomplete application of the method. This suggests the work is not yet mature enough for publication.
3.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a substantial number of typographical errors, grammatical inconsistencies, and major formatting issues, most notably a completely broken reference section. This level of carelessness detracts from the paper's credibility and makes a thorough assessment of its context within the literature difficult.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on a meticulous examination of the paper's details:

*   **Ambiguity in Loss Formulation:** The definition of the hyperparameter β in Equation (2) is critical. The authors state in Section 4.1.1 that "it usually varies about 0.1, as we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements in attention map and batch size for each layer." This is an unprincipled heuristic, not a methodologically sound choice. Why 10^3? How was this value determined? A proper ablation study on the effect of β is absent. Furthermore, the normalization of attention maps `Q/‖Q‖2` in Eq. (2) is susceptible to division-by-zero if an attention map happens to be all zeros, however unlikely. A more robust formulation would include a small epsilon in the denominator.

*   **Weakness of Gradient-Based AT Contribution:** The entire gradient-based attention transfer section feels premature. The authors themselves state they "constrained ourselves to Network-In-Network without batch normalization and CIFAR dataset, due to the need of complex automatic differentiation." They further admit that "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Devising a method that is incompatible with a ubiquitous component like batch normalization and only validating it on a single, non-standard configuration severely weakens this as a contribution. The results in Table 3, where activation-based AT outperforms all gradient-based variants in the same setting, further question the value of this section.

*   **Incomplete ImageNet Experiments:** The ImageNet results are presented as the paper's strongest empirical evidence, yet the authors explicitly state, "we also did not have time to tune any hyperparameters and kept them from finetuning experiments" and "we plan to update the paper with losses on all 4 groups of residual blocks." A top-tier conference paper should present completed, not planned, work. The claim of being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is bold and requires exceptionally strong and complete evidence, which is not provided here.

*   **Superficial Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison with "full-activation transfer" (F-ActT) is crucial for justifying the "attention" component. However, the details of this baseline are sparse. The authors mention using 1x1 convolutions but provide little information on the training specifics. Given that methods like FitNets have their own complexities, a more thorough and carefully implemented baseline is needed to make a convincing claim of superiority.

*   **Formatting and Clarity Issues:** The paper is rife with minor but cumulative flaws.
    *   **References:** The bibliography is unusable. Entries like `Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E, Bahdanau et al\.,` are nonsensical and repeated, indicating a failure in the toolchain and a lack of proofreading.
    *   **Typos/Grammar:** There are numerous grammatical errors (e.g., "was shown that it could learn," "symmetry norm is one the best performing attention norms," "in favor of simpler meanstd normalization").
    *   **Notation:** The use of Matlab-style notation (`abs(Ai).∧p`) is unconventional. The reuse of `p` for the norm type in Equation (2) after defining it as an exponent in the attention functions is confusing.
    *   **Informal Tone:** Phrases like "we think that knowledge transfer needs to be revisited" and admissions of running out of time are not appropriate for a formal publication.

**4. Additional Feedback**

Despite my recommendation, I believe the core idea of using compressed activation statistics for knowledge transfer is interesting and potentially valuable. To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   Focus the paper exclusively on activation-based AT, which is the more mature and promising contribution. The gradient-based section could be reserved for future work once the technical challenges (e.g., interaction with batch normalization) are resolved.
*   Provide a principled justification or at least a thorough sensitivity analysis for the hyperparameter β. Show how performance changes as this crucial parameter is varied.
*   Complete the ImageNet experiments. This includes applying transfer losses to all relevant layers and performing at least some minimal hyperparameter tuning to demonstrate that the results are robust.
*   Conduct a more rigorous comparison against FitNets, ensuring the baseline is implemented faithfully and tuned properly.
*   Thoroughly proofread the manuscript to fix all grammatical errors, typos, and, most critically, the entire reference section. A polished presentation is essential for conveying the work's quality.

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you provide a more formal motivation for the scaling factor β formulation (`10^3 / (N * B)`)? Is there an intuition that the magnitude of the attention loss should be inversely proportional to the size of the feature map and batch?
*   Table 2 shows that the choice of the power `p` in `Fpsum` impacts performance, with `p=2` being optimal in that case. How sensitive is the method to this choice on other datasets/architectures? Is `p=2` a universally good choice?
*   In the Related Work, you state you take an "opposite to FitNets approach." Could you clarify this? FitNets also transfers knowledge from a larger teacher to a smaller student using intermediate representations. Your method appears to be a refinement of this concept, not its opposite.
