### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge distillation where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct ways to define these spatial attention maps:
1.  **Activation-based AT:** Attention maps are derived from intermediate activation tensors by summarizing features across the channel dimension (e.g., using the sum or max of powered absolute values). This method is computationally efficient and easy to implement.
2.  **Gradient-based AT:** Attention is defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image, capturing input sensitivity. This method is more computationally intensive, requiring a second backpropagation step.

The core contribution is the idea that transferring these compressed, spatially-focused attention maps can be more effective for improving student performance than transferring the full, high-dimensional activation tensors, as done in prior work like FitNets. The authors provide extensive empirical validation for their claims, showing consistent performance improvements across a variety of architectures (NIN, ResNets, Wide ResNets) and datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and notably, ImageNet).

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper presents a simple, intuitive, and novel approach to knowledge transfer. The core idea—that a compressed representation of *where* a network looks is a more effective "hint" than the full feature map—is compelling and well-supported by the results. The proposed activation-based AT is particularly strong due to its simplicity and low computational overhead.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The authors rigorously test their method across a wide range of settings. The consistent gains on CIFAR, fine-tuning tasks, and especially ImageNet provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness and generality. The direct comparison showing AT outperforms full-activation transfer is a crucial result that strengthens the paper's main claim.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's strengths lie in both its conceptual simplicity and its thorough experimental backing.

**On the Method's Novelty and Insight:** The idea of knowledge distillation is not new, but the authors reframe the problem of what knowledge is most valuable to transfer. Instead of forcing the student to replicate the teacher's high-dimensional internal representations (as in FitNets), AT forces the student to replicate the teacher's *focus*. This is a significant conceptual shift. The finding that this low-dimensional summary (the attention map) leads to better final accuracy than the full activation tensor is a surprising and important insight for the field. It suggests that abstracting *what is important* is more beneficial than a direct feature mimicry.

**On the Experimental Rigor:**
*   **Breadth of Evaluation:** The method is not just tested on a single toy problem. Its success on CIFAR with modern WRN architectures, on fine-grained (CUB) and scene classification (Scenes) tasks, and on the large-scale ImageNet dataset demonstrates its robustness.
*   **Strong Baselines and Ablations:** The authors compare against strong student baselines and also show that AT can be combined with traditional knowledge distillation (KD) for further gains. The ablation study on different activation-based attention functions (Table 2) and the effect of using multiple transfer losses are valuable additions that build confidence in the results.
*   **ImageNet Success:** Applying knowledge transfer to ImageNet has historically been challenging. The authors report a significant 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for a ResNet-18 student, a non-trivial gain at this scale. Their claim that this may be the first successful application of a hint-based transfer method on ImageNet is a strong point. While traditional KD has been attempted, the authors' method seems more straightforwardly successful in this context.
*   **Gradient-based Exploration:** While the gradient-based method is less practical due to its computational cost and complexities with batch normalization, its inclusion is valuable. It provides an alternative perspective on defining attention and the proposed "symmetry norm" is an interesting regularization technique in its own right. The authors are transparent about the limitations of these experiments, which is commendable.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

1.  **Clarity on Hyperparameter β:** The explanation for setting the loss weight β is brief: "it usually varies about 0.1, as we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements in attention map and batch size for each layer." This sounds somewhat ad-hoc. A short discussion on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter or a more principled justification for this heuristic would be beneficial for reproducibility and practical application.

2.  **Framing of "Attention":** The introduction frames the work in the context of human cognitive attention. While a good motivation, the proposed mechanisms are essentially summaries of feature statistics. It might be helpful to briefly clarify that the term "attention" here is used in a functional sense (i.e., identifying important spatial regions for a network's decision) and is distinct from other mechanisms like the self-attention in Transformers.

3.  **Visualization:** The paper mentions that "student’s attention maps indeed look more similar to teacher’s (Fig. 6, Appendix)." This is a key piece of qualitative evidence supporting the paper's premise. Given its importance, if space permits, moving this figure from the appendix into the main body of the paper would strengthen the narrative.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  In the activation-based AT, the maps are created by summarizing along the channel axis. This implicitly assumes that all channels contribute to a single, unified attention map for a given layer. Have you considered alternatives, such as learning a weighted combination of channels or clustering channels to form multiple attention maps, to see if more complex spatial information could be transferred?

2.  The gradient-based AT experiments were limited to a network without batch normalization due to uncertainty about its behavior during the second backpropagation. Could you elaborate on the specific technical challenge? Is the issue whether the BN statistics should be re-calculated, kept fixed, or if they should also receive gradients from the second pass?

3.  The claim of being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is very strong. Could you clarify the scope of this claim? For instance, does it refer specifically to hint-based/intermediate-representation transfer methods, as opposed to standard output-logit distillation? A more precise statement with citations to related (even if less successful) attempts on ImageNet would further solidify this contribution.
