### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method called Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network's performance by training it to mimic the "attention" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors define attention as spatial maps that highlight the regions of an input image that a network focuses on to make its decision. Two distinct methods for generating these attention maps are introduced: 1) an activation-based method, where maps are summary statistics (e.g., sum of squared values) computed across the channel dimension of activation tensors, and 2) a gradient-based method, where maps are derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. A loss function is added to the student's training objective to minimize the L2 distance between its attention maps and the teacher's at corresponding layers. The paper provides extensive experimental validation, demonstrating that AT consistently improves student network performance across a variety of architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN) and datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet). Notably, the authors show that activation-based AT outperforms transferring full activation tensors (as in FitNets) and is the first hint-based knowledge transfer method to show significant gains on the ImageNet dataset.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method of using summarized spatial attention maps for knowledge transfer is a simple, intuitive, and novel extension of hint-based distillation. It provides a new perspective on what information is most valuable to transfer from a teacher to a student.
2.  The paper presents strong and comprehensive empirical evidence supporting its claims. The method shows consistent improvements across multiple datasets, tasks, and network architectures, including a notable +1.1% top-1 accuracy gain on ImageNet, where previous hint-based methods have not demonstrated success.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper tackles the well-established problem of knowledge distillation/transfer. It is well-motivated by positioning its contribution against prior work like Knowledge Distillation (KD) by Hinton et al. and FitNets by Romero et al. While KD transfers knowledge via softened output logits and FitNets uses full intermediate feature maps as "hints," this work proposes that a compressed representation of intermediate features—spatial attention—is a more effective form of knowledge to transfer.

**Novelty and Technical Soundness:** The primary novelty lies in the formulation of attention maps as the medium for knowledge transfer. The two proposed formulations are technically sound.
*   **Activation-based AT:** This method is simple to implement and computationally cheap. The definition of attention maps via summary statistics (e.g., `F_sum^p(A) = sum(|A_i|^p)`) is clearly defined in Section 3.1. The intuition that the magnitude of activations correlates with importance is plausible and supported by visualizations (Fig. 2, 4). The comparison against transferring full activations (Table 1, column "F-ActT") provides direct evidence for the authors' claim that attention maps are a more effective form of hint, which is a significant finding.
*   **Gradient-based AT:** This method connects knowledge transfer to input sensitivity maps. The formulation in Section 3.2 is more complex, requiring a second-order derivative (double backpropagation), but the derivation is clear and correctly linked to prior work. While the experiments for this method are more limited, they still demonstrate a performance gain over the baseline (Table 3).

**Empirical Support:** The paper's claims are well-supported by a rigorous experimental evaluation.
*   **CIFAR Experiments (Section 4.1):** The results in Table 1 show consistent improvements for various teacher/student pairs of Wide Residual Networks. AT outperforms the student baseline and provides complementary gains when combined with KD. The ablation study on different attention mapping functions (Table 2) is useful and justifies the choice of `F_sum^2`.
*   **Large-scale Experiments (Section 4.2):** The experiments on CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet are crucial for demonstrating the method's general applicability. The results on ImageNet, improving a ResNet-18 baseline by 1.1% top-1 accuracy using a ResNet-34 teacher, are particularly strong. The authors claim this is the first successful application of a hint-based transfer method to ImageNet, which, if correct, is a significant contribution. The failure of their KD implementation on ImageNet further highlights the practical utility of the proposed AT method in challenging, large-scale settings. The qualitative results in the Appendix (Fig. 6) also support the claim that the student's attention maps become more similar to the teacher's after training.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound method, supports its claims with strong empirical results, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation.

### **4. Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   **Intuition for AT's Superiority:** The paper states it is "quite interesting that attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" but offers limited intuition as to why. A more detailed discussion would be beneficial. For example, does summarizing across the channel dimension act as a form of regularization, forcing the student to learn *where* to look without being overly constrained by *how* the teacher represents features?
*   **Gradient-based AT Limitations:** The authors are transparent about the limitations of their gradient-based AT experiments (limited to NIN on CIFAR without Batch Normalization). While understandable, this makes the gradient-based portion of the contribution less convincing than the activation-based one. Further investigation into handling BN during the second backpropagation would significantly strengthen this part of the work.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The weighting parameter `β` is critical. The paper describes a heuristic for setting it (`10^3` divided by map size and batch size). A sensitivity analysis of `β` would provide readers with a better understanding of the method's robustness and ease of use.
*   **Clarity of Figures:** Some figures could be improved. For instance, in Figure 4, the captions could more explicitly guide the reader on what to observe (e.g., "Note the stronger focus on the wolf's face in ResNet-101 compared to NIN").

### **5. Questions for Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
1.  Could you elaborate on the intuition for why transferring summarized attention maps is more effective than transferring full activation tensors (FitNets-style)? Does forcing the student to only match the spatial focus while allowing it freedom in its channel-wise feature representation explain the superior performance?
2.  Your ImageNet experiments show that AT succeeds where your implementation of KD failed. This is a very interesting result. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameters you tried for KD on ImageNet? Is it possible that KD requires substantially different hyperparameters (e.g., temperature, `α` weighting) in large-scale settings compared to CIFAR?
3.  Regarding gradient-based AT, you note the challenge of handling Batch Normalization in the second backpropagation step. Did you consider or experiment with simpler approaches, such as freezing the running statistics of the BN layers during the second pass, and could you comment on the potential theoretical difficulties?
4.  How sensitive is activation-based AT to the choice of layers where the transfer loss is applied? Your CIFAR experiment suggests using all groups is best, but is there a general heuristic, especially when student and teacher depths differ significantly? For example, is it more important to match attention in later, more semantic layers?
