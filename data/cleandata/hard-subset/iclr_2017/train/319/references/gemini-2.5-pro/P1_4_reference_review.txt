### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the spatial attention patterns of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose and investigate two distinct definitions of attention maps: (1) activation-based maps, derived from summary statistics (e.g., sum of squared activations) across the channel dimension of a feature tensor, and (2) gradient-based maps, defined by the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image. The core idea is to add a loss term that penalizes the difference between the student's and teacher's attention maps at various layers.

The key contributions are:
1.  The proposal of using spatial attention maps as the medium for transferring knowledge, offering a middle ground between transferring final logits (Knowledge Distillation) and full intermediate feature maps (FitNets).
2.  The introduction and evaluation of both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer mechanisms.
3.  Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN).
4.  Empirical evidence showing that AT provides significant performance gains, can be combined with Knowledge Distillation, and crucially, outperforms a FitNets-style transfer of full activation tensors.
5.  A demonstration of the method's effectiveness on the large-scale ImageNet dataset, where the authors claim it is one of the first successful applications of this type of knowledge transfer.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and intuitive idea, backed by solid experimental results.

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach:** The core concept of transferring attention is an elegant and novel contribution to the knowledge distillation literature. It provides a compelling hypothesis about what kind of information is most valuable to transfer from a teacher: not the raw features, but *where* the network is focusing its representational capacity. This is a more targeted and potentially less noisy signal than transferring entire high-dimensional activation tensors.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their claims across a diverse set of benchmarks and architectures. The results are consistently positive. The comparison showing that AT is superior to transferring full activations (Section 4.1.2) is a particularly strong piece of evidence for the central thesis. Furthermore, the successful application to ImageNet is a significant result that demonstrates the scalability and practical utility of the proposed method.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strength lies in its simple, well-motivated idea and the rigorous experiments that support it. The motivation, drawn from an analogy to human perception, is intuitive and helps frame the problem effectively. The formulation of activation-based attention maps is straightforward and computationally efficient, making it a highly practical method. The authors perform valuable ablation studies, such as comparing different attention mapping functions (Table 2) and evaluating the contribution of losses at different network depths.

The finding that AT outperforms full-activation transfer is significant. It suggests that forcing a student to match a teacher's exact feature representation might be too restrictive, whereas guiding its *focus* provides a more effective form of regularization, allowing the student to learn its own efficient representations while still benefiting from the teacher's guidance.

The gradient-based attention transfer, while less extensively evaluated, introduces interesting ideas, particularly the symmetry-based regularizer (Eq. 6), which connects to principles of equivariance. Though the experiments are limited, this section adds depth to the paper's exploration of "attention."

Finally, the positive results on fine-tuning tasks (CUB, Scenes) and the large-scale ImageNet training are crucial. The observation that AT is more beneficial than KD on the fine-grained CUB dataset provides strong evidence for the utility of transferring intermediate spatial information, which is likely critical for distinguishing between subtle object classes.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help improve it further. These are suggestions for refinement and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Hyperparameters:** The explanation for setting the loss weight `β` is a bit vague ("it usually varies about 0.1, as we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements..."). Providing a more concrete table of the `β` values used for each experiment or a more formal justification for this scaling rule would improve reproducibility.
*   **Gradient-based AT Experiments:** The experiments for the gradient-based method are limited to a simplified setting (NIN without batch normalization). While the authors provide a reason (complexity of second-order derivatives with BN), this section feels less complete than the activation-based section. It would be beneficial to either expand on the theoretical challenges of BN during double backpropagation or to more explicitly frame this part as preliminary work that opens avenues for future research.
*   **The "First on ImageNet" Claim:** The claim in Section 4.2.2 that AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is very strong. While the authors correctly note that many prior works focus on smaller datasets, some forms of distillation have been explored on ImageNet. It would be safer and more precise to soften this claim to "one of the first" or to state that it "demonstrates strong performance in a setting where prior methods like FitNets and KD have reported difficulties, particularly between architectures of different depths."
*   **Visualizations:** The paper's core idea is highly visual. While some visualizations are provided (and more are in the appendix), including a strong, comparative figure in the main text showing the attention maps of (a) the teacher, (b) the student baseline, and (c) the student after AT for a compelling ImageNet example could make the paper's message even more impactful.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide more intuition as to *why* transferring summary statistics (attention maps) works significantly better than transferring the full activation tensors? Is the primary benefit a form of regularization that prevents overfitting to the teacher's specific feature implementation, or is it that the full activations contain noisy, irrelevant information that harms the student's training?
2.  What is the computational overhead of activation-based AT during training compared to a baseline student training? And how does it compare to the overhead of the gradient-based AT? A simple metric like percentage increase in wall-clock time per epoch would be very informative for practitioners.
3.  In the activation-based method, `p=2` was chosen for the `F_sum^p` function. How sensitive is the final performance to the choice of this exponent `p`? Does a very large `p` (which would approximate a `max` operation on the most activated neuron) degrade performance significantly?
