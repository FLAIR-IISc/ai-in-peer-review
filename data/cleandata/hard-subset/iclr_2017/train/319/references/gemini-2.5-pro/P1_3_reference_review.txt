### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method called Attention Transfer (AT). The central idea is to improve a smaller "student" network by training it to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The paper introduces two distinct ways to define these attention maps: (1) activation-based, where maps are generated by summarizing activation tensors across the channel dimension (e.g., sum of absolute values), and (2) gradient-based, where maps are defined by the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. The authors formulate a loss function that encourages similarity between the student's and teacher's normalized attention maps at various layers. Through extensive experiments on CIFAR, CUB, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, the paper demonstrates that AT significantly improves student network performance. Key findings include that AT can be combined with traditional Knowledge Distillation (KD) for further gains, and, notably, that transferring these compressed attention maps is more effective than transferring the full, unsummarized activation tensors, a method similar to that used in FitNets. The paper also presents a successful application of knowledge transfer to ImageNet, improving a ResNet-18 model using a ResNet-34 teacher, a scenario where they report other methods like KD struggle.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The core contribution—using spatial attention maps as the medium for knowledge transfer—is a simple, intuitive, and novel idea. It provides a new perspective on what constitutes transferable knowledge, shifting the focus from mimicking output distributions (KD) or exact feature representations (FitNets) to mimicking the spatial focus of a network.

2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evidence:** The paper provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of Attention Transfer across a variety of datasets, tasks (classification from scratch, fine-tuning), and modern architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). The result on ImageNet, showing a significant 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18, is particularly strong and addresses a challenging large-scale benchmark where successful knowledge transfer has been less commonly reported.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's claims are well-supported by its rigorous experimental setup.

*   **Superiority over Alternatives:** A crucial experiment is the direct comparison between Attention Transfer (AT) and transferring full activations (F-ActT). The results in Table 1 and Figure 7(b) clearly show that the proposed method of transferring summarized attention maps yields substantially better final accuracy. This finding is non-obvious and suggests that forcing a student to replicate the teacher's exact feature implementation is less effective than guiding it on *where* to focus its representational capacity. This is a significant insight.
*   **Generality and Robustness:** The method's effectiveness is demonstrated not just in one setting but across multiple scenarios. It works for student/teacher pairs of the same depth (WRN-16-2/WRN-16-1) and different depths (WRN-40-1/WRN-16-1). It provides benefits when training from scratch (CIFAR, ImageNet) and during fine-tuning (CUB, Scenes). Furthermore, its ability to combine additively with Knowledge Distillation (AT+KD) enhances its practical utility.
*   **Significant Impact on a Major Benchmark:** The successful application to ImageNet is a major strength. The authors claim that AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" in this context. While this is a bold claim, their reported failure to get KD to work between different ResNet architectures on ImageNet aligns with anecdotal evidence that cross-architecture distillation can be challenging. The positive result from AT in this difficult setting makes the contribution particularly impactful. The 1.1% gain for ResNet-18 is not trivial and pushes a standard architecture to a stronger performance level.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

1.  **Intuition on AT vs. Full Activation Transfer:** The paper empirically establishes that transferring summarized attention is better than transferring full activations, which is a key finding. However, the paper could benefit from a deeper discussion on the potential reasons for this. Is it because AT acts as a form of spatial regularization? Does it give the student network more freedom in learning its own feature representations, as long as the spatial saliency is correct? A more developed hypothesis here would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

2.  **Gradient-Based Attention Transfer:** The experiments for gradient-based AT are interesting but feel preliminary compared to the comprehensive results for activation-based AT. They are limited to a non-standard NIN architecture on CIFAR without batch normalization. The authors are transparent about the technical challenges (e.g., interaction with batch norm during the second backpropagation), but this limits the generality of the claims for this part of the method. It might be better to frame this section as a promising preliminary exploration rather than a contribution on par with the activation-based method.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The loss weighting parameter `β` is described as being set based on a heuristic involving `10^3` and the size of the attention map. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter? A brief ablation study on the effect of `β` would add to the paper's completeness. Similarly, for the ImageNet experiments, the authors state they did not have time to tune hyperparameters. While the result is already strong, it raises the question of whether even greater gains are possible.

4.  **Clarification on the KD Baseline for ImageNet:** The paper makes a strong claim about KD struggling on ImageNet when the teacher and student have different architectures. To make this claim fully convincing, it would be helpful to provide more details about the KD experiments that were attempted. Specifically, which temperature and `α` values were used? Given KD's known sensitivity to these hyperparameters, more detail would help readers assess whether the observed negative result is fundamental or potentially due to a suboptimal hyperparameter choice.
