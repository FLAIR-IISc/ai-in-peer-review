### **Review of "Paying More Attention to Attention"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel knowledge transfer method called Attention Transfer (AT). The core idea is to improve the training of a smaller "student" network by compelling it to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The authors propose two distinct ways to define these spatial attention maps:

1.  **Activation-based AT:** Attention maps are generated by summarizing the activation tensors of intermediate convolutional layers across the channel dimension. The paper explores several summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values, sum of p-th powers) to capture the spatial regions the network focuses on. This method is simple, intuitive, and computationally efficient.
2.  **Gradient-based AT:** Attention is defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image, creating a saliency map that indicates input regions most influential to the final decision. This method is more computationally intensive, requiring a double backpropagation step.

The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10/100, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). They demonstrate that AT consistently improves the performance of student models, can be effectively combined with traditional knowledge distillation (KD), and, notably, outperforms a FitNets-style transfer of full activation tensors. A key claim is that this is one of the first knowledge transfer methods to show significant gains on the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Effective Method:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful new perspective on knowledge distillation. By focusing on transferring a summarized, spatial representation of "where the network looks" rather than the full feature maps or only the final logits, the method provides a compelling middle ground that proves highly effective. The activation-based approach, in particular, is a practical and valuable contribution.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evidence:** The claims are backed by extensive experiments across a variety of standard benchmarks and modern network architectures. The consistent improvements, the successful combination with KD, and the direct comparison showing superiority over full-activation transfer provide a robust validation of the proposed technique. The successful application to ImageNet is a particularly significant result that strengthens the paper's impact.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths lie in its clear motivation, simple execution, and strong results.

**Motivation and Methodology:** The analogy to human attention is well-drawn and provides an intuitive foundation for the work. The formulation of activation-based attention maps is straightforward and elegant. The exploration of different summary functions (`F_sum^p`, `F_max^p`) is a good piece of ablation that validates the design choices. The authors make a crucial finding in Section 4.1.2: transferring these compressed attention maps yields better results than transferring the full activation tensors (as in FitNets). This is a non-obvious result that suggests that attention maps act as a more potent, regularized form of "hint" for the student network, filtering out teacher-specific feature noise while preserving the most critical spatial information.

The gradient-based method, while less extensively evaluated, connects the work to the field of saliency and network interpretability. The proposed symmetry loss (Eq. 6) based on flip invariance is a clever and novel regularization technique in its own right, showing promising results in its limited setting.

**Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup is thorough. The authors compare their method against strong baselines and relevant competing approaches like KD and full-activation transfer (F-ActT). The results in Table 1 are convincing, showing that AT provides a significant boost on its own (~0.8% on WRN-16-1) and offers additive gains when combined with KD (~1.2% improvement). The transfer learning experiments on CUB and Scenes (Section 4.2.1) demonstrate the method's utility in fine-tuning scenarios, where it effectively closes the gap between a ResNet-18 and a ResNet-34.

The most impressive result is the successful application to ImageNet, improving a ResNet-18 by 1.1% top-1 accuracy. The authors' claim that other distillation methods often struggle in this setting is plausible, and demonstrating success here significantly elevates the contribution's importance and practical relevance.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could further improve its quality and clarity:

*   **Clarification of "Attention":** The term "attention" is heavily used in the literature to refer to learnable, differentiable modules within a network (e.g., Bahdanau attention, self-attention). The "attention" in this paper is a post-hoc interpretation or saliency map. It would be beneficial to explicitly state this distinction early in the introduction to manage reader expectations and properly position the work.
*   **Analysis of the AT vs. Full-Activation Transfer Result:** The paper empirically shows that AT outperforms F-ActT but offers little intuition as to why. A paragraph discussing potential reasons would be very insightful. For instance, is it because the summary operation acts as a form of regularization, forcing the student to learn a more abstract spatial priority map rather than perfectly replicating the teacher's specific feature values, which may be unachievable?
*   **Gradient-Based AT Experiments:** This section feels less developed than the activation-based part. The experiments are limited to a non-standard NIN architecture on CIFAR-10, with the authors noting technical challenges related to batch normalization in the second backpropagation step. While the results are interesting, this part of the contribution feels preliminary. The paper could be strengthened by either expanding these experiments or framing them more cautiously as a proof-of-concept for future work.
*   **Hyperparameter `β`:** The description of how `β` is set is somewhat ad-hoc ("we set it to 10^3 divided by number of elements..."). A brief sensitivity analysis or a more principled justification for this heuristic would improve reproducibility and understanding.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you provide more intuition on why transferring a *summary* of activations (attention map) is more effective than transferring the full activation tensor? Does this imply that the exact feature representations are less important than the spatial focus of the network?
*   What is the computational overhead of AT? For the activation-based method, it seems negligible. For the gradient-based method, the "double backpropagation" sounds costly. Could you quantify the increase in training time relative to a standard baseline?
*   In the gradient-based method, have you considered using smoother gradient visualization techniques like Grad-CAM instead of the raw input gradient? Since Grad-CAM maps are often more class-discriminative and less noisy, they might provide a better signal for transfer.
*   Did you explore the relative importance of transferring attention from different depths in the network? For example, is transferring high-level attention from the final block more or less important than transferring mid-level attention from earlier blocks?
