### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer from a large, powerful "teacher" network to a smaller "student" network. The core idea is to guide the student's training by forcing its "attention" to mimic that of the teacher. The authors propose two distinct definitions for spatial attention maps: (1) an activation-based method, where attention is defined by summary statistics (e.g., sum of absolute values of activations) across feature channels at a given layer, and (2) a gradient-based method, where attention is defined as the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image. The student is trained with a combined loss function that includes the standard task loss (e.g., cross-entropy) and an attention transfer loss, which penalizes the L2 distance between the teacher's and student's normalized attention maps. The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN), demonstrating that AT consistently improves student performance. Notably, the authors show that activation-based AT outperforms transferring full activation maps (a-la FitNets) and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). They also claim this is the first hint-based knowledge transfer method to show significant improvements on the ImageNet dataset.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novelty and Intuition:** The paper proposes a novel and intuitive form of knowledge transfer. Instead of transferring raw, high-dimensional intermediate representations (as in FitNets), AT transfers a compressed, salient summary of "where the network is looking." This is a well-motivated idea that bridges network visualization techniques with knowledge distillation.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The claims are backed by extensive and convincing experiments. The activation-based AT method shows consistent and significant performance gains across a variety of tasks, datasets, and modern network architectures. The comparison against strong baselines, including vanilla student training, knowledge distillation, and full-activation transfer, compellingly demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed method. The positive results on ImageNet are particularly noteworthy.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its simple yet effective core idea and the thoroughness of its experimental validation for the activation-based method.

*   **Activation-based AT is well-executed:** The definition of activation-based attention maps is straightforward and computationally cheap. The authors perform valuable ablation studies, exploring different aggregation functions (`sum` vs. `max`, different powers `p`) and the impact of placing transfer losses at different network depths. The finding that sum-based functions outperform max-based ones is an interesting insight.
*   **Superiority over Full-Activation Transfer:** The direct comparison showing that AT provides much better final accuracy than transferring full activation tensors (Table 1 and Fig. 7(b)) is a crucial result. It suggests that transferring a compressed summary of spatial importance is more beneficial than forcing the student to exactly replicate the teacher's feature space, which might be an overly restrictive constraint. This finding provides a significant contribution to the understanding of hint-based knowledge transfer.
*   **Success on ImageNet:** The demonstration of a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for a ResNet-18 student on ImageNet is a strong result. The authors state that prior hint-based methods like FitNets and even standard KD have struggled to show benefits on this scale. If this claim holds, it positions AT as a highly practical and impactful technique for training efficient models.
*   **Gradient-based AT as an interesting direction:** While less developed experimentally, the gradient-based AT and the proposed symmetry regularization are interesting concepts. Using double backpropagation to align input sensitivity maps is a creative idea, and showing that it provides improvements (Table 3) opens up a promising research avenue, even if it is computationally more demanding.

### Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Gradient-based AT Limitations:** The experiments for gradient-based AT are limited to a non-standard setup (thin NIN without batch normalization on CIFAR). The authors rightly acknowledge the difficulty of integrating batch norm with double backpropagation. It would be beneficial to be more upfront about these limitations in the main text and frame this section more as a preliminary investigation or a proof-of-concept, to manage reader expectations.
*   **Discussion on "Why AT > Full-Activation Transfer":** The paper empirically shows that AT is better than full activation transfer but could benefit from a deeper discussion on *why* this might be the case. Is it a form of regularization? Does transferring only spatial importance give the student more freedom to learn its own efficient feature representations while still benefiting from the teacher's high-level guidance? Adding a paragraph with such hypotheses would enhance the paper's contribution.
*   **Strengthening the Claim Against Knowledge Distillation on ImageNet:** The paper claims KD struggles on ImageNet when teacher and student architectures differ, but the evidence provided is that it "hurts convergence with the same hyperparameters as on CIFAR." This is a very strong claim against a widely used method based on limited evidence. It is possible that KD simply requires different (and perhaps more careful) hyperparameter tuning for ImageNet. I would suggest softening this claim to state that AT was more straightforward to get working and showed strong results in their experiments, rather than making a general declaration about KD's ineffectiveness in this setting.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper gives a rule of thumb for setting the loss weight `β`. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the method to this hyperparameter would be valuable for practitioners looking to apply this technique. For instance, how much does performance vary within a reasonable range of `β` values?

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you quantify the computational overhead of both activation-based and gradient-based AT? Activation-based AT seems very cheap (a few tensor operations on the forward pass), but the double backpropagation for gradient-based AT seems expensive. A comparison of training time (e.g., hours per epoch) for the baseline, AT (activation), and AT (gradient) would be very useful.
2.  Regarding the ImageNet experiments, did you perform any hyperparameter search for Knowledge Distillation (e.g., for the temperature `T` and the loss weighting `α`), or were the CIFAR-tuned parameters used directly? Clarifying this would help contextualize the comparison between AT and KD on ImageNet.
3.  You mention that normalizing the vectorized attention maps (`Q/||Q||_2`) is "important for the success of the student training." Could you elaborate on what happens if this normalization is omitted? Does the training become unstable, or does it simply lead to suboptimal performance?
