### **Summary**

This paper proposes "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel method for knowledge transfer where a smaller "student" network is trained to mimic the spatial attention maps of a larger, pre-trained "teacher" network. The core idea is that the teacher's "focus" on specific spatial regions of an input image contains valuable information that can guide the student's training process. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps: 1) activation-based maps, which are derived by summarizing activation statistics (e.g., sum of squared values) across the channel dimension of a feature map, and 2) gradient-based maps, which represent the sensitivity of the network's output to its input. Through extensive experiments, the paper demonstrates that adding an "attention transfer loss" that encourages similarity between student and teacher attention maps consistently improves the student's performance. The method is shown to be effective across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNets, Wide ResNets). Notably, the authors show that transferring these compressed attention maps is more effective than transferring full activation tensors (as in FitNets) and that AT can be effectively combined with traditional knowledge distillation.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The paper introduces a simple, intuitive, and novel mechanism for knowledge transfer. The idea of distilling knowledge in the form of spatial attention maps, rather than logits or full feature maps, is a compelling contribution to the field. The activation-based method, in particular, is computationally cheap and easy to implement, making it a highly practical technique.

2.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The claims are backed by a strong and comprehensive set of experiments. The method yields consistent and significant improvements over well-chosen baselines across multiple datasets, architectures, and tasks (training from scratch and fine-tuning). The positive results on the large-scale ImageNet dataset are particularly impressive and demonstrate the scalability and effectiveness of the proposed approach.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The core strength of this paper lies in its central idea and the thoroughness of its empirical validation.

First, the concept of transferring "attention" is an elegant distillation of the "hints" idea from prior work like FitNets. Instead of forcing the student to match every detail of the teacher's high-dimensional intermediate representations, AT focuses on a compressed, spatially-aware summary. The empirical result that AT outperforms full-activation transfer (Table 1, Fig. 7b) strongly supports the hypothesis that these summary statistics capture information more crucial for effective knowledge transfer, perhaps by acting as a better regularizer or by focusing the transfer on spatial reasoning over fine-grained feature representation.

Second, the experimental results are robust and convincing. The authors demonstrate the method's value not just on CIFAR-10 but also on more challenging fine-grained classification (CUB) and scene recognition tasks. The ImageNet experiment, where AT improves a ResNet-18 by 1.1% top-1 accuracy using a ResNet-34 as a teacher, is a significant achievement. The claim that this may be the first successful application of such an intermediate-representation-based knowledge transfer method to ImageNet is a strong one and highlights the paper's practical impact. The ablations on different attention mapping functions (Table 2) and the analysis of combining AT with knowledge distillation (KD) further strengthen the empirical claims.

While the gradient-based transfer portion of the paper is less developed, the activation-based transfer method alone is a sufficiently strong contribution for acceptance. The paper is well-written, the method is clearly explained, and the findings are likely to be influential for future research in knowledge distillation and model compression.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for the decision.

1.  **Clarity on the term "Attention":** The term "attention" is used in an intuitive but heuristic sense, primarily as a summary statistic of activation magnitudes. This differs from the learnable, top-down attention mechanisms commonly found in other areas (e.g., NLP). It would be beneficial to briefly acknowledge this distinction in the introduction or related work to better position the work and manage reader expectations. Framing it as "spatial activation map distillation" could be a more precise, albeit less catchy, description.

2.  **Underdeveloped Gradient-Based Method:** The section on gradient-based AT feels preliminary compared to the activation-based section. The experiments are confined to a simplified setting on CIFAR-10 without standard components like batch normalization, and the authors note the practical challenges. I would suggest either expanding this section with a more thorough investigation (e.g., exploring ways to handle BN) or reframing it as a promising direction for future work, which would allow the paper to focus more on its main, highly successful contribution of activation-based AT.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions how the loss weight `β` is set, but it appears somewhat ad-hoc. Including a small study on the sensitivity of the final model performance to `β` for a key experiment would enhance the paper's reproducibility and provide readers with practical guidance for implementation.

4.  **Details on Baselines:** The comparison to full-activation transfer ("F-ActT") is a cornerstone of the paper's claims. Providing a few more implementation details for this baseline (e.g., initialization and learning rate for the 1x1 convolutions used for shape matching) would help solidify the fairness of the comparison.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The finding that transferring a single-channel attention map is more effective than transferring the full multi-channel activation tensor is very interesting. Do you have a deeper intuition for why this is the case? Could it be that compressing the hint acts as a form of regularization, preventing the student from overfitting to the teacher's specific feature implementations and forcing it to focus only on the high-level spatial importance?

2.  In your gradient-based AT experiments, you noted the difficulty of integrating the required second backpropagation with batch normalization. Do you have any thoughts on how this could be resolved? For example, would it be valid to use the batch statistics computed during the first forward pass as fixed constants during the second backpropagation step?

3.  You report that traditional knowledge distillation (KD) struggled in your ImageNet experiments when the teacher and student had different depths. This is a very interesting empirical result that strongly motivates your work. Could you provide more detail on these experiments, such as the hyperparameters you tried and how specifically the training "hurts convergence"?

4.  In Section 3.1, you state that L2-normalizing the vectorized attention maps before computing the transfer loss is "important for the success of the student training." Could you quantify this with an ablation study showing the performance with and without this normalization step in Equation 2?
