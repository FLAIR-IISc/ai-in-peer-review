### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces "Attention Transfer" (AT), a novel and intuitive method for knowledge distillation. The core idea is to improve a smaller "student" network's performance by training it to mimic the "attention maps" of a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The paper proposes two distinct ways to define these spatial attention maps: (1) activation-based, where statistics (e.g., sum of squared values) are computed across the channel dimension of activation tensors to create a 2D map of important spatial locations, and (2) gradient-based, which uses the gradient of the loss with respect to the input image as a sensitivity map. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that transferring this distilled spatial information significantly boosts student performance across a variety of datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, WRN). Key claims include that activation-based AT is more effective than transferring full activation tensors (a la FitNets) and that it is one of the first knowledge transfer methods to show a significant improvement on the ImageNet dataset.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The proposed method, particularly the activation-based variant, is simple, well-motivated, and elegant. The concept of summarizing a high-dimensional activation tensor into a 2D spatial map as the "hint" for knowledge transfer is a novel insight that appears more effective than transferring the entire, potentially noisy, activation tensor.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The paper provides compelling and comprehensive experimental evidence for its claims. The method consistently yields improvements across multiple datasets, network architectures, and training regimes (from scratch, fine-tuning). The successful application to ImageNet, where a ResNet-18 student gains over 1% top-1 accuracy from a ResNet-34 teacher, is a particularly strong result that many knowledge distillation papers fail to demonstrate.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear hypothesis and rigorous validation. The motivation, drawing an analogy to human attention, is intuitive. Instead of forcing a student to match the teacher's complex internal representations feature-for-feature, AT guides the student on *where* to focus its representational capacity. This acts as a powerful regularizer and a form of curriculum learning.

The experimental setup is thorough. On CIFAR-10, the authors not only show that AT works but also provide crucial ablations. Table 2 explores different formulations of the activation-based maps, finding sum-based methods to be superior. More importantly, Table 1 directly compares AT to transferring full activations ("F-ActT"), showing that the proposed summarization of activations into attention maps is indeed more beneficial than using the raw activations as hints. This finding challenges the assumption that "more information is always better" in hint-based distillation and is a significant contribution in itself.

The results on large-scale datasets solidify the paper's claims. The success in the fine-tuning scenarios on CUB and Scenes is valuable, but the ImageNet experiment is the standout result. The authors report that standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) struggled in their setup, yet AT delivered a 1.1% top-1 improvement. This demonstrates the method's practical utility and potential impact, positioning it as a strong candidate for model compression and performance enhancement in realistic, large-scale settings.

While the gradient-based method is less developed experimentally (notably, its interaction with batch normalization is left as future work), its inclusion is valuable. It presents an alternative perspective on attention and the proposed "symmetry norm" regularizer is an interesting idea. The fact that the simpler, more efficient activation-based AT outperforms it on CIFAR-10 is an important finding for practitioners.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could further improve its clarity and impact:
1.  **Positioning the Gradient-Based Method:** The gradient-based AT experiments are limited and the method is computationally expensive due to the double backpropagation. It would be beneficial to be more explicit about these limitations in the main text and frame this part of the work as a preliminary exploration, while highlighting the activation-based method as the primary, practical contribution.
2.  **Soften the "First on ImageNet" Claim:** The claim that AT "is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is very strong and may be difficult to defend definitively. I suggest softening the language to something like: "demonstrates a significant and successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet, a challenging benchmark where many hint-based methods have not been shown to work effectively."
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The setting of the loss weight `β` is described as `10^3` divided by the number of elements. While this provides a heuristic, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the model's performance to this hyperparameter would strengthen the paper's claims of robustness and improve its reproducibility.
4.  **Qualitative Analysis:** Figure 6 in the appendix provides a nice qualitative visualization of attention maps. Integrating a similar, compelling visual example into the main body of the paper (e.g., in Section 3.1) would help build intuition for the reader about what is actually being transferred.

### **Questions for Authors**

1.  What is your core intuition for why transferring summarized 2D attention maps works better than transferring the full 3D activation tensors? Does the summarization act as a noise filter, or does forcing a match on the full tensor over-constrain the student network?
2.  You mention that standard Knowledge Distillation (KD) struggled to work on ImageNet in your experiments. Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameters you tried (e.g., temperature, `α`) and the nature of the failure (e.g., slower convergence, worse final accuracy)? This would be valuable context for the community.
3.  Could you provide a rough estimate of the computational overhead (e.g., increase in training time) for activation-based AT compared to a baseline student training, standard KD, and full-activation transfer?
4.  Regarding the gradient-based AT, you note that it is unclear how batch normalization should behave during the second backpropagation. Do you have any preliminary thoughts or hypotheses on how this could be handled? For instance, should the batch statistics be frozen, or recalculated?
