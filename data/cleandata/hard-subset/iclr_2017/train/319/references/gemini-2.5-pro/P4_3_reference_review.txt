### 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces Attention Transfer (AT), a novel knowledge transfer mechanism designed to improve the performance of a smaller "student" network by leveraging a larger, more powerful "teacher" network. The core idea is to compel the student network to mimic the spatial attention patterns of the teacher. The authors propose two distinct methods for defining these spatial attention maps:

1.  **Activation-based AT:** Attention maps are derived from the intermediate activation tensors of a convolutional network. The paper defines several functions to summarize activations across the channel dimension into a 2D spatial map (e.g., sum of absolute values of activations). The training objective then minimizes the L2 distance between the normalized attention maps of the student and the teacher at corresponding layers.
2.  **Gradient-based AT:** Attention is defined as the gradient of the loss function with respect to the input image, creating a sensitivity map. The transfer loss minimizes the L2 distance between the student's and teacher's input gradients. This requires a second backpropagation step during training.

The paper provides extensive experimental validation across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet, Wide ResNet). The key claims are that AT significantly improves student network performance, that activation-based AT is more effective than transferring full, unsummarized activation tensors (as in FitNets), that AT can be effectively combined with traditional knowledge distillation (KD), and that it is the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied to the large-scale ImageNet dataset.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons
**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel, intuitive, and simple-to-implement form of knowledge transfer. The concept of transferring summarized spatial attention, rather than logits or full intermediate representations, is a conceptually clear and original contribution to the field of knowledge distillation.
2.  The proposed method is supported by strong and comprehensive empirical evidence across a variety of datasets, network architectures, and tasks (classification and fine-tuning). The reported performance gains, especially the successful application on ImageNet, demonstrate the method's effectiveness and generality.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision
The paper's primary strength lies in its central idea. While knowledge distillation (KD) transfers knowledge at the output layer and methods like FitNets transfer it at intermediate layers, AT proposes a middle ground. Instead of transferring the entire, high-dimensional intermediate activation tensor, it transfers a compressed 2D summary that captures the network's spatial focus. This is a well-motivated and elegant idea that connects the fields of knowledge transfer and network interpretability.

The technical soundness of the proposed methods is high. The activation-based approach is particularly compelling due to its simplicity. The formulation of attention maps via simple statistics (sum/max of powered absolute values) is straightforward and requires no additional parameters, making it computationally efficient and easy to integrate into existing training pipelines. The gradient-based approach, while more complex due to its reliance on second-order derivatives, is also well-grounded in existing literature (e.g., double backpropagation).

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing.
*   On CIFAR-10, the authors demonstrate consistent improvements across different teacher-student pairs of Wide ResNets. The ablation studies effectively show that combining losses from multiple layers is beneficial (Section 4.1.1) and that sum-based attention functions outperform max-based ones (Table 2).
*   Crucially, the comparison in Section 4.1.2 shows that AT provides significantly better accuracy than transferring full activation tensors (F-ActT), supporting the claim that the summarized attention map contains more valuable information for transfer than the full representation.
*   The results on large-scale datasets are the most significant contribution. The performance improvements on CUB-200, MIT Scenes, and especially ImageNet (a 1.1% top-1 gain for ResNet-18) are substantial. The claim that this is the first successful application of such a hint-based knowledge transfer method to ImageNet is a major result that highlights the practical utility and scalability of the proposed technique.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The motivation, methods, and experimental setup are explained logically and concisely, making the work easy to understand and reproduce. The public release of code further strengthens the paper's contribution to the community.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors
The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

**Feedback:**
*   The gradient-based AT section is less developed than the activation-based one. The experiments are confined to a specific setting (NIN without batch norm on CIFAR-10), and the authors rightly acknowledge the uncertainty regarding its interaction with batch normalization. While the results are interesting, this part of the work feels preliminary compared to the comprehensive analysis of activation-based AT. Strengthening this section with more experiments or framing it as a direction for future work could improve the paper's focus.
*   The statement in Section 4.2.2 that the authors "could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature" may be slightly too strong. While it is true that such applications are not common and often do not show strong results, a more cautiously worded statement might be appropriate.

**Questions:**
1.  In Equation 2, the vectorized attention maps (Q) are L2-normalized before the transfer loss is computed. The paper states that this normalization is "important for the success of the student training." Could you provide more intuition or an ablation study on the impact of this normalization? Does it primarily serve to balance the magnitude of the AT loss relative to the cross-entropy loss, or does it make the optimization landscape smoother?
2.  The ablation study in Table 2 shows that sum-based attention functions (F_sum) work better than max-based ones (F_max). What is the intuition behind this result? Does it suggest that for attention transfer, it is more important to capture the collective contribution of multiple feature channels at a given spatial location, rather than just the single most active channel?
3.  For the gradient-based method, you mention the challenge of incorporating batch normalization (BN) in the second backpropagation step. Have you considered potential solutions, such as freezing the BN running statistics during the second pass, or re-computing batch statistics for the gradient inputs? A brief discussion of these possibilities would be insightful.
4.  How sensitive is the activation-based method to the choice of the loss weight hyperparameter β? The paper describes a heuristic for setting it. How much tuning was required to find a good value, and does the optimal β vary significantly across different datasets or architectures?
