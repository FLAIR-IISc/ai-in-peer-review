1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes to transfer “attention” from a teacher CNN to a student CNN as a form of knowledge transfer. It defines spatial attention maps at intermediate layers in two ways: (a) activation-based maps that aggregate the absolute activation magnitudes across channels (with optional power p), and (b) gradient-based maps that use input gradients as sensitivity maps. It introduces simple, normalized losses to align student attention maps to teacher maps across corresponding layers, optionally in combination with knowledge distillation. The method is architecture-agnostic and adds minimal overhead for activation-based transfer. Experiments on CIFAR-10, CUB-200-2011, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet show consistent gains over baselines, including improvements for ResNet-18 on ImageNet using a ResNet-34 teacher. Ablations suggest sum-based activation attention (e.g., squared sum) performs best, multi-layer transfer helps, and activation-based attention transfer outperforms regressing full activations. The paper also explores gradient-based attention transfer using double backpropagation and a flip-invariance regularizer, demonstrating benefits on CIFAR.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and simple formulation of attention-based knowledge transfer, with clear motivation and broad applicability, yielding consistent improvements across datasets and architectures, including ImageNet.
- Empirical support with ablations (mapping functions, layer placement, comparison to full activation regression) indicating the proposed attention signals are particularly effective for transfer.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets whether attention information (where a network focuses spatially) can be transferred to improve a student’s performance. The use of spatial maps connects with prior work on visualization and aligns well with the intuition that spatial selectivity encodes discriminative cues. This is well placed in the literature (attention visualization, CAM/Grad-CAM, knowledge distillation, FitNets), and the paper clearly distinguishes its contributions (attention transfer via spatial maps; use of both activation- and gradient-based forms; normalization).
- Technical soundness: The activation-based attention maps are defined by straightforward channel-wise statistics (sum or max of |A|^p), and the transfer loss aligns normalized attention vectors, which is a reasonable way to remove scale effects; the importance of normalization is stated (although an explicit ablation would strengthen this). The gradient-based variant is correctly derived via double backpropagation with a well-defined symmetry regularizer. Implementation considerations and potential issues with batch normalization are acknowledged.
- Empirical support: 
  - CIFAR-10: Multiple teacher–student pairs (WRNs) show consistent error reductions; ablations indicate sum-based maps outperform max-based, and that multi-layer transfer is beneficial. Activation-based AT gives larger gains than full-activation regression while also accelerating convergence.
  - Gradient-based AT: On CIFAR without batch norm, double backprop, symmetry regularization, and teacher–student gradient alignment each help, with activation-based AT performing best overall in that setting.
  - Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34; on CUB, AT outperforms KD, consistent with the importance of mid-level spatial cues in fine-grained tasks.
  - ImageNet: With ResNet-34 teacher and ResNet-18 student, AT yields +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 without extensive tuning, indicating scalability to large-scale settings.
- Rigor and limitations: Some results are reported as single runs; several details (e.g., the exact β schedules, statistical variation across runs, compute/memory overhead) are not fully quantified; gradient-based AT is demonstrated only on CIFAR with architectural constraints; KD tuning on ImageNet appears limited. References and proofing can be improved. Nonetheless, the core claim—attention maps provide effective transfer signals—is supported across datasets and models.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide ablations on the role of l2 normalization in Eq. (2) and sensitivity to the choice of p in |A|^p. Report whether l1 normalization alters outcomes.
- Quantify the compute and memory overhead (training time and GPU RAM) for activation-based AT and for gradient-based AT (double backprop), relative to baseline and KD.
- Report mean ± std over multiple runs for CIFAR and provide exact numbers for CUB and Scenes with/without AT and with KD for completeness.
- Expand KD comparisons: tune temperature and α on ImageNet, and compare to stronger feature distillation baselines (e.g., FitNets-style with careful matching and tuning).
- For ImageNet, include AT across all residual groups and assess cross-architecture transfer (e.g., ResNet teacher to MobileNet student).
- Clarify handling of mismatched spatial resolutions (interpolation details) and whether attention maps are class-agnostic or class-conditional; consider comparing to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ as attention definitions.
- For gradient-based AT, address batch normalization explicitly (fwd mode, parameter updates during second pass), and explore using BN-enabled architectures to make conclusions more general.
- Improve reference formatting and fix typographical issues.

Questions for the authors
- Normalization: How critical is the l2 normalization of attention vectors in Eq. (2)? Please provide an ablation comparing no normalization, l1, and l2.
- Hyperparameters: How is β set and scheduled across layers and datasets? Is the reported 10^3/(#elements × batch) generally optimal? How sensitive are results to β and p?
- Layer selection: How does performance vary with which layers/groups receive AT losses on ImageNet? Do earlier layers help or hurt?
- Compute cost: What are the training time and memory overheads for AT (activation-based and gradient-based) relative to baseline and KD on CIFAR and ImageNet?
- KD on ImageNet: With tuned temperature and α, can KD recover the slowdown or negative impact you observed for R18/R34? Did you try matching logits only after convergence or staged training?
- Attention similarity: Can you quantify how AT increases teacher–student attention similarity (e.g., cosine similarity or correlation) and relate this to accuracy gains?
- Class-conditional attention: Did you try class-specific attention (e.g., Grad-CAM) for transfer? If so, how does it compare to the class-agnostic activation-based maps?
- Generalization: Does AT improve robustness (e.g., under common corruptions) or calibration? Any evidence that AT changes error modes?
- Gradient-based AT with BN: Which strategy for BN in the second backprop works best (frozen stats vs. recomputed)? Can you provide results with modern BN-enabled architectures?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, well-motivated, and effective approach to transferring spatial attention, with convincing empirical evidence, particularly for activation-based transfer.
