Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism from a teacher CNN to a student, with the aim of improving the student’s performance by encouraging it to focus on similar spatial regions.
- Two families of attention are defined: activation-based (simple channelwise spatial aggregations such as sum of |A|^p and max |A|^p) and gradient-based (input saliency maps via ∂L/∂x), with corresponding transfer losses. The activation maps are l2-normalized before matching; gradient-based AT uses a double-backprop-like objective and a proposed flip-invariance regularizer.
- Empirically, AT is shown to improve over student baselines on CIFAR-10 (various WRN/NIN settings), on transfer learning tasks (CUB-200 and MIT Scenes) using ResNets, and on ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher), with a reported +1.1% top-1 gain. The authors further claim activation-based AT outperforms matching full activations and can be combined with KD.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
1) Insufficiently rigorous and comprehensive empirical validation, especially for the more novel gradient-based AT; key comparisons and ablations are either missing or limited, and several claims (e.g., “first successful transfer on ImageNet”) are not substantiated.
2) Methodological and reporting gaps (incomplete tables/figures, inconsistent training protocols, limited statistical analysis), which make it difficult to assess robustness and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Comparisons and baselines: While there is some comparison to KD and a “full activation transfer” baseline, the study does not adequately benchmark against established transfer/distillation methods beyond KD/FitNets-style hints (e.g., Jacobian matching and related regularizers, more recent distillation approaches). The claim that KD “does not work” on ImageNet is based on a single set of hyperparameters and partial training; this is not a fair or conclusive comparison. Several reported numbers are single runs; medians are given for only one table. More systematic tuning for each method is needed to draw strong conclusions.
- Gradient-based AT: This is the more unconventional component, yet its empirical support is restricted to a small NIN on CIFAR-10 without batch normalization and with limited augmentation. The method requires double backprop and raises nontrivial questions around BN and efficiency; the paper explicitly leaves BN behavior unresolved and does not provide a clear computational overhead analysis. The strongest results overall come from activation-based AT, while gradient-based AT is not demonstrated at scale.
- Ablations and sensitivity: The paper shows limited ablations (some p choices and placement of losses on CIFAR). There is no thorough sensitivity analysis of β, normalization choice, number and location of transfer layers, or teacher/student depth/width mismatch on larger datasets. The ImageNet setting uses only two AT losses with “no hyperparameter tuning”. The improvements there, while potentially interesting, are modest and lack variance estimates.
- Claim strength vs. evidence: Statements such as being the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet are not convincingly supported. Knowledge distillation has been applied to large-scale tasks in prior work; if the novelty is specifically “attention transfer,” the claim should be framed accordingly and backed with stronger, carefully controlled experiments.
- Reporting and clarity: Several referenced tables/figures are missing or incomplete in the draft (e.g., Table 1/5, some figure panels), references have formatting issues, and some experimental details are opaque (e.g., exact ImageNet training schedule, data augmentation, learning-rate schedules, stopping criteria, number of runs). This hinders assessment and reproducibility.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines and fairness: Include tuned, strong KD baselines on each dataset; for activation matching, reproduce a careful FitNets baseline with appropriate regressors and training protocol. Where relevant, compare to Jacobian-matching or related gradient regularizers. Ensure comparable compute, data augmentation, and hyperparameter budgets per method.
- Robustness and significance: Report mean/std over multiple runs on CIFAR and, where feasible, multiple seeds on ImageNet/transfer learning. Provide learning curves and calibration metrics. Include sensitivity studies for β, normalization (l1 vs l2), the choice of p, and the number/placement of transfer layers.
- Gradient-based AT at scale: Resolve and document BN handling in double backprop, quantify training-time and memory overhead, and demonstrate results on at least one BN-based architecture with modern augmentation. If impractical at ImageNet scale, provide solid medium-scale evidence beyond toy settings.
- Broaden evaluations: Include additional tasks where spatial attention plausibly matters (e.g., detection, weakly supervised localization), or provide stronger qualitative/quantitative analysis showing that AT meaningfully aligns student attention across layers and improves localization or robustness.
- Theoretical/diagnostic insight: Analyze why normalization of attention maps is crucial; explore connections to Jacobian/feature matching regularization. Provide controlled studies isolating whether AT helps optimization, regularization, or both (e.g., low-data regimes, robustness to corruptions).
- Clarity and completeness: Fix references, include all missing tables/figures, and release complete training recipes and code for reproducibility. Calibrate claims around “first on ImageNet.”

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are the ImageNet gains to β, the choice and number of AT layers, and the attention mapping function? Please report multi-run statistics and tuned KD baselines.
2) What is the exact training setup for ImageNet (epochs, schedule, augmentation, label smoothing, EMA, weight decay, etc.) and how does AT affect convergence speed and final accuracy across seeds?
3) For gradient-based AT, how exactly is BN handled in the second backprop? What is the additional wall-clock and memory cost relative to standard training? Can you demonstrate this variant on a BN-enabled architecture?
4) How do AT gains vary with teacher capacity/accuracy and student capacity? Is there a regime where AT underperforms KD or full activation transfer?
5) Can you provide quantitative evidence that student attention maps become more class-discriminative/localization-accurate (e.g., via pointing game or CAM-based localization metrics), beyond visual anecdotes?
6) The paper claims KD failed on ImageNet in your setup. What temperatures, α, and schedules were tried? Did you attempt architecturally matched teachers/students or logit-ensemble teachers?

Overall, the idea of matching simple, normalized activation-based attention maps is appealing and lightweight, and some results are promising. However, the current evidence does not meet the level of rigor and breadth needed for acceptance.
