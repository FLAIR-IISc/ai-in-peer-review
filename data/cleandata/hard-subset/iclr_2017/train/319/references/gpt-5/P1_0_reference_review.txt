1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps at intermediate layers and encourage the student to match the teacher’s attention. Two attention definitions are used:
- Activation-based attention: spatial maps formed by per-location statistics over channels (sum of absolute activations, sum/max of absolute activations raised to p), with l2 normalization, and an L2 loss between teacher and student attention maps across corresponding layers or layer groups.
- Gradient-based attention: input-gradient saliency maps; the student is trained to match the teacher’s input gradients via a double backprop loss; a symmetry regularizer enforcing flip-invariance is also explored.

Empirically, activation-based AT consistently improves student performance across CIFAR-10 (various WRN/ResNet/NIN pairs), transfer learning on CUB and MIT-Indoor with ResNet-18/34, and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student improved using a ResNet-34 teacher). Activation-based AT outperforms full activation regression (FitNets-style) and can complement knowledge distillation (KD). Gradient-based AT and the symmetry regularizer yield additional improvements on CIFAR-10 in a controlled NIN setting. Code is provided.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Simple, general idea with clear motivation and strong empirical utility: matching intermediate attention is an effective knowledge-transfer signal that outperforms full activation regression and often complements KD.
- Evidence spans multiple architectures and scales, including ImageNet, where few distillation-like methods have shown gains.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper situates attention transfer within both attention visualization literature (e.g., CAM, Grad-CAM, guided backprop) and knowledge distillation/fit-hints literature. It argues that attention maps capture spatially discriminative information that is more transferable than full activations. This is intuitive and well-motivated.
- Methodological clarity: The activation-based attention maps are defined precisely; normalization of attention vectors is emphasized and justified. The placement of losses across groups/blocks is described with options for different/same depths. The gradient-based AT is formulated rigorously with required second-order derivatives and an efficient implementation path via autodiff.
- Empirical support:
  - CIFAR-10: Activation-based AT improves multiple student-teacher pairs; ablation shows sum-based attention functions perform consistently better than max; distributing losses across groups helps; AT yields bigger final accuracy improvements than full-activation regression despite both accelerating convergence.
  - Gradient-based section: While limited to a BN-free NIN setting, it shows that double backprop, symmetry regularization, and gradient-based AT all improve over baseline, with activation-based AT strongest in that regime.
  - Transfer learning: On CUB and MIT-Indoor, AT narrows the ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap and, on CUB, surpasses KD, aligning with the intuition that intermediate spatial attention is crucial in fine-grained tasks.
  - ImageNet: ResNet-18 gains +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 using only two AT losses, despite minimal tuning; KD under similar settings struggles, matching prior observations that KD can be brittle across depths/architectures.

- Practicality: AT is lightweight to compute during forward pass (activation-based) and integrates easily with KD. Code availability helps reproducibility.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Statistical rigor and reporting:
  - Provide mean±std over multiple runs for all key results (especially ImageNet and transfer-learning experiments).
  - Report exact baseline and AT numbers (top-1/top-5) in the main paper for ImageNet, with training schedule and seeds.
- Larger-scale and broader evaluations:
  - Include CIFAR-100 and/or SVHN for additional diversity; consider other student capacities and architectures (e.g., MobileNet-like) and teacher-student mismatches.
  - Evaluate AT+KD on ImageNet with more tuning; test AT across more layers (all residual groups) as promised.
- Ablations and diagnostics:
  - Sensitivity to β and to the choice of p in Fpsum; compare l1 vs l2 normalization; effect of placing losses at different depths.
  - Quantify attention similarity improvements (e.g., cosine similarity of maps), not only qualitative figures; optionally relate to external saliency/ground-truth localization when available.
  - Analyze computational and memory overhead per iteration (activation-based is cheap; gradient-based incurs double backprop).
- Gradient-based AT:
  - The section is promising but underdeveloped. Clarify how to handle batch normalization in second backprop (frozen vs updating statistics) and report experiments with BN-enabled networks, or move this section to appendix if unresolved.
- Claims and positioning:
  - Temper the claim about being the first successful transfer method on ImageNet; frame it as “to our knowledge” and provide citations for any concurrent/related attempts.
- Presentation:
  - Clean up references and figures; ensure all tables/figures critical to conclusions are in the main text.

Questions for the authors
- ImageNet setup:
  - What are the exact baseline vs AT top-1/top-5 numbers for ResNet-18, with variance over multiple runs? What schedule, optimizer, and β values were used? Any sensitivity observed?
  - How much wall-clock and memory overhead does activation-based AT add per iteration and overall?
- Hyperparameters and design choices:
  - How sensitive are results to β, to the normalization choice (l1 vs l2), and to the placement/number of AT losses? Is there a simple heuristic for setting β across layers?
  - For Fpsum, how does performance vary with p, and is there a principled way to pick p per depth?
- Scope and robustness:
  - Does AT still help if the teacher is only marginally better than the student or even worse (negative control)? How does performance scale with the teacher-student accuracy gap?
  - Does AT risk transferring teacher biases/attention to spurious regions? Any evidence on robustness or calibration?
- Gradient-based AT:
  - How do you propose to handle BN in the second backprop? Can you provide experiments on gradient-based AT with BN or an alternative formulation that avoids second-order complications?
  - Can symmetry regularization be combined with activation-based AT on larger datasets, and is the gain complementary?

Overall, the paper offers a simple and impactful idea with convincing evidence, especially for activation-based attention transfer. Addressing the above points would further strengthen the contribution.
