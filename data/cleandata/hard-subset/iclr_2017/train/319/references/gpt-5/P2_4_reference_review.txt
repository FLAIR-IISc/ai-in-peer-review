1) Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps at intermediate layers—either activation-based (channel-aggregated maps such as Fsum or Fsum with p>1) or gradient-based (input saliency)—and to encourage the student to match the teacher’s attention during training. The paper:
- Defines several activation-based attention mappings (sum, sum of powers, max of powers) and studies their properties.
- Proposes a loss that aligns ℓ2-normalized attention maps across corresponding layers of teacher and student.
- Proposes gradient-based AT via a double-backprop-like objective (and a flip-symmetry regularizer).
- Presents empirical results on CIFAR-10, CUB-200-2011, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet showing consistent improvements, with activation-based AT outperforming full activation matching and combining well with knowledge distillation (KD) on some datasets. Notably, AT yields a reported +1.1% top-1 gain on ImageNet for ResNet-18 with a ResNet-34 teacher using only two attention alignment losses.
Code and models are released, and implementation details are given.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: using normalized spatial attention maps for distillation is a simple, well-motivated idea that is different from both KD on logits and full activation regression.
- Empirical significance: consistent gains across architectures and datasets, including a nontrivial improvement on ImageNet, suggest practical impact and broad applicability.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper is well-placed within literature on attention visualization (Grad-CAM, guided backprop, CAM) and knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets). The rationale for transferring where the teacher “looks,” rather than exact feature tensors or only output distributions, is compelling: attention aggregates semantically meaningful spatial information while being lower-dimensional and less brittle than full activations. This is articulated and supported with qualitative visualizations and quantitative improvements.
- Method soundness:
  - Activation-based AT: The definitions are straightforward. The use of ℓ2 normalization for attention vectors is well-justified and emphasized as crucial. The mapping functions are reasonable; the empirical finding that sum-of-powers (e.g., F2sum) outperform max-based is plausible and backed by an ablation (Table 2).
  - Gradient-based AT: The formulation follows double backprop logic and is implementable with autodiff. The flip-symmetry regularizer is a nice addition with a clear intuition and observed regularization effect.
- Empirical support:
  - CIFAR-10: Multiple teacher/student pairs with residual and WRN architectures show consistent improvements; AT+KD typically outperforms either alone. Ablations across attention functions and placement of losses (one per group vs all) are informative. An explicit comparison shows AT > full activation regression in final accuracy while both speed convergence.
  - Gradient-based AT: On CIFAR with NIN (albeit without BN and with limited augmentation), improvements over baseline and over double backprop are shown; the symmetry constraint is competitive. This clarifies where gradient-based AT can work and surfaces practical concerns (interaction with BN during second backprop).
  - Transfer learning: On CUB and MIT Scenes, AT closes much of the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. This supports the claim that attention alignment at intermediate layers is especially useful in fine-grained settings where spatial cues are critical.
  - ImageNet: A +1.1% top-1, +0.8% top-5 absolute gain for ResNet-18 with a ResNet-34 teacher, with minimal tuning, is a strong result. The paper notes KD underperformed in this setup (with caveats on hyperparameters), underscoring the distinct value of AT.
- Reproducibility and impact: Public code/models and concrete guidance (e.g., β scaling, layer choices, normalization) support adoption. The approach is simple to integrate, making potential impact high.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
- Reporting and rigor:
  - Provide complete numeric results in the main text (means, standard deviations over multiple seeds where applicable) for all tables/experiments. Some claims are described as “significant” without full numbers in the provided text; tighter reporting will strengthen the case.
  - For ImageNet and transfer learning experiments, include training curves and more ablations (e.g., number/placement of AT losses, sensitivity to β, effect of p in Fpsum).
  - For gradient-based AT, repeat with BN-enabled architectures and clarify the precise treatment of BN during the second backprop (frozen stats vs batch-updated; whether teacher/student BN are in training/eval mode in the second pass).
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Revisit KD hyperparameters on ImageNet; KD is known to be sensitive (temperature, α, label smoothing interactions). Reporting a tuned KD baseline with the same training budget would be fairer.
  - Include a FitNets-style hint baseline (with 1×1 alignment) on ImageNet if feasible; on CIFAR you already compare to full activation regression.
- Analysis:
  - Quantify how closely student attention matches teacher attention over training (e.g., cosine similarity trajectories), and correlate attention similarity with accuracy gains.
  - Report computational overhead for AT (activation-based and gradient-based), in wall-clock and FLOPs, including the double-backprop cost and memory footprint.
- Clarity and polish:
  - Clean up grammar and reference formatting; ensure figures/tables are legible and all hyperparameters are specified (β schedules, normalization choices, loss placements).
  - Explicitly state the student-only inference cost (unchanged) to emphasize deployment benefits.

Questions for the authors
- Layer selection: How sensitive are results to which layers/groups are used for AT? On ImageNet, do four group-level losses outperform two materially? Any diminishing returns?
- Normalization: You use ℓ2 normalization of attention maps; have you tried ℓ1 or temperature scaling? How sensitive is performance to this choice?
- Mapping functions: Beyond sum/max-of-powers, did you try learned channel weights or attention via learned 1×1 projections before channel aggregation?
- β scheduling: What schedule works best when combining AT with KD? Any principled way to balance them over training?
- KD on ImageNet: With tuned temperature/α and label smoothing, does KD remain inferior to AT for ResNet-18/34? Could combining KD+AT on ImageNet yield additive gains?
- Gradient-based AT with BN: What is your recommended protocol for BN during the second backprop? Did you try accumulating BN stats only on the first pass and freezing them on the second?
- Generalization: Have you tried AT on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization, where spatial attention is critical? Any preliminary results?
- Compute/memory: What is the training overhead of activation-based AT versus baseline and versus KD (per step and total time), and how does gradient-based AT scale to large models?
