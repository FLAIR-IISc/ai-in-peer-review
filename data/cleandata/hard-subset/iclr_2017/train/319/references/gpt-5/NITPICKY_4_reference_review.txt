1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism between a teacher and a student CNN. Attention is defined as spatial maps derived either (i) from activations by aggregating across channels (sum of absolute values, sum/max of powers), or (ii) from gradients with respect to the input (input sensitivity). The core idea is to enforce the student’s attention maps to match the teacher’s at selected layers via a normalization-based loss. The paper also introduces a symmetry (horizontal flip) constraint on gradient attention. Empirically, the authors report consistent gains on CIFAR-10 across several teacher–student pairs, improvements on CUB and MIT Scenes via finetuning from ImageNet, and a 1.1% top-1 improvement on ImageNet for ResNet-18 using a ResNet-34 teacher. The method is simple, compatible with distillation, and purportedly adds little overhead (for activation-based AT).

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The empirical evidence, especially for gradient-based AT and ImageNet, is incomplete, inconsistently reported, and not sufficiently rigorous. Several crucial details are missing or deferred (“we did not have time”), and many results are single-run without variance.
- The paper’s presentation has numerous technical, methodological, and formatting issues (broken references, missing figures/tables, unclear hyperparameters/normalization, ad hoc choices), undermining confidence in the claims despite the potentially useful idea.

3) Supporting arguments
Technical clarity and motivation:
- The activation-based attention definitions (|A|^p aggregated over channels) are intuitive but ad hoc. There is little principled motivation, analysis, or discussion of what properties are desirable for an “attention map” beyond correlational observations. Choice of p, normalization, and placement across layers is not systematically justified.
- The gradient-based AT depends on double backprop and uses gradients of the loss w.r.t. input. It is unclear whether gradients for the ground-truth class, predicted class, or total loss are used; why class-selective logits/score gradients were not considered; and how the approach relates to prior Jacobian matching/regularization literature beyond citing double backprop.
- The combination with KD is underspecified (temperature, alpha, teacher eval mode, etc.), and the claim that AT is the first successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet is likely overstated and at least unsubstantiated here.

Experimental rigor:
- CIFAR results: Some tables report medians over 5 runs, others single runs; no standard deviations are given. Ablations are limited (a small comparison of mapping functions; no systematic study of p, normalization type, layer placements, β schedules, or effect of teacher strength).
- The “full activation vs attention” comparison is interesting but narrow and may not be fairly tuned for the full-activation baseline (e.g., 1×1 adapters, normalization choices, β decay) across all settings.
- Gradient-based experiments are limited to a thin NIN without batch norm, with single runs, and the authors explicitly state uncertainty about batch norm behavior in double backprop—this weakens the generality claim.
- ImageNet: only two AT losses are used; hyperparameters are reused without tuning; variance is not reported; KD “hurts convergence” is reported without a serious attempt to find settings that are known to work; several statements defer further experiments due to lack of compute.
- For transfer learning (CUB/Scenes), improvements are promising but again lack variance, and training/finetuning details are sparse (e.g., which layers receive AT, precise β values, optimizer, schedules, crop strategy for Scenes).

Methodological details:
- The attention matching loss uses l2-normalized attention vectors. No epsilon is specified to avoid division by zero; the necessity of normalization is asserted but not ablated.
- β selection is ad hoc (“10^3 divided by number of elements…”), later decayed “to simplify learning harder examples,” without consistent protocol or ablation.
- It is unclear whether teacher runs in eval mode (BN frozen) during AT/KD; this matters for reproducibility and for fairness.
- Overhead claims: activation-based AT is said to be cheap, but no timing/memory overheads are reported; gradient-based AT requires double backprop with unspecified costs.

Presentation quality:
- Multiple missing/placeholder figures and tables are referenced (e.g., fig. 4–7, table 1 and 5 not present). Appendix content appears truncated and includes raw figure labels. The references have numerous formatting errors and inconsistencies. There are several grammatical issues and informal statements (“we did not have time,” “we plan to update the paper”), which are inappropriate for a polished submission.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide a complete, reproducible experimental section: report means and standard deviations over multiple runs; detail all training hyperparameters (optimizers, schedules, β and decay schedules, normalization epsilons), data augmentations, teacher eval/train mode, and number/locations of AT losses. Include timing and memory overheads.
- Add systematic ablations: effect of p, normalization (l1, l2, unnormalized), number/placement of AT layers, layer-resolution matching strategies, teacher strength, and robustness to mismatched architectures.
- For gradient-based AT: clarify the exact gradients used (w.r.t. which objective/class), how BN is handled, and provide results beyond a single toy setting. Compare to other Jacobian/gradient regularization and matching baselines.
- For KD: tune properly and compare fairly; prior works have ImageNet results—either show when/why KD struggles here or adjust settings.
- Substantiate claims about ImageNet novelty, or soften them. Include final, converged ImageNet numbers with variance.
- Improve writing, fix all references, include all figures/tables with clear captions, and remove informal statements.

Questions for the authors
- Exact details of the normalization in Eq. (2): do you add epsilon to the norm? Did you try unnormalized or l1-normalized maps?
- Which class/objective is used for gradient-based AT (Eq. 4)? Did you try matching logits gradients per class or only the loss gradient?
- How are batch norm layers handled for teacher and student during AT and during double backprop?
- What are the precise β values and decay schedules used per dataset/layer? How sensitive are results to β?
- For ImageNet, what are the exact top-1/top-5 numbers (mean±std over seeds), training schedule, and runtime overhead? Which layers received AT? Did you try AT on all residual groups?
- Can you quantify the similarity increase between teacher and student attention maps (e.g., correlation metrics) alongside accuracy?
- How does AT perform when the teacher is only marginally better than the student, or when the teacher is weaker?
