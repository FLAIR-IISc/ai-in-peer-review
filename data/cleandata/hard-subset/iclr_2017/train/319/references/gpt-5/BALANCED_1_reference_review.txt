1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple, general mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The core idea is to define spatial attention maps at various layers—either activation-based (channel-wise reductions of absolute activations, e.g., sum of |A|^p) or gradient-based (saliency w.r.t. input)—and to train the student to match the teacher’s attention (with l2-normalized attention vectors). Two implementations are developed: (i) activation-based AT, which is computationally cheap and easily inserted at multiple layers; and (ii) gradient-based AT, which uses double backpropagation and can incorporate a symmetry regularizer (horizontal flip invariance). The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across CIFAR-10, fine-grained (CUB) and scene classification (MIT Indoor), and ImageNet, often complementing or outperforming knowledge distillation (KD). They further show activation-based AT outperforms full activation regression, and that AT provides faster convergence and meaningful teacher–student attention alignment. Code is released.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Practical, simple, and general technique that yields consistent accuracy gains across datasets and architectures, including ImageNet.
- Clear, well-motivated formulation of attention maps and transfer losses, with insightful empirical analysis (e.g., normalization, mapping choices, layer placement), and compatibility with KD.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem: The paper tackles whether and how attention information in CNNs can be exploited for model compression/transfer. This is an important and timely problem given the broad use of teacher–student training.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well situated amid attention visualization (CAM/Grad-CAM/guided backprop), knowledge distillation, and FitNets. The shift from regressing full features/hints to spatial attention is motivated by (i) capturing where models focus and (ii) architectural invariance (channel dimension can differ). The discussion contrasting F_sum vs F_max, and the role of p, shows thoughtful design.
- Claims and evidence:
  - Activation-based AT consistently improves students on CIFAR with multiple WRN teacher–student pairs; combining AT with KD often helps further. Ablations over attention choices (sum vs max, p), placement across groups, and comparison to full activation regression support the central claim that attention maps are more effective transfer targets.
  - Gradient-based AT: While less comprehensive, the CIFAR experiments show improvements over baseline, double backprop, and KD in a constrained setting. The proposed flip-invariance regularizer improves performance, supporting the claim that shaping gradient attention is beneficial.
  - Larger-scale validity: Activation-based AT improves ResNet-18 on ImageNet with a ResNet-34 teacher without elaborate tuning, and narrows the gap on CUB/Scenes in transfer learning. This underscores practicality beyond toy datasets.
- Rigor: The methodology is described sufficiently to reproduce; the normalization choice and β scaling are specified; multiple runs reported for some settings; and code is provided. While not theoretically grounded, the empirical evidence is robust for activation-based AT.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all impact the decision)
- Statistical rigor: Report mean ± std over multiple runs for all key experiments (esp. ImageNet/CUB/Scenes) and include significance analyses where feasible.
- Fair baselines and stronger comparisons: Compare against more transfer/distillation variants beyond KD and FitNets-style regression (e.g., logit matching variants, feature-based distillation with 1×1 adapters, attention/representation similarity metrics). Measure whether AT complements or subsumes them.
- Computational cost: Quantify wall-clock and memory overhead of AT (both activation- and gradient-based), and the effect on convergence speed. For gradient-based AT, detail the double-backprop overhead and any engineering tricks.
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide sensitivity to β, choice of p and norm, number/locations of AT layers, and teacher quality. Discuss negative transfer scenarios (e.g., weaker teacher).
- BatchNorm and gradient-based AT: Clarify and experiment with how BN is handled in the second backprop. Evaluate gradient-based AT with BN-enabled networks to strengthen that line.
- Broader tasks: Since attention is spatial, a small pilot on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization would further substantiate the claim that AT transfers spatial priors.
- Clarity and presentation: Improve language/typos, fix reference formatting, ensure all figures/tables are visible and self-contained, and summarize the key hyperparameters in an appendix.

Questions for the authors
- Sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to β and the l2 normalization of attention vectors? Did you try other normalizations (l1, temperature scaling) or cosine losses?
- Mapping choice: Beyond F_sum vs F_max and p, did you test softmax-normalized attention over spatial positions to better align distributions?
- Layer selection: What is the marginal gain of adding AT at each successive group on ImageNet? Does early-layer AT help or hurt?
- Teacher quality: How does AT behave when the teacher is only marginally better than the student, or when architectures differ significantly (e.g., Inception teacher → ResNet student)?
- KD combination: On which datasets does AT+KD outperform AT or KD alone, and is the benefit additive or overlapping? Any guidance on weighting schedules?
- Gradient-based AT: Can you provide concrete timing/memory numbers vs baseline and clarify BN handling in the second backward? Would per-example gradient penalties (e.g., using ghost norms) help?
- Claim of first on ImageNet: Can you contextualize this claim relative to contemporaneous distillation/transfer results on ImageNet? Any results with same-depth teacher–student where KD is known to be stronger?
- Failure modes: Did you observe cases where AT over-constrains the student (e.g., harmful when capacities diverge), or where attention alignment degrades diversity of features?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and practically impactful contribution. With strengthened experiments (especially for gradient-based AT) and clearer reporting, it will make a solid addition to the literature on knowledge transfer.
