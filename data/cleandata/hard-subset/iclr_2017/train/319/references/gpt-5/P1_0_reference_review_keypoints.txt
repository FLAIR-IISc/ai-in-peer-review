- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT) to transfer knowledge from teacher to student by matching spatial attention maps at intermediate layers.  
- Defines two AT types: activation-based (channel-aggregation maps with L2-normalized L2 loss) and gradient-based (input-gradient saliency matched via double backprop plus symmetry regularizer).  
- Activation-based AT consistently improves student performance across CIFAR-10, transfer learning (CUB, MIT-Indoor), and ImageNet.  
- Activation-based AT outperforms full activation regression (FitNets-style) and complements Knowledge Distillation (KD).  
- Gradient-based AT and symmetry regularizer yield additional gains in a controlled BN-free NIN CIFAR-10 setting.  
- Empirical ImageNet result: ResNet-18 gains +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 using two AT losses with minimal tuning.  
- Code is provided, aiding reproducibility.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: simple, general, well-motivated idea with broad empirical utility across architectures and scales.  
- Positions AT within attention visualization and distillation literatures, arguing spatial attention is more transferable than full activations.  
- Methodology is clearly defined, with attention map formulas, normalization rationale, and loss placement options explained.  
- Gradient-based AT formulation is rigorous and implementable via autodiff despite second-order derivatives.  
- CIFAR-10 ablations: sum-based attention outperforms max, distributed losses help, and AT yields larger final accuracy gains than activation regression.  
- Transfer learning: AT narrows ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap and can outperform KD on fine-grained CUB.  
- Practicality: activation-based AT is lightweight at forward pass and integrates easily with KD.  
- Recommend reporting mean±std over multiple runs, especially for ImageNet and transfer experiments.  
- Suggest expanding evaluations to CIFAR-100/SVHN, more student capacities (e.g., MobileNet), and varied teacher-student mismatches.  
- Ask for more tuning/evaluation of AT+KD on ImageNet and testing AT across more residual groups/layers.  
- Request ablations: sensitivity to β, choice of p in Fpsum, l1 vs l2 normalization, and loss placement depth.  
- Propose quantifying attention similarity improvements and relating to saliency/ground-truth localization where possible.  
- Ask for computational and memory overhead analysis per iteration (activation- vs gradient-based).  
- Gradient-based AT needs clarification on handling BatchNorm in second backprop and BN-enabled experiments or moving to appendix.  
- Advise tempering claims about being first successful ImageNet transfer method and cleaning references/figures.  
- Questions for authors: provide exact baseline vs AT ImageNet top-1/top-5 numbers, variances, schedules, optimizers, and β sensitivity.  
- Questions: how sensitive are results to β, normalization choice, AT loss placement/number, and is there a heuristic for β or p selection?  
- Questions: does AT help with marginal or worse teachers, how does benefit scale with teacher-student gap, and does AT risk transferring spurious attention or biases?  
- Questions: how to handle BN in second backprop; can gradient-based AT and symmetry regularizer combine with activation-based AT on larger datasets and are gains complementary?
