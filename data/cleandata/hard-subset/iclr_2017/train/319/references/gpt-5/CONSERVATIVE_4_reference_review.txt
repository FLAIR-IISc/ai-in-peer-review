Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple mechanism to transfer knowledge from a teacher to a student CNN by matching spatial attention maps. Two attention definitions are used: (i) activation-based maps obtained by aggregating absolute activations across channels (e.g., sum, sum of powers), and (ii) gradient-based maps using input gradients (double backprop).
- A loss combines standard cross-entropy with l2 distances between teacher and student attention maps (l2-normalized), optionally combined with knowledge distillation (KD).
- Empirical results are presented on CIFAR-10, CUB-200-2011, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet. The paper reports consistent gains over student baselines; gains from activation-based AT exceed those from full-activation matching and often complement KD. On ImageNet, ResNet-18 benefits by ~1.1% top-1 when guided by ResNet-34.
- Code is promised, and there is some analysis of attention definitions (sum vs max, p), placement of losses, and normalization.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
1) The activation-based attention transfer is simple, well-motivated, and empirically effective across several architectures and datasets, including a meaningful improvement on ImageNet—something many KD-style methods struggle to demonstrate.
2) While novelty is incremental (conceptually close to hints/feature-matching), the specific spatial aggregation and normalization choices form a practical recipe that appears robust and low-overhead. However, the gradient-based AT component is underdeveloped and lacks rigorous evaluation.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The work is situated within knowledge distillation and attention visualization literature. The activation-based attention maps are a reasonable heuristic—matching spatial “energy” distributions across layers aligns with the idea that teachers can guide “where to look.” This is a plausible and well-placed extension of hints/feature matching that explicitly targets spatial focus rather than full features.
- Methodological clarity: The activation-based AT objective is clear, with normalization choices justified empirically. Layer selection for transfer is explained for same/different depths. The gradient-based AT is correctly derived (double backprop) and related to prior work.
- Empirical support: 
  - CIFAR: Multiple teacher–student pairs (WRNs) and ablations on attention definitions and placement show consistent gains. AT outperforms full-activation transfer, which is a strong and somewhat surprising result supporting the paper’s core claim.
  - CUB/Scenes: Finetuning experiments indicate that AT can close much of the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, suggesting utility in low-data regimes.
  - ImageNet: The reported +1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18 from a ResNet-34 teacher, with minimal tuning and only two AT losses, is noteworthy and supports practical relevance.
- Limitations:
  - The gradient-based AT section is limited to a constrained CIFAR setting without BN and largely single runs; several open questions about BN handling in second backprop remain. As such, this part feels preliminary.
  - Comparisons to KD are mixed; on ImageNet KD reportedly hurts convergence under the chosen hyperparameters. The KD baseline appears under-tuned relative to AT, and broader baselines (e.g., FitNets with tuned hints, other feature-matching schemes) are not exhaustively covered.
  - Statistical rigor: For CIFAR, some medians over 5 runs are provided; for larger datasets and ImageNet, variance/seed sensitivity is not reported. The ImageNet result is single-setting and would benefit from more thorough evaluation.
  - The definition of “attention” via simple channel-aggregation is heuristic; while visually plausible, there is limited quantitative analysis of why certain mappings (sum vs max, p) work and how sensitive AT is to these choices across tasks.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen baselines and tuning: 
  - Provide a KD hyperparameter sweep (temperature, alpha) for each setting, including ImageNet. Report the best KD and AT+KD results fairly.
  - Include a strong feature-matching baseline (e.g., l2 between intermediate features with 1×1 adapters, MMD/Gram matching) at the same layers where AT is applied.
- Statistical reporting: On CUB/Scenes/ImageNet, report mean±std over multiple seeds. For ImageNet, show learning curves and final accuracy across at least 3 runs, and quantify the added computation and wall-clock time for AT.
- Ablations: 
  - Loss placement sensitivity across blocks/layers; cumulative effects versus single-layer AT for ImageNet.
  - Normalization choices (l1 vs l2) and whether normalization is per-sample or per-batch.
  - Attention functions across tasks (sum, sum^p, max) and effect of p; justify F^2_sum choice more broadly.
- Practical details: Quantify the overhead of AT (FLOPs/time) per iteration and total training time. For the gradient-based method, clarify memory requirements and whether mixed precision/backprop checkpointing is used.
- Generalization: Evaluate AT on an object detection or weakly-supervised localization task to test the spatial guidance hypothesis in settings where spatial precision matters more.
- Robustness and calibration: Check whether AT affects robustness (e.g., to corruptions) or calibration, since redistributing attention might change these properties.
- Presentation: Clean up references and typos, and ensure figures/tables are legible and self-contained.

Questions for the authors
1) ImageNet: What are the exact baseline and AT accuracies (top-1/top-5) and their variance over multiple runs? What training schedule, augmentations, and hyperparameters (including β schedule) were used? How much extra training time does AT add?
2) KD baselines: What KD temperature/alpha sweeps were tried on ImageNet and CIFAR? Can you report the best KD and AT+KD numbers to ensure a fair comparison?
3) Sensitivity to attention definitions: How consistent are gains across F_sum, F_sum^p (varying p), and F_max^p on ImageNet and fine-grained tasks? Any principled guidance on choosing p?
4) Layer selection: How sensitive are results to where AT losses are placed? Do earlier-layer AT losses help on ImageNet if computationally feasible?
5) Gradient-based AT: How do you propose handling batch normalization in the second backprop pass in practice? Do you plan to evaluate gradient-based AT with BN on standard modern architectures?
6) Beyond classification: Have you attempted AT on detection/segmentation? If so, does matching attention at multiple scales improve localization metrics?
