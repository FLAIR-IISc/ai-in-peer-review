1) Summary of contributions (positive, generous)
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a new mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps at intermediate layers and encourage the student to match the teacher’s attention. Two complementary instantiations are presented: (i) activation-based attention maps, built from channel-wise statistics of absolute activations (sum and p-sum/max variants), and (ii) gradient-based attention maps, defined as input sensitivities (double backprop), with an additional flip-invariance regularizer. The method is simple, architecture-agnostic, and can be inserted at multiple layers. Empirically, AT yields consistent improvements on CIFAR-10 across ResNets and WRNs, shows that attention transfer outperforms full-activation regression (FitNets-style hints), combines beneficially with KD on small datasets, and scales to larger image settings: finetuning on CUB and MIT Scenes (substantial gains) and ImageNet (ResNet-34 → ResNet-18) with meaningful top-1/top-5 improvements. The paper provides ablations on attention functions, layer placement, and normalization, and makes code available.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and bold idea: using layer-wise spatial attention as the main vehicle for knowledge transfer is conceptually fresh relative to KD/FitNets, and opens a new direction for interpretability-aware distillation.
- Broad and convincing empirical signal: consistent gains across architectures and datasets, including promising ImageNet results, and a clear finding that attention maps are more effective for transfer than full activations.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Can we define attention for CNNs and leverage it for improving students? The paper clearly specifies attention as spatial maps, with well-motivated activation- and gradient-based constructions tied to prior visualization literature (saliency, CAM, Grad-CAM). This is well-placed in both attention visualization and knowledge distillation literatures.
- Method clarity and simplicity: The activation-based formulation is concise (p-sum over channels, L2-normalized matching) and easy to implement; the gradient-based variant builds on double backprop with a symmetry regularizer.
- Empirical support: 
  • CIFAR-10: AT consistently improves across teacher/student depth/width pairings; combining AT with KD often helps further. Ablations show sum-based attention works best, matching across multiple groups helps, and normalization matters.
  • FitNets-style baselines: Full activation regression helps convergence but underperforms AT in final accuracy, supporting the central claim that “where to look” is a more transferable signal than “what exact features.”
  • Gradient-based AT: While evaluated in a restricted setting, improvements over baseline and double backprop are shown; the flip-invariance regularizer is a neat addition with measurable gains.
  • Larger data: On CUB and Scenes, AT closes much of the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34; ImageNet results show non-trivial gains for ResNet-18 from a ResNet-34 teacher with minimal tuning.
- Novelty/impact: Positioning attention as the distilled object is both novel and useful. The approach is broadly applicable and computationally light for activation-based AT, which increases potential adoption.

Reservations (but not decision-critical for me):
- Gradient-based AT is only demonstrated on small-scale models without BN; interaction with BN in the second pass is unresolved.
- KD baselines appear under-tuned on ImageNet; the claim that AT is the first knowledge transfer to work on ImageNet should be toned down or more carefully justified.
- Some experiments use single runs (for gradient-based AT) and limited tuning; confidence intervals would help.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines: Include a stronger FitNets baseline with properly tuned adapters and multiple hint layers; further tune KD on ImageNet to ensure fairness.
- Report compute: Quantify overhead for activation-based AT (should be negligible) and for gradient-based AT (double backprop), with wall-clock and memory impacts.
- Clarify implementation: Specify whether attention is computed pre/post-activation and pre/post-BN; detail interpolation strategy for mismatched spatial resolutions; report β schedules and sensitivity.
- Statistical rigor: Provide mean ± std over multiple seeds on CIFAR and finetuning tasks; for ImageNet, at least two runs or checkpoint-averaging to reduce variance.
- Qualitative analysis: Add more qualitative attention visualizations showing student-vs-teacher alignment across layers and failure cases.
- Temper claims: Rephrase “first on ImageNet” unless a comprehensive survey confirms; frame as “to our knowledge” and acknowledge potential contemporaneous efforts.
- References/typos: Clean up citations and formatting, and ensure consistent notation (e.g., vector normalization Q/||Q||2 vs. l1).
- Future scope: Detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization would be natural domains where spatial attention transfer could shine; even a small pilot would strengthen the story.

Questions for the authors
- KD tuning on ImageNet: What temperatures/α and optimization settings were tried? Could you share why KD harmed convergence and whether label smoothing or logit normalization helped?
- Normalization choice: Why L2 normalization for attention vectors instead of L1 or cosine-only loss? Any sensitivity analysis across datasets?
- Placement strategy: How does performance vary with number and location of AT losses on ImageNet (e.g., all four groups vs. last two)? Any diminishing returns?
- Teacher quality: How does AT gain correlate with teacher accuracy? Is there a point where a much stronger teacher’s attention becomes too fine/granular for a smaller student to mimic?
- Gradient-based AT with BN: Can you propose or test a practical recipe (e.g., frozen BN in the second pass) and report initial results?
- Computational overhead: Please quantify training-time slowdowns for gradient-based AT and memory implications on modern GPUs.
- Beyond classification: Have you tried AT for dense prediction tasks or cross-task transfer (e.g., classification teacher to detector backbone)?
