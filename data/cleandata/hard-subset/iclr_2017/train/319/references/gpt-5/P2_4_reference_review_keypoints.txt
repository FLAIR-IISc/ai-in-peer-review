- Proposes attention transfer (AT) to distill spatial attention maps from teacher to student using activation- or gradient-based maps and L2-normalized alignment loss.
- Defines several activation-based attention mappings (sum, sum-of-powers, max-of-powers) and studies their properties.
- Introduces gradient-based AT via a double-backprop objective and a flip-symmetry regularizer.
- Empirical results on CIFAR-10, CUB-200-2011, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet show consistent improvements and practical impact.
- Activation-based AT often outperforms full activation regression and combines well with KD on some datasets.
- Reports a notable +1.1% top-1 ImageNet gain for ResNet-18 distilled from ResNet-34 using only two AT losses.
- Code and models are released with implementation details to support reproducibility.
- Decision: Accept, citing conceptual novelty, clarity, and empirical significance across architectures/datasets.
- Motivation: Transferring spatial attention is argued to be semantically meaningful, lower-dimensional, and less brittle than full activation or logits-only KD.
- Method soundness: L2 normalization of attention maps is crucial; sum-of-powers mappings (e.g., F2sum) outperform max-based in ablations.
- Gradient-based AT is implementable with autodiff; the flip-symmetry term provides useful regularization.
- CIFAR-10 experiments show consistent gains across teacher/student pairs; AT+KD typically beats either alone.
- Ablations on attention functions and loss placements are informative; AT speeds convergence more than full activation regression.
- Gradient-based AT shows gains on CIFAR but highlights practical issues with BatchNorm during the second backprop.
- Transfer learning: AT substantially reduces the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 on fine-grained CUB/MIT Scenes.
- ImageNet: AT gives strong gains with minimal tuning; KD underperformed in the reported setup (hyperparameter caveat).
- Reproducibility/impact: public code, guidance on β, layer choices, and normalization make adoption straightforward.
- Reporting improvements requested: include full numeric results (means, stds), training curves, and more ablations for ImageNet and transfer tasks.
- Clarify BN treatment in gradient-based AT (frozen vs updated stats) and repeat with BN-enabled architectures.
- Fairness/baselines: tune KD hyperparameters on ImageNet and include FitNets-style hint baselines where feasible.
- Analysis requests: report attention-similarity trajectories, correlate with accuracy, and provide computational overhead (wall-clock/FLOPs/memory).
- Clarity/polish: fix grammar/refs, ensure legible figures/tables, and state student-only inference cost explicitly.
- Ask about sensitivity to layer selection and whether more AT losses (e.g., four vs two groups) yield diminishing returns.
- Ask whether normalization choice (L2 vs L1 or temperature scaling) or learned channel weights/1×1 projections were tried.
- Request guidance on β scheduling when combining AT with KD and whether KD can be tuned to match or complement AT on ImageNet.
- Ask for recommended BN protocol for the second backprop in gradient AT and whether accumulating/frozen stats were tried.
- Inquire about extending AT to detection/segmentation/weakly supervised localization and any preliminary results.
- Request quantified training overhead of activation-based and gradient-based AT versus baseline and KD (per step and total).
