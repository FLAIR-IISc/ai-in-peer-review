Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism from a teacher to a student CNN. Attention is defined as spatial maps derived either from hidden activations (activation-based) or from input gradients (gradient-based).
- For activation-based AT, attention maps are constructed by aggregating absolute activations across channels (sum, power-sum, max). Training adds losses that encourage the student’s normalized attention maps at selected layers/groups to match the teacher’s.
- For gradient-based AT, attention is the gradient of the loss w.r.t. input; the student is trained to match the teacher’s gradient map via a double-backprop objective. A flip-invariance regularizer on gradients is also proposed.
- Empirically, the method is applied to CIFAR-10 (WRN, NIN), two transfer-learning datasets (CUB, MIT Scenes), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student with ResNet-34 teacher). Reported gains include consistent improvements on CIFAR, reduced gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 on CUB/Scenes, and +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet for ResNet-18. Activation-based AT appears to outperform full activation regression and can be combined with KD.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical evaluation lacks rigor and fair, comprehensive baselines—particularly for knowledge distillation and FitNets—undermining claims of superiority. 2) The gradient-based AT is insufficiently validated (limited, atypical setting without batch normalization, single runs), making that contribution premature.

Supporting arguments
- Baseline completeness and fairness: The comparison to FitNets is not faithful; the authors regress full activations with 1x1 adapters but do not implement the two-stage hints procedure that was essential to FitNets. This makes the “AT better than full activation transfer” conclusion tenuous. For KD, the hyperparameters are taken from CIFAR and then reused for ImageNet; the paper itself notes KD can be sensitive and “struggles” when teacher/student differ. A thorough KD baseline requires temperature/weight sweeps, matching training budgets, and architecture-matched variants (e.g., same depth/width) to substantiate the claim that KD underperforms AT.
- Statistical rigor: Many CIFAR results are reported as single numbers; only a subset report medians over 5 runs. Gradient-based experiments are single deterministic runs without BN, no data augmentation beyond flips, and even turn off weight decay due to optimization difficulty. This makes it hard to assess robustness. The ImageNet result is reported without hyperparameter tuning and with only two AT losses; no multiple seeds, no error bars, and no exploration of layer placement.
- Gradient-based AT: The double-backprop-based AT and symmetry loss are interesting, but validation is limited to a small NIN without BN on CIFAR-10 with single-run reporting. The method has substantial computational implications (second-order mixed partials) and potential complications with BN; the authors acknowledge uncertainty about correct BN handling. Without broader evaluation and practical training recipes, it is difficult to endorse this component.
- Ablations and design choices: The paper asserts that l2 normalization of attention maps is “important” but does not systematically ablate normalization choices (l1 vs l2 vs none), β weighting schedules, choice of p in Fpsum, or placement of AT losses. A limited ablation is provided on attention mapping functions (Table 2), but broader sensitivity analyses are missing. Claims that activation-based AT “greatly speeds convergence” are not quantified or consistently shown across datasets.
- Scope and claims: The paper claims AT is the first knowledge transfer method “successfully applied on ImageNet,” yet the KD baseline is under-tuned and under-explored, and other related transfer mechanisms (e.g., logit matching variants, attention/feature similarity regularizers) are not compared on ImageNet. The gradient symmetry regularizer is positioned as an additional contribution but receives minimal empirical support outside CIFAR-10.
- Reproducibility and reporting: While code is provided, many critical details (exact training schedules per dataset, AT loss weights per layer, computational overhead measurements, BN handling in gradient-based AT) are either missing or described informally.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Implement and compare to a faithful FitNets baseline (two-stage hints training) and report strong KD baselines with systematic tuning on CIFAR and ImageNet (temperature, loss weights, data augmentation, same vs different architecture/depth).
- Provide thorough ablations: normalization (l1/l2/none), β schedules, placement and number of AT losses, attention mapping functions (including p), and effect of interpolating attention to mismatched resolutions.
- Report statistical variability (mean ± std over multiple seeds) on CIFAR and at least multiple runs on ImageNet. Include training curves quantifying convergence speed.
- Quantify computational overhead: wall-clock, FLOPs, memory for activation-based vs gradient-based AT. Clarify BN handling in the second backprop pass and provide a recommended implementation.
- Strengthen gradient-based AT evaluation: include BN-equipped architectures, standard augmentations, CIFAR-100 and at least one higher-resolution dataset; compare against double backprop and other regularizers under matched training budgets.
- Broaden comparisons on ImageNet and fine-grained transfer beyond KD: e.g., feature-similarity losses, logit matching, attention-based visualization-guided regularizers, and modern distillation variants.
- Temper claims about being first on ImageNet unless stronger evidence and baselines are provided.

Questions for the authors
1) How does AT compare to a faithful FitNets implementation (two-stage hints) on CIFAR and ImageNet, with matched training budgets?
2) What is the sensitivity of AT to β, normalization choice, p in Fpsum, and the set of layers/groups where AT is applied? Please provide systematic ablations.
3) For ImageNet, can you report multiple runs with standard training schedules and a tuned KD baseline (sweeping temperature and α), and compare convergence speed and final accuracy?
4) What is the actual training-time and memory overhead of activation-based AT and gradient-based AT? Please quantify relative to baseline training.
5) How should BN be handled in the second backprop for gradient-based AT? Did you evaluate different strategies (frozen vs updating BN stats/affine parameters)? Results?
6) Does AT improve robustness (e.g., to corruptions or augmentations) or calibration? Any evidence beyond top-1/5 accuracy?
7) Can you evaluate AT on CIFAR-100 and a detection/localization task to substantiate the claim that higher-resolution attention benefits tasks where spatial information matters?

Overall, the idea of transferring spatial attention is appealing and simple, and the activation-based AT results are promising. However, stronger and fairer baselines, broader validation (especially for the gradient-based variant), and more rigorous analysis are needed before I can recommend acceptance.
