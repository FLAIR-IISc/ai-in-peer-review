Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a general mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student, where “attention” is defined as spatial maps derived from either activations (channel-wise statistics of absolute activations) or gradients w.r.t. input (input sensitivity).
- For activation-based AT, the authors introduce simple mapping functions across channels (sum, sum of powers, max of powers), l2-normalize the resulting attention vectors, and add similarity losses at multiple layers/groups. They also show AT can be combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD).
- For gradient-based AT, they match input gradients between teacher and student via a double-backprop-like second-order step, and additionally propose a flip-invariant gradient regularizer.
- Empirically, they demonstrate consistent gains on CIFAR-10 with WRNs and NIN, show that activation-based AT outperforms full activation transfer, provide finetuning improvements on CUB and MIT-Scenes, and show a nontrivial ImageNet improvement (+1.1% top-1) for ResNet-18 using a ResNet-34 teacher. Code is provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Novel and simple formulation of attention transfer with broad applicability and very low overhead (for activation-based AT), yielding consistent, meaningful accuracy gains across architectures and datasets, including ImageNet. 
2) Careful empirical evidence that attention maps are a better transfer target than full activations and are complementary to KD, with ablations on mapping functions, layer placement, and gradient-based variants.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Improving student performance via teacher guidance is central to model compression and transfer. Using spatial attention as the transfer signal targets what the network “looks at,” which is intuitive, architecture-agnostic, and potentially more robust than logits alone.
- Originality: While AT relates to FitNets’ hints and KD, the specific construction of normalized spatial attention maps from activations and the explicit student-teacher gradient matching are distinct and well-motivated. The flip-invariant gradient regularizer is a neat addition.
- Empirical support:
  - CIFAR-10: AT consistently improves multiple WRN teacher-student pairs. Combining AT with KD further improves results; ablations show sum-based attention mapping functions outperform max-based, and placing losses across layer groups helps.
  - Comparison to full activation transfer: Activation-based AT gives better final accuracy despite both accelerating convergence—strong evidence that spatial attention is the right abstraction to transfer.
  - Gradient-based AT: Demonstrates gains and compares against double backprop and symmetry regularization; although constrained to small NIN without BN, it suggests generality.
  - Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 after finetuning, and can outperform KD on fine-grained data, consistent with the hypothesis that mid-level attention matters more.
  - ImageNet: A practical and nontrivial top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 with only two AT losses and minimal tuning—compelling given that many KD-like methods struggle at ImageNet scale.
- Rigor and clarity: The method is precisely specified (losses, normalization, layer selection). Empirical results include ablations that isolate design choices. The computational overhead discussion is realistic (activation-based AT is cheap; gradient-based AT requires second backprop but is implementable with AD).

Caveats and limitations (do not undermine acceptance, but temper claims)
- Statistical rigor: Some results are medians over 5 runs, others single runs (gradient-based experiments). Variance/error bars are largely absent, and hyperparameter tuning fairness (especially for KD on ImageNet) is limited.
- Gradient-based AT: Only demonstrated on small models without BN; the interaction with BN in second backprop is acknowledged but unresolved.
- ImageNet: Only two layer groups were used and hyperparameters were not tuned; a fuller exploration (all groups, KD comparisons with careful tuning) would strengthen the claims. The “first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet” assertion would benefit from a more systematic literature check and qualifiers.
- Scope: No experiments on detection/segmentation where spatial attention may shine; this is mentioned as future work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report mean and standard deviation across multiple runs for key settings, especially CIFAR and finetuning, and include learning curves. For ImageNet, provide at least two seeds if possible.
- Expand KD baselines with stronger tuning (temperature, alpha, schedule), and include FitNets-style hints (where applicable) on ImageNet or large-scale finetuning to better contextualize AT’s advantage.
- Analyze sensitivity to β, choice of p in Fpsum, and the number/placement of AT losses (e.g., all four ResNet groups on ImageNet). Provide guidance for practitioners.
- Provide a computational cost breakdown (wall-clock, FLOPs) for training with AT vs. baselines, and inference cost (unchanged, but clarity helps).
- Clarify the normalization choice (l2 vs l1), and whether normalization is across spatial positions or entire maps per sample; discuss why normalization is crucial.
- For gradient-based AT, explicitly detail the handling of BN during the second backprop step (frozen vs. updated statistics/parameters), and explore a BN-compatible variant.
- Improve reference formatting and fix minor typos; ensure figure captions/results are self-contained.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are the gains to the β schedule and to the choice of layers where AT is applied? Could you provide a simple rule-of-thumb (e.g., “use last two groups with β scaled by 1/numel”)?
2) Why does sum-based attention consistently outperform max-based attention? Do you have quantitative evidence (e.g., sparsity vs. stability) or diagnostics of attention similarity?
3) Can you quantify how much AT improves calibration or robustness (e.g., to occlusions), given that it constrains spatial focus?
4) For ImageNet, did you try KD with matched architectures and more extensive tuning? If yes, how does AT+KD perform compared to AT alone at scale?
5) For gradient-based AT with BN: did you try freezing BN statistics/parameters during the second backprop, or performing a separate forward pass with fixed BN? Any preliminary results?
6) Can attention transfer be effective for cross-architecture or cross-domain teachers (e.g., Inception teacher to ResNet student; scene classification teacher to object classification student)?
7) Do you observe failure cases where AT hurts (e.g., when teacher attention is mislocalized)? Would a confidence-aware or class-conditional AT help?

Overall, this is a clear, simple, and impactful contribution with convincing empirical benefits and good practical value.
