1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a simple, general mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps that summarize where a network focuses, and to train the student so that its attention matches the teacher’s. Two definitions of attention are explored:
- Activation-based attention: spatial maps formed by aggregating absolute activations across channels (sum, sum of powers, or max of powers), with losses on l2-normalized maps at corresponding layers.
- Gradient-based attention: input sensitivity maps (gradients of the loss w.r.t. input), with a double backpropagation objective to align student and teacher input gradients; an additional horizontal flip-invariance regularizer is proposed.

The method is lightweight to add to standard training (especially the activation-based variant) and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). Experiments on CIFAR-10 with NIN/ResNet/WRN, on transfer learning benchmarks (CUB-200-2011 and MIT Scenes) with ResNet-18/34, and on ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher) show consistent gains. Notably, AT improves over baseline training and over transferring full activations; it can complement KD; and it yields a +1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18 on ImageNet with minimal tuning. The paper provides ablations on attention mapping choices, placement of transfer losses across layers, and comparisons to KD and full activation transfer. Code is released.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated formulation of attention-based knowledge transfer that is simple, generic, and complements existing distillation methods.
- Empirical evidence across multiple datasets and architectures (including ImageNet) supporting consistent improvements, with ablations indicating the importance of the attention design.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity
- The definition of activation-based attention maps is precise, with clear mapping functions and a normalized loss across corresponding layers. The rationale for normalization is explained.
- The gradient-based formulation is grounded in double backpropagation, and implementation considerations are described.
- The experimental section clearly delineates settings for small-scale (CIFAR) and large-scale (transfer learning and ImageNet) evaluations, and presents focused comparisons: AT vs baseline, AT vs KD, AT vs full activation transfer, and ablations on attention function and layer placement.

Technical soundness
- The activation-based AT objective is technically sound and computationally light, relying on differentiable aggregation and normalization; matching at multiple resolution-matched layers is standard practice in representation transfer.
- The gradient-based AT follows established second-order training (double backprop) and adds a symmetry regularizer; the discussion acknowledges the cost and complications (notably batch normalization behavior in the second pass) and limits experiments accordingly.
- The study shows that attention aggregation via sum of squared activations outperforms max-based variants, matching intuition about aggregating discriminative evidence across channels.

Novelty and positioning
- While prior work visualizes attentions (e.g., gradients, guided backprop, CAM/Grad-CAM) and transfers knowledge via logits (KD) or full activations (FitNets), using spatial attention maps explicitly as the knowledge to transfer is a distinct and well-placed idea.
- The demonstration that activation-based AT outperforms full activation transfer for the student’s final accuracy is an informative and practically relevant finding.
- Results on ImageNet are notable; the paper argues AT is among the first successful knowledge transfer methods applied at this scale, and shows concrete gains with a minimal setup.

Empirical support
- CIFAR-10: AT yields consistent and sizable improvements across multiple teacher/student pairs and depths; combining AT with KD helps further in some settings. Ablations on the number and placement of AT losses and on attention functions are included.
- Gradient-based AT: improves over baseline and over double backprop alone; symmetry regularization further helps. The scope is limited (single-run, thin NIN, no BN), but results are consistent.
- Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, and outperforms/equals KD depending on the dataset, aligning with the intuition that spatial attention is valuable for fine-grained tasks.
- ImageNet: +1.1% top-1 with two AT losses and no tuning is meaningful for a compact model.

Limitations
- Some experiments lack multiple seeds/variances (e.g., gradient-based AT is single-run and excludes BN). Hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., β) and computational overhead are only briefly discussed.
- The ImageNet KD comparison is not fully tuned; the “first successful” claim is not comprehensively evidenced against all contemporaneous transfer variants.
- References contain formatting issues; some implementation details are in the appendix or code but could be summarized more fully.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not decisive to the recommendation)
- Provide multiple runs with mean/std for key results, especially on CIFAR and ImageNet, to quantify variance and robustness.
- Report training/inference overhead for AT (GPU-hours, additional memory, latency). For gradient-based AT, quantify the extra cost of the second backprop.
- Expand ablations: sensitivity to β, normalization choice (l1 vs l2), number and choice of layers for AT, and p in Fpsum. Include calibration metrics if possible.
- Clarify the handling of batch normalization under gradient-based AT’s second derivative step, and whether freezing BN improves stability.
- For ImageNet, attempt modest tuning for KD and report learning curves/compute budgets for a fairer comparison.
- Consider qualitative comparisons of student vs teacher attention over more examples to strengthen the interpretation that AT aligns meaningful regions.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the β coefficient and the choice of normalization (l1 vs l2)? Is there a principled way to set β across layers with different spatial sizes?
- Which layers benefit most from AT in ResNets/WRNs? Does adding AT at all groups always help, or is there a diminishing return after a few higher-level layers?
- For activation functions: beyond p=2, do larger p consistently hurt due to oversharpening? Would learned channel weights (e.g., SE-style) for attention aggregation improve transfer?
- What is the precise training and inference overhead introduced by activation-based AT per batch and overall wall-clock (including teacher forward pass)?
- In gradient-based AT, how do you handle batch normalization during the second backprop? Did you try freezing BN statistics and parameters during the second pass?
- On ImageNet, did you attempt AT between same-depth architectures to compare with the different-depth setting? Any observations on convergence stability?
- Can you share failure cases where AT degrades performance? Are there tasks where attention alignment is detrimental (e.g., heavily occluded or multi-object scenes)?
- Would the method extend to dense prediction (detection/segmentation) by applying AT at feature pyramid levels? Any preliminary results?
