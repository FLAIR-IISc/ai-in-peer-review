- Proposes "attention transfer" (AT) to transfer spatial attention maps from teacher CNNs to students.
- Defines activation-based AT: aggregate channel activations to H×W maps, l2-normalize, and match with l2 loss.
- Defines gradient-based AT: match input-sensitivity gradients via a double-backprop objective and add flip-invariance regularizer.
- Shows consistent CIFAR gains with Wide-ResNets and NIN; AT outperforms full-activation regression.
- Combining AT with knowledge distillation (KD) can further improve CIFAR performance.
- Demonstrates transfer learning gains on CUB and MIT Scenes, narrowing ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap and sometimes beating KD.
- Reports ImageNet improvement: +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 from ResNet-34 to ResNet-18 without heavy tuning.
- Provides preliminary, limited results for gradient-based AT and symmetry regularization on CIFAR-NIN.
- Decision: Weak Accept due to novelty, practicality, and empirical benefits across datasets including ImageNet.
- Activation-based AT is practical, lightweight, and consistently better than full-activation matching, highlighting spatial focus as key.
- Conceptually clear link between interpretability (attention maps) and knowledge transfer.
- Methodologically simple and general: small auxiliary loss on normalized attention maps with little overhead.
- Empirical breadth across datasets, architectures, and teacher-student depth combinations is a strength.
- Useful ablations on aggregation methods, grouping, and normalization demonstrating design choices matter.
- Gradient-based AT and flip-invariance are principled and interesting additions, though preliminary.
- Gradient-based AT experiments are under-explored and conducted in non-standard settings (single-seed, no BN, no weight decay).
- Limited statistical rigor: many single-run results, lacking confidence intervals and multiple seeds, especially for ImageNet.
- Incomplete baselines on large-scale: KD and FitNets not fully tuned or compared on ImageNet.
- Missing critical implementation details for reproducibility (layer pairings, β schedules, loss weights, temperatures, overhead).
- Some broad claims (e.g., first successful transfer on ImageNet) should be softened or better supported.
- Recommend reporting multiple runs, training curves, and accuracy distributions to show stability.
- Request full experiment tables and exact configs (layers used, attention resolutions, β per layer, KD schedules).
- Ask to report computational overhead (wall-clock and memory) for AT vs KD vs FitNets and cost of gradient-based AT.
- Suggest deeper ablations: exponent p, normalization types, per-channel normalization, loss locations, widths/depths, attention resolution.
- Urge stronger baselines: tuned KD on ImageNet, FitNets with good hints, Jacobian/Sobolev training, and class-conditional AT variants.
- Suggest quantifying attention similarity (correlations) and relating it to accuracy gains.
- Propose exploring other invariances (rotation, scale) for symmetry loss and discussing interaction with data augmentation.
- Question sensitivity to layer pairing and normalization choices (L2 vs cosine; per-location vs per-feature normalization).
- Ask about β scheduling across datasets and robustness to β choices when combined with KD.
- Request training time and memory overhead comparisons for activation- and gradient-based AT vs baseline and KD on CIFAR/ImageNet.
- Inquire which KD temperatures and loss weights were tried on ImageNet and whether same-architecture teachers were tested.
- Ask for clarification on feasible autodiff strategies for gradient-based AT with BN (e.g., freezing BN stats) and any preliminary BN results.
- Wonder if AT improves sample efficiency or robustness to corruptions/occlusions and whether symmetry loss aids flip robustness.
- Ask whether student attention becomes more class-discriminative (e.g., Grad-CAM overlap with ground truth) and if that correlates with accuracy.
