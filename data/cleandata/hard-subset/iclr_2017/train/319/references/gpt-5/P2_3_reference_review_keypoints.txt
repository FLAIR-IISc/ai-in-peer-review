- Problem: Can a teacher CNN improve a student by transferring spatial attention maps (where it looks) rather than only logits?
- Method: Define attention as spatial maps from internal activations (sum/psum/max) and input gradients; match l2-normalized attention maps at selected layers, optionally with KD.
- Gradient AT: For gradient-based attention transfer use double backprop and a flip-invariance regularizer on input gradients.
- Experiments: Extensive activation-based AT on CIFAR-10, fine-tuning on CUB and MIT Scenes, and ResNet-34→ResNet-18 ImageNet transfer; ablations on mapping, per-group vs single-layer, AT vs full activation regression, and combination with KD.
- Main claims: AT yields consistent gains, activation-based AT outperforms full activation matching, AT complements/outperforms KD often, and shows promising ImageNet results where KD struggled.
- Decision: Weak accept — method is simple, general, well-motivated, and backed by consistent improvements including ImageNet.
- Reproducibility: Code provided and implementation/hyperparameters outlined, though some settings could be more complete.
- Novelty/significance: Using spatial attention as a supervisory signal is conceptually distinct from KD and FitNets and is architecture-agnostic and plug-and-play.
- Soundness: Loss is well-defined, differentiable, inexpensive per-layer L2; normalization avoids scale confounds; BN caveats noted for gradient AT.
- CIFAR results: Consistent gains across depth/width configs; AT > full activation transfer; sum-based mappings and multi-group transfer perform better.
- Fine-grained tasks: On CUB and Scenes, AT narrows ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap and outperforms KD on CUB.
- ImageNet result: Reported +1.1% top-1 over ResNet-18 with two AT losses, indicating scalability where KD underperformed in their setup.
- Ablations: Clear analyses of mapping choices (sum vs max), p in psum, per-layer contributions, and combination with KD.
- Limitations: Gradient-based AT only shown on CIFAR/NIN without BN, limited runs, and suggestive but inconclusive evidence.
- Baseline concerns: ImageNet KD baselines may be under-tuned; the “first successful” ImageNet transfer claim needs stronger baselining.
- Statistical rigor: Often medians of 5 runs and some single runs; variance reporting is limited.
- Suggested improvements: Provide full numerical results, mean±std over seeds, and full training curves for key experiments.
- Suggested improvements: Strengthen KD baselines (temperature, alpha, label smoothing, schedule), report compute budgets, and compare to stronger feature distillation baselines.
- Suggested controls: Test random teacher attention, self-teacher AT, and unnormalized vs normalized attention matching.
- Suggested measurements: Quantify per-iteration time/memory overhead for activation- and gradient-based AT.
- Gradient AT fixes: Show BN handling strategies (freeze BN stats, separate forward) and evaluate on at least one larger dataset/architecture.
- Hyperparameter guidance: Provide sensitivity analysis for β, p in Fpsum, and layer selection with a simple heuristic.
- Presentation fixes: Tighten language, unify notation, fix citations, move key ablations to main text, and add side-by-side attention map visualizations.
- Questions for authors: KD hyperparameters/training schedules tried on ImageNet, sensitivity of AT to β/p/layer choices, control results with randomized/weaker teachers, training time/memory overheads, BN handling in gradient AT, robustness/calibration/OOD results, detection/segmentation preliminary results, and broader comparisons to other feature-distillation methods on ImageNet.
