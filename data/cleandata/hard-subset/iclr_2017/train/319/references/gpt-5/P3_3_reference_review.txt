Summary
- This paper proposes using spatial “attention” maps as a vehicle for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. Two families of attention are considered: activation-based (spatial maps obtained by aggregating hidden activations across channels, e.g., sum of absolute values, sum of powers, or max of powers) and gradient-based (input saliency maps computed as gradients of the loss wrt input). The key idea is to supervise the student so that its attention maps match the teacher’s, in addition to the standard supervised loss.
- The activation-based attention transfer (AT) uses l2-normalized attention maps, matched at multiple layers (per residual group or block). The loss can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).
- The gradient-based variant uses a double-backprop style objective to match teacher/student saliency maps; the paper also explores a symmetry regularizer enforcing horizontal flip consistency.
- Experiments on CIFAR-10/100 with NIN/ResNet/WRN, transfer learning on CUB/Scenes, and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student with ResNet-34 teacher) show consistent gains. The paper reports that activation-based AT outperforms full-activation regression (FitNets-style hints) and that AT can complement KD. On ImageNet, AT yields +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 for ResNet-18 without hyperparameter tuning.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The method is simple, well-motivated, and broadly applicable across architectures and tasks. It offers a clear alternative to KD/hints with a more interpretable, spatially structured signal.
- Empirical gains are consistent and, notably, include an ImageNet improvement with a strong baseline, which is rare for knowledge transfer papers.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well placed in the literature. It connects to attention/saliency visualization work (Simonyan, Zeiler, Grad-CAM) and to knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets). The argument that attention maps provide a compact, spatially meaningful target that is less brittle than full activation matching is sensible and supported by results.
- Methodology: The activation-based mapping functions are reasonable and the analysis of their properties (sum vs sum-of-powers vs max-of-powers) is helpful. The normalization of spatial maps prior to matching is an important and justified design choice (and empirically emphasized). The layer pairing strategy (per residual group or per block) is straightforward and practical.
- Empirical evidence: 
  - CIFAR: Multiple teacher–student pairs (varying depth/width) and ablations (which layers to match, which attention functions) show consistent improvements. The finding that AT outperforms full-activation transfer is meaningful and practically valuable.
  - Gradient-based AT: The results show that gradient matching and symmetry regularization can help, though this part is less mature (limited to NIN without BN, single-run reporting). Still, it supports the broader thesis that “attention supervision” can be effective across definitions.
  - Larger-scale: On CUB/Scenes, AT narrows the gap between RN-18 and RN-34 and compares favorably to KD, especially on fine-grained CUB where mid-level attention likely matters more. On ImageNet, the +1.1% top-1 for RN-18 from only two AT losses (and no tuning) is a strong and practically relevant result; it also strengthens the claim that AT scales.
- Rigor: The CIFAR activation-based experiments include ablations and multi-run medians (in some tables). The paper discusses hyperparameters (β scheduling), temperature for KD, and design choices. Code/models availability further supports reproducibility.

Caveats and limitations (do not preclude acceptance)
- Gradient-based AT experiments are preliminary: only on NIN without BN, single runs, and limited analysis of BN’s role in second-order backprop. Claims around this component should be toned down or expanded with more evidence.
- KD baselines on ImageNet are underwhelming and the explanation (architecture/depth mismatch) is plausible but would benefit from stronger baselines (e.g., KD with tuned temperature/α, same-depth teacher).
- The claim that this is the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet should be framed carefully; while FitNets/KD are often not demonstrated convincingly at ImageNet scale, the literature is evolving and assertions should be conservative.
- The assumption that absolute activation magnitudes reflect “attention” is practical but somewhat heuristic. It would be helpful to quantify attention-map quality (e.g., pointing game, CAM overlap) beyond qualitative visuals, and to relate attention similarity to accuracy gains.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Expand the analysis of normalization: compare l1 vs l2 normalization of attention maps, discuss sensitivity to β and to the choice/number of layers used for AT. Provide a sensitivity plot.
- Report compute/throughput overhead: additional memory and time cost for teacher forward, attention computation, and (for gradient-AT) the second backprop. Clarify training-time vs inference-time costs.
- Provide more systematic multi-run statistics on all settings, especially for gradient-based AT. Include variance/error bars and significance tests where feasible.
- Strengthen ImageNet baselines: run KD with tuned temperatures and same-architecture teacher; possibly compare against improved hint-based activation matching with 1x1 adapters to rule out weak baselines.
- Clarify implementation details for layer alignment and interpolation of attention maps; confirm teacher gradients are stop-grad, and whether teacher parameters are frozen and in what precision.
- Improve references and formatting; some citations are malformed.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is AT to the β schedule and to the number and placement of AT losses? Does adding AT to earlier groups consistently help on ImageNet?
- Did you try l1 normalization or other normalizations of attention maps? Any benefit to temperature-scaling attention magnitudes before normalization?
- For activation-based AT, how do you handle spatial resolution mismatches precisely (bilinear interpolation, learned up/downsampling)? Have you tried multi-scale attention fusion?
- Can you quantify the correlation between attention similarity (e.g., cosine of normalized maps) and accuracy improvements during training? Does attention alignment predict generalization?
- How robust is AT to a weaker or miscalibrated teacher? What happens when teacher accuracy is similar to or below the student’s?
- For gradient-based AT with BN: have you tried a “stop-statistics” mode (frozen BN) during second backprop or recomputing with fixed BN stats? Any empirical guidance?
- What is the training-time overhead for activation-based AT and for gradient-based AT? Please provide wall-clock factors and memory usage.
- Could class-conditional attention (e.g., Grad-CAM on the teacher’s predicted or ground-truth class) be used for transfer, potentially improving class-discriminativeness over plain gradients?

Overall, this paper presents a simple, general, and effective idea with solid evidence, particularly for activation-based attention transfer. Despite some limitations in the gradient-based part and large-scale KD comparison, the contribution is impactful and practically relevant.
