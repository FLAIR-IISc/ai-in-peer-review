Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a new, simple mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN by supervising the student’s spatial attention maps to match those of the teacher. Attention is operationalized as spatial maps computed from intermediate feature activations (channel-wise statistics such as sum of squared activations) or as input gradients (sensitivity maps).
- Two concrete instantiations are presented:
  1) Activation-based AT: defines attention as channel-wise aggregation of absolute (or powered) activations; uses normalized L2 losses across selected layers to align student/teacher attention.
  2) Gradient-based AT: aligns input gradients via a double-backprop loss; also proposes a flip-invariance regularizer on gradient attention maps.
- AT is shown to be complementary to knowledge distillation (KD) and superior to FitNets-style full feature regression in several settings. It yields consistent gains on CIFAR (WRN and NIN), improves fine-tuning on CUB and MIT-Scenes, and provides a notable +1.1% top-1 gain for ResNet-18 on ImageNet using a ResNet-34 teacher with minimal tuning.
- Ablations explore attention definitions (sum vs. max, effect of p), depth alignment (per-block vs. per-group), normalization, and combinations with KD. Code is promised.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: The paper introduces a novel and compelling idea—using attention as the object of transfer—that is simple, broadly applicable, and empirically effective, including on ImageNet. Despite some incomplete validations (especially for gradient-based AT), the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to influence subsequent work.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty: Reframes knowledge transfer from matching logits or full features to matching spatial attention—a distinct, intuitive signal that is more “where-to-look” than “what-to-represent.” This goes beyond FitNets/hints by focusing on pooled spatial energy rather than high-dimensional matching, and connects to the attention visualization literature in a new way.
- Simplicity and generality: The activation-based AT loss is lightweight, architecture-agnostic, and easy to add at multiple layers. L2-normalized attention vectors stabilize training. It composes naturally with KD.
- Empirical evidence:
  - CIFAR: AT provides consistent and meaningful gains across WRN teacher–student pairs. Ablations show sum-based maps outperform max-based, and multiple AT losses (per group) help.
  - AT vs full activation regression: AT improves final accuracy more than full-activation transfer while also accelerating convergence.
  - Gradient-based AT: Preliminary but promising results; double backprop and flip-invariance regularizers both help; gradient-based AT improves over baseline and is competitive with KD in the tested setting.
  - Transfer learning: On CUB and MIT-Scenes, AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and -34, and outperforms KD on CUB—plausibly due to the salience of spatial attention for fine-grained tasks.
  - ImageNet: A +1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 using a ResNet-34 teacher with only two AT losses and minimal tuning is impressive and practically relevant.
- Insight: The observation that attention maps carry more transferable information than full activations is interesting and potentially impactful for distillation research.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
- Clarify the relationship to “hints”/feature mimic methods beyond FitNets (e.g., other feature-based distillation baselines). A stronger comparison set would sharpen the contribution.
- Report more training details and sensitivity:
  - Exact β schedules, normalization choices (L1 vs L2), layer selection strategy.
  - Compute/memory overhead, wall-clock impact when adding AT (especially with multiple losses).
  - Statistical significance: multiple runs and confidence intervals on key results (CIFAR, ImageNet).
- Gradient-based AT:
  - Provide a clearer procedure for handling batch normalization during the second backward pass. Even a recommended recipe would aid adoption.
  - Extend to a BN-enabled setting and report multi-run robustness.
- Explore class-specific attention maps (e.g., Grad-CAM targets) vs the class-agnostic activation energy used here. Are class-conditioned attention maps more beneficial for transfer?
- Investigate broader tasks where spatial attention is critical (detection, segmentation, weakly supervised localization). This would strengthen the claim that AT leverages spatial signals better than KD.
- Improve presentation: fix reference formatting; ensure figures are legible and include qualitative “before vs after” attention comparisons in the main text.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the choice of layers for AT? Is there a simple heuristic (e.g., last block of each stage) that generalizes across architectures?
2) How do β values and normalization type (L1 vs L2) affect stability and final accuracy? Any rule-of-thumb for scaling β with feature map size or batch size?
3) On ImageNet, did you try AT at all four groups and/or class-specific attention targets? Do gains compound?
4) What is the additional compute/memory cost of AT in practice (activation-based vs gradient-based), and how does it scale with the number of AT sites?
5) Could you clarify whether attention maps are computed from pre- or post-ReLU activations, and whether using squared activations is crucial vs absolute value?
6) For gradient-based AT with BN, which implementation choice worked best (e.g., frozen BN stats vs recomputed in the second pass)?
7) Do student attention maps converge to teacher-like patterns even when the student has different depth/width? Any qualitative failure cases?

Overall, the paper offers a clear, novel direction with convincing empirical evidence. I recommend acceptance.
