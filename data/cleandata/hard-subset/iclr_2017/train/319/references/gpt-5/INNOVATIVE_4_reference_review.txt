Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a new mechanism for knowledge transfer between neural networks. Instead of distilling logits or regressing full intermediate activations, the student is trained to match the teacher’s spatial attention maps. Two complementary notions of attention are explored:
- Activation-based spatial attention: channel-wise statistics (sum/psum/max of absolute activations) at selected layers, l2-normalized, matched via an auxiliary loss.
- Gradient-based attention: input gradients of the loss as sensitivity maps, including a symmetry regularizer (horizontal flip invariance) and a student–teacher gradient matching objective implemented with double backpropagation.

The paper claims AT yields consistent improvements across CIFAR, fine-tuning on CUB/Scenes, and ImageNet (ResNet-18 improved by +1.1% top-1 vs baseline with only two AT losses). It also reports that activation-based AT outperforms full activation regression and can complement knowledge distillation (KD). Code and models are released.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: reframing distillation as alignment of spatial attention, via simple, architecture-agnostic mappings, is a compelling new direction.
- Broad empirical signal: AT consistently helps across architectures/datasets, including a notable ImageNet gain with minimal tuning; activation-based AT beats full activation transfer, suggesting the idea targets what matters.

Supporting arguments
- Well-motivated and placed in the literature: The paper connects to saliency/CAM/Grad-CAM and distillation/fitnets, but importantly shifts transfer from channel-wise feature regression to normalized spatial attention. This sidesteps the need for adaptation layers and focuses on where the teacher looks rather than what exact representation it carries. The gradient-based variant and symmetry regularizer build on double backprop, but repurpose it for teacher–student transfer and equivariance constraints—an interesting twist that broadens the toolset.
- Methodological simplicity and generality: The activation mappings are simple, low-overhead, and easy to integrate at multiple layers. Normalizing the attention maps is intuitive and empirically important. The approach works with residual and non-residual nets, and can be combined with KD.
- Empirical support:
  - CIFAR: Consistent improvements for multiple WRN teacher–student pairs; ablation across attention mappings favors sum-based variants; combining AT with KD further helps; AT > full activation transfer in final accuracy despite both accelerating convergence.
  - Gradient-based AT: On CIFAR/NIN, gradient regularization and flip-invariance both help; student–teacher gradient AT helps; activation-based AT is best among tested options in this setup.
  - Transfer learning (CUB/Scenes): AT narrows the ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap, sometimes outperforming KD (notably on fine-grained CUB where mid-level attention plausibly matters most).
  - ImageNet: A +1.1% top-1 gain for ResNet-18 with only two AT losses and no hyperparameter tuning suggests scalability; KD struggled in the same setting, highlighting the distinct value of attention alignment.
- Impact: The idea is broadly applicable, incurs little overhead (especially the activation-based variant), and offers a fresh way to control inductive bias in students by steering spatial focus.

Concerns and weaknesses (do not block acceptance)
- Gradient-based AT is only demonstrated on small NIN without batch norm; BN interaction with double backprop is acknowledged but unresolved experimentally.
- Limited ablations on hyperparameters (β schedule), number/placement of transfer points, and normalization choices beyond l2; compute overheads and wall-clock training cost are not quantified.
- ImageNet: encouraging gain, but only one teacher–student pair, two AT losses, no error bars, and KD baselines may not be fully optimized; the “first successful transfer on ImageNet” claim should be toned down or better substantiated.
- Presentation: Some figures/tables are referenced but not shown; reference formatting is inconsistent; minor clarity issues (e.g., precise implementation details for aligning resolutions, interpolation method, and per-layer β).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Report standard deviations over multiple runs where feasible, especially on CIFAR and ImageNet; quantify compute/training-time overhead from AT and from double backprop.
- Provide a more complete ablation: β magnitude/schedule; normalization type (l1 vs l2); number and exact placement of AT losses; effect of p in Fpsum; sensitivity to attention map interpolation method.
- Expand ImageNet experiments: add AT at all groups; try other teacher–student pairs (including different architectures), and a tuned KD baseline to strengthen claims.
- Clarify BN handling in gradient-based AT and evaluate it with modern BN-equipped models; detail the second backprop implementation in the presence of BN/Dropout.
- Evaluate cross-architecture transfer (e.g., ResNet → MobileNet/VGG) and heterogeneous capacities to test generality.
- Report qualitative attention alignment before/after AT across layers; where possible, relate attention to ground-truth regions (e.g., bounding boxes/part annotations) to support the interpretability narrative.
- Clean up references and figure/table presentation.

Questions for the authors
1. β scheduling and normalization: How sensitive are results to the exact β per layer and its decay schedule? Did l1 normalization or temperature scaling of attention maps help?
2. Placement of AT losses: How does accuracy vary with using early vs mid vs late layers, and with the number of AT losses on ImageNet?
3. Compute overhead: What is the per-iteration wall-clock/time and memory overhead of activation-based AT and gradient-based AT? Any issues for large-batch training?
4. Cross-architecture transfer: Have you tried transferring from networks with different macro-structures (e.g., Inception/WRN → ResNet-18, or teacher with SE/attention modules)? Does AT still help?
5. BN with double backprop: What behavior do you observe if you freeze BN statistics/parameters during the second backprop vs not? Any recommended practice?
6. Robustness and calibration: Does AT affect adversarial robustness, input gradient smoothness, or calibration (ECE)? Your gradient regularization and symmetry terms suggest possible benefits.
7. Failure modes: Are there cases where AT hurts (e.g., weak teachers, domain shift)? What happens if the teacher’s attention is mislocalized or overly sparse?
8. ImageNet KD: With more tuning (temperature, α, label smoothing), can KD match AT, or is AT consistently easier to optimize?

Overall, the paper introduces a simple and fresh concept—aligning spatial attention for knowledge transfer—with solid empirical evidence and high potential impact. I recommend acceptance.
