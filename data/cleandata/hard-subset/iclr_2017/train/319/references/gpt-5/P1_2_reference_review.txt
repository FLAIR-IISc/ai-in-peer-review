Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. “Attention” is defined as spatial maps derived from intermediate representations.
- Two families of attention are introduced: activation-based (channel-wise spatial statistics such as sum of |A|^p) and gradient-based (input sensitivity maps ∂L/∂x). The authors argue these capture where a network “looks” to make decisions.
- A simple, normalized alignment loss is used to match teacher and student attention at selected layers. AT can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).
- Empirical results across CIFAR-10, CUB-200-2011, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet show consistent gains. Activation-based AT generally outperforms full activation regression (FitNets-style hints) and often complements or exceeds KD. A horizontal flip symmetry regularizer for gradient attention is also explored.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The core idea—transferring spatial attention rather than full activations or soft targets—is simple, well-motivated, and demonstrates consistent empirical benefit, including on ImageNet where many distillation variants struggle.
2) The method is broadly applicable, lightweight to implement, and compatible with diverse architectures and tasks. The empirical evidence, while not exhaustive, is sufficiently convincing for an ICLR contribution.

Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The work addresses how to define attention for CNNs and how to transfer that attention from a stronger teacher to a smaller student to improve performance. This is positioned as a complementary avenue to KD and feature-hint methods.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper situates itself at the intersection of attention visualization (e.g., saliency, CAM, Grad-CAM) and knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets). Using spatial attention maps as the representation to transfer is a natural intermediary between raw features and logits, and the discussion of different attention mappings (sum vs max, effect of p) is thoughtful. The relation to double backprop and equivariance (via symmetry loss) is appropriate.

Do the results support the claims?
- CIFAR: Multiple teacher–student pairs (WRN and ResNet) show clear gains from AT; activation-based AT beats full activation regression and often improves further when combined with KD. Ablations over mapping functions indicate sum-based maps are better than max-based; using multiple layers helps more than a single layer.
- Gradient-based AT: On CIFAR with a thin NIN (no BN), double backprop, flip symmetry, and teacher–student gradient AT each yield gains; activation-based AT still performs best. The gradient-based experiments are more limited (no BN, deterministic single runs), but the findings are coherent.
- Transfer learning: On CUB and MIT Scenes, AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and -34 and sometimes outperforms KD, which aligns with the intuition that intermediate attention benefits fine-grained tasks.
- ImageNet: ResNet-18 gains 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 using AT from ResNet-34 with two transfer losses, without tuning. This is a compelling result given known KD difficulties on ImageNet.

Rigor and clarity:
- The normalization of attention maps is highlighted as important; the loss is simple and reproducible. Code is promised.
- Some experimental details are missing or ad hoc (e.g., “no time to tune” on ImageNet; gradient-based AT limitations due to BN and double backprop), and several results lack variance reporting. Nonetheless, the breadth across datasets and models, plus consistent qualitative and quantitative trends, give credibility.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily affecting decision)
- Report variability: Provide mean ± std over multiple seeds for the main tables (CIFAR, transfer learning), not only medians or single runs. For ImageNet, even a few runs or partial training curves comparing baselines would strengthen claims.
- Fair comparisons: Ensure identical data augmentation and training schedules for baseline, AT, KD, and full-activation transfer. Clarify when architectural tweaks (e.g., removing ReLUs before pooling) are applied to teacher vs student and why.
- Hyperparameters: Expand on β scheduling, choice of layers for AT, and the role of l2 normalization vs l1. Provide guidance to practitioners on selecting Fsum vs F^2sum, number of AT layers, and β magnitudes relative to batch size and map size.
- Computational cost: Quantify overhead (time and memory) for AT and especially for gradient-based AT with double backprop, and discuss scalability with BN. Provide a practical recipe if/when gradient-based AT is recommended.
- Clarify gradient attention: Specify whether gradients are of the loss w.r.t. the true label, or of logits/probabilities (and which class). Discuss class-discriminativity vs generic sensitivity and the implications for transfer.
- Broaden baselines: Where feasible, include FitNets-style hints with modern residuals, and a stronger KD baseline on ImageNet (e.g., tuned temperature/α), to contextualize the ImageNet gain.
- Editing: Clean up reference formatting and minor typos; improve figure captions to be self-contained.

Questions for the authors
1) Attention normalization: You emphasize l2 normalization as crucial. Did you try l1 normalization or temperature scaling of maps? How sensitive are results to normalization choice and β, especially on ImageNet?
2) Layer selection: How does performance vary with which layers receive AT (early vs mid vs late) when controlling for the number of losses? Any guidance beyond “one per group”?
3) Class specificity: For gradient-based AT, which gradient is used (loss w.r.t. ground-truth, or a target class logit/probability)? Do class-conditional gradients (e.g., Grad-CAM-style) change outcomes?
4) KD combination: On which settings does AT+KD help vs hurt? Can you provide tuned KD results on ImageNet, or explain why KD underperforms there relative to AT?
5) Robustness and interpretability: Does AT improve robustness to input perturbations or occlusions? Do student attention maps become more class-discriminative or human-aligned (quantified, e.g., pointing game)?
6) Cost and practicality: What is the training time overhead for AT and gradient AT relative to baseline on CIFAR and ImageNet? Any memory-saving tricks?
7) Batch normalization with double backprop: Can you clarify a principled way to handle BN parameters during the second backprop? Did you try freezing BN statistics/parameters for the second pass?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, simple, and broadly useful contribution with convincing empirical evidence. I recommend acceptance.
