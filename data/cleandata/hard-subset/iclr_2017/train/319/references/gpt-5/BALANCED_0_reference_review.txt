1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple, broadly applicable mechanism for transferring knowledge from a teacher CNN to a student by aligning their spatial attention. Two forms of attention are considered: (i) activation-based maps that aggregate channel activations at each spatial location (e.g., sum of absolute values, or sum of squared activations), and (ii) gradient-based maps that use input saliency (∂L/∂x). For activation-based AT, the method matches l2-normalized attention maps across selected layers (or groups) of teacher and student via an auxiliary loss, and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). For gradient-based AT, the paper derives a tractable “double backprop” training objective and also proposes a symmetry regularizer enforcing horizontal flip consistency of input gradients.

Empirically, activation-based AT yields consistent gains on CIFAR-10 across WRN and ResNet architectures, outperforms full activation transfer, and combines effectively with KD. It improves transfer learning on CUB-200 and MIT Scenes when finetuning ResNet-18 from a ResNet-34 teacher, and yields 1.1% top-1 improvement on ImageNet for a ResNet-18 student with a ResNet-34 teacher (with minimal hyperparameter tuning). Gradient-based AT also shows gains on CIFAR-10 in a controlled (non-BN) NIN setting. Code is provided.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: aligning spatial attention is a novel and well-motivated form of inductive bias for distillation that is easy to add to many CNNs, and shown to work across datasets and architectures.
- Solid empirical evidence: consistent improvements over strong baselines on CIFAR, meaningful transfer learning gains, and a tangible ImageNet improvement with minimal tuning. Clear ablations (e.g., attention functions, placement of losses, comparison to full-activation transfer).

3) Supporting arguments
Problem and motivation: The paper targets a timely question—leveraging where a teacher “looks” to guide a student. The motivation is grounded in both interpretability (attention maps reflect discriminative regions) and efficiency (attention is a lower-dimensional target than full feature tensors). The placement in the literature is appropriate, relating to saliency/visualization (Grad-CAM, guided backprop, CAM) and distillation (KD, FitNets). The contrast with full activation regression is experimentally and conceptually clear.

Approach: The activation-based AT loss is straightforward, with sensible design choices: channel-wise aggregation to obtain spatial maps, l2 normalization to eliminate scale, and aligning across corresponding resolution levels. The gradient-based AT is technically more involved but correctly derived via double backprop; the symmetry regularizer is an interesting addition that improves performance and is orthogonal to AT.

Empirical support and rigor: 
- CIFAR: Improvements are consistent across multiple teacher/student pairs; ablations compare different attention mappings (sum vs max, effect of p) and locations (per-group). The result that attention-map transfer outperforms full activation transfer is a strong point.
- Transfer learning: Gains on CUB and Scenes suggest AT is useful when mid-level attention matters (fine-grained recognition), and qualitative maps look more teacher-like.
- ImageNet: A 1.1% top-1 gain for ResNet-18 with a ResNet-34 teacher is practically meaningful; this is notable given limited tuning.
- Gradient-based AT: Shows promise; the symmetry norm is competitive. The authors candidly discuss limitations with BN and computational aspects.

Caveats:
- Statistical rigor: Many results are single runs or medians of only 5 runs; report variance more systematically.
- Gradient-based AT evaluation is limited (simplified architecture, no BN); the framework-level questions (BN in second backprop) remain open.
- KD baselines on ImageNet appear under-tuned; negative results make the AT vs KD comparison less conclusive at scale.
- Limited sensitivity analyses (β, layer selection, normalization choice), and no cost/overhead measurements.
- The “first on ImageNet” claim should be tempered or better supported.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decisive for acceptance)
- Clarify normalization: Why l2 over l1? How sensitive are results to normalization choice and to β? A short sensitivity plot would strengthen the case.
- Layer selection: Provide a brief study on which layers matter most, and whether aligning only late or only mid-level attention suffices on large-scale tasks.
- Cost analysis: Quantify additional training time/memory for AT (and for gradient-based AT’s second backprop). Inference cost is presumably unchanged; state this explicitly.
- KD combination: On ImageNet, try modest hyperparameter sweeps for KD temperature and α; current negative results may be due to lack of tuning.
- Gradient-based AT: Demonstrate with BN (even on CIFAR/ResNet) and clarify how the second pass handles BN statistics/parameters. Report compute overhead.
- Alternative attention definitions: Briefly report results with other channel aggregation schemes (e.g., learned channel weights, softmax-normalized attention) to show robustness.
- Robustness and generalization: Test under stronger data augmentations (e.g., Cutout/mixup) to see if AT still adds value. Consider preliminary results on a dense prediction task to support claims about spatially sensitive problems.
- Claims and presentation: Soften the ImageNet “first” claim or support it with a thorough survey. Clean up references (formatting inconsistencies) and ensure all figures/tables are clearly legible and self-contained.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is AT to the choice of p in F_psum, to β, and to the exact set of layers used? Can you provide a small grid or trend analysis?
- Do you compute teacher attention online during training, or precompute/store them? What is the measured training overhead (time and memory) for AT and for gradient-based AT?
- When teacher and student depths differ, how exactly do you align spatial resolutions (interpolation method, any aliasing concerns)?
- Does AT still help if the student is as large as (or larger than) the teacher? How does the benefit scale with teacher quality?
- For gradient-based AT with BN, what implementation do you recommend for the second pass (frozen BN stats vs updating)? Any empirical guidance?
- Beyond qualitative figures, have you quantified student–teacher attention similarity (e.g., cosine similarity) and its correlation with accuracy?
- On ImageNet, did you try adding AT losses to all four groups and modestly tuning β? Any preliminary numbers?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, practical contribution with clear empirical benefits. With minor additions to rigor and clarity, it will be a strong paper.
