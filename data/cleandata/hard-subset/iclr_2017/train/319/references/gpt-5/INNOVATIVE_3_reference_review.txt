1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a new mechanism for knowledge transfer between neural networks: a teacher provides spatial “where to look” signals to a student via attention maps. Two complementary definitions of attention are explored: activation-based maps (channel statistics over feature activations) and gradient-based maps (input sensitivity). The authors introduce simple mapping functions (e.g., sum of absolute activations, with exponent p) and an AT loss that matches l2-normalized attention maps across selected layers. They also propose a symmetry regularizer enforcing horizontal-flip invariance for gradient-based attention.

Empirically, the paper reports:
- Consistent CIFAR-10 gains across multiple teacher–student pairs, both same and different depths/widths, with AT outperforming full activation transfer and being complementary to knowledge distillation (KD).
- Gradient-based AT and double backprop–style regularization improving thin NIN baselines; a flip-invariance regularizer performs strongly.
- On fine-tuning tasks (CUB, MIT Scenes), AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, sometimes outperforming KD.
- On ImageNet, AT from ResNet-34 to ResNet-18 yields +1.1% top-1, +0.8% top-5 without extensive tuning; KD is reported to underperform in this setting.
The method is simple to integrate, general across architectures, and the authors release code.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and bold idea: transferring “attention” as knowledge is a fresh and compelling direction beyond logits or full activations, with clear conceptual appeal and practical simplicity.
- Empirical evidence shows consistent, meaningful gains across datasets and architectures, including ImageNet, and demonstrates complementarity with KD.

3) Supporting arguments
- Conceptual novelty: Framing knowledge transfer as alignment of spatial focus distinguishes this paper from KD (label-space) and FitNets (hidden activation regression). The use of normalized, low-dimensional spatial summaries appears to be the right inductive bias for transfer.
- Practicality and generality: The AT loss is straightforward to add at a few layers, requires minimal extra compute for activation-based AT, and plays well with standard training. The approach is architecture-agnostic and can be combined with KD.
- Empirical breadth: The paper spans CIFAR-10 (various WRNs), fine-tuning on CUB and MIT Scenes, and ImageNet (ResNet-18/34). The reported improvements are consistent. Notably, AT > full activation transfer and AT+KD > KD in several cases. The fine-tuning results are particularly convincing for the “where to look” hypothesis in fine-grained tasks.
- Insightful ablations: The paper compares attention mapping functions (sum vs max, effect of p), shows the importance of normalization, and explores layer placement. It also examines gradient-based AT and flip-invariance regularization, which, while early-stage, broaden the scope and underscore the general idea.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
These points aim to strengthen the work; they are not all critical to the accept decision.
- Strengthen statistical reporting: Provide mean ± std over multiple runs for key results on CIFAR and ImageNet; report significance where possible. Some tables show single medians or single runs.
- KD baselines: The KD baselines on ImageNet are underwhelming and possibly under-tuned. Include a fair KD tuning sweep (temperature, α, label-smoothing, schedule) and a same-architecture KD baseline to isolate “architecture mismatch” effects.
- Compute/overhead: Quantify the training-time overhead for activation-based AT and especially for gradient-based AT (second backprop), and confirm the negligible test-time cost.
- Placement and scale: Provide a clearer recipe for selecting which layers to match and how to set β across layers; include a sensitivity analysis (e.g., 1 vs 2 vs all residual groups).
- Attention mapping choices: Explore l1 normalization, per-location vs per-channel normalization, and more systematically vary p in Fpsum. An intuitive rationale for why sum-based > max-based would be useful (e.g., robustness to channel redundancy).
- Gradient-based AT: Results are currently limited to a non-BN NIN on CIFAR with deterministic training. While acceptable for a first look, even a small BN-compatible demonstration would increase confidence and clarify how BN is handled in the second backprop.
- Claims: The statement that this is the first successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet should be tempered or backed by a thorough literature check/comparison.
- Writing and references: Clean up typos and reference formatting; a figure/table indexing pass would help readability. Include more qualitative visualizations showing student attention aligning with teacher after training.

Questions for the authors
- KD on ImageNet: What hyperparameter sweeps were attempted (temperature, α, schedules)? Can KD perform competitively when trained longer or with same architecture/depth?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are gains to β, attention layer placement, and choice of mapping function? Can you provide a small grid showing stability?
- Overheads: What are the exact training-time overheads for activation-based AT and gradient-based AT? Any observed wall-clock numbers?
- Generalization: Does AT improve robustness (e.g., to crops, flips beyond symmetry, minor corruptions) or calibration? Any preliminary evidence?
- BN in gradient AT: How do you handle BN during the second backprop? Have you tried freezing BN stats or a separate forward pass with fixed BN?
- Cross-architecture transfer: How does AT fare when teacher and student have very different topologies (e.g., Inception to ResNet) or dramatically different capacities?
- Beyond classification: Any preliminary results on detection/localization where spatial attention is arguably more central?

Overall, this is a fresh, impactful idea supported by solid initial evidence across multiple settings. Despite some incomplete validations, it opens a promising direction and is worth accepting.
