- Proposes Attention Transfer (AT) to transfer knowledge by aligning spatial attention maps between teacher and student CNNs.
- Defines two attention types: activation-based (channel-collapsed statistics: Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) and gradient-based (input-sensitivity gradients) with a symmetry regularizer.
- Uses a normalized attention-matching loss at multiple layers; emphasizes l2-normalization of attention vectors and compatibility with knowledge distillation (KD).
- Activation-based AT is lightweight, practical, and accompanied by open-source code.
- Empirically improves student performance across CIFAR-10 (ResNets, WRNs), transfer learning (CUB-200, MIT Scenes), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 +1.1% top-1).
- AT outperforms full activation matching (FitNets-style) and is complementary to KD on small-scale tasks.
- Gradient-based AT and the symmetry norm improve CIFAR-10 in settings without batch normalization, though activation-based AT remains strongest in presented experiments.
- Paper situates AT relative to interpretability (saliency, CAM) and KD/FitNets, arguing channel-collapsed spatial attention is compact and architecture-agnostic.
- Method descriptions are clear: definitions of attention maps, AT loss, normalization, multi-layer placement, and gradient-based double backprop derivation are well specified.
- Strong empirical ablations show importance of multiple AT losses, sum-based attention outperforming max-based, faster convergence, and complementarity with KD.
- Transfer-learning results show AT closes the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, especially on fine-grained tasks.
- ImageNet result is promising and demonstrates scalability but is limited in scope and tuning.
- Practicality and reproducibility are supported by code release and some hyperparameter discussion and multi-run statistics on CIFAR.
- ImageNet experiments lack breadth: only one teacher–student pair, two loss placements, untuned hyperparameters, and limited seeds.
- Gradient-based AT evaluation is limited by exclusion of batch normalization due to double backprop complications, restricting generality claims.
- Comparisons are incomplete: missing stronger/optimized FitNets baselines, Jacobian matching beyond input gradients, and other distillation variants on large-scale tasks.
- Analysis could be deeper: more systematic attention-accuracy correlations, negative controls with mismatched teachers, and quantitative diagnostics are missing.
- Writing and references need fixing: typos, formatting issues, and inconsistent references should be corrected.
- Questions for authors include sensitivity to layer selection and weights, effects of other normalizations, negative-control behavior with weaker/mismatched teachers, and computational overheads.
- Additional questions target gradient-based AT’s BN compatibility, class-conditional attention matching, stability/variance on ImageNet, KD tuning when combined with AT, and choice of p/max vs sum across datasets.
- Constructive suggestions: expand ImageNet experiments (placements, seeds, baselines), provide sensitivity studies for β/p/normalization, correlate attention similarity with accuracy, clarify implementation details, and quantify overhead.
