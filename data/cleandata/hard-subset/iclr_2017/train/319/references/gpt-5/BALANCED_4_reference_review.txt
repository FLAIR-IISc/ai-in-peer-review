Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple, general mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps at intermediate layers and encourage the student to match the teacher’s attention while training on the task loss.
- Two families of attention are considered: activation-based (channel-wise reductions of absolute activations, e.g., sum of |A|^p) and gradient-based (input gradients of the loss). The paper motivates these choices via the intuition that high activations/gradients indicate where the network “looks.”
- A practical loss is introduced that compares L2-normalized attention maps across multiple layers/groups, optionally combined with knowledge distillation (KD). The method is inexpensive for activation-based AT and can be implemented for gradient-based AT via double backprop.
- Empirically, activation-based AT provides consistent improvements on CIFAR-10/WRN and ResNet architectures, improves finetuning on CUB and MIT-Scenes, and yields +1.1% top-1 on ImageNet (ResNet-18 student with ResNet-34 teacher) with minimal tuning. Ablations show AT outperforms full activation regression (FitNets-style) and that sum-based attention variants perform best.
- The paper also explores gradient-based AT on CIFAR with a compact NIN model, shows that gradient regularizers (double backprop, flip invariance) help, and that gradient-based AT improves over baselines in this constrained setting.

Decision: Accept (weak)
Primary reasons:
1) The approach is simple, well-motivated, and broadly applicable; it delivers consistent, non-trivial gains across architectures and datasets, including a credible ImageNet improvement with minimal overhead for activation-based AT.
2) The paper provides solid empirical support with useful ablations (attention definitions, AT vs full activation transfer, placement of losses), and positions the idea clearly relative to KD and FitNets.

Supporting arguments
- Specific problem tackled: whether spatial attention (as defined from activations or gradients) can serve as a compact, semantically meaningful channel for teacher-to-student knowledge transfer. This is well-scoped and relevant to the community.
- Motivation and placement: The work bridges visualization/attention literature (saliency/CAM/Grad-CAM) with knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets). Using spatial attention as the transferred signal is novel and intuitively attractive: it emphasizes “where to look” rather than reproducing high-dimensional features or softened logits alone.
- Claims vs. evidence:
  • On CIFAR-10 with WRNs and ResNets, AT consistently improves over strong baselines; combining with KD helps further in many cases. Ablations across attention mappings (Fsum, F2sum, F4sum, Fmax) and across which layers to transfer add credibility.
  • AT vs full activation regression shows AT is both more effective and faster to converge, supporting the core claim that attention is a better transfer signal than raw features.
  • On transfer learning (CUB, MIT-Scenes), AT closes a substantial part of the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, and outperforms KD on CUB, aligning with intuition that mid-level attention is critical for fine-grained tasks.
  • On ImageNet, a +1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 with only two attention losses is promising and suggests scalability.
  • Gradient-based AT results are more limited but still indicate benefits, and the flip-invariance regularizer is an interesting, orthogonal contribution.

Caveats and weaknesses (did not prevent acceptance but temper enthusiasm):
- The definition of attention is heuristic and not uniquely justified; quantitative evidence that “better attention” correlates with better recognition is anecdotal (mostly visualizations). A localization or pointing-game metric would strengthen this.
- Gradient-based AT evaluation is narrow (no BN, single-run, small model) and leaves open questions about practicality and fairness vs stronger baselines. Its compute overhead and stability with BN are not fully addressed.
- Some comparisons may be under-tuned: KD on ImageNet is reported to underperform, but hyperparameters might be suboptimal. A tuned KD baseline (and FitNets-style matching) on the same setups would clarify relative merits.
- Statistical rigor is mixed: some results use medians over 5 runs; others are single runs. Confidence intervals or multiple seeds would be helpful for key tables.
- Clarity/presentation: references and figure descriptions have formatting issues; some implementation choices (β scheduling, exact layer selection) are dispersed or informal.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not necessarily decisive for the score)
- Provide quantitative evidence linking attention quality to task performance (e.g., pointing game, weakly supervised localization) to support the “attention contains useful information” hypothesis.
- Strengthen baselines:
  • Tune KD and FitNets-style feature regression carefully on ImageNet/CIFAR with comparable compute and schedules.
  • Include CIFAR-100 and, if feasible, Tiny-ImageNet to test generalization across label cardinality.
- Expand ablations:
  • Sensitivity to p in Fsum^p, number and placement of attention losses, and β schedules. Report training stability and compute overhead.
  • Class-conditional attention transfer (e.g., Grad-CAM/score-specific maps) vs class-agnostic maps.
- Clarify gradient-based AT:
  • Interaction with batch normalization in double backprop; memory/compute overhead; stability tricks.
  • Compare gradient-based AT to other gradient penalties (Sobolev/TV norms) and to Jacobian matching methods.
- Reporting: provide mean±std over seeds for key results; detail hyperparameters used for each baseline; include training time multipliers.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are gains to the choice of layers for AT and to β scheduling? Could you provide a small grid/ablation on ImageNet?
2) Can you provide tuned KD and FitNets baselines on ImageNet and CUB with comparable training budgets? Does the observed KD underperformance persist?
3) Have you evaluated attention transfer with class-conditional maps (e.g., Grad-CAM for the ground-truth class) versus the proposed class-agnostic activation maps?
4) Do improvements persist on CIFAR-100 and other datasets with many classes? Any results on Tiny-ImageNet?
5) For gradient-based AT: how does BN affect the second backprop in practice? Can you share implementation details and overhead (memory/time) relative to standard training?
6) Can you quantify the correlation between attention similarity (student-teacher) and accuracy improvements, rather than relying on qualitative visualizations?
7) Are there failure modes where AT hurts performance (e.g., teacher’s attention is mislocalized or brittle)? Any robustness tests under distribution shifts or occlusions?

Overall, this is a simple, well-motivated idea with promising and practical empirical gains. Despite some missing comparisons and limited analysis for gradient-based AT, the contribution is likely to be impactful and useful to practitioners.
