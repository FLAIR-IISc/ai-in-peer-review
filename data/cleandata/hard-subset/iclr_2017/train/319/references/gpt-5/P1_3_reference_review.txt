1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple, general mechanism for transferring knowledge from a teacher CNN to a student CNN by aligning their spatial attention. Two attention families are defined:
- Activation-based attention: spatial maps computed from activation tensors via simple channel-wise statistics (e.g., sum of absolute activations, or sum/max of absolute activations to a power p).
- Gradient-based attention: input-gradient maps (saliency) derived from the loss, optionally with symmetry constraints via flip-invariance.

The method adds losses that match l2-normalized attention maps at one or more corresponding layers of teacher and student (with optional interpolation if resolutions differ). AT can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD) with negligible extra cost. Experiments on CIFAR-10 for WRNs and NIN, transfer learning to CUB-200 and MIT Scenes with ResNet-18/34, and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher) show consistent gains. Ablations suggest that activation-based AT outperforms transferring full activations (FitNets-style), that sum-based attention works better than max-based, that multi-layer losses help, and that normalization is important. Preliminary gradient-based AT also improves performance and relates to double backprop regularization.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated idea that uses spatial attention as a compact, architecture-agnostic transfer signal.
- Strong empirical evidence across multiple architectures/datasets (including ImageNet) showing consistent, practically relevant gains, with ablations supporting design choices.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper is well-placed in the lines of attention visualization (Grad-CAM, saliency, deconvnet) and knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets). The intuition—students benefit from learning “where to look”—is compelling and bridges interpretability and distillation.
- Methodological clarity: The activation-based attention functions are clearly defined, the normalization choice is explicit, and loss placement across residual groups is described for both same-depth and different-depth scenarios. The gradient-based formulation and its relation to double backprop are clearly laid out, including the need for a second backward pass and discussion of BN challenges.
- Empirical support:
  - CIFAR-10: Across WRN teacher/student pairs, AT yields consistent and non-trivial error reductions, further improved with KD. Ablations show: (a) aligning multiple layers helps; (b) sum-based attention variants are robust; (c) AT > full-activation transfer in final accuracy despite both speeding convergence.
  - Gradient-based AT: Even with a constrained setup (no BN, small NIN), double backprop, symmetry regularization, and grad-based AT all reduce error, with activation-based AT performing best in that setting.
  - Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, in some cases surpassing KD. This supports the claim that intermediate attentions are particularly valuable for fine-grained tasks.
  - ImageNet: With minimal tuning and only two attention losses, ResNet-18 improves by 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 over baseline, demonstrating scalability and practical impact where prior distillation approaches often struggle.
- Practicality: For activation-based AT, overhead is small (teacher attention maps come “for free” in forward pass). Code is promised, aiding reproducibility.

4) Suggestions to improve (not strictly part of decision)
- Reporting and rigor:
  - Provide full tables for all experiments (some are summarized in text), report means and standard deviations over multiple runs for key results (beyond the few medians reported), and include statistical significance where possible.
  - Expand baselines: in addition to KD and FitNets-style activation regression, consider other contemporary transfer objectives (e.g., logit matching with temperature sweeps, within-layer hinting variants).
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity:
  - Detail β schedules, p choices, normalization ablations (l1 vs l2), and layer selection strategies. Provide sensitivity analyses to guide practitioners.
  - For ImageNet, include results with attention losses on all residual groups if possible, and a modest hyperparameter sweep to strengthen the claim of robustness.
- Gradient-based AT:
  - Clarify the treatment of batch normalization in the second backward pass and explore a BN-compatible implementation; provide results beyond a single-run setup.
  - Report computational overhead for double backprop and grad-AT vs. activation-AT.
- Fairness and setup:
  - Ensure teacher/student use comparable augmentations and training protocols; explicitly report training time and compute costs with and without AT.
- Claims and positioning:
  - The claim of being the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet should be carefully qualified and supported by citations or toned down.
- Writing and references:
  - Clean up typos and inconsistent reference formatting; improve figure/table cross-referencing and ensure all referenced tables/figures are present.

Questions for the authors
- Could you provide per-run means/standard deviations for the main CIFAR and ImageNet results, and learning curves to quantify convergence speed differences more rigorously?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of attention function (Fsum vs Fpsum with p>1) and to attention normalization (l1 vs l2)? Any guidance for selecting p and β across datasets?
- How do you select which layers/groups to align in practice? Have you tried attention transfer at early layers only vs mid vs late, and do some layers dominate the gains?
- For ImageNet, what is the training time overhead of AT relative to baseline and to KD? Does AT change the optimal learning-rate schedule?
- Can you expand the comparison to FitNets on ImageNet (or explain constraints), and to other distillation variants (e.g., logit + intermediate hint combinations)?
- For gradient-based AT with BN: can you clarify whether you fixed BN stats/parameters during the second backward pass, or what strategy you recommend? Any initial results with BN-compatible implementations?
- Beyond classification, have you tried AT in detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization, where spatial attention might be even more critical?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, interpretable, and effective idea with solid empirical backing and useful ablations. With minor improvements in rigor and clarity, it should be a strong contribution.
