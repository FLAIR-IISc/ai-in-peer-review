Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism from a teacher CNN to a student. It defines spatial attention maps at multiple layers in two ways: (i) activation-based maps via simple channel-wise reductions (sum or max over |A|^p), and (ii) gradient-based maps via input gradients. The student is trained to match the teacher’s attention maps (after l2 normalization) via auxiliary losses in addition to the standard task loss; AT can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). The authors provide empirical evidence on CIFAR-10, CUB-200-2011, MIT Scenes, and ImageNet, showing consistent gains over baselines, that activation-based AT outperforms full activation matching (FitNets-style), and that simple gradient-regularization variants (double backprop and flip-symmetry) can also help.

Decision: Weak Reject
Key reasons: (1) While the activation-based AT idea is simple and potentially useful, the empirical support is uneven and not sufficiently rigorous across settings (limited multi-run statistics, inconsistent training protocols, and incomplete baselines, especially on large-scale ImageNet). (2) The gradient-based AT component is not convincingly validated (only on a restricted setup without batch norm, single runs, and with acknowledged uncertainty about BN handling), yet it is a central advertised contribution.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses whether “attention” from a teacher can guide a student to focus on salient spatial regions and improve performance. This is reasonably motivated and positioned relative to KD and FitNets; the notion of attention maps via activation statistics or input gradients follows prior visualization work. However, the definition of attention is largely heuristic (e.g., |A|^p reductions) and the paper does not deeply justify why these particular reductions are optimal vs other spatial summaries or regularizers.

- Methodological soundness:
  • Activation-based AT is clearly specified (choice of Fsum/Fpsum/Fpmax, normalization, placement of losses). The normalization step is a sensible design choice.
  • Gradient-based AT requires double backprop and raises important implementation questions, especially with batch normalization. The paper explicitly avoids BN in these experiments and leaves the BN treatment unresolved, which undermines the generality of this contribution.
  • Fairness of comparisons is a concern. In several places, training protocols differ across methods (e.g., toggling weight decay, augmentation choices), and many results are single runs. This makes it hard to ascribe gains to AT rather than training variance or regularization differences.

- Empirical evidence:
  • CIFAR: Activation-based AT provides consistent improvements for multiple teacher–student pairs and shows that sum-based maps outperform max-based ones; combining with KD helps. There is a useful ablation on number/location of AT losses. However, statistical rigor is limited: medians over 5 runs are reported only for a subset; other results lack variance/CI. FitNets-style full activation matching is included but not exhaustively tuned.
  • Gradient-based: The best-performing “gradient attention” variant is actually a symmetry regularizer; double backprop helps; student–teacher gradient AT helps modestly. But these are on a thin NIN without BN, single runs, and with altered architectures. As presented, the gradient-based AT story is interesting but preliminary.
  • Transfer learning (CUB/Scenes): AT reduces the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. The result is promising, especially on CUB. However, KD is not always tuned fairly (authors note KD can be sensitive), and there are many degrees of freedom (which blocks are frozen, BN behavior) that could confound conclusions.
  • ImageNet: A 1.1% top-1 gain for ResNet-18 using ResNet-34 attention is encouraging, but only two AT losses were used and no hyperparameter tuning was done; there are no error bars, and KD was reported to “struggle” with the same CIFAR hyperparameters. The claim that this is the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet seems premature without stronger baselines and training-time-matched KD/activation regression setups.

- Relation to literature: The work is connected to visualization (saliency, CAM, Grad-CAM) and distillation/transfer (KD, FitNets). However, the paper could better articulate how AT differs from and subsumes “hint” losses beyond being a spatially aggregated target, and compare to other spatial/statistical feature matching (e.g., matching feature norms or Gram/statistics at spatial level). The paper’s claim that AT is superior to full activation matching is plausible but needs stronger, more controlled evidence across datasets.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Provide multi-seed mean±std for all reported numbers, especially on CIFAR and ImageNet. Report training budgets and ensure equal data augmentation, regularization, and early stopping criteria across methods.
- Strengthen baselines: (i) Carefully tuned KD on ImageNet with temperature and α sweeps; (ii) FitNets-style hints with 1×1 adapters and schedules; (iii) simple spatial regularizers (e.g., match per-location feature l2-norms, Gram/statistics) to test if gains stem from spatial norm alignment rather than “attention” per se.
- Clarify computational cost: training-time overhead (extra forward/backward, memory), and inference-time cost (should be zero); quantify wall-clock multipliers.
- Sensitivity analyses: β schedule, choice of p in |A|^p, layer selection and number of transfer points, attention map interpolation strategy, and normalization type (l1 vs l2).
- Gradient-based AT: Resolve BN handling in double backprop and demonstrate on modern BN-equipped architectures. Otherwise, present this as a preliminary exploration and focus the paper on activation-based AT.
- Improve clarity and consistency: fix reference formatting; detail data augmentation and freezing policies; provide more quantitative ImageNet results (e.g., top-1/top-5 over epochs, vs KD/activation regression) and possibly more AT loss placements.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is activation-based AT to β, p, and the number/placement of AT losses? Can you provide a small grid with mean±std?
- Can you standardize training protocols and report multiple seeds for all methods, including KD and FitNets-style baselines?
- On ImageNet, what is the training-time overhead of AT? Could you report results with AT losses on all residual groups and a tuned β schedule? How do results compare to a tuned KD?
- How does AT perform when teacher and student have different architectures (e.g., ResNet → MobileNet) and different capacities?
- Can you include a baseline that matches per-location feature norms or Gram statistics without absolute-value or power transforms, to isolate whether “attention” vs simple spatial norm alignment drives the gains?
- For gradient-based AT, how do you handle BN in double backprop? Can you demonstrate results with BN and provide multi-run statistics, or otherwise narrow the claim?
