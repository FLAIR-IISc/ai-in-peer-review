Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT): matching spatial attention maps between a teacher and a student to improve the student’s performance. Two attention definitions are explored: (i) activation-based maps obtained by channel-wise statistics (sum or max) over absolute activations, and (ii) gradient-based maps defined as sensitivity of the loss w.r.t. input. For activation-based AT, the loss penalizes the l2 distance between l2-normalized teacher/student attention maps at selected layers. For gradient-based AT, the loss penalizes the l2 distance between input gradients, requiring double backpropagation; a symmetry regularizer (flip invariance) is also proposed.
- Empirical evidence is presented on CIFAR-10 with WRNs/NIN, and on CUB, MIT Indoor, and ImageNet with ResNet-18/34. Activation-based AT consistently improves over baseline students and outperforms full activation regression; it can be combined with KD. Gradient-based AT shows gains in a limited NIN/CIFAR setting.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical evaluation lacks rigor and completeness (insufficient baselines, limited runs, weak tuning protocol, and incomplete reporting), undermining the strength of the claims, especially on ImageNet and for gradient-based AT.
2) The paper suffers from substantial clarity and presentation issues (notation, hyperparameter specification, training details, references/formatting), and several claims are insufficiently supported or overstated.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The idea of transferring spatial “where” information is intuitive and well-motivated relative to FitNets/KD and saliency/CAM/Grad-CAM literature. However, novelty is incremental: the activation maps are simple channel-wise reductions with absolute values; the loss reduces to cosine-like similarity between normalized maps. The gradient-based AT is conceptually related to double backprop and sensitivity regularization.
- Rigor of experiments:
  - CIFAR: Improvements are shown, but reporting is inconsistent. In some places you report medians of 5 runs (Table 2), elsewhere single runs (gradient-based section) with fixed seed. No standard deviations/confidence intervals. ZCA and other augmentations are modified ad hoc relative to prior WRN baselines, complicating comparisons. β is chosen via a heuristic scaling (10^3 divided by map size and batch size) and then decayed when combined with KD; no tuning curves or sensitivity analyses are provided. KD temperatures and α are fixed without thorough tuning for each teacher–student pair, yet conclusions are drawn about KD vs AT.
  - Gradient-based AT: Restricted to a fragile setting (NIN without BN, no random crops, turn off weight decay) due to ambiguity of BN under double backprop. Results are single-run; architectural mismatch exists between teacher and student; the best numbers are actually from activation-based AT, not gradient-based AT. Thus, support for gradient-based AT is weak and non-generalizable.
  - Transfer learning (CUB/Scenes): The procedure (freezing BN and first block, attention losses on last two groups) makes sense, and qualitative statements are promising, but quantitative results in the provided text are sparse (exact top-1 improvements on CUB/Scenes are not listed here; Figure/Table references are incomplete).
  - ImageNet: The stated 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 with AT (teacher ResNet-34) is compelling, but hyperparameters were not tuned; losses applied only to last two groups; no multiple runs; training/compute details (epochs, LR schedule, optimizer, augmentation, label smoothing, weight decay) are missing; and no direct comparison against properly tuned KD under fair settings is provided. The claim that this is the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet is not substantiated (and may depend on tuning).
- Methodological details:
  - Normalization and loss: Eq. (2) uses l2-normalized maps and l2 distance; this is essentially equivalent to maximizing cosine similarity. This insight is not discussed; alternative divergences (cosine loss, KL over softmaxed attention) and the effect of normalization choice (l1 vs l2) are not ablated.
  - Choice of attention maps: The absolute value discards sign information; the rationale is not fully justified, especially for pre-activation ResNets and networks with BN/ReLU patterns. Max-based reductions underperform; why?
  - Placement of losses: The selection of layers (per-block vs per-group) is discussed but not systematically ablated across architectures/datasets. Interpolation details for mismatched resolutions are glossed over.
  - Computational overhead: Activation-based AT is cheap, but gradient-based AT effectively doubles backward passes and increases memory; overhead is not quantified.
- Presentation and formatting:
  - Numerous typos/grammar issues (e.g., “Bolzmann,” “traning,” “parallelizm”), inconsistent capitalization, and awkward phrasing.
  - References contain formatting artifacts and missing venues/years (e.g., \Q2014\E, dangling commas, inconsistent styles).
  - Figures/tables are referenced but not consistently presented in the text provided; some claims rely on unseen figures (e.g., qualitative attention maps, convergence plots).
  - Notation ambiguities (e.g., division by vector norms inline, p-norm for difference but fixed p=2, “we set β to 10^3 divided by number of elements...”). 

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen the empirical section: report mean±std over multiple runs; standardize data augmentation and baselines; provide full training details (optimizer, LR schedule, epochs, weight decay, label smoothing, batch size, BN handling).
- Fair KD comparison: thoroughly tune KD (temperature, α) per setting; include FitNets-style hints with matched capacity and report results on ImageNet if claiming KD fails.
- Ablations: β sensitivity; normalization (l1 vs l2 vs none); distance (cosine vs l2 on normalized maps vs KL over spatial softmax); p in Fpsum; per-layer loss placement; effect of interpolating attention between resolutions.
- Gradient-based AT: resolve BN behavior under double backprop (e.g., frozen BN vs recalculated statistics) and evaluate on modern BN-equipped architectures; quantify compute/memory overhead.
- Clarify the theory: discuss why absolute activations are a good proxy for importance; relate the l2-normalized l2 loss to cosine similarity; analyze when sum vs max reductions are preferable.
- Clean up writing, figures, and references; ensure all claims are supported with visible tables/plots.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to β, normalization type (l1 vs l2), and the choice of distance (cosine vs l2 on normalized maps)? Did you try spatial softmax + KL divergence?
2) Why absolute activations? Did you consider signed channel aggregations, learned channel weights, or CAM/Grad-CAM-derived attention as targets?
3) Can you provide multi-run statistics for CIFAR, CUB/Scenes, and ImageNet, along with full training details and compute/memory overhead for AT (activation-based) and AT (gradient-based)?
4) For KD on ImageNet, what temperatures/α, label smoothing, and schedules were tried? Could poorly tuned KD explain its underperformance?
5) How does layer selection affect performance? Do earlier-layer attention losses help or hurt on ImageNet?
6) For gradient-based AT with BN, what is the correct and practical treatment? Can you demonstrate it on a BN-equipped WRN/ResNet with robust gains?
7) Does AT improve calibration or robustness (e.g., to corruptions, occlusions), beyond top-1 accuracy?
