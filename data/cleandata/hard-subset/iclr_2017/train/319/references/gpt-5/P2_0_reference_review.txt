1. Summary of contributions
- Problem: The paper asks whether “attention” information from a trained CNN can be used to improve a student CNN. It defines spatial attention maps at multiple layers and proposes transferring them from teacher to student.
- Methods:
  - Activation-based attention: aggregate channel activations at each spatial location (e.g., sum of |A|^p, max of |A|^p), L2-normalize the resulting map, and penalize the Lp distance between teacher and student attention maps across one or more layers.
  - Gradient-based attention: use input gradients as attention maps and minimize the distance between student and teacher gradients via double backprop; also propose a flip-invariance regularizer on gradient attention.
- Claims: (i) attention transfer (AT) improves performance across architectures and datasets; (ii) activation-based AT is more effective than transferring full activations; (iii) AT complements knowledge distillation (KD) in some settings; (iv) AT scales to ImageNet and fine-grained transfer learning.
- Evidence: Improvements on CIFAR-10 using WRN/ResNet, ablations over attention mappings and layer placement, comparisons against KD and full activation transfer; gradient-based AT and symmetry regularization on CIFAR with NIN; ImageNet ResNet-18 gains using ResNet-34 teacher; transfer-learning gains on CUB and MIT Scenes. Code is available.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The approach is simple, general, and well-motivated, with clear connections to both attention visualization and knowledge transfer.
- Empirical support is strong for activation-based AT: consistent gains across architectures/datasets, useful ablations, and non-trivial improvements on ImageNet and fine-grained transfer.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses how to define attention for CNNs and whether aligning student attention to teacher attention improves learning. This is clearly articulated and practically relevant.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The work bridges visualization/attention (saliency, CAM/Grad-CAM, deconv, guided backprop) and distillation/hints (KD, FitNets). The motivation for using spatial attention (capturing “where” information instead of full feature tensors) is persuasive and supported by qualitative observations and the normalization-based loss design. The related work is mostly adequate, though references are unevenly formatted and some prior distillation variants are not discussed in depth.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - Activation-based AT improves performance: Tables on CIFAR show consistent error reductions across teacher/student pairs (e.g., WRN-16-2→WRN-16-1, WRN-40-2→WRN-16-2). Layer-wise placement ablation shows each group’s loss helps, and combined losses help slightly more. On ImageNet, ResNet-18 gains +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 using AT with two losses, which is meaningful given limited tuning. Transfer learning to CUB/Scenes shows AT closes much of the ResNet-18 vs. ResNet-34 gap, especially on CUB where spatial attention plausibly matters more; qualitative attention maps also align better after AT.
  - AT vs. full activation transfer: The FitNets-style activation regression yields minimal improvements under comparable settings, while AT yields larger gains and faster convergence. This directly supports the claim that spatial attention is a more transferable/useful signal than full activations.
  - KD vs. AT and complementarity: On CIFAR, KD helps and AT+KD can further improve results; on ImageNet, KD under the given hyperparameters degraded convergence, whereas AT helped, suggesting AT is a robust alternative when KD struggles with depth/architecture mismatch.
  - Gradient-based AT: The CIFAR NIN experiments show that (i) double backprop (min-l2) and (ii) flip-invariant gradient regularization both improve generalization; (iii) gradient-based AT also helps; (iv) in this setting, activation-based AT performs best. This supports the viability of gradient-based attention, though the evaluation is narrower and uses single runs without BN, leaving this part less conclusive than activation-based AT.
- Rigor and correctness: The core loss formulations are standard and sound. The normalization of attention maps is a reasonable and important design (noted as critical, though a direct ablation would strengthen the case). Empirical methodology is generally solid with sensible baselines and ablations. Some results are medians over multiple runs; others are single-run, especially for gradient-based AT, which weakens statistical confidence there. ImageNet experiments are limited (two loss sites, minimal tuning) but still show notable gains.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Statistical reporting: Provide means and standard deviations over multiple seeds for all CIFAR experiments (including gradient-based AT), and validate ImageNet gains over at least 2–3 runs if feasible.
- Compute/overhead: Quantify the training time and memory overhead of AT, particularly for gradient-based AT (double backprop), and the runtime cost of computing teacher attention during student training.
- Normalization and β sensitivity: Include ablations showing the effect of attention-map normalization (none vs. L1 vs. L2) and the sensitivity to β (including the described scaling by map size and batch size), and learning-rate schedules.
- Layer selection: Explore attention losses at all residual groups on ImageNet and report the effect of layer placement density and which layers contribute most.
- KD interplay: For ImageNet, try a brief hyperparameter sweep for KD (temperature, blending α) and report if KD can be made to help alongside AT or if AT is consistently preferable.
- Broader tasks: Since spatial attention is central, evaluate on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization to test whether AT improves spatial reasoning tasks.
- Gradient-based AT with BN: Clarify and experiment with gradient AT in BN models (e.g., fixed BN stats in the second pass vs. full recomputation), as the paper notes this is unresolved.
- Terminology: Be cautious in equating channel-aggregated activations with “attention”; this is a pragmatic proxy. Minor: clean up references and figure/table presentation.

5. Questions for the authors
- How critical is the L2 normalization of attention maps? Can you provide an ablation quantifying its impact vs. no normalization and L1 normalization?
- How sensitive are results to β and to the choice of p in Fpsum? Is there a principled way to set β across layers beyond scaling by map size?
- What is the training-time increase for activation-based AT and for gradient-based AT (double backprop), and what is the memory footprint during student training when computing teacher attentions?
- For ImageNet, do AT gains persist when losses are added at all residual groups? Are the gains additive with label smoothing or other regularizers?
- Can KD be tuned to help on ImageNet in your setup, and if not, do you have hypotheses beyond depth/architecture mismatch?
- How robust are the gains across random seeds, data augmentation strength, and training schedules?
- Have you tried cross-architecture transfer (e.g., Inception teacher to ResNet student) and cross-capacity transfer (teacher only slightly better than student)? Do AT gains persist?
