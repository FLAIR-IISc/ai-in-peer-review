Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a form of knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. Attention is defined as spatial maps derived from intermediate feature activations (activation-based) or from gradients w.r.t. input (gradient-based).
- For activation-based AT, the authors aggregate channel-wise activations via simple statistics (sum of absolute values, power-sum, or max-p) to obtain spatial attention maps and add l2 losses aligning student and teacher attention (with l2-normalization of maps).
- For gradient-based AT, the student is trained to match the teacher’s input gradient maps using a double-backprop-like objective; a flip-invariance “symmetry norm” is also proposed.
- Empirical results suggest AT improves over baselines on CIFAR-10, and activation-based AT provides additional gains when combined with knowledge distillation (KD). On CUB and MIT Scenes (finetuning), AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. On ImageNet, ResNet-18 reportedly gains +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 vs. baseline when using a ResNet-34 teacher. The authors claim activation-based AT outperforms direct full-activation transfer.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
1) Insufficient experimental rigor and clarity, especially for the gradient-based method, and incomplete/tuned-inconsistent baselines (e.g., KD) make it hard to confidently attribute gains to the proposed mechanisms.
2) Numerous presentation and formatting issues (references, typos, missing/unclear figures/tables) and several unsubstantiated or overstated claims (e.g., first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet) undermine confidence.

Supporting arguments
- Methodological clarity and completeness:
  - Activation-based AT is clearly specified (Eq. 2) with sensible normalization; however, the role of normalization is asserted as “important” without systematic ablations (e.g., compare l1 vs l2 normalization, or no normalization). The effect of the exponent p is only lightly explored; F2sum is used “for simplicity” rather than based on a clear empirical optimum.
  - The placement of AT losses (per block vs per group) is underexplored; only a single WRN case shows minor differences. Generalization across architectures and depths is not systematically studied.
  - Teacher parameters appear fixed but this is not explicitly stated; if not, gradient flow into the teacher would change the problem. Please clarify.
  - Gradient-based AT requires second-order mixed partials. The authors explicitly avoid batch normalization and complex architectures, hence the gradient-based method is validated only on a thin NIN without BN and without standard augmentations. This severely limits external validity. The interaction with BN is raised as an open problem; nevertheless, the paper claims benefit of gradient-based AT without resolving this key practicality.
  - The symmetry regularizer (Eq. 6) is interesting, but its effectiveness is demonstrated in the same constrained NIN setup only; no ablation vs stronger data augmentation or group-equivariant architectures.

- Experimental rigor and baselines:
  - Many choices differ across experiments (BN on vs off, augmentations inconsistent, weight decay toggled off “because the network struggles to fit,” ZCA omitted, different architectures for teacher/student in gradient-based section). These confounders make comparisons difficult and gains ambiguous.
  - Reporting is inconsistent: some results are medians over 5 runs (Table 2), others appear single runs (Table 3). No confidence intervals or standard deviations are provided, especially for ImageNet and transfer learning where variability can be large.
  - KD baselines: The paper notes KD “hurts convergence” on ImageNet with these hyperparameters, and no tuning is attempted due to resource constraints. It is difficult to conclude AT is superior when baselines are not tuned comparably. Prior work often finds KD sensitive to temperature/alpha; fair tuning is needed. Similarly, FitNets-style baselines are only briefly attempted; the 1×1 alignment and normalization choices may not be optimal.
  - ImageNet: The +1.1% top-1 gain for ResNet-18 with only two AT losses and “no hyperparameter tuning” is promising, but is reported without multiple seeds, confidence intervals, or stronger baselines (e.g., tuned KD, feature-hint baselines, or other transfer methods). The claim that this is “the first” successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet is not substantiated and seems overstated.
  - Compute/efficiency: AT is stated to add “very little” computation (activation-based), but no quantitative overhead is provided (memory/time). For gradient-based AT, the (approx.) 2× backprop cost is noted but not benchmarked.

- Novelty and placement:
  - Using spatial attention maps as knowledge to transfer is a reasonable idea linking visualization and distillation. Relative to FitNets (hints) and KD, the novelty is moderate but meaningful.
  - Related work coverage is broad, but several references are misformatted; also, comparisons to alternative spatial normalization/attention transfer strategies (e.g., class-conditional attention like CAM/Grad-CAM) are not quantitatively explored.

- Presentation issues:
  - Numerous typos and stylistic issues (e.g., “Restricted Bolzmann,” “traning,” “one the best,” capitalization inconsistencies).
  - References are heavily misformatted with artifacts (\Q2016\E, question marks, etc.) and incomplete venue details. This is below conference standards.
  - Several figures/tables referenced in text are missing or unclear in the provided draft (e.g., Table 1, 5; Fig. 1, 3–7; Appendix images appear garbled). This prevents verifying claims (e.g., faster convergence plots).

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide comprehensive, controlled experiments:
  - Standardize training protocols across methods (augmentations, BN, weight decay) and report results with multiple seeds and error bars.
  - Thoroughly tune KD and FitNets baselines; include other strong modern baselines where possible.
  - On ImageNet, provide multiple runs, report training curves, compute/memory overhead, and compare more loss placements (all four groups vs two).
  - For gradient-based AT, resolve BN interaction and evaluate on modern BN-enabled architectures; quantify the extra compute.
- Ablate design choices:
  - Normalization type (none vs l1 vs l2), exponent p, sum vs max, layer selection and number of AT losses, β scheduling, attention interpolation strategies.
  - Examine cross-architecture transfer (e.g., Inception/WRN teacher to ResNet student).
- Clarify and temper claims:
  - Avoid “first on ImageNet” unless supported by exhaustive literature review and stronger baselines.
  - Clearly state whether the teacher is frozen; specify all hyperparameters and schedules.
- Improve writing/formatting:
  - Fix references and citations; correct typos; ensure all figures/tables are present, legible, and self-contained.

Questions for the authors
1) Is the teacher always frozen during AT? If not, how is stability ensured, and do gradients flow into the teacher?
2) How sensitive is performance to the l2-normalization of attention maps? Can you provide ablations comparing none/l1/l2 normalizations and discuss the impact on stability?
3) How does the choice of p (in Fpsum/Fpmax) affect results across datasets/architectures? Why was F2sum selected beyond “simplicity”?
4) What is the exact compute and memory overhead of activation-based AT per training step (vs baseline), and for gradient-based AT (double backprop)?
5) Can you provide tuned KD baselines on ImageNet (varying temperature and alpha) and report whether AT still outperforms?
6) For gradient-based AT with BN, what behavior did you observe when computing second derivatives? Did you try freezing BN stats/parameters during the second pass?
7) How robust are ImageNet gains across seeds and different teacher capacities (e.g., ResNet-50 teacher)? Do more AT losses (all groups) yield further improvements?
8) Did you evaluate cross-architecture transfer and class-conditional attention (e.g., Grad-CAM-based AT)? If so, how do results compare?
9) For transfer learning (CUB/Scenes), can you report per-class improvements and whether AT primarily helps fine-grained localization-sensitive categories?
