Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a form of knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. Attention is operationalized as spatial maps derived either from intermediate activations (activation-based AT) or gradients w.r.t. the input (gradient-based AT). For activation-based AT, the authors define channel-aggregation functions over absolute activations (sum, powered sum, max) to produce per-layer 2D attention maps and align teacher and student maps via a normalized l2 loss inserted at matched stages (e.g., after residual groups). For gradient-based AT, the authors align input sensitivity maps via a double-backprop–like objective and also explore a flip-symmetry regularizer for attention. Empirically, activation-based AT yields consistent gains across CIFAR-10, fine-grained (CUB), scene classification (MIT Scenes), and ImageNet, often complementing knowledge distillation (KD) and outperforming full activation regression. The reported improvements include ~0.8–1.1% top-1 on ImageNet when transferring from ResNet-34 to ResNet-18 and significant gains on CIFAR with Wide ResNets. The approach is simple, architecture-agnostic, inexpensive (for activation-based AT), and compatible with KD.

Decision: Accept (clear, simple idea with broad applicability and strong empirical support)
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and practicality: treating spatial attention as a transferable supervision signal is simple, well-motivated, and easy to add to existing pipelines with negligible overhead (for activation-based AT).
- Strong, broad empirical evidence: consistent improvements across architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN), dataset scales (CIFAR to ImageNet), and tasks (fine-grained, scenes). Activation-based AT beats full activation regression and often synergizes with KD.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the gap between feature-level transfer (FitNets, KD) and interpretability methods by using attention maps as a mid-level, task-relevant supervisory signal. The hypothesis—that spatial energy/localization learned by a strong model is informative and easier to mimic than full features—is compelling and borne out in experiments.
- Methodological soundness: The activation-based maps are well-defined and tied to known visualization tools; the normalization of attention vectors before alignment is a sensible design detail and empirically important. Placing losses at multiple depths and normalizing for map magnitude reduce brittleness.
- Comparative baselines: The authors compare AT to KD, to full activation regression (FitNets-style), to double backprop (for gradient-based AT), and show complementary behavior with KD on CIFAR and wins over full activation transfer. The observation that powered sums outperform max and that multi-layer alignment helps is valuable guidance.
- Significance: The ImageNet result (ResNet-34→ResNet-18 +1.1% top-1) is practically meaningful, especially given minimal tuning and few loss insertion points. Demonstrating knowledge transfer that reliably helps on ImageNet is non-trivial.

Limitations and concerns
- Gradient-based AT is underdeveloped: Limited to NIN on CIFAR without batch normalization, single-run results, and notable engineering caveats (BN behavior during second derivative, computational cost). While interesting, this line feels preliminary and less convincing than activation-based AT.
- Experimental rigor on ImageNet: Only a single teacher-student pair (ResNet-34→ResNet-18), only two insertion points, minimal hyperparameter search, and no variance estimates. The claim of being the “first successful” transfer on ImageNet should be toned down or better substantiated.
- Ablations: While some ablations are provided (mapping choices, number of insertion points), more systematic sensitivity analyses would help: effect of β across layers; p in powered sums; normalization choice (l1 vs l2 vs cosine); resolution matching strategy (interpolation effects); and the impact on calibration or robustness.
- Fairness of KD comparisons: The KD setups that fail on ImageNet may be sensitive to temperature/α and loss placement. A stronger KD baseline (e.g., logit matching with tuned temperature, longer schedule) would strengthen the claim that AT succeeds where KD struggles.
- Theory and failure modes: The paper is primarily empirical. Discussion of when AT could hurt (e.g., teacher focusing on spurious background features, domain shift) and diagnostics for harmful attention transfer are missing.

Suggestions for improvement (not decisive for acceptance)
- Gradient-based AT:
  - Clarify and standardize BN handling in the second backprop. Report compute/memory overhead and wall-clock training multipliers. Provide multi-run statistics and stronger baselines (with/without BN) on CIFAR100.
  - Explore attention alignment at intermediate feature layers (gradients w.r.t. feature maps) to reduce dimensionality and BN complications.
- Activation-based AT:
  - Include more ablations: layer-wise β scheduling, cosine similarity loss vs l2, l1 normalization, and impact of attention interpolation method.
  - Compare to class-aware attention (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM-based maps) as transfer signals, particularly on fine-grained tasks where class-discriminativeness may matter.
  - Report calibration (ECE) and robustness (e.g., corruption benchmarks) to assess whether AT changes what evidence the student relies on.
  - Extend ImageNet experiments: add more insertion points, try other teacher/student pairs (e.g., ResNet-50→ResNet-18; cross-architecture), and provide variance across seeds.
- Related work positioning:
  - Discuss contemporaneous representation/attention matching approaches and differences (e.g., FitNets hints, KD variants). Calibrate the ImageNet novelty claim cautiously.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Clean up references and figure cross-references. Provide more qualitative visualizations comparing teacher/student attention before vs after AT.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to the normalization choice and the loss form (cosine similarity vs l2 on normalized maps)? Did you try per-layer temperature/β schedules?
2. For ImageNet, how many runs and what variance did you observe for the +1.1% gain? Does the gain persist with more loss locations or different teachers (e.g., ResNet-50)?
3. Did you try class-conditional attention maps (e.g., Grad-CAM) for transfer? If so, how did they compare to class-agnostic activation maps?
4. Can you quantify training overhead for activation-based AT and gradient-based AT (compute, memory)? For gradient-based AT, how do you handle BN precisely during the second backprop?
5. Does AT affect model calibration or robustness? Any observations on whether AT causes the student to inherit teacher biases (e.g., background reliance)?
6. How does AT perform under domain shift in fine-tuning (e.g., teacher trained on ImageNet, student fine-tuned on a domain with different statistics) compared to KD?

Overall, this is a strong, timely paper with a simple idea that works well in practice. Despite some gaps in the gradient-based part and limited breadth on ImageNet, the activation-based AT contributions and empirical evidence justify acceptance.
