1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple, general mechanism to transfer knowledge from a high-capacity teacher CNN to a smaller student by aligning spatial attention maps at intermediate layers. The authors define two families of attention:
- Activation-based: construct a 2D spatial map from a 3D activation tensor by aggregating channelwise absolute activations (sum of |A|^p or max |A|^p). Loss aligns l2-normalized attention maps across selected layers.
- Gradient-based: define attention as the gradient of the loss w.r.t. the input; align student and teacher input gradients via a “double backprop” objective; additionally propose a symmetry regularizer enforcing horizontal-flip consistency of input gradients.

They demonstrate:
- On CIFAR-10 with WRNs and NIN, activation-based AT consistently improves over the student baseline, outperforms regressing full activations (FitNets-style hints) given equal budgets, and combines additively with knowledge distillation (KD) on small datasets.
- Gradient-based AT improves performance over baselines on CIFAR in a controlled (no-BN) setting; symmetry regularization also helps.
- On transfer learning benchmarks (CUB-200-2011 and MIT Indoor Scenes), activation-based AT yields substantial gains for ResNet-18 students using ResNet-34 teachers, narrowing the gap.
- On ImageNet, adding attention losses on the last two residual groups improves ResNet-18 by +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 over baseline, with minimal tuning; KD was not found effective in this setup.

The authors emphasize that AT is simple to implement, computationally cheap for activation-based variants, and compatible with KD.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: spatial attention alignment is a compelling, architecture-agnostic way to distill spatial priors, distinct from KD and full-activation regression.
- Empirical impact: consistent and meaningful gains across datasets and architectures, including a notable improvement on ImageNet with a strong baseline, and clear ablations showing attention maps are more effective than full activation regression.

3) Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Well-motivated: The paper situates AT within the literature on attention visualization (CAM, Grad-CAM, guided backprop) and knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets). It draws a clean line between aligning “where to look” versus “what features to match,” which is intuitively appealing and practically useful.
- Methodological simplicity: The activation-based attention definitions (sum |A|^p, max |A|^p) are simple, robust, and easy to compute; l2-normalization of attention vectors is a pragmatic choice to mitigate scale differences across layers and networks.
- Broad applicability: AT is shown with both residual and non-residual nets, same-depth and different-depth pairs, and both from scratch (CIFAR) and fine-tuning regimes (CUB/Scenes).
- Comparative evidence: The comparison to full-activation regression is important; AT yields better final accuracy while also accelerating convergence. The KD combination experiments on CIFAR show complementarity.
- Preliminary but interesting gradient-based AT: Although limited, results suggest input-gradient alignment itself is a useful regularizer/distillation signal; the flip-symmetry regularizer is a nice, general idea.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Gradient-based AT is underdeveloped experimentally: restricted to a small NIN without BN on CIFAR, with many caveats about second-order differentiation and BN behavior. This limits the strength of claims for this variant.
- Limited ablations: While there is a small layer-placement and attention-map function study, other critical ablations are missing or only asserted (e.g., the necessity of l2-normalization; sensitivity to p; the weighting of per-layer AT losses; effect of spatial alignment strategies for different-depth pairs).
- Baseline tuning asymmetry: For ImageNet, KD is reported to hurt or converge slowly, but hyperparameters were not tuned due to resource constraints. This weakens the comparison; it remains unclear whether KD could perform similarly with tuning or temperature annealing and data augmentations tailored to ImageNet.
- The claim of being the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet may be too strong without a broader, tuned comparison (e.g., FitNets-style intermediate regression, self-distillation, or recent concurrent works).
- Evaluation scope: Improvements on fine-grained and scene classification are compelling, but the core hypothesis—spatial priors transfer—is best tested on tasks where spatial reasoning matters (localization, detection, segmentation). This is deferred to future work.

Scientific correctness and rigor
- The losses and optimization are clearly specified. The computational overhead for activation-based AT is negligible; gradient-based AT doubles backprop cost.
- Results are generally plausible and consistent. However, many CIFAR experiments report single medians or single seeds; broader reporting (means ± std across multiple runs) would strengthen claims. ImageNet experiments are minimal (two AT losses, no tuning), which both underscores simplicity and leaves performance headroom.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide more comprehensive ablations:
  - Explicitly quantify the effect of l2-normalizing attention maps vs. not normalizing.
  - Sensitivity to p and to the choice of sum vs. max across more teacher/student pairs.
  - Weighting of per-layer AT losses and the importance of early vs. late groups.
  - Different spatial resolutions and interpolation strategies for mismatched feature maps.
- Strengthen KD and FitNets baselines, especially on ImageNet: tune temperature, alpha, and data augmentations; try self-distillation and intermediate-hint variants. This would better substantiate the claim that AT is preferable.
- Clarify label usage in gradient-based AT: do teacher gradients use ground-truth labels or teacher predictions? Using ground truth may give an advantage over KD that uses softened predictions.
- Address batch normalization in double backprop: offer a principled treatment (e.g., stop-gradient through BN stats or separate forward pass with frozen BN) and demonstrate gradient-based AT with BN on at least CIFAR.
- Report variability: include multiple seeds and confidence intervals for key results.
- Consider visual and quantitative alignment metrics: show correlation/IoU between teacher and student attention maps over training, to support the mechanism.
- Explore alternative attention definitions: e.g., Grad-CAM or CAM features as supervisory signals; channel-wise learned weights for attention aggregation.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is AT to the normalization choice and p? Could layer-wise learned scaling of attention losses further improve stability?
- For different-depth pairs, how do you choose which layers to align beyond spatial resolution matching? Have you tried learned alignment (e.g., small convs) while still supervising attention rather than full activations?
- In gradient-based AT, do you backpropagate w.r.t. cross-entropy on ground-truth labels or teacher soft labels? How does this choice affect performance and fairness vs. KD?
- Can you provide tuned KD and activation-regression baselines on ImageNet? If tuned KD remains inferior, what do you hypothesize as the reason?
- What is the runtime overhead of activation-based AT and gradient-based AT in practice? Any memory issues for large models?
- Have you tried AT on detection/segmentation, where spatial supervision should matter more? If so, any preliminary results?

Overall, the paper introduces a simple, general idea with solid empirical benefits, particularly for activation-based AT. The gradient-based variant is promising but preliminary. With stronger baselines and ablations, this work would be a valuable contribution.
