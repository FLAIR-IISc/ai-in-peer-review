Summary and contributions
- The paper investigates whether “attention” information from a teacher CNN can be used to improve a student CNN. It defines two families of spatial attention maps: activation-based (statistics over channel activations per spatial location) and gradient-based (input sensitivity maps). It proposes to align student and teacher attention maps via auxiliary losses across selected layers.
- Activation-based AT: defines F_sum, F_sum^p, and F_max^p on |A|, uses l2-normalized attention vectors and an l2 loss across multiple layer pairs, optionally combined with Knowledge Distillation (KD).
- Gradient-based AT: aligns ∂L/∂x between teacher and student via a “double backprop” second-order update; also proposes a horizontal-flip symmetry regularizer on gradient attention.
- Experiments: CIFAR-10/100 with WRN/ResNet/NIN show consistent gains from activation-based AT and modest gains from gradient-based AT; activation-based AT outperforms direct full-activation regression. Finetuning on CUB and MIT Scenes shows AT narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34. On ImageNet, ResNet-18 gains ~1.1% top-1 with AT; the authors report KD performing poorly in this setting.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Numerous presentation, formatting, and citation issues alongside incomplete and informal experimental reporting (“we did not have time to tune…”, “we plan to update…”), weaken scientific rigor and clarity.
2) Empirical support is uneven: gradient-based AT is only tested in a constrained CIFAR/NIN setting without BN; ImageNet results are under-explored and lack strong baselines and ablations. Claims (e.g., “first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet”) are insufficiently substantiated.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The idea of transferring spatial attention rather than full activations is reasonable and timely, and the paper relates to CAM/Grad-CAM/visualizations and to KD/FitNets. However, the conceptual definition of attention as simple channel-wise activation statistics is heuristic; no quantitative analysis links these maps to class-discriminative regions beyond anecdotal figures. The paper does not justify why l2-normalized attention alignment is preferable, nor provide ablations for normalization choices (l1 vs l2) or the scaling heuristic for β.
- Methodological clarity: For gradient-based AT, the role of BN during the second backprop is flagged as unclear but left unresolved; consequently, experiments disable BN and turn off weight decay, making results hard to generalize. The flip-invariance regularizer is promising but only tested in a very specific setting; its interaction with AT/KD is not systematically explored.
- Empirical rigor: 
  - CIFAR experiments show consistent gains, and the comparison vs full activation regression is a nice and informative result. However, several details are ad hoc (β set as 10^3 divided by map size and batch; decayed when combining with KD; no sensitivity analysis).
  - Gradient-based AT: limited to a thin NIN without BN, single-seed runs, and disabled weight decay. The method’s computational overhead is not quantified; stability and convergence properties are not analyzed.
  - ImageNet: only 2 AT losses used, no tuning, minimal ablations, no time/memory overhead. KD underperforms but hyperparameters are not re-tuned; the comparison is therefore inconclusive. The assertion that this is the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet is not convincingly supported and lacks thorough baselines (e.g., FitNets-style activation regression, recent KD variants).
  - Finetuning on CUB/Scenes: positive results but still limited ablations (e.g., which layers to align, number of transfer points, p in F_sum^p).
  - Statistical robustness: several results appear as single runs; elsewhere “median of 5 runs” is reported but not consistently across all settings. 
- Reproducibility and completeness: A code link is provided, which is good. However, the paper text frequently refers to figures/tables not shown here (e.g., “table 1/5,” “Fig. 4/5/6/7”), and the appendix figures are corrupted or mismatched. Implementation choices (e.g., exact schedules, optimizer hyperparameters, KD temperatures across datasets, the precise set of aligned layers) are sometimes mentioned but not systematically specified for all experiments.
- Presentation quality: The manuscript contains numerous typos, grammar issues, and serious reference formatting errors (e.g., “\Q2016\E,” inconsistent venues, missing full bibliographic info). The presence of informal statements in a results section (“we did not have time,” “we plan to update”) further detracts from polish and perceived rigor.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all affecting my decision)
- Clarify and justify the attention definition: Provide quantitative analyses demonstrating that activation-based maps correlate with class-discriminative evidence (e.g., IoU with CAM/Grad-CAM regions, perturbation-based relevance tests).
- Ablate key design choices thoroughly: normalization (l1 vs l2), p in F_sum^p, placement and number of AT losses, β scaling and schedule, p-norm in the loss, interpolation effects for mismatched resolutions.
- Strengthen ImageNet results: include fair KD and FitNets baselines with tuned hyperparameters; report wall-clock/memory overhead; test more transfer points; report multiple seeds or confidence intervals.
- Gradient-based AT: resolve BN handling formally and experimentally; quantify overhead and stability; evaluate on BN-enabled models and larger datasets; compare to Grad-CAM-based targets or feature-space Jacobian alignment; show synergy or complementarity with activation-based AT.
- Provide broader benchmarks: object detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization, where spatial attention is critical, as suggested in the conclusion.
- Improve writing and references: fix citation formatting, remove informal language, ensure all figures/tables are present and legible, and provide complete implementation details.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to the l2 normalization of attention maps? Did you try l1 normalization or no normalization? Please provide an ablation.
2) How was β chosen per layer/dataset, and how sensitive is performance to this choice and its decay schedule?
3) For ImageNet, what happens when aligning attention at all residual groups? Can you provide results with tuned KD and FitNets baselines?
4) For gradient-based AT, how exactly do you handle batch normalization in the second backprop? Can you provide experiments comparing alternatives and their stability/accuracy?
5) What is the training-time and memory overhead of activation-based and gradient-based AT across datasets?
6) Can you quantify the correlation between your activation-based attention maps and known saliency/attribution maps, and show that “better” attention alignment causally improves accuracy (e.g., via perturbation tests)?
