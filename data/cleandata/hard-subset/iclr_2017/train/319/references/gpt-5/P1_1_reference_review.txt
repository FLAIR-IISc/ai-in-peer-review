Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT) as a new form of knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN by aligning spatial attention maps. Two complementary definitions of attention are explored: (i) activation-based attention maps that aggregate per-location activations across channels via simple statistics (sum of absolute values, sum/max with exponent p); and (ii) gradient-based attention defined as input sensitivity (gradients of the loss w.r.t. input), with an additional symmetry regularizer enforcing horizontal-flip consistency.
- The method introduces a normalized attention-matching loss placed at multiple layers (or groups of layers) and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD). The authors emphasize the importance of l2-normalization of attention vectors prior to matching.
- Empirically, activation-based AT consistently improves student performance across CIFAR-10 with ResNets and Wide ResNets, two transfer-learning settings (CUB-200 and MIT Scenes), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student improved by 1.1% top-1 over a strong baseline using a ResNet-34 teacher). AT outperforms full activation matching (FitNets-style) and is complementary to KD on small-scale tasks. Gradient-based AT and the proposed symmetry norm also improve CIFAR-10 performance in a setting without batch normalization. Code is provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The idea of aligning spatial attention maps is simple, well-motivated, and novel relative to prior KD/FitNets that match logits or full intermediate activations; (2) Strong empirical evidence, including ablations, across multiple architectures and datasets, with especially compelling gains on CIFAR and a positive result on ImageNet; (3) The approach is practical (low overhead for activation-based AT), easy to adopt, and accompanied by open-source code.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper clearly connects psychological and interpretability notions of attention to a transferable inductive bias for CNNs. It situates the work relative to visualization (saliency, deconvnet, CAM/Grad-CAM) and knowledge transfer (KD, FitNets), and explains why channel-collapsed spatial attention may carry the most relevant supervisory signal while being compact and architecture-agnostic.
- Method clarity: Activation-based maps are precisely defined (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax), with discussion of their properties. The AT loss is well specified, including the crucial l2 normalization and where to place losses for same vs different-depth architectures. The gradient-based AT formulation and the need for double backprop are correctly derived, and the symmetry regularization is well motivated and straightforward.
- Empirical support: 
  - CIFAR-10: Consistent, nontrivial error-rate reductions for multiple teacher–student pairs. Ablations show: (a) multiple AT losses across groups matter; (b) sum-based attention maps outperform max-based; (c) AT improves final accuracy more than full-activation regression while also accelerating convergence; (d) AT and KD can be complementary in small-scale settings.
  - Gradient-based AT: Even simple gradient-norm regularization helps; the new symmetry norm is a strong regularizer; gradient-based AT improves over baseline; activation-based AT remains best in the presented setting.
  - Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): AT significantly closes the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34, outperforming KD on CUB, suggesting that intermediate spatial attention is especially helpful for fine-grained tasks.
  - ImageNet: A meaningful 1.1% top-1 gain for ResNet-18 with only two AT losses and minimal tuning is compelling and demonstrates scalability.
- Practicality and reproducibility: The technique is lightweight for activation-based AT and comes with code. The paper discusses hyperparameters (β scaling, temperature for KD) and provides some multi-run statistics (medians) on CIFAR.

Areas for improvement (not blocking acceptance)
- Scope and rigor on large-scale: ImageNet results, while positive, are limited to one teacher–student pair, two loss placements, and untuned hyperparameters; more thorough experiments (e.g., placing AT at all groups, multiple seeds, stronger baselines) would strengthen claims.
- Gradient-based AT: Evaluation is restricted to a specific CIFAR setup without batch normalization due to double backprop complications with BN. This limits conclusions about the generality of the gradient-based variant.
- Comparisons: Full-activation transfer is compared on CIFAR, but additional baselines (e.g., FitNets with optimized hint layers, Jacobian matching beyond input gradients, other distillation variants) on large-scale tasks would contextualize gains further.
- Analysis: While some qualitative attention visualizations are provided, more systematic analyses (e.g., correlation between teacher–student attention similarity and accuracy, negative controls with mismatched teachers) would deepen understanding.
- Writing and references: Several typos/formatting issues and inconsistent reference formatting should be corrected.

Questions for the authors
1. Layer selection and weighting: How sensitive are results to which layers receive AT and to the relative weights across layers? Have you tried adaptive per-layer β or curriculum schedules?
2. Normalization choice: You emphasize l2 normalization of attention maps. How do l1 normalization or per-location normalization (e.g., spatial softmax) affect results?
3. Negative controls: What happens if the student aligns attention to a weaker teacher or a teacher trained on a different task/domain? Does AT still regularize/help, or can it hurt?
4. Computational cost: Can you quantify training overhead (time/memory) for activation-based AT and gradient-based AT? For gradient-based AT, any path forward to support batch normalization reliably?
5. Class-conditional attention: Have you explored class-specific attention matching (e.g., using Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++) vs the class-agnostic activation-based maps? Does class-conditioning help on fine-grained tasks?
6. Stability and variance: On ImageNet and transfer-learning tasks, what is the variance across runs? Are the reported gains statistically significant?
7. Combining with KD on ImageNet: You report KD struggled. Did tuning the temperature/α or matching intermediate logits help? Any insights into why AT succeeds where KD fails in this setting?
8. Choice of p and max vs sum: On CIFAR, sum-based maps outperform max-based. Is this consistent on larger datasets? Any intuition or quantitative evidence about when max-based maps might help (e.g., sparse activation regimes)?

Constructive suggestions
- Expand ImageNet experiments with more AT placements, multiple seeds, and a stronger baseline schedule; report top-1/top-5 with confidence intervals.
- Provide a short sensitivity study for β, p, and the normalization choice; include a recommended default.
- Add a brief discussion of the inductive bias enforced by AT (e.g., encouraging spatial selectivity and consistency) and a diagnostic correlating attention similarity with accuracy.
- Clarify implementation details (e.g., interpolation of maps, exact β scaling formula, schedules when combined with KD) and quantify overhead.
- Clean up typos and reference formatting; temper claims about being “first” on ImageNet or provide citations/justification.

Overall, the paper presents a simple, well-motivated idea that is broadly useful and empirically effective; I recommend acceptance.
