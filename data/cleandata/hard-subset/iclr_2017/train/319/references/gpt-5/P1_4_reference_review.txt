Summary and contributions
The paper proposes attention transfer (AT) as a knowledge transfer mechanism from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps at intermediate layers and encourage the student’s attention to match the teacher’s. Two complementary attention definitions are explored:
- Activation-based attention: channel-wise spatial statistics of absolute activations (sum, sum of powers, max of powers), with L2-normalized attention maps and an auxiliary loss aligning teacher–student maps at matched layers/groups.
- Gradient-based attention: input-gradient maps viewed as sensitivity/attention, matched via a double-backprop loss; a flip-invariance regularizer for gradients is also proposed.

The authors show consistent improvements across CIFAR-10 (WRN and NIN), transfer learning on CUB-200-2011 and MIT Indoor Scenes with ResNet-18/34, and ImageNet (ResNet-18 student, ResNet-34 teacher), where AT yields +1.1% top-1/+0.8% top-5 without hyperparameter tuning. They also show AT outperforms regressing full activations (FitNets-style hints) and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD) on smaller datasets. Code and models are released.

Decision: Accept
Main reasons:
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated, and novel use of spatial attention as the distilled signal.
- Empirically effective and fairly general: improvements across architectures, depths, and datasets, including a meaningful gain on ImageNet.
- Useful analysis/ablations (attention functions, placement of losses, comparison to KD and full-activation transfer) and practical guidance (importance of normalization).

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper identifies a gap in knowledge transfer methods: regressing full activations or logits may not be the most informative or structure-preserving signal; instead, spatial attention summarizes “where” the network focuses, which is both interpretable and potentially more invariant. This is well positioned relative to attention visualization literature (saliency, deconvnet, CAM/Grad-CAM) and prior distillation (Hinton et al., FitNets, WRN).
- Method soundness: The activation-based attention definitions are simple, architecture-agnostic, and cheap. The normalization choice is justified and shown to be important. Layer/group alignment is handled cleanly for equal/different depths. The gradient-based AT formulation is principled (double backprop) with a symmetry regularizer that is intuitive and beneficial.
- Empirical support: 
  - CIFAR-10: Consistent and nontrivial error reductions across teacher–student pairs; sum-based attention variants beat max-based; AT > full activation regression; AT+KD often best.
  - Transfer learning: On CUB and MIT Indoor, AT closes much of the ResNet-18/34 gap, with particularly strong gains on fine-grained CUB, in line with the hypothesis that mid-level attention is crucial.
  - ImageNet: A +1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 from a ResNet-34 teacher using only two AT losses, with no tuning, is compelling and practically relevant.
  - Gradient-based: While limited to NIN on CIFAR (no BN), results show double-backprop, symmetry, and teacher–student gradient AT all help; activation-based AT still does best in that setting.
- Novelty and impact: Using spatial attention as the distilled object, with concrete, effective definitions and training recipes, is new and likely to be adopted due to simplicity and performance. The ImageNet result helps establish significance beyond small datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited gradient-based experiments: The gradient AT section is promising but constrained to a special setting without BN; practical guidance for modern BN-equipped networks is missing.
- Reporting rigor: Some results are single runs (gradient AT), others report medians over 5 runs; standard deviations/confidence intervals are generally absent. More comprehensive tables (including full CIFAR tables mentioned in text) would strengthen claims.
- Comparisons: While KD and FitNets-style activation regression are covered, comparisons to contemporary distillation variants (e.g., attention-like hints, Jacobian matching, or feature-map similarity without 1x1 adapters) are limited. The claim that this is the first successful transfer on ImageNet should be carefully qualified/checked.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: β scheduling, the necessity of L2 normalization, choice of p in Fpsum, and number/placement of loss sites are partially explored but not systematically. The method appears robust, but clearer guidance would help reproducibility.
- Computational cost: Activation-based AT is cheap, but training-time overheads (especially for gradient-based AT) are not quantified.

Suggestions for improvement (to help, not essential to my decision)
- Provide aggregate statistics (mean±std over multiple seeds) for key tables, including ImageNet if feasible; add full CIFAR tables and training curves in the appendix.
- Include a sensitivity study for β, normalization type (L1 vs L2), exponent p, and number/placement of AT losses; report a simple selection heuristic.
- Quantify overhead (FLOPs/time) for AT vs baseline/KD, and the added memory cost.
- Clarify and, if needed, temper the “first successful transfer on ImageNet” claim; cite and compare against any close contemporaries.
- Expand gradient-based AT to modern BN setups, or detail a practical recipe (e.g., frozen BN in the second backprop); include at least a small BN-enabled experiment.
- Consider class-conditional attention (e.g., Grad-CAM) as an alternative signal, or discuss why class-agnostic activation attention is preferable.
- Evaluate robustness/regularization side effects (e.g., corruption robustness, calibration), especially given the gradient norms and symmetry regularizer.
- Minor: unify notation (Lp norms vs L2-normalized vectors), and clarify that L2 distance between unit-normalized vectors approximates a cosine dissimilarity.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is AT to β, the normalization choice, and the exponent p? Can you propose default settings that work across datasets?
- How do you select which layers/groups to align for different-depth pairs? Does adding AT at all groups monotonically help on ImageNet as it did on CIFAR?
- What is the training-time and memory overhead of activation-based AT and of gradient-based AT, relative to baseline and KD?
- Does AT still help when the teacher and student have the same capacity or when the teacher is only marginally better? Any evidence of negative transfer?
- Have you tried class-specific attention signals (e.g., Grad-CAM) rather than class-agnostic activation attention? Do they help/hurt?
- Why does KD hurt ImageNet convergence here? Is it due to architectural mismatch, temperature/α choices, or label space calibration? Any tuned KD results?
- For gradient-based AT with BN: can you propose an implementation strategy (e.g., detach BN stats or use ghost BN) and do you expect comparable gains to activation AT?
- Do improvements persist in data-limited regimes and on tasks emphasizing spatial reasoning (detection/WSOL), as hypothesized?
