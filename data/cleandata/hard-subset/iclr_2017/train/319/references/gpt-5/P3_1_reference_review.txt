Summary
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a mechanism for knowledge transfer between a teacher and a student CNN. The core idea is to define spatial attention maps for intermediate layers and to train the student so that its attention maps match the teacher’s, in addition to the standard supervised loss. The authors study two families of attention: (i) activation-based maps that aggregate absolute activations across channels (e.g., sum, sum of powers, max of powers), and (ii) gradient-based maps defined as input gradients (saliency). They introduce simple, normalized losses to align teacher and student attentions across corresponding layers/groups, and show that AT improves performance across CIFAR-10, CUB-200, MIT-Scenes, and ImageNet. They also report that activation-based AT yields larger gains than regressing full activations (FitNets-style), is complementary to knowledge distillation (KD), and can be implemented with negligible overhead. Gradient-based AT, along with a symmetry regularizer based on horizontal flips, is explored on CIFAR.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual and practical novelty: matching spatial attention (simple channel-wise statistics or input sensitivity) rather than full activations provides a lightweight, architecture-agnostic transfer signal that is empirically more effective than full activation matching and complementary to KD.
- Strong empirical evidence, including improved ImageNet performance of a ResNet-18 student using a ResNet-34 teacher, with minimal tuning and overhead.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The work is well-situated relative to KD (Hinton et al.) and FitNets (intermediate hints). The intuition that spatial attention captures “where” a network focuses (and is more transferable than channel-wise details) is appealing and clearly articulated. The paper also connects to attention visualization literature (saliency, deconvnet, guided backprop, CAM, Grad-CAM), justifying the attention constructs.
- Methodology: The activation-based attention family is simple yet flexible (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax). The design choice to l2-normalize attention vectors before matching is well motivated and empirically highlighted as important. The layer selection strategy (per block or per group) is practical across architectures. Gradient-based AT is derived cleanly, relates to double backprop, and the symmetry constraint is a nice regularization idea.
- Empirical support:
  - CIFAR: Consistent gains across WRN teacher/student pairs. Ablations indicate sum-based maps outperform max-based ones; placing losses at multiple stages helps. AT improves final accuracy more than full-activation transfer, though both speed convergence.
  - Gradient-based AT: On a constrained CIFAR setting, gradient regularizers and the symmetry constraint improve performance; gradient-based AT shows gains, though activation-based AT performs best.
  - Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): AT closes much of the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 and outperforms/equals KD depending on dataset, supporting the claim that intermediate spatial attention is particularly valuable for fine-grained tasks.
  - ImageNet: A 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 with only two AT losses is meaningful; reporting that KD struggled in this setting is candid and useful to the community.
- Practicality: Activation-based AT adds negligible cost (only teacher forward and simple reductions), is easy to integrate with KD, and requires no architectural changes. The code release strengthens reproducibility.

Limitations and concerns
- Gradient-based AT is under-explored: experiments avoid batch normalization and use a narrow NIN; the BN issue during the second backprop is acknowledged but unresolved. As a result, the gradient-based contribution feels preliminary compared to the activation-based part.
- Rigor and reporting: Some results are single-run; others report medians of 5 runs. For major claims (e.g., CIFAR gains, AT vs. activation regression), comprehensive statistics (mean/std across multiple seeds) would increase confidence. Exact hyperparameters (β per layer, normalization choices, schedules) are only partially specified.
- Comparisons: Full-activation transfer baselines are described, but a stronger FitNets-style baseline (with tuned 1×1 adapters, layer choices, normalization, and optimal β schedules) would help solidify the claim that attention maps are superior. On ImageNet, comparisons to other regularizers (e.g., label smoothing) or distillation settings (temperature, α) would contextualize the 1.1% gain.
- Theoretical grounding: The choice of absolute activations and specific reductions is heuristic. While the intuition is reasonable and supported visually, analysis of when different F functions excel (e.g., sensitivity to class granularity, sparsity) is limited.

Suggestions for improvement (not required for acceptance)
- Provide more systematic ablations:
  - Effect of normalization (l1 vs l2), β scheduling, and loss placement (every block vs selected layers) on CIFAR and ImageNet.
  - Sensitivity to the exponent p and to the attention function family across datasets.
  - Memory/time overhead measurements for training with AT (with/without KD).
- Strengthen baselines:
  - A tuned FitNets-style activation regression baseline, and a KD baseline on ImageNet with a careful temperature/α sweep.
  - Report multiple seeds and confidence intervals for key tables.
- Clarify implementation details:
  - Precise β per layer, normalization constants (division by map size and batch size), and decay schedules.
  - For gradient-based AT, explicitly detail how BN layers are handled during the second backprop, even if the current experiments avoid BN.
- Extend qualitative analysis: provide side-by-side teacher/student attention maps before and after AT across layers and classes, with quantitative similarity metrics (e.g., correlation).
- Discuss broader applicability: brief experiments or discussion on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization would highlight the spatial nature of AT.

Questions for the authors
- Normalization: You emphasize l2 normalization of attention maps is crucial. Did you compare l1 vs l2 vs no normalization systematically across datasets? How sensitive is performance to the normalization choice?
- β scheduling: How are β values chosen per layer and over training? Can you share a principled guideline (e.g., scale-invariance via dividing by map size) and sensitivity analysis?
- Attention definition: Why absolute activations? Did you try signed sums (pre-ReLU) or channel-wise energy (mean square) and how did they fare?
- FitNets baseline: What effort went into tuning 1×1 adapters, layer selection, and normalization for full-activation transfer? Could a stronger FitNets-style setup close the gap to AT?
- KD on ImageNet: You report negative results with KD for ResNet-18/34. Did you try varying temperature/α, matching pre-softmax logits, or using intermediate KD? Any insight into why KD struggled here?
- Gradient-based AT and BN: How would you implement the second backprop with BN in practice? Freeze BN statistics/affines, or run a separate forward with fixed BN? Any preliminary results?
- Cross-architecture transfer: Have you tried teacher/student from different families (e.g., Inception to ResNet) on ImageNet/CUB? Does AT remain effective when spatial resolutions or downsampling schedules differ more substantially?
- Overhead: What is the actual training-time overhead for activation-based AT and for gradient-based AT (factor over baseline) on CIFAR and ImageNet?

Overall, this is a simple, well-motivated idea with clear empirical utility and low integration cost. Despite some gaps (especially for gradient-based AT and stronger baselines), the contribution is novel and impactful. I recommend acceptance.
