- Proposal: Use spatial attention maps to transfer knowledge from teacher CNN to student CNN, supervising student to match teacher attention in addition to standard loss.
- Two attention families: activation-based (spatial aggregation across channels) and gradient-based (input saliency maps via loss gradients).
- Activation-based AT: l2-normalized attention maps matched at multiple layers (per residual group/block); can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD).
- Gradient-based AT: matches teacher/student saliency maps via double-backprop and explores a horizontal flip symmetry regularizer.
- Experiments: evaluated on CIFAR-10/100 (NIN/ResNet/WRN), transfer to CUB/Scenes, and ImageNet (RN-18 student, RN-34 teacher).
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements across tasks; ImageNet yields +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 for RN-18 without hyperparameter tuning.
- AT outperforms full-activation regression (FitNets-style hints) and complements KD in many settings.
- Motivation and positioning: well connected to attention/saliency and knowledge transfer literature; attention maps are argued to be compact and less brittle than full-activation matching.
- Methodology strengths: reasonable attention mapping functions, useful analysis (sum vs powers vs max), and justified normalization of spatial maps.
- Layer pairing strategy is practical and straightforward (per residual group/block).
- CIFAR experiments include ablations and multiple teacher–student pairs, showing robustness of activation-based AT.
- Gradient-based AT results are promising but preliminary (limited to NIN without BN, single-run reporting).
- Larger-scale results: AT narrows RN-18 vs RN-34 gap on CUB/Scenes and shows strong ImageNet improvement, supporting scalability.
- Rigor: some multi-run statistics and ablations provided; hyperparameters discussed; code/models available for reproducibility.
- Caveat: gradient-based AT needs more evidence and analysis, especially regarding BN and second-order backprop.
- Caveat: ImageNet KD baselines are weak and should be strengthened (tuned temperature/α, same-depth teacher) before strong claims.
- Caveat: avoid asserting uniqueness of being first successful ImageNet transfer; literature is evolving.
- Caveat: using absolute activation magnitudes as “attention” is heuristic; attention-map quality metrics and quantitative analyses would help.
- Suggestion: analyze normalization choices (l1 vs l2), β sensitivity, and number/placement of AT losses with sensitivity plots.
- Suggestion: report compute/throughput and memory overheads for activation- and gradient-based AT (training vs inference).
- Suggestion: provide systematic multi-run statistics and significance tests, especially for gradient-based AT.
- Suggestion: strengthen ImageNet baselines (tuned KD, same-architecture teacher) and compare improved hint-based matching with adapters.
- Suggestion: clarify implementation details for layer alignment, interpolation, stop-gradient for teacher, BN freezing, and precision.
- Suggestion: fix references/formatting issues in the paper.
- Questions: sensitivity to β schedule and placement of AT losses; effect of adding earlier-group AT on ImageNet?
- Questions: have you tried l1 or other normalizations, or temperature-scaling attention magnitudes before normalization?
- Questions: how are spatial resolution mismatches handled (bilinear/learned up/downsampling), and have you tried multi-scale attention fusion?
- Questions: is there a measurable correlation between attention similarity and accuracy gains during training?
- Questions: how robust is AT to weaker or miscalibrated teachers (teacher accuracy ≤ student)?
- Questions: for gradient-based AT with BN, have you tried frozen BN stats or other strategies to stabilize second backprop?
- Questions: what are wall-clock training-time and memory overheads for activation- and gradient-based AT?
- Question: could class-conditional attention (e.g., Grad-CAM) improve class-discriminativeness for transfer?
- Overall decision: Accept — simple, general, and effective idea with solid evidence, especially for activation-based attention transfer.
