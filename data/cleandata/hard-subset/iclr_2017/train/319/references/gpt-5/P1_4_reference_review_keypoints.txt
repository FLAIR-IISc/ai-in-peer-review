- Proposes attention transfer (AT) to distill spatial attention maps from teacher to student CNNs (activation- and gradient-based).  
- Activation-based AT: channel-wise spatial stats (sum, sum of powers, max of powers), L2-normalized attention maps, auxiliary loss aligning matched layers/groups.  
- Gradient-based AT: input-gradient sensitivity maps matched via double-backprop loss with a flip-invariance symmetry regularizer.  
- Shows consistent improvements on CIFAR-10 (WRN, NIN), transfer learning on CUB-200-2011 and MIT Indoor (ResNet-18/34), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 from ResNet-34: +1.1% top-1/+0.8% top-5).  
- AT outperforms regressing full activations (FitNets-style hints) and can be combined with knowledge distillation (KD) on smaller datasets.  
- Contributions: simple, well-motivated, novel use of spatial attention as the distilled signal with practical training recipes and released code/models.  
- Method soundness: activation AT is architecture-agnostic and cheap; normalization choice is justified; layer/group alignment handled cleanly.  
- Gradient AT is principled (double backprop) and symmetry regularizer is intuitive and beneficial.  
- Empirical ablations cover attention functions, loss placement, and comparisons to KD and full-activation transfer; activation-based AT typically performs best.  
- ImageNet result is practically relevant and supports generality beyond small datasets.  
- Novelty/impact: likely to be adopted due to simplicity, effectiveness, and ImageNet-scale gains.  
- Weakness: gradient-based experiments are limited to non-BN networks; guidance for BN-equipped models is missing.  
- Weakness: reporting lacks rigor—many single runs, few std/confidence intervals, and missing full CIFAR tables.  
- Weakness: limited comparisons to other contemporary distillation variants (attention-like hints, Jacobian matching, feature-map similarity).  
- Weakness: hyperparameter sensitivity (β scheduling, normalization type, exponent p, number/placement of losses) not systematically explored.  
- Weakness: computational/training overhead (especially for gradient AT) not quantified.  
- Suggestion: provide aggregate statistics (mean±std), full CIFAR tables, and training curves in appendix.  
- Suggestion: run sensitivity studies for β, normalization (L1 vs L2), exponent p, and loss placements and give default heuristics.  
- Suggestion: quantify FLOPs/time/memory overhead for AT vs baseline/KD and clarify/temper the “first ImageNet transfer” claim with comparisons.  
- Suggestion: extend gradient-based AT to BN setups or propose practical implementations (e.g., frozen BN) and test class-conditional attention (Grad-CAM) alternatives.  
- Suggestion: evaluate robustness/regularization effects (corruption robustness, calibration) and unify notation (Lp vs L2-normalized vectors).  
- Questions: sensitivity to β, normalization, and exponent p; how layers/groups are selected and whether adding AT at all groups always helps.  
- Questions: training-time and memory overheads for activation- and gradient-based AT; behavior when teacher and student have equal/marginal capacity; evidence of negative transfer.  
- Questions: results with class-specific attention (Grad-CAM); why KD hurts ImageNet here and whether tuned KD fixes it; BN implementation strategy for gradient AT; persistence of gains in data-limited regimes and spatial tasks (detection/WSOL).
