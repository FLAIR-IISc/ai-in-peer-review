Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Attention Transfer (AT) as a simple, general mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN by aligning spatial attention maps across layers. Attention is defined either (i) activation-based: channel-wise statistics over absolute activations (sum, p-sum, p-max) with l2 normalization; or (ii) gradient-based: input sensitivity maps requiring double backprop. The authors also propose a flip-symmetry regularizer on gradient attention.
- The method is architecture-agnostic, incurs little overhead (activation AT requires only a teacher forward pass and simple reductions), and can be combined with distillation.
- Empirically, AT yields consistent gains across CIFAR-10 (WRN/ResNet/NIN), transfer learning on CUB and MIT Scenes (ResNet-18 students closing part of the gap to ResNet-34 teachers), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 gains +1.1% top-1, +0.8% top-5 with only two AT losses). AT outperforms “full activation transfer” (FitNets-style) and often complements KD; gradient-based AT and the symmetry loss also help on CIFAR.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Conceptually novel and bold idea—transferring intermediate spatial attention rather than logits or full activations—framed in a simple, general, and implementable way. (2) Compelling empirical signal across multiple datasets and architectures, including a notable improvement on ImageNet with a lightweight setup. The conceptual advance outweighs some experimental incompleteness in gradient-based AT and large-scale tuning.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and clarity: The definition of activation-based attention as channel-reduced maps (e.g., sum of squared activations) with per-map normalization is a fresh, intuitive perspective on “what to transfer.” It’s distinct from KD and FitNets and aligns well with how practitioners visualize and reason about CNNs. The gradient-based AT and flip symmetry regularizer further broaden the idea (even if experiments for these are limited).
- Generality and practicality: AT is easy to add to existing pipelines, requires only a teacher forward pass (for activation AT), and avoids adding parameters. The loss is defined at multiple layers with flexible spatial alignment/interpolation, and combines with KD.
- Empirical breadth: The paper shows consistent improvements on CIFAR with multiple teacher/student depth/width configurations; activation-based AT consistently beats full activation regression; combining with KD tends to help on CIFAR. On transfer learning (CUB and Scenes), AT reduces the ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap and qualitatively aligns student attention to the teacher. On ImageNet, achieving +1.1% top-1 for ResNet-18 with minimal tuning is meaningful for such a simple auxiliary loss.
- Insightful negatives: The finding that KD can struggle on ImageNet under cross-depth settings, while AT still helps, is useful to the community, and underscores that spatial supervision is a distinct and valuable signal.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not all required for acceptance)
- Quantify overhead: Report training-time cost for activation AT and gradient AT (FLOPs or wall-clock), and memory impact, especially on ImageNet. Clarify inference cost (none).
- Statistical rigor: Provide mean/std over multiple seeds on CIFAR (done partially) and, if feasible, two runs on ImageNet/transfer datasets to show robustness.
- Ablations:
  - Systematically vary p in Fpsum/Fpmax; compare l1 vs l2 normalization of attention maps; study per-layer weighting and the number/placement of AT losses.
  - Cross-architecture transfer (e.g., ResNet teacher to MobileNet/ShuffleNet student) to highlight generality.
  - Teacher strength: What happens when the teacher is only modestly better or even weaker? Does AT hurt?
  - Compare to class-discriminative attention (e.g., Grad-CAM) as attention targets, and/or CAM-based attention without architectural changes.
- ImageNet: While the current gains are promising, adding AT losses to all residual groups (as planned) and reporting sensitivity to β would strengthen the claim. So would a small hyperparameter sweep for KD to ensure a fair comparison before concluding it “fails.”
- Gradient-based AT: Clarify handling of batch normalization and numerical stability during the second backprop; demonstrate on a BN-enabled setup and, if possible, a larger dataset or a ResNet block to validate scalability.
- Positioning: Calibrate the “first successful transfer on ImageNet” claim by surveying contemporaneous/later distillation works that report ImageNet (if any) and clarifying the setting differences.

Questions for the authors
1. Sensitivity and normalization:
   - How sensitive are results to the choice of p and to l2 vs l1 normalization? Can you provide a small grid on CIFAR and a single ablation on ImageNet?
   - Are attention maps normalized per-image or per-batch? Did batch-wise normalization hurt/help?
2. Computational cost:
   - What is the training-time overhead (percent) for activation-based AT and for gradient-based AT, including memory? Please report for CIFAR and ImageNet.
3. Gradient-based AT:
   - How exactly do you handle batch normalization during the second backprop? Do you freeze BN stats/affine params? Any divergence/stability issues?
   - Did you try gradient AT at intermediate feature layers (not only input), and if so, what were the trade-offs?
4. ImageNet and KD:
   - Which temperatures and α did you try for KD on ImageNet? Did label smoothing or different optimization settings change the outcome?
   - With AT on only two groups, do the gains saturate? How do per-group weights affect accuracy?
5. What drives AT’s advantage over full activation regression?
   - Can you rule out that the benefit is just stronger spatial regularization by comparing to simpler spatial penalties (e.g., matching feature norms or low-rank spatial summaries)?
   - Have you tried random or shuffled teacher attention (as a control) to confirm the necessity of coherent teacher attention?
6. Transfer learning details:
   - In CUB/Scenes, which layers were frozen in student vs teacher, and were BN layers fixed in both? Did unfreezing earlier layers change AT’s impact?

Overall, this is a clean, novel idea with solid empirical evidence and practical relevance. Despite some incomplete explorations (especially for gradient AT), the conceptual contribution and demonstrated breadth merit acceptance.
