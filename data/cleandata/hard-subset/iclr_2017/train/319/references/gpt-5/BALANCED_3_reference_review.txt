1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a simple, general mechanism for knowledge transfer between CNNs. It defines spatial attention maps derived either from activations (e.g., sum of absolute activations across channels, optionally raised to a power p) or from input gradients (saliency), and trains a student to match a teacher’s attention at selected layers while also optimizing the task loss. Key ideas include: (i) l2-normalized attention map matching at multiple stages, (ii) several attention mapping functions with analysis of their properties, (iii) a gradient-based AT variant related to double backprop with an additional symmetry regularizer, and (iv) compatibility with knowledge distillation (KD). Empirically, activation-based AT yields consistent improvements on CIFAR-10 across several WRN/ResNet/NIN configurations, narrows gaps in transfer learning on CUB and MIT Scenes, and improves ImageNet ResNet-18 with a ResNet-34 teacher. The paper also shows that AT outperforms directly matching full activations and can combine with KD.

2) Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated method that consistently improves performance across multiple datasets and architectures, including a meaningful gain on ImageNet.
- Clear empirical evidence and ablations supporting the design choices (attention mapping functions, placement of losses, normalization), with practical guidance and code availability.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper asks whether aligning a student’s spatial “where to look” patterns with a teacher’s can improve learning. It operationalizes attention in CNNs via activation and gradient-derived spatial maps.

Motivation and placement in literature: The work bridges two lines of research—attention visualization (Grad-CAM/CAM/saliency) and knowledge transfer (KD/FitNets). Unlike KD (output-level) or FitNets (feature regression with adapters), AT emphasizes spatial focus and uses simple, parameter-free attention statistics, making it lightweight and applicable across depths/widths. The related work is broadly adequate and positions the approach fairly; the connection to double backprop for the gradient-based variant is explicit.

Evidence and rigor:
- Activation-based AT: On CIFAR-10, consistent improvements across teacher–student pairs (same and different depths/widths), with sum-based attention (especially Fsum/F^2sum) outperforming max-based. Ablations show benefit from placing losses at multiple groups and that attention map matching improves final accuracy more than matching full activations (despite similar convergence speed-ups).
- Gradient-based AT: Demonstrates viability on CIFAR-10 with NIN, showing benefits over baseline and double backprop; symmetry regularization helps further. This section is limited in scope (no BN, small model), but still informative.
- Larger images: In transfer learning (CUB/Scenes), AT closes much of the ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap and sometimes outperforms KD. On ImageNet, AT improves ResNet-18 by 1.1% top-1 (0.8% top-5) with only two attention losses and minimal tuning, which is practically meaningful. The authors note KD struggled in this setup, a useful negative result.

Overall, the claims about activation-based AT being broadly effective and complementary to KD are supported. The gradient-based AT claims are more preliminary but reasonable.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify and systematize hyperparameters: Provide exact β settings per layer (scaling rule, schedule), p values explored, normalization choice (l1 vs l2) comparisons, and sensitivity analyses. A short hyperparameter robustness study would strengthen the practical utility.
- Layer selection: Add a concise guideline (or ablation) on which layers/groups matter most across architectures, and how to handle mismatched stages between teacher and student.
- Compute/overhead: Quantify training-time overhead for activation-based AT (negligible?) and gradient-based AT (double backprop cost), and any inference-time cost (none).
- Gradient-based AT: Expand on batch normalization handling in the second backward pass. If possible, include results with BN-enabled models and/or clarify best practices (e.g., freezing BN stats vs. recomputing).
- More ImageNet detail: Provide exact training settings for baselines and AT runs, confidence intervals or multiple seeds if feasible, and an apples-to-apples comparison to logit-matching or label smoothing baselines. If longer runs for KD are infeasible, at least document the learning dynamics to substantiate the “hurts convergence” claim.
- Additional baselines: Include a spatially pooled feature-matching baseline (e.g., match per-location l2 norms) to isolate whether spatial normalization alone explains gains. Consider a “centered” attention-matching (subtract spatial mean) to test robustness to global scaling/bias.
- Interpretability: Quantify attention similarity (e.g., rank correlation/IoU on binarized maps) alongside qualitative figures to connect improved performance with more teacher-like attention.
- Writing and references: Fix citation formatting and minor typos; ensure all variables are introduced when first used and that figures/tables are self-contained with clear captions.

Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameters: How sensitive are results to β magnitude and schedule, to the choice of p in Fpsum, and to l1 vs l2 normalization of attention maps? Can you provide a small grid/sensitivity study?
- Layer/stage alignment: For different-depth teacher/student pairs, how do you choose the groups to match? Did you try matching early layers as well on ImageNet and what was the effect?
- KD interaction: On ImageNet, did you try tuning temperature and α, label smoothing, or logit matching instead of soft targets to mitigate the KD convergence issues?
- Gradient-based AT specifics: How exactly is BN handled during the second backprop pass? Is JT computed using the true labels or teacher’s predicted labels? What is the wall-clock overhead?
- Generality: Have you tried cross-architecture transfers (e.g., Inception/WRN to ResNet) on ImageNet or other large datasets? Any failure cases where AT provides little or no benefit?
- Robustness: Does AT confer any robustness benefits (e.g., to corruptions or mild distribution shift) or improved calibration due to more aligned spatial focus?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and practically useful contribution with convincing activation-based results, including on ImageNet. The paper will benefit from clearer hyperparameter guidance, more detail on computational overhead, and stronger characterization of the gradient-based variant.
