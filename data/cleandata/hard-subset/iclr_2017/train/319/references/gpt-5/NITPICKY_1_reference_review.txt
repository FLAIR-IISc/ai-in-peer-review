Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a mechanism for knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student. “Attention” is defined as spatial maps computed at intermediate layers either from activations (channel-wise statistics of |A|, e.g., sum or power-sum) or from gradients with respect to input (double backprop). The student is trained to match the teacher’s normalized attention maps (plus standard cross-entropy), optionally combined with knowledge distillation (KD).
- The authors show empirical gains on CIFAR-10 with ResNet/WRN and NIN, on CUB and MIT Scenes via finetuning, and a small ImageNet gain when transferring from ResNet-34 to ResNet-18. They report that activation-based AT often outperforms direct full-activation matching and can complement KD. They also explore a gradient-based AT and a symmetry constraint on input gradients.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical evidence is not sufficiently rigorous or controlled to support the breadth of claims (limited/uneven comparisons to KD and fit-hints; underpowered ImageNet experiment; gradient-based AT confined to a fragile, non-standard setting without BN; inconsistent reporting and few statistical tests).
2) Numerous methodological, clarity, and presentation issues (missing and inconsistent details for reproducibility; ambiguous choices like normalization and β scheduling; unclear cost/overhead; substantial formatting and reference problems) undermine confidence despite an interesting idea.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement. The idea of transferring spatial attention is intuitive and potentially impactful. However, claims such as being “the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” are not convincingly substantiated (ImageNet experiment uses only two AT losses, no tuning, and KD is deemed ineffective under mismatched hyperparameters; this does not establish a strong comparative baseline). The relation to class-discriminative explanations (CAM/Grad-CAM) versus non-discriminative maps used here could be discussed more clearly.

- Methodological clarity.
  • Activation maps: attention is defined via channel reductions of |A| (sum, power-sum, max). The choice of using absolute value and the specific layer positions (pre/post-activation, pre/post-downsampling, pre/post-addition within ResNets) are not consistently specified. The normalization Q/||Q||2 used in the loss needs an epsilon for numerical stability; it is not mentioned.
  • Loss definition: Eq. (2) uses a p-norm on the difference of normalized maps; only p=2 is used, but the rationale for normalization and its effect is not analyzed beyond “important for success.”
  • β selection/scheduling: β is described as “103 divided by number of elements... and batch size” and “decayed during training” but no exact schedule or sensitivity analysis is provided. This is critical for reproducibility.
  • Gradient-based AT: Implementation requires second-order mixed partials and interacts non-trivially with batch normalization. The paper explicitly notes uncertainty on BN behavior and therefore restricts experiments to NIN without BN, turning off weight decay, and using deterministic single runs. This setting is atypical and limits the external validity of the results. Moreover, costs (time/memory) for double backprop and AT are not quantified.
  • Full activation transfer baseline: The FitNets-like baseline uses 1×1 convs to match shapes but details (where inserted, parameter counts, normalization, loss weights, schedules) and fairness (e.g., comparable added capacity and tuning) are insufficiently documented. The conclusion that attention maps are “more important” than full activations may be premature.

- Experimental rigor.
  • Reporting is inconsistent: CIFAR AT results sometimes report medians of 5 runs, while gradient-based AT uses single runs with fixed seeds; standard deviations/confidence intervals are largely absent.
  • KD comparisons: KD hyperparameters are tuned for CIFAR but not for ImageNet; negative KD results on ImageNet with mismatched hyperparameters are not informative. For CIFAR, AT+KD often helps, but systematic cross-validation is missing.
  • ImageNet: AT gains (≈+1.1% top-1) are promising but the setup uses only 2 AT losses, no hyperparameter tuning, and no multiple runs. KD is declared ineffective without thorough tuning or architecture-matching controls, and training was stopped early due to resources. Claims based on this are weak.
  • Finetuning (CUB/Scenes): Details such as the exact data preprocessing (bbox usage, crop protocol), which layers receive AT losses, batch sizes, learning rates, and the precise freezing of BN are only partially specified. The role of attention similarity is shown qualitatively; quantitative analyses are missing.
  • Ablations: Limited exploration of p, normalization choices (l1 vs l2), placement/number of AT losses, schedule for β, and sensitivity to teacher strength. Negative transfer scenarios are not studied.

- Presentation and formatting.
  • Numerous typos and grammatical issues.
  • Equation and notation inconsistencies (spacing, references).
  • Figures/tables are referenced but not clearly included; some appendix content looks corrupted (“videostore … dentaloffice”, “Table 5” not present).
  • References contain malformed entries (e.g., “\Q2014\E”), inconsistent venues, missing details, and nonstandard formatting.
  • Some claims rely on figures not present in the submission.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive)
- Provide a thorough, controlled comparison against KD and FitNets-style hints with matched training budgets, tuned hyperparameters on each dataset, and multiple seeds with error bars.
- Quantify the computational and memory overhead of AT (activation-based) and double backprop (gradient-based) and discuss practical tradeoffs.
- Clarify attention definitions: exact layer locations (pre/post-ReLU, pre/post-add for ResNets), normalization with epsilon, and the rationale for absolute value. Provide ablations over p, normalization (l1 vs l2), and layer selection.
- For gradient-based AT, resolve BN handling in double backprop (e.g., frozen statistics vs training behavior), and re-run on mainstream architectures with BN. Include more than one run and standard deviations.
- On ImageNet, include AT at all residual groups, tune β and schedules, run multiple seeds, and provide a fair, tuned KD baseline (including same-depth/arch teacher-student).
- Analyze when AT helps/hurts: vary teacher strength, architecture mismatch, and dataset size; check for negative transfer from suboptimal teacher attentions.
- Consider class-discriminative attention maps (e.g., Grad-CAM) versus the current non-discriminative maps; report results.
- Clean up formatting: fix references, insert all figures/tables, and correct typos.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you handle near-zero attention maps during l2 normalization (Q/||Q||2)? Do you add an epsilon? Did you observe instability?
2) Which exact tensors are used to compute attention in ResNets: pre-activation vs post-activation, and before vs after residual addition? Does the choice affect results?
3) Can you provide a sensitivity analysis for β and for the number/placement of AT losses across layers? What schedule works best?
4) What is the training time and peak memory overhead of activation-based AT and of gradient-based AT relative to baseline (on CIFAR and ImageNet)?
5) For gradient-based AT with BN, what is the correct and practical implementation? Have you tried freezing BN statistics during the second backprop?
6) On ImageNet, if you tune KD and match teacher/student depths/architectures, how does KD compare to AT and AT+KD? Please provide controlled results with multiple seeds.
7) Did you evaluate class-discriminative attention (e.g., Grad-CAM-based) for AT? If so, how does it compare to your activation-based maps?
8) In the “full activation transfer” baseline, how many 1×1 adapters were used and what were their parameter counts? Could the weak gains be due to insufficient tuning or capacity mismatch?
