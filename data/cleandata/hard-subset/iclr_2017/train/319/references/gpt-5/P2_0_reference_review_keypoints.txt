- Problem: Can attention maps from a trained CNN teacher be used to improve a student CNN by transferring spatial attention at multiple layers.
- Method (activation-based): Aggregate channel activations per spatial location (sum/max of |A|^p), L2-normalize maps, and penalize Lp distance between teacher and student maps across layers.
- Method (gradient-based): Use input gradients as attention maps, minimize distance via double backprop, and add a flip-invariance regularizer.
- Claims: AT improves performance across architectures/datasets; activation-based AT outperforms full-activation transfer; AT can complement KD; AT scales to ImageNet and fine-grained transfer.
- Evidence: CIFAR experiments (WRN/ResNet) with ablations, ImageNet ResNet-18 gains (+1.1% top-1, +0.8% top-5), transfer gains on CUB/Scenes, and available code.
- Decision: Accept.
- Accept rationale: Simple, general, well-motivated approach with strong empirical support for activation-based AT including non-trivial ImageNet and transfer improvements.
- Problem framing: Clearly articulated and practically relevant (defining attention for CNNs and testing alignment benefits).
- Literature placement: Good bridge between visualization (saliency/CAM) and distillation/hints (KD/FitNets), though some references and prior variants need fuller coverage.
- Activation-AT results: Consistent error reductions on CIFAR, layer-wise losses helpful, and meaningful ImageNet/fine-grained transfer gains; qualitative maps align better after AT.
- AT vs full-activation transfer: Spatial attention transfer yields larger gains and faster convergence than FitNets-style activation regression under comparable settings.
- KD vs AT: KD helps on CIFAR and can combine with AT; on ImageNet KD degraded under given hyperparameters while AT remained helpful, suggesting AT can be a robust alternative.
- Gradient-based AT findings: Double backprop and flip-invariance help on CIFAR NIN, gradient AT is viable but evaluated narrowly (single runs, no BN) and was outperformed by activation-based AT in experiments.
- Rigor/correctness: Loss formulations are standard and sound; attention normalization is important; experimental methodology is mostly solid but statistical confidence varies (some single-run results).
- Statistical reporting suggestion: Report means and standard deviations over multiple seeds for all CIFAR experiments and multiple runs for ImageNet if feasible.
- Compute/overhead suggestion: Quantify training time and memory cost of AT, especially double backprop and computing teacher attentions.
- Normalization/β sensitivity suggestion: Ablate effects of normalization choices (none/L1/L2) and sensitivity to β, p, and learning-rate schedules.
- Layer-selection suggestion: Explore attention losses at more residual groups on ImageNet to see which layers contribute most and whether denser placements help.
- KD interplay suggestion: Run KD hyperparameter sweeps (temperature, α) on ImageNet to test complementarity or failure modes with AT.
- Broader evaluation suggestion: Test on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization to validate improvements in spatial reasoning.
- Gradient-AT with BN suggestion: Clarify and experiment with BN behavior in gradient-based AT (fixed stats vs recompute) to resolve current unresolved points.
- Terminology/nit: Treat channel-aggregated activations as a pragmatic attention proxy and clean up references/figures for presentation clarity.
- Questions for authors to address: ablation of L2 vs none/L1 normalization; sensitivity to β and p and principled β setting; training-time/memory overheads for activation and gradient AT; whether ImageNet gains persist with losses at all residual groups and with label smoothing; whether KD can be tuned to help on ImageNet and why it may fail; robustness across seeds/augmentations/schedules; cross-architecture and cross-capacity transfer results.
