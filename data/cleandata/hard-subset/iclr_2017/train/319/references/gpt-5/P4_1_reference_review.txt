1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper studies whether “attention” in CNNs can be defined in a principled way and used as a transferable signal from a teacher to a student network. It proposes:
- Activation-based spatial attention maps: per-layer 2D maps formed by channel-wise statistics over absolute activations (e.g., sum |A|, sum |A|^p, max |A|^p). A loss matches l2-normalized teacher/student attention maps across selected layers.
- Gradient-based attention transfer: matching input-gradient maps via a double-backprop objective; also a flip-invariance regularizer on gradient attention.
- Empirical results showing consistent improvements across CIFAR-10 (WRN and NIN), finetuning on CUB and MIT Scenes with ResNet-18/34, and ImageNet (ResNet-34→ResNet-18), with activation-based attention transfer (AT) outperforming full activation regression and complementing knowledge distillation (KD). Code is available.

The work is motivated by the hypothesis that spatial attention encodes where networks “look,” and that aligning these patterns transfers useful inductive bias while being cheaper and more flexible than matching full feature tensors.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and simple knowledge transfer mechanism that is well-motivated, broadly applicable, and complementary to KD, with consistent accuracy gains including on ImageNet.
- Technically sound formulation for activation-based AT with clear design choices (normalization, layer selection) and ablations; empirical evidence supports core claims.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
- What problem is tackled? Defining attention for CNNs (activation- and gradient-based spatial maps) and testing whether aligning student attention with a stronger teacher improves performance across architectures and datasets.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It differentiates from prior transfer methods (FitNets, KD) by distilling spatial focus instead of full activations or softened logits. It connects to visualization methods (Grad-CAM, guided backprop, CAM) and to double backprop; the flip-invariance term relates to equivariance. The paper positions itself clearly and explains why attention maps may transfer more essential, compressible information than full features.
- Do the results support the claims with rigor? Largely for activation-based AT:
  - CIFAR-10: Multiple teacher–student pairs show consistent gains; AT > full activation transfer; AT combines well with KD; ablations indicate sum-based maps outperform max-based; using multiple layers helps.
  - Transfer learning (CUB, Scenes): AT materially narrows the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34; on CUB AT outperforms KD, reinforcing the value of mid/high-level spatial attention in fine-grained tasks.
  - ImageNet: ResNet-18 gains ~1.1% top-1 without heavy tuning using only two attention losses, notable given the difficulty of effective KD on ImageNet in prior work.
For gradient-based AT, results are promising but less mature: small NIN/CIFAR experiments show benefits of gradient matching and symmetry regularization, and parity or better than KD in that restricted setting. The paper candidly notes open issues around batch normalization and second derivatives.

Limitations and concerns:
- Statistical rigor: Some CIFAR results are medians over runs; other parts (especially gradient-based AT) are single-run with fixed seeds; confidence intervals are not reported.
- Gradient-based AT is evaluated only in a constrained setup without batch norm and with limited augmentation; practical recipes for modern architectures are not yet demonstrated.
- The comparison to KD on ImageNet is not exhaustively tuned; the claim of being the first successful transfer method on ImageNet is plausible but would benefit from a broader survey and stronger baselines (e.g., tuned KD, FitNets variants).
- Clarity/formatting: Minor typos and reference formatting issues; however, the core method and losses are clearly specified.

Overall, the activation-based AT method is technically sound, well motivated, and empirically validated on diverse settings, including a strong ImageNet result. The gradient-based component is a valuable but preliminary extension.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all affecting the decision)
- Rigor and reporting:
  - Provide means/std or confidence intervals across multiple seeds for key results (CIFAR, CUB/Scenes, ImageNet).
  - Report training cost overhead (time, memory) for AT vs baseline (and for gradient-based AT vs baseline) and any inference-time costs (should be none).
  - Detail β schedules, normalization choices, attention layers used, and sensitivity analyses.
- Ablations:
  - Sensitivity to the attention mapping (p in sum |A|^p), and to l1 vs l2 normalization. Include an ablation showing the necessity of normalization claimed in the text.
  - Vary which layers receive attention losses; quantify how many layers and where matter most for different tasks.
  - Compare to matching spatially pooled feature norms or feature Gram matrices to separate “attention” from other regularizers.
  - For gradient-based AT, compare to matching Grad-CAM maps or other class-discriminative gradient-derived maps.
- Comparisons:
  - On ImageNet, include a carefully tuned KD baseline and, if feasible, a FitNets-style hint baseline for completeness.
  - Include cross-architecture transfers (e.g., Inception→ResNet) to test robustness.
- Theory/interpretability:
  - Provide intuition or analysis of why attention normalization is crucial and why sum-based maps outperform max-based.
  - Evaluate whether AT improves robustness (e.g., to occlusions or corruptions) and whether student attention becomes more class-discriminative.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of normalization (l1 vs l2) and to the exponent p in sum |A|^p across datasets and architectures?
- What is the training overhead (compute/memory) for activation-based AT and for gradient-based AT? Can you quantify scaling with number of attention layers?
- Does AT still help when teacher and student architectures are very different or when the teacher is only marginally better than the student?
- On ImageNet, did you tune KD temperature/α for the ResNet-34→ResNet-18 pair? Could the negative KD result be due to suboptimal hyperparameters?
- How do you handle batch normalization in gradient-based AT during the second backprop pass? Have you verified a practical recipe that works with BN on modern nets?
- Does AT improve calibration or robustness (e.g., under occlusion tests or common corruption benchmarks)? Do student attention maps become closer to teacher maps in class-conditional ways (e.g., via Grad-CAM qualitative/quantitative metrics)?
- Have you evaluated using class-conditional attention maps (e.g., Grad-CAM) for transfer instead of input-gradient magnitude?
