Summary of contributions
- The paper studies whether “attention” information from a teacher CNN can be used to improve a student CNN. It operationalizes attention as spatial maps derived from internal activations or from input gradients.
- It proposes activation-based attention maps computed by simple channel-wise statistics over absolute activations (sum, sum of powers, max of powers) and transfers them by minimizing the distance between l2-normalized teacher and student attention maps at multiple layers.
- It proposes gradient-based attention transfer, matching the teacher’s and student’s gradients of the loss w.r.t. input via a double-backprop objective, and adds a flip-invariance regularizer on input gradients.
- Empirically, the method yields consistent accuracy gains over strong baselines across CIFAR-10 (WRNs and NIN), transfer learning on CUB and MIT Scenes (ResNet-18/34), and ImageNet (ResNet-18 with ResNet-34 teacher). Activation-based AT outperforms transferring full activations and complements knowledge distillation (KD). Code is released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A simple, well-motivated, and broadly applicable mechanism for knowledge transfer that consistently improves performance, including on ImageNet. (2) Technically sound formulation with ablations (attention definitions, placement, normalization) demonstrating the importance of design choices and complementarity to KD.

Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
1) Problem tackled: The paper asks how to define “attention” in CNNs and whether transferring this attention from a teacher can improve a student’s performance. It focuses on spatial attention maps that capture where the network focuses across layers.

2) Motivation and placement in literature: The approach is well motivated by prior visualization methods (saliency, CAM/Grad-CAM, deconvs) and knowledge transfer/distillation (KD, FitNets). The novelty lies in using spatial attention maps—rather than full activations or softened logits—as the medium of transfer, encouraging the student to focus on similar regions. The gradient-based variant connects to double backprop regularization and proposes a practical teacher-student matching objective. The paper contrasts AT with KD and full-activation transfer, showing complementarity or superiority depending on the setting.

3) Support for claims and rigor:
- Methodological soundness: The activation-based loss matches l2-normalized attention vectors, removing scale sensitivity and making matching well-posed; multi-layer placement is discussed for same/different-depth settings. The gradient-based objective and the flip-invariance regularizer are correctly derived and feasible with autodiff.
- Empirical support: On CIFAR-10, AT improves WRN student test error (e.g., WRN-16-1 student with WRN-16-2 teacher: 8.77 to 7.93 with F^2_sum). AT+KD further helps in some settings. Sum-based mappings outperform max-based, and placing losses at multiple groups helps. AT improves over FitNets-style full activation regression. Gradient-based AT and related norms also help, with activation-based AT strongest in the reported setup.
- On CUB and MIT Scenes, AT narrows the ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap. On ImageNet, AT yields +1.1% top-1 on ResNet-18 with only two attention losses and no tuning; KD did not help in these settings, aligning with reports that KD can be sensitive across architectures/depths. The claim that AT is among the first successful transfer methods on ImageNet is plausible given the reported difficulties with KD, though it would benefit from precise framing.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details and code link are provided.

Limitations and caveats:
- Gradient-based AT is demonstrated in a constrained setting (no batch norm) due to second-backprop complexity; generality to modern architectures with BN is discussed but not shown.
- Statistical rigor is mixed: some medians over 5 runs are reported, while other results (especially gradient-based) are single runs. ImageNet results are promising but limited (partial layer placement, no hyperparameter tuning, no multiple seeds).
- Comparisons focus on KD and full-activation transfer; broader comparisons to other representation-transfer methods are limited.
- Computational overhead of gradient-based AT (memory/time) is not quantified.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Clarify and tighten the positioning relative to prior transfer methods using intermediate features (e.g., FitNets/hints), and better articulate why spatial attention is a more effective inductive bias than full activation regression.
- Provide a sensitivity study for β, normalization choice (l1 vs l2), p in F^p_sum, and the number/placement of attention losses, especially on ImageNet.
- Quantify the computational and memory overhead of AT, particularly for gradient-based AT (double backprop), and report training-time multipliers.
- Strengthen statistical rigor: report means/std or confidence intervals over multiple seeds for key results (CIFAR, CUB/Scenes, ImageNet).
- For gradient-based AT, include experiments with batch normalization (e.g., using ghost BN or frozen BN during second backprop) and discuss stability.
- Temper or precisely qualify the claim regarding being first to achieve successful transfer on ImageNet; include a comprehensive survey of contemporaneous results if available.
- Improve reference formatting and minor typos; ensure all figures referenced are legible and self-contained with captions explaining attention maps.

Questions to authors
1) Normalization: You use l2 normalization of attention vectors. Did you try l1 or softmax normalization, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
2) Attention mapping hyperparameters: How does performance vary with p in F^p_sum across datasets/architectures? Is there a principled way to set p?
3) Placement: For ResNets on ImageNet, what is the gain from adding attention losses to all four groups vs only the last two? Any diminishing returns?
4) Beta selection: You scale β by map size and batch size. Please provide a sensitivity plot and practical guidance for choosing β across tasks.
5) Gradient-based AT with BN: Can you share results or best practices for handling batch normalization in the second backprop? Does freezing BN statistics suffice?
6) Compute/memory cost: What are the wall-clock and memory overheads of activation-based AT and gradient-based AT, relative to baseline training and KD?
7) KD interplay: On CIFAR you show AT+KD is beneficial. On ImageNet, did you try varying KD temperature/α or matching teacher features/logits at intermediate layers?
8) Robustness/interpretability: Do AT-trained students exhibit more class-discriminative saliency (e.g., Grad-CAM) or improved robustness to occlusions/perturbations?
9) Teacher choice: How sensitive is AT to teacher quality and architecture mismatch? Does a much stronger teacher always help, or can overly peaky attention hurt?
10) Beyond classification: Have you tried AT on detection/segmentation or weakly supervised localization, where spatial attention seems especially pertinent?
