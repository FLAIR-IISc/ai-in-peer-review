Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a form of knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. The key idea is to define spatial attention maps at intermediate layers, and to train the student so that its attention maps match those of the teacher. Two attention definitions are explored:
- Activation-based: reduce a C×H×W feature tensor to an H×W spatial map by aggregating absolute activations across channels (sum, sum of powers, max of powers), with l2 normalization and an l2 matching loss across aligned layers.
- Gradient-based: define attention as input sensitivity (∂L/∂x) and match student and teacher input gradients using a “double backprop” objective. A flip-invariance regularizer on gradient attention is also proposed.

The authors show:
- Consistent gains on CIFAR with Wide-ResNets and NIN, with AT outperforming direct full-activation regression (FitNets-style). Combining AT with knowledge distillation (KD) can further help on CIFAR.
- Transfer learning on CUB and MIT Scenes where AT narrows the ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34 gap, sometimes outperforming KD.
- On ImageNet, AT from ResNet-34 to ResNet-18 yields +1.1% top-1 and +0.8% top-5 without extensive tuning.
- Early results for gradient-based AT and symmetry regularization on CIFAR-NIN.

Decision: Weak Accept
Primary reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated idea that bridges interpretability (attention maps) and knowledge transfer, with clear empirical benefits across architectures and datasets (including large-scale ImageNet).
- The activation-based formulation is practical, computationally light, and consistently outperforms full activation regression, indicating the spatial focus is the key transferable signal.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: defining spatial “where to look” signals from hidden activations is intuitive and connects to extensive attention visualization literature (CAM, Grad-CAM, guided backprop).
- Methodological simplicity and generality: a small auxiliary loss on normalized attention maps at a few layers is easy to implement and incurs little overhead (especially compared to full-activation matching).
- Empirical breadth: results shown on CIFAR, fine-grained CUB, indoor Scenes, and ImageNet; across residual and non-residual backbones; and with both same-depth and different-depth teacher-student pairs.
- Useful ablations: aggregation choices (sum vs max), per-group vs all groups, comparison to full-activation regression; normalization importance is highlighted.
- Gradient-based AT and flip-invariance regularization are interesting, principled additions; even simple gradient-norm regularization helps.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Gradient-based AT is under-explored: only on CIFAR with NIN, single-seed, no BN, weight decay off; the practicality with BN and modern training is acknowledged as open. This currently reads as a promising but preliminary branch.
- Limited statistical rigor: several results appear to be single runs (except a table reporting medians for one ablation), with no confidence intervals. The ImageNet gain (+1.1% top-1) is promising but needs multiple seeds and stronger baselines to rule out tuning variance.
- Incomplete baselines on large-scale: KD is reported to struggle on ImageNet with the chosen settings, but the space of KD temperatures, loss weights, and teacher-student compatibility is large; stronger KD or FitNets baselines on ImageNet would better contextualize AT’s advantages.
- Missing implementation details critical for reproducibility: exact layer pairing schemes, β schedules, loss weights when combining AT with KD, temperature values per setup, and computational overhead measurements.
- Some claims are broad (e.g., first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet) and should be softened or supported with stronger evidence and literature coverage.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (non-blocking)
- Report multiple runs with mean ± std for CIFAR and at least two seeds for ImageNet/CUB/Scenes. Include training curves and final accuracy distributions to substantiate stability claims.
- Provide full tables for all experiments in the main text or appendix (some referenced tables/figures are not visible here). Include exact student/teacher configs, layer indices used for AT, attention map resolutions, β per layer, and the schedule when combined with KD.
- Compute and report overhead: wall-clock and memory for AT vs KD vs FitNets; teacher forward reuse; cost of gradient-based AT (second backprop) relative to baseline.
- Deepen ablations: vary the exponent p per layer; L1 vs L2 normalization vs cosine loss; per-channel normalization before aggregation; location of AT losses (all blocks vs subset); different widths and depths; and effect of attention map resolution (e.g., low-res vs high-res layers).
- Strengthen baselines: tuned KD on ImageNet; FitNets with well-chosen hint layers; other regularizers (Jacobian norm, Sobolev training). Consider class-conditional AT (matching attention for the predicted/target class using CAM/Grad-CAM).
- Quantify attention similarity improvements (e.g., correlation metrics between student and teacher attention over the dataset) and relate them to accuracy gains.
- Explore additional invariances (rotation, scale) in the symmetry loss, and discuss its interaction with data augmentation.

Questions for the authors
1) Layer pairing and normalization: How sensitive is performance to which layers are used for AT and to the choice of L2 normalization vs cosine similarity? Did you try per-spatial-location normalization or per-feature-map normalization before aggregation?
2) β scheduling: What schedules were used across datasets and when combining with KD? How robust is AT to β choice?
3) Computational cost: What is the training time and memory overhead of activation-based AT and of gradient-based AT relative to baseline and KD, on CIFAR and ImageNet?
4) KD on ImageNet: Which temperatures and loss weights were attempted? Did you try same-architecture teachers to control for architecture mismatch issues commonly cited with KD?
5) Gradient-based AT with BN: Can you clarify the exact autodiff strategy you consider feasible (e.g., freezing BN stats and parameters in the second pass)? Any preliminary results with modern BN training?
6) Robustness and generalization: Does AT improve sample efficiency or robustness (corruptions, occlusions)? Does the symmetry loss improve robustness to flips beyond accuracy?
7) Attention semantics: Have you quantified that student attention becomes more class-discriminative (e.g., using Grad-CAM overlap with ground-truth regions) after AT, and whether that correlates with the observed gains?

Overall, this is a timely, simple, and useful idea with broad potential impact. With strengthened experimental rigor and details, it would make a solid contribution.
