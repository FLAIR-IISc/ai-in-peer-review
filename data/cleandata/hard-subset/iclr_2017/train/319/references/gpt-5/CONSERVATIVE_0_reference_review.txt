Summary
The paper proposes “attention transfer” (AT) as a form of knowledge transfer from a teacher CNN to a student CNN. Attention is operationalized as spatial maps derived either from hidden activations (channel-wise reductions of absolute activations, with l2 normalization) or from input gradients (saliency). The method adds losses that encourage the student’s attention maps to match the teacher’s at selected layers. The authors claim: (i) activation- and gradient-based AT are effective regularizers that improve student accuracy across architectures and datasets; (ii) activation-based AT outperforms full activation transfer and can complement knowledge distillation (KD); and (iii) AT yields consistent gains on CIFAR and noticeable improvements on transfer learning tasks and even ImageNet.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical evidence, while suggestive, lacks the rigor and breadth expected for a new training paradigm: limited reporting of numbers, few runs, weak baselines in some settings, and insufficient ablations and sensitivity analyses. (2) The gradient-based AT component is underdeveloped and evaluated in a constrained setting that undermines the strength of the overall claims.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement. The idea of transferring “where” a network attends is intuitive and related to established visualization work (saliency, CAM/Grad-CAM) and hint-based training (FitNets). The motivation is reasonable and the activation-based construction is simple. However, the positioning w.r.t. existing knowledge transfer methods is incomplete. Beyond KD and a basic full-activation regression baseline, the paper does not engage with stronger representation-matching baselines used in the literature (e.g., multi-layer feature regression with learned adapters), nor does it clearly disentangle whether the gains stem from attention per se versus generic feature-map regularization.

- Methodological clarity. The activation-based AT loss is clearly specified and easy to implement; the importance of l2 normalization is noted. The gradient-based AT requires second-order mixed partials; the paper acknowledges practical complications with batch norm but does not resolve them. Given the claimed generality, the gradient-based component reads more like a preliminary exploration than a validated method.

- Empirical evaluation.
  • CIFAR: The activation-based AT results are promising, but reporting is thin. Table 2 shows a single teacher/student pair with medians over 5 runs; other experiments appear to be single runs. There is no variance reporting, limited sensitivity to β, p, layer selection, and no CIFAR-100 or SVHN. The comparison to full activation transfer is helpful but minimal (no feature-adapter exploration beyond 1×1). The “each group contributes” statement is qualitative with small deltas and no error bars.
  • Gradient-based AT: Experiments are on a thin NIN without batch norm, single-seed, without random cropping, and even require turning off weight decay. This setting is atypical and weak. Improvements over the baseline and double backprop are small and may not generalize. This section does not provide convincing evidence for gradient-based AT.
  • Transfer learning (CUB/Scenes): The qualitative statement that AT “closes the gap” is interesting, but exact numbers are missing from the main text; robustness (seeds), sensitivity to which layers are used, and comparisons to KD/feature matching are not adequately reported.
  • ImageNet: The reported +1.1% top-1 on ResNet-18 using ResNet-34 teacher is potentially impactful. However, the setup is under-specified (exact baseline and AT accuracies, training schedule, compute budget, number of runs, sensitivity to β/layers). The claim that KD “hurts” is not conclusive without tuning and fair treatment. The strong claim that this is the “first” successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet is not substantiated and should be toned down and supported with citations.

- Scientific rigor. Several claims (e.g., “attention maps correlate with stronger networks,” “mid-level attention focuses on discriminative parts”) are asserted qualitatively with figures, but there is no quantitative evaluation (e.g., pointing-game or localization metrics, correlation analyses across accuracy tiers). Without such measurements, it is hard to conclude that “attention” as constructed is the causal driver of performance improvements rather than a proxy for feature magnitude patterns.

- Reproducibility. Code availability is positive. Yet crucial training details, hyperparameters, and full result tables are missing from the main text, and many experiments are single-run. For a method that adds nontrivial losses, stability and sensitivity analyses are important.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide complete, quantitative results for all experiments in the main paper: mean ± std over multiple seeds, exact top-1/top-5 numbers, and training curves.
- Strengthen baselines: (i) multi-layer feature regression with learned 1×1 adapters at the same layers used for AT; (ii) tuned KD with temperature/α sweeps and matching teacher-student architectures; (iii) stronger modern distillation baselines if applicable. Ensure compute and data augmentations are matched.
- Expand datasets and settings: at least CIFAR-100, SVHN, and additional teacher/student pairs (wider/narrower, same/different depths). For transfer learning, include more tasks and report full numbers.
- Ablations: sensitivity to β, p, normalization type, number and placement of AT layers, attention resolution, and interpolation choices. Report wall-clock and memory overhead for teacher attention computation.
- Quantify “attention quality”: use established localization metrics (e.g., pointing game, GT overlap) to substantiate claims about discriminative focus and its relation to accuracy.
- Clarify gradient-based AT: resolve batch norm handling, evaluate on standard training regimes, and compare fairly to double backprop and Jacobian regularization baselines. If preliminary, consider moving to appendix and focusing the main story on activation-based AT.
- Temper or justify the “first on ImageNet” claim, and provide citations and a careful survey of related distillation on large-scale settings.
- Clean up references and improve figure/table legibility; include all key numbers in the main text.

Questions for the authors
1) What are the exact top-1/top-5 accuracies (mean ± std over ≥3 seeds) for all CIFAR, CUB/Scenes, and ImageNet experiments, including baselines, AT, AT+KD, KD, and full-activation transfer?
2) How sensitive are results to β, the choice of p, normalization (l1 vs l2), number/placement of AT layers, and attention map interpolation? Please include sweeps.
3) How much training-time and memory overhead does AT add for CIFAR and ImageNet? Is the teacher kept in training mode or eval mode (BN stats) during AT?
4) Can you provide a fair, tuned KD baseline on ImageNet (with temperature/α sweeps) and a feature-regression baseline at the same layers used for AT?
5) For gradient-based AT, how do you handle batch normalization in the second backprop? Can you demonstrate results in a standard BN-enabled setting?
6) Can you quantify “attention quality” (e.g., pointing game or CAM overlap) and correlate it with performance improvements when using AT?
7) Does AT still help when teacher and student have the same architecture and similar capacity (i.e., removing capacity confounds)?
