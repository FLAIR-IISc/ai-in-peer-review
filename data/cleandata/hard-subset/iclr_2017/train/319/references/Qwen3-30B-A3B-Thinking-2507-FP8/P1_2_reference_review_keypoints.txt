- Proposes transferring neural network attention (activation- and gradient-based) instead of only outputs or full activations to improve student performance.
- Defines activation-based attention (hidden activation statistics) and gradient-based attention (input sensitivity maps) with experimental validation.
- Shows activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer across datasets and architectures.
- Demonstrates experiments on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet using ResNet, NIN, and WRN models.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — method is well-motivated, validated, and shows consistent improvements.
- Strength: Novel focus on attention patterns fills a gap in knowledge transfer literature.
- Strength: Rigorous, comprehensive experiments support the claims.
- Strength: Clear technical definitions and implementation details for both attention methods.
- Strength: Practical significance claimed via successful ImageNet application.
- Weakness: Gradient-based attention transfer is underexplored and tested only on small networks without batch normalization.
- Feedback: Clarify and substantiate the claim that this is the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet with more literature comparison.
- Feedback: Expand experiments or discussion for gradient-based attention, or clearly state activation-based AT is the main contribution.
- Feedback: Provide a theoretical or intuitive explanation for why attention maps transfer better than full activations.
- Feedback: Explain the role and necessity of l2 normalization before transfer losses.
- Question: Provide comprehensive comparisons with prior methods (FitNets, KD) on ImageNet to support the ImageNet claim.
- Question: How do you distinguish whether stronger attention maps indicate causation of better performance versus mere correlation?
- Question: Give specific examples of how attention patterns differ across layers (low-level vs. high-level) and relate to decisions.
- Question: Clarify whether gradient-based AT was tested or works on ImageNet, or if ImageNet results are only for activation-based transfer.
