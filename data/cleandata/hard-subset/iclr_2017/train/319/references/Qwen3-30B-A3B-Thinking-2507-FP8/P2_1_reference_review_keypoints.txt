- Proposes attention as a transferable signal for knowledge transfer with attention defined as spatial maps of network focus.  
- Introduces two attention map types: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Shows transferring attention maps yields significant performance gains across datasets and architectures, including ImageNet.  
- Activation-based attention transfer (AT) outperforms full-activation transfer (FitNets-style) and can be combined with knowledge distillation.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Novelty: highlights that attention maps contain valuable information beyond final predictions.  
- Well-motivated paper connecting human attention to artificial attention, with thorough related work.  
- Strong experimental evidence across CIFAR-10/100, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NiN).  
- Ablation studies convincingly show AT superiority and that sum-based attention mappings work better than max-based.  
- Claimed as the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet with a reported 1.1% top-1 improvement.  
- Paper is clear, well-structured, with helpful visualizations and reproducible methodological details.  
- Revision request: add statistical significance (p-values or confidence intervals) for reported improvements.  
- Revision request: provide more comprehensive comparisons to other knowledge distillation methods on ImageNet.  
- Revision request: discuss gradient-based AT limitations (e.g., batch-norm and second backpropagation) and analyze when it helps.  
- Revision request: quantify computational cost (training time/memory) relative to standard knowledge distillation.  
- Questions for authors: comparisons to recent KD techniques on ImageNet; why KD struggles on ImageNet and how AT overcomes it; effectiveness when transferring between different architecture families.
