

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer approach using attention maps from a teacher network to improve the performance of a student network. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps—activation-based and gradient-based—and demonstrate how these maps contain valuable information that can be transferred to improve student network performance across multiple datasets and architectures. The key contributions include: (1) proposing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) introducing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing significant performance improvements across various datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

The paper makes a strong case for attention as a more effective knowledge transfer mechanism than traditional methods, with consistent improvements across CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, and ImageNet datasets. The authors provide thorough experimental validation, including ablation studies on different attention mapping functions and a comparison with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

My key reasons for this recommendation are:
1. The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer that addresses a clear gap in the literature.
2. The experimental results are thorough, consistent, and demonstrate significant improvements over baselines across multiple datasets and architectures.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for attention-based knowledge transfer. The motivation is well-founded, connecting artificial attention to human attention mechanisms and identifying a gap in the literature regarding how attention information can be leveraged for knowledge transfer. The authors properly situate their work within the existing literature on attention mechanisms (both in human cognition and neural networks), knowledge distillation, and attention visualization techniques.

The experimental validation is strong and comprehensive. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet), architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), and transfer scenarios (same depth, different depth). Their ablation studies on attention mapping functions (Fsum, F²sum, F⁴sum, F¹max) and comparison with full activation transfer provide valuable insights into the mechanism.

The claim that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer (as shown in Table 1 and Figure 7b) is particularly compelling. The paper demonstrates that attention maps contain more discriminative information for transfer than full activation tensors, which challenges some assumptions in the literature about knowledge transfer.

However, I have some concerns about the scientific rigor that need to be addressed in revisions:
- The claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is not sufficiently supported. The authors should provide a more comprehensive literature review to verify this claim.
- The experimental setup for gradient-based attention transfer on CIFAR omits batch normalization, which is standard practice in modern CNNs. The authors should justify this choice or repeat experiments with batch normalization.
- The paper mentions that "on ImageNet though KD converges significantly slower" but doesn't provide sufficient details about the training setup or hyperparameters to understand this observation.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following revisions:

1. **Clarify the ImageNet claim**: Provide a more comprehensive literature review to verify that attention transfer is indeed the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet. If other methods exist, acknowledge them and explain why attention transfer is different or more effective.

2. **Include gradient-based attention results with batch normalization**: The authors should repeat the gradient-based attention experiments with batch normalization to make the results more applicable to modern CNN practice. This would also address the concern about the "unusual" experimental setup.

3. **Expand the analysis of why attention transfer works better**: The paper hints at why attention maps might contain more valuable information than full activations (e.g., "attention maps focus on different parts for different layers"), but doesn't provide a thorough explanation. A more detailed analysis would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

4. **Provide more computational cost analysis**: The paper mentions that "attention transfer adds very little computational cost," but doesn't quantify this. Including computational time comparisons would be valuable for practitioners.

5. **Clarify the relationship to other attention visualization techniques**: The paper mentions Grad-CAM and other attention visualization methods, but doesn't clearly explain how their attention transfer approach differs from or relates to these techniques.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide evidence for your claim that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet"? Are there other knowledge transfer methods that have been applied on ImageNet that you're not aware of?

2. Why did you choose not to use batch normalization in the gradient-based attention transfer experiments on CIFAR? How would the results change if batch normalization were included?

3. The paper states that "on ImageNet though KD converges significantly slower" but doesn't provide enough details about the training setup. Can you share more details about the hyperparameters used for KD on ImageNet compared to CIFAR?

4. The paper claims that attention maps contain more valuable information than full activations, but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why this is the case. Can you provide more insight into what specific information in attention maps is beneficial for transfer?

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution that makes a meaningful advance in knowledge transfer. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent contribution to the ICLR community.
