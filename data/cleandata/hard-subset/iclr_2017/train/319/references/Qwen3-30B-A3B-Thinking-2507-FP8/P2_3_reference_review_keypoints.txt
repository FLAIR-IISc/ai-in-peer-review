- Proposes attention transfer (activation- and gradient-based attention maps) as a novel mechanism for knowledge distillation.  
- Claims attention maps contain richer transferable information than class probabilities or full activations, with consistent improvements across datasets and architectures.  
- Demonstrates first successful application of activation-based attention transfer on ImageNet (ResNet-34 → ResNet-18, +1.1% top-1).  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Strong empirical evaluation across CIFAR-10, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet and multiple architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN).  
- Evidence that attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and prior distillation methods.  
- Paper situates work well within literature on human and model attention and knowledge distillation.  
- Suggest clarifying the conceptual relationship and interaction between attention transfer and traditional knowledge distillation.  
- Request deeper analysis of why attention maps outperform full activations and which attention information is most valuable.  
- Ask for discussion/comparison of computational overhead, especially double backprop for gradient-based attention.  
- Recommend a systematic layer-specific analysis to show how different layers' attention contributes to transfer.  
- Q1: Why does F^2sum (sum of squared activations) work best and is this consistent across datasets?  
- Q2: Why does attention transfer benefit fine-grained recognition (CUB) more than other tasks?  
- Q3: Please provide concrete examples or visualizations contrasting activation-based vs full-activation transfer.  
- Q4: Why did standard KD fail on ImageNet while attention transfer succeeded—what causes this difference?  
- Q5: How can attention transfer be applied in resource-constrained real-world scenarios given gradient-based costs?
