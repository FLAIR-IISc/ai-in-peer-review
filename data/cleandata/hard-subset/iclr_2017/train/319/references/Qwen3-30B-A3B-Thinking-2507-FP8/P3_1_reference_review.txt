

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps. The authors propose two methods for defining spatial attention maps—activation-based (using statistics of hidden neuron activations) and gradient-based (using input sensitivity maps)—and demonstrate how these can be transferred from a teacher network to a student network to improve performance. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) establishing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism, (2) proposing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing significant performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer.

The paper is well-motivated by psychological theories of attention and builds on existing work in attention visualization and knowledge distillation. The experimental results across CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet demonstrate consistent improvements, particularly with activation-based attention transfer, which the authors show can be combined with knowledge distillation for further gains.

## 2. Decision

**Recommendation: Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer that shows significant empirical improvements across multiple datasets.
2. The distinction between attention-based and full-activation transfer is well-justified with compelling experimental results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—using attention maps rather than full activations for knowledge transfer—represents a meaningful advancement in the field. The authors provide a thorough exploration of different attention mapping functions (sum, sum-of-powers, max) and demonstrate that attention maps contain more useful information for transfer than full activations. The experimental results (Tables 1-3) are convincing, showing consistent improvements across different network architectures and datasets.

The connection to psychological attention mechanisms is well-justified and provides a strong theoretical foundation. The paper's claim that "attention maps contain valuable information that can be used for significantly improving the performance" is well-supported by the experiments, particularly in the CIFAR experiments where attention transfer consistently outperforms full-activation transfer.

The paper's claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is problematic. Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) has been successfully applied to ImageNet, and there are other knowledge transfer methods like FitNets that have been applied to ImageNet. This claim should be revised to avoid misleading readers.

The gradient-based attention transfer section feels less developed than the activation-based approach. The implementation details for gradient-based transfer (especially regarding batch normalization in second backpropagation) are not fully addressed, and the results in Table 3 are less impressive than the activation-based results. The authors should either provide more justification for why gradient-based attention is less effective or focus more on the stronger activation-based approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the ImageNet claim**: The statement about being the "first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet" is inaccurate and should be removed or substantially revised to reflect the actual literature.

2. **Theoretical justification**: While the experiments show attention transfer works better than full activation transfer, the paper lacks a theoretical explanation for *why* attention maps contain more useful information. A brief discussion of the information-theoretic properties of attention maps versus full activations would strengthen the paper.

3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: The paper mentions using "β usually varies about 0.1" but doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to this hyperparameter. A brief sensitivity analysis would be valuable.

4. **Implementation details**: The paper states "we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" for ImageNet experiments. This is concerning for a high-impact venue like ICLR. The authors should either report more thorough hyperparameter tuning or acknowledge this limitation.

5. **Comparison to related work**: The paper mentions "FitNets-style hints" but doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from FitNets (which also transfers intermediate features). A more detailed comparison would help position their contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer, but doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations. Can you elaborate on this?

2. The paper states that "gradient-based attention transfer leads to improved performance," but the results in Table 3 show only modest improvements (11.2% error vs. 12.1% for activation-based AT). Why is the gradient-based approach less effective, and what architectural changes might improve it?

3. The claim about being the "first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet" is problematic. What specific knowledge transfer methods have been applied to ImageNet in the literature, and how does your method differ from them?

4. For the gradient-based attention transfer, how do you plan to handle batch normalization during the second backpropagation step? This appears to be a critical issue for practical implementation.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms in neural networks. With minor revisions to address the points above, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
