

# Peer Review for "Attention Transfer"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention mechanisms. The authors propose two types of attention maps—activation-based and gradient-based—that encode where a network focuses its attention when making predictions. They demonstrate that transferring these attention maps from a teacher network to a student network significantly improves the student's performance on various vision tasks. The key contributions include:

- Proposing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer between networks
- Introducing activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps
- Showing consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network)
- Demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation

The paper provides a comprehensive literature review, thorough experimental analysis, and clear explanations of their methods. The results are impressive, with consistent improvements across different settings, and the authors provide insightful visualizations of attention maps to support their claims.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.

**Key reasons:**
1. The paper addresses an important and timely problem in deep learning—knowledge transfer between networks—with a novel perspective on how attention mechanisms can be leveraged for this purpose.
2. The experimental results are robust, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, which strongly support the authors' claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for attention transfer as a powerful knowledge transfer technique. The motivation is well-founded: the authors correctly identify that previous knowledge distillation approaches have largely focused on transferring feature representations without considering the attention mechanisms that guide those representations. They also correctly note that deeper networks don't always provide better representations, and that the focus should shift to more efficient transfer methods.

The experimental results are particularly convincing. For instance, in Table 3, the authors show that their activation-based attention transfer method achieves 11.2% test error on CIFAR-10, compared to 13.5% for the baseline. This represents a significant improvement of over 2% in accuracy. The paper also provides an insightful comparison between attention transfer and full-activation transfer (Table 1), showing that attention transfer provides substantially better results than transferring full activation tensors.

The paper's approach is well-placed in the literature. The authors correctly situate their work within the context of knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015), attention mechanisms in vision tasks (Xu et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2015), and attention visualization techniques (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Zhou et al., 2016). They also correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches, such as the assumption that deeper networks always learn better representations.

The paper provides sufficient evidence to support their claims. The experimental section is thorough, with multiple datasets, multiple architectures, and multiple ablation studies. The authors show that attention transfer works even when the teacher and student have different depths, and they demonstrate that activation-based attention transfer outperforms gradient-based attention transfer in most cases.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas that could be improved:

1. **Theoretical justification for attention maps**: The paper assumes that activation magnitudes correlate with attention importance, but provides limited theoretical justification. A brief theoretical discussion explaining why activation magnitudes might serve as good attention indicators would strengthen the foundation of their approach.

2. **Comparison with recent attention-based methods**: The paper mentions attention mechanisms in the context of neural machine translation and image captioning, but doesn't compare with more recent attention-based methods in computer vision (e.g., Vision Transformers). A brief comparison would strengthen the context of the work.

3. **Computational cost analysis**: While the paper mentions that attention transfer adds "very little computational cost," a more concrete analysis of the computational overhead, including training time comparisons between attention transfer and standard knowledge distillation, would be valuable.

4. **Ablation studies on attention map normalization**: The paper uses l2-normalized attention maps but doesn't provide ablation studies on why l2 normalization works better than other normalization methods. A small study comparing different normalization techniques would strengthen this aspect.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Table 2, the authors report that F^2sum performs best among the attention mapping functions. However, they don't provide a clear explanation for why the squared sum works better than other functions. Could the authors elaborate on this observation and provide more theoretical insights?

2. The paper states that gradient-based attention transfer requires "second backpropagation" which has "approximately the same cost as first backpropagation." Could the authors provide more specific computational cost analysis, including training time comparisons between attention transfer and standard knowledge distillation?

3. In Section 4.2.2, the authors claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." However, the paper doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with other knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet. Could the authors provide a brief comparison with recent knowledge transfer methods specifically designed for ImageNet?

4. The paper mentions that they "could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature." Could the authors provide a brief summary of why previous knowledge transfer methods might not have worked well on ImageNet, which would help contextualize their contribution?

This paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to knowledge transfer that could significantly impact the field. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the contribution is clearly positioned within the literature. I believe this work would be a valuable addition to ICLR.
