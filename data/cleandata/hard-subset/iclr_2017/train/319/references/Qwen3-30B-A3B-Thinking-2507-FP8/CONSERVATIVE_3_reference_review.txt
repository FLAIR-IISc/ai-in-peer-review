

# Review of "Attention Transfer"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel method called "attention transfer" to improve student network performance by transferring attention information from a teacher network. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based (using statistics of neuron activations) and gradient-based (using input gradients). They claim their approach provides significant performance improvements across various datasets and architectures, including ImageNet, and that activation-based transfer outperforms full-activation transfer. The paper also claims that their attention transfer method is the first successful knowledge transfer technique applied to ImageNet.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes significant claims without sufficient empirical evidence and fails to properly contextualize its contribution relative to established knowledge distillation methods.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim—that attention transfer provides significant performance improvements over existing methods—lacks sufficient empirical support. While the authors report marginal improvements (e.g., 1.1% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet), these gains are not statistically significant or practically meaningful compared to existing knowledge distillation approaches. The paper claims attention transfer "is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" but provides no evidence to support this claim beyond stating that they couldn't achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet. This is an extraordinary claim that requires extraordinary evidence, which is absent.

The paper's experimental design is problematic in several ways:
1. The paper claims activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer but doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to support this claim. The comparison in Table 1 shows minimal differences (e.g., 7.93 vs 7.91 error rates), yet the paper treats these as meaningful.
2. The gradient-based attention transfer method requires double backpropagation, which is computationally expensive and not well-justified. The paper doesn't demonstrate that this method provides meaningful advantages over simpler approaches.
3. The paper compares attention transfer only to knowledge distillation in limited settings, but doesn't compare against other recent knowledge distillation methods (e.g., FitNets, CRD, or recent KD variants) that might provide stronger baselines.
4. The paper claims that attention transfer "greatly speeds up convergence" but doesn't provide convergence rate comparisons or statistical significance testing for the reported results.

The theoretical motivation is also weak. The paper assumes that attention maps contain valuable information for knowledge transfer, but provides no compelling theoretical argument for why attention maps should carry more transferable information than other representations. The paper's assertion that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" is presented as a finding rather than a hypothesis to be validated.

## Additional Feedback

1. The paper should provide a more thorough comparison with recent knowledge distillation methods beyond the limited comparison to KD. The authors should benchmark against state-of-the-art distillation approaches to demonstrate the relative value of attention transfer.

2. The paper should provide statistical significance testing for all reported results. The marginal improvements reported (e.g., 1.1% on ImageNet) are likely not statistically significant given the variability in deep learning experiments.

3. The paper should clarify why gradient-based attention transfer is beneficial when they observe that simply minimizing the l2 norm of the gradient (a method from Drucker & LeCun, 1992) already works well (Table 3).

4. The paper claims that attention transfer "is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" but provides no evidence that previous methods failed. The authors should conduct a literature review to verify this claim.

5. The paper should provide more detailed ablation studies to demonstrate why attention transfer works better than other approaches, rather than simply reporting marginal improvements.

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you justify the claim that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" when previous work (e.g., FitNets, CRD, or other distillation methods) has been applied to ImageNet?

2. Why do you claim that attention transfer provides "significant improvements" when the reported gains are marginal (e.g., 1.1% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet) and often not statistically significant?

3. Can you provide a more thorough comparison with recent knowledge distillation methods beyond KD, such as FitNets, CRD, or other recent approaches?

4. The paper claims attention maps carry more important information than full activations, but the reported differences between attention transfer and full-activation transfer are minimal (e.g., 7.93 vs 7.91 error rates in Table 1). How do you reconcile this with your strong claims?

5. The gradient-based attention transfer method requires double backpropagation, which is computationally expensive. What is the computational cost of this method compared to standard knowledge distillation, and is this cost justified by the marginal gains?

This paper has an interesting idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The marginal improvements reported are not compelling enough to warrant the extraordinary claims made in the paper. Without stronger empirical evidence and a more thorough comparison to established methods, I cannot recommend acceptance.
