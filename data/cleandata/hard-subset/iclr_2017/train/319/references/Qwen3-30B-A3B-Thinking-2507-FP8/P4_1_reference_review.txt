

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer approach using attention information from a teacher network to improve a student network's performance. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based (using statistics of hidden layer activations) and gradient-based (using input gradients). They demonstrate that attention transfer provides consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet, Scenes, CUB) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), outperforming both standard knowledge distillation and direct transfer of full activations. The paper shows that activation-based attention transfer is particularly effective, can be combined with knowledge distillation, and provides insights into how attention mechanisms vary across network depths and architectures.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in knowledge transfer research. The experimental results are rigorous and demonstrate significant improvements across multiple benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper clearly addresses a relevant problem: improving student network performance through attention-based knowledge transfer rather than just output distributions or full activations. The motivation is well-placed within the literature, with appropriate references to related work on attention visualization (Grad-CAM, CAM), knowledge distillation (Hinton et al.), and intermediate layer transfer (FitNets).

The technical approach is sound: the authors provide clear mathematical definitions for both attention types and transfer mechanisms, with well-justified choices for attention mapping functions (sum-based vs. max-based). The experimental section is thorough, with ablation studies showing the importance of attention mapping functions, transfer layer placement, and the benefits of combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation.

The results are scientifically rigorous: the paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network). The ablation studies (Tables 1-2) provide convincing evidence that attention transfer is more effective than transferring full activations, and that activation-based attention transfer outperforms gradient-based methods. The visualizations in Figures 2, 4, and 6 support the claim that attention maps capture meaningful spatial information that correlates with network performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The claim that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is an overstatement. Knowledge distillation has been successfully applied on ImageNet (e.g., Hinton et al., 2015), so this claim should be revised to be more precise.
- The paper should provide a more detailed computational cost analysis of attention transfer compared to standard knowledge distillation, as this is an important practical consideration for real-world applications.
- The authors could strengthen the theoretical connection between attention maps and network performance by discussing how the attention maps relate to the network's decision boundaries or feature representations.
- The paper mentions limitations with batch normalization in gradient-based attention transfer but doesn't provide a clear path forward. A discussion of potential solutions or future work addressing this limitation would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Can you clarify which specific knowledge transfer methods you are comparing against, and why they failed on ImageNet? This claim needs careful qualification.

2. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, you mention uncertainty about batch normalization behavior during second backpropagation. Could you elaborate on why batch normalization causes issues in this context, and what steps you took to address this limitation?

3. The paper shows activation-based attention transfer outperforms gradient-based attention transfer (Table 3, Figure 7). Can you provide more insight into why activation-based attention might be more effective for knowledge transfer, particularly given that gradient-based methods are commonly used for attention visualization?

4. In Table 1, attention transfer combined with knowledge distillation (AT+KD) shows the best performance. However, the paper doesn't clearly explain why these two methods are complementary rather than redundant. A deeper analysis of their synergy would strengthen the paper.
