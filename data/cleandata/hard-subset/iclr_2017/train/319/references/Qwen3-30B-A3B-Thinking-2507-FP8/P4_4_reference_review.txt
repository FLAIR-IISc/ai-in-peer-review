

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention information. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) that encode where a network focuses for making decisions. They introduce methods to transfer these attention maps from a teacher network to a student network, demonstrating significant performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures. The key contributions include: (1) proposing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism, (2) introducing activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing consistent improvements across various datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated approach with scientifically rigorous experimental validation that demonstrates clear improvements over baselines. The method is novel in its specific application of attention maps for knowledge transfer, and the results are consistently positive across multiple datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's approach is well-motivated by the observation that attention maps correlate with network performance and contain valuable information. The authors carefully distinguish their work from related methods like FitNets (which transfers full activations) and knowledge distillation (which transfers softened class distributions), establishing a clear research gap. The problem statement is precise and the motivation is well-anchored in the literature.

The experimental validation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous. The authors conduct experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network). They provide ablation studies showing that multiple attention transfer losses yield better results than single losses, and that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer. The results are consistent across different network depths and widths, with statistically significant improvements (e.g., 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement on ImageNet with ResNet-18 student using ResNet-34 teacher). The authors also address computational considerations for gradient-based attention transfer, acknowledging its double backpropagation cost.

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of attention transfer as a knowledge transfer mechanism, particularly highlighting how activation-based attention transfer provides superior results compared to previous approaches. The connection to human attention mechanisms provides a reasonable theoretical foundation for the approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- Clarify the novelty claim regarding ImageNet. The paper states "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but knowledge distillation has been applied to ImageNet before. The authors should clarify that they mean "attention-based knowledge transfer" specifically, rather than knowledge distillation in general.

- Provide more detailed computational cost analysis for gradient-based attention transfer. While the paper mentions it has "approximately the same cost with first backpropagation," a more concrete comparison with other methods (including knowledge distillation) would strengthen the paper.

- Include a discussion on why attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer (FitNets-style). The paper presents empirical evidence but could benefit from a more theoretical explanation of why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations.

- Consider adding a comparison with recent attention-based knowledge transfer methods to better establish the novelty of the proposed approach.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you clarify the claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet"? How does this differ from previous knowledge distillation work on ImageNet?

2. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, the paper mentions "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have you explored alternative approaches to handle batch normalization in this context, and how might they affect performance?

3. The paper states that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer. Could you provide more insight into why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations, beyond the empirical results?

4. For the ImageNet experiments, the paper mentions "we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters." How sensitive are the results to hyperparameter choices, and what would be the expected performance gain with full hyperparameter tuning?
