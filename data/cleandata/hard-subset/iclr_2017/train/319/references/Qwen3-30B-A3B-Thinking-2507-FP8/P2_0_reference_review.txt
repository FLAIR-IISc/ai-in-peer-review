

# Review of "Attention Transfer"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks using attention maps. The authors propose two methods for defining spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate that transferring attention information from a teacher network to a student network can significantly improve the student's performance across multiple datasets and architectures. The key contributions include: (1) defining attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) proposing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing significant improvements across various datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

The paper makes a strong case that attention maps contain more valuable information for knowledge transfer than full activations, which is a significant insight that builds upon existing knowledge distillation work. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, with consistent improvements in accuracy.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** the paper with the following key reasons:
1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer that addresses an important gap in the literature.
2. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core insight—that attention maps contain more useful information for knowledge transfer than full activations—is well-supported by the experiments. The authors demonstrate that activation-based attention transfer (AT) consistently outperforms full-activation transfer (F-ActT) across multiple datasets and network architectures (Table 1, Table 2, and Fig. 7(b)). This is a significant contribution as it challenges the assumption that transferring full activations is optimal.

The experimental design is robust, with multiple ablation studies showing:
- The importance of using attention maps rather than full activations (Table 1, column F-ActT)
- The effectiveness of different attention mapping functions (Table 2)
- The benefits of combining AT with knowledge distillation (Table 3)
- The consistency of results across different datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet)

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough review of related work on attention mechanisms, visualization techniques, and knowledge distillation. The authors clearly position their work as a novel approach that differs from previous methods like FitNets (which transfers full activations) and standard knowledge distillation.

The most compelling evidence is the ImageNet results (Table 5 and Fig. 7(a)), where ResNet-18 with attention transfer achieved 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 better validation accuracy than the baseline. The authors also make a strong claim that their activation-based attention transfer is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet, which is significant given the difficulty of applying knowledge distillation to large-scale datasets.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the "why" behind attention maps being more effective**: The paper states that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" but doesn't provide a deeper theoretical or empirical explanation. Adding a section that analyzes why attention maps contain more discriminative information would significantly strengthen the paper.

2. **Expand on the ImageNet knowledge distillation failure**: The paper mentions "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet" but doesn't explain why. A more detailed analysis of why knowledge distillation fails on ImageNet while working well on CIFAR would add significant value.

3. **Provide computational cost analysis**: While the paper mentions that attention transfer "adds very little computational cost," it would be valuable to provide specific numbers comparing the training time and memory requirements of attention transfer versus standard knowledge distillation.

4. **Discuss limitations of the approach**: The paper should acknowledge limitations, such as whether attention transfer works equally well for all network architectures or if it's particularly effective for certain types of tasks (e.g., fine-grained recognition vs. general classification).

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" (Section 4.1.2). Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why this is the case, beyond just showing the experimental results?

2. In the ImageNet experiments, you mention that knowledge distillation "actually hurts convergence" with the same hyperparameters as on CIFAR. What specific hyperparameters were used for ImageNet, and how did you determine that the hyperparameters were "the same" as on CIFAR? 

3. You claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Can you clarify what you mean by "successfully applied"? Are there other knowledge transfer methods that have been applied to ImageNet that you're aware of, and why did they fail?

4. The paper uses l2 normalization for attention maps. How sensitive is the performance to this choice? Have you tried other normalization schemes (e.g., l1 normalization), and if so, what were the results?

5. In Section 3.2, you mention that "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have you explored alternative approaches to handle batch normalization in this context, and if so, what were the results?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms, with strong experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. With the suggested improvements, this paper could become a significant reference in the knowledge distillation literature.
