

# Review

## Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention information rather than just output predictions. The authors propose two methods for attention transfer: activation-based (using statistics of hidden activations) and gradient-based (using input sensitivity maps). They argue that attention maps contain valuable spatial information about what the network is focusing on, which can significantly improve the performance of a student network when transferred from a teacher network. The paper demonstrates this approach across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN), showing consistent improvements over standard knowledge distillation and baseline methods. The core novelty lies in treating attention maps as a new form of "knowledge" to transfer, rather than just output logits.

## Decision

Accept

## Supporting Arguments

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to knowledge transfer that aligns with my review priorities. The main contribution—using attention maps as the basis for knowledge transfer—is genuinely novel and conceptually significant. While knowledge distillation is a well-studied area, the authors successfully shift the paradigm from transferring output representations to transferring spatial attention information, which is a meaningful conceptual advance.

The paper is well-motivated, drawing strong connections between human attention mechanisms and artificial attention. The related work section adequately positions the paper within the literature on attention visualization and knowledge distillation, highlighting the gap that their approach fills. The authors provide compelling evidence that attention maps correlate with network performance (stronger networks have more focused attention maps) and that these maps contain valuable spatial information.

The empirical results are promising and support the main claims. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing different attention mapping functions, showing attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer) provide strong evidence for the validity of their approach. The results across multiple datasets and architectures demonstrate the generalizability of their method. The claim that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is supported by their experiments, though I'd be interested to see more discussion about why standard knowledge distillation methods might not work as well on ImageNet.

While the paper has some minor methodological limitations (e.g., preliminary ImageNet experiments without full hyperparameter tuning), these don't undermine the core novelty. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe the significant conceptual contribution of this paper outweighs these minor shortcomings.

## Additional Feedback

1. The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations. The results in Table 1 show attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer, but a more detailed theoretical justification would strengthen the argument.

2. The gradient-based attention transfer section mentions challenges with batch normalization in second-order derivatives. This is an important practical consideration that could be expanded upon, as it might affect the applicability of the method to many modern architectures.

3. The claim that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" needs more context. While the authors don't cite specific ImageNet knowledge transfer papers, there are several works that use knowledge distillation on ImageNet (e.g., FitNets, Hinton et al.). Clarifying the distinction between their attention-based transfer and previous knowledge distillation methods would be helpful.

4. The paper could include a discussion of the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer, especially the second-order derivatives. The paper mentions it has "approximately the same cost with first backpropagation," but this might be misleading for some architectures.

5. The relationship between attention transfer and other attention-based methods (like Grad-CAM) could be more explicitly discussed. How does attention transfer relate to existing attention visualization techniques?

## Clarifying Questions

1. The paper mentions that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer." Could you provide more intuition on why attention maps carry more useful information than the full activation tensors?

2. The paper claims attention transfer is the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet. Can you clarify why standard knowledge distillation methods (like Hinton et al.) don't work well on ImageNet with different architecture/depth teacher-student pairs, and how attention transfer overcomes this limitation?

3. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, you mention the need for "second order mixed partial derivative calculation." Could you elaborate on how this is implemented in practice, especially in frameworks like PyTorch that support automatic differentiation?
