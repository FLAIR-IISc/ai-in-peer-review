

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention information rather than traditional feature or output distribution matching. The authors propose two methods for attention transfer: activation-based and gradient-based. They claim that attention maps contain valuable information that can significantly improve student network performance, and that this information is more useful for transfer than full activation tensors. The paper demonstrates that attention transfer provides consistent improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Network-in-Network, Wide Residual Networks). The authors further claim that their activation-based attention transfer is the first successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet, which represents a significant contribution to the field.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper introduces a bold, novel concept that fundamentally rethinks knowledge transfer in neural networks. Despite some methodological limitations, the core idea of using attention maps as transferable knowledge is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary contribution is its conceptual novelty: attention maps as a transferable knowledge source. This is a fresh perspective that goes beyond the standard knowledge distillation paradigm. The authors convincingly demonstrate that attention maps contain information more valuable for transfer than full activations (as shown in Table 1 and Figure 7b), which is a counterintuitive and important finding. Their approach is well-motivated by the observation that modern deep networks can achieve good performance with fewer layers (when parameter count is held constant), making attention transfer a more efficient alternative to training deeper students.

The experimental results are compelling and show consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures. The ablation studies (Tables 1-2) provide strong evidence for the importance of their specific attention mapping functions and the value of attention transfer over traditional activation transfer. The ImageNet results (Section 4.2.2) are particularly significant, as they demonstrate the first successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet, which is a major benchmark in computer vision.

While the paper has some methodological limitations (such as limited analysis of why attention transfer works better than traditional methods, and some sparse experimental details), these do not undermine the core novelty and significance of the approach. The authors' bold claim about ImageNet is supported by their results, and the paper provides a clear direction for future research.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following:

1. Clarify the ImageNet claim: The paper states that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but Hinton et al. (2015) and FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) both applied knowledge distillation on ImageNet. Please clarify what makes your approach different from these prior works, particularly in terms of why traditional knowledge distillation fails on ImageNet while your method succeeds.

2. Expand the theoretical justification: While the empirical results are strong, a more detailed theoretical explanation for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations would strengthen the paper. Why do attention maps capture information that is more relevant for the student to learn?

3. Address batch normalization in gradient-based attention: The paper notes that "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step required during gradient-based attention transfer." Please elaborate on this issue and how you plan to resolve it in future work.

4. Include more visualization of attention maps: While Figure 6 shows some attention map comparisons, more detailed visualizations across different datasets and architectures would better illustrate how attention transfer affects the student's attention patterns.

5. Provide more details on computational cost: The paper mentions that attention transfer "adds very little computational cost," but doesn't quantify this. Please include a brief analysis of the computational overhead compared to standard knowledge distillation.

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to knowledge transfer with significant potential impact on the field. The core idea is bold, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental evidence. With minor clarifications, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
