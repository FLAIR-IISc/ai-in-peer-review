

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of knowledge distillation by proposing attention transfer as a novel mechanism for transferring knowledge from teacher to student networks. The authors define two types of attention maps - activation-based (using statistics of neuron activations across channels) and gradient-based (using gradients with respect to input) - and demonstrate that these maps contain valuable information that can significantly improve student network performance. The key contributions include: (1) proposing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) introducing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating the first successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet.

The paper is well-motivated by the observation that humans use attention to focus on discriminative regions of scenes, and that similar mechanisms exist in neural networks. The authors provide a thorough literature review connecting their work to attention mechanisms in human cognition, computer vision, and knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-supported approach to knowledge transfer that demonstrates significant improvements across multiple benchmarks, including ImageNet where previous knowledge distillation methods have struggled.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper's approach is well-placed in the literature and addresses a meaningful gap in knowledge distillation. While previous work has focused on transferring class probabilities (Hinton et al., 2015) or full activations (FitNets, Romero et al., 2014), this paper demonstrates that attention maps contain more useful information for transfer than either of these approaches. The experimental results are rigorous and thorough, with evaluations across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet), multiple architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN), and both activation-based and gradient-based attention methods.

The most compelling evidence is the ImageNet results (Section 4.2.2), where the authors demonstrate a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18 using attention transfer from ResNet-34. This is significant because, as the authors note, "we can assume that proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

The paper also provides valuable insights into why attention transfer works better than full activation transfer (Section 4.1.2), noting that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations." This is a key conceptual contribution that moves beyond previous knowledge distillation approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the conceptual relationship between attention transfer and knowledge distillation**: The paper mentions combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation but doesn't clearly explain how these two approaches interact. A more detailed explanation of how they complement each other would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

2. **Provide more insight into why attention transfer works better**: While the paper shows that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer, it doesn't fully explain why. The paper could benefit from a deeper analysis of what specific information in attention maps is most valuable for transfer.

3. **Discuss computational overhead**: The paper notes that gradient-based attention transfer requires double backpropagation, which is computationally expensive. A more detailed discussion of the computational cost compared to standard knowledge distillation would help readers understand practical implications.

4. **Expand on layer-specific attention analysis**: The paper mentions that attention maps focus on different parts of the image for different layers (Section 3.1), but doesn't systematically analyze how different layers contribute to the transfer performance. A more detailed analysis of layer-specific contributions would strengthen the paper.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In Table 2, you report that F^2sum (sum of squared activations) outperforms other attention mapping functions. Could you provide more insight into why this particular function works best, and whether this holds across different datasets?

2. The paper mentions that attention transfer works particularly well on fine-grained recognition (CUB) compared to other tasks (Section 4.2.1). Could you elaborate on why spatial attention might be more important for fine-grained recognition than for other tasks?

3. You state that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" (Section 4.1.2). Could you provide more concrete examples or visualizations to help readers understand the specific differences between these two approaches?

4. In the ImageNet experiments (Section 4.2.2), you note that "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet." Could you provide more details on why KD fails on ImageNet while attention transfer succeeds, particularly since both methods are based on transferring information from teacher to student?

5. How do you envision attention transfer being applied in real-world scenarios where computational resources are constrained, given the additional cost of gradient-based attention transfer?

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution that advances the field of knowledge distillation. The experimental results are convincing, and the paper makes a meaningful contribution to understanding how attention mechanisms can be leveraged for knowledge transfer. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
