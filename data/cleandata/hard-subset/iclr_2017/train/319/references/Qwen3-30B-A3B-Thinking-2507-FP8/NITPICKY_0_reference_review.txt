

# Review of "Attention Transfer for Knowledge Distillation"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge distillation by transferring attention maps from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based and gradient-based, and demonstrate that transferring these maps can significantly improve student network performance across multiple datasets and architectures. They claim that attention transfer outperforms traditional knowledge distillation and full activation transfer methods, and they present experimental results on CIFAR and ImageNet datasets showing promising improvements.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies, insufficient experimental details, and questionable claims that undermine its scientific rigor and prevent confident assessment of its contributions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Technical Inconsistencies and Presentation Flaws:**
- The paper contains numerous mathematical notation inconsistencies (e.g., "F psum(A)" vs. "F^p_sum(A)", "F pmax(A)" vs. "F^p_max(A)"), which undermine clarity and professionalism.
- The paper claims "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" without providing a theoretical justification for this observation, relying solely on empirical results without explaining why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations.
- In section 3.2, the description of gradient-based attention transfer contains critical technical inaccuracies. The paper states "the second backpropagation has approximately the same cost with first backpropagation, excluding forward propagation" without providing any justification or supporting evidence for this claim. The computational complexity analysis is insufficient for a conference paper.
- The paper references "Fig. 2" and "Fig. 4" but provides no figures, making it impossible to verify their claims about visual attention maps.

**Experimental Rigor Issues:**
- The paper states "we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" for ImageNet experiments, which is a serious shortcoming for a paper claiming to present a new method with significant improvements. This lack of hyperparameter tuning undermines the validity of their ImageNet results.
- The paper claims "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" without providing a thorough literature review to substantiate this claim. Several prior works (e.g., FitNets, Hinton et al.) have applied knowledge distillation on ImageNet, though not necessarily with attention transfer.
- The paper reports "median of 5 runs" for some experiments but doesn't specify whether this was done for all experiments, nor does it provide statistical significance testing for the reported improvements. The 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement on ImageNet is presented without absolute accuracy numbers for comparison.
- In Table 3, the paper claims "activation-based AT" achieves 11.2% error rate but doesn't provide the baseline error rate for comparison, making it impossible to assess the magnitude of improvement.

**Methodological Concerns:**
- The paper claims that "normalization of attention maps is important for the success of the student training" but provides no empirical evidence or theoretical justification for why l2 normalization (as opposed to l1 or other normalizations) is optimal.
- The paper's claim that "attention maps contain valuable information that can be used for significantly improving the performance" is not adequately supported by the experiments. The paper doesn't explain what specific information in the attention maps is being transferred or why it's more useful than other forms of knowledge.
- The paper's implementation of horizontal flip invariance (equation 6) is described as "similar to Group Equivariant CNN approach" but without explaining how this relates to attention transfer or why it's beneficial for the specific task.

## 4. Additional Feedback

**To Improve the Paper:**
1. Provide complete mathematical notation with consistent formatting throughout the paper (e.g., use proper superscripts for exponents: F^p_sum instead of F psum).
2. Include all figures referenced in the text (Figs. 1-7) to validate the claims about attention maps.
3. Provide detailed hyperparameter tuning procedures for all experiments, especially for ImageNet, rather than stating "we did not have time to tune hyperparameters."
4. Conduct statistical significance testing for all reported performance improvements.
5. Provide absolute accuracy numbers for ImageNet results, not just percentage improvements.
6. Expand the literature review to properly address prior work on knowledge distillation on ImageNet and clarify the novelty claim.
7. Provide a more thorough theoretical justification for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations.

**Questions for Authors:**
1. What specific information in the attention maps (beyond simple spatial activation patterns) is being transferred that makes it more effective than transferring full activations?
2. How do the authors justify the choice of l2 normalization over other normalization schemes for attention maps?
3. Can you provide convergence curves showing the training dynamics of attention transfer versus knowledge distillation on ImageNet?
4. How does the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer compare to standard knowledge distillation in terms of training time and memory usage?
5. What evidence do you have that attention transfer is fundamentally different from previous knowledge distillation methods, rather than just another form of distillation?

The paper presents an interesting idea but fails to adequately support its claims with rigorous technical details and experimental validation. The numerous technical inconsistencies, insufficient experimental details, and questionable claims prevent me from recommending acceptance.
