

# Review of "Attention Transfer"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks using attention maps as the transfer medium. The authors define two types of attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate their utility in improving student network performance. The key contributions include: (1) framing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism, (2) proposing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple critical methodological inconsistencies and misrepresentations that undermine its claims and prevent confidence in the results.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Fundamental Misrepresentation of Prior Work**: The paper claims "activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" (Section 4.2.2), which is factually incorrect. Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) have been successfully applied on ImageNet for years. The authors cite Hinton et al. (2015) but then claim KD "struggles to work if teacher and student have different architecture/depth" without providing evidence for this specific claim on ImageNet. This misrepresentation severely damages the paper's credibility.

2. **Critical Notational Inconsistencies**: The paper defines F^p_sum(A) = ∑|A_i|^p but describes it as "sum of absolute values raised to the power of p" (which would be (∑|A_i|)^p). This fundamental notational error in the mathematical definition is not merely a typo but a conceptual mistake that affects the entire method's interpretation. Similarly, the normalization in equation (2) is ambiguously described and implemented—normalizing before subtraction (Q/||Q||_2) is not standard practice for distance metrics, and the authors provide no justification for this unconventional approach.

3. **Misleading Experimental Claims**: The paper claims "significant improvements" across datasets, but the magnitude of improvements is modest (e.g., 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement on ImageNet). More critically, the paper fails to establish that attention transfer provides *meaningful* improvement beyond standard knowledge distillation. For example, in Table 3, activation-based AT achieves 11.2% error on CIFAR-10, while KD achieves 12.1%—a 0.9% improvement that the paper claims is "significant" without statistical significance testing or comparison to other baselines.

4. **Inconsistent Interpretation of Prior Literature**: The paper misinterprets Zagoruyko & Komodakis (2016), claiming their work "showed that, after a certain depth, the improvements came mostly from increased capacity of the networks." This misrepresents the actual contribution of Wide Residual Networks, which focused on width (not depth) as a means to improve performance with fewer layers. This error suggests a lack of careful engagement with the cited literature.

5. **Methodological Ambiguities**: The paper claims "attention maps focus on different parts for different layers in the network" but provides no quantitative evidence for this claim—only vague references to "Fig. 4" (which is not provided). Similarly, the gradient-based attention section describes "double backpropagation" without explaining why this is necessary or how it differs from standard backpropagation, making the method difficult to reproduce.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify the Core Contribution**: The paper should explicitly state what makes attention transfer *different* from existing knowledge distillation methods. Is the improvement due to the attention mechanism itself, or is it merely a better way to implement distillation?

2. **Correct Notation and Definitions**: The paper must fix the inconsistent mathematical definitions (F^p_sum vs. the verbal description) and clarify the normalization process in equation (2). Provide a clear explanation for why normalizing attention maps before subtraction is beneficial.

3. **Expand Experimental Validation**: The paper should include statistical significance testing for all reported improvements. The modest 1.1% accuracy gain on ImageNet requires rigorous statistical validation to be meaningful.

4. **Correct Literature Context**: The paper must accurately represent prior work on knowledge distillation, especially on ImageNet. The claim that attention transfer is "the first successful method" is simply false and must be removed.

5. **Improve Experimental Design**: The paper should compare attention transfer directly against standard knowledge distillation with identical hyperparameters (not just "AT+KD" as an additive term). The current comparison is misleading because the authors don't control for how they combine the methods.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide a clear theoretical justification for why attention maps (vs. full activations) carry more useful information for transfer? The paper claims attention maps "carry information that is more important for transfer" but provides no evidence beyond empirical results that don't clearly isolate this effect.

2. Why did you claim attention transfer is "the first successful method" for knowledge transfer on ImageNet when prior work (Hinton et al., 2015; Romero et al., 2014) has demonstrated this?

3. What is the specific advantage of activation-based attention transfer over knowledge distillation alone? The paper claims attention transfer "gives better improvements than full-activation transfer," but how does it compare to standard KD?

4. The paper states "the main motivation for residual networks was increasing depth," but Zagoruyko & Komodakis (2016) introduced Wide Residual Networks as a way to increase *width* while reducing *depth*. How does this align with your statement?

The paper's claims are undermined by these critical issues, and the minor improvements reported are not sufficient to justify the paper's significance. The methodological inconsistencies and misrepresentations of prior work are too substantial to overlook.
