- Proposes attention transfer for knowledge transfer between networks using activation- and gradient-based spatial attention maps.  
- Shows transferring attention maps from teacher to student improves student performance across vision tasks.  
- Introduces activation-based and gradient-based attention-map formulations.  
- Demonstrates consistent performance gains across datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NiN).  
- Activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and complements knowledge distillation.  
- Provides comprehensive literature review, thorough experiments, ablations, and attention-map visualizations.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: tackles an important problem with a novel perspective and robust experimental support.  
- Motivates attention transfer by noting prior distillation methods ignore attention mechanisms.  
- Experimental evidence: substantial error reduction (e.g., CIFAR-10 11.2% vs 13.5% baseline) and better results than full-activation transfer.  
- Well-situated in related work (KD, attention in vision, visualization techniques).  
- Shows method works across teacher/student depth differences; activation-based AT usually outperforms gradient-based AT.  
- Improvement suggestions: provide theoretical justification for using activation magnitudes as attention indicators.  
- Suggests comparing with recent vision attention methods (e.g., Vision Transformers).  
- Requests concrete computational cost analysis and training-time comparisons versus KD.  
- Recommends ablation studies on attention-map normalization (why L2 normalization is best).  
- Asks authors to explain why F^2sum performs best and provide theoretical insight.  
- Requests specific computational cost numbers for gradient-based AT’s extra backprop.  
- Requests direct comparisons with other knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet.  
- Asks why prior methods (FitNets, KD) haven't been applied successfully to ImageNet.
