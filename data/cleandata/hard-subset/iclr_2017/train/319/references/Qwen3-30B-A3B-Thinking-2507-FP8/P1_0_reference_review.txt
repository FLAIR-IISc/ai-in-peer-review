

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention information. The authors propose transferring spatial attention maps (both activation-based and gradient-based) from a teacher network to a student network, arguing that these attention maps contain valuable information that can improve the student's performance. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) it defines attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) it proposes two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based), (3) it demonstrates significant performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, and (4) it shows that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

The paper is well-motivated by the observation that different networks with varying performance levels exhibit different attention patterns that correlate with their accuracy. The authors provide a thorough literature review connecting their work to both human attention mechanisms and previous work on attention visualization in CNNs, as well as knowledge distillation approaches. They carefully define their attention maps, explain the attention transfer methodology, and provide extensive experimental validation across multiple datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN).

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.

Key reasons for acceptance: The paper addresses a meaningful and underexplored aspect of knowledge transfer that has significant potential to advance the field. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, and the approach is well-justified by the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the knowledge distillation literature by introducing a new form of knowledge transfer that leverages attention maps rather than just output logits or hidden activations. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their approach by connecting it to human attention mechanisms and the observation that higher-performing networks have more focused attention patterns.

The experimental validation is particularly strong. The authors conduct thorough experiments across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) with various architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN). For example, on ImageNet, they demonstrate that ResNet-18 with attention transfer achieves 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 better validation accuracy than the baseline, which is significant given the difficulty of ImageNet. The comparison with knowledge distillation and full activation transfer shows that attention transfer provides meaningful improvements, with the most notable results on ImageNet where the authors claim their method is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet."

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and properly situates its work within the broader literature. The attention transfer approach is well-defined, and the authors provide sufficient detail about their implementation and experimental setup. The results are statistically sound, with multiple runs reported where appropriate, and the paper makes a compelling case that attention maps contain information that is more valuable for transfer than full activations.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the "first on ImageNet" claim**: The paper states that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" but doesn't provide sufficient literature evidence to support this. Please provide a more thorough literature review to verify this claim, including specific examples of previous knowledge transfer methods that failed on ImageNet and why they failed.

2. **Explain the significance of modest improvements**: The improvements on ImageNet (1.1% top-1 accuracy) are statistically significant but relatively modest. Please discuss the practical implications of these improvements, including the computational cost of attention transfer versus the performance gain. This would help readers understand whether the approach is worth adopting.

3. **Address batch normalization in gradient-based attention**: The paper mentions challenges with batch normalization in the second backpropagation step for gradient-based attention but doesn't provide results with batch normalization, which is standard practice for ImageNet training. It would be valuable to include results with batch normalization or provide a detailed explanation of why it's problematic.

4. **Provide more visualization of attention maps**: While the paper includes some visualizations (Fig. 4), more detailed attention map visualizations across different datasets and architectures would help readers better understand how attention transfer changes the student network's focus.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Can you provide specific examples from the literature where previous knowledge transfer methods (like KD or FitNets) failed on ImageNet, and why they failed?

2. The paper states that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" but the differences in Table 1 are very small (7.93 vs 7.96 for WRN-16-2/WRN-16-1). Could you elaborate on why these small differences are considered "much better" in the paper, and whether these differences are statistically significant?

3. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, the paper mentions "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have you explored any alternative approaches to handle batch normalization in this context, and if not, why not?

4. The paper claims that attention transfer "closes the gap between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 in accuracy" on CUB and Scenes. Could you provide more details on how much the gap was closed (e.g., the absolute difference in accuracy before and after attention transfer)?

5. The paper states that "attention maps focus on different parts for different layers in the network." Could you provide more details about how the attention patterns change across layers (e.g., low-level features vs. high-level features) and how this relates to the network's performance?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the knowledge distillation literature. With the suggested clarifications and additional details, it would be an excellent addition to ICLR.
