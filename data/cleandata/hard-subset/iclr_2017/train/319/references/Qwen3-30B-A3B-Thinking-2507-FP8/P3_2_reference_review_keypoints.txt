- Paper proposes transferring activation- and gradient-based spatial attention maps from teacher to student to improve knowledge transfer.  
- Claims: attention transfer is a novel mechanism, introduces two attention map types, shows consistent improvements, and that activation-based AT outperforms full-activation transfer and complements KD.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason 1: Novelty is questionable because attention transfer is similar to prior intermediate-representation KD methods (FitNets, Hinton KD) and authors misstate prior ImageNet results.  
- Main reason 2: Experimental evidence is insufficient to support key claims, especially that attention maps are more informative than full activations.  
- Main reason 3: No theoretical justification for why attention maps should be superior to full activations.  
- Main reason 4: Weak statistical analysis—median over 5 runs reported without significance testing, so reported gains (e.g., 1.1% on ImageNet) may be within noise.  
- Supporting critique: The claim of being first to apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet is false and undermines credibility.  
- Experimental recommendations: Provide broader comparisons across architectures/datasets, full hyperparameter tuning, and statistical significance testing.  
- Theoretical recommendation: Offer a principled explanation or analysis of information content in attention maps versus full activations.  
- Practical recommendation: Analyze computational cost and implementation details of gradient-based (double backprop) attention transfer, including batch-normalization handling.  
- Questions for authors: Clarify how attention transfer fundamentally differs from KD, provide comprehensive comparisons supporting AT vs full activations, correct ImageNet claim and compare precisely to prior work, and explain implementation/BN handling for gradient-based AT.
