

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) that encode where a network focuses its attention during decision-making. They demonstrate that transferring these attention maps from a teacher network to a student network significantly improves the student's performance across multiple image classification datasets (CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN). The paper also shows that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation for further improvements. The work is well-motivated by the relationship between human attention mechanisms and artificial attention, and it situates itself within the literature on attention visualization and knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a novel contribution to knowledge transfer methods in neural networks by introducing attention maps as a transferable knowledge source, and the experimental results provide strong evidence supporting this approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's key strength is the novel concept of using attention maps as transferable knowledge, which is distinct from previous work on knowledge distillation. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for defining attention maps (both activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate their effectiveness across multiple datasets and architectures. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough ablation studies comparing different attention mapping functions, transfer methods, and combinations with knowledge distillation. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple benchmarks (CIFAR-10, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet), with particularly strong results on ImageNet where the authors claim their method is the first to successfully apply knowledge transfer. The attention transfer approach also shows advantages over full-activation transfer (FitNets-style), with the authors providing empirical evidence that attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:
- Provide more thorough comparison with other knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet, especially since they claim their approach is the first successful application on this dataset.
- Include more detailed analysis of why attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer, beyond empirical results.
- Discuss the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer compared to activation-based, particularly regarding the double backpropagation requirement.
- Expand the discussion of the theoretical relationship between attention maps and network performance, potentially linking to existing theories of neural network representations.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims that attention transfer is the first successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet. Could you provide more context on why previous methods (like knowledge distillation) have not worked well on ImageNet, and how your approach specifically overcomes these limitations?

2. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, you note that it's unclear how batch normalization should behave during the second backpropagation step. Could you elaborate on this issue and whether you've explored potential solutions that would make gradient-based attention transfer more broadly applicable?

3. The paper shows that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer but doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for why attention maps contain more useful transferable information. Could you discuss the theoretical basis for why attention maps are more informative than full activations for knowledge transfer?
