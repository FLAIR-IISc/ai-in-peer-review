- Paper proposes attention transfer (AT) for knowledge transfer, using spatial attention maps from teacher to student.
- Defines two types of attention maps: activation-based and gradient-based.
- Claims AT improves student performance and can combine with knowledge distillation.
- Shows extensive experiments across datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN).
- Reports notable ImageNet gains (ResNet-18: +1.1% top-1, +0.8% top-5) and claims AT is first successful transfer method on ImageNet.
- Finds activation-based AT outperforms full-activation transfer in many cases.
- Motivated by observation that higher-performing networks have more focused attention; connects to human attention and prior visualization work.
- Paper is well-written, well-structured, and provides implementation/experimental details and multiple runs where appropriate.
- Recommendation: accept, because the approach addresses an underexplored area and shows consistent improvements.
- Suggestion: clarify and substantiate the claim that AT is the first successful method on ImageNet with literature evidence.
- Suggestion: discuss practical significance and computational cost of modest ImageNet improvements.
- Issue: batch normalization complicates gradient-based attention (second backpropagation) and results with BN are missing.
- Suggestion: add more attention map visualizations across datasets and architectures to illustrate effects.
- Question: provide examples of prior transfer methods failing on ImageNet and explain why.
- Question: justify claiming activation-based AT is “much better” when numerical differences are very small and report statistical significance.
- Question: report whether alternative methods were tried to handle batch normalization in gradient-based AT.
- Question: quantify how much AT “closes the gap” between ResNet-18 and ResNet-34 on CUB and Scenes.
- Question: detail how attention patterns change across layers (low-level vs high-level) and how that relates to performance.
