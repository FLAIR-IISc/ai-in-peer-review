

# ICLR Review

## Summary of Paper Claims

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and demonstrate that transferring these maps from a teacher network to a student network can significantly improve the student's performance across multiple datasets and architectures. The key contributions include: (1) proposing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism, (2) introducing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing consistent performance improvements across various tasks, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## Decision

**Reject**

**Key Reasons:**
1. The paper's contribution is not clearly differentiated from existing knowledge distillation approaches, particularly FitNets and Hinton's KD.
2. The experimental evidence supporting key claims (e.g., attention maps being more informative than full activations) is insufficiently rigorous.

## Supporting Arguments

### 1. Questionable Novelty

The paper's main claim—that attention maps can be used for knowledge transfer—is not sufficiently novel. Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) have already established that transferring intermediate representations between networks improves performance. The authors' attention transfer is essentially a specific type of intermediate representation transfer, but they fail to clearly articulate how it differs from or improves upon existing methods.

The paper claims their activation-based attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this is demonstrably false—Hinton et al. (2015) applied knowledge distillation to ImageNet, and FitNets has also been applied to ImageNet. The authors' claim appears to be based on a misunderstanding of prior work, which undermines their credibility.

### 2. Insufficient Evidence for Key Claims

The paper makes a strong claim that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" (Section 4.1.2), but the evidence is limited to a single comparison (table 1, column F-ActT). This is insufficient to support such a broad claim. The authors should provide more comprehensive comparisons across multiple architectures and datasets to validate this assertion.

Additionally, the paper reports improvements on ImageNet (1.1% top-1 accuracy) without proper hyperparameter tuning (Section 4.2.2: "we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters"). This is a significant limitation, as the results may be suboptimal and not representative of the method's true potential.

### 3. Lack of Theoretical Justification

The paper lacks a theoretical foundation for why attention maps would be more informative than full activations for knowledge transfer. The explanation provided ("these correlations also tend to be higher in networks with higher accuracy") is anecdotal rather than theoretical. Without a principled justification, the paper's claims remain unsupported.

### 4. Weak Statistical Analysis

The paper reports median test errors over 5 runs for some experiments but provides no statistical significance testing. For instance, the 1.1% improvement on ImageNet could be within the margin of error. Without proper statistical analysis, it's impossible to determine if the improvements are meaningful.

## Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Clarify the novelty**: Explicitly state how your attention transfer method differs from knowledge distillation and FitNets. Is it the attention maps themselves, the transfer mechanism, or something else? Provide a clear comparison table.

2. **Strengthen experimental evidence**: 
   - Conduct more comprehensive comparisons between attention transfer and full-activation transfer across multiple architectures.
   - Provide statistical significance testing for all reported results.
   - Report complete hyperparameter tuning details for ImageNet experiments.

3. **Correct the false claim about ImageNet**: Acknowledge that knowledge distillation has been successfully applied to ImageNet and reframe your contribution as "attention transfer as a specific form of knowledge distillation" rather than claiming novelty where none exists.

4. **Provide theoretical justification**: Explain why attention maps contain more useful information for transfer than full activations. Consider analyzing the information content of attention maps versus full activations.

5. **Improve the gradient-based attention section**: The double backpropagation approach is computationally expensive, yet the paper doesn't provide any analysis of the computational cost versus accuracy gains. This is a significant limitation for practical applications.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify exactly how your attention transfer method differs from knowledge distillation in a fundamental way? The paper states that attention transfer is a "mechanism of transferring knowledge," but it's not clear how this differs from distilling soft targets.

2. You claim that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer." Could you provide a more comprehensive comparison across multiple architectures and datasets to support this claim?

3. You state that "activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Given that Hinton et al. (2015) and Romero et al. (2014) have both applied knowledge distillation to ImageNet, could you clarify this claim and provide a more precise comparison?

4. For the gradient-based attention transfer, how did you handle batch normalization during the second backpropagation step? The paper notes this is "unclear," but you must have implemented some solution for your experiments.

This paper has potential but needs significant revision to clarify its novelty, strengthen its evidence, and correct its factual claims before it can be considered for publication at ICLR.
