

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention mechanisms. The authors define attention as spatial maps that encode where a CNN focuses for making output decisions, and introduce two types of attention maps: activation-based and gradient-based. They propose methods to transfer these attention maps from a teacher network to a student network to improve the student's performance. The key contributions include: (1) a new mechanism for knowledge transfer using attention maps, (2) the use of both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps, (3) experimental validation showing significant improvements across multiple datasets and architectures (including CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet), and (4) the demonstration that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a technically sound contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in knowledge transfer and neural network efficiency. The core idea is well-motivated and supported by rigorous experiments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a clear problem: how to improve smaller student networks by leveraging attention information from larger teacher networks, rather than simply transferring activations or outputs. The authors provide a thorough literature review that properly positions their work within the attention visualization and knowledge distillation literature.

The approach is well-motivated by the observation that modern networks often have excessive depth for many tasks, and that attention mechanisms contain valuable information that could be leveraged for more efficient knowledge transfer. The paper clearly articulates how attention maps differ across layers and how they correlate with network performance.

The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. The experiments on CIFAR datasets show consistent improvements across multiple architectures and settings. The ImageNet results are particularly compelling, demonstrating a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement with minimal hyperparameter tuning. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing different attention mapping functions, the necessity of multiple attention transfer layers, and the comparison between attention transfer and full-activation transfer) provide convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The paper also demonstrates good scientific rigor by exploring both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer methods, providing theoretical formulations for both, and carefully analyzing their properties. The use of multiple datasets and architectures strengthens the generalizability of the claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, consider the following improvements:

1. The claim that "activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" needs stronger justification. A brief survey of prior work on knowledge transfer specifically on ImageNet would help substantiate this claim.

2. The experiments on ImageNet are limited to ResNet-18 as student and ResNet-34 as teacher with attention transfer only on the last two groups. Adding results with attention transfer on all four groups of residual blocks would provide more comprehensive evidence for the ImageNet results.

3. The gradient-based attention transfer section is less developed than the activation-based approach. Including more results on larger datasets or a more thorough comparison with other gradient-based methods would strengthen this part of the paper.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of when attention transfer is most beneficial (e.g., for fine-grained recognition vs. general classification) and why.

5. A discussion of computational cost (beyond noting it adds "very little computational cost") would be helpful for practitioners.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that attention transfer "is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Can you provide a more comprehensive literature review to support this claim, particularly regarding prior attempts at knowledge transfer on ImageNet?

2. In the ImageNet experiments, the authors note they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters." How would the results change with full hyperparameter optimization, and how much improvement might be expected?

3. The paper demonstrates that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer. Could you provide more insight into why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations?

4. The gradient-based attention transfer method requires double backpropagation. How does this method scale to larger architectures and datasets, and what are the practical computational constraints?

5. In the ablation study comparing activation-based attention transfer to full-activation transfer, the authors note that "AT gives much better final accuracy improvement than full-activation transfer." Could you provide more insight into why attention maps contain more useful information for transfer than full activations?
