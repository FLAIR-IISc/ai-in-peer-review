

# Review

## Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions
This paper introduces a novel paradigm for knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps as a new form of knowledge. The authors propose two types of attention maps—activation-based and gradient-based—that capture where a network focuses its processing during inference. They demonstrate that transferring these attention patterns from a teacher network to a student network leads to significant performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, including ImageNet. The key contribution is reframing knowledge transfer not as transferring output distributions (as in traditional knowledge distillation) but as transferring spatial attention patterns, which they show carries more valuable information than full activation transfer. The paper provides experimental evidence across CIFAR and ImageNet datasets, demonstrating that attention transfer outperforms standard knowledge distillation in certain scenarios, particularly on ImageNet where previous methods struggled.

## Decision
Accept

## Supporting Arguments
This paper presents a highly novel and bold approach to knowledge transfer that fundamentally shifts the paradigm from transferring output distributions to transferring spatial attention patterns. While knowledge distillation has been extensively studied, the authors' insight that attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations is both original and conceptually significant. The paper makes a strong case for why this approach is necessary: existing knowledge distillation methods often assume deeper networks always learn better representations, but the authors convincingly argue that capacity (number of parameters) may be more important than depth, and attention transfer provides a new way to transfer knowledge to thinner networks.

The novelty of the approach is substantial—this is not just an incremental improvement to existing methods but a new conceptual direction. The experimental results are compelling, particularly the ImageNet results where attention transfer achieves 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement over ResNet-18 without tuning hyperparameters. The paper's claim that this is the first successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet (despite previous attempts with standard distillation) is well-supported by their experiments and the cited literature. The authors demonstrate that attention transfer works better than full activation transfer, provides significant improvements over standard knowledge distillation on ImageNet, and combines well with distillation.

While the experimental section could benefit from more thorough ablation studies (e.g., why activation-based attention maps outperform gradient-based ones across all tasks), the core idea is strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper's boldness in redefining knowledge transfer, combined with its empirical validation, makes it a significant contribution to the field.

## Additional Feedback
1. The claim that "activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" needs stronger justification. Please cite specific prior work that attempted similar methods on ImageNet and explain why those approaches failed (beyond just stating "KD struggles to work if teacher and student have different architecture/depth").

2. The paper mentions "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet" but doesn't provide a clear explanation. A brief analysis of why KD fails on ImageNet with different architectures (vs. CIFAR) would strengthen the paper.

3. The gradient-based attention transfer section has some technical concerns about the double backpropagation implementation. Please clarify how batch normalization should be handled during the second backpropagation step, as this is critical for reproducing results.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of *why* attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. A brief discussion of the information-theoretic perspective (e.g., attention maps may contain more discriminative features) would add depth.

5. The experimental results on ImageNet are limited to just two network architectures (ResNet-18 as student, ResNet-34 as teacher). Expanding to more architectures would strengthen the generality claim.

## Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide more details about why attention transfer works better than full activation transfer? Is it because attention maps compress the most discriminative information, or is there a different theoretical reason?

2. You state that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer." Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why this is the case, beyond the empirical results?

3. The paper mentions that gradient-based attention transfer with symmetry norm achieved 11.8% error on CIFAR-10, while activation-based AT achieved 11.2%. What specific architectural differences between the teacher and student networks led to this performance gap, and how generalizable is this finding?

4. The paper claims attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Could you provide a more thorough literature review of prior attempts to apply knowledge distillation on ImageNet with different architectures, and explain why those attempts failed?
