

# Review

## Summary
The paper proposes a novel attention transfer mechanism for knowledge distillation in CNNs, using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps. The authors claim that their method can significantly improve student network performance across various datasets and architectures by transferring attention information from a teacher network. The paper presents two main approaches: activation-based attention transfer (using sum/max of absolute values of activations) and gradient-based attention transfer (using input gradients). The authors demonstrate improvements on CIFAR and ImageNet datasets, with the activation-based approach showing particular promise.

## Decision
Reject

## Supporting Arguments
The paper presents an interesting idea, but the execution contains multiple significant issues that undermine the paper's credibility and scientific rigor. These issues are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the potential value of the underlying concept.

1. **Misrepresentation of prior work**: The paper claims "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," which is factually incorrect. Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) has been successfully applied to ImageNet for years. The authors fail to acknowledge this, creating a misleading context for their work. This is a serious methodological and ethical concern.

2. **Mathematical inconsistencies**: The paper contains multiple mathematical inconsistencies that make the method difficult to understand and reproduce:
   - In equation (2), the notation "‖ QjS ‖QjS‖2 − QjT ‖QjT ‖2 ‖p" is mathematically confusing and should be written as "‖ QjS/‖QjS‖2 - QjT/‖QjT‖2 ‖p".
   - In section 3.1, the definition of attention mapping functions uses inconsistent notation (e.g., "F psum(A) = ∑C i=1 |Ai|p" should be "F^p_sum(A) = ∑_{i=1}^C |A_i|^p").
   - The paper claims "the absolute value of a hidden neuron activation can be used as an indication about the importance of that neuron," but provides no theoretical justification for this claim.

3. **Experimental weaknesses**: The paper lacks sufficient experimental rigor:
   - No standard deviations or confidence intervals are reported for results, making it impossible to assess statistical significance.
   - The claim of "significant improvements" on ImageNet (1.1% top-1 improvement) is marginal in the context of modern CNNs, but the paper doesn't contextualize this improvement appropriately.
   - The paper states "we plan to update the paper with losses on all 4 groups of residual blocks" for ImageNet experiments, indicating incomplete validation of their approach.
   - The paper claims gradient-based attention transfer "leads to improved performance" (Table 3), but the numbers show activation-based AT (11.2%) is better than gradient-based AT (12.1%), creating a contradiction in the narrative.

4. **Inconsistent terminology and notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout (e.g., "Network-InNetwork" vs. "Network-In-Network," "F 2sum" vs. "F^2sum"), which makes it difficult to follow the method.

5. **Mischaracterization of prior work**: The paper misrepresents FitNets: "FitNets Romero et al. (2014) tried to learn a thin deep network using a shallow one with more parameters." In reality, FitNets learns a deeper network from a shallower one, not a "thin deep network" from a "shallow one with more parameters."

## Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the misleading claim about being the "first knowledge transfer method on ImageNet" by properly acknowledging prior work (including Hinton et al. 2015 and others).

2. Fix all mathematical notation inconsistencies and clarify the mathematical formulation of attention transfer.

3. Report standard deviations for all results and provide a more nuanced discussion of the significance of the improvements (e.g., 1.1% top-1 improvement on ImageNet is modest).

4. Clarify the relationship between activation-based and gradient-based attention, as the paper's description of these two approaches is confusing.

5. Provide a more detailed ablation study to justify their choice of attention mapping functions (F^psum vs. F^pmax).

6. Resolve the contradiction between the claim that "gradient-based attention transfer leads to improved performance" and the numbers in Table 3.

7. Ensure consistent terminology throughout the paper (e.g., "Network-In-Network" with hyphens, proper use of superscripts).

8. Include all figures referenced in the text (e.g., Fig. 6, Appendix) in the main paper.

9. Clarify the exact meaning of "significant improvements" in the context of modern CNNs.

10. Provide more detailed experimental setup information (e.g., crop sizes for CUB dataset, specific hyperparameter values).

## Clarifying Questions
1. How do the authors justify the claim that "the absolute value of a hidden neuron activation can be used as an indication about the importance of that neuron"? Is there theoretical basis for this, or is it purely empirical?

2. The paper claims "we can assume that proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." How do the authors reconcile this with the fact that knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) has been successfully applied to ImageNet for years?

3. In Table 3, activation-based AT achieves 11.2% error while gradient-based AT achieves 12.1% error. How can the paper claim "using gradient-based attention transfer leads to improved performance" when the numbers show the opposite?

4. The paper states "we plan to update the paper with losses on all 4 groups of residual blocks" for ImageNet experiments. What are the expected benefits of adding losses to all groups, and why weren't these experiments performed before submission?
