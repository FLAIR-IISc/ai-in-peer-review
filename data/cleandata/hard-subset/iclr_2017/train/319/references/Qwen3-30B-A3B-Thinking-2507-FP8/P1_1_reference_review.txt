

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by focusing on attention mechanisms rather than just output distributions. The authors propose two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) to represent where a network focuses its attention when making predictions, and demonstrate how these maps can be transferred from a teacher network to a student network to significantly improve the student's performance. The key contributions include: (1) establishing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) introducing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation. The paper is well-motivated, thoroughly experiments, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important gap in knowledge transfer research by focusing on attention as a transferable knowledge component rather than just output distributions. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed attention transfer approach across multiple datasets and architectures.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's central question—whether attention information can be used to improve student network performance—is well-motivated and timely. The authors effectively position their work within the literature on attention mechanisms (in both human cognition and neural networks), attention visualization techniques, and knowledge distillation. They clearly identify the limitation of current knowledge distillation approaches for thin deep networks, which provides strong justification for their novel attention transfer method.

The experimental results provide compelling evidence for the authors' claims. Across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN), attention transfer consistently outperforms baselines, including knowledge distillation alone. The ablation studies are particularly valuable—they demonstrate that activation-based attention transfer significantly outperforms full activation transfer, and that the choice of attention mapping function (sum vs. max) matters. The paper also provides insightful visualizations showing how student attention maps become more similar to teacher maps after transfer.

The paper's claim that activation-based attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is supported by the authors' careful experimental design and thorough literature review. They explicitly address why knowledge distillation alone doesn't work well on ImageNet with different architectures, which strengthens their position.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further improve the paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations.** The paper states this as a key finding (section 4.1.2) but doesn't provide a theoretical explanation. A brief discussion of why attention maps might capture more discriminative information than full activations would strengthen the paper.

2. **Expand the discussion of computational cost.** The gradient-based attention transfer requires double backpropagation, which is computationally expensive. A brief analysis of the computational overhead compared to standard knowledge distillation would help readers understand the trade-offs.

3. **Include more comparisons with recent knowledge distillation methods.** While the paper compares to standard knowledge distillation (KD), it would be valuable to include comparisons with more recent KD variants (e.g., relational knowledge distillation, mutual knowledge distillation) to better position their contribution.

4. **Clarify the ImageNet results.** The paper claims attention transfer is the first successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet, but it's unclear if this means no prior work has attempted it, or no prior work has succeeded. A brief literature check on whether other knowledge transfer methods have been applied to ImageNet would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper shows that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer, but doesn't explain why. Could the authors provide additional insight into what specific information in attention maps makes them more transferable than full activations?

2. The authors state that knowledge distillation struggles with different architecture/depth teacher-student pairs on ImageNet, while it works well on CIFAR. What do you hypothesize is the key difference between these two settings that causes this discrepancy?

3. For gradient-based attention transfer, the paper mentions batch normalization behavior during second backpropagation is unclear. Could you elaborate on your experiments with batch normalization and your recommended approach for handling it in future work?

4. The paper claims attention transfer is the first successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet. Have you checked if other knowledge transfer methods (e.g., FitNets) have been successfully applied to ImageNet in the literature, or if they're simply less common due to computational constraints?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with clear experimental evidence supporting the effectiveness of attention transfer. With minor revisions addressing the points above, it would be an excellent addition to the ICLR proceedings.
