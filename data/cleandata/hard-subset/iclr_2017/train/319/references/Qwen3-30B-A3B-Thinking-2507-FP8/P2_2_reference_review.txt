

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks using attention mechanisms. The authors define attention as spatial maps that encode where a network focuses its attention for making decisions, and propose two types of attention maps: activation-based (using statistics of hidden neuron activations) and gradient-based (using gradients with respect to input). The key contribution is that attention information contains valuable knowledge that can be transferred from a teacher network to a student network to improve performance beyond traditional knowledge distillation. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NIN), showing that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **weak acceptance** with major revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

### Strengths

The paper addresses an important problem in deep learning: how to transfer knowledge between neural networks more effectively. The idea of using attention maps specifically for knowledge transfer is novel and well-motivated by the literature on attention in both human cognition and neural networks. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their approach and demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, which is compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness.

The experimental results are generally well-designed and cover a wide range of scenarios (different architectures, datasets, and transfer scenarios). The authors show that attention transfer provides significant improvements even when combined with knowledge distillation, and they provide evidence that attention maps contain more useful information than full activation transfers.

### Concerns

Despite these strengths, the paper has several significant concerns that prevent it from being accepted as-is:

1. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" (Section 5), but this is not properly supported. The paper states that "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature," but this is an unverified claim. The authors should provide a more thorough literature review of knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet to properly contextualize their contribution.

2. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper describes gradient-based attention transfer as requiring "double backpropagation," but provides no analysis of the computational cost. The authors claim this has "approximately the same cost with first backpropagation," but this needs to be verified with actual measurements. Without this information, readers cannot assess the practical viability of the method.

3. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: The paper claims that attention maps contain more useful information than full activations but provides limited theoretical explanation for this. The evidence is primarily empirical (Table 1), but without a theoretical foundation, the claim lacks depth.

4. **Incomplete ImageNet results**: The paper's ImageNet results (Section 4.2.2) show a 1.1% top-1 improvement for ResNet-18 with attention transfer, but they don't compare against a properly tuned knowledge distillation baseline using the same hyperparameters. The paper mentions that "KD actually hurts convergence with the same hyperparameters as on CIFAR," but doesn't provide a fair comparison.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Reframe the "first on ImageNet" claim**: Instead of making a strong claim about being the first, the authors should provide a more nuanced comparison with existing knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet. If they truly have the first successful implementation, they need to provide a thorough literature review to support this claim.

2. **Add computational cost analysis**: Include training time and memory usage comparisons between attention transfer and standard knowledge distillation, especially for gradient-based attention transfer. This is critical for practitioners who need to understand the practical trade-offs.

3. **Provide theoretical justification**: Add a section explaining why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations. The authors could discuss how attention maps capture spatial relationships that full activations might miss.

4. **Improve ImageNet experimental setup**: Provide a fair comparison between attention transfer and knowledge distillation on ImageNet with properly tuned hyperparameters. Include training curves and convergence analysis to demonstrate the practical benefits.

5. **Clarify the gradient-based attention section**: The description of gradient-based attention transfer is technically complex but lacks sufficient detail about implementation challenges. The authors should clarify how they handled batch normalization during the second backpropagation step.

6. **Add ablation studies**: Include more ablation studies to show how different attention mapping functions, β values, and layer choices affect performance. This would provide more insight into when and why attention transfer works best.

## Questions for the Authors

1. Can you provide evidence supporting your claim that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet"? What specific knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet have you reviewed, and why do you believe they failed?

2. How did you determine the β values for different datasets and architectures? Is there a systematic approach, or is it primarily heuristic? How sensitive are the results to β?

3. Can you provide more details about the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer? How much longer does training take compared to standard knowledge distillation?

4. Why did you choose to use F^2sum as the default attention mapping function? How do the results change with different mapping functions across different architectures?

5. The paper states that "attention maps focus on different parts for different layers in the network." Can you provide more analysis on how the attention maps change across layers for different tasks (e.g., object recognition vs. fine-grained recognition)?

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the knowledge transfer literature. With the suggested revisions, it could become a strong addition to the field. The core idea of using attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism is promising, but the paper needs to be more rigorous in its experimental design and claims.
