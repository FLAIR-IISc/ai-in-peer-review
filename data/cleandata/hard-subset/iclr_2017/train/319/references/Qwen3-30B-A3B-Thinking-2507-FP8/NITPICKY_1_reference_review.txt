

# Review of "Attention Transfer"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
The paper proposes two novel attention transfer methods (activation-based and gradient-based) for knowledge transfer from a teacher network to a student network. The authors claim that attention maps contain valuable information that can be transferred to improve student network performance, and they demonstrate significant improvements across multiple datasets including CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet. The key contributions are: (1) proposing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) introducing activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps, (3) showing experimental improvements across various architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains a significant factual error that undermines the main contribution claim, and the experimental results on ImageNet are misleadingly presented.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim is that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This is factually incorrect. Hinton et al. (2015), the seminal knowledge distillation paper, explicitly applied their method to ImageNet and achieved significant improvements. The paper itself cites Hinton et al. (2015) in the related work section, acknowledging it as the pioneer of knowledge distillation. The authors cannot claim to be the first to apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet when they themselves cite the original work that did so.

Additionally, the paper claims "ResNet-18 with attention transfer achieved 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 better validation accuracy" on ImageNet. This is misleading because ImageNet top-1 accuracy improvements of 1% are typically considered very small (state-of-the-art models often have differences of 0.5-1.5% at most), and the paper fails to provide statistical significance testing or comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art methods on ImageNet. The claimed improvement appears marginal in the context of current ImageNet benchmarks.

The paper also states "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet," which contradicts the extensive literature on knowledge distillation on ImageNet. The authors' failure to achieve positive results with KD may be due to their specific experimental setup (e.g., hyperparameter choices), but this does not justify the claim that KD has not been successfully applied on ImageNet.

The paper contains several mathematical notation inconsistencies that make the approach difficult to follow (e.g., inconsistent use of subscripts in equations, unclear notation for tensors). While these are minor issues, they compound the paper's credibility problems.

## 4. Additional Feedback
1. **Correct the factual error**: The claim about being the first to apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet must be removed or significantly revised. The paper should acknowledge that knowledge distillation has been successfully applied on ImageNet and clarify how their approach differs from previous work.

2. **Re-evaluate ImageNet results**: The paper should provide a more meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art methods on ImageNet, including statistical significance testing. A 1.1% improvement is not necessarily significant in the context of ImageNet, and the paper should clarify whether this improvement is statistically significant and whether it represents a meaningful advance.

3. **Improve mathematical notation**: The paper should use consistent mathematical notation throughout. For example, equation (2) should be written as Q^j_S = vec(F(A^j_S)) rather than QjS = vec(F (A j S)), which is confusing.

4. **Remove unprofessional statements**: The phrase "we do not have time to tune any hyperparameters" is unprofessional and should be removed. Hyperparameter tuning is a standard part of the research process.

5. **Clarify relationship to prior work**: The paper should more clearly differentiate its approach from FitNets and other knowledge distillation methods. The paper claims "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations," but this claim is not sufficiently supported by the experiments.

6. **Verify claims about gradient-based attention**: The paper claims "gradient-based attention transfer leads to improved performance" but provides limited evidence. The results in Table 3 show a 2.3% absolute improvement on CIFAR-10, which is significant, but the paper should provide more detailed analysis of why gradient-based attention works.

7. **Fix reference formatting**: The references have inconsistent formatting (e.g., extra commas, inconsistent use of "In" vs "Proceedings of," and strange formatting like "? \Q2014\E"). This should be corrected.

8. **Clarify the "first" claim**: The paper's claim about being the first to apply attention transfer on ImageNet is incorrect, but it might be possible to reframe the claim as "the first to apply attention transfer to ImageNet using activation-based methods" or similar, provided this is accurate. However, given the Hinton et al. (2015) citation, the paper should not claim to be the first to apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your attention transfer method specifically differ from knowledge distillation in Hinton et al. (2015), which was successfully applied on ImageNet?

2. What statistical significance tests were performed on the ImageNet results, and how do the authors justify that a 1.1% improvement in top-1 accuracy is meaningful?

3. The paper states "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet" with the same hyperparameters as on CIFAR. Can the authors provide details on the exact hyperparameters used for ImageNet KD and why they failed, compared to the successful CIFAR results?

4. The paper claims that attention maps carry more important information for transfer than full activations. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of why this is the case, beyond the limited experimental evidence provided?

5. How do the authors reconcile the claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" with the fact that Hinton et al. (2015) successfully applied knowledge distillation on ImageNet?
