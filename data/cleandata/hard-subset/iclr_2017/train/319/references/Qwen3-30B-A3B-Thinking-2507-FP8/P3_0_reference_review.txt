

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by focusing on attention mechanisms rather than standard feature or output knowledge distillation. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps—activation-based and gradient-based—and demonstrate that transferring these attention patterns from a teacher to a student network can significantly improve student performance across various datasets and architectures. The key contributions include: (1) establishing attention as a valuable knowledge transfer mechanism, (2) proposing two attention map definitions, (3) showing consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets and network architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

The paper is well-motivated by the observation that modern networks with similar depth but different capacity (e.g., wider vs. deeper) can learn comparable representations, suggesting that knowledge transfer should focus on information beyond mere depth. The authors provide compelling visual evidence that attention maps correlate with network performance and contain spatial information that can guide student training.

## 2. Decision

**Reject** with the following key reasons:
1. The claim that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is unsubstantiated and contradicts existing literature (FitNets was applied to ImageNet in Romero et al. 2014).
2. The experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous, particularly for the ImageNet results, which were not properly tuned and lack meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art knowledge transfer methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution—attention transfer as a knowledge transfer mechanism—is interesting and potentially valuable. However, the paper fails to properly situate its approach in the existing literature and provides inadequate evidence to support its key claims.

First, the claim about being the "first knowledge transfer method on ImageNet" is problematic. Romero et al. (2014) explicitly applied FitNets (a knowledge transfer method using intermediate layer features) to ImageNet. The authors' assertion that "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature" is factually incorrect, undermining their credibility. This misrepresentation of the literature is a significant weakness.

Second, the experimental results for ImageNet are weakly supported. The authors state they "did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" and kept them from finetuning experiments. This is unacceptable for a paper claiming significant improvements on ImageNet, which is a major benchmark. Their reported 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18 with attention transfer is marginal compared to the typical 2-3% improvements seen in the literature with proper hyperparameter tuning.

Third, the paper lacks a clear theoretical justification for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. While they show empirically that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer, they don't explain why this is the case. This is a fundamental limitation for a paper claiming to establish attention as a new knowledge transfer mechanism.

Fourth, the gradient-based attention transfer section is underdeveloped. The paper describes a method that resembles "double backpropagation" (Drucker & LeCun, 1992) but doesn't compare it to existing methods that use similar techniques. The experimental results for gradient-based transfer on CIFAR are promising but not compared to relevant baselines.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Literature Review:** Correct the misrepresentation of prior work on knowledge transfer for ImageNet. FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) was indeed applied to ImageNet, and this should be properly acknowledged and compared to.

2. **Experimental Rigor:** For the ImageNet results, conduct a proper hyperparameter search and include comparisons to state-of-the-art knowledge transfer methods (e.g., FitNets, AT, or other attention-based methods). The current results are insufficient to support the claim of "significant improvements."

3. **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more rigorous explanation for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. Consider analyzing the information content of attention maps versus full activations using information theory metrics.

4. **Ablation Study:** Expand the ablation study to include comparisons with other attention-based knowledge transfer methods (e.g., recent work on attention transfer in vision transformers).

5. **Clarity on "Why Attention Transfer Works":** The paper claims attention maps contain "valuable information" but doesn't explain what this information is or how it differs from the information in full activations. This is a critical gap that needs to be addressed.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," yet FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) was applied to ImageNet. Could you clarify this discrepancy and provide a proper comparison to FitNets on ImageNet?

2. The ImageNet results for ResNet-18 with attention transfer show only 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement. How does this compare to the typical improvements seen with standard knowledge distillation (e.g., Hinton et al., 2015) on ImageNet when properly tuned? Did you use the same hyperparameters as Hinton et al.?

3. The paper states that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations." Could you provide a theoretical explanation for this phenomenon, or at least a more detailed empirical analysis of what specific information is being transferred?

4. In Table 3, the gradient-based attention transfer (grad-based AT) shows 11.8% error, while activation-based AT shows 11.2%. Given that the authors claim activation-based attention transfer is superior, why does the gradient-based method perform better in this case? Is this an anomaly or a general trend?

5. The paper mentions that "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature." Could you provide specific references to the literature you reviewed that led to this conclusion? This seems to contradict established work in the field.

This paper has interesting ideas but requires substantial revision to properly situate its contribution in the literature and provide rigorous experimental validation of its claims. Without addressing these issues, the paper's contribution remains unclear and its claims unsubstantiated.
