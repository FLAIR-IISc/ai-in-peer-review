- Proposes attention-based knowledge transfer using spatial attention maps to improve student networks.  
- Introduces two attention map types: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Shows activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.  
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements across datasets (CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, WRN, NiN).  
- Provides thorough experimental validation including ablation studies on attention mapping functions.  
- Recommends acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Strength: well-motivated connection to human and neural attention literature.  
- Strength: extensive experiments across transfer scenarios and architectures.  
- Concern: Claim that activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method applied on ImageNet is not sufficiently supported.  
- Concern: Gradient-based attention experiments on CIFAR omit batch normalization, which is nonstandard and needs justification or rerun.  
- Concern: Insufficient details about KD training setup/hyperparameters, especially regarding slower KD convergence on ImageNet.  
- Suggestion: Expand analysis explaining why attention maps transfer better than full activations.  
- Suggestion: Quantify computational cost of attention transfer (runtime/overhead).  
- Suggestion: Clarify relationship to other attention visualization methods (e.g., Grad-CAM).  
- Questions for authors: provide evidence for the ImageNet claim, justify omission of batch norm, share KD hyperparameters on ImageNet, and analyze what specific information in attention maps aids transfer.
