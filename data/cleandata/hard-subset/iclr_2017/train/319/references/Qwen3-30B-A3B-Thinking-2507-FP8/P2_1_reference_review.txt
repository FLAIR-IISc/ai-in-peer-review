

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer approach that uses attention mechanisms as the transferable signal between neural networks. The authors define attention as spatial maps indicating where a network focuses its processing, and propose two types of attention maps: activation-based and gradient-based. They demonstrate that transferring these attention maps from a teacher to a student network leads to significant performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures, including ImageNet. The key contributions are: (1) proposing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) introducing both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps, (3) showing consistent performance improvements across diverse datasets and architectures, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

**Accept (with minor revisions)**

The paper makes a compelling case for attention as a knowledge transfer signal, with strong experimental evidence supporting their claims. The novelty lies in recognizing that attention maps contain valuable information beyond just the final predictions, and demonstrating that this information can be effectively transferred to improve student network performance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The introduction effectively connects human attention mechanisms to artificial attention, providing a strong theoretical foundation for their approach. The related work section thoroughly covers attention mechanisms, attention visualization techniques, and knowledge distillation, establishing a clear position for their contribution.

The experimental results are generally strong and consistent. The authors demonstrate improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), showing that attention transfer provides significant benefits over baseline methods. The ablation studies are particularly convincing - they show that attention transfer is superior to transferring full activations (FitNets-style), and they carefully analyze different attention mapping functions (sum-based vs. max-based), with sum-based functions performing better.

The paper's claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is supported by their experimental results. Their ImageNet results (1.1% top-1 improvement) are notable as they're the first successful knowledge transfer on ImageNet, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with good visualizations of attention maps that help illustrate their concepts. The methodological details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following minor revisions to strengthen it further:

1. **Statistical Significance**: The paper reports "median of 5 runs" in Table 2 but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the improvements. Adding p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the empirical claims.

2. **ImageNet Comparison**: The claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" needs more evidence. The authors should provide a more comprehensive comparison with other knowledge distillation methods on ImageNet to fully support this claim.

3. **Gradient-Based Attention**: The paper acknowledges technical challenges with gradient-based attention transfer (particularly regarding batch normalization in second backpropagation). The authors should discuss these limitations more thoroughly and potentially provide a more detailed analysis of when gradient-based attention is beneficial versus when activation-based is better.

4. **Computational Cost**: The paper mentions that attention transfer "adds very little computational cost" compared to knowledge distillation, but doesn't quantify this. Providing a comparison of training times or memory usage between standard KD and their method would be helpful.

5. **Questions for Authors**:
   - How does your attention transfer method compare to other recent knowledge distillation techniques (e.g., using feature maps or intermediate layers) on ImageNet?
   - Could you clarify why knowledge distillation struggles on ImageNet with different architectures, and how your attention transfer method overcomes this limitation?
   - Do you observe any differences in attention transfer effectiveness when transferring between networks with different architectural components (e.g., ResNet vs. Inception)?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of knowledge distillation. The attention transfer approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated across multiple challenging benchmarks. The minor revisions suggested would make an already strong paper even more compelling for the ICLR audience.
