- Proposes attention-based knowledge transfer using activation- and gradient-based spatial attention maps.  
- Shows activation-based attention transfer improves student performance and can combine with knowledge distillation.  
- Claims attention transfer is a valuable alternative to full activation transfer with visual evidence across datasets and architectures.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Major reason: Incorrect claim of being the first method applied to ImageNet; FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) contradicts this.  
- Major reason: Insufficient experimental rigor on ImageNet—no hyperparameter tuning and weak comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.  
- Reported ImageNet gain (1.1% top-1) is marginal relative to typical tuned gains (2–3%).  
- Paper lacks theoretical justification for why attention maps are more transferable than full activations.  
- Gradient-based attention transfer section is underdeveloped and resembles prior “double backpropagation” work without proper comparisons.  
- Experimental comparisons for gradient-based transfer are incomplete and missing relevant baselines.  
- Recommendation: Correct literature review to acknowledge FitNets and related prior work.  
- Recommendation: Perform proper hyperparameter search on ImageNet and compare to SOTA transfer methods.  
- Recommendation: Provide theoretical or information-theoretic analysis explaining why attention maps transfer better.  
- Recommendation: Expand ablation studies to include other attention-based transfer methods and clarify what information attention maps capture.  
- Questions for authors: Clarify the FitNets/ImageNet discrepancy and provide proper comparisons; report tuning details and KD baselines; explain why gradient-based AT sometimes outperforms activation-based AT; list literature reviewed supporting original claims.
