

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method using attention maps from a teacher network to improve the performance of a student network. The authors define attention as spatial maps indicating where a CNN focuses during decision-making, and propose two types of attention maps: activation-based (using statistics of neuron activations) and gradient-based (using gradients with respect to input). They demonstrate that attention transfer consistently improves student network performance across multiple datasets and architectures (CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet), with activation-based attention transfer outperforming full-activation transfer and being compatible with traditional knowledge distillation. The paper makes a clear contribution by introducing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism beyond standard output-based distillation.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound approach that makes a novel contribution to knowledge distillation literature with comprehensive experimental validation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper clearly addresses a gap in knowledge distillation research: most methods transfer output logits or intermediate activations without considering spatial attention patterns. The authors provide strong motivation by connecting to psychological theories of attention and demonstrating that attention maps correlate with network performance. Their experimental validation is thorough across multiple datasets (CIFAR-10, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), with ablation studies that support their claims about attention map properties. The activation-based attention transfer consistently outperforms baseline student networks and full-activation transfer (Table 1), with improvements of up to 1.1% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet. The paper also provides clear theoretical justification for their attention transfer loss formulation (equation 2) and demonstrates compatibility with knowledge distillation.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with strong connections to prior work on attention visualization (Grad-CAM, CAM) and knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., FitNets). The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods and why attention information is valuable for knowledge transfer.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- For the ImageNet results, the paper states "we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" - while the results are promising, providing a more thorough hyperparameter search would strengthen the claims, especially for the ImageNet experiments.
- The claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" would be more convincing with a direct comparison to other knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet (e.g., FitNets, other distillation approaches).
- The gradient-based attention transfer section could benefit from more discussion of computational costs beyond "second backpropagation has approximately the same cost with first backpropagation."
- The paper mentions "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" but doesn't provide clear evidence of why this is the case. A deeper analysis of what information attention maps capture that full activations don't would strengthen the contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In Table 5, the ImageNet results show a 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement for ResNet-18 with attention transfer. How does this compare to state-of-the-art knowledge distillation results on ImageNet for comparable network sizes?
2. The paper states that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer." Can you provide more insight into why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations?
3. For gradient-based attention transfer, the paper mentions "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Could you elaborate on this issue and whether you found any practical solutions during your experiments?
4. The paper mentions that "on CIFAR both AT and KD work well in this case and improve convergence and final accuracy, on ImageNet though KD converges significantly slower." Could you provide more details about the specific hyperparameters used for KD on ImageNet that led to this behavior?
