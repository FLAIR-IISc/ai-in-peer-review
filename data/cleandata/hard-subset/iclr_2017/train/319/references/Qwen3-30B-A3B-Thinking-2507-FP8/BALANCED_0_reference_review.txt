

**Review**

1. **Summary of contributions**: The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging spatial attention maps, both activation-based and gradient-based. The authors claim that attention maps contain valuable information that can significantly improve student network performance, and demonstrate this across multiple architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network) and datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, Scenes, CUB). They show that attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation to achieve better results. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms in neural networks.

2. **Decision**: Reject. The main reason for rejection is that the paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for the proposed attention transfer mechanism, and the empirical results, while promising, are not sufficiently rigorous or comprehensive to justify the claims made.

3. **Supporting arguments**:
   - The paper claims that attention maps contain "valuable information" that can improve performance, but provides no theoretical justification for why this should be the case. The authors observe correlations between attention maps and network performance but do not establish causality or provide a principled explanation for why attention transfer works.
   - The experimental section has several issues:
     * The paper claims that attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this is not supported by a thorough literature review. The authors cite that "KD struggles to work if teacher and student have different architecture/depth" but don't provide evidence that previous work didn't address this issue.
     * The ImageNet experiments are underpowered - they only add two losses in the last two groups of residual blocks, and don't tune hyperparameters. The claim of "1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 better validation accuracy" is presented without proper statistical significance testing or comparison to strong baselines.
     * The gradient-based attention transfer section has implementation concerns: the paper states "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step," yet they proceed with experiments without addressing this fundamental issue.
     * The paper claims that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations," but the comparison between attention transfer and full activation transfer (FitNets-style) is not properly controlled - they use different network architectures for the two methods.

4. **Additional feedback for improvement**:
   - Provide a stronger theoretical foundation for why attention transfer should work. What is the underlying principle that makes attention maps more useful for transfer than full activations?
   - Conduct more comprehensive experiments on ImageNet, including hyperparameter tuning and testing on all four groups of residual blocks as mentioned in the paper.
   - Address the batch normalization issue in gradient-based attention transfer experiments. If the authors cannot resolve this, they should remove the gradient-based experiments or clearly state the limitations.
   - Provide statistical significance testing for all reported results, especially on ImageNet where the improvements are marginal.
   - Compare more thoroughly with existing knowledge distillation methods on ImageNet to properly justify the claim that this is the "first" successful method.

5. **Questions for authors**:
   - The paper claims attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Can you provide a more comprehensive literature review that addresses this claim, particularly regarding knowledge distillation methods that have been applied to ImageNet?
   - For the gradient-based attention transfer, the paper states "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have you explored alternative implementations of batch normalization during the second backpropagation to resolve this issue?
   - The paper states that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations." Can you provide a more rigorous comparison between attention transfer and full activation transfer (FitNets-style), controlling for network architecture and hyperparameters?
   - The ImageNet results show a 1.1% improvement in top-1 accuracy. How does this compare to the state-of-the-art on ImageNet for the same student architecture? What is the statistical significance of this improvement?

The paper presents an interesting idea that could be valuable for the community, but the current version lacks the rigor needed for publication at ICLR. With additional theoretical grounding, more comprehensive experiments, and better statistical analysis, this could be a strong contribution to the field.
