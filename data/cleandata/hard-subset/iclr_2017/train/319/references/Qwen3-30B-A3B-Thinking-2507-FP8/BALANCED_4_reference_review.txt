

# Review

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks using attention maps. The authors define attention as spatial maps indicating where a network focuses for its output decision, proposing two types: activation-based and gradient-based. They claim these attention maps contain valuable information that can significantly improve student network performance when transferred from a teacher network. The paper demonstrates that attention transfer (AT) provides consistent improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN), outperforming full-activation transfer and sometimes knowledge distillation. The authors also show that activation-based AT works better than gradient-based AT and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents interesting empirical results but lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and comprehensive experimental validation to support its core claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The primary weakness is the lack of clear theoretical justification for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. While the authors demonstrate that AT outperforms full-activation transfer (F-ActT) in experiments, they don't establish *why* this is the case. The paper claims "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" but provides no theoretical explanation or analysis of what specific information is being transferred. Without this, the core contribution remains empirical rather than conceptual.

The experimental validation is also insufficient. For ImageNet, the authors claim AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" but fail to provide adequate comparisons to other methods (e.g., FitNets, KD with different hyperparameters, or other attention-based methods). The ImageNet results are preliminary (only two losses on the last two residual blocks), and the paper doesn't explain why KD fails on ImageNet while working well on CIFAR. The authors note "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature," but this is an insufficient justification for claiming their method is the first to work on ImageNet.

The gradient-based attention transfer section has methodological concerns. The authors use a thin NIN model without batch normalization for experiments, which they acknowledge is different from the teacher network. They don't properly address how batch normalization would affect gradient-based attention transfer, which is a significant limitation given that batch normalization is standard in modern networks.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a theoretical analysis explaining why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. This could involve analyzing the information content of attention maps versus full activations, or examining which features of the attention maps correlate with performance gains.

2. Conduct more comprehensive ImageNet experiments. This should include:
   - Comparing AT with other knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet
   - Testing AT with batch normalization
   - Using more comprehensive loss placement (all residual blocks, not just the last two)
   - Providing ablation studies on hyperparameters

3. Clarify the relationship between attention maps and network performance. The paper notes that "stronger networks have peaks in attention where weak networks don't" but doesn't explore whether these attention patterns correlate with specific performance improvements.

4. Address the claim that AT is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Provide proper comparisons to existing methods and explain why they fail on ImageNet (e.g., due to architectural differences or hyperparameter sensitivity).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer, but doesn't explain why. What specific properties of attention maps (beyond just being normalized) make them more transferable?

2. The ImageNet results are preliminary. How would the performance change if you applied attention transfer to all four groups of residual blocks (not just the last two), as you mention planning to do? Would this significantly improve the results?

3. You note that KD struggles on ImageNet when using different architectures (ResNet-18 vs ResNet-34), but works well on CIFAR. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why this architectural mismatch affects KD but not AT? Is there a specific property of attention maps that makes them more robust to architectural differences?

4. The paper states that "attention maps focus on different parts for different layers in the network." How does this layer-specific attention information contribute to the transfer learning process? Could you provide specific examples from your experiments where different layers' attention maps contributed to different aspects of performance?
