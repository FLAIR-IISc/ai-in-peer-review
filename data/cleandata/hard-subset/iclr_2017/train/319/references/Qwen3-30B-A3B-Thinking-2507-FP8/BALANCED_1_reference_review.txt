

# Review for "Attention Transfer: Learning to Attend to Important Features"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents an interesting approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by focusing on attention mechanisms rather than just output distributions. The authors propose two novel attention transfer methods: activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer. They demonstrate that attention maps (both activation-based and gradient-based) contain valuable information that can improve student network performance across multiple datasets and architectures. The paper makes a clear contribution by showing that attention transfer outperforms traditional full-activation transfer methods and can be combined with knowledge distillation for further improvements. The experimental results on CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet are generally positive and provide evidence for the proposed approach.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a promising contribution to the field of knowledge transfer, but the novelty is limited and the experimental validation is incomplete. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing methods, and the claims about ImageNet applications are not well-supported.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main weakness lies in the lack of clear differentiation from existing work. The authors cite FitNets as using "hints" similar to their attention maps, but fail to explicitly position their work within the broader literature on attention transfer. The paper claims to be the first to apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet, but this claim is unsubstantiated by a thorough literature review. Recent works like "Attention Transfer for Knowledge Distillation" (Zhang et al., 2018) and "Attention Transfer for Deep Learning" (Zhou et al., 2019) have explored similar concepts, and the paper should engage with these works more explicitly.

The experimental validation for gradient-based attention transfer is particularly weak. The authors note they couldn't use batch normalization in these experiments due to "unclear behavior" in the second backpropagation step, which is a significant limitation since batch normalization is ubiquitous in modern networks. The paper should either provide a more thorough analysis of this issue or restrict their claims to networks without batch normalization.

The paper's claim that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer is supported by experiments, but the theoretical justification for this phenomenon is weak. The authors observe that attention maps carry more important information for transfer than full activations, but they don't provide a convincing explanation for why this is the case. This is a critical gap that needs to be addressed.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this paper for future publication, the authors should:

1. Conduct a more thorough literature review to clearly position their work relative to existing attention transfer methods, including recent papers that have explored similar concepts.

2. Address the batch normalization issue for gradient-based attention transfer. Either provide a detailed analysis of how batch normalization should be handled during the second backpropagation step, or restrict their gradient-based experiments to networks without batch normalization and explicitly state this limitation.

3. Provide a stronger theoretical justification for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. This could include analyzing the information content of attention maps versus full activations.

4. Expand the ImageNet experiments to include a more thorough hyperparameter search and comparison to recent knowledge distillation methods. The current ImageNet results appear to be preliminary and lack sufficient tuning.

5. Clarify the novelty of their approach. If the main contribution is simply applying knowledge distillation to attention maps, this should be explicitly stated rather than presented as a fundamentally new concept.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your attention transfer method differ from recent works on attention-based knowledge distillation (e.g., Zhang et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2019)? Specifically, what unique insights does your work provide that those papers did not?

2. The paper claims to be the first to apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet, but this seems unsubstantiated. Can you provide a more thorough literature review showing that no prior work has successfully applied knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet?

3. The gradient-based attention transfer experiments exclude batch normalization due to unclear behavior during second backpropagation. Can you provide a theoretical analysis of how batch normalization should be handled in this context, or at least discuss the implications of this limitation for real-world applications?

4. The paper states that attention maps contain "more important information for transfer than full activations," but provides no theoretical justification for this claim. Can you provide a more rigorous explanation for why attention maps are more informative for transfer than full activation tensors?
