

# Review of "Attention Transfer"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks using attention maps. Specifically, it introduces two methods for attention transfer: activation-based (using statistics of hidden neuron activations) and gradient-based (using gradients with respect to input). The authors claim that their method can significantly improve student network performance compared to traditional knowledge distillation, with improvements of up to 1.1% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet. The paper presents experimental results across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), arguing that attention maps contain more useful information for transfer than full activations.

## 2. Decision

Reject.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling claim that attention maps can be used for knowledge transfer, but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support this claim with the rigor expected by the ICLR community. My decision to reject is based on two key concerns:

First, the empirical evidence supporting the claimed improvements is insufficiently rigorous. The reported improvements on ImageNet (1.1% top-1 accuracy) are marginal in the context of current computer vision research, where such small gains are typically not considered significant. The paper compares against limited baselines (primarily knowledge distillation) and doesn't demonstrate consistent, substantial improvements across diverse architectures and tasks. For instance, the paper claims attention transfer "is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this contradicts the extensive literature on knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and other transfer learning methods that have been successfully applied on ImageNet.

Second, the theoretical justification for why attention maps would be better than other forms of knowledge transfer is weak. The paper cites human attention as motivation but fails to establish a clear connection between human attention mechanisms and the computational approach they propose. The paper claims that "attention maps contain valuable information that can be used for significantly improving the performance of convolutional neural network architectures," but provides minimal theoretical grounding for this assertion. The evidence for why attention maps would be more useful than other forms of knowledge transfer (e.g., intermediate layer activations, logits) is limited to a single comparison on CIFAR-10 (Table 1), which is insufficient to support such a broad claim.

The paper's overstatement of the novelty and significance of their approach is particularly concerning. The activation-based attention transfer is essentially a variant of knowledge distillation with a different loss function, and the gradient-based method requires computationally expensive double backpropagation. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why these approaches would be preferable to established methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. The paper should provide a more thorough comparison with established knowledge transfer methods. For example, how does attention transfer compare to other distillation approaches (e.g., FitNets, CRD) on ImageNet? The marginal 1.1% improvement on ImageNet is not impressive when compared to the large body of work on knowledge distillation that has achieved much larger gains on ImageNet.

2. The paper should provide a theoretical justification for why attention maps would be better than other forms of knowledge transfer. The current justification is largely based on empirical observations without clear explanation of why attention maps contain "more valuable information."

3. The paper claims that attention transfer "is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," which is factually incorrect. Knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and other methods have been successfully applied on ImageNet. The paper should revise this claim and properly situate their work within the existing literature.

4. The gradient-based attention transfer method requires double backpropagation, which is computationally expensive. The paper should provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of this method compared to standard training and other distillation approaches, including training time and memory requirements.

5. The paper should clarify why their method works better than knowledge distillation. The paper claims attention transfer improves performance, but doesn't explain why attention maps would be more useful than the logits used in standard knowledge distillation.

6. The paper should provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component of their method (e.g., the effect of using different attention mapping functions, the effect of different layers for attention transfer).

7. The paper should address why knowledge distillation fails on ImageNet with ResNet-18-ResNet-34 pairs while attention transfer succeeds. This is a critical question that needs to be answered to justify their approach.

To improve this paper, the authors should focus on providing stronger evidence for their claims, properly situating their work within the existing literature, and addressing the methodological concerns raised above. Without these improvements, the paper's contributions remain marginal and insufficiently justified for publication in ICLR.
