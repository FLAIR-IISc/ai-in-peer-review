

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by focusing on the transfer of attention information from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define two types of attention maps—activation-based and gradient-based—and demonstrate that transferring these spatial attention maps can significantly improve the performance of smaller student networks across multiple datasets and architectures. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) it conceptualizes attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer; (2) it introduces both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps; (3) it shows that activation-based attention transfer provides significant improvements across various datasets and architectures, including ImageNet; and (4) it demonstrates that attention transfer can be combined with knowledge distillation.

The authors provide thorough empirical validation of their approach across CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, with experiments comparing different attention mapping functions and transfer strategies. Their results consistently show that attention transfer outperforms both standard knowledge distillation and full-activation transfer methods, with particularly strong results on ImageNet where they claim to be the first to successfully apply knowledge transfer to this scale.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of knowledge transfer by introducing a new perspective on how attention information can be leveraged to improve network performance. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by comprehensive experimental evidence across multiple datasets and architectures.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's approach is well-motivated by the human attention mechanism and properly situated within the literature. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous knowledge transfer approaches (like FitNets) and position their work as complementary to knowledge distillation rather than a replacement. Their theoretical foundation for attention maps is sound, and the experimental design is rigorous.

The empirical results are particularly compelling. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across different architectures and datasets, with the most significant results on ImageNet where they achieve 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement over baseline ResNet-18. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing different attention mapping functions, transfer loss placements, and the combination of attention transfer with knowledge distillation) provide strong evidence for the key components of their approach.

The paper's claim that attention maps contain more valuable information for transfer than full activations is well-supported by their results, which show that attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer despite the latter being a more direct form of information transfer. The authors provide a clear explanation for this phenomenon, noting that attention maps capture the most discriminative regions of the input, which is more valuable for learning than the full activation tensor.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the novelty claim**: The paper states "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This claim needs stronger justification. Several knowledge transfer methods have been applied to ImageNet (e.g., FitNets, knowledge distillation), so the authors should provide a more precise comparison that clarifies what specific methods they've ruled out and why they believe their approach is uniquely successful on ImageNet.

2. **Expand theoretical justification**: While the empirical results are strong, a more formal theoretical argument for why attention maps contain valuable transferable information would strengthen the contribution. The authors could explore the relationship between attention maps and feature representations or provide a more principled explanation for why attention maps are more transferable than full activations.

3. **Provide more details on computational cost**: The paper mentions that gradient-based attention transfer involves double backpropagation, which has "approximately the same cost with first backpropagation." This should be quantified with actual timing measurements to help readers understand the practical implications of using this method.

4. **Include more analysis of attention maps**: The paper includes visualizations of attention maps, but it would be valuable to provide more analysis of how the transferred attention maps differ from the student's original attention maps and how these differences correlate with performance improvements.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer." Could you provide more insight into why attention maps contain more valuable information than full activations, beyond the empirical results?

2. Regarding the ImageNet results, you state "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet." Could you clarify whether this is due to hyperparameter sensitivity or fundamental limitations of knowledge distillation on ImageNet? Have you tried standard KD hyperparameters for ImageNet?

3. The paper mentions that "we plan to update the paper with losses on all 4 groups of residual blocks" for ImageNet results. Could you provide an estimate of how much additional improvement this might bring, and whether the current results (with only 2 losses) already represent the optimal transfer configuration?

4. For the gradient-based attention transfer, you state "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have you explored specific techniques for handling batch normalization during the second backpropagation, and what were the results?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of knowledge transfer, and with the suggested clarifications, it will be an even stronger addition to the ICLR proceedings. The authors have demonstrated a novel and effective approach to improving network performance through attention transfer, with results that are both theoretically interesting and practically valuable.
