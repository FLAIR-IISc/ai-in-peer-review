

# Review for "Attention Transfer for Knowledge Distillation"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge distillation by transferring attention information from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define two types of attention maps—activation-based and gradient-based—and demonstrate how these can be used to improve the performance of smaller student networks across various datasets and architectures. The key contributions include: (1) establishing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) proposing activation-based and gradient-based attention maps, (3) showing significant performance improvements across multiple datasets, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer. The paper provides thorough experimental validation on CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet datasets, with ablation studies examining different attention mapping functions and transfer strategies.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution to knowledge distillation, but the claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous experimental validation and theoretical justification. The novelty is overstated, and the paper fails to adequately address key limitations that would be required for acceptance at ICLR.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main weakness lies in the limited theoretical justification for why attention maps would be more effective than other knowledge distillation approaches. The authors claim that attention maps carry "more important information for transfer than full activations," but provide insufficient evidence to support this assertion. The comparison between attention transfer and full-activation transfer (FitNets-style) is limited to a single experiment (Table 1) without sufficient ablation studies to isolate the factors contributing to the performance difference.

Furthermore, the claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is problematic. Knowledge distillation methods like FitNets, Hinton's KD, and others have been successfully applied on ImageNet, as evidenced by numerous papers in the literature (e.g., Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016; Romero et al., 2014). The paper does not properly contextualize its approach within this existing work.

The experimental validation on ImageNet is also limited. The authors state that "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet" and claim that "KD converges significantly slower," but they provide no comparative analysis with other distillation methods or ablation studies on hyperparameter sensitivity. This undermines their claim about the novelty of their approach on ImageNet.

The connection to psychological attention mechanisms is mentioned in the introduction but is not meaningfully integrated into the technical contribution, which weakens the motivation for the proposed approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper significantly, the authors should:

1. **Re-evaluate novelty claims**: Provide a more comprehensive literature review that properly contextualizes their approach within existing knowledge distillation methods on ImageNet. The claim about being "the first" is unsubstantiated and should be removed or thoroughly justified.

2. **Expand theoretical justification**: Provide a more rigorous explanation for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. This could include analyzing the information content of attention maps versus full activations using information-theoretic measures.

3. **Improve experimental validation**: 
   - Conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of attention transfer versus other components (e.g., normalization, symmetry constraints).
   - Compare against a wider range of distillation methods on ImageNet, not just KD.
   - Provide statistical significance testing across multiple random seeds for all results.
   - Include more detailed analysis of the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer.

4. **Strengthen connection to psychology**: If the authors wish to maintain the psychological motivation, they should more clearly articulate how their technical approach relates to psychological findings about attention, rather than merely citing human attention as motivation.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide a more detailed comparison between your attention transfer method and other knowledge distillation approaches (e.g., FitNets, Hinton's KD) on ImageNet? Specifically, how do your results compare with those reported in Zagoruyko & Komodakis (2016) and Romero et al. (2014)?

2. The paper claims that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" (Section 4.1.2), but this is only demonstrated through a single experiment on CIFAR. Could you provide more evidence for this claim, perhaps through information-theoretic analysis of the attention maps versus full activations?

3. You state that "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet" (Section 4.2.2). What specific hyperparameters did you use for KD, and how do they compare to those used in the literature? Could you share the training curves for KD on ImageNet to demonstrate the convergence issues you observed?

4. The paper mentions that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" (Section 1). Can you provide more insight into why this is the case, particularly regarding the relationship between attention maps and the network's decision boundaries?

5. In Section 4.1.3, you note that "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step required during gradient-based attention transfer." Could you elaborate on this challenge and how it might affect the applicability of your gradient-based method to standard CNN architectures?
