

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps as a mechanism for information transfer. The authors propose two types of attention maps—activation-based and gradient-based—and demonstrate how these can be used to transfer knowledge from a teacher network to a student network. Their key claim is that attention maps contain more valuable information for transfer than full activations or logits, leading to significant performance improvements across multiple datasets and architectures. The paper presents experimental evidence showing that attention transfer (AT) provides consistent improvements over baselines, with the best results achieved when combined with knowledge distillation. The authors position their work as a new direction in knowledge transfer that challenges the conventional wisdom that deeper networks always learn better representations.

## Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution—using attention maps as a knowledge transfer mechanism—is highly novel and aligns perfectly with the reviewer's priorities. While knowledge distillation has been extensively studied, the specific approach of transferring attention information rather than full activations or logits represents a bold new direction. The authors convincingly demonstrate that attention maps carry information that is more useful for transfer than full activations (as shown in Table 1 and Figure 7), which is a significant insight.

The experimental results across multiple datasets (CIFAR, Scenes, CUB, ImageNet) provide strong empirical support for the approach. The authors show consistent improvements over baselines, with particularly compelling results on ImageNet where they achieve 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement. The paper makes a strong case for why attention transfer could be beneficial, citing observations that higher-performing networks have more discriminative attention maps.

The paper's novelty is further strengthened by the theoretical foundation they provide for attention transfer, which is well-placed within the literature on attention visualization and knowledge distillation. The authors properly contextualize their work within the existing literature while clearly articulating how their approach differs from prior methods.

The only significant concern is the questionable claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Knowledge distillation has been successfully applied on ImageNet in numerous papers (e.g., Hinton et al., 2015; Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016), so this statement needs significant revision to avoid misleading readers. This is a minor issue that can be addressed with a clear clarification in the revised version.

## Additional Feedback
1. Clarify the ImageNet claim: The statement about being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is problematic. Instead, clarify that their specific attention transfer method (as defined in their paper) is the first to achieve these results on ImageNet, while acknowledging that knowledge distillation has been successfully applied on ImageNet.

2. Provide more theoretical justification: While the empirical results are strong, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of *why* attention transfer works better than other methods. What specific properties of attention maps make them superior for transfer?

3. Discuss limitations: The paper should acknowledge limitations of their approach, such as computational cost (especially for gradient-based attention transfer) and potential failure cases.

4. Improve experimental details: For ImageNet experiments, provide more details about hyperparameter choices and training procedures. The statement "we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" weakens the experimental rigor.

## Questions for Authors
1. Can you clarify exactly what you mean by "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet"? How does your approach fundamentally differ from previous knowledge distillation methods that have been applied to ImageNet?

2. The paper claims that attention maps contain more valuable information than full activations, but doesn't provide a theoretical justification. Can you elaborate on why attention maps might be more informative for transfer than full activations?

3. In the gradient-based attention transfer section, you mention "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have you explored alternative approaches to handle batch normalization during the second backpropagation, such as freezing batch norm parameters during the second pass?

4. The paper mentions that "attention transfer greatly speeds up convergence" but doesn't provide evidence of this. Could you include training curves showing convergence speed for attention transfer versus baseline methods?
