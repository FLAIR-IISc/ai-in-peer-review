

# Peer Review: "Attention Transfer"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention mechanisms. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based (using various statistics of neuron activations) and gradient-based (using input gradients). The key contribution is demonstrating that transferring attention information from a teacher network to a student network can significantly improve student performance, often outperforming traditional knowledge distillation approaches. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network), showing consistent improvements in accuracy. The paper also makes the important observation that attention transfer works better than transferring full activation tensors, and that activation-based transfer can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms, with well-motivated methodology and compelling empirical results across multiple settings. The key reasons for acceptance are the clear novelty of the approach, the thorough experimental validation, and the practical significance of the findings.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful gap in knowledge transfer research. The authors effectively connect human attention mechanisms to artificial attention in neural networks, establishing a strong theoretical foundation. Their core insight—that attention maps contain more transferable information than full activation tensors—was validated through extensive experiments, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The methodology is sound and well-executed. The authors clearly define their attention mapping functions and provide a theoretically grounded loss function for attention transfer. The ablation studies in Section 4.1.2 are particularly strong, demonstrating that attention transfer consistently outperforms full activation transfer, with the F²sum mapping function working best. The experimental results across multiple datasets and architectures are convincing, with the ImageNet results being especially noteworthy as they represent a significant practical application.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of how attention mechanisms operate in deep networks. The observation that attention maps focus on different parts of images at different layers (low-level features in early layers, discriminative regions in middle layers, and full objects in top layers) provides useful insights into network behavior.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

### Major Improvements Needed

1. **Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides strong empirical evidence, it lacks a theoretical explanation for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activation tensors. A brief theoretical argument (even if speculative) would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors should address why attention maps are more informative for knowledge transfer than the full activation tensors.

2. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper doesn't adequately position itself against recent attention-based distillation methods. For example, Wang et al.'s "Attention Transfer" (ICLR 2017) and Zhang et al.'s "Attention Distillation" (ICML 2018) are directly relevant. The authors should clarify how their approach differs and why it's superior, particularly since they claim to be "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" (which appears to be inaccurate given the prior work).

3. **Gradient-Based Attention Details**: The paper mentions challenges with batch normalization during gradient-based attention transfer but doesn't provide a clear solution. The authors should either provide a more detailed explanation of how they handled batch normalization in their experiments or acknowledge this as a limitation.

### Minor Improvements

1. **Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper claims attention transfer "adds very little computational cost" but doesn't quantify this. A brief analysis of the computational overhead compared to standard knowledge distillation would be helpful for practitioners.

2. **ImageNet Results Clarification**: The paper states that "on ImageNet, KD converges significantly slower" but doesn't explain why. This is an important observation that deserves more discussion.

3. **Reproducibility**: The paper mentions code availability but could provide more specific details about hyperparameters, training procedures, and computational resources used for ImageNet experiments to improve reproducibility.

### Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" (Section 4.1.2). Can you provide a more detailed explanation or theoretical justification for this observation, beyond the empirical results?

2. The paper states that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This claim appears to be inaccurate given prior work (e.g., FitNets, KD on ImageNet). Could you clarify how your method differs from previous approaches and why it's the first to work on ImageNet?

3. In Section 3.2, you mention challenges with batch normalization during gradient-based attention transfer. How exactly did you handle this in your ImageNet experiments? What specific modifications did you make to the training procedure?

4. You report significant improvements on ImageNet with ResNet-18 using ResNet-34 as a teacher (1.1% top-1 improvement). How does this compare to the state-of-the-art for knowledge distillation on ImageNet? What's the relative improvement over standard knowledge distillation?

5. The paper mentions that "activation-based attention transfer gives better improvements than full-activation transfer." Could you elaborate on why this is the case? Is there a specific characteristic of attention maps that makes them more transferable?

## Conclusion

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation. The attention transfer approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements across multiple datasets and architectures. With the suggested revisions addressing the theoretical justification and better positioning against recent work, this paper will make an excellent contribution to ICLR. The practical implications of the work are significant, as attention transfer provides a simple yet effective method for improving neural network performance without increasing model complexity.
