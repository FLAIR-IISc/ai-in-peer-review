

# Review of "Attention Transfer: A New Perspective on Knowledge Distillation"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper proposes a novel knowledge transfer method called "attention transfer" that aims to improve student network performance by transferring attention maps from a teacher network. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based (using statistics of hidden neuron activations) and gradient-based (using input sensitivity maps). They claim that attention maps contain valuable information that can improve student network performance beyond standard knowledge distillation. The authors present experiments on CIFAR-10/100 and ImageNet datasets, claiming significant improvements across various network architectures, including ResNets and Wide ResNets. They specifically argue that attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer (FitNets-style) and is the first knowledge transfer method successfully applied on ImageNet.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation and theoretical grounding for its claims. The proposed method appears to be an incremental variation of existing knowledge distillation approaches without compelling evidence that it fundamentally improves upon established methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary issue with this paper is its lack of rigorous empirical validation against established baselines. While the authors present some positive results, they fail to provide sufficient evidence to support their central claim that attention transfer provides significant improvements over standard knowledge distillation.

First, the paper claims that attention transfer "gives better improvements than full-activation transfer" but doesn't provide a thorough comparison against the most relevant baselines. For example, they don't compare against state-of-the-art knowledge distillation methods like AT (Attention Transfer) or other recent variants. The paper states that "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature," but this is incorrect - there are numerous papers applying knowledge distillation to ImageNet (e.g., the work by Hinton et al. on distillation, FitNets, and more recent methods).

Second, the experimental setup for ImageNet appears superficial. The authors note they "didn't have time to tune any hyperparameters" and kept them from finetuning experiments, yet claim "ResNet-18 with attention transfer achieved 1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 better validation accuracy." This is not a convincing demonstration of the method's effectiveness, especially given the large number of hyperparameters that could significantly impact performance.

Third, the paper lacks proper ablation studies to isolate the contribution of attention transfer. For instance, they claim attention transfer "greatly speeds up convergence," but don't provide evidence that the attention component itself is responsible for the speedup rather than the overall training setup.

The paper also makes unsubstantiated claims about novelty, such as stating that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This is contradicted by numerous existing papers applying knowledge distillation to ImageNet, which the authors fail to cite or compare against.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a thorough comparison against established knowledge distillation methods (not just FitNets) on ImageNet, with proper hyperparameter tuning.

2. Provide more detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of attention transfer versus other training components.

3. Address the following specific questions:
   - What is the theoretical basis for why attention maps contain more useful information for transfer than full activations?
   - Why does the paper claim their method is the "first" to work on ImageNet when numerous papers have applied knowledge distillation to ImageNet?
   - How does the proposed attention transfer method compare against state-of-the-art knowledge distillation approaches like AT (Attention Transfer) or other recent variants?
   - What are the computational costs of the proposed method compared to standard knowledge distillation?

4. Provide more comprehensive analysis of the attention maps themselves, including visualizations of how the attention maps change during training and how they correlate with prediction accuracy.

5. Include error analysis to determine if attention transfer helps the student network make different types of errors than standard distillation, which would provide deeper insights into the method's effectiveness.

The paper has the potential to be valuable if it provides compelling evidence that attention transfer offers meaningful advantages over established knowledge distillation techniques, but as currently presented, it lacks the rigor and thorough empirical validation required for ICLR. The claims are overstated, the comparisons are insufficient, and the novelty is questionable. The authors should significantly strengthen their experimental validation before considering submission to a top-tier venue.
