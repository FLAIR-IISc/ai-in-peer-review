- Proposes attention-based knowledge transfer with two spatial attention map types: activation-based and gradient-based.  
- Shows activation-based attention transfer (AT) improves student performance across multiple datasets and architectures.  
- Finds AT outperforms full-activation transfer (F-ActT) and can be combined with knowledge distillation for further gains.  
- Provides thorough experimental validation on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet with multiple ablation studies.  
- Claims attention maps contain more transferable/discriminative information than full activations, challenging prior assumptions.  
- Highlights strong ImageNet results: ResNet-18 with AT improves ~1.1% top-1 and 0.8% top-5 accuracy over baseline.  
- Recommends acceptance due to novelty, motivation, and comprehensive empirical results.  
- Requests deeper theoretical or empirical explanation for why attention maps are more effective than full activations.  
- Asks for expanded analysis on why knowledge distillation failed on ImageNet and specifics of the ImageNet hyperparameters used.  
- Requests concrete computational cost and memory comparisons between attention transfer and standard knowledge distillation.  
- Suggests discussing limitations: architecture/task dependence and scenarios where AT may not work well.  
- Asks about sensitivity to l2 normalization and whether other normalization schemes (e.g., l1) were tried.  
- Questions how batch normalization should be handled in the second backpropagation step and whether alternatives were explored.  
- Seeks clarification of the claim that activation-based AT is the "first" successful knowledge transfer method on ImageNet and what "successfully applied" means.
