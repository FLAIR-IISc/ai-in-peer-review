- Introduces attention-based knowledge transfer using activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps.  
- Proposes attention mapping functions (sum, sum-of-powers, max) and methods to transfer them from teacher to student.  
- Shows activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.  
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements across CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet with multiple architectures.  
- Motivates approach with psychological attention theories and prior attention visualization work.  
- Claim that activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method applied on ImageNet is inaccurate and should be revised.  
- Gradient-based attention transfer is less developed: weaker results and missing implementation details (e.g., batch norm during second backprop).  
- Recommendation: Accept — novel, well-motivated, and empirically strong.  
- Suggest adding theoretical explanation for why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations.  
- Recommend a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (e.g., β ≈ 0.1) and more thorough ImageNet hyperparameter tuning.  
- Ask for clearer comparison and differentiation from related work like FitNets and existing knowledge distillation on ImageNet.  
- Question: Please elaborate why activation-based attention maps outperform full activations theoretically.  
- Question: Why is gradient-based attention less effective and what architectural changes might improve it?  
- Question: Which prior knowledge transfer methods have been applied to ImageNet and how does this method differ?  
- Question: How will batch normalization be handled during the second backpropagation step in gradient-based transfer?
