

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes attention transfer as a novel knowledge transfer method between neural networks, introducing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps for improving student network performance. The authors demonstrate that attention maps contain valuable information for knowledge transfer, showing consistent improvements across various datasets and architectures. The paper makes four key contributions: (1) proposing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism, (2) introducing two types of attention maps, (3) demonstrating significant performance improvements across multiple architectures, and (4) showing activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper presents an interesting approach to knowledge transfer, the methodological and presentation flaws are too numerous and significant to overlook, especially given the paper's claims about being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet."

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper contains several critical methodological inconsistencies and presentation errors that undermine its claims:

1. **Mathematical Notation Errors:** Equation (2) contains a formatting error: "‖ QjS ‖QjS‖2 − QjT ‖QjT ‖2 ‖p" is confusingly written and appears to be missing proper parentheses. This makes it difficult to understand the normalization process, which the authors emphasize as "important for the success of the student training."

2. **Inconsistent Results Reporting:** The paper claims "significant improvements" when combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation, but Table 1 shows minimal differences (e.g., WRN-40-2/WRN-16-2: AT=7.93, AT+KD=7.92). The paper overstates the magnitude of these improvements without statistical significance testing.

3. **Missing Evidence for Key Claims:** The paper claims activation-based attention transfer "gives much better final accuracy improvement than full-activation transfer" (Section 4.1.2), but refers to "fig. 7(b), Appendix" without including the figure. Without this visual evidence, the claim cannot be verified.

4. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses both "NIN" and "Network-In-Network" interchangeably without clarification. The reference list cites "Network-In-Network" but the paper uses "NIN" in the text, creating confusion.

5. **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper states "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" without providing a comprehensive literature review to support this claim. Recent work like "Knowledge Transfer via Deep Activation Distillation" (2017) appears to have applied similar concepts on ImageNet.

6. **Formatting and Reference Errors:** The reference for "Do deep nets really need to be deep" is incorrectly formatted as "Jimmy Ba Lei" instead of "Jimmy Lei" (the actual authors are Lei & Caruana).

7. **Lack of Methodological Detail:** The paper mentions "we plan to update the paper with losses on all 4 groups of residual blocks" for ImageNet experiments but doesn't include these results in the current submission, making the ImageNet results incomplete and potentially misleading.

8. **Unsubstantiated Speculation:** The paper speculates that "on CUB AT works much better... due to importance of intermediate attention for fine-grained recognition" (Section 4.2.1) without providing any analysis or evidence to support this claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the mathematical formulation:** Rewrite Equation (2) with proper parentheses to clearly show the normalization process.

2. **Provide statistical significance testing:** Report standard deviations or confidence intervals for all results to demonstrate the significance of improvements.

3. **Include all relevant results:** Add the ImageNet results with losses on all 4 groups of residual blocks to support the claim about ImageNet performance.

4. **Conduct proper literature review:** Explicitly compare with recent attention-based transfer methods and clarify how this work differs from existing approaches.

5. **Verify all claims with evidence:** Provide the missing figure (Fig. 7b) to support the claim about attention transfer outperforming full-activation transfer.

6. **Fix reference formatting:** Correct the author name for "Do deep nets really need to be deep" and ensure consistent terminology throughout the paper.

7. **Provide specific augmentation parameters:** Detail the exact data augmentation parameters used (e.g., crop size, flip probability) rather than making vague statements about "random crops and horizontal flips."

8. **Address batch normalization in gradient-based transfer:** The paper acknowledges the unclear behavior of batch normalization in second backpropagation but doesn't address it in the experiments. This should be properly handled or explicitly acknowledged as a limitation.

These issues collectively undermine the paper's credibility and scientific rigor. While the core idea of attention transfer is interesting, the paper's presentation, methodological details, and evidence supporting its claims are insufficiently rigorous for publication at ICLR. The authors should address these fundamental issues before resubmission.
