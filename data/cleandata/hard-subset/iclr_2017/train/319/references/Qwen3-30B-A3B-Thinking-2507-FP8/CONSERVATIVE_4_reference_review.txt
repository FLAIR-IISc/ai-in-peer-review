

**Review**

1. **Summary of Contributions**: The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention information from a teacher to a student network. The authors define two types of spatial attention maps (activation-based and gradient-based) and propose methods to transfer these maps to improve student network performance. They claim significant improvements across various datasets and architectures, including ImageNet, where they assert their method is the first successful knowledge transfer approach.

2. **Decision**: Reject.

3. **Supporting Arguments**: While the paper addresses a relevant problem in knowledge transfer, it fails to meet ICLR's standards for methodological rigor and novelty. The core contribution appears to be a minor variation of existing knowledge distillation techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach. Key deficiencies include:

   a) **Questionable Novelty**: The paper claims to be the first successful application of attention transfer on ImageNet, but this is unsubstantiated. The authors cite FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) but don't properly compare their method to established knowledge transfer approaches on ImageNet. The original Hinton et al. (2015) paper demonstrated knowledge distillation with different architectures, contradicting the paper's assertion that KD "struggles to work if teacher and student have different architecture/depth." The paper's claim of novelty is overstated without proper literature context.

   b) **Insufficient Experimental Rigor**: The experimental section lacks proper baselines and consistent methodology. For instance, the paper claims "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature," which is demonstrably false (FitNets was applied to ImageNet in Romero et al., 2014). The CIFAR experiments show modest improvements, but the paper doesn't adequately explain why attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer (Table 1) or provide theoretical justification for this observation.

   c) **Weak Theoretical Foundation**: The paper asserts that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" without providing a compelling theoretical argument. The empirical results alone cannot justify this claim, especially without ablation studies on what specific information is being transferred.

   d) **Inconsistent Experimental Setup**: The experiments use different data augmentations, batch normalization, and training protocols across datasets (CIFAR vs. ImageNet). For example, on CIFAR they use batch normalization and data augmentation, but on ImageNet they claim they couldn't apply batch normalization due to "unclear behavior in second backpropagation" without providing evidence for this claim. This inconsistency undermines the validity of their results.

4. **Additional Feedback**: To improve the paper, the authors should:

   - Provide a thorough comparison to established knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet, including FitNets and KD with proper implementation details.
   - Conduct deeper analysis of why attention transfer works better than full activation transfer, potentially through information-theoretic analysis or ablation studies.
   - Clarify the relationship between their method and existing attention visualization techniques (e.g., Grad-CAM), explaining how their approach differs and why it's better suited for knowledge transfer.
   - Ensure consistent experimental setup across all datasets with detailed hyperparameter settings and training protocols.

   **Questions for Authors**:
   1. Can you provide evidence supporting your claim that attention transfer is the "first successful knowledge transfer method" on ImageNet, particularly in comparison to FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) and other KD methods?
   2. Why does the paper claim that knowledge distillation "struggles to work if teacher and student have different architecture/depth" (on CIFAR), when the original Hinton et al. (2015) paper demonstrated KD with different architectures?
   3. Can you provide a theoretical justification for why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations, rather than simply asserting it based on empirical results?
   4. How do you explain the performance difference between activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer (Table 3), and why does activation-based transfer work better?

This paper's claims are overstated, the experimental validation is insufficient, and the novelty is questionable. The authors need to substantially revise the paper to properly position their work in the literature, provide rigorous comparisons to established methods, and offer a stronger theoretical foundation before it could be considered for publication at ICLR.
