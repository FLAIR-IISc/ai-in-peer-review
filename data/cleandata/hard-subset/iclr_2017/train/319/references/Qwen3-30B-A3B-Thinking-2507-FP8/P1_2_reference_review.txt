

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by focusing on attention information rather than just output predictions or full activations. The authors propose two methods for defining and transferring attention: activation-based attention transfer (using statistics of hidden neuron activations) and gradient-based attention transfer (using input sensitivity maps). The key contributions include demonstrating that attention maps contain valuable information for improving student network performance, showing that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer, and providing experimental evidence across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (ResNet, NIN, WRN). The paper makes a compelling case that attention mechanisms in neural networks can be leveraged for more effective knowledge transfer, potentially advancing the field of knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the knowledge distillation literature by proposing a new method that transfers attention information rather than just output distributions. The approach is well-motivated, experimentally validated, and shows consistent improvements across multiple benchmarks. The paper's claims are generally well-supported by the experimental results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's key strengths are:

1. **Novel contribution**: The paper successfully identifies and addresses a gap in knowledge transfer research by focusing on attention patterns rather than output predictions or full activations. This is well-motivated by the authors' discussion of human attention mechanisms and the observation that different networks "see" the same scene differently.

2. **Rigorous experimental validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments across multiple datasets and architectures. The results consistently show that attention transfer (particularly activation-based) provides significant improvements over baselines, including knowledge distillation. The comparison between activation-based transfer and full-activation transfer is particularly convincing.

3. **Clear technical contributions**: The paper clearly defines two distinct attention transfer methods (activation-based and gradient-based) with precise mathematical formulations and implementation details. The activation-based method is particularly well-executed and demonstrates strong empirical results.

4. **Practical significance**: The paper demonstrates successful application of attention transfer on ImageNet, which the authors claim is the first successful application of knowledge transfer on this dataset, a notable contribution to the field.

The paper's main weakness is the limited exploration of gradient-based attention transfer (only tested on small networks without batch normalization), but this doesn't undermine the main contribution of activation-based attention transfer, which is the primary focus of the paper and shows the strongest results.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify the "first successful application on ImageNet" claim**: The paper states "we can assume that proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." However, the paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with other methods that might have been applied but not reported. Please provide a more detailed literature comparison to support this claim.

- **Expand gradient-based attention discussion**: The gradient-based attention section is underdeveloped compared to the activation-based approach. Consider either expanding this section with more comprehensive experiments (e.g., on larger networks with batch normalization) or explicitly stating that the focus should be on activation-based transfer as the primary contribution.

- **Explain why attention transfer works better**: While the paper demonstrates that attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer, it doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for why attention maps contain more transferable information. A brief discussion of this would strengthen the paper.

- **Clarify implementation details**: The paper mentions using "l2 normalization before transfer losses" but doesn't explain why this is important. A brief explanation would help readers understand the method better.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that "activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Can you provide a more comprehensive comparison with other knowledge transfer methods that have been applied to ImageNet (e.g., FitNets, KD with different architectures), including references to specific papers that attempted but failed on ImageNet?

2. In Figure 4, the attention maps for ResNet-101 show higher activation in the wolf's face compared to ResNet-34. However, the ResNet-101 has higher accuracy than ResNet-34. Is it possible that the attention maps are simply reflecting the network's ability to detect the wolf's face better, rather than being the cause of better performance? How do you distinguish between correlation and causation in this case?

3. The paper states that "attention maps focus on different parts for different layers in the network." Can you provide more specific examples of how attention patterns differ between layers (e.g., low-level vs. high-level features) and how this relates to the network's decision-making process?

4. In Table 3, the gradient-based attention transfer (AT) outperforms knowledge distillation (KD) by 0.9% on CIFAR-10. However, the paper states that "on ImageNet, though KD converges significantly slower" and "we could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature." Can you clarify whether the gradient-based AT method also works well on ImageNet, or if the ImageNet results were only tested with activation-based transfer?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the knowledge distillation literature with a well-executed and promising approach. With minor clarifications to strengthen the claims and discussion, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
