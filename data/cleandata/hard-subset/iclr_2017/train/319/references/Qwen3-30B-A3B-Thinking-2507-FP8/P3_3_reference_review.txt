

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention information. The authors propose two mechanisms for defining and transferring attention: activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps. They demonstrate that transferring attention information from a teacher network to a student network consistently improves performance across multiple datasets (CIFAR, ImageNet, CUB, Scenes) and architectures (ResNet, Wide ResNet, Network-in-Network). The key contributions include: (1) establishing attention as a mechanism for knowledge transfer, (2) proposing two types of attention maps, (3) showing significant performance improvements over baselines, and (4) demonstrating that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge distillation by introducing attention transfer as a novel mechanism. The empirical results are compelling and well-supported across multiple experimental settings. The work is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in the literature regarding how attention information can be leveraged for knowledge transfer.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is well-motivated by the observation that more accurate networks exhibit more discriminative attention maps that focus on relevant features. The authors demonstrate this through careful visualization across different network architectures and datasets. Their key insight—that attention maps contain more useful transferable information than full activations—is well-supported by the experiments showing that attention transfer consistently outperforms full-activation transfer (Table 1, Figure 7).

The experimental design is thorough, covering multiple datasets, architectures, and attention mapping functions. The ablation studies (Tables 1-2) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of attention transfer, particularly with the F²sum attention mapping function. The ImageNet results (Section 4.2.2) are especially compelling as they demonstrate the first successful application of knowledge transfer to ImageNet using this approach, which the authors correctly note is a significant gap in the literature.

The paper's connection to related work is strong, particularly in the attention visualization literature (Grad-CAM, guided backpropagation) and knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., FitNets). The authors correctly position their work as complementary to existing knowledge distillation approaches, rather than a replacement.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, there are several areas where it could be improved:

1. **Theoretical justification:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence but lacks a theoretical explanation for why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations. The authors should explore this more deeply, perhaps by analyzing the information content of attention maps versus full activations.

2. **Computational cost:** The paper mentions that gradient-based attention transfer involves double backpropagation with "approximately the same cost as first backpropagation." A more precise analysis of the computational overhead would be valuable, especially for larger networks and datasets.

3. **ImageNet claim clarification:** The paper claims attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but this needs qualification. While the authors may be correct that previous knowledge distillation methods struggled with ImageNet, the FitNets paper (Romero et al., 2014) did demonstrate knowledge distillation on ImageNet. The authors should clarify this claim to avoid potential misrepresentation.

4. **Ablation study completeness:** The paper provides good ablation studies for attention mapping functions (Table 2), but could further explore the impact of different layer choices for attention transfer. For example, how does the performance change when transferring attention from different layers (early vs. late) in the network?

5. **Reproducibility:** The paper mentions using Torch and provides a GitHub link, but it would be helpful to include specific hyperparameter settings (beyond just β values) and training details for ImageNet experiments to improve reproducibility.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer, but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations. Could you provide a theoretical or information-theoretic explanation for this observation?

2. In Table 3, the gradient-based attention transfer shows only modest improvements over the baseline (13.5% vs. 12.1% error). How does this compare to knowledge distillation (KD) on the same setup, and why does activation-based attention transfer (11.2%) outperform gradient-based attention transfer (12.1%)?

3. The paper claims attention transfer is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Given that FitNets (Romero et al., 2014) demonstrated knowledge distillation on ImageNet, could you clarify this claim and better position your work relative to previous knowledge distillation approaches on ImageNet?

4. For the gradient-based attention transfer, the paper mentions "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Could you share your experimental observations about this issue and whether you found any specific approaches to handle batch normalization during double backpropagation?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. With the minor revisions suggested above, it would be an excellent addition to the ICLR proceedings. The results are convincing, the methodology is sound, and the work opens up new avenues for knowledge transfer research.
