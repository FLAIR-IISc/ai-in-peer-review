

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by transferring attention maps from a teacher network to a student network. The authors define two types of attention maps: activation-based (using statistics of neuron activations) and gradient-based (using gradients with respect to input). They claim that transferring these attention maps leads to significant performance improvements across various datasets and architectures, including ImageNet. The paper presents empirical results showing that attention transfer outperforms transferring full activations and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a significant claim about attention transfer being a more effective knowledge transfer method than existing approaches, but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support this claim. The experimental results are limited in scope, the improvements are marginal, and the paper lacks rigorous comparison with strong baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim is that attention transfer provides significant improvements over existing knowledge transfer methods, particularly for larger networks like ResNet on ImageNet. However, several critical issues undermine this claim:

First, the reported improvements are marginal. For example, on ImageNet, the ResNet-18 with attention transfer achieves only 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement over the baseline ResNet-18. In the context of modern computer vision, such marginal gains are common and often not considered significant without clear evidence of why the approach would be substantially better than existing methods.

Second, the paper fails to properly compare against strong baselines. The authors claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," but provide no evidence of this. They acknowledge that knowledge distillation struggles with different architectures on ImageNet but don't provide a comprehensive comparison with other knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet. The paper also doesn't compare against more recent knowledge transfer methods beyond FitNets and standard knowledge distillation.

Third, the experimental design lacks rigor. The paper uses different training protocols for different datasets (e.g., no batch normalization on some CIFAR experiments, different data augmentation strategies), making comparisons difficult. The ImageNet experiments are particularly limited, using only ResNet-18 as student and ResNet-34 as teacher with attention transfer only on the last two groups of residual blocks, which is a narrow exploration of the method's potential.

Fourth, the paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to establish why attention transfer works better than transferring full activations. While they show attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer, they don't provide a strong theoretical justification for why attention maps contain more transferable information. The claim that "attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations" is not sufficiently supported.

Finally, the paper doesn't provide evidence of the computational cost of attention transfer. For a method to be practically useful, it should not only improve accuracy but also be efficient. The paper mentions that attention transfer "adds very little computational cost," but doesn't quantify this or compare it to other methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. Conduct a more comprehensive set of experiments on ImageNet with multiple student-teacher pairs and different attention transfer configurations. The current ImageNet results are too limited to support the paper's main claim.

2. Provide a thorough comparison against recent knowledge transfer methods on ImageNet, not just the basic knowledge distillation and FitNets. The claim that "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" needs substantial evidence.

3. Include ablation studies that explore why attention transfer works better than other forms of knowledge transfer. What specific information in the attention maps is most valuable? How does this relate to the network's internal representation?

4. Provide a more rigorous analysis of the computational cost of attention transfer compared to standard knowledge distillation and other methods. How much additional time does attention transfer require per epoch?

5. Clarify the relationship between attention transfer and existing methods. The paper mentions that attention maps are similar to "hints" in FitNets, but doesn't explain how their approach differs fundamentally from FitNets.

6. Address the question of why attention transfer works better than transferring full activations. The current explanation is insufficient. A more detailed analysis of what information is being transferred and why it's beneficial would strengthen the paper.

7. Include more detailed results on how the choice of attention mapping functions (Fsum, F^psum, F^pmax) affects performance. The paper mentions that F^2sum works best, but doesn't explain why.

The paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to knowledge transfer, but the current evidence is insufficient to support the strong claims made. With more rigorous experiments and thorough comparisons against relevant baselines, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to the field.
