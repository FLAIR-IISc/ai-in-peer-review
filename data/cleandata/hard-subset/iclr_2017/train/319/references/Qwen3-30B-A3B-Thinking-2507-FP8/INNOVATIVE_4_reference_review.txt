

# Review of "Attention Transfer: Learning to Transfer Knowledge from Attention Maps"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to knowledge transfer between neural networks by leveraging attention maps as the medium for transfer, rather than output distributions (as in traditional knowledge distillation) or full activations (as in FitNets). The authors propose two types of attention maps—activation-based and gradient-based—and demonstrate that transferring these maps from a teacher network to a student network can significantly improve the student's performance across various datasets and architectures. The paper provides a theoretical foundation for attention maps, describes the transfer mechanisms, and presents empirical evidence showing that attention transfer outperforms traditional knowledge distillation and full activation transfer, particularly on ImageNet where it's claimed to be the first successful application of knowledge transfer.

## Decision
Accept

## Key Reasons for Acceptance
1. **Novel Concept**: The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to knowledge transfer that represents a significant conceptual shift from existing methods. The idea of using attention maps as the primary medium for knowledge transfer is bold, original, and represents a fresh perspective on how to improve neural network performance.

2. **Significant Results**: The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on ImageNet, where previous knowledge distillation methods struggled. The 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement on ImageNet with ResNet-18 using ResNet-34 as a teacher is particularly noteworthy, as they claim this is the first successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet.

## Supporting Arguments
This paper stands out for its conceptual novelty, which aligns with ICLR's preference for bold, novel ideas over methodological perfection. The authors' central insight—that attention maps contain more valuable transfer information than full activations or output distributions—is supported by their empirical results, which consistently show attention transfer outperforming both knowledge distillation and full activation transfer.

The paper is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature. It clearly distinguishes its approach from prior work on attention visualization and knowledge distillation, and the connection to human attention mechanisms provides a strong conceptual foundation. The authors' exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps demonstrates a thorough understanding of the landscape.

While the methodology is relatively straightforward (the core idea is simple to implement), the results are compelling enough to justify the novelty. The paper makes a strong case that attention information is a valuable new dimension for knowledge transfer, which could inspire future research directions in neural network architecture design and knowledge transfer.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. A more detailed discussion of why attention maps contain more transferable information than full activations. The paper shows that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer (Table 1), but the authors should provide more theoretical insight into this phenomenon.

2. A more comprehensive ablation study examining the impact of different attention mapping functions (Fsum, F^psum, F^pmax) across different network architectures and datasets. The current ablation (Table 2) is limited to one architecture pair.

3. Clarification on how this approach relates to recent work on attention in neural networks (e.g., transformers), as the paper's attention concept differs from the attention mechanisms used in those models.

4. More detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding computational cost and scalability to larger models.

## Questions for Authors
1. You claim your attention transfer is the first successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet. Could you provide more context on why previous knowledge distillation approaches failed on ImageNet, and how your approach specifically overcomes these limitations?

2. In Figure 4, the attention maps show that higher-accuracy networks have more focused attention on discriminative regions. Could you elaborate on whether this focus on discriminative regions is the primary reason attention transfer works better than full activation transfer?

3. How does your approach scale to very large vision transformers, where attention mechanisms are already built into the architecture? Would your attention transfer technique be applicable in this context?

4. The paper mentions that gradient-based attention transfer requires double backpropagation, which is computationally expensive. Have you explored ways to approximate this efficiently for larger models?

This paper represents a significant conceptual contribution to the field of knowledge transfer in neural networks. The novelty of using attention maps as the primary medium for knowledge transfer, combined with strong empirical results on ImageNet, makes this an important contribution that deserves acceptance at ICLR.
