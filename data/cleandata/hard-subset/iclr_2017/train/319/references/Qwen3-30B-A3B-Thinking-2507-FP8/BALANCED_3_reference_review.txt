

# Review of "Attention Transfer for Knowledge Distillation"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach for knowledge transfer between neural networks using attention maps. The authors define two types of attention maps—activation-based (using sum, sum of powers, and max of absolute values of feature activations) and gradient-based (using input gradients). They demonstrate that transferring these attention maps from a teacher to a student network can improve the student's performance across multiple datasets and architectures. The paper claims significant improvements over standard knowledge distillation on CIFAR-10, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet, with the authors stating this is the first successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's claims of novelty and superiority over existing methods are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The most critical issue is the incorrect claim that the authors' method is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet," which contradicts established literature on knowledge distillation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is the use of attention maps for knowledge transfer, but it fails to adequately justify why attention maps are superior to standard knowledge distillation. The experimental results on CIFAR-10 show modest improvements (e.g., 0.84% reduction in error rate for WRN-16-2/WRN-16-1), and the authors don't convincingly explain why attention maps contain more useful information than full activations (as shown in Table 1, the improvement from attention transfer is modest compared to knowledge distillation).

The most problematic claim is that this is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." This is factually incorrect—Hinton et al. (2015) applied knowledge distillation to ImageNet, and subsequent work by Zagoruyko & Komodakis (2016) and others has demonstrated successful application of knowledge distillation on ImageNet. The authors' claim that "we were not able to achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet" with their specific hyperparameters doesn't invalidate the success of knowledge distillation on ImageNet as a general method.

Furthermore, the paper doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why attention transfer should work better than knowledge distillation. The claim that attention maps "contain valuable information that can be used for significantly improving the performance" lacks depth—how does this information differ from what knowledge distillation captures?

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the incorrect claim about ImageNet and provide a proper comparison of their method against established knowledge distillation techniques on ImageNet. This would require re-running experiments with standard hyperparameters used in previous work.

2. Provide a more detailed analysis of why attention maps are better than full activation transfer. Instead of just showing marginal improvements, they should analyze what specific information attention maps capture that full activations don't, and how this contributes to improved performance.

3. Expand the discussion of related work to properly situate their method within the broader context of knowledge distillation and attention visualization techniques. They should clearly distinguish their contribution from existing methods like FitNets and knowledge distillation.

4. Provide more thorough analysis of the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer. The paper mentions it's "similar to the double backpropagation technique" but doesn't quantify the additional computational burden compared to standard knowledge distillation.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims "proposed activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet." Please clarify this claim with specific references to prior work on knowledge transfer on ImageNet, and explain why your method differs from previous successful applications.

2. In Table 1, the attention transfer (AT) method shows 7.93% error rate compared to 8.77% for the baseline, but knowledge distillation (KD) alone also shows 8.11% error rate. How do you explain that the combination of AT+KD (7.93%) is only marginally better than KD alone (8.11%), suggesting that attention transfer doesn't add substantial value beyond standard knowledge distillation?

3. The paper states "it seems quite interesting that attention maps carry information that is more important for transfer than full activations." Can you provide specific examples of how the attention maps capture information that full activations miss, and why this information is particularly valuable for transfer learning?

4. For the gradient-based attention transfer, the paper mentions "it is so far unclear how batch normalization should behave in the second backpropagation step." Have you explored alternatives to batch normalization for the student network that might make gradient-based attention transfer more practical?
