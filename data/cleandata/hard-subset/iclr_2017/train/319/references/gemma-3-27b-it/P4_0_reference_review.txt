This paper investigates knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps. The authors propose to define attention as activation-based or gradient-based spatial maps and demonstrate that transferring these maps from a teacher network to a student network can improve performance across various datasets and architectures. They propose methods for transferring attention and show that activation-based transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**Reject**

The paper presents a reasonable idea but lacks sufficient rigor in its experimental evaluation and analysis. While the authors demonstrate improvements in many settings, the gains are often marginal, and the statistical significance isn't adequately addressed. The experimental setup feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a clear justification for many design choices, making it difficult to assess the true impact of the proposed methods.

The primary reasons for rejection are a lack of convincing evidence of substantial improvement and insufficient justification for methodological choices. 

Here's a detailed breakdown:

1. **Limited Statistical Significance:** Tables 1, 2, and 3 present results without any statistical significance tests. The reported improvements are often small (e.g., 0.1-0.3% on CIFAR), and it’s unclear if these differences are statistically significant. Without statistical tests, it's impossible to determine if the observed gains are due to the proposed method or random chance.

2. **Ad-hoc Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors mention limited hyperparameter tuning, which raises concerns about the optimality of the results. The choice of β in Equation 2, for example, seems arbitrary (“usually varies about 0.1”). A more systematic exploration of hyperparameters and their impact on performance is needed.

3. **Inconsistent Results and Explanations:** The authors report gradient-based attention transfer is successful on CIFAR but note they “could not achieve positive results with KD on ImageNet.” This discrepancy isn’t fully explained. The statement that the architecture of the student NIN without batch normalization is different, and impacts results is confusing, as the preceding sections discuss BN networks.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the paper explores different attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax), a comprehensive ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of the proposed approach is missing. For example, the impact of l2 normalization of attention maps is mentioned as important, but there is no experiment demonstrating its effect in isolation.

5. **Vague Experimental Setup:** The ImageNet experiments are particularly weak. The statement "We also did not have time to tune any hyperparameters and kept them from finetuning experiments" is a significant limitation. This suggests a lack of commitment to rigorous evaluation. The claim that “activation-based AT is the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” is an overstatement, given the extensive literature on knowledge distillation.

6. **Figure Quality & Clarity:** The figures in the appendix (Fig. 6 and Fig. 7) are not well-integrated into the main text, and their relevance isn’t clearly explained. Figure 7(b) is difficult to interpret without further context.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the novelty:** The paper needs to better articulate its novel contributions beyond simply applying attention mechanisms for knowledge transfer. Is the combination of activation-based transfer and KD novel? If so, highlight this more clearly.
*   **Provide a more thorough literature review:** The related work section could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding knowledge distillation techniques applied to image classification.
*   **Explain the choice of datasets and architectures:** Provide a rationale for selecting specific datasets and network architectures for the experiments.
*   **Address limitations:** Explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the study, such as the limited hyperparameter tuning and the lack of statistical significance testing.
*   **Expand the discussion of gradient-based methods:** The gradient-based experiments are less developed than the activation-based ones. A deeper investigation of these methods is warranted.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you provide statistical significance tests for all reported results?
*   What criteria were used to select the value of β in Equation 2? Could you present a sensitivity analysis of this parameter?
*   Why did knowledge distillation fail to improve performance on ImageNet? What specific challenges were encountered?
*   Can you elaborate on the differences in the NIN architecture used for gradient-based experiments and its impact on the results?
*   How do the computational costs of attention transfer and knowledge distillation compare?
*   Could you provide more details on the implementation of the double backpropagation method for gradient-based attention transfer?
*   What are the computational resources required to reproduce the ImageNet experiments?
