- Paper proposes transferring spatial attention maps (from activations and gradients) from teacher to student CNNs to improve student performance.
- Experiments show attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and complements knowledge distillation across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet with multiple architectures.
- Key finding: activation-based attention transfer is particularly effective; gradient-based attention and symmetry regularization are also explored.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions due to novelty, strong empirical results, and thorough evaluation.
- Clear problem definition and motivation linking attention transfer to human visual perception.
- Novel contribution: explicit attention-map transfer as a form of knowledge distillation, including both activation- and gradient-based methods.
- Strong empirical validation with ablation studies and notable ImageNet improvements.
- Thorough exploration of variants (attention mapping functions, KD combination) and multiple architectures.
- Code and models are provided, aiding reproducibility.
- Need more analysis of computational cost of attention transfer alone and its impact on training time.
- Request sensitivity analysis for the β loss-weighting hyperparameter across datasets and architectures.
- Suggest deeper theoretical justification for why attention transfer works (e.g., information-theoretic or representational arguments).
- Gradient-based attention section needs clearer description of challenges (e.g., batch norm issues) and limitations.
- ImageNet experiments require more details: pretrained weights, KD settings, and hyperparameters for reproducibility.
- Questions for authors: why attention maps beat activations, solutions for batch-norm in gradient-based transfer, visual differences in student vs teacher attention, limitations/ failure cases, and applicability to detection/localization.
