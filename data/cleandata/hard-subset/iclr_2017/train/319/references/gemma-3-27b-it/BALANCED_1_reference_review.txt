This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps, specifically activation-based and gradient-based approaches. The core idea is to improve a student network's performance by providing it with information about *where* a teacher network is looking (i.e., its attention). The authors define attention as spatial maps and propose methods to transfer this information, demonstrating improvements across various datasets and architectures.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea but suffers from a lack of clarity in experimental design, insufficient novelty, and a disconnect between the motivating claims and the presented results. While improvements are reported, the scale and consistency of these improvements, particularly on larger datasets, are not compelling enough for acceptance at ICLR.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The paper builds upon existing concepts of attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation. While the specific combination of transferring attention maps is a contribution, the approach feels more like an exploration of existing techniques than a fundamentally new method. The claims of novelty in the introduction ("We propose attention as a mechanism…") are overstated. Many previous works already utilize attention for knowledge transfer, even if not framed *exactly* as presented here.  The authors acknowledge related work but don’t sufficiently differentiate their approach.

2. **Experimental Design & Reporting:** The experimental section is convoluted and difficult to follow. The results on CIFAR are presented with numerous ablation studies and comparisons (different attention mapping functions, KD, etc.), but the narrative is fragmented. The ImageNet results are particularly weak – a 1.1% top-1 improvement is modest and not thoroughly justified given the experimental setup. Furthermore, the authors mention not fully tuning hyperparameters for ImageNet, which undermines the credibility of these findings. The justification for choosing specific architectures and loss functions feels arbitrary.  The figures in the appendix (referenced in the main text) are poorly labelled and hard to interpret.  The statement “We also could not find applications of FitNets, KD or similar methods on ImageNet in the literature.” is simply incorrect; there’s considerable related work.

3. **Insufficient Motivation & Impact:** The initial motivation framing attention transfer as a way to address different attentional strategies based on observer knowledge feels disconnected from the technical implementation. The paper focuses primarily on performance improvement, losing sight of the initial psychological framing. The reported improvements, even when significant, don't demonstrate a substantial leap forward in our understanding of attention or knowledge transfer.  The authors claim attention maps carry more information than full activations, yet don't provide a compelling theoretical explanation for *why* this is the case.

**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Clarify the core contribution:** Is this paper about a new knowledge transfer technique, a novel way to define/visualize attention, or an investigation of the properties of attention maps? Focusing on one central theme would improve the paper’s coherence.
* **Strengthen the experimental setup:** Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter search, especially for ImageNet experiments. Include error bars or confidence intervals to show the statistical significance of the results.
* **Provide a more robust comparison to baselines:** Compare the proposed method against strong, state-of-the-art knowledge distillation techniques tailored to the specific datasets.
* **Improve clarity and organization:** Rewrite the experimental section to be more concise and focused. Clearly explain the rationale behind each experiment. Use consistent terminology throughout the paper.
* **Address the theoretical gap:** Provide a more compelling explanation for why transferring attention maps is more effective than transferring full activations.
* **Expand the analysis of attention maps:** Conduct a more detailed qualitative analysis of the transferred attention maps. Visualize the differences between teacher and student attention.
* **Explore alternative attention map definitions:** Investigate other methods for defining attention maps beyond the proposed sum/max/power functions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of activation functions used to generate attention maps (sum, max, power)? What are the theoretical properties that make these functions suitable for capturing relevant attention information?
* The ImageNet results seem limited. Were any attempts made to scale up the experiments (e.g., using larger student networks, longer training times)?
* You mention the difficulty of applying gradient-based attention transfer with batch normalization. Have you explored alternative techniques to address this issue?
*  The introduction alludes to psychological motivations for understanding different attentional strategies. How does this work contribute to that understanding? Could you elaborate on how the observed differences in attention maps relate to differing observer knowledge or goals?
* The claim that no other methods have been applied to ImageNet is incorrect. Can you address this?
