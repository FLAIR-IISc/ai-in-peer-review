## ICLR 2024 Review: Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention information from a teacher convolutional neural network (CNN) to a student CNN to improve performance. The authors define attention as spatial maps derived from activations and gradients, exploring their properties across different datasets and architectures. They propose several methods for transferring this attention, demonstrating significant performance improvements in various settings, including image classification tasks on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet. Key findings include the effectiveness of activation-based attention transfer over full activation transfer and its compatibility with knowledge distillation. The work provides a valuable exploration into leveraging attention mechanisms for knowledge transfer, potentially offering a compelling alternative to traditional knowledge distillation methods.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons**

**Accept** with minor revisions. 

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer using attention maps. The empirical results are generally positive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods across a range of datasets and architectures. The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention, along with the insightful comparison to full activation transfer, strengthens the contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of transferring attention maps as a form of knowledge transfer is interesting and relatively unexplored. The authors effectively link this to theories of human attention and its role in perception.
* **Methodological Soundness:** The definitions of activation-based and gradient-based attention are clear and reasonable. The proposed transfer methods, along with the loss function, are well-defined and appear appropriate for the task.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments across diverse datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). The observed performance improvements, particularly on ImageNet with ResNets, are encouraging.
* **Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies comparing different attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpssum, Fpmax) and the comparison to full activation transfer provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and structured, with clear explanations of the methods and experimental setup.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement)**

* **Gradient-Based Attention Detail:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer feels less fleshed out than the activation-based section.  More details on the implementation, challenges faced (e.g., handling batch normalization during the second backpropagation), and a deeper analysis of the results would be beneficial. While acknowledging the complexity, a more thorough exploration would strengthen the contribution.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, but doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the results to the β parameter in the attention transfer loss. Providing a brief analysis of how performance changes with different β values would be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer process would be useful. While the authors mention minimal computational cost when combined with knowledge distillation, quantifying this overhead would provide a more complete picture.
* **Visualization:** While the paper includes some visualization of attention maps, more extensive and comparative visualizations (e.g., comparing attention maps of teacher and student networks) could further illustrate the effectiveness of the transfer.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the motivation draws from psychological theories of attention, exploring potential theoretical connections between attention transfer and generalization performance could add another layer of depth.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Regarding Gradient-Based Attention:**  You mention challenges with batch normalization during the second backpropagation step. Could you elaborate on the specific issues encountered and the chosen resolution? Are there alternative approaches to addressing this challenge?
* **Regarding β Parameter:**  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter? Did you perform a hyperparameter search to optimize β for each experiment?
* **Regarding Computational Cost:** Can you provide a more quantitative estimate of the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer process, compared to training a student network without attention transfer?
* **Regarding Attention Map Similarity:** You state that student attention maps become more similar to teacher maps after transfer. Can you quantify this similarity using a metric such as cosine similarity or intersection-over-union?
* **Regarding Knowledge Distillation:** On ImageNet you mention that KD did not help. Do you have a hypothesis about why this might be the case (given that it worked well on CIFAR)?  Did you try different KD temperatures or loss weights?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution. Addressing the suggested feedback will further enhance its impact and clarity.  I am confident that this work will be of interest to the ICLR community.
