- Investigates transferring activation- and gradient-based attention maps from teacher to student CNNs to improve performance.  
- Decision: Accept with Minor Revisions.  
- Novelty: Proposes attention transfer as a novel, well-motivated alternative to full activation transfer or standard distillation.  
- Empirical: Strong, consistent improvements on CIFAR; activation-based attention often outperforms full activation transfer; positive but less conclusive ImageNet results.  
- Exploration: Thorough evaluation of attention map definitions (sum, max, power), loss functions, and inclusion of gradient-based methods.  
- Presentation: Paper is clear, well-organized, and includes effective visualizations.  
- ImageNet results need deeper hyperparameter tuning and discussion of challenges applying gradient-based methods at scale.  
- Computational cost: Paper should report and discuss the overhead of attention transfer relative to other distillation techniques.  
- Beta ablation: Requires a systematic sensitivity study of the weighting hyperparameter β across datasets and architectures.  
- Qualitative analysis: Recommend more systematic comparison of teacher vs student attention maps beyond appendix examples.  
- Interpretability connection: Strengthen links to CNN interpretability literature (e.g., Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients).  
- Q1: Request specifics on challenges and limitations encountered during ImageNet hyperparameter tuning.  
- Q2: Ask how gradient-based attention transfer interacts with batch normalization and whether alternatives (e.g., freezing BN stats) were considered.  
- Q3: Request the range of β values tested and performance variation on CIFAR.  
- Q4: Inquire whether students sometimes develop different but still effective attention maps and why.  
- Q5: Ask how computational cost scales with image size and network depth, and strategies to mitigate cost in constrained environments.
