This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention mechanisms. The authors propose to define attention as spatial maps – either activation-based or gradient-based – and utilize these maps to guide the learning of a student network from a pre-trained teacher network. They demonstrate improvements across several datasets and network architectures, showing that attention-based transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.

**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting idea and shows promising results, the lack of detailed analysis and sufficient ablation studies, coupled with the somewhat superficial experimental evaluation, prevents me from recommending acceptance at this time. The contributions, while present, do not represent a significant advancement to warrant publication in ICLR.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Insufficient Depth in Analysis:** The paper identifies that attention maps carry valuable information, but the analysis of *what* specifically makes them valuable is limited. The discussion on different activation-based mapping functions (sum, max, power) is descriptive, but lacks a rigorous investigation into why certain functions perform better than others. Furthermore, the comparison to full activation transfer feels somewhat cursory; a deeper understanding of the differences in the information encoded within attention maps versus full activations would be beneficial.  The reason for the failure of KD on ImageNet is not investigated.

2. **Limited Experimental Scope & Reporting:** While experiments are conducted on multiple datasets, the hyperparameter tuning seems minimal, particularly for the ImageNet experiments. Reporting the best result *with* a mention of limited tuning is insufficient.  The ablation studies are not comprehensive, particularly concerning the number of transfer losses (using all groups vs. individual groups).  The discussion of CIFAR experiments feels fragmented, with different setups and justifications (e.g., the change in network architecture for gradient-based transfer). The presentation of results is also sometimes unclear – for instance, Table 3 doesn’t clearly define “min-l2”.  The code availability is good, but the results aren’t easily reproducible without considerable effort. The statement that the proposed method is the first to successfully apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet feels like a strong claim that needs more robust justification.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for why attention transfer should work. While the motivation is inspired by human attention, the connection between the proposed method and cognitive science is superficial. A more formal analysis of the information encoded in attention maps and how it facilitates learning could strengthen the paper. The reliance on empirical observation, while valuable, is not enough to establish a convincing argument.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies. Specifically, investigate the impact of: (a) different numbers of transfer layers, (b) different loss weights (β), (c) different normalization schemes for attention maps (L1 vs L2), (d) the effect of different KD temperatures.
*   **Analysis of Attention Maps:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned attention maps. Visualize and compare attention maps from different layers, different architectures, and different datasets.  Quantify the similarity between teacher and student attention maps using metrics beyond visual inspection.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter search for the ImageNet experiments. Report the range of hyperparameters explored and the corresponding results.
*   **Theoretical Framework:**  Explore potential theoretical justifications for attention transfer. Could information-theoretic concepts (e.g., mutual information) be used to quantify the information transfer?
*   **Clarify Presentation:** Improve the clarity of the experimental setup and results presentation. Define all terms clearly and provide more detailed explanations of the different experimental configurations.
*   **Address Gradient-based Transfer Limitations**: Investigate *why* the gradient-based attention transfer was limited by batch normalization. Is the issue simply a matter of backpropagating through batch norm statistics, or is there a deeper incompatibility?
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Assess the robustness of the approach to noisy or corrupted attention maps.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide more insight into why certain activation-based mapping functions (e.g., sum of squares) outperform others? What properties of these functions are crucial for effective attention transfer?
*   What attempts were made to optimize knowledge distillation for the ImageNet dataset? What specific challenges were encountered that prevented it from working effectively?
*   The paper states that the attention maps “contain valuable information”. Can you quantify this “value” using metrics beyond the performance improvement of the student network?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the attention transfer method to the choice of the teacher network? Would a poorly trained teacher network still provide beneficial attention maps?
*   Can you explain the motivation behind the horizontal flip invariance constraint in the gradient-based attention transfer method? Does it improve generalization performance, and if so, why?
