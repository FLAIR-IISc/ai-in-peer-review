This paper investigates knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps, proposing activation-based and gradient-based methods to identify salient regions and subsequently transfer this information from a teacher to a student network. The authors demonstrate improvements across various datasets and architectures, showcasing the efficacy of attention transfer, particularly activation-based approaches, and its compatibility with knowledge distillation.

**Reject.** While the idea of transferring attention maps for knowledge distillation is reasonable and the empirical results demonstrate some improvements, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies in methodology, presentation, and experimental setup that collectively undermine confidence in the reported findings. The level of detail is insufficient for reproducibility, and several claims lack robust support.

The primary concerns stem from the following:

1. **Lack of Rigor in Experimental Design & Reporting:** The experimental section feels rushed and lacks sufficient detail. For example, the specific hyperparameters used for knowledge distillation (temperature, alpha) are mentioned but not consistently applied or clearly explained for each experiment. The justification for specific choices (e.g., not using ZCA whitening) is weak. The claim of not having time to tune hyperparameters on ImageNet feels like an admission of incomplete work. Table 5 lacks crucial details like standard deviations, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the reported gains. The description of data augmentation is also inconsistent and vague.

2. **Inconsistent Methodology and Unclear Ablations:** The paper presents a variety of attention mapping functions but lacks a comprehensive ablation study to justify the choice of F<sup>2</sup><sub>sum</sub> as the preferred method. The brief comparison in Table 2 is insufficient. Furthermore, the implementation details regarding the gradient-based approach, particularly the handling of batch normalization during the second backpropagation step, are glossed over. The authors acknowledge this as an issue, further weakening the confidence in these results.  The shift in approach (fixing batch norm layers during finetuning) without clear justification is also concerning.

3. **Overstated Claims and Lack of Novelty:** While the improvements reported are positive, they are often marginal and presented with excessive enthusiasm. The claim of being “the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” is dubious, given the extensive literature on knowledge distillation and transfer learning in that domain. The contribution regarding gradient-based attention transfer feels incremental, relying heavily on established techniques like double backpropagation.

4. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper is riddled with minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of notation, unclear referencing). The figures, while present, are often poorly labeled and difficult to interpret. The quality of writing, particularly in the experimental section, is subpar and hampers clarity. There are numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings.  The appendix feels tacked on and lacks proper organization.

5. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations:** The authors do not adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example, the computational cost of the gradient-based attention transfer is not thoroughly discussed. The sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters is also not adequately explored.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide a detailed experimental setup, including all hyperparameters, data augmentation strategies, and training procedures. Release the code.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to justify the design choices and assess the contribution of each component of the proposed approach.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report standard deviations and p-values to demonstrate the statistical significance of the reported results.
*   **Clarify Claims:** Temper the claims and avoid hyperbole. Be more precise in describing the novelty and contributions of the work.
*   **Improve Writing and Formatting:** Proofread the paper carefully to eliminate grammatical errors, improve clarity, and ensure consistency in formatting.
*   **Address Limitations:** Discuss the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research.




**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why ZCA whitening was omitted from the experiments?
*   What specific criteria were used to choose the hyperparameters for knowledge distillation, and why were they kept constant across all experiments?
*   What is the computational overhead associated with the gradient-based attention transfer compared to standard training?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter in the attention transfer loss? Have you explored a range of values?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how batch normalization is handled during the second backpropagation step in the gradient-based approach?



In summary, while the ideas presented in this paper are potentially interesting, the numerous flaws in methodology, presentation, and experimental validation prevent me from recommending acceptance. A significant revision addressing the concerns outlined above is required before this work could be considered for publication.
