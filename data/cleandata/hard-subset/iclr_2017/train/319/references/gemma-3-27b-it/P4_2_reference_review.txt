This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention mechanisms. The authors propose using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps to facilitate learning in a student network guided by a teacher network. They demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures, and show that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer.

**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea but lacks sufficient rigor and clarity in its experimental setup and analysis to warrant acceptance. While improvements are reported, the claims are not convincingly supported due to a lack of detailed ablation studies, insufficient comparison to strong baselines, and a somewhat disorganized presentation of results.

The primary issue is a lack of clarity regarding the experimental methodology. The paper mentions numerous variations of attention transfer and knowledge distillation, but often presents results without clearly specifying which combinations were used for each experiment. For instance, in the CIFAR experiments, the effect of different attention mapping functions is presented in Table 2, but the exact training procedure (hyperparameter settings, optimization algorithm) is not specified for each configuration, making it difficult to assess the significance of the observed differences. Furthermore, the statement that “KD converges significantly slower” on ImageNet without presenting any quantitative data feels anecdotal.

The experiments themselves, while extensive, lack sufficient comparison to strong baselines. While FitNets is briefly mentioned, a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art knowledge distillation methods is missing, particularly on the ImageNet dataset. The claim that this is “the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” is overly strong given the rich literature on knowledge distillation and transfer learning. Showing that their *specific* method achieves better results than existing methods on ImageNet is critical.

Finally, the writing is somewhat convoluted and lacks a logical flow. The introduction is lengthy and could be more concise. The experimental sections are dense and difficult to follow without carefully parsing the text. Figure 6, which aims to visualize the similarity of attention maps, is not effectively explained and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate the benefit of the proposed method.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:**  A more thorough ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of each component of the proposed approach. For example, what is the effect of using different normalization techniques for the attention maps? How does the choice of the loss function (l2 vs. other distances) impact performance?
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Provide a clear explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process. Were hyperparameters tuned separately for each experiment? What search space was used?
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to chance.
*   **Code Availability:** While code is mentioned, ensuring the code is readily accessible and well-documented would greatly improve the reproducibility of the results.
*   **Clarify Terminology:** Define key terms more precisely. What exactly is meant by "thin" and "wide" networks?
*   **Rewrite for Clarity:**  The paper would benefit from a significant rewrite to improve clarity and flow. Break down complex ideas into smaller, more digestible chunks.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a detailed table outlining the hyperparameter settings (learning rate, batch size, weight decay, beta values for transfer losses, KD temperature, etc.) used for each experiment reported in the paper?
2.  What specific knowledge distillation methods did you compare against on ImageNet? If none were compared, what was the reasoning behind this decision?
3.  Can you provide more details on the methodology used to generate Figure 6? How was the similarity of attention maps quantified?
4.  You mention difficulties with KD on ImageNet. Were any alternative KD temperature schedules or loss functions explored?
5.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the second backpropagation step in the gradient-based attention transfer method? Is this a significant bottleneck in practice?
