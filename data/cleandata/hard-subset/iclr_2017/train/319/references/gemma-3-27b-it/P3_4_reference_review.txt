## ICLR 2024 Review: Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention information from a powerful “teacher” convolutional neural network to a smaller “student” network to improve performance. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps, exploring their characteristics across different datasets and architectures. They propose several novel methods for transferring this attention, demonstrating significant performance improvements across various tasks, including image classification on CIFAR and ImageNet.  A key finding is that transferring attention maps is more effective than directly transferring full activations, and that attention transfer can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer, demonstrating empirically significant performance gains. The exploration of different attention map definitions and transfer methods is thorough, and the results are compelling, particularly on ImageNet. The combination with knowledge distillation is a positive addition.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of explicitly transferring attention maps as a form of knowledge transfer is a valuable contribution. The motivation stemming from the cognitive science idea of attention guiding perception is well articulated. The observation that attention maps contain useful information beyond activations is intriguing and supported by the experimental results.
* **Thoroughness of Experiments:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments across diverse datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet). The comparisons between different attention map definitions (sum, max, powers) and transfer methods (attention vs. full activation) are particularly valuable.  The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer, while more complex, expands the scope of the investigation.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The reported improvements on ImageNet are particularly noteworthy, as achieving gains on this challenging dataset is often difficult.  The consistency of improvements across different architectures and datasets lends credibility to the approach. The visualization of attention maps (Figures 2, 4, and 6 in the appendix) provides qualitative support for the claim that attention transfer leads to more focused and discriminative attention in the student network.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the proposed methodology and experimental setup. The equations are clearly presented and the figures are informative.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Deeper Dive into Gradient-Based Attention:** The gradient-based attention transfer section feels less fleshed out than the activation-based approach. The implementation details are briefly mentioned, but a more detailed discussion of the challenges of second-order backpropagation and how they were addressed would be helpful.  The results on CIFAR are promising but limited. Exploring this method on larger datasets (potentially with careful parameter tuning) would strengthen the paper.
* **Ablation Studies on Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that hyperparameters were not extensively tuned for the ImageNet experiments. Providing some insight into the sensitivity of the results to the β parameter (attention transfer loss weight) would be valuable.  A small ablation study could demonstrate the robustness of the findings.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the additional computational cost associated with attention transfer (beyond the standard training cost) would be useful.  This is especially relevant for gradient-based attention, which involves second-order backpropagation.
* **Clarify the Relationship to Existing Attention Mechanisms:** The paper focuses on *transferring* attention. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss how this work relates to existing attention mechanisms *within* a single network (e.g., self-attention, channel attention). Is the transferred attention fundamentally different from attention learned during training?
* **More Detailed Appendix:** While the appendix provides helpful visualizations, expanding it with more examples of attention maps for different layers and datasets would further enhance the understanding of the proposed approach.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the choice of activation functions for attention mapping (Fsum, Fpsun, Fpmax):** What is the intuition behind the superiority of sum-based functions over max-based ones? Are there specific scenarios where max-based functions might be preferred?
* **Regarding gradient-based attention:**  Could you elaborate on the challenges of implementing second-order backpropagation with batch normalization? Did you explore alternative strategies for handling batch normalization during this process?
* **Regarding ImageNet experiments:**  Why did knowledge distillation not yield positive results on ImageNet, while it did on CIFAR? Do you have any hypotheses regarding this difference?
* **Regarding future work:** Beyond object detection and weakly-supervised localization, are there other domains where you believe attention transfer could be particularly beneficial?



Overall, this is a well-executed and impactful piece of research.  Addressing the minor points above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field. I strongly recommend accepting this paper for publication at ICLR 2024.
