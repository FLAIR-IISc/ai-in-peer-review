This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps, proposing to use both activation-based and gradient-based attention to improve student network performance. The authors define attention as spatial maps indicating network focus and demonstrate improvements across various datasets and architectures. The core contribution lies in framing attention as a transfer mechanism and proposing novel methods for attention-based knowledge distillation.

**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but lacks sufficient rigor and clarity in its experimental design and analysis to warrant acceptance. While improvements are reported, the lack of ablation studies and clear justification for hyperparameter choices weakens the conclusions. The claims of novelty are also overstated, as many components build heavily on existing knowledge distillation and attention visualization techniques.

The primary issues are:

1. **Insufficient Ablation & Control Experiments:** The paper demonstrates performance improvements but doesn't adequately dissect *why* these improvements occur.  For example, the comparison between full activation transfer and attention transfer (Table 1) is valuable, but lacks depth.  What specific properties of attention maps lead to the observed gains? Similarly, the impact of different attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) isn’t convincingly explained beyond a descriptive comparison. There’s a lack of exploration of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters like β.
2. **Limited Novelty:** The core ideas – using attention maps for knowledge transfer and exploring gradient-based attention – build directly on established work in knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) and attention visualization (Simonyan et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2016). While combining these concepts isn’t trivial, the paper doesn’t clearly articulate a substantial novel contribution *beyond* this combination. The claim of being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is potentially misleading without clarifying the specific transfer mechanism and comparing it directly to existing KD methods on the same setup.
3. **Lack of Clarity & Detail:**  The experimental setup and justification for certain design choices are often unclear. The statement that "we did not have time to tune any hyperparameters" (regarding ImageNet experiments) is a significant weakness. A rigorous evaluation *requires* hyperparameter tuning. The description of the gradient-based attention transfer in Section 3.2 is complex and could benefit from a more simplified explanation. The discussion of batch normalization in the gradient-based section is also confusing.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Quantitative Analysis:** Provide more rigorous quantitative analysis. Include error bars on results and statistical significance tests to support claims of improvement.
* **Visualization:** While attention maps are visualized, their interpretation is often superficial. More detailed analysis of *what* the networks are attending to could provide valuable insights.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Perform a sensitivity analysis of the β parameter (attention transfer weight) to understand its impact on performance.
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead of the proposed attention transfer methods, particularly the double backpropagation required for gradient-based attention.
* **Code Release:**  Emphasize the importance of code release for reproducibility.
* **Clarify the relationship to existing work:** The "Related Work" section is a good start, but the paper could more clearly position its contributions relative to existing literature.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the justification for choosing the specific forms of the attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax)? Have you explored other options, and if so, what were the results?
2.  What is the rationale behind the choice of the β parameter value? Did you perform a hyperparameter search, and if so, what were the optimal values found?
3.  The paper mentions challenges with knowledge distillation on ImageNet. Can you provide more details on the specific issues encountered and why the proposed attention transfer methods didn't alleviate them?
4.  In the gradient-based attention transfer, how is the second backpropagation step implemented in practice, specifically concerning batch normalization layers?
5. Could you provide a more detailed explanation regarding the claim of being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet?" What specific comparison was made with existing methods, and why was this transfer considered new?
