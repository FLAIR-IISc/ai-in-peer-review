- Paper studies transferring spatial attention maps (from activations and gradients) from teacher to student CNNs to improve student performance across architectures and tasks.
- Main contribution: framing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism and systematically comparing attention map definitions, showing improvements, especially with knowledge distillation.
- Decision: Reject — improvements are promising but presentation, novelty, and analysis are insufficient for acceptance.
- Limited novelty: attention transfer overlaps substantially with prior knowledge distillation and hint-based learning and lacks a clear distinction or fundamental breakthrough.
- Clarity and organization: paper is disorganized, transitions are abrupt, and figures (e.g., Fig. 1) and motivations are not well explained.
- Insufficient analysis: results show that attention transfer helps but the paper fails to explain why—e.g., whether it acts as regularization or guides feature learning.
- Fragmented experiments: experiments span datasets, architectures, and methods without a unifying narrative, and ImageNet results are preliminary and under-detailed.
- Hyperparameter and comparability concerns: inconsistent hyperparameter tuning across experiments undermines result comparability.
- Gradient-based transfer: implementation challenges (double backprop, batch-norm issues) and limited success make this section underdeveloped and weakly justified.
- Suggest strengthening motivation and related work to clearly differentiate from prior distillation/hint literature and expand psychological rationale for attention.
- Recommend clearer organization, simpler writing, and more descriptive figures and experimental descriptions.
- Urge in-depth analysis: ablation studies, visualizations, similarity quantification between teacher and student attention maps, and breakdowns of transfer loss effects.
- Advise focusing experiments on fewer architectures/datasets with thorough hyperparameter searches for more definitive conclusions.
- Either better justify or remove gradient-based transfer; if kept, address implementation issues and alternatives.
- Suggest expanding evaluation to other tasks (e.g., detection, segmentation) to demonstrate broader applicability.
- Questions for authors to address: quantify computational cost of attention transfer, rationale for L2 normalization vs alternatives, why activation-based attention beats full activation transfer, ImageNet training challenges, and sensitivity/selection of the β loss parameter.
