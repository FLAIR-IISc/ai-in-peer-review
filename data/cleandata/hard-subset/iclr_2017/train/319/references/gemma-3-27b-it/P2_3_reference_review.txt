## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper explores the transfer of knowledge between convolutional neural networks using attention maps. The authors propose utilizing both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps to guide the learning process of a student network, benefiting from a pre-trained teacher network. They present several methods for transferring this attention information and demonstrate significant improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). A key finding is that transferring attention maps is more effective than directly transferring activations, and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.

**Decision:** **Accept with Minor Revisions**

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate consistent improvements across a range of scenarios. The exploration of both activation and gradient-based attention, and the clear demonstration of the superiority of attention transfer over full activation transfer, are strong contributions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the problem of leveraging attention mechanisms for knowledge transfer, linking it to human visual perception and the benefits of attention in neural networks. The motivation for exploring different attention map types and transfer strategies is well-articulated.
* **Novelty:**  While knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms are individually well-studied, the combination of transferring *attention maps* specifically, and the comparison between activation and gradient-based approaches, feels novel.  The demonstration of benefit on ImageNet (even with limited hyperparameter tuning) is particularly significant.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering diverse datasets and architectures.  The consistent improvements observed with attention transfer, especially the comparison to full activation transfer and the combination with knowledge distillation, strongly support the claims. The visualization of attention maps (in the appendix) helps to understand the effectiveness of the approach.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The structure is logical, and the explanations are clear.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the computational cost of gradient-based attention transfer is similar to backpropagation, a more detailed discussion of the computational overhead of attention transfer compared to standard training or knowledge distillation would be valuable.
* **Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies on CIFAR are good, but expanding these to other datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would strengthen the conclusions. For instance, demonstrating the impact of using different numbers of layers for attention transfer.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors acknowledge limited hyperparameter tuning on ImageNet, exploring the sensitivity of the results to the β parameter (attention transfer weight) could provide further insights.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper relies heavily on empirical evidence. Providing some theoretical justification for *why* attention transfer is effective (e.g., relating it to information bottleneck principles or representation learning) could further strengthen the work.
* **Clarify the Normalization:** The explanation for the l2 normalization of attention maps feels slightly incomplete. Is this just for scaling, or does it have a deeper theoretical motivation?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that KD struggles on ImageNet with differing architectures. Did you investigate any techniques to mitigate this (e.g., adapter layers, intermediate feature matching)?
2.  The ablation study on CIFAR showing better performance with all three attention transfer layers vs. individual layers is interesting. Do you have any intuition why using all layers provides a slight improvement over using just the most informative one(s)?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of attention mapping function (Fsum, F2sum, etc.)? Are there specific datasets or architectures where certain mapping functions consistently outperform others?
4.  You briefly mention exploring gradient-based attention transfer with batch normalization in future work. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered when combining double backpropagation with batch normalization?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms.  The minor revisions suggested above would further strengthen the work and make it even more impactful.
