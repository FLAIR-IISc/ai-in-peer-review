## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention mechanisms between convolutional neural networks (CNNs) as a means of knowledge transfer. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial maps and propose several methods for transferring this attention from a teacher network to a student network.  They demonstrate empirically that their approach leads to significant performance improvements across various datasets and network architectures, including both residual and non-residual networks.  Furthermore, they show that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.

**Decision:** **Accept**

**Key Reasons:**

*   **Novelty & Potential Impact:** The work presents a novel approach to knowledge transfer focusing specifically on attention maps. The idea of transferring *where* a network looks, rather than just *what* it predicts, is intriguing and has demonstrated positive results. This has the potential to be a valuable tool for improving the performance of smaller models and for understanding the internal workings of CNNs.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide comprehensive experimental results across a diverse set of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures, consistently demonstrating the effectiveness of their proposed methods. The ablation studies (comparing different attention mapping functions, transfer methods, and the combination with knowledge distillation) are particularly strong.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology. The motivation for focusing on attention transfer is well-articulated, linking it to human visual perception.  The definitions of activation-based and gradient-based attention are sensible and the proposed transfer methods are logically sound.  

The experimental section is particularly impressive.  The results on CIFAR demonstrate consistent improvements with attention transfer, both alone and in combination with knowledge distillation.  The analysis of different attention mapping functions is insightful.  Extending the experiments to larger datasets like CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet further strengthens the conclusions, showing the generalizability of the approach.  The visualization of attention maps (referenced in the appendix, though not prominently featured in the main text) is a valuable addition, providing some qualitative evidence for the effectiveness of the transfer process. The inclusion of comparisons to full activation transfer and methods like FitNets reinforces the value of the proposed approach.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Visualization:** While the paper mentions visualizing attention maps, these are relegated to the appendix.  Including representative examples of attention maps in the main text (e.g., side-by-side comparisons of teacher and student attention) would significantly enhance the paper’s clarity and impact.  Showcase examples where the attention transfer *clearly* changes the student's focus.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions limited hyperparameter tuning for the ImageNet experiments. Discussing the sensitivity of the results to the transfer loss weight (β) and the KD temperature would be helpful.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more in-depth discussion of *why* attention transfer works could be beneficial. Is there a theoretical link between the attention maps and the learned representations?
* **Gradient-based clarity**: The gradient-based attention section is less clear than the activation based method. Explain why the results are less strong and clarify the computational expense.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Regarding visualization:** Could you provide a few compelling examples of attention maps showing the impact of transfer learning in the main text? Specifically, where does the student network's attention shift after learning from the teacher?
2.  **Regarding ImageNet:** Have you explored different methods for combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation on ImageNet? You mention KD did not help; could you elaborate on the specific settings tried and why you believe it failed to improve performance?
3. **Regarding gradient-based attention:** You note the difficulty implementing gradient-based methods with batch normalization. Can you clarify if this is a fundamental limitation, or a solvable implementation challenge?




Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to knowledge transfer. The extensive experimental validation and clear writing make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
