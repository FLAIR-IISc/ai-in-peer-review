This paper investigates knowledge transfer via attention maps between convolutional neural networks. The authors propose using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps to improve the performance of a student network, guided by a teacher network. They explore different methods for defining these attention maps and demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of sufficient rigor and clarity in the experimental evaluation. While the authors present a wide range of experiments, the details are often vague, and the improvements reported are not consistently substantial or convincingly demonstrated. The second reason is the limited novelty of the core idea. Transferring knowledge via intermediate representations is a well-established field (knowledge distillation), and applying this to attention maps, while a reasonable direction, doesn't represent a groundbreaking contribution without stronger evidence of its effectiveness.

The experimental section suffers from several weaknesses. The hyperparameter tuning process is not described, making it difficult to assess whether the reported improvements are sensitive to specific settings. The ablation studies, while present, are not comprehensive enough to fully understand the contribution of each component of the proposed approach. For example, the comparison between attention transfer and full activation transfer is valuable, but a more detailed analysis of the impact of different attention mapping functions (sum, max, power) would be beneficial. The claim of being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" requires significantly more justification and should be toned down. There's a considerable body of work on knowledge distillation *on* ImageNet, and it's unclear what specific limitation this work overcomes. The presentation of results is also inconsistent, with some tables lacking clear error bars or statistical significance tests. Regarding the gradient-based approach, the statement that it was limited to NIN architectures due to the complexity of automatic differentiation is a significant limitation and requires further explanation. Why is the double backpropagation so computationally expensive for more complex architectures?

The paper would be significantly improved by addressing the following points:

*   **Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide a complete description of the hyperparameters used in all experiments, including learning rates, batch sizes, weight decay, and the specific settings for knowledge distillation (temperature, alpha).
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all comparisons to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to chance.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed approach (e.g., different attention mapping functions, different loss weights).
*   **Clarity on ImageNet Claim:** Re-evaluate and revise the claim of being the first to successfully apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet, providing a thorough comparison to existing knowledge distillation methods.
*   **Gradient-Based Limitations:** Elaborate on the computational challenges associated with the gradient-based approach for more complex architectures. Explore potential approximations or optimizations to address these limitations.
*   **Visualization:** Provide more informative visualizations of the attention maps, potentially including comparisons between teacher and student attention maps.
*   **Discussion of Negative Results:** Discuss any experiments that did not yield positive results and provide potential explanations for the lack of improvement.

Questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the weight of the attention transfer loss (beta)?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the gradient-based approach is limited to simpler architectures? Are there any alternative methods for computing the second-order derivatives that could be more efficient?
*   Have you explored using different normalization techniques for the attention maps (e.g., L1 normalization)?
*   What is the computational overhead of the attention transfer mechanism compared to standard knowledge distillation?
*   Could you elaborate on the connection between the observed attention patterns and the learned features in the networks? Do the attention maps highlight semantically meaningful regions of the images?
*   Why did KD not work well on ImageNet with the tested parameters?
