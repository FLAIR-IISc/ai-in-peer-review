## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**Reviewer:** AI Assistant

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention mechanisms between convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors propose using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps to represent where a network “looks” when making predictions. They demonstrate that these attention maps contain valuable information and can be leveraged to improve the performance of smaller “student” networks by transferring knowledge from larger, more powerful “teacher” networks. The paper presents several novel methods for this attention transfer, showing significant performance gains across diverse datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). A key finding is that activation-based attention transfer outperforms simply transferring full activations and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer that yields promising results. The experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of attention transfer across a range of scenarios. The proposed method is a valuable addition to the field and has the potential to improve the performance of CNNs, especially in resource-constrained settings.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The idea of transferring attention maps as a form of knowledge transfer is a logical extension of research on both attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation. The hypothesis that attention patterns encode valuable information about learned representations is well-reasoned and grounded in the analogy to human visual attention.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across multiple datasets and architectures are convincing. The improvements observed, particularly on ImageNet, are substantial.  The ablation studies comparing different attention mapping functions (sum, max) and transfer methods (attention vs. full activation) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors clearly describe the experimental setup, including network architectures, training procedures, and hyperparameters. The inclusion of results for both same-depth and different-depth teacher-student setups is valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and organized, making the technical details accessible. The figures and tables are helpful in visualizing the results and understanding the different methods.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Deeper Dive into Gradient-Based Transfer:** The gradient-based attention transfer experiments are somewhat less extensive than the activation-based ones. Expanding these experiments, particularly exploring the impact of symmetry constraints and the challenges with batch normalization, would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the β parameter (controlling the weight of the attention transfer loss) would be beneficial.  A sensitivity analysis could reveal optimal values for β across different datasets and architectures.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the minimal computational cost of attention transfer combined with knowledge distillation, a more quantitative analysis of the added computational overhead would be useful. 
* **Qualitative Analysis of Attention Maps:**  While the paper shows attention maps visually, a more in-depth qualitative analysis comparing the attention maps of the teacher and student networks *before* and *after* attention transfer could provide further insights into the learning process.  Specifically, visualizing the difference between teacher and student attention maps would be enlightening.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the choice of norm (L2) in the attention transfer loss (Equation 2):**  Have you experimented with other norms (e.g., L1, cosine similarity)? If so, how did the performance compare?
* **Regarding the impact of network depth on KD success:** You mention KD struggling on ImageNet with the same architecture and depth. Is this issue specific to ImageNet, or have you observed it on other large-scale datasets? What is your hypothesis as to *why* this happens?
* **Regarding practical application:** In a real-world scenario where a pre-trained teacher network is not available, how would you suggest obtaining a suitable teacher network for attention transfer? Could self-training or semi-supervised learning be used to generate a teacher network?
* **Regarding computational efficiency:** Can you provide a rough estimate of the percentage increase in training time when incorporating attention transfer compared to training without it?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. Addressing the additional feedback points and answering the questions above would further enhance its impact and ensure its acceptance.
