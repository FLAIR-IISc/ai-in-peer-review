- Paper studies transferring attention maps between CNNs as a form of knowledge transfer.  
- Defines activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps and proposes methods to transfer them.  
- Empirically demonstrates consistent performance improvements across datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (residual and non-residual).  
- Activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.  
- Strong empirical validation with comprehensive ablation studies comparing attention mappings, transfer methods, and KD combination.  
- Clear, well-structured methodology and sensible motivation linking to human visual perception.  
- Visualizations of attention maps are included but relegated to the appendix rather than the main text.  
- Comparison to full activation transfer and methods like FitNets reinforces the approach’s value.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Suggestion: Include representative teacher–student attention map examples in the main text to better illustrate impact.  
- Suggestion: Report sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters (transfer loss weight β and KD temperature), especially for ImageNet experiments.  
- Suggestion: Provide more theoretical discussion of why attention transfer works and its relation to learned representations.  
- Criticism: Gradient-based attention section is less clear, shows weaker results, and needs clarification on computational cost.  
- Question: Please add compelling attention-map examples showing how student attention shifts after transfer.  
- Question: Elaborate on ImageNet KD experiments—what settings were tried and why KD did not help there?  
- Question: Clarify whether the batch-normalization difficulties with gradient-based attention are fundamental or an implementation issue.
