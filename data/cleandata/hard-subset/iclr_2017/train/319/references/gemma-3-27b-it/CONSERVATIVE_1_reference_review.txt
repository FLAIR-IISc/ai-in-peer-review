This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks using attention maps – specifically, activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention – as a mechanism for improving student network performance. The authors propose methods for transferring this attention information from a teacher network to a student, demonstrating improvements across various datasets and network architectures, including residual and non-residual networks. They also show that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full-activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging attention maps for knowledge transfer is intuitively appealing, the presented work lacks the rigorous validation and comparative analysis expected at ICLR. The improvements demonstrated are often incremental and not convincingly shown to be substantial, particularly given the added complexity. The paper feels exploratory rather than conclusive, and lacks a strong justification for the observed benefits.

The core issue is a lack of robust baselines. The comparisons to "full-activation transfer" are a good start, but the paper would greatly benefit from comparisons to well-established knowledge distillation techniques (beyond a brief mention of KD), and other commonly used transfer learning methods. Simply stating improvements "across a variety of datasets" without quantifying and contextualizing those improvements is insufficient. The improvements on ImageNet, while reported, are presented without sufficient detail (e.g., training time, hyperparameter sensitivity).  Furthermore, the gradient-based transfer experiments feel underdeveloped and lack a clear rationale for the specific choices made (e.g., why NIN without batch normalization?). 

The experimental setup, particularly regarding hyperparameter tuning, is not clearly described.  The claim that hyperparameters were not tuned for the ImageNet experiments is concerning and diminishes the significance of the reported results. It suggests the improvements might be highly sensitive to parameter settings and not generally applicable. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing different attention mapping functions) are useful, but lack sufficient depth; the rationale for *why* certain functions perform better is not explored. The authors mention a dependence on normalization of attention maps, but do not fully explain the implications of this. 

Finally, the presentation could be improved. The frequent reference to figures within the text (e.g., "see Fig. 4") feels disconnected without those figures being immediately present in the review. The writing is often verbose and could be more concise.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Strengthen Baselines:** Include thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art knowledge distillation methods, transfer learning techniques, and appropriate ablation studies.
*   **Detailed ImageNet Results:** Provide comprehensive details about the ImageNet experiments, including training time, hyperparameter settings, and a more in-depth analysis of the results.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more theoretical understanding of *why* attention transfer works. What properties of the attention maps are crucial for effective transfer?
*   **Gradient-Based Transfer:** Clarify the rationale behind the choices made in the gradient-based transfer experiments.
*   **Concise Writing:** Streamline the writing style and remove unnecessary verbosity.
*   **Complete Figures:** Ensure all referenced figures are readily available for review.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational overhead of implementing the proposed attention transfer methods compared to standard knowledge distillation?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of β (the transfer loss weight) in Equation 2?
*   Could you elaborate on why ImageNet experiments were not subject to extensive hyperparameter tuning?
*   What specific challenges prevented the successful application of knowledge distillation on the ImageNet dataset in your experiments?
*   What insights did you gain from visualizing the attention maps that helped you understand the effectiveness of different attention mapping functions?
