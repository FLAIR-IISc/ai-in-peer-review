This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via the transfer of attention maps – specifically activation-based and gradient-based maps – from a teacher to a student network. The authors propose different methods for defining these attention maps and demonstrate improvements in performance across several datasets and network architectures, including comparisons to full activation transfer and knowledge distillation. They investigate the impact of different attention mapping functions and symmetry constraints.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging attention maps for knowledge transfer is intuitively appealing, the presented work lacks the methodological rigor and comprehensive validation needed to convincingly demonstrate its significance. The improvements reported are often marginal and the experimentation feels somewhat exploratory rather than systematic. The numerous ablations, while insightful, don’t consistently build a strong case for the proposed methods.

The primary concern is the lack of robust baselines and a comprehensive comparison against well-established knowledge transfer techniques. While knowledge distillation is mentioned, the results suggest it doesn’t consistently work well in their setup, yet this isn’t thoroughly investigated. A more detailed comparison against other KD variations or more sophisticated transfer learning approaches would be crucial. The novelty of the approach isn’t substantial enough to justify the somewhat limited gains observed. Simply achieving "some improvement" across various settings isn’t compelling enough; the magnitude of improvement needs to be significant and consistently demonstrated.

Furthermore, the gradient-based attention transfer experiments are limited to a single network architecture and dataset, making it difficult to assess its generalizability. The explanation for the lack of success with KD on ImageNet is weak. It's unclear why KD struggles and if it's related to the specific implementation or hyperparameters, or a fundamental limitation. The claims around being “the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” are overstated – countless papers use transfer learning on ImageNet.

The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in the experimental setup. The hyperparameters are often not clearly reported or justified. The effect of different data augmentation techniques is not consistently evaluated. The ablation studies, while numerous, are presented in a fragmented manner, making it difficult to draw clear conclusions. The appendix provides figures, but lacks detailed captions and analysis.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the novelty:** The contribution of transferring attention maps needs to be more clearly articulated. How does this differ substantially from existing feature-based transfer learning methods?
*   **Strengthen baselines:** Perform a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art knowledge transfer methods, including various KD techniques, FitNets, and potentially adversarial training-based approaches.
*   **Robustness analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters, data augmentation, and network initialization.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support the claims of improvement.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While not strictly required, a theoretical analysis of why attention transfer works could strengthen the paper. Is there a connection to information theory or representational similarity?
*   **Clearer organization:** The presentation of the experimental results is somewhat disorganized. Consider presenting the results in a more structured manner, with clear tables and concise explanations.
*   **Gradient-Based Experiments:** Expand the gradient-based attention transfer experiments to more architectures and datasets.
*   **Explain ImageNet KD Failure:** Thoroughly investigate why KD doesn't work well on ImageNet. Provide a detailed analysis of the training dynamics and potential reasons for the failure.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational overhead of adding the attention transfer loss compared to standard training?
2.  Why did you choose to normalize the attention maps using l2 normalization? Have you experimented with other normalization techniques?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter search process, specifically for β in Equation 2?
4.  The paper mentions “thin deep networks”. What specific criteria define a network as “thin” in your context?
5.  Regarding the ImageNet experiments: What specific KD hyperparameters were used and how do they compare to those used in successful KD implementations in other papers?
