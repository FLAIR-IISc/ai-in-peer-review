This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via the transfer of attention maps – specifically, activation-based and gradient-based maps – with the aim of improving student network performance. The authors propose several methods for transferring this attention information and demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures, including comparisons to full activation transfer and knowledge distillation.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging attention maps for knowledge transfer is intuitive, the paper presents a somewhat scattered collection of experiments lacking the rigor and depth expected for a significant contribution. The improvements demonstrated, while often statistically significant, are frequently modest and not consistently observed across all settings. The analysis lacks a critical comparison to well-established knowledge transfer methods beyond basic knowledge distillation, and the experimental setup appears somewhat ad-hoc, with hyperparameter tuning described as limited.

The core issue is a lack of convincing evidence that the proposed attention transfer methods provide a substantial advantage over existing techniques. The claim of being the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is overstated; many prior works successfully utilize knowledge distillation and other transfer learning techniques on ImageNet. The reported 1.1% and 0.8% improvements on ImageNet with attention transfer are not compelling enough, given the computational cost and complexity added. The comparison to full activation transfer is crucial and shows attention transfer performs better, but the *magnitude* of this improvement needs to be more substantial to justify the approach. The CIFAR experiments are more promising, but the lack of extensive hyperparameter tuning and the variable impact of different attention mapping functions (Fsum, F2sum, etc.) raise concerns about the robustness of the findings. The gradient-based attention transfer section feels particularly underdeveloped, reliant on a specific network architecture and lacking comprehensive comparison.

Furthermore, the paper suffers from a somewhat disorganized presentation. The numerous ablation studies (different attention mapping functions, KD combinations, symmetry norms) are presented without a clear guiding narrative, making it difficult to synthesize the overall findings. The appendix provides valuable visualizations, but some (like Figure 6) are not referenced within the main text, diminishing their impact. 

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Strengthen Baselines:** Include more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art knowledge transfer methods like FitNets, and advanced distillation techniques. Consider exploring teacher-student combinations with different architectures and capacities more systematically.
*   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** Perform a more thorough hyperparameter search, particularly for the weighting factor (β) in the attention transfer loss. Demonstrate the sensitivity of the results to this parameter.
*   **Ablation Study Narrative:** Provide a clearer explanation of the rationale behind each ablation study and how the results contribute to a better understanding of the proposed methods.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Expand on the theoretical motivations for why attention transfer should be effective. What specific information is being transferred, and why is it beneficial for the student network?
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Quantify the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer process. Is the improvement in performance worth the added cost?
*   **Explain the failure of KD on ImageNet:** Provide insight into why KD did not work as expected on ImageNet and whether this is related to the architecture or other factors.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The authors mention limited hyperparameter tuning. Can you provide more detail on the specific hyperparameters explored and the rationale behind the chosen values?
2.  The ImageNet results are modest. Have you explored alternative attention transfer losses or loss weighting schemes that might yield more significant improvements?
3.  The choice of attention mapping functions (Fsum, F2sum, etc.) appears somewhat arbitrary. What criteria were used to select these functions, and why do you believe they are representative of the space of possible attention mappings?
4.  Why did the gradient-based attention transfer experiments focus on a specific network architecture and dataset? Are there limitations that prevent it from being applied more broadly?
5.  Can you provide more intuition about the regularization effect of horizontal flip invariance in the gradient-based approach? What specific aspects of the attention maps are being regularized?
