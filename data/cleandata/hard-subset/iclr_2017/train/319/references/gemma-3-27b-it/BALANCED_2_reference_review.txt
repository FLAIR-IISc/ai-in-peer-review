This paper investigates knowledge transfer via attention maps between convolutional neural networks. The authors propose utilizing both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps to improve the performance of a student network guided by a pre-trained teacher network. They demonstrate improvements across several datasets and network architectures.

**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor and clarity to support the claims. While improvements are reported, the presentation of results is often incomplete and lacks statistical significance testing, making it difficult to ascertain the reliability of the findings. The ablation studies are limited, and the hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly investigated. Furthermore, the connection between the proposed methods and existing literature on knowledge distillation and attention mechanisms is not always clearly articulated.

The main weaknesses stem from the experimental evaluation and presentation. The tables often present results without standard deviations or p-values, making it challenging to determine whether observed gains are statistically significant. For example, Table 1 presents improvements on CIFAR, but lacks error bars. The lack of comparison to strong baselines, especially more recent knowledge distillation techniques beyond the mentioned FitNets and Hinton et al. (2015), weakens the impact of the presented results. The section on gradient-based attention transfer is particularly sparse, relying heavily on a single, specific network architecture (NIN) and dataset (CIFAR).  The discussion of implementation details is also limited – for example, the justification for specific hyperparameters (like β) is missing. The claim of being “the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” is bold but needs much more justification, given the extensive literature on transfer learning and knowledge distillation. Simply stating it doesn't make it so.

Additionally, while the authors explore different activation mapping functions, the rationale behind choosing the specific functions and powers used (e.g., p=2) is not clearly explained. The connection between the choice of these functions and the underlying neuroscience of attention, as alluded to in the introduction, is not sufficiently developed.  The justification for the horizontal flip invariance regularization in the gradient-based approach also needs further elaboration.  Finally, the description of the experimental setup for ImageNet is brief, and details like the training schedule and optimizer settings are missing.

However, the core idea of leveraging attention maps for knowledge transfer is promising. The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention offers a valuable contribution. The initial results, even without rigorous statistical backing, suggest that attention information can be effectively transferred to improve student network performance. The attempt to combine attention transfer with knowledge distillation is also a positive aspect.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Statistical Significance:**  Provide standard deviations or confidence intervals for all reported results, and perform statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests) to validate the observed improvements.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** Compare the proposed methods against more recent and relevant knowledge distillation techniques, including those specifically designed for convolutional neural networks.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components (e.g., different attention mapping functions, KD, symmetry loss).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters, such as the weighting factor β.
*   **Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide a more detailed description of the experimental setup, including training schedules, optimizer settings, and data augmentation strategies.
*   **Justification of Choices:** Provide a clearer rationale for the choices made, such as the selection of attention mapping functions, regularization techniques, and hyperparameters.
*   **Expand Gradient-Based Experiments:**  Extend the gradient-based attention transfer experiments to more diverse architectures and datasets, beyond the single NIN-CIFAR setup.
*   **Clarify the Claim Regarding ImageNet:**  Provide strong evidence and comparisons to existing work to support the claim of being the first to successfully apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet.
*   **Connect to Neuroscience:** Elaborate on the link between the proposed attention maps and the biological basis of attention.
*   **Visualizations:**  Include more visualizations of the attention maps to help understand how the information is being transferred and utilized by the student network.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the hyperparameter β?  Did you perform a grid search or other optimization to determine the optimal value?
2.  Why was the gradient-based attention transfer limited to the NIN architecture and CIFAR dataset? Were there any challenges encountered when applying it to more complex architectures or datasets?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the implementation of the double backpropagation for gradient-based attention transfer, including any optimizations used to improve efficiency?
4.  What is the computational overhead associated with attention transfer, both during training and inference?
5.  How do the attention maps learned by the student network compare to those of the teacher network? Can you provide examples of attention maps for both networks on the same input images?
6.  Can you explain why KD didn’t improve results on ImageNet, while it did on CIFAR? Were different hyperparameters used?
