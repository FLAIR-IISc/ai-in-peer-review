## ICLR 2024 Review: Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper explores the transfer of attention information from a teacher convolutional neural network to a student network as a method for improving performance. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps and propose novel methods for transferring this information.  They demonstrate improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet) suggesting that attention maps contain valuable knowledge that can be effectively leveraged for knowledge transfer. The paper also investigates combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation and explores gradient-based attention with symmetry constraints.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the paper presents an interesting line of investigation and demonstrates some positive empirical results, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and rigor to confidently support the claims. The experimental design is somewhat fragmented, with varying levels of detail and control across different experiments, making it difficult to assess the generalizability and significance of the findings.  Crucially, the improvements reported, particularly on larger datasets like ImageNet, are modest and the lack of ablation studies makes it difficult to pinpoint the exact contributions of the proposed attention transfer methods.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Lack of Rigorous Ablation Studies:**  The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of attention transfer. For instance, the impact of different attention map definitions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) is briefly touched upon, but not systematically analyzed across all datasets and architectures.  Similarly, the benefit of combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation needs more thorough investigation.  Without these ablations, it’s hard to determine whether the observed improvements are genuinely due to attention transfer or other factors.
* **Inconsistent Experimental Detail:** The level of detail varies significantly between experiments. The CIFAR experiments are more detailed than the ImageNet experiments, and the gradient-based attention experiments feel less fleshed out. The justification for certain design choices (e.g., freezing batch normalization layers during finetuning on CUB/Scenes) is often missing.
* **Modest Improvements on Larger Datasets:** The reported improvements on ImageNet (1.1% top-1 accuracy) are relatively small, and while statistically significant, may not be practically meaningful.  The authors acknowledge they didn't fully tune hyperparameters, but this weakens the claim of demonstrating a significant performance gain.
* **Unclear Significance of Gradient-Based Results:** The gradient-based attention transfer experiments are limited in scope (thin NIN network on CIFAR). While the results are promising, it's unclear whether this approach will generalize to more complex architectures and datasets. The discussion of the double backpropagation and its implementation complexities feels cursory.
* **Weak Comparison to Existing Methods:** The comparison to FitNets-style full activation transfer is helpful, but a more thorough comparison to other knowledge distillation and transfer learning techniques would strengthen the paper. The claim of being the "first" successful application of knowledge transfer on ImageNet needs to be substantiated with a more comprehensive literature review.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarify the Definition of Attention:** While the paper proposes two definitions of attention (activation-based and gradient-based), the intuitive connection between these definitions and the underlying notion of “attention” from human vision remains somewhat unclear. A more thorough discussion of this connection would be beneficial.
* **Provide More Visualizations:**  Include more visualizations of the attention maps, particularly for the larger datasets (ImageNet, CUB, Scenes). Show how the student attention maps converge towards the teacher attention maps during training. This would help build intuition and demonstrate the effectiveness of the transfer process.
* **Improve the Structure and Clarity:** The writing is occasionally dense and could benefit from clearer organization and more concise explanations.  Consider using a consistent notation throughout the paper.
* **Address the Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the β parameter (loss weight) and other hyperparameters.  Provide guidance on how to choose appropriate values for different datasets and architectures.
* **Expand the Related Work:** The related work section is somewhat limited. Include a discussion of recent advances in attention mechanisms in computer vision, such as transformers and efficient attention mechanisms.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the ImageNet experiments, what was the primary reason for not fully tuning the hyperparameters?  Do you believe that more thorough tuning would lead to larger improvements?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the computational cost associated with gradient-based attention transfer, particularly the double backpropagation step?  How does this cost compare to the computational cost of knowledge distillation?
3.  In the CIFAR experiments, you mention that attention transfer works well with both knowledge distillation and on its own.  Can you explain under what circumstances one approach might be preferred over the other?
4.  Why did knowledge distillation fail to improve performance on ImageNet? Do you have any hypotheses regarding the causes of this failure?
5. The paper states the importance of normalization of attention maps, could you explain the impact of using different normalization schemes (L1 vs L2)?



In conclusion, while the paper presents a promising idea, it requires significant revisions to address the concerns raised above. A more rigorous experimental evaluation, clearer presentation, and a more thorough comparison to existing methods are needed to support the claims and justify acceptance.
