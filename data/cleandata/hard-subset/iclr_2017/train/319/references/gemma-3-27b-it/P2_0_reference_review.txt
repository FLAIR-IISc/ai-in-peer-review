## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of knowledge between convolutional neural networks via attention mechanisms. The authors propose defining attention as activation-based and gradient-based spatial maps, and explore methods for transferring these maps from a teacher network to a student network to improve performance. They demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures, showing that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.  The work also touches upon gradient-based attention transfer and symmetry constraints.

**Decision:** **Accept (with minor revisions)**

**Key Reasons:**

1. **Novelty and Relevance:** The idea of transferring attention maps as a form of knowledge transfer is a novel and potentially valuable contribution.  The exploration of different attention map definitions (activation vs. gradient-based) and transfer methods adds to the originality.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results showing consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet) and network architectures. The ablation studies comparing different attention mapping functions and transfer strategies are particularly convincing.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents its methodology and results. The motivation for exploring attention transfer is well-established, connecting to human visual perception and the need to understand and improve neural network learning.  

* **Clear Definitions:**  The definitions of activation-based and gradient-based attention maps are clearly articulated. The exploration of different statistical functions for generating activation-based maps (sum, max, power) is a strength.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors demonstrate their approach on a diverse range of datasets and architectures. The inclusion of both same-depth and different-depth teacher-student scenarios adds robustness to the findings.  The comparisons to full activation transfer and knowledge distillation are informative.
* **Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies on CIFAR (Table 1 & 2) clearly demonstrate the benefits of attention transfer and the impact of different attention mapping functions.
* **ImageNet Results:** While preliminary, the results on ImageNet are encouraging and suggest the potential for scaling the approach to larger datasets.
* **Code Availability:** The commitment to releasing the code is a significant positive aspect.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Gradient-Based Attention:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer feels less developed than the activation-based transfer.  The results are also less compelling.  I suggest either strengthening this section with more detailed experiments or streamlining it.  The discussion of the complexities of double backpropagation with batch normalization could be expanded.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors acknowledge limited hyperparameter tuning for the ImageNet experiments.  Further optimization could lead to even more significant improvements.  A brief discussion of the computational cost of the proposed methods (especially the double backpropagation) would be useful.
* **Visualization:**  While Fig. 6 shows a comparison of attention maps, more visualizations throughout the paper, particularly on the larger datasets, would help to illustrate the impact of attention transfer.  Specifically, visualizing the attention maps for gradient-based transfer would be helpful.
* **Discussion of Limitations:**  A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach would be beneficial. For example, are there specific architectures or datasets where attention transfer is less effective?
* **Related Work:** The related work section is quite extensive. While thorough, it could benefit from more focused discussion on techniques specifically related to knowledge transfer via intermediate representations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the choice of `β` in Equation 2, how sensitive are the results to this parameter? Did you perform a hyperparameter search over a range of values?
2.  The paper mentions that KD struggled on ImageNet with the same architecture teacher and student. Was this tested with different values of the temperature parameter?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why you believe activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer? What specific information is captured by the attention maps that is lost in the full activation tensors?
4.  Can you elaborate on the potential challenges and future directions for applying gradient-based attention transfer to more complex architectures with batch normalization?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach. The empirical results are convincing, and the authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the relevant literature. I believe that with the suggested minor revisions, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the ICLR conference.
