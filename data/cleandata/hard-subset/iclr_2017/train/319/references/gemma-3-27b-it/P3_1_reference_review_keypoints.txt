- Paper proposes transferring activation- and gradient-based attention maps from teacher to student to improve student performance.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions—solid contribution with extensive empirical validation.  
- Strong empirical results: consistent performance gains across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet and multiple architectures; attention often beats full activation transfer.  
- Novelty & motivation: attention-map transfer is a novel idea, motivated by cognitive insights that different observers attend to different image regions.  
- Thorough exploration: compares activation- vs gradient-based attention, several attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax), and combination with knowledge distillation; includes horizontal flip invariance.  
- Clear presentation: well-written paper with effective figures and tables.  
- Computational cost: claim of “very little” overhead lacks quantitative measurement of added training time.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: need deeper analysis of β (attention loss weight) sensitivity and robustness.  
- Qualitative analysis: recommend more visual comparisons of teacher vs student attention maps, possibly overlaid with object segmentation.  
- Theoretical justification: empirical results strong but paper would benefit from theory (e.g., connections to information theory or representational similarity).  
- ImageNet evaluation: promising results but hyperparameters not fully tuned; more thorough ImageNet experiments recommended.  
- Gradient-based attention question: batch normalization complicates second-order backprop—ask if approximations or alternative techniques were considered.  
- Attention normalization question: used L2 normalization—ask if L1, softmax, or other normalizations were tried and how they performed.  
- Beta selection question: β set to 10^3/(attention map elements × batch size)—ask for intuition and robustness of this rule of thumb.  
- Generalization question: ask whether attention transfer would extend to tasks beyond classification (detection, segmentation) and what modifications would be needed.  
- Reproducibility request: ask authors to release code used for experiments.
