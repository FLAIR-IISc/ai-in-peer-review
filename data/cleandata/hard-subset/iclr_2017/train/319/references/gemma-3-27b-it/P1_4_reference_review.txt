## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**Reviewer:** AI Assistant

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attentional information from a powerful “teacher” convolutional neural network to a smaller “student” network to improve performance. The authors define attention as spatial maps derived from both activation patterns and gradients, exploring how these maps differ across datasets and architectures. They propose and evaluate several methods for transferring this attention, demonstrating significant performance improvements across a variety of tasks and network types, including residual and non-residual networks. Importantly, they show that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** – This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer using attention mechanisms. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent performance gains across a variety of settings. The findings offer valuable insights into how attention can be leveraged to improve the efficiency and performance of CNNs.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of transferring knowledge from larger, more powerful networks to smaller ones, and argues convincingly for the importance of attention as a potential transfer mechanism, drawing parallels to human visual perception.
*   **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** The concept of transferring attention maps, both activation-based and gradient-based, is novel and well-defined. The proposed methods (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) are simple yet effective, and the mathematical formulation of the transfer losses (Equation 2 & 4) is clear and concise.
*   **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach across a diverse range of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). The ablation studies comparing different attention mapping functions and transfer methods are particularly insightful.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently show significant performance improvements with attention transfer, both in isolation and in combination with knowledge distillation. The improvement on ImageNet is a particularly noteworthy achievement.
*   **Comparison to Existing Work:** The paper provides a thorough review of related work on attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation, clearly positioning the contributions within the existing literature.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

*   **Gradient-Based Attention Detail:** While the gradient-based attention transfer is mentioned, the results presented are less extensive than the activation-based methods. Providing more detailed results and analysis for gradient-based transfer (particularly on larger datasets) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the β parameter in the transfer loss, would be beneficial. How does performance vary with different values of β?
*   **Computational Cost:** A brief analysis of the computational overhead introduced by attention transfer would be valuable. Is the performance gain worth the additional computational cost?
*   **Visualization of Attention Maps:** While the paper mentions visualizing attention maps (e.g., Fig. 2), providing more representative visualizations of the transferred attention maps (student vs. teacher) in different experimental settings would further illustrate the effectiveness of the approach. A clearer visual comparison of attention maps with and without transfer would be helpful.
*    **Discussion of Limitations:**  A dedicated section briefly discussing the limitations of the approach (e.g., sensitivity to teacher network architecture, applicability to different tasks) would enhance the paper’s objectivity.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding the gradient-based attention transfer, why was it limited to the NIN architecture and CIFAR dataset? Are there specific challenges that prevent its application to more complex architectures or larger datasets?
*   You mention that KD struggled on ImageNet. Could you elaborate on the specific issues encountered? Was it a matter of hyperparameter tuning, optimization difficulties, or something else?
*   The paper touches on the regularization effect of horizontal flip invariance (Equation 6). Could you provide more insight into *why* this regularization is beneficial?
*   For the Fpsum and Fpmax attention maps, what is a good rule of thumb for selecting the value of 'p'? How does the choice of 'p' impact the types of features that are emphasized during transfer?
*   How does the proposed method perform when the teacher and student networks have significantly different architectures beyond simply differing in depth (e.g., ResNet vs. Vision Transformer)?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms. The experimental results are convincing, and the approach has the potential to be widely applicable. Addressing the suggestions above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
