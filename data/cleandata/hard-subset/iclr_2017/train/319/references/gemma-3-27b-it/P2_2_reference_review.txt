## ICLR Review

**Paper:** Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**Decision:** Reject

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention information from a teacher network to a student network to improve performance. The authors define attention based on activation and gradient maps and propose methods to transfer this information during training. They demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures, highlighting the benefits of attention-based transfer over full activation transfer and its compatibility with knowledge distillation. The core idea of leveraging attention maps for knowledge transfer is interesting and potentially valuable.

**Reasons for Rejection:**

The paper, while exploring a promising idea, suffers from a lack of clarity in presentation, insufficient experimental rigor and a failure to clearly demonstrate the *why* behind the observed improvements. The numerous experiments feel somewhat scattered and lack a cohesive narrative. The benefits over simpler baselines aren't always convincingly demonstrated, and the explanations for *why* attention transfer works remain largely speculative.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Clarity & Organization:** The paper feels quite dense and wanders. The introduction is lengthy and somewhat repetitive. The transition between sections feels abrupt, and the connection between different experiments isn't always clear. The numerous equations and parameters (β, p, etc.) are introduced without sufficient motivation or explanation of their impact.

2. **Experimental Rigor & Baselines:** While the authors perform a considerable number of experiments, the presentation of results is often unclear. Tables are frequently referenced without sufficient contextualization. More importantly, the comparison to strong baselines is often lacking.  For example, in the CIFAR experiments, the baseline network’s performance is not competitive with state-of-the-art results, making it difficult to assess the true impact of the proposed method. The justification for choices like not using batch normalization in the gradient-based transfer experiments is weak. The claim of being the “first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” is unsupported and feels overly strong; many knowledge distillation methods already exist for ImageNet.

3. **Insufficient Explanation of Mechanism:** The paper demonstrates *that* attention transfer works, but offers little insight into *why*.  The authors speculate that attention maps carry more informative signals than full activations, but this is not deeply explored. A more detailed analysis of the transferred attention maps – what specific features are being transferred, and how they influence the student network’s decision-making process – would significantly strengthen the paper.  Simply showing that attention maps become more similar isn't enough; showing *how* that similarity translates to improved performance is crucial.

4. **Overly Complex Implementation Details:** The appendix details a complex implementation with many hyperparameters. This complexity, coupled with the lack of ablation studies, makes it difficult to determine which aspects of the method are truly contributing to the improvements.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Streamline the presentation:** Focus on a smaller number of key experiments and present the results in a more concise and visually appealing manner.
*   **Strengthen baselines:** Compare against stronger and more commonly used baselines, particularly on CIFAR.
*   **Ablation studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to determine the impact of different components of the proposed method (e.g., different attention mapping functions, different loss weights, the use of knowledge distillation).
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide more detailed qualitative analysis of the transferred attention maps. Visualize the attention maps of both the teacher and student networks and highlight the differences and similarities.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Explore potential theoretical justifications for the observed improvements. Could information theory be used to quantify the amount of information transferred via attention maps?
*   **Focus the scope:** Consider narrowing the scope of the paper. For example, focusing solely on activation-based attention transfer, or on a specific dataset, might allow for a more in-depth and focused analysis.




**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why you chose to use l2 normalization for the attention maps? Does this choice have a significant impact on the results?
*   What is the computational overhead associated with the proposed attention transfer methods, particularly the gradient-based approach with the second backpropagation step?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β hyperparameter? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis?
*   Can you provide examples of the specific features that are being transferred from the teacher to the student network via the attention maps?
*   What efforts have been made to address the potential for overfitting when transferring attention information?



In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea, the lack of clarity, insufficient experimental rigor, and limited mechanistic explanation prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The authors would benefit from a significant revision addressing the concerns outlined above.
