## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**Reviewer:** AI Assistant

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention information from a "teacher" convolutional neural network to a "student" network, with the goal of improving the student's performance. The authors define attention as spatial maps derived from both activation and gradient information, exploring their utility as a knowledge transfer mechanism. They propose several methods for transferring this attention, and demonstrate significant performance improvements across a variety of datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet).  A key finding is that transferring attention maps is more effective than directly transferring full activations, and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation. The paper also explores gradient-based attention transfer and its regularization potential through symmetry constraints.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer, demonstrating consistent and significant improvements in performance.  The experimental validation is thorough, covering a good range of datasets and architectures. The insights regarding the superiority of attention transfer over full activation transfer are particularly compelling.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of knowledge transfer in CNNs and frames it through the lens of attention mechanisms, drawing parallels to human visual perception. This connection adds a strong motivational basis.
*   **Novelty:**  The idea of explicitly transferring attention maps, rather than weights or activations, as a form of knowledge distillation is a valuable contribution. The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based approaches, along with the symmetry regularization for gradient-based attention, demonstrates a creative exploration of the concept.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently show performance gains across diverse datasets and network architectures. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing different attention mapping functions, full activation transfer vs. attention transfer) provide convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The reported improvements on ImageNet are particularly noteworthy.
*   **Thorough Exploration:** The authors experiment with different techniques like Knowledge Distillation (KD) and various attention map functions, showcasing a strong grasp of the research space.
* **Implementation Details:** The authors make their code and models available, improving the reproducibility of their research.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions that attention transfer adds little computational cost when combined with KD, a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead of attention transfer *alone* would be beneficial.  How does the calculation of attention maps impact training time?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning. A sensitivity analysis of the β parameter (loss weighting) across different datasets and architectures would be helpful to understand its impact on performance.  
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more in-depth theoretical discussion of *why* attention transfer works could strengthen the paper. Is there a connection to information theory or representational similarity?
* **Clarity in Gradient-Based Experiments:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer is less detailed than the activation-based transfer section.  Elaborating on the challenges encountered with batch normalization and the limitations of the current setup would enhance the clarity.
* **ImageNet Details:** Provide more detail on the ImageNet experiments. What pre-trained weights were used?  What specific settings were used for KD (if attempted)? More details on the hyperparameters would be beneficial for reproducibility.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the observation that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer: Can you hypothesize *why* focusing on attention maps is more effective than directly transferring activations? Does this relate to the disentanglement of features or the removal of noise?
2.  For the gradient-based attention transfer, you mention challenges with batch normalization. Have you explored alternative approaches to address this, such as using synchronized batch normalization or estimating batch statistics differently during the second backpropagation step?
3.  In the ImageNet experiments, did you observe any differences in the quality of attention maps learned by the student network compared to the teacher network, beyond the performance improvement? (e.g., visual inspection of the attention maps).
4. Could you elaborate on the limitations of the proposed methods? Are there specific scenarios where attention transfer might *not* be effective, and if so, why?
5.  You mention plans to explore attention transfer in object detection and weakly-supervised localization. Could you briefly discuss any preliminary thoughts or anticipated challenges in applying this approach to these tasks?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms. The experimental results are compelling, and the insights gained are likely to be useful for future research. Addressing the minor feedback points will further strengthen the paper.
