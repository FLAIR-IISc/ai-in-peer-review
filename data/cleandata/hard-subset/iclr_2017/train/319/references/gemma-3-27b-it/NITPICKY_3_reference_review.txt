This paper investigates knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via the transfer of attention maps, exploring both activation-based and gradient-based approaches. The authors propose novel methods for defining and utilizing these attention maps to improve the performance of student networks, demonstrating improvements across various datasets and network architectures. The contributions include a framework for attention-based knowledge transfer, a comparative analysis of activation and gradient-based attention, and empirical evidence of performance gains.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting idea and a comprehensive experimental evaluation, the numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality and prevent a confident acceptance. The presentation lacks rigor in several areas, and the level of detail provided is insufficient to fully assess the validity of the claims.

The primary concerns stem from a lack of clarity and precision in the methodology, experimental setup, and analysis. Specifically:

1.  **Inconsistent Reporting & Hyperparameter Tuning:** The experimental sections mention a lack of extensive hyperparameter tuning, which is a significant weakness. While acknowledged, it's not adequately addressed; claiming improvements without demonstrating optimized performance is problematic. The varying levels of detail across experiments (e.g., ImageNet vs. CIFAR) make comparisons difficult. The decision to omit ZCA whitening on CIFAR, and the reasoning behind it, feels arbitrary and is not justified.
2.  **Ambiguous Implementation Details:** The description of the gradient-based attention transfer is vague. Specifically, the implementation of the "double backpropagation" technique lacks sufficient detail. The explanation concerning batch normalization within this process is insufficient and raises concerns about its correctness. The statement that the second backpropagation has “approximately the same cost with first backpropagation” requires justification and is potentially misleading.
3.  **Overstated Claims & Lack of Statistical Significance:** Claims of "significant improvements" are frequently made without accompanying statistical tests or confidence intervals. The results are often presented as single runs or medians of a small number of runs, which makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the findings.
4.  **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies, including inconsistent notation (e.g., use of Matlab notation within mathematical equations) and a slightly disorganized presentation of tables and figures. The figures themselves are often small and lack sufficient detail, making it difficult to interpret the results. The appendix is disorganized and lacks clear labeling.
5. **Redundancy & Verbosity:** The introduction is overly verbose and could be significantly condensed. The related work section is descriptive but lacks critical analysis.

These issues, while individually minor, collectively create a sense of sloppiness and undermine confidence in the research. 

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Clarify Implementation:** Provide a much more detailed and precise description of the gradient-based attention transfer implementation, including pseudo-code or a clear algorithmic outline. Explain how batch normalization is handled during the double backpropagation step.
*   **Statistical Rigor:** Include statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support all claims of improvement. Report confidence intervals.
*   **Hyperparameter Search:** Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter search for all experiments, or at least provide a clear justification for the chosen hyperparameters.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed approach (e.g., different attention mapping functions, knowledge distillation, symmetry constraints).
*   **Improved Presentation:** Revise the paper to eliminate formatting inconsistencies and improve the clarity and organization of tables, figures, and the appendix.
*   **Expand Related Work:**  Provide a more critical analysis of the related work, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and how the proposed method addresses these limitations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the double backpropagation technique is implemented in the presence of batch normalization layers?
*   What criteria were used to select the hyperparameters for each experiment? Why was a more extensive hyperparameter search not conducted?
*   What statistical tests were used to assess the significance of the observed improvements? Can you provide confidence intervals for the reported results?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the norm type (p) in the attention transfer loss function (Equation 2)?
*   What is the computational cost of the double backpropagation method compared to standard backpropagation? Is the claim of "approximately the same cost" accurate, considering the additional forward pass?
