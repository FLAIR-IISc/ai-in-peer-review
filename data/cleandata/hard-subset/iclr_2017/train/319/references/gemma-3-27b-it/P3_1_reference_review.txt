## ICLR 2024 Review: Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper explores the transfer of attention information from a teacher convolutional neural network to a student network as a means of improving performance. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps and propose methods for transferring this information. They demonstrate, through extensive experimentation on datasets like CIFAR, CUB, Scenes and ImageNet, that attention transfer can significantly boost the performance of student networks, often outperforming full activation transfer and even complementing knowledge distillation. The work is well-motivated by the observation that different networks, even with the same architecture, can focus on different parts of an image, implying valuable information in their attention patterns.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of knowledge transfer and attention mechanisms. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach across a variety of architectures and datasets.  The novelty of using attention maps specifically for transfer, and the extensive empirical validation, make this a valuable addition to the literature.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation.  The consistent improvements observed across different datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet) lend credibility to the claims. The ablation studies comparing different attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) and transfer methods (attention vs. full activation) are insightful.
* **Novelty and Motivation:** The concept of directly transferring attention maps, rather than just activations or predictions, is novel.  The motivation drawing from cognitive science (different observers, different attention) is compelling and provides a good grounding for the work.  The observation that attention maps carry more useful information than full activations is a key finding.
* **Thorough Exploration:** The authors explore both activation-based and gradient-based attention transfer, providing a comparative analysis. They also consider the combination of attention transfer with knowledge distillation. The inclusion of horizontal flip invariance in gradient-based transfer is an interesting addition, showcasing a good understanding of potential regularization techniques.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures and tables are clear and effectively illustrate the results.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions that attention transfer adds "very little computational cost," a more quantitative analysis of the overhead would be valuable.  Specifically, how much does calculating and transferring the attention maps increase training time?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the β parameter (controlling the weight of the attention transfer loss) could be explored in more detail. How sensitive are the results to different values of β? A sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Qualitative Analysis:**  While quantitative results are strong, a deeper qualitative analysis of the learned attention maps would be insightful.  Visualizing the attention maps of the teacher and student networks side-by-side, perhaps with overlaid segmentation of important objects, could provide a better understanding of *what* the student network is learning to attend to.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more theoretical justification for why attention transfer works could strengthen the paper. Is there a connection to information theory or representational similarity?
* **ImageNet Results:**  The ImageNet results are exciting, but it's noted that the hyperparameters weren't fully tuned.  Including a more thorough evaluation on ImageNet, even with increased computational cost, would significantly enhance the impact of the paper.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding gradient-based attention:** You mention challenges with batch normalization during second-order backpropagation. Have you considered any approximations or alternative techniques to address this issue?
* **Regarding attention map normalization:**  You used L2 normalization of attention maps.  Did you experiment with other normalization techniques (e.g., L1 normalization, softmax) and if so, what were the results?
* **Regarding hyperparameter selection:** You state β is set to 10^3 / (number of elements in attention map and batch size). What is the intuition behind this choice, and how robust is this rule of thumb across different architectures and datasets?
* **Regarding generalization:** Do you anticipate that this attention transfer technique will generalize to other tasks beyond image classification, such as object detection or semantic segmentation? If so, what modifications might be necessary?
* **Could you provide the code used for the experiments?** This would facilitate reproducibility and further research in this area.



Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable research contribution. The authors have presented a compelling case for the effectiveness of attention transfer as a knowledge transfer technique. Addressing the minor feedback points outlined above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its impact on the field.
