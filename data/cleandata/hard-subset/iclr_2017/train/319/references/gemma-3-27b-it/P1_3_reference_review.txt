## ICLR 2024 Review: Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention information from a "teacher" convolutional neural network to a "student" network to improve performance. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps and propose methods for transferring this attention. They demonstrate experimentally that their approach yields significant improvements across various datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and network architectures (NIN, ResNet). They further show that activation-based attention transfer is more effective than simply transferring activations and can be combined with knowledge distillation. The work offers a novel perspective on knowledge transfer, framing it through the lens of attention mechanisms.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a clear idea, solid experimental results, and a well-structured approach. The findings suggest a promising direction for knowledge transfer, and the practical improvements observed are noteworthy. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:**  The idea of explicitly transferring attention maps as a form of knowledge transfer is relatively novel and provides a valuable contribution to the field. It moves beyond simply transferring weights or activations, focusing on *where* the network is looking.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides consistent improvements across a diverse range of datasets and architectures. The results on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and especially the initial ImageNet results are encouraging. The ablation studies comparing different attention mapping functions and transfer methods (with and without KD) strengthen the claims.
* **Clear Methodology:** The definitions of activation-based and gradient-based attention are clearly articulated, and the proposed transfer methods are well-defined. The explanation of the loss functions and implementation details is sufficient for reproducibility.
* **Thorough Investigation:** The authors explore different attention mapping functions, transfer methods, and combinations with knowledge distillation, demonstrating a comprehensive investigation of the proposed approach.
* **Well-placed in Literature:** The related work section is well-structured and provides context for the proposed approach, referencing key papers in attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **ImageNet Results Detail:**  While the ImageNet results are promising, more detail is needed. Specify the exact architectures used for teacher and student networks, the training schedule (epochs, learning rate), and a more comprehensive comparison with other knowledge distillation or transfer learning methods on ImageNet.  A clear table with Top-1 and Top-5 accuracy would be helpful.
* **Gradient-Based Attention Exploration:** The gradient-based attention experiments are limited to NIN on CIFAR. Expanding these experiments to more complex architectures and datasets would strengthen the findings.  The explanation of the second backpropagation step could also be more detailed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (particularly β in the loss function) would be valuable. Are the results robust to changes in β, or does it require careful tuning for each dataset and architecture?
* **Visualization Enhancement:** While some visualizations are provided, more examples of attention maps for different layers and datasets could further illustrate the effectiveness of the transfer.  Consider overlaying attention maps onto the input images to visually demonstrate the areas the networks are focusing on.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** A dedicated section discussing the limitations of the approach would be beneficial. For example, the computational cost of calculating gradient-based attention or the potential for negative transfer if the teacher network is poorly trained.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the ImageNet experiments: Can you provide a detailed table with Top-1 and Top-5 accuracies, along with the specific hyperparameters (learning rate, weight decay, etc.) used for both teacher and student networks? How does your method compare to other state-of-the-art knowledge distillation techniques on ImageNet?
2.  Concerning the gradient-based attention transfer: The second backpropagation step seems complex. Could you elaborate on the implementation details and provide an estimate of the computational overhead compared to standard backpropagation?
3.  For the activation-based attention transfer: Have you explored using different normalization techniques for the attention maps besides L2 normalization? Did you observe any significant differences in performance?
4.  The paper mentions potential negative transfer. Have you observed any instances of this during your experiments, and if so, what factors seemed to contribute to it?
5.  How does the choice of layer to transfer attention from (e.g. early layers vs. later layers) affect the performance of the student network? Did you experiment with transferring attention from multiple layers simultaneously?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling idea and solid experimental validation. Addressing the suggestions above will further strengthen the work and increase its impact on the field.
