## ICLR 2024 Review: Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention information from a “teacher” convolutional neural network to a “student” network, with the goal of improving the student's performance. The authors define attention as spatial maps derived from both activation patterns and gradients, exploring how these maps vary across datasets and architectures. They propose several methods for transferring this attention, demonstrating improvements across a variety of tasks and network structures, including residual and non-residual networks.  The core contributions lie in framing attention as a knowledge transfer mechanism, systematically studying different attention map definitions, and showcasing the effectiveness of attention transfer, especially when combined with knowledge distillation.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents a reasonably well-explored idea with some promising empirical results, the presentation is often unclear, lacks sufficient novelty beyond incremental exploration, and lacks a convincing demonstration of the *why* behind the observed improvements. The experimental setup feels somewhat fragmented and lacks a strong, focused analysis. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of knowledge transfer via attention isn’t entirely novel. Previous work in knowledge distillation and hint-based learning already explore transferring information between networks.  The paper doesn’t sufficiently distinguish itself from these lines of work, primarily presenting an exploration of different attention map definitions within this established framework. While the findings regarding which attention map types work best are interesting, they don't represent a fundamental breakthrough.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper suffers from a somewhat disorganized presentation. The transitions between sections feel abrupt, and the motivation for certain experimental choices is not always clear.  The writing style is dense and frequently refers to figure numbers without sufficient explanation within the text.  For example, Fig. 1 is referenced but doesn’t clearly convey the attention transfer mechanism to a reader unfamiliar with the concept.
* **Insufficient Analysis:** The experimental results demonstrate performance improvements, but the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *why* attention transfer works. Is it simply regularization? Is it guiding the student network towards more informative features?  The discussion focuses primarily on *that* it works, rather than *how* or *why*.  The paper claims the attention maps contain valuable information, but doesn’t convincingly demonstrate what specifically makes this information valuable.
* **Fragmented Experimental Setup:**  The experiments jump between different datasets, architectures, and transfer methods without a unifying narrative. While breadth is appreciated, it hinders a focused and in-depth understanding of the proposed methods.  The ImageNet results, in particular, are presented as preliminary and lack sufficient detail.  The lack of consistent hyperparameter tuning across experiments also casts doubt on the comparability of the results.
* **Gradient-based Transfer Issues:** The discussion around gradient-based attention transfer highlights significant implementation challenges (double backpropagation, batch norm issues) and acknowledges limited success. This section feels underdeveloped and lacks a clear justification for its inclusion.

**4. Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

* **Strengthen the Motivation & Related Work:**  Clearly articulate how this work differs from and builds upon existing knowledge distillation and hint-based learning techniques.  A more detailed discussion of the psychological motivations behind attention (as alluded to in the introduction) could strengthen the argument.
* **Improve Clarity & Organization:** Re-structure the paper for better flow.  Use more descriptive figure captions and thoroughly explain all experimental setups.  Simplify the writing style and remove unnecessary jargon.
* **In-depth Analysis:** Conduct ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components (e.g., activation map types, transfer losses).  Visualize and analyze the attention maps to gain insights into what the student network is learning from the teacher.  Quantify the similarity between teacher and student attention maps.
* **Focus the Experimental Evaluation:**  Prioritize a smaller set of experiments with more thorough analysis.  Focus on a few key architectures and datasets to draw more definitive conclusions. Perform a hyperparameter search to ensure fair comparisons.
* **Address Gradient-based Limitations:** Either provide a more compelling justification for the inclusion of gradient-based transfer or remove it. If kept, address the implementation challenges more thoroughly and explore alternative approaches.
* **Explore Transfer to Different Tasks:** Consider expanding the experimental evaluation to tasks beyond image classification, such as object detection or segmentation.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* You mention that attention transfer “adds very little computational cost.” Can you quantify this cost relative to training without attention transfer?
*  What is the rationale behind using the l2 norm for normalizing attention maps? Have you experimented with other normalization techniques (e.g., l1 norm)?
*  You observed that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer. Can you speculate on why this is the case? Is it because attention maps filter out irrelevant information?
*  The ImageNet results are presented as preliminary. What were the primary challenges in achieving stable training with attention transfer on this dataset?
*  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter in the loss function? Have you explored methods for adaptively adjusting β during training?



In conclusion, while the paper tackles an interesting research question, the lack of novelty, clarity, and in-depth analysis prevents me from recommending acceptance at this time. A significant revision addressing the points outlined above would be necessary to improve the paper’s contribution and impact.
