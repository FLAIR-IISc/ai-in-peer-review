This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps. The authors propose utilizing both activation-based and gradient-based attention as a mechanism to improve the performance of a student network guided by a teacher network. They demonstrate improvements across various datasets and architectures, showing that attention-based transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a reasonable exploration of attention transfer, the experimental validation feels incomplete and lacks sufficient rigor. Numerous minor methodological inconsistencies and a lack of thorough analysis weaken the claims of significant improvement. The presentation is also somewhat verbose and lacks clarity in certain areas.

The primary concerns stem from the experimental section. The CIFAR experiments, while showing improvements, are marred by inconsistent experimental setups (e.g., batch normalization being turned off for gradient-based transfer, differing data augmentation strategies) making it difficult to directly compare methods. The justification for these variations isn’t sufficiently provided. Furthermore, the claim of a "significant" improvement on CIFAR is undermined by the relatively small absolute gains reported.  The ImageNet results are even less convincing. The authors admit to limited hyperparameter tuning and a lack of exploration of knowledge distillation, which raises questions about the true effectiveness of their approach on a more challenging dataset.  The statement that this is "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is a strong claim and not substantiated. Several papers already demonstrate successful knowledge distillation on ImageNet.

Several specific points further contribute to the rejection:

*   **Ambiguous Loss Function:** Equation (2) defines the loss function but lacks clarity on the weighting factor β. The authors mention it’s set to 10<sup>3</sup> divided by the number of elements, but this feels arbitrary and lacks justification. How sensitive are the results to this value?
*   **Inconsistent Reporting:** Tables 1 and 2 report results in slightly different formats, making comparisons harder.  Table 3 presents results for a different network architecture and setup, making it difficult to generalize the findings.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:**  Many results are presented for a single run (e.g., the gradient-based transfer experiments). Without error bars or multiple runs, it's impossible to assess the statistical significance of the reported improvements.
*   **Figure Quality:** Some figures (e.g. Fig. 6) are low resolution and difficult to interpret. The accompanying text doesn’t clearly explain the observed visual differences in attention maps.
*   **Terminology:** The repeated use of phrases like “presumably” and “we speculate” weakens the confidence in the conclusions drawn.
*   **Repetitive Introduction:** The introduction is overly lengthy and could be more concise. The connection between human attention and the proposed methods isn't sufficiently elaborated.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

*   **Standardize Experimental Setup:** Ensure a consistent experimental setup across all experiments, particularly concerning data augmentation and network configurations.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Conduct thorough hyperparameter tuning for all methods and datasets.
*   **Statistical Analysis:** Report results with error bars (standard deviation or confidence intervals) based on multiple runs.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components of the proposed approach (e.g., different attention mapping functions, knowledge distillation).
*   **Compare to Strong Baselines:** Compare the proposed method to stronger baselines, including state-of-the-art knowledge distillation techniques.
*   **Clarify the Novelty:** Precisely define the novelty of the contribution, avoiding overstatements and properly citing related work.
*   **Improve Figure Quality:** Provide high-resolution figures with clear captions and explanations.

Questions for the authors:

*   What is the theoretical justification for choosing β as 10<sup>3</sup> divided by the number of elements in the attention map?
*   Why was batch normalization turned off for the gradient-based transfer experiments on CIFAR?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with gradient-based attention transfer, particularly the second backpropagation step?
*   Have you investigated the performance of your approach on datasets beyond image classification, such as object detection or semantic segmentation?
*   Can you provide a more comprehensive comparison to existing knowledge distillation methods on ImageNet?
