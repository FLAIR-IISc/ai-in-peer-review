This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via the transfer of attention maps – specifically, activation-based and gradient-based maps – aiming to improve the performance of student networks. The authors propose several methods for transferring this attention information and demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures. They further show that activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.

**Reject.** While the idea of transferring attention is intuitively plausible, the paper lacks the rigorous validation and comparative analysis necessary to convince me of its significance. The improvements reported, particularly on larger datasets, appear marginal given the complexity introduced, and the experimental setup doesn’t sufficiently isolate the contribution of attention transfer from other factors. Furthermore, the lack of a clear, compelling advantage over established knowledge distillation techniques raises serious concerns.

The primary weakness lies in the insufficient benchmarking against strong baselines. The comparisons to "full activation transfer" are helpful, but the paper doesn't adequately compare against more sophisticated knowledge distillation methods or other transfer learning approaches. Presenting results *only* with improvements – and not showing cases where attention transfer fails to provide an advantage – creates a biased view. The ImageNet results, presented as a significant achievement, are very modest (1.1% top-1 improvement) and require a much more thorough evaluation. The claim of being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is unsubstantiated and likely inaccurate, given the extensive literature on knowledge distillation and transfer learning. 

The experimental details are also somewhat lacking. While the authors mention implementation details, a clearer description of hyperparameters, training procedures, and computational resources used would improve reproducibility and allow for a more accurate assessment of the results. The discussion of the double backpropagation method for gradient-based attention transfer feels underdeveloped and the reasoning for omitting random crops with batch normalization is not clear. The reliance on single-run experiments for gradient-based attention transfer on CIFAR raises concerns about the reliability of the findings. 

Finally, the paper suffers from a degree of redundancy and imprecise language. The exploration of different activation mapping functions feels somewhat exhaustive without a clear justification for why these specific functions were chosen, and the conclusions drawn from these experiments lack depth.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include comparisons against more advanced knowledge distillation techniques (e.g., variants employing different temperature schedules, data augmentation strategies, or intermediate feature matching) and other transfer learning methods (e.g., fine-tuning, feature extraction).
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of attention transfer. For example, systematically remove layers from the attention transfer process to determine which layers provide the greatest benefit.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to random chance. Report results as means and standard deviations across multiple runs.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, β, temperature).
*   **Error Analysis:** Perform an error analysis to understand *why* attention transfer improves performance. Are there specific types of images or classes where the improvement is most pronounced?
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Offer a more theoretical justification for why attention transfer should work. Is there a connection to information theory, representation learning, or other relevant concepts?
*   **Clarity and Conciseness:**  Streamline the writing and remove redundant phrases. Clearly articulate the novelty of the proposed approach.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Why were the specific activation mapping functions chosen, and what is the theoretical basis for their selection?
*   Why does the ImageNet result show no benefit from KD when it helps on CIFAR?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the challenges encountered when implementing the double backpropagation method for gradient-based attention transfer, and why batch normalization was excluded in that experiment?
*   What is the computational overhead of attention transfer compared to standard knowledge distillation?
*   Could you clarify the claim of being the first to successfully apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet? What existing work is this claim based upon?
