## ICLR 2024 Review: Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the transfer of attention information from a teacher convolutional neural network (CNN) to a student network to improve performance. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps and propose methods to transfer this information.  They demonstrate through experiments on CIFAR and ImageNet datasets that transferring attention, particularly activation-based attention, leads to significant performance improvements and can even outperform full activation transfer or knowledge distillation alone. The paper posits that attention maps carry crucial information beyond that contained in activations, providing a more targeted knowledge transfer mechanism.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to knowledge transfer, supported by compelling empirical results across multiple datasets and network architectures. The central claim – that attention maps are a valuable medium for transferring knowledge – is convincingly demonstrated. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of explicitly transferring *attention* rather than full activations or simply distilling soft targets is a valuable contribution.  The authors clearly articulate the motivation rooted in cognitive science (human attention) and the limitations of existing knowledge transfer methods. Framing attention as a potentially more efficient and informative transfer mechanism is insightful.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on CIFAR are particularly strong, consistently showing improvements across different architectures and attention map definitions.  The finding that activation-based attention outperforms full activation transfer is particularly noteworthy and well-supported. The positive, though less conclusive, results on ImageNet are also encouraging.
* **Thorough Exploration:** The authors explore various attention map definitions (sum, max, power) and loss functions, demonstrating a careful consideration of different design choices. The inclusion of gradient-based attention transfer, alongside activation-based approaches, shows breadth of investigation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The figures effectively illustrate the attention maps and the transfer process.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **ImageNet Results – Deeper Dive:** While the ImageNet results are positive, the paper acknowledges limited hyperparameter tuning.  A more thorough exploration of hyperparameters (particularly β) and potentially the architecture of the student network could strengthen these findings.  Discussing the challenges of applying the gradient-based method to ImageNet networks would also be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** A brief discussion of the computational overhead introduced by attention transfer would be valuable.  Is it significantly more expensive than other knowledge distillation techniques?
* **Ablation Studies on Beta:** While the paper mentions that β is typically around 0.1, a more systematic ablation study exploring the sensitivity of performance to different values of β across different datasets and architectures would be insightful.
* **Qualitative Analysis of Attention Maps:**  Beyond the visual examples in the appendix, providing a more systematic qualitative analysis comparing the attention maps of the teacher, student (with and without attention transfer) would help solidify the claim that the transfer is indeed leading to similar attentional focus.
* **Connection to Existing Literature on Interpretability:** The paper touches upon visualizing attention. A stronger connection to the broader literature on CNN interpretability (e.g., Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients) and discussion of how this work relates to these techniques would be helpful.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **ImageNet Tuning:**  What specific challenges did you encounter when attempting to tune hyperparameters for the ImageNet experiments?  Could you provide more detail on the limitations that prevented a more extensive hyperparameter search?
2. **Gradient-Based Attention & Batch Norm:**  As mentioned in the paper, the interaction between gradient-based attention transfer and batch normalization is unclear. Have you considered alternative approaches to address this issue (e.g., freezing batch norm statistics during the second backpropagation)?
3. **Beta Sensitivity:**  What is the range of β values you experimented with, and how did performance change across this range on the CIFAR datasets?
4. **Attention Diversity:** Did you observe any cases where the student network developed *different* but still effective attention maps compared to the teacher? If so, what might explain this divergence?
5. **Scalability:**  How does the computational cost of attention transfer scale with the size of the input image and the depth of the network? Are there strategies for mitigating the cost in resource-constrained environments?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of knowledge transfer. Addressing the feedback above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
