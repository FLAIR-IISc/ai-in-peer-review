This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps. The authors propose defining attention using both activation-based and gradient-based methods and demonstrate that transferring this attention information from a teacher network to a student network can significantly improve performance across various datasets and network architectures. The core idea is compelling: leveraging where a well-performing network *looks* to guide the learning of a smaller network.

**Decision: Reject**

While the idea is promising and the experiments are extensive, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and justification for several key design choices and the reported improvements feel incremental given the complexity of the proposed methods. The presentation is also somewhat convoluted, making it difficult to fully grasp the contributions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Baseline Comparison for Attention Transfer:** The paper frequently compares against "no attention transfer," but lacks a strong baseline for knowledge transfer *without* attention. Existing knowledge distillation techniques (beyond a brief mention) aren't rigorously compared. It’s unclear whether the performance gains are specifically due to the proposed attention transfer mechanism or simply a result of a well-implemented knowledge distillation pipeline. The authors mention FitNets but don’t present a direct comparison.  A controlled experiment showing the benefit of attention transfer *over* standard distillation would strengthen the argument considerably.

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** The authors acknowledge limited hyperparameter tuning, particularly for ImageNet.  The sensitivity of the results to the β parameter (loss weighting) and the different attention mapping functions (Fsum, F2sum, etc.) isn’t adequately addressed. The choice of which function to use seems somewhat arbitrary.  Without a proper ablation study, it's difficult to ascertain which components contribute most to the observed improvements. The report of 1.1% improvement on ImageNet, without extensive tuning, feels less convincing.

3. **Gradient-Based Attention Complexity & Results:** The gradient-based attention transfer section introduces significant complexity with double backpropagation and symmetry constraints. However, the reported results aren’t dramatically better than simpler methods and the section feels less fully developed than the activation-based approach. The comment about batch normalization issues introduces uncertainty and suggests the results might not be generalizable.

4. **Presentation and Clarity:** The paper is dense and reads somewhat repetitively. The descriptions of the different attention mapping functions are difficult to follow without carefully examining the equations. While the figures help, more concise explanations and a clearer narrative flow would significantly improve readability.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies.  Specifically, compare different attention mapping functions, β values, and the impact of adding attention transfer to different knowledge distillation techniques.
* **Stronger Baselines:** Include a rigorous comparison against state-of-the-art knowledge distillation methods (e.g., variants of Hinton et al., FitNets) as baselines.
* **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance for all experimental results.
* **Visualization Improvements:** Enhance the visualizations of the attention maps. Consider using more intuitive color schemes and overlays to clearly show the correspondence between attention maps and image features.  Show more qualitative examples.
* **Simplify Presentation:** Restructure the paper for better clarity. Break down complex concepts into smaller, more digestible parts.  Reduce redundancy.
* **Justification of Design Choices:** Provide a more detailed rationale for the design choices, such as the specific forms of the attention mapping functions and the normalization techniques used.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:**  Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer methods.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  Can you provide a direct comparison of your attention transfer method against standard knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) with comparable hyperparameter tuning?
*  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter? Have you performed a grid search to identify optimal values for different datasets and architectures?
*  Why were the gradient-based methods less successful, and do you believe the batch normalization issue is a fundamental limitation?
*  What is the computational cost of performing attention transfer compared to training a student network from scratch or using standard knowledge distillation?
*  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the rationale behind the specific attention mapping functions you explored?  What properties of the image or network representations motivated these choices?
