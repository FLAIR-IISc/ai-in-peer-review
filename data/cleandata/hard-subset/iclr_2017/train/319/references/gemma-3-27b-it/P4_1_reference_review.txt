This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps. The authors define attention as activation-based or gradient-based spatial maps and propose methods to transfer this attention from a teacher network to a student network to improve performance. They demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures, showing that attention-based transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of rigor and clarity in the experimental evaluation. While the paper demonstrates some improvements, the experimental setup feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility or a convincing assessment of the method’s effectiveness. The second reason for rejection is the lack of novelty of the individual components. The ideas presented are largely combinations of existing techniques (knowledge distillation, attention mechanisms, gradient-based saliency) without a substantial new theoretical contribution.

The paper's claims are supported by experimental results, but the extent of support is weak. The results presented are often qualitative (e.g., visualization of attention maps) or lack statistical significance. For instance, the CIFAR experiments show improvements, but the table reporting them lacks standard deviations or confidence intervals. The ImageNet results are even less convincing, as the authors admit to not fully tuning hyperparameters. The comparison to existing methods is limited; the authors claim activation-based transfer outperforms full activation transfer, but the experimental details and statistical significance of this claim are not sufficiently established. The ablation studies performed (e.g., varying attention mapping functions) are helpful, but lack a clear rationale for the specific choices made and their potential impact. Furthermore, the benefit of symmetry norm in gradient-based attention is reported on a single run experiment, making it difficult to draw conclusions.

Additional feedback:

*   **Reproducibility:** The experimental setup needs to be much more detailed. Specify all hyperparameters used, training procedures, and data augmentation techniques. The code should be released to allow for verification of the results.
*   **Statistical Significance:**  Report statistical significance for all comparisons. Use appropriate statistical tests and provide confidence intervals.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Provide a more comprehensive analysis of the different attention mapping functions and transfer loss weights.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are interesting, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of why attention transfer works. Provide more insight into the information contained within the attention maps and how it facilitates knowledge transfer.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** More thoroughly connect the proposed approach to the existing literature on knowledge distillation, attention mechanisms, and transfer learning. Discuss the limitations of the proposed method compared to other approaches.
*   **Clarity:** The presentation could be improved. Some sections are dense and difficult to follow. Clearer explanations of the notation and technical details would be helpful. Figures could be improved with better labeling and clearer visualization.
*   **Motivation:** Expand on the motivation for transferring attention specifically. What benefits does transferring attention offer over other knowledge transfer techniques like feature-based transfer or output-based distillation?

Questions for the authors:

*   What is the computational overhead of incorporating the attention transfer loss during training?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β parameter in the attention transfer loss? Was a systematic hyperparameter search performed?
*   Why does KD seem to perform poorly on ImageNet in your experiments?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why transferring attention outperforms transferring full activations?
*   How do the attention maps generated by different layers of the network contribute to the overall performance improvement?
*   What is the impact of different normalization techniques (e.g., L1 vs. L2) on the attention maps and the transfer process?
*   Could you provide a more rigorous comparison against existing knowledge distillation methods (e.g., FitNets, DKN) on ImageNet?
