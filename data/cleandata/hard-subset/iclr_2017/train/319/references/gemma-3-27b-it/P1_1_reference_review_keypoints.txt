- Paper proposes transferring activation- and gradient-based spatial attention maps from teacher to student CNNs, showing performance gains across CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, and ImageNet.  
- Key finding: activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and complements knowledge distillation in many cases.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Novelty: Attention-map transfer is an interesting, relatively unexplored approach linked to human attention theory.  
- Methodology: Definitions and loss for activation- and gradient-based attention transfer are clear and reasonable.  
- Empirical evaluation: Comprehensive experiments across datasets (CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet) and architectures (NIN, ResNet) with encouraging ImageNet results.  
- Ablations: Useful comparisons of attention mapping functions (Fsum, Fpssum, Fpmax) and against full activation transfer.  
- Presentation: Paper is generally well-written and structured.  
- Gradient-based attention: Section feels underdeveloped; needs more implementation detail, handling of batch-norm during second backprop, and deeper analysis.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Provide analysis of sensitivity to the β weight in the attention-transfer loss.  
- Computational cost: Quantify the overhead introduced by attention transfer, even when combined with KD.  
- Visualization: Include more comparative visualizations of teacher vs student attention maps.  
- Theoretical justification: Consider exploring links between attention transfer and generalization theory.  
- Questions for authors: clarify batch-norm/backprop issues and solutions; report β tuning and sensitivity; quantify computational overhead; measure teacher-student attention similarity (e.g., cosine or IoU); explain why KD didn’t help on ImageNet and whether KD temperatures/weights were varied.
