This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps, proposing both activation-based and gradient-based approaches. The core idea – leveraging *where* a network is looking as a means of improving another’s performance – is compelling and taps into the cognitive science notion of attention guiding perception. The authors demonstrate improvements across several datasets and architectures, showing attention transfer can outperform full activation transfer and be effectively combined with knowledge distillation.

**Reject.** While the idea is interesting and the initial results promising, the paper feels exploratory and lacks the depth of analysis required for acceptance at ICLR. The improvements reported are often modest, and the justification for certain design choices feels lacking. The experimental setup, while broad, lacks thorough ablation studies and a clear understanding of *why* attention transfer works.

Here’s a more detailed breakdown:

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of attention as a transfer mechanism is a potentially valuable direction. It’s a clear departure from typical knowledge distillation approaches.
*   **Broad Exploration:** The authors test a variety of attention map definitions (sum, max, powers), transfer methods (activation, gradient), and architectures (ResNet, NIN).
*   **Positive Results:**  Improvements are consistently observed, suggesting the approach has merit.
*   **Connection to Cognitive Science:** The grounding in human attention mechanisms is a nice touch and provides a motivating context.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest Improvements:** The reported gains, particularly on ImageNet, aren’t overwhelmingly significant.  A 1.1% top-1 accuracy improvement feels incremental given the complexity introduced.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit greatly from a more systematic ablation of different components.  For instance, what’s the contribution of each loss term (attention transfer vs. knowledge distillation)? What's the impact of different normalization schemes on gradient-based transfer? Why are certain attention mapping functions (Fsum vs. Fmax) more effective?
*   **Insufficient Justification:** The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., β) feels somewhat arbitrary. There’s little discussion of how these parameters were selected or their sensitivity.  The reasoning behind using l2 normalization is stated, but lacks a strong justification.
*   **Gradient-based Transfer Concerns:** The section on gradient-based transfer feels less developed and more computationally complex. The authors acknowledge challenges with batch normalization, which raises questions about the robustness of this method.  The limited results (only on NIN without batch norm) are concerning.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** There’s a lack of qualitative analysis. Showing examples of attention maps from the teacher and student networks, and highlighting differences, would strengthen the paper. Visualizing where the student *learns* to attend due to the transfer would be compelling.
*   **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The comparison to FitNets is somewhat superficial.  A more in-depth comparison, including a discussion of the theoretical differences and potential advantages of the proposed approach, would be valuable.

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

*   **Deepen the Analysis:** Focus on understanding *why* attention transfer works. Is it simply regularizing the student network, or is it truly transferring valuable representational information?
*   **Expand Ablation Studies:** Systematically evaluate the impact of different components, hyperparameters, and architectural choices.
*   **Improve Visualization:**  Include more informative visualizations of attention maps and error cases.
*   **Strengthen the Gradient-based Section:** Either provide more compelling results for gradient-based transfer or acknowledge its limitations and focus on activation-based methods.
*   **Discuss Computational Cost:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead associated with attention transfer.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific insights did you gain from visualizing the attention maps that led you to believe attention transfer is beneficial?
*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the β parameter in Equation 2, and discuss its sensitivity?
*   Why do you observe better results with activation-based transfer compared to full activation transfer? What information is lost when transferring full activations?
*   Regarding gradient-based transfer, what steps would be required to address the challenges with batch normalization? Do you believe this method has the potential to be competitive with activation-based transfer?
*   Have you explored other norms besides the l2 norm for measuring the distance between attention maps? If so, what were the results?
