This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention mechanisms, proposing to leverage both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps to improve student network performance. The authors demonstrate improvements across various datasets and architectures, suggesting a novel and potentially impactful approach to network training and compression.

**Reject.** While the idea of transferring attention is interesting, the paper lacks the depth of analysis and rigorous validation needed for acceptance at ICLR. The experimental results, while generally positive, are presented without sufficient detail and comparative analysis to convincingly demonstrate the significance of the proposed method beyond a proof-of-concept. The paper feels exploratory rather than conclusive.

The primary concern lies in the lack of a compelling argument for *why* attention transfer works as well as it does. The authors observe correlations between attention maps and performance, but don’t delve into the underlying mechanisms. Is it simply regularization? A more effective initialization strategy? A richer representation of network behavior? Without addressing this, the work remains largely empirical. 

Furthermore, the experimental section feels fragmented. The CIFAR results are presented with more detail than the ImageNet experiments, making direct comparison difficult. The choice of hyperparameters (especially β) appears somewhat arbitrary, and sensitivity to these parameters isn’t explored.  The claim of being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" feels overstated, as other KD methods exist, even if they don't show comparable results - a more nuanced discussion of existing literature is required. The justification for the chosen attention map functions (sum, max) is weak. Why these specific statistics?  A more systematic evaluation of different attention map definitions would strengthen the work. The gradient-based results are particularly sparse and the explanation for the difficulties encountered with batch normalization is insufficient.

Finally, while the authors acknowledge the potential for deeper investigation (e.g., applying this to object detection), the current paper doesn’t fully capitalize on the novelty of the idea.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** A thorough ablation study is crucial. How does performance change with different layers used for attention transfer? What is the impact of varying the β parameter? How does the choice of attention map function (Fsum, Fpsom, Fpmax) affect results?
*   **Comparison to Strong Baselines:**  More rigorous comparison to existing knowledge distillation techniques, especially on ImageNet, is necessary. Include more recent KD methods, and provide a fair comparison.
*   **Visualization:**  While some attention map visualizations are provided, more examples, particularly on the ImageNet dataset, would be helpful. Show a clear qualitative comparison of attention maps between teacher and student networks.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Even a limited theoretical exploration of *why* attention transfer might work would add significant value. Could it be framed as a form of regularization or a better initialization strategy?
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer process.
*   **Clarify notation**: The equations could benefit from better explanations, especially regarding the normalization step. What is the purpose of using l2 normalization?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the intuition behind using the specific statistics (sum, max) to define the attention maps? Have you experimented with other statistics, and if so, what were the results?
*   Why do you believe that attention transfer is more effective than directly transferring full activations, as observed in your CIFAR experiments?
*   Could you elaborate on the challenges you faced when applying Knowledge Distillation to ImageNet and why you believe your approach outperforms it?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of your results to the β parameter, and explain how you chose its value?
*   How does the performance of your approach scale with the size of the datasets and the complexity of the network architectures?
