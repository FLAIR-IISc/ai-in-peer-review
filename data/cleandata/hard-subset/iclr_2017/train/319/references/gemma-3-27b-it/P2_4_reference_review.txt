## ICLR Review

**Paper:** Attention Transfer for Convolutional Neural Networks

**Overall Decision:** **Reject**

**Summary:** This paper investigates the transfer of attention information from a teacher convolutional neural network to a student network to improve performance. The authors define attention using both activation-based and gradient-based spatial maps and propose methods for transferring this information during training. They demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures, claiming that attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and can be combined with knowledge distillation.

**Key Reasons for Rejection:**

* **Lack of Novelty & Incremental Contribution:** While the ideas presented are interesting, the paper largely feels like an exploration of established concepts (attention mechanisms, knowledge distillation) applied in a relatively straightforward manner. The novelty of *how* attention is transferred feels limited, and the empirical gains, while present, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance given the existing body of work.
* **Insufficient Rigor & Ablation:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient rigor. Hyperparameter tuning seems minimal, and crucial ablations are missing.  For instance, the impact of different loss weights for attention transfer vs. the primary task loss isn't thoroughly explored.  The claims about the superiority of activation-based transfer over full activation transfer are presented without a convincing justification beyond the empirical results (which are themselves not overwhelmingly conclusive).



**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Motivation & Related Work:** The introduction effectively sets the stage and links attention mechanisms in CNNs to cognitive science. The related work section is adequate but doesn’t critically engage with recent advances in attention visualization or knowledge transfer. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context, but it doesn’t demonstrate a strong need for this specific approach.
2. **Technical Approach:** The definitions of activation-based and gradient-based attention maps are reasonable.  The idea of using these maps as a transfer mechanism is intuitive.  However, the loss function (Equation 2) feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a clear theoretical justification. The l2 normalization of attention maps is mentioned as important, but the rationale isn’t fully explained.  The gradient-based transfer section mentions double backpropagation but the discussion on handling batch normalization is a significant weakness.
3. **Experiments & Results:** 
    * **CIFAR Experiments:** The results on CIFAR are modest.  While improvements are reported, they aren't dramatic. The comparisons to FitNets-style transfer are interesting, but the authors acknowledge the limitations of the network architecture used (NIN without batch norm).  The claim that attention transfer is superior to full activation transfer requires stronger evidence.
    * **ImageNet Experiments:** The ImageNet experiments are less convincing. The lack of extensive hyperparameter tuning is a concern.  The statement that KD didn’t work well is presented without a detailed investigation. The claim that this is the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is demonstrably false; knowledge distillation has been applied to ImageNet extensively.
    * **Visualization:** The visualizations of attention maps are helpful but aren't used to provide a deeper understanding of *why* the attention transfer works (or doesn’t work) in certain cases.
4. **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is reasonably well-written and organized.  However, the notation can be a bit dense, and the descriptions of the experimental setup could be more detailed.  The appendix is helpful, but feels a bit disorganized.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **More Thorough Ablation Studies:**  Conduct ablations to assess the impact of different attention mapping functions, loss weights, layers for transfer, and the presence/absence of knowledge distillation.
* **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more theoretical grounding for the choice of loss function and the use of l2 normalization.  Explain *why* attention transfer is expected to be beneficial.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Compare your approach to more recent and sophisticated knowledge transfer methods.  For example, how does it compare to techniques based on feature matching or adversarial learning?
* **Address Batch Normalization:** Provide a more detailed discussion of how batch normalization is handled in the gradient-based attention transfer setting.
* **Error Analysis:** Analyze cases where attention transfer fails or leads to unexpected results.  What are the limitations of the approach?
* **Reproducibility:**  Release the code and trained models to facilitate reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* What is the intuition behind using the l2 norm for normalizing the attention maps?  Are other normalization schemes possible, and if so, have you experimented with them?
* The paper claims activation-based attention transfer performs better. Is this consistent across different architectures and datasets? What characteristics of the network or data might influence this result?
* Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, particularly for the ImageNet experiments?
* What steps have you taken to ensure the gradients are well-behaved during the double backpropagation process?




In conclusion, while the paper presents a reasonable exploration of attention transfer, it lacks the novelty, rigor, and compelling results necessary for acceptance at ICLR. The ideas are interesting but need further development and validation.
