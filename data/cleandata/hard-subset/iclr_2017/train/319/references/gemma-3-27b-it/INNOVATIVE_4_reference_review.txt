This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention mechanisms, proposing to leverage both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps as a conduit for transferring knowledge from a teacher network to a student network. The authors demonstrate improvements across various datasets and architectures, showcasing that attention transfer can outperform full activation transfer and synergize with knowledge distillation.

**Reject.** While the idea of transferring attention is intriguing and the initial results are promising, the paper lacks the depth of analysis and rigorous validation needed for acceptance at ICLR. The improvements reported, while statistically significant in some cases, appear modest given the complexity introduced, and the paper feels more like a proof-of-concept exploration rather than a fully developed contribution. The experimental setup, especially concerning hyperparameter tuning and the scope of architectures/datasets tested, feels somewhat limited.

The core argument – that transferring attention is a useful mechanism for knowledge transfer – is reasonably well motivated, building on prior work in attention mechanisms and knowledge distillation. The exploration of both activation-based and gradient-based attention is a positive aspect, and the initial observations regarding the differences in attention maps between networks with varying performance are insightful. However, the analysis of *why* attention transfer works remains superficial. Simply observing that attention maps correlate with objects isn't sufficient; a deeper understanding of *what* information is being transferred is crucial.

Several aspects weaken the paper's claims. The CIFAR experiments, despite being well-documented, feel somewhat self-contained and don't provide a compelling case for the broader applicability of the method. The ImageNet results are reported but lack crucial details on hyperparameter tuning and the computational cost, making it difficult to assess their significance. The claim of being "the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is overstated, as knowledge distillation itself has been successfully applied on ImageNet. Furthermore, the presentation of results is inconsistent (e.g., single-run results presented alongside median values), hindering direct comparison. The ablation studies, while present, are not exhaustive enough to convincingly demonstrate the importance of each component of the proposed approach (e.g., the impact of different attention mapping functions).

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Deeper Analysis of Attention Maps:**  Go beyond simply showing that attention maps correlate with objects. Quantify the transferred information. For example, measure the similarity between attention maps, analyze the entropy of the attention distributions, or visualize the gradients flowing through the attention transfer pathway.
*   **More Rigorous Evaluation:**  Expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse architectures, datasets (particularly larger-scale object detection and segmentation datasets), and hyperparameter settings. Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter search and report the results consistently.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:**  Clearly quantify the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer mechanism. This is particularly important for practical applicability.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform more extensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (different attention mapping functions, KD integration, symmetry constraints).
*   **Theoretical Justification:**  While not strictly necessary, exploring a theoretical justification for why attention transfer works could strengthen the paper.  Is there a connection to information theory or representation learning?



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Regarding the ImageNet experiments, what specific hyperparameters were used, and how was the learning rate schedule tuned?  Could you provide a more detailed report of the training process?
2.  You mention that KD didn't work well on ImageNet. Can you elaborate on the specific issues encountered (e.g., instability, slow convergence)? What was the temperature setting used for KD?
3.  What is the computational overhead of calculating and applying the attention transfer losses compared to a standard training run? Can you provide a rough estimate in terms of training time?
4.  You present different attention mapping functions. Do these functions exhibit different sensitivities to noise or adversarial perturbations in the input data?
5.  In the case of gradient-based attention transfer, you mention challenges with batch normalization. Could you elaborate on these challenges and what approaches you considered to address them?
