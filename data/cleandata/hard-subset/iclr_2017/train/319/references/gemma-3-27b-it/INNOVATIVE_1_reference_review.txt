This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention maps, proposing both activation-based and gradient-based methods. The core idea – leveraging *where* a network looks, rather than just *what* it predicts – is a compelling and potentially valuable direction. The authors demonstrate improvements across several datasets and architectures, showcasing the effectiveness of attention transfer, particularly the activation-based approach. The work also highlights that transferring attention can outperform simply transferring activations.

**Reject.** While the idea is novel and interesting, the paper feels somewhat exploratory and lacks the depth of analysis needed for a strong acceptance. The experimental results, while positive, are presented without sufficient statistical rigor, and the ablated studies (e.g., comparing different activation functions) are not exhaustive enough to provide strong justification for the chosen methods. The success on ImageNet is mentioned but lacks detail. The writing also suffers from some redundancy.

Here’s a more detailed breakdown:

**Supporting Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation:** While improvements are reported, the lack of standard deviations or multiple runs makes it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the results. Improvements of 1-2% are not always conclusive without such analysis. The presentation of results across different tables (e.g. Table 1, Table 3) feels disjointed and makes a direct comparison difficult.
* **Insufficient Ablation:** The exploration of different activation mapping functions (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) feels superficial. The authors state that sum-based functions work best, but the differences between them are not thoroughly investigated. A more systematic ablation study is needed to justify the choices made. The ablation comparing attention transfer to full activation transfer *is* a good contribution, but further ablations on the attention transfer components themselves are needed.
* **ImageNet Results are Vague:** The claim of a 1.1% top-1 improvement on ImageNet is significant, but the paper lacks crucial details. What specific architecture was used? What hyperparameters were used? Was it trained to convergence? Without these details, the result is difficult to evaluate. Mentioning resource constraints is not a substitute for presenting the experimental setup.
* **Gradient-Based Section Feels Underdeveloped:** The gradient-based section feels like an afterthought. It's introduced with less detail and justification than the activation-based method, and the results are less compelling. The comment about the difficulty of using batch normalization in the double backpropagation step raises concerns about the validity of this approach and warrants further investigation.
* **Writing and Redundancy:** The introduction is lengthy and repetitive. The related work section is descriptive but lacks critical analysis. Several phrases are repeated throughout the paper, impacting readability.

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

* **Statistical Significance:** Include standard deviations or confidence intervals for all results. Report multiple runs to demonstrate the robustness of the findings.
* **Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide a complete and reproducible experimental setup for all experiments, including hyperparameters, optimization algorithms, data augmentation techniques, and hardware specifications.
* **Thorough Ablation Studies:** Conduct more systematic ablation studies to evaluate the impact of different design choices, such as the choice of activation mapping function, the number of layers used for attention transfer, and the weight of the attention transfer loss.
* **More In-Depth Analysis:** Analyze the attention maps themselves. Are the transferred attention maps qualitatively similar to those of the teacher network? Provide visualizations and quantitative metrics to support this claim.
* **Address Batch Normalization Issue:** Investigate the challenges of using gradient-based attention transfer with batch normalization. Either find a workaround or acknowledge the limitations of this approach.
* **Streamline Writing:** Revise the paper to improve clarity and conciseness. Eliminate redundant phrases and focus on the key contributions.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*  Can you provide the standard deviations or confidence intervals for all reported results?
*  What specific hyperparameters were used for the ImageNet experiment, and can you provide more details about the training process?
*  Have you explored other methods for combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation?
*  Why does the symmetry norm perform well in the gradient-based experiments? What is the intuition behind this?
*  Have you considered using attention maps from intermediate layers of the *student* network to guide the transfer process? Would a reciprocal transfer mechanism improve performance?
