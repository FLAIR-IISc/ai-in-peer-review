This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via attention mechanisms, proposing both activation-based and gradient-based spatial attention maps. The authors demonstrate improvements in performance across various datasets and architectures by transferring attention from a teacher network to a student network. They further investigate combining attention transfer with knowledge distillation and explore symmetry constraints in gradient-based attention.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a relatively novel idea – utilizing attention maps as a direct transfer mechanism – the execution and presentation are riddled with inconsistencies, lack of rigor, and a superficial exploration of the proposed methods. The numerous minor flaws, when accumulated, undermine confidence in the reported results and the overall contribution.

The primary issue is the lack of methodological clarity and control. The hyperparameter tuning appears ad-hoc (β values derived arbitrarily, inconsistent KD temperatures), and the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. The ablation studies are minimal; for example, the claim that activation-based attention outperforms full activation transfer is presented without a truly controlled comparison – simply stating the results rather than demonstrating *why* this is the case. The justification for choosing specific activation functions (sum, max, powers) feels entirely empirical and lacks theoretical grounding. 

Furthermore, the writing is often imprecise. The terminology is sometimes used inconsistently (e.g., "hints" in relation to FitNets), and the explanations of the technical details (especially the double backpropagation) are vague. The repeated reference to figures without clear connection to the text is frustrating (e.g., "see Fig. 1", "see Fig. 3"). The conclusions are overstated given the limited depth of the analysis; claiming this is the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is a significant claim that needs far more justification, especially considering the extensive literature on knowledge distillation. 

The experimental section contains further issues. Reporting median test errors for some experiments and single-run results for others is questionable. The justification for turning off weight decay in the CIFAR experiments is weak. The lack of statistical significance testing for the reported improvements raises doubts about their robustness. The claim that gradient-based attention transfer improves performance is difficult to evaluate without a proper comparison to simply using the l2 regularization on gradients, as that also improved results. 

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting decision):**

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide a detailed description of all hyperparameters used, including learning rates, weight decay, data augmentation strategies, and batch sizes. Release the code.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the proposed methods. For instance, what is the effect of different attention mapping functions? How does the choice of β impact performance?
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support the claims of performance improvement.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more theoretical justification for the choice of activation functions and the overall approach to attention transfer.
*   **Clarity:** Revise the writing to be more precise and consistent. Define all terms clearly and provide a more detailed explanation of the technical details. Ensure figures are explicitly referenced and contribute to the understanding of the text.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Justify the hyperparameter settings. Perform a systematic hyperparameter search to optimize the performance of the proposed methods.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Ensure fair comparison with existing methods. Properly compare the proposed method with appropriate baselines, including full activation transfer, knowledge distillation, and FitNets.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the theoretical justification for using the absolute value of activations as a measure of importance?
2.  Why were different error metrics (median vs. single-run) used for different experiments?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the β parameter was chosen for each experiment?
4.  What is the computational overhead introduced by the attention transfer mechanism compared to standard training?
5.  How do the proposed attention maps compare to existing class activation mapping (CAM) techniques in terms of visualization quality and discriminative power?
6.  Regarding the claim of being the "first" to apply knowledge transfer on ImageNet: how does the proposed method differentiate itself from existing knowledge distillation approaches that are commonly used on ImageNet?
