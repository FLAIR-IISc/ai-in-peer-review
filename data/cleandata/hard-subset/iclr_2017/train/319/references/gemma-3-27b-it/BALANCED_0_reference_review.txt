This paper investigates knowledge transfer via attention maps between convolutional neural networks. The authors propose using both activation-based and gradient-based attention maps to improve the performance of a student network, guided by a teacher network. They explore different ways of defining these attention maps and demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures. The core idea is sound and addresses an interesting question about leveraging attention mechanisms for knowledge distillation.

**Reject** – While the paper presents a thorough investigation and experiments showing positive results, the novelty feels incremental and the impact is not significant enough for acceptance at ICLR. The experiments, while extensive, lack a strong comparative analysis against existing knowledge distillation techniques beyond a cursory mention. Moreover, the explanation of *why* these attention maps are effective for transfer remains largely unexplored.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of a compelling argument demonstrating the superiority of this attention-transfer approach compared to established knowledge distillation methods. The authors acknowledge this partially in the CIFAR section, where they show attention transfer performs well *alongside* KD, but don't explicitly compare performance with KD *alone* in a consistent manner across all experiments. The claim that attention transfer is the "first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet" is unsubstantiated and potentially inaccurate, given the established body of work on KD. The benefits on ImageNet appear modest (1.1% top-1) and could be a result of hyperparameter tuning rather than the core method itself. The discussion of why attention transfer works better than full activation transfer remains superficial.

The second concern relates to the experimental rigor. The paper mentions different settings (with/without batchnorm, ReLU before pooling) impacting results but doesn’t consistently explore these variations. The lack of error bars or multiple runs on larger datasets makes it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the reported improvements. The extensive ablation study on the different activation-based attention functions is appreciated, but the justification for the final choice (F2sum) is somewhat arbitrary. The presentation of results is often descriptive rather than analytical. For example, Table 3 describes the impact of various methods but lacks quantitative comparison in terms of statistical significance.

Despite these concerns, the paper has several strengths. The exploration of different attention map definitions (activation-based vs. gradient-based) is well-executed. The visualization of attention maps (mentioned but not included in the submitted version - this is a major drawback) likely provides valuable insights. The combination of attention transfer with knowledge distillation is a sensible extension. The code availability is a definite plus.

Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Stronger Baseline Comparison:** Conduct a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art knowledge distillation techniques (Hinton et al., FitNets, etc.) across all datasets, including ablation studies. Show that attention transfer offers a demonstrable advantage, not just comparable performance.
*   **Investigate *Why* it Works:** Go beyond simply showing *that* attention transfer works. Provide a deeper analysis of the learned attention maps. What features are being transferred? How do the attention maps align with the underlying visual concepts? Are there specific layers where attention transfer is most effective?
*   **Error Analysis & Statistical Significance:** Include error bars or report results from multiple runs to demonstrate the robustness of the findings. Conduct statistical significance tests to support the claimed improvements.
*   **Address Batch Normalization:** Carefully investigate the interaction between attention transfer and batch normalization, and report the results consistently.
*   **Visualization:** Include the visualizations of attention maps discussed in the paper – this is crucial for understanding the method.
*   **Clarify Claims:** Re-evaluate and potentially revise the claim regarding being the "first" to apply knowledge transfer to ImageNet.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Perform a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameter β to understand its impact on performance.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Why was F2sum chosen over other activation-based mapping functions? Was this determined through a hyperparameter search or based on specific observations about its properties?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the attention-transfer method appears to work better than directly transferring activations? Is it related to the sparsity of attention maps, or other factors?
3.  What is the computational overhead of the gradient-based attention transfer method, especially considering the second backpropagation step?
4.  You mention that KD struggled on ImageNet. What hyperparameters were tried? Could you explain why you believe attention transfer works better than KD in that scenario, given that KD is a more established and widely used technique?
5.  How do the learned attention maps change during training? Can you provide examples of how the student network's attention maps converge towards those of the teacher network?
