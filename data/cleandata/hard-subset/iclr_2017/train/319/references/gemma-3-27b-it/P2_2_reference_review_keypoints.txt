- Decision: Reject.

- Core idea: Transfer attention maps (activation and gradient-based) from teacher to student to improve performance; demonstrated across datasets and architectures.

- Positive: Attention-based transfer is an interesting, potentially valuable direction and works alongside knowledge distillation.

- Main reason for rejection: Presentation lacks clarity, experiments are scattered, and the paper fails to explain why attention transfer improves performance.

- Clarity & organization issues: Dense, repetitive introduction, abrupt transitions, and many unexplained equations/parameters (β, p, etc.).

- Experimental rigor & baselines: Results presentation is unclear, CIFAR baselines are not competitive, comparisons to strong baselines (and prior ImageNet KD claims) are insufficient.

- Mechanism unexplained: Paper shows that attention maps align but doesn’t analyze what features are transferred or how that alignment leads to better decisions.

- Complex implementation: Appendix reveals many hyperparameters and no ablation studies, making it unclear which components matter.

- Suggested improvement: Streamline presentation and focus on a smaller set of key experiments with clearer visualization of results.

- Suggested improvement: Strengthen baselines and comparisons, especially on CIFAR and prior ImageNet knowledge transfer work.

- Suggested improvement: Conduct thorough ablation studies on attention mappings, loss weights, and interplay with distillation.

- Suggested improvement: Provide qualitative visualizations of teacher/student attention maps and analyze transferred features.

- Suggested improvement: Explore theoretical justification (e.g., information-theoretic analysis) for why attention maps help.

- Questions for authors: Rationale and impact of l2 normalization; computational overhead of gradient-based transfer; sensitivity to β; examples of transferred features; measures to prevent overfitting.
