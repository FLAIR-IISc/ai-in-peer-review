- Proposal: Transfer spatial attention maps (activation- and gradient-based) from a teacher CNN to a student to improve performance.  
- Key finding: Activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer and complements knowledge distillation, yielding consistent gains across datasets and architectures, including ImageNet.  
- Decision: Accept — novel, well-motivated approach with comprehensive experiments and consistent empirical improvements.  
- Motivation: Clear problem definition—using attention for teacher→student knowledge transfer is analogous to human visual perception.  
- Novelty & formulation: Attention map transfer (Fsum, Fpsum, Fpmax) is novel, simple, and mathematically well-defined (losses in Eq. 2 & 4).  
- Experimental breadth: Evaluated on CIFAR, CUB, Scenes, ImageNet and on NIN and ResNet, with informative ablation studies.  
- Empirical strength: Strong, consistent performance improvements, notably on ImageNet, both standalone and combined with KD.  
- Related work: Thorough literature review and clear positioning relative to existing attention and distillation methods.  
- Gradient-based transfer: Under-explored—results are limited compared to activation-based methods and need expansion on larger datasets/architectures.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Missing analysis of sensitivity to β in the transfer loss and its effect on performance.  
- Computational cost: Lacks brief analysis of the computational overhead introduced by attention transfer relative to performance gains.  
- Visualizations: More representative student vs. teacher attention map visual comparisons (with/without transfer) are needed.  
- Limitations: Recommend an explicit discussion of limitations (e.g., sensitivity to teacher architecture, task applicability).  
- Q1 for authors: Why was gradient-based transfer limited to NIN/CIFAR—what prevents scaling to complex architectures/datasets?  
- Q2 for authors: Why did KD struggle on ImageNet—hyperparameter tuning, optimization difficulties, or other issues?  
- Q3 for authors: Why does horizontal flip invariance act as beneficial regularization—provide more insight.  
- Q4 for authors: How to choose 'p' for Fpsum/Fpmax—rule of thumb and how 'p' affects emphasized features?  
- Q5 for authors: How does the method perform when teacher and student have substantially different architectures (e.g., ResNet vs. Vision Transformer)?
