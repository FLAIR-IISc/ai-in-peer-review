This paper explores knowledge transfer between convolutional neural networks via the sharing of attention maps, defined as either activation-based or gradient-based spatial representations. The authors propose methods for transferring these attention maps from a teacher network to a student network, aiming to improve the student’s performance. They demonstrate improvements across various datasets and network architectures, including residual and non-residual networks, and show that activation-based attention transfer is superior to simply transferring full activations.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging attention maps for knowledge transfer is intuitively appealing, the presented work lacks the rigorous validation and comparative analysis expected for a strong ICLR submission, particularly given the novelty of the approach. The empirical results, while positive, are not presented with sufficient detail or statistical significance to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed methods. 

My primary concerns stem from several areas:

1. **Limited Baselines & Ablation:** The comparison to standard knowledge distillation is present, but lacks depth. A more thorough comparison against established knowledge transfer methods (e.g., FitNets, other distillation variations) is needed. Furthermore, the ablation studies within the paper (e.g., comparing different attention mapping functions) are not always conclusive and the reasons for observed differences are often speculative. The claim that activation-based attention is superior to full-activation transfer needs more robust justification beyond the presented results.

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Reproducibility:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters but doesn’t detail the process. This is particularly crucial for the attention transfer weight (β) which appears sensitive. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of β on performance is missing.  The lack of a clear statement regarding random seed control and the reliance on single-run results for some experiments (e.g., gradient-based transfer) raises concerns about reproducibility.

3. **Gradient-Based Transfer Concerns:** The section on gradient-based attention transfer, while interesting, feels less mature. The complexity of the double backpropagation technique, coupled with the limited scope of experiments (only NIN architecture on CIFAR), casts doubt on its generalizability. The remark about batch normalization compatibility is a significant caveat and suggests the method may be limited in practical application without further work.

4. **Overstated Claims:** The claim of “the first knowledge transfer method to be successfully applied on ImageNet” with attention transfer is a bold statement that requires more substantial evidence and is likely inaccurate. Many prior knowledge transfer techniques *have* been applied to ImageNet, even if not specifically framing it as “attention transfer”. The lack of a comprehensive ImageNet evaluation with thorough hyperparameter tuning weakens this claim.



**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide standard deviations or confidence intervals for all reported results.
*   **Detailed Hyperparameter Search:**  Include a table outlining the hyperparameter search space and the final values chosen for each experiment.
*   **Code Release:**  Releasing the code would significantly increase the impact and credibility of the work.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Explore a more theoretical justification for *why* attention transfer works. Is there a connection to information theory or representational similarity?
*   **Visualization Improvements:** While visualizations are included, they could be improved. Clearer visualizations of attention map similarity between teacher and student networks would be helpful.
*   **Clarify the contribution:** The contribution of the paper remains a bit blurry. Is the main contribution the specific attention map definitions, the transfer methods, or the empirical validation?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for why activation-based attention transfer outperforms full activation transfer, beyond the observed empirical results?
*   What steps were taken to ensure reproducibility of the results, specifically regarding random seed initialization?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the β hyperparameter, and what strategies were used to determine its optimal value?
*   Can you clarify the claim of being the first to apply knowledge transfer to ImageNet and provide a justification that acknowledges existing work in the field?
*   What are the computational costs associated with the double backpropagation step in the gradient-based attention transfer method, and how does it compare to the cost of standard backpropagation?
