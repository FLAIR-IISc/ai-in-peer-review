## ICLR 2024 Review: Emergent Communication with Grounding in Natural Language

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent reinforcement learning setting, specifically a referential game. The authors aim to understand how agents develop language-like systems from scratch and, importantly, how to shape the environment to promote the development of interpretable communication, even to humans. They explore the impact of sender architecture (agnostic vs. informed) and environmental modifications (class-level image representations) on the resulting "language". They further investigate grounding this emergent language through a combined interactive learning and supervised image labeling task, demonstrating improved human interpretability. The work is well-motivated by the need for agents to communicate effectively with humans and builds upon a rich history of research in game theory, linguistics, and multi-agent systems.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of emergent communication. The experimental setup is well-designed, the results are compelling, and the exploration of factors influencing interpretability is particularly insightful. The grounding experiments significantly enhance the practical relevance of the work.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Strong Motivation & Novelty:** The problem of emergent communication is fundamental to AI and the authors clearly articulate the challenges of moving beyond purely supervised approaches. The focus on *interpretable* communication, rather than simply successful coordination, is a crucial and novel contribution.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The experimental framework is thoughtfully designed, borrowing from Lewis’ signaling game while adapting it for a neural network-based RL setting. The comparison of different sender architectures and the systematic exploration of environmental factors provide robust evidence for the authors’ claims. The use of SVD to analyze vocabulary redundancy is also a clever approach.
*   **Compelling Results:** The results demonstrate that agents *can* learn to communicate effectively from scratch. The performance improvements with the informed sender and the class-level image representations suggest concrete strategies for fostering interpretable language. The grounding experiments, with the high human interpretability scores, provide strong evidence that the learned communication can be aligned with human understanding.
*   **Thorough Literature Review:** The paper provides a comprehensive and well-contextualized review of relevant work, including connections to game theory, linguistics, and multi-agent systems. This positions the work effectively within the broader research landscape.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarity of Architectures:** While Figure 1 is helpful, a more detailed textual description of the informed sender architecture, specifically the convolutional filters, would be beneficial. Explain the intuition behind the 2x1 kernel size and its impact on feature extraction.
*   **Statistical Significance:** While the paper reports significant p-values for some analyses, it would be stronger to consistently report statistical significance for all key findings (e.g., purity scores, performance comparisons).
*   **Ablation Study of Supervised Learning:** The supervised learning component is impactful, but a more detailed ablation study isolating its contribution would be useful. For example, what happens if the supervised labels are randomly shuffled? How does the amount of supervised data affect performance?
*   **Qualitative Examples:** Provide more qualitative examples of the emergent symbols and their corresponding meanings, beyond the examples in Figure 4. This would help readers gain a deeper understanding of the types of concepts the agents are learning to communicate.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A more explicit discussion of the limitations of the study would be valuable. For example, the limited vocabulary size, the specific image dataset used, and the potential for bias in the human evaluation.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Regarding the informed sender’s 2x1 convolutions:** Why was this specific kernel size chosen? Did you experiment with other kernel sizes or dimensions, and if so, what were the results?
2.  **Regarding human interpretability:** The human evaluation results are very promising. Could you provide more detail about the demographics of the CrowdFlower participants? Were there any filters in place to ensure the quality of the annotations?
3.  **Regarding vocabulary size:** How did you arrive at the vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100? Did you explore other vocabulary sizes, and if so, how did they affect the learning dynamics and interpretability of the emergent language?
4.  **Regarding convergence:** You mention that agents converge to coordination quickly. Is there any analysis of the learning curve beyond the initial phase? Do agents continue to refine their communication strategies over longer training periods?
5.  **Future Directions:** The paper mentions the goal of developing conversational machines. What are the next key steps towards achieving this goal, building on the findings of this work?  Specifically, how would you scale this approach to more complex environments and more realistic communication scenarios?



I believe this paper is a significant step forward in understanding the emergence of communication in artificial agents, and the focus on interpretability and grounding makes it particularly relevant and promising. Addressing the suggestions above will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
