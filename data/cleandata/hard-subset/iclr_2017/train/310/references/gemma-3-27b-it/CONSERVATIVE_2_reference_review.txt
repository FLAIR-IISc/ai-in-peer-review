This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting, specifically focusing on referential games where agents must learn to coordinate using a novel language. The authors aim to understand whether agents can develop a functional language from scratch, and whether features of that language align with human conceptual understanding. They explore different sender architectures and investigate the impact of introducing supervised learning to ground the emergent language in natural language concepts.

**Reject.** While the research question is interesting and the experimental setup is well-defined, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence to support its claims of “emergent language” and alignment with human intuition. The results presented, while positive, are not substantial enough to overcome the inherent skepticism required for such a novel and ambitious undertaking. The reliance on comparisons to chance performance and qualitative observations leaves too much room for interpretation and does not convincingly demonstrate a significant advancement in the field.

The primary concern lies with the overstatement of the "language" that emerges. Demonstrating successful coordination in a limited, artificial environment is not equivalent to demonstrating the emergence of a genuinely interpretable or generalizable language. The reported purity scores, while above chance, are not overwhelmingly high and could be achieved through relatively simple strategies. Furthermore, the reliance on t-SNE visualizations is insufficient to demonstrate semantic coherence; t-SNE is a dimensionality reduction technique prone to artifacts and subjective interpretation. The claim of "metonymic" links established with human participants in the grounding experiment, while intriguing, is based on a small sample and lacks rigorous statistical support. The 68% success rate, while better than chance, doesn’t definitively prove that humans are interpreting the *intended* meanings of the agent-generated symbols. It's plausible participants are engaging in post-hoc rationalization.

The comparison to existing literature is adequate but somewhat superficial. While the authors acknowledge related work, they don’t sufficiently engage with the challenges and limitations identified in those works. For instance, the potential for trivial solutions (e.g., learning to associate arbitrary symbols with object classes) is acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed. The authors lean heavily on the Lewis signaling game framework, but don't delve deeply into the theoretical challenges of convergence and equilibrium selection within that framework, despite recognizing them in the introduction.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Baselines:** The paper lacks comparison to simpler baselines. For example, how do the agents perform when using pre-defined, randomly assigned symbols? Or when using a fixed, hand-crafted communication protocol? Establishing these baselines would provide a clearer understanding of the value of the learned communication strategy.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more detailed ablation studies to isolate the impact of specific architectural choices (e.g., the informed sender’s convolutions) and training parameters.
*   **Quantitative Human Evaluation:** Replace the subjective qualitative assessments with more robust quantitative measures of human interpretability. For instance, a forced-choice experiment where participants are presented with multiple plausible interpretations of a symbol and asked to select the most likely one.
*   **Generalization:** The experiments are limited to a specific dataset and game setup. Explore the generalizability of the learned communication strategies to different environments, object categories, and task objectives.
*   **Symbol Usage Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the symbol usage patterns. How often are symbols reused across different image pairs? What is the distribution of symbol usage?
*   **Scalability:** The current setup uses a small vocabulary size. Investigate how the performance scales with increasing vocabulary size and task complexity.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more rigorous analysis of the baseline performance (e.g., random symbol assignment) to demonstrate the improvement offered by the learning-based approach?
2.  What specific measures were used to ensure the human subjects were not simply guessing or engaging in post-hoc rationalization when interpreting the agent-generated symbols?
3.  How do the learned communication strategies generalize to novel object categories or game environments not encountered during training?
4.  Given the theoretical challenges of convergence in Lewis signaling games, what steps were taken to ensure that the agents actually converged to a stable communication protocol?
5.  Can you explain why a two-symbol vocabulary is often sufficient for the agnostic sender and discuss the implications of this finding for understanding the nature of the learned communication?
