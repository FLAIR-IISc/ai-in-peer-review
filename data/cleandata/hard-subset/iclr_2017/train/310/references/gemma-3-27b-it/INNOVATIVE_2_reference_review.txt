This paper introduces a novel framework for studying the emergence of communication between agents, framing it as a multi-agent coordination game. The core idea – that language can bootstrap from interaction rather than solely from supervised data – is compelling and directly addresses a fundamental challenge in AI: enabling agents to communicate effectively with humans and each other. The authors wisely draw parallels to linguistic theory and game theory, grounding their work in existing literature, and present a well-defined experimental setup using referential games. The initial results demonstrating successful coordination and the exploration of semantic properties of the emergent language are promising. The attempt to ground the language in human-understandable concepts via supervised learning is a particularly strong and exciting direction.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a valuable and interesting line of inquiry, and the results are encouraging, I believe it falls short of acceptance at this time due to a lack of depth in analysis and clarity around the emergent language’s structure. The work feels like a strong first step, a proof of concept, but doesn’t yet offer sufficient insight to warrant acceptance to ICLR.

The primary concern lies in the somewhat superficial analysis of the emergent language. While the authors demonstrate *that* agents can coordinate and that some semantic structure emerges (via purity metrics and t-SNE visualizations), the nature of this structure remains poorly understood. The claim that the informed sender develops “more word-like symbols” feels under-substantiated – simply using more symbols doesn’t equate to meaningfulness. The observed "living vs non-living" distinction, while intriguing, feels like a lucky byproduct rather than a deliberate outcome of the environment or architecture. The human evaluation is a strong addition, but the 68% accuracy, while significantly above chance, doesn’t establish *robust* human interpretability. The description of “metonymic” links feels anecdotal, lacking quantitative backing. 

Furthermore, the paper leans heavily on analogies to linguistic theory without fully exploring the implications. For example, the discussion of Lewis’ signaling game and Nash equilibria feels cursory. The authors acknowledge the potential for multiple equilibria but don't adequately address whether their observed language corresponds to a particular, theoretically interesting equilibrium. The impact of the vocabulary size on the emergent communication is also not thoroughly investigated. 

Finally, the connection between the supervised learning phase and the improvement in human interpretability, while positive, could be strengthened. Is the improvement due to actual grounding in human concepts, or simply the agents learning to mimic labels during the supervised phase and then carrying that over to the game?

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Deeper Linguistic Analysis:** Conduct a more rigorous analysis of the emergent language. Examine the distributional properties of symbols. Are there patterns in co-occurrence? Can we identify any rudimentary syntactic structures? 
*   **Ablation Studies:** Systematically vary the parameters of the environment (e.g., image complexity, vocabulary size, reward structure) and the agent architectures to understand their impact on the emergent language.
*   **Equilibrium Analysis:** Relate the observed communication patterns to theoretical concepts from game theory, such as Nash equilibria.
*   **Human Evaluation:** Increase the scale of the human evaluation and include more detailed analysis of the types of errors humans make. Specifically, what kinds of misinterpretations are most common?
*   **Visualization:** Explore alternative visualization techniques beyond t-SNE to better understand the structure of the emergent semantic space. Consider dimensionality reduction techniques specifically designed for high-dimensional data.
* **Quantitative Analysis of Metonymy:** Instead of describing the metonymy anecdotally, attempt to quantify it. What is the frequency of similar semantic shifts across all pairs?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide examples of image pairs where the human subjects *incorrectly* identified the target image and explain the potential reasons for these errors?
*   How does the performance of the agents change as the vocabulary size increases? Is there a point of diminishing returns?
*   Have you explored different reward structures, and if so, how did they affect the emergent language?
*   What specific Nash equilibria (if any) do you believe your observed communication patterns correspond to?
*   Could you clarify the relationship between the supervised learning phase and the emergent language? Does the supervised learning primarily teach the agents to *mimic* human labels, or does it genuinely ground their communication in human concepts?
