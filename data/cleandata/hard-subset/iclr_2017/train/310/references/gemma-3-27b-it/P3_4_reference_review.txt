## ICLR 2024 Review: Emergent Communication in Multi-Agent Referential Games

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting using a referential game. The authors aim to understand if agents can learn to communicate *de novo* and whether the resulting “language” exhibits properties resembling human language – specifically, relating to conceptual categories and human interpretability. They explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and introduce a clever strategy of grounding the emergent language via a supervised image classification task to enhance human interpretability. The results demonstrate successful coordination, evidence of conceptual categorization in the emergent codes, and improved interpretability when grounding with supervised learning.  The work builds on Lewis’ signaling game and connects to broader research in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, providing empirical insights into the conditions fostering interpretable communication in AI agents.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. 

The paper tackles an important and challenging problem – the emergence of communication – with a well-designed experimental framework and clear, insightful results.  The grounding experiment significantly elevates the work, demonstrating a pathway towards more human-understandable agent communication. The connection to both the game theory literature (cheap talk games) and linguistic theory is well-established. 

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Sound Methodology:** The experimental setup is well-defined and motivated. The choice of ImageNet as a visual source provides a realistic, noisy input, and the reinforcement learning framework is appropriate for this type of interactive environment. The comparison between the ‘agnostic’ and ‘informed’ senders is a valuable contribution, offering insights into architectural biases and their effect on language emergence.
* **Strong Results:** The primary finding that agents *can* learn to coordinate effectively is significant.  Demonstrating that this coordination is associated with conceptual categories (above-chance purity) strengthens the claim that the emergent language isn't merely based on low-level features.  The grounding experiment, achieving 68% human interpretability, provides compelling evidence of a bridge between agent communication and human understanding.
* **Clear Connection to Literature:** The authors do an excellent job situating their work within related fields, referencing relevant work in multi-agent systems, linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory.  The discussion of Lewis’ signaling game and “cheap talk” games is particularly well-integrated.
* **Novelty:**  While emergent communication in multi-agent settings has been explored before, this work stands out by explicitly focusing on interpretability and actively exploring ways to align the emergent language with human intuitions. The grounding experiment is a novel and impactful contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement)**

* **Statistical Significance & Error Bars:** While the authors report *p*-values for the purity results, it would be beneficial to include error bars (e.g., standard deviation) on all reported performance metrics (coordination success rate, purity, interpretability) to give a clearer sense of the variability in the results.
* **Ablation Study of Supervised Task:**  The grounding experiment is promising, but a more thorough ablation study could strengthen the findings.  For example, what happens if the supervised task uses *fewer* labels? Does performance degrade gracefully?  How does the *type* of supervised task affect the emergent language?
* **Qualitative Analysis of Emergent Symbols:**  The authors mention qualitative evaluations but don’t provide much detail.  Including a few representative examples of how different symbols are used in the referential game (e.g., showing a set of images consistently associated with a particular symbol) would be highly valuable and make the findings more concrete.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational resources required for training (e.g., GPU hours) would be helpful for reproducibility.
* **Scaling to More Complex Environments:** The current environment is relatively simple. Discussing the challenges and potential approaches for scaling this work to more complex environments (e.g., with more objects, more agents, more complex communication protocols) would be a valuable addition.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Regarding the informed sender's convolutions:** Why were 1D convolutions chosen specifically, rather than 2D? Does this architectural choice have a specific linguistic motivation, or was it primarily a performance optimization?
* **Regarding human interpretability:**  In the human evaluation, were the participants aware that the symbols were learned by AI agents? Could participant bias (e.g., wanting to find a meaningful connection) have influenced the results? Would a control condition with randomly assigned symbols reveal the level of “noise” in the human ratings?
* **Regarding convergence:** The paper mentions convergence issues in cheap talk games.  Did the authors observe any sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, temperature) that could affect convergence in their experiments?
* **Regarding the 50-dimensional decomposition of the sender matrix:**  You mention redundancy, but 50 dimensions still seems high.  Was any further analysis done on the structure of these dimensions to identify potential underlying semantic axes?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling research question, solid methodology, and promising results. The focus on interpretability is particularly noteworthy, and the grounding experiment provides a concrete pathway towards more human-understandable AI communication. Addressing the minor feedback points will further strengthen the paper and contribute significantly to the field.
