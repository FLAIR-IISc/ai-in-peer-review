This paper introduces a novel framework for studying the emergence of communication in multi-agent settings, framed as a referential game. The authors aim to understand how agents can bootstrap a language from scratch and whether that language can exhibit properties aligning with human language – specifically, referencing objects based on conceptual categories rather than low-level visual features, and exhibiting some degree of interpretability for humans. They explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and investigate the impact of manipulating common knowledge through variations of the game setup. A crucial and particularly interesting component is the integration of a supervised learning task to ground the emergent language in human labels, demonstrating promising results in terms of human interpretability.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling and ambitious research direction, and the preliminary results are encouraging, the work feels significantly underdeveloped to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The novelty is high – the game-theoretic approach to emergent communication, combined with grounding via supervised learning, is a valuable contribution – but the analysis lacks the depth and rigor needed to convincingly demonstrate the claims.

My primary concern revolves around the evaluation. While the authors show *that* agents learn to communicate and *that* the resulting "language" shows some correlation with semantic categories and human understanding, they don’t fully articulate *how* or *why*. The evidence for human interpretability, while exciting, is based on a crowdsourced survey that, while statistically significant, feels somewhat superficial. Showing 68% accuracy in identifying the target image given a symbol is a good start, but doesn't reveal *what* aspects of the learned representations drive this performance. Further, the reliance on a pre-trained CNN for feature extraction and the limited vocabulary size (even in the 100-symbol case) constrain the potential complexity and expressiveness of the emergent language, potentially hindering a truly insightful investigation into its properties.

Furthermore, the comparisons between the "informed" and "agnostic" senders, while presented, aren’t fully exploited. The performance differences are noted, but a more thorough analysis of *why* the informed sender converges faster and produces a more interpretable language is missing. Is it simply the inductive bias, or are there other factors at play? The SVD analysis of symbol usage is a good attempt, but more robust dimensionality reduction and visualization techniques could provide more clarity. The experiments tweaking "common knowledge" are promising, but the effect size is modest, and a more detailed ablation study is needed to understand the impact of each manipulation.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **More detailed analysis of learned representations:** The paper should include visualizations of the embedding spaces learned by the sender and receiver. Tools like t-SNE or UMAP could be used to explore the structure of these spaces and identify meaningful clusters or patterns.
*   **Qualitative examples of communication:** Provide more concrete examples of how the agents are using symbols to communicate. Show specific image pairs and the corresponding symbols, and explain why the authors believe those symbols were chosen.
*   **Ablation studies:** Conduct more systematic ablation studies to evaluate the impact of different hyperparameters, network architectures, and training procedures.
*   **Exploration of more complex communication protocols:** The current setup uses a simple single-symbol communication protocol. Exploring more complex protocols, such as sequences of symbols or back-and-forth interactions, could reveal richer and more nuanced communication patterns.
*   **Comparison to baselines:** Compare the performance of the proposed approach to relevant baselines, such as supervised learning methods or other emergent communication frameworks.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  The supervised learning phase seems crucial for grounding the emergent language. How sensitive are the results to the size and quality of the supervised dataset? Would a larger and more diverse dataset lead to further improvements in human interpretability?
2.  You mention that the informed sender utilizes convolutions which might combine image embeddings dimension-by-dimension. Can you elaborate on *why* this architecture leads to more interpretable symbols? Is there a theoretical justification for this design choice?
3.  The human evaluation relies on associating symbols with ImageNet labels. Given that these labels are themselves not always perfectly aligned with human intuition, how confident are you that the observed interpretability is not simply a reflection of the biases inherent in the ImageNet dataset?
4.  What strategies might be employed to encourage the development of more complex and compositional language structures within the proposed framework?
