This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting, specifically focusing on referential games where agents must learn to coordinate using a developing “language” to identify objects. The authors present a framework and experimental setup designed to examine whether agents can successfully learn to communicate and whether the resulting communication displays properties resembling human language – namely, association with conceptual categories and interpretability by humans. They explore variations in agent architecture (informed vs. agnostic senders) and introduce a grounding mechanism through supervised learning on object names.

**Reject.** While the research question is interesting and aligns with a significant area of AI research (grounded language acquisition), the paper presents incremental progress on a well-studied problem with insufficient evidence to justify acceptance at a venue like ICLR. The results, while demonstrating *some* degree of emergent communication, are not sufficiently compelling or novel given the significant body of existing work in this area. The reliance on relatively simple environments and agent architectures, combined with a lack of robust comparison to established baselines, weakens the claims.

The core issue lies in the lack of clear differentiation from prior work. The authors acknowledge related research (Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) but don’t demonstrate a substantial advancement beyond it. The observed conceptual categorization, while interesting, is modest and doesn’t decisively show a qualitatively different or superior approach to language emergence. Furthermore, the human interpretability results, while encouraging, are based on a limited crowdsourced experiment and could benefit from more rigorous evaluation, including a control condition assessing baseline human performance on the same task without the agent-generated “language.”

The supervised learning component improves interpretability, but this feels like a rather direct intervention rather than a natural outcome of the emergent communication process. It's not clear how much of the improved human understanding stems from genuine linguistic alignment and how much from simple label association. The claim of “metonymic” links is intriguing but lacks quantitative support. The descriptions of the experimental setup, while detailed, lack crucial information regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and the stability of the learned communication protocols. The fact that hyperparameters were "set without tuning" is concerning and casts doubt on the robustness of the findings. The gains from the informed sender are not adequately explained beyond simply stating an inductive bias.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Baseline Comparisons:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing the performance of these agents to more established methods in communication and language learning. Have the authors considered comparing to agents trained with pre-defined communication protocols or utilizing more sophisticated reinforcement learning algorithms?
*   **Ablation Studies:** More comprehensive ablation studies are needed to understand the contribution of each component (informed vs. agnostic sender, supervised learning) and their interactions.
*   **Quantitative Analysis of Language:** The analysis of the emergent “language” is largely qualitative. More quantitative metrics are needed to assess its complexity, structure, and efficiency. For example, the authors could measure the entropy of the symbol distribution or the diversity of the generated communication patterns.
*   **Robustness and Generalization:** The authors should explore the robustness of the learned communication protocols to changes in the environment, such as introducing new objects or altering the task requirements.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance for all comparisons, including the purity scores and human interpretability results.
*   **Detailed Hyperparameter Reporting:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameters used and any preliminary attempts to tune them.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific limitations of Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) does this work address? Can you provide a quantitative comparison demonstrating these improvements?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters? Did you perform a hyperparameter search, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the emergent “language” beyond the purity scores and t-SNE visualizations? For example, can you quantify the degree of systematicity in the symbol assignments?
4.  What are the computational resources required to train these agents? Is the approach scalable to more complex environments and larger vocabularies?
5.  What control experiments were performed to establish a baseline for human performance on the interpretability task? This is necessary to determine the extent to which the human participants are genuinely understanding the agent-generated “language” versus simply guessing.
