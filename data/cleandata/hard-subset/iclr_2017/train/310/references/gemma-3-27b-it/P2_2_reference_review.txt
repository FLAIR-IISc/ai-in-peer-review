## Review of "Learning to Communicate"

**1. Summary:**

This paper explores the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting through a novel referential game. The authors investigate whether agents can learn to coordinate without pre-programmed communication protocols and whether the resulting "language" exhibits properties resembling human language, specifically concerning conceptual grounding and interpretability. They experiment with different sender architectures (agnostic and informed) and demonstrate successful coordination, finding evidence that the informed sender develops more interpretable symbols, associating them with broader object categories. They further explore grounding through a supervised learning component, showing improved human interpretability of the emergent communication. The work is well-motivated by the limitations of supervised learning for communication and draws connections to linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of emergent communication, demonstrating a compelling setup and insightful results. The experiments are well-designed and the analysis provides evidence supporting the claims made by the authors. The grounding experiments are particularly promising.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of enabling communication in AI agents, highlighting the shortcomings of purely supervised approaches. The motivation rooted in the need for functional, coordinated communication – as exemplified by the opening quote – is strong.
* **Well-Designed Experimental Framework:** The referential game provides a controlled environment for studying emergent communication. The use of ImageNet images offers realism, while the agent architectures are thoughtfully designed and compared. The controlled manipulations (vocabulary size, sender architecture, supervised learning) allow for a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing language emergence.
* **Supporting Results:** The authors demonstrate robust convergence to successful communication across various configurations. The observed differences between the agnostic and informed senders are significant and provide valuable insights. The purity metrics and human interpretability results offer quantitative support for the claim that the emergent language exhibits properties aligned with human conceptualization. The improvement in human interpretability with supervised learning is a particularly strong result.
* **Connection to Relevant Literature:** The paper effectively situates itself within the broader context of multi-agent systems, linguistics, and game theory, acknowledging related work and differentiating its contributions. The discussion of Lewis' signaling game and cheap talk games demonstrates a solid understanding of the theoretical foundations.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarify the Role of Randomness:** The reliance on sampling (image pairs, training data) introduces randomness. While acknowledged, further discussion of the robustness of the results to different random seeds would be beneficial.  Were multiple runs conducted with different seeds? Reporting variance in results would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Deeper Dive into Failure Cases:** While successful coordination is demonstrated, a deeper analysis of *when* and *why* the agents fail to communicate would be valuable.  Are there specific image pairs or concepts that consistently lead to miscommunication?
* **Qualitative Analysis of Emergent Symbols:** The paper focuses on quantitative metrics like purity and human interpretability. Providing more qualitative examples of the symbols learned by the agents, alongside the images they’re used to represent, would offer a more intuitive understanding of the emergent language.  Visualizing the embeddings learned by the agents could also be insightful.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Briefly mentioning the computational resources required for training would provide a more complete picture of the approach.
* **Explore Alternative Supervised Tasks**: Could incorporating other supervised tasks beyond object naming further improve the grounding and interpretability of the emergent language?

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide the variance in the purity metrics across multiple runs with different random seeds?
* Could you share some examples of the most and least interpretable symbols learned by the agents, along with the images they are typically used for?
* What is the computational cost (e.g., training time, hardware requirements) for training the agents?
* Have you considered exploring alternative supervised tasks in conjunction with the referential game, and if so, what were the results?
