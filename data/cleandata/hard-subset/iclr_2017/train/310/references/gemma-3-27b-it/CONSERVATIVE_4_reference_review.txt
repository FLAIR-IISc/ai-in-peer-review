This paper explores the emergence of communication in multi-agent reinforcement learning settings, specifically within a referential game. The authors aim to design environments that foster the development of interpretable language, examining whether agents learn to associate symbols with conceptual properties and whether their “language” can be understood by humans. They investigate two sender architectures—agnostic and informed—and explore the impact of supervised learning on grounding the emergent communication.

**Reject.** While the research question is interesting and aligns with a growing area of interest in AI, the paper presents an incremental contribution with limited empirical validation, particularly regarding the human interpretability claims. The presented results, while positive, are not sufficiently convincing to warrant acceptance at a highly competitive conference like ICLR.

The primary concern lies with the scope and robustness of the human evaluation. Presenting only a single experiment with crowdsourced evaluations, and reporting a 68% success rate which, while statistically significant, isn’t overwhelmingly strong given the controlled nature of the task (choosing between two images given a single word), is insufficient to claim "grounding" in human language. The presented analysis of “metonymic” links feels anecdotal and lacks a more systematic investigation. The reliance on ImageNet labels as the sole grounding mechanism seems limiting, and the observed success may simply reflect the inherent semantic structure present in the labels themselves. The results would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating generalization to novel concepts or scenarios not seen during the supervised training phase. 

Furthermore, the novelty of the proposed architectures (“informed sender”) is relatively minor. The use of 1D convolutions, while potentially beneficial, does not represent a substantial departure from existing approaches. The exploration of the environment’s impact on language emergence, while mentioned, is not deeply investigated. The paper primarily focuses on demonstrating *that* communication emerges, rather than thoroughly analyzing *why* certain environmental features lead to more interpretable languages.  The discussion of cheap talk games feels largely disconnected from the core empirical work – a more direct connection to the theoretical framework would be valuable. The impact of vocabulary size on the quality and interpretability of the emergent language needs to be further investigated.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Human Evaluation:** Significantly expand the human evaluation. Consider A/B testing against baseline labeling strategies. Investigate the types of errors humans make to understand the limitations of the emergent language. Include qualitative feedback from human participants.
*   **Architectural Exploration:** Conduct a more comprehensive ablation study of the informed sender architecture. How does the number of filters impact performance and interpretability? Compare against other convolutional architectures or attention mechanisms.
*   **Environment Design:** Systematically vary environmental parameters (e.g., number of objects, visual complexity, similarity of distractors) and analyze the resulting language properties.
*   **Generalization:** Evaluate the learned communication protocol on a transfer task. Can the agents successfully communicate about novel objects or in a different environment?
*   **Theoretical Connection:** Strengthen the connection to game theory and linguistics. How do the observed results relate to existing theoretical predictions about the emergence of language in signaling games?
*   **Quantitative Analysis of Symbol Usage:** Beyond purity, explore other metrics for quantifying the semantic richness and coherence of the emergent language.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The human evaluation relies on a binary choice task. How confident are you that this task accurately reflects the level of human understanding of the agent’s language?
2.  You mention "metonymic" links. Can you provide a more quantitative analysis of these links, rather than relying on anecdotal examples?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of ImageNet labels for grounding? Would different labeling schemes lead to different emergent languages?
4.  What is the computational cost of the informed sender architecture compared to the agnostic sender? Is the improved performance worth the increased complexity?
5.  You mention potential scalability issues with human-in-the-loop approaches. How does your game-based paradigm address these concerns in practice?
