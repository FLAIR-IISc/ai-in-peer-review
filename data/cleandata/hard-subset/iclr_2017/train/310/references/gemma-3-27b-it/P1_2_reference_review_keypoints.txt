- Paper studies emergence of communication in a referential multi-agent RL game, comparing raw pixel vs. category-level inputs.  
- Core contribution: environment design (input abstraction) influences whether emergent language aligns with human-interpretable semantics.  
- Agents successfully learn to coordinate and show evidence of conceptual grounding in learned symbols.  
- Authors propose combining interactive RL and supervised learning to further ground emergent language for human interpretability.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Strength: Well-motivated problem framed in broader AI, linguistics, and cognitive science contexts.  
- Strength: Simple, controlled referential game enables clear experimental comparisons.  
- Strength: Comparison of ‘agnostic’ vs. ‘informed’ sender architectures and input manipulations is informative.  
- Strength: Robust results including purity scores and human evaluation support claims of interpretability.  
- Strength: Thorough connection to related literature and clear positioning of contributions.  
- Novelty: Emphasis on shaping environment to promote interpretable emergent communication is a notable advance.  
- Revision request: Clarify RL algorithm details (hyperparameters, exploration strategy) beyond Reinforce mention.  
- Revision request: Include ablation studies on the supervised component (different datasets, amount of supervised data).  
- Revision request: Add more qualitative examples of symbol–image associations to illustrate emergent language.  
- Revision request: Expand discussion of limitations (vocabulary size, game complexity) and future work directions.  
- Question: Why does the informed sender converge faster and use more symbols—purely due to inductive bias or other factors?  
- Question: Were there systematic biases or consistent human judgment errors in the ReferItGame evaluation?  
- Question: Can the authors elaborate on observed metonymic links and recurring patterns in symbol–image associations?  
- Question: Did different initializations or learning dynamics lead to different emergent language equilibria, given multiple possible Nash equilibria?
