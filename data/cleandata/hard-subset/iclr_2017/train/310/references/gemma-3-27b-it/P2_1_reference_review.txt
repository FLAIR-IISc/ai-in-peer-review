## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning to Communicate

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a research program exploring the emergence of communication in multi-agent settings. The authors investigate whether agents can learn to coordinate via language from scratch, and what environmental features encourage the development of interpretable "languages." They present a referential game where two agents need to identify target images, and compare different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) with varying vocabulary sizes. They demonstrate successful coordination and evidence of semantic structure in the emergent communication, linking symbol usage to object categories and, with supervised grounding, to human-understandable labels.  The work builds on foundations in linguistics, game theory, and multi-agent reinforcement learning, offering insights into both the mechanisms of language emergence and the design of environments that foster interpretable communication.

**Decision:** **Accept** (with minor revisions)

**Key Reasons:**

This paper tackles a significant and challenging problem – understanding how communication emerges – and presents a solid first step towards addressing it. The experimental setup is well-designed, the results are convincing, and the connection to relevant literature (linguistics, game theory, and multi-agent RL) is clearly established. The grounding experiment significantly strengthens the work, demonstrating a pathway towards human-interpretable communication.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Well-Motivated Problem:** The introduction clearly articulates the importance of communication for intelligent agents interacting with humans and frames the problem effectively, drawing a compelling analogy to language acquisition.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The referential game provides a simple yet effective environment for studying emergent communication. The choice of image data (ImageNet) and the use of pre-trained CNN features are sensible. The comparison between agnostic and informed senders allows for a nuanced understanding of architectural impacts. The use of Reinforce is appropriate for the task.
*   **Convincing Results:** The authors demonstrate successful coordination, and the analysis of symbol usage (purity, SVD decomposition, t-SNE visualization) provides evidence for semantic structure. The supervised grounding experiment is a strong contribution, showing alignment with human language.
*   **Rigorous Evaluation:** The inclusion of a baseline comparison (chance performance) for purity, and the human evaluation of grounded language, adds rigor to the evaluation.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the experimental setup and results.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** While the comparison of agnostic and informed senders is helpful, additional ablation studies could strengthen the findings. For example, what impact does the embedding dimensionality have on performance and interpretability?
*   **Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses primarily on successes. Discussing instances where communication *fails* could provide valuable insights. Are there specific image pairs or object categories that are consistently difficult to communicate about?
*   **Quantitative Analysis of Grounding:** While the 68% human accuracy for grounded language is promising, providing confidence intervals or further statistical analysis would strengthen the claim. How does performance vary based on image category?
*   **Computational Resources:** Briefly mentioning the computational resources required for the experiments (e.g., number of GPUs, training time) would enhance reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The supervised grounding experiment utilizes a subset of ImageNet classes. How sensitive are the results to the specific choice of these classes? Would a different subset yield similar performance?
2.  You mention the potential for "metonymic" links in the grounded language. Could you provide more specific examples of these links from your human evaluation? What features of the images seem to trigger these metonymic associations?
3.  The SVD decomposition of the symbol-usage matrix revealed redundancy. How might this redundancy be exploited to compress the emergent language or improve its efficiency?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of temperature parameter (τ) in the Gibbs distributions? Was this parameter tuned, or was it fixed throughout the experiments? How sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of emergent communication. The authors have presented a well-motivated, well-executed, and thoroughly evaluated study. The acceptance recommendation is based on the strength of the results and the potential for this work to inspire further research in this exciting area.
