This paper investigates the emergence of communication in multi-agent reinforcement learning environments, specifically focusing on referential games where agents must learn to coordinate by developing a shared language. The authors explore factors influencing the “naturalness” of the emergent language, examining whether it reflects conceptual categories and is interpretable by humans. They propose a framework and demonstrate that agents can indeed learn to communicate and that modifications to the environment can nudge the language towards more semantic and human-interpretable forms. The inclusion of supervised learning to ground the communication in human labels further improves interpretability.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting research direction and demonstrates promising initial results, the presentation is riddled with inconsistencies, lacks sufficient methodological rigor in several key areas, and the claims are often overstated given the limited evidence provided. The numerous minor flaws, when considered cumulatively, significantly detract from the paper’s overall quality and make a positive assessment difficult.

The primary concern lies with the experimental details and reporting. The statement “hyperparameters were set without tuning” is concerning. While acknowledging the computational cost of hyperparameter optimization is reasonable, claiming *no* tuning whatsoever suggests a lack of sensitivity analysis to ensure the reported results are robust and not artifacts of a particularly lucky initialization or a suboptimal hyperparameter configuration. The choice of embedding dimensionality (50), filter sizes (20, 1), and temperature (10) are presented as arbitrary, without justification or exploration of alternatives. 

Furthermore, the description of the “informed sender” architecture is vague. The rationale behind using 1D convolutions with a kernel size of 2x1 applied “dimension-by-dimension” is unclear and lacks a visual illustration beyond Figure 1, which itself isn’t detailed enough. The claim that this architecture provides an “inductive bias towards combining the two images dimension-by-dimension” is hand-wavy and requires stronger justification. What *specifically* is being combined and why does this architecture promote meaningful interaction?

The evaluation of semantic properties is also problematic. The use of purity as a metric is reasonable, but the comparison to a random permutation baseline feels weak. A more rigorous baseline would involve comparing the observed purity to that obtained from randomly assigning symbols to objects *while preserving the category distribution*.  The t-SNE visualization, while visually suggestive, lacks quantitative analysis. There's no statistical test to demonstrate that objects with similar semantic properties are indeed clustered together in the embedding space. The connection drawn between the CNN features and the semantic interpretation feels speculative without further validation.

The human evaluation, while a positive addition, is presented with insufficient detail. What specific instructions were given to the CrowdFlower workers? What was the demographic composition of the workers? The reported 68% accuracy is impressive, but without details about the difficulty of the task and the potential for biases in the annotation process, it's difficult to assess the significance of this finding.  The mention of “metonymic” links is intriguing, but the evidence is limited to a few anecdotal examples.

Finally, the writing style is often imprecise and repetitive. The frequent citations to foundational works (e.g., Lewis, Wittgenstein) feel unnecessary and contribute to the paper’s length without adding substantial insight. The extensive list of related work is comprehensive but lacks critical analysis. Simply stating that other approaches have limitations isn't sufficient; the authors need to articulate *why* their approach is superior or offers a unique perspective.  There are also minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, unclear figure captions).

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Methodological Rigor:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters. Provide a detailed justification for the architectural choices. Include quantitative analysis of the t-SNE visualizations (e.g., silhouette score, Davies-Bouldin index).
*   **Human Evaluation:** Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including instructions given to participants, demographic information, and a discussion of potential biases.
*   **Clarity and Precision:** Revise the writing style to be more concise and precise. Avoid vague statements and hand-wavy explanations. Strengthen the arguments and provide more concrete evidence to support the claims.
*   **Related Work:** Provide a more critical analysis of related work, highlighting the specific contributions of this paper and how it advances the field.
*   **Figure & Table formatting**: Ensure consistency in table and figure captions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What was the motivation for choosing the specific vocabulary sizes (10 and 100)? How might the results change with different vocabulary sizes?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the 1D convolutional layers in the informed sender? What is the expected effect of this architecture on the learned representations?
3.  What steps were taken to ensure the robustness of the results to different random seeds and initializations?
4.  Regarding the human evaluation, can you share the exact instructions given to the CrowdFlower workers, and provide a summary of their demographic characteristics?
5.  Can you elaborate on the observed "metonymic" links in the human evaluation with specific examples and a more systematic analysis?
