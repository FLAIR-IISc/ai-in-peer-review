This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting, framing it as a referential game where agents learn to coordinate by developing a shared language. The authors explore how environmental features impact the development of interpretable codes, even attempting to ground these codes in human-understandable concepts through a combined supervised learning approach. The motivation, drawing parallels to challenges in natural language acquisition, is clearly articulated.

**Reject.** While the research area is interesting, the paper suffers from a lack of convincing evidence supporting its core claims. The emergent "languages" are presented as promising, but the analysis relies heavily on post-hoc interpretation and lacks rigorous quantitative validation against established baselines. The grounding experiments, while a step in the right direction, demonstrate only modest improvements in human interpretability and rely on a somewhat contrived setup.

My primary concern lies with the limited robustness of the reported results and the somewhat optimistic interpretation of the observed phenomena. The claim that the agents develop language “resembling human language” is not substantiated by sufficiently compelling evidence. The purity metrics, while statistically significant, are not particularly high and could be achieved through trivial strategies. Furthermore, the analysis of the spectral decomposition of the symbol usage matrix feels exploratory and doesn't provide strong support for the assertion that the informed sender utilizes a more nuanced vocabulary. The observed success in human interpretability is also not overwhelming (68% accuracy), and crucially, the study lacks a control condition comparing this performance to random guessing or other simple heuristics. Simply demonstrating that humans can *sometimes* understand the agent’s signals is insufficient to claim meaningful grounding.

The experimental setup, while logically sound, feels somewhat limited in scope. The use of a relatively simple game and a small vocabulary size restricts the complexity of the emergent communication. Moreover, the parameters (embedding dimensionality, learning rate, etc.) were chosen *without tuning*, raising concerns about the sensitivity of the results to these choices. The reliance on pre-trained VGG features, while standard, introduces a potential bias and limits the extent to which the agents are truly learning from scratch. 

Additional feedback:

*   **Baselines:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by including comparisons to established baselines, such as random communication, or communication protocols based on hand-engineered features.
*   **Ablation Studies:** A more thorough ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of different components of the informed sender architecture.
*   **Quantitative Analysis:** Provide more quantitative metrics to assess the semantic similarity between the agent’s symbols and human concepts.
*   **Scalability:** The paper does not address the scalability of the approach to more complex environments or larger vocabularies.
*   **Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis of the human interpretability experiments would be valuable to identify the types of symbols that are most and least interpretable.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the vocabulary size? Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization?
2.  What is the performance of a simple baseline communication strategy (e.g., random symbol selection) in the same experimental setup?
3.  Could you provide more details about the qualitative evaluation of the emergent languages? What specific linguistic features (e.g., compositionality, recursion) were observed, if any?
4.  How does the performance of the agents change when presented with novel images outside of the ImageNet dataset?
5.  The phrasing "grounding in natural language" feels somewhat strong given the results. Was any effort made to impose linguistic constraints (e.g. grammatical structure) on the emergent communication?
