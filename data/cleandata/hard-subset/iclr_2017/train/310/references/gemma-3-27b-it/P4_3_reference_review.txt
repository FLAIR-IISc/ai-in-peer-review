This paper introduces a multi-agent coordination communication game as a framework for studying the emergence of language. The core idea is to have agents learn to communicate from scratch in a simple environment, aiming to understand what environmental features lead to interpretable languages, potentially even those transferable to human communication. The authors explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and investigate the effects of varying vocabulary sizes and the introduction of supervised learning for grounding.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting research direction and initial results, the claims regarding the "naturalness" or "interpretability" of the emergent language, particularly concerning human alignment, are not sufficiently supported by the provided evidence. The connection between the observed behavior and established linguistic principles remains tenuous.

The primary reason for rejection stems from a lack of rigorous evaluation of the emergent language’s properties. The purity metrics, while statistically significant, offer a coarse measure of semantic alignment. The human evaluation, while promising, is based on a limited crowdsourced experiment with potential biases (e.g., participants guessing based on prior knowledge rather than the learned language). Furthermore, the reported 68% success rate in human interpretation, while statistically significant, doesn't demonstrate a strong alignment with how humans *naturally* communicate. A success rate only marginally above chance doesn’t constitute a substantial breakthrough toward human-interpretable AI languages. The authors demonstrate *a* level of interpretability, but not one that is convincingly closer to human language.

Secondly, the paper lacks sufficient exploration of the limitations of the proposed framework. The simplicity of the environment and the limited complexity of the agents raise concerns about the generalizability of the findings. It's unclear whether the observed phenomena would hold in more complex scenarios. The introduction of the supervised learning task provides some grounding, but the experiment's design doesn’t fully disentangle the effects of the game itself from the effects of the supervised signal.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Quantitative Analysis of Language Structure:** Explore quantitative metrics beyond purity to characterize the emergent language. For example, analyze the distribution of symbol usage, the co-occurrence patterns of symbols, or the dimensionality of the symbol embedding space. This would provide a more nuanced understanding of the language's structure.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to systematically investigate the contribution of different components of the framework (e.g., the informed sender architecture, the supervised learning task).
*   **Comparative Analysis:** Compare the emergent language to human language in more detail. For instance, analyze the types of semantic errors made by the agents and compare them to common errors observed in human language acquisition.
*   **Human Experiment Design:** Improve the rigor of the human evaluation. Consider using a more controlled experimental design, incorporating a baseline condition (e.g., random symbol assignment) and employing more sophisticated measures of human understanding (e.g., rating scales, think-aloud protocols). Perhaps an information theoretic approach to assessing interpretability would be beneficial.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Acknowledge and discuss the limitations of the current framework more explicitly. This includes the simplicity of the environment, the limited complexity of the agents, and the potential biases in the human evaluation.
*   **Clarify “Naturalness”:** Define more precisely what is meant by “natural language.” The paper uses this term loosely and without a clear theoretical foundation.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the VGG ConvNet features (fc vs. sm) and how this choice affects the emergent language?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameter settings (e.g., embedding dimensionality, temperature parameter)?
3.  What is the computational cost of training the agents, and how does it scale with vocabulary size and the complexity of the environment?
4.  Can you provide more examples of the “metonymic” links observed in the human evaluation?
5.  How would you address the concern that the human participants may have relied on prior knowledge or heuristics rather than the learned language when interpreting the symbols?
6.  Were any attempts made to analyze the emergent language using established linguistic frameworks (e.g., distributional semantics, information theory)?
