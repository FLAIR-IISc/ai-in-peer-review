- Paper studies emergent communication in a referential multi-agent game, testing whether agents learn human-like, interpretable language.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Uses ImageNet inputs and reinforcement learning to study communication emergence between agnostic and informed sender architectures.  
- Introduces grounding via a supervised image classification task to improve human interpretability of emergent symbols.  
- Results show successful coordination, above-chance conceptual category purity, and 68% human interpretability after grounding.  
- Strong methodological design and appropriate experimental framework for interactive environments.  
- Clear connections to Lewis’ signaling game, cheap talk literature, linguistics, and cognitive science.  
- Novel contribution: explicit focus on interpretability and a grounding experiment to align emergent language with human intuitions.  
- Request to add error bars / variability measures and more thorough statistical reporting across metrics.  
- Suggests ablation studies on the supervised grounding task (e.g., fewer labels, different task types) to test robustness.  
- Recommends more qualitative examples of symbol usage to illustrate emergent meanings.  
- Asks for details on computational cost (e.g., GPU hours) to aid reproducibility.  
- Encourages discussion on scaling to more complex environments, more agents, and richer protocols.  
- Question about why 1D convolutions were used in the informed sender versus 2D and whether this was linguistically motivated or for performance.  
- Queries about potential participant bias in human interpretability tests and the value of a random-symbol control condition.  
- Asks about sensitivity to hyperparameters (learning rate, temperature) and convergence behavior in cheap talk settings.  
- Requests further analysis of the 50-dimensional sender decomposition to identify redundant or semantic axes.
