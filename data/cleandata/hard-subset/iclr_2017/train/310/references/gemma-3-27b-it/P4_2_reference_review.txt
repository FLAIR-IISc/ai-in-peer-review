This paper introduces a multi-agent coordination communication game designed to foster the development of interpretable language. The authors investigate whether agents can learn to communicate effectively from scratch and explore environmental features that promote language resembling human communication, focusing on grounding symbols in conceptual properties and human interpretability. They present experiments with different sender architectures and explore the impact of removing low-level common knowledge and supervised learning on the emergent language.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to resubmit after significant revisions. While the research direction is promising and addresses an important problem – the emergence of communication and its grounding – the paper lacks sufficient clarity and rigor in its presentation of results and analysis to convincingly support its claims. The core issue is the reliance on qualitative observations and statistical tests without sufficient explanation of effect sizes and practical significance.

The paper’s primary argument is that agents can learn to communicate and that specific environment modifications (informed sender, removing low-level knowledge, supervised learning) improve the interpretability of the learned language. However, the evidence for this claim is presented in a manner that is difficult to evaluate. For example, stating that "purity is significantly above chance" (multiple instances) is insufficient without reporting the actual p-values, effect sizes, and a clear definition of what constitutes a meaningful difference from chance. Similarly, the claim of 68% human agreement on symbol meaning, while encouraging, needs to be contextualized. What is the baseline agreement rate for a similar task? Is this performance statistically significant? What is the inter-annotator agreement? The qualitative descriptions of "metonymic" links are interesting but not a robust form of evidence.

Further, the experimental setup, while described, lacks crucial details that hinder reproducibility. The hyperparameter choices are stated as "without tuning," which is a reasonable starting point but doesn’t justify their selection. Were sensitivity analyses performed to understand the impact of these parameters? The training details are limited; understanding the convergence behavior and potential instabilities is crucial. The "temperature" parameter in the Gibbs sampling is stated but its effect on the resulting language isn’t discussed. 

The connection to the broader literature, while extensive, sometimes feels tangential. The authors accurately situate their work within game theory, linguistics, and multi-agent systems, but the discussion lacks a focused critique of how their approach differs and advances the state-of-the-art beyond the cited works. For example, what specific limitations of previous approaches are addressed by the proposed framework?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Statistical Rigor:** Provide p-values, effect sizes, and confidence intervals for all statistical comparisons. Clearly define the statistical tests used and justify their appropriateness.
*   **Quantitative Analysis:** Replace qualitative observations with quantitative measures wherever possible. For example, instead of saying “the informed sender uses more varied and word-like symbols,” quantify the diversity of symbol usage.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform more targeted ablation studies to isolate the effect of each environmental modification. For instance, what happens if supervised learning is applied *without* removing low-level knowledge?
*   **Reproducibility:** Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including all hyperparameter values and training procedures. Consider releasing the code and data to facilitate replication.
*   **Baselines:** Compare the performance of the proposed approach to relevant baselines, such as simple random communication protocols or existing communication frameworks.
*   **Clarity:** The writing could be more concise and focused. Avoid jargon and explain technical concepts clearly. The introduction, while setting the stage, is lengthy and could be streamlined.
*   **Human Evaluation:** Expand the human evaluation study to include a larger number of participants and more rigorous analysis. Include a control condition where humans are asked to interpret randomly assigned symbols.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What are the computational costs associated with training the agents, and how does the complexity scale with vocabulary size and image dataset size?
2.  You mention the potential for language redundancy. How do you differentiate between true synonymy and simply ineffective symbol usage?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the pre-trained CNN (VGG) and the layer used for image representation (fc vs. sm)?
4.  The temperature parameter in the Gibbs sampling is mentioned, but its impact on the resulting language isn’t discussed. How does varying this parameter affect the diversity and interpretability of the emergent communication?
5.  Can you provide more details about the supervised learning task, specifically the criteria for selecting the 100 ImageNet labels? Did the choice of labels impact the emergence of interpretable symbols?
