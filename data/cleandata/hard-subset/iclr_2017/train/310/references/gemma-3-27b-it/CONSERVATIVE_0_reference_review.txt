This paper investigates the emergence of communication in multi-agent reinforcement learning environments, specifically focusing on referential games where agents must learn to coordinate using a novel “language” to identify objects. The authors aim to understand how environmental factors influence the development of interpretable communication protocols and explore grounding this emergent language in human-understandable concepts through a combination of interactive learning and supervised object naming.

**Reject.** While the research direction is potentially interesting, the paper presents a largely exploratory study lacking sufficient rigor and comprehensive evaluation to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The claims regarding “naturalness” and human interpretability are weakly supported and rely heavily on qualitative observations and limited experiments. The lack of comparison to established baselines, particularly in the grounding phase, is a significant concern.

My primary concerns stem from the methodological soundness and the strength of evidence presented. First, the experimental setup, while conceptually clear, lacks thorough justification for design choices. For example, the specific network architectures chosen (agnostic vs. informed sender) are motivated intuitively but not rigorously compared in terms of their capacity or inductive biases. A more systematic ablation study exploring different architectures and hyperparameters would strengthen the conclusions. 

Second, the claims regarding the “naturalness” of the emergent language are not convincingly demonstrated. While the authors show that the learned symbols correlate with broader object categories and exhibit some degree of human interpretability, this is assessed through limited experiments (purity scores and a small-scale crowdsourced evaluation). The purity scores, while statistically significant, are not overwhelmingly high, and the crowdsourced experiment, while promising, only evaluates a small subset of the learned vocabulary in a relatively constrained setting. Critically, there is no baseline for comparison: how does the human interpretability of this emergent language compare to, say, random symbol assignments or even simple human-defined labels?

Third, the grounding phase, while innovative, lacks crucial controls. The authors combine referential game playing with supervised object naming but do not compare the resulting language to a purely supervised learning approach. Did the interaction with the game *actually* improve grounding beyond what could be achieved through supervised learning alone? Furthermore, the selection of 100 ImageNet labels seems arbitrary. The authors state that the supervised objective “has no negative effect on communication success” but offer no quantitative evidence to support this. A detailed analysis showing the effect of the supervised task on the emergent language’s complexity or efficiency is missing.

Finally, the connection to existing literature, especially game theory, feels superficial. While the authors acknowledge related work, they do not engage in a critical comparison of their findings to theoretical predictions about equilibrium selection in signaling games. The discussion of convergence and equilibrium is largely descriptive rather than analytical.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Establish Baselines:** Include comparisons to relevant baselines throughout the study. This includes simpler communication strategies, random symbol assignments, and supervised learning approaches.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study to investigate the impact of different architectural choices, hyperparameters, and environmental factors on the emergent language.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation of Grounding:** Provide a quantitative assessment of the impact of supervised learning on the interpretability and efficiency of the emergent language.
*   **Connect to Game Theory:** Engage more deeply with the game-theoretic literature and explore how the findings relate to theoretical predictions about equilibrium selection in signaling games.
*   **Expand Human Evaluation:**  Conduct a more extensive and controlled human evaluation of the emergent language, comparing it to other forms of communication (e.g., human-generated descriptions).
*   **Provide More Detailed Analysis:** Offer more in-depth analysis of the learned symbols, including their semantic properties and how they evolve over time.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific design choices were made in the network architectures, and how were these choices justified?
*   How does the performance of the proposed approach compare to simpler communication strategies or random symbol assignments?
*   Can you provide quantitative evidence demonstrating the benefits of combining interactive learning with supervised object naming?
*   What theoretical predictions from game theory are most relevant to your findings, and how do your results compare to these predictions?
*   Were any steps taken to prevent the agents from exploiting statistical biases in the training data?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of vocabulary size?
