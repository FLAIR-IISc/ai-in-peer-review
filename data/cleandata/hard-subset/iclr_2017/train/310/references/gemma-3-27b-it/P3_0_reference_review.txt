## ICLR 2024 Review: Emergent Communication in Multi-Agent Referential Games

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting using a referential game. The authors aim to understand how agents, starting as “tabula rasa,” can develop a language to coordinate and whether features of the environment can encourage the development of interpretable “codes” resembling human language. They explore this through two primary lenses: association with conceptual properties and human interpretability. They demonstrate successful coordination, observe differences between informed and agnostic sender architectures, and show that manipulating the environment (removing low-level visual cues) and incorporating supervised learning for object naming can significantly enhance the semantic coherence and human interpretability of the emergent language. The work builds upon Lewis' signaling game and related research in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, with a focus on developing practical AI capable of communication.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of emergent communication. The experimental setup is well-defined, the results are compelling, and the connection to relevant literature is clearly articulated. The groundedness experiments, in particular, are a noteworthy advancement, demonstrating a path towards more human-interpretable agent communication.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of developing agents capable of natural language-based coordination, highlighting the limitations of purely supervised learning approaches. The framing within the context of Ted Chiang’s “Story of Your Life” is effective in motivating the core problem.
* **Rigorous Experimental Design:** The experimental setup using referential games is well-structured and provides a controlled environment for studying emergent communication. The comparison between agnostic and informed sender architectures, along with the manipulations of the environment (class-level properties) and the introduction of supervised learning, provide valuable insights.
* **Compelling Results:** The consistent demonstration of successful coordination across various settings is a key achievement. The results concerning the purity of the emergent language (association with conceptual categories) and the significant improvement in human interpretability with supervised learning are particularly convincing. The t-SNE visualizations effectively support the qualitative claims about semantic coherence.
* **Strong Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper thoroughly reviews relevant work in multi-agent systems, linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, situating their contributions within the broader research landscape. The connection to Lewis’ signaling game and the discussion of Nash equilibria are particularly insightful.
* **Novelty:** The focus on designing environments that foster interpretable language, and the grounding experiments, are novel and important contributions.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Deeper Analysis of Failure Cases:** While the paper demonstrates successful communication, a more detailed analysis of failure cases could provide valuable insights. What types of image pairs or symbols lead to miscommunication? This could reveal limitations in the current setup.
* **Ablation Studies on Supervised Learning:**  More detailed ablation studies could examine the impact of different supervised learning parameters (e.g., the size of the supervised vocabulary, the ratio of game-playing vs. supervised learning) on the quality and interpretability of the emergent language.
* **Generalization to More Complex Environments:** The current environment is relatively simple. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the challenges of scaling this approach to more complex environments and potential strategies for addressing them.
* **Qualitative Examples of Language Use:**  Providing more concrete examples of the emergent “language” – showing which symbols agents use for specific image pairs – would enhance the reader’s understanding and appreciation of the results.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Briefly mention the computational resources required for training these agents.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  You mention that the informed sender reached higher coordination levels more quickly. Can you elaborate on *why* you believe this is the case? Is it solely due to the inductive bias towards dimension-by-dimension comparison, or are there other factors at play?
* The supervised learning phase introduces external knowledge (ImageNet labels).  How sensitive are your results to the quality and coverage of these labels?  Would using a different, potentially noisier, labeling source significantly impact the interpretability of the emergent language?
*  You observe a "metonymic" link between the learned symbols and the images in the human evaluation.  Could you speculate on *why* agents might develop this kind of indirect mapping?  Is it a byproduct of the limited vocabulary size, or does it reflect a more fundamental aspect of how communication emerges?
*  In the supervised phase, you share the embedding layer between the game and the classification task. Have you considered using separate embeddings and if so, how did that affect performance?



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of emergent communication. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions as outlined above.
