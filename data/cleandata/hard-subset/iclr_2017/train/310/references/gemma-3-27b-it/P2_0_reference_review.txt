## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** This paper introduces a research program investigating the emergence of communication in multi-agent reinforcement learning environments, specifically focusing on referential games. The authors aim to understand how agents can develop a language from scratch, and what environmental factors contribute to the development of interpretable “words” – both for other agents and, crucially, for humans. They explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and investigate the impact of grounding the emergent language through supervised learning on image labels. They demonstrate successful coordination, evidence of semantic clustering in the emergent language, and improved human interpretability through grounding.

**Decision:** **Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper tackles a fascinating and important problem in AI – the emergence of communication – and makes a solid contribution to the field. The experimental setup is well-designed, the results are compelling, and the grounding experiments are particularly insightful. While the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of the emergent languages and clearer presentation in certain sections, the overall quality and potential impact warrant acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the need for AI systems that can communicate effectively with humans and other agents. It positions the work well within the existing literature on multi-agent communication, grounded language learning, and the evolution of language. The framing with the opening quote from Ted Chiang effectively illustrates the core challenge.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate that agents *can* learn to coordinate successfully in their referential game, even starting from a tabula rasa state. The comparison between the “informed” and “agnostic” sender architectures provides valuable insights into the role of inductive biases in shaping the emergent communication. The human interpretability results, while not perfect, are highly encouraging and demonstrate the potential for grounding emergent language in human-understandable concepts.
* **Novelty & Significance:** The exploration of how to design environments to foster interpretable language is a significant contribution.  The addition of supervised learning to ground the emergent language and the resulting improvements in human interpretability is a novel and promising direction.
* **Rigorous Methodology:** The use of reinforcement learning, coupled with careful experimental design and quantitative evaluation (purity metrics, t-SNE visualizations, human evaluation), lends credibility to the findings.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Language Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates that *some* level of semantic clustering emerges, a more detailed linguistic analysis of the emergent “languages” would be beneficial.  For example, could the authors provide examples of common symbol sequences or patterns that arise?  Could they quantify the level of ambiguity in the language?  
* **Ablation Studies:** The impact of specific hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, temperature) on the performance and interpretability of the emergent language could be investigated through ablation studies.
* **Clarify Section 2:** The “General Framework” section (Section 2) feels a little abstract. While mathematically sound, it could be made more accessible with a more concrete example.
* **Presentation of Results:** Some of the figures (e.g., Figure 3) could benefit from more detailed captions and explanations.  Highlighting key observations within the figures would improve clarity.
* **Discuss Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the study would be valuable. For instance, the games are relatively simple, and the ImageNet images may introduce biases.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Emergent Language Examples:** Could you provide specific examples of how the agents use different symbols in the emergent language?  For example, for a specific pair of images, what symbol was used and what images tend to elicit that symbol?
2.  **Scalability:** Do you foresee any challenges scaling this approach to more complex environments or a larger number of agents? What modifications would be necessary?
3.  **Alternative Grounding Strategies:** Besides the supervised learning approach, did you explore other methods for grounding the emergent language in human concepts? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
4. **Human Evaluation Detail:** Could you provide more detail about the crowd-sourcing experiment and the subjects involved. For example, what demographics were represented in the participant pool?



In conclusion, this is a well-written and compelling paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of emergent communication. The authors have convincingly demonstrated that agents can learn to communicate effectively in a simple game environment and that this communication can be grounded in human concepts. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
