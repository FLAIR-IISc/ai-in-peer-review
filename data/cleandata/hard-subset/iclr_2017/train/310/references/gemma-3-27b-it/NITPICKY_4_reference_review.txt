This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent reinforcement learning setting, specifically focusing on referential games. The authors aim to design environments that foster the development of interpretable language, both for other agents and for humans. They explore two sender architectures—agnostic and informed—and evaluate the semantic properties of the emergent language by assessing conceptual clustering and human interpretability through a crowdsourced experiment. They also demonstrate improved grounding of the language via a supervised learning component.

**Reject.** While the research question is interesting and the overall framework is sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, issues – from imprecise language and lack of statistical rigor to inconsistent reporting and formatting errors – that collectively detract from its overall quality and credibility. A significantly more thorough revision addressing these details is required before this work would be suitable for publication at ICLR.

The core arguments supporting this rejection are: 1) insufficient statistical analysis to support claims of significance and 2) numerous minor methodological inconsistencies and imprecise descriptions that undermine confidence in the results.

Firstly, the statistical analysis is weak. While the authors mention significance, they primarily rely on qualitative observations and descriptive statements like “significantly above chance” without providing p-values, effect sizes, or confidence intervals. Table 1 reports purity values, but lacks any statistical comparison to baseline or control conditions. The claim of “significantly above chance” requires proper statistical justification. Similarly, the human interpretability results (68% accuracy) are presented without any baseline comparison or statistical test to determine if this performance is significantly better than random guessing (50%). This is crucial for establishing the efficacy of the grounding method. The authors also state that the “observed purity difference” increases, yet the magnitude and statistical significance of this difference are not quantified. 

Secondly, there are numerous minor inconsistencies and imprecise descriptions. For example, the number of filters in the informed sender is stated as 20 in the General Training Details section but appears different in Figure 1. The description of the “informed sender” architecture is unclear; the text mentions 1D convolutions, but the dimensionality of these convolutions is not consistently defined (2x1 is used, but then also “fx1”). The explanation of how the images are represented (VGG activations from the softmax or fc layer) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification. The reinforcement learning details are vague; the choice of 32 as batch size and 50k iterations feels ad-hoc without explanation or sensitivity analysis. The authors mention a "sampled" matrix for SVD analysis but do not detail the sampling procedure.  The statement about the supervised objective having "no negative effect on communication success" lacks a quantitative comparison. The authors should demonstrate that the performance in this setup is no worse than in setups without supervised learning.  The use of “intriguingly” feels unprofessional in a scientific paper. The formatting is also inconsistent; for example, different citation styles are used throughout the text.

Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):

*   **Clarify the experimental setup:** Provide more details on the image sampling process, the VGG layer choice, and the reinforcement learning hyperparameters. Explain the rationale behind these choices.
*   **Statistical Rigor:** Conduct rigorous statistical tests to support all claims of significance. Report p-values, effect sizes, and confidence intervals. Include baseline comparisons whenever possible.
*   **Architectural Details:** Provide precise descriptions of the neural network architectures, including layer sizes, activation functions, and initialization methods.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Consider performing ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the system, such as the informed sender architecture or the supervised learning component.
*   **Human Evaluation:** Increase the number of participants in the human evaluation and provide more detailed demographic information.
*   **Address Limitations:** Discuss the limitations of the study and potential avenues for future research.
*   **Proofread Carefully:** Thoroughly proofread the paper to eliminate grammatical errors, inconsistencies, and imprecise language.
* **Reference Style:** Ensure a consistent reference style throughout the paper.

Questions for the Authors:

1.  Can you provide the statistical justification (p-values, effect sizes) for the claim that the observed purity values are significantly above chance?
2.  What is the rationale for choosing a batch size of 32 and running the reinforcement learning for 50k iterations? Have you performed a sensitivity analysis to determine the robustness of your results to these hyperparameters?
3.  Can you clarify the dimensionality of the 1D convolutions used in the informed sender architecture and explain why a kernel size of "fx1" is used?
4.  Why were the softmax and fc layers considered for the VGG representation? What is the impact of using one versus the other?
5.  Could you provide a quantitative comparison demonstrating that the supervised learning component does not negatively impact communication success?
