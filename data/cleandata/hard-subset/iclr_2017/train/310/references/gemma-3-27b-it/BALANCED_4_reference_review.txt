This paper introduces a research program exploring the emergence of communication in multi-agent coordination games, aiming to understand how agents can develop a language portable to new situations and communication partners, including humans. The authors focus on a referential game where agents learn to associate symbols with objects, investigating whether agents successfully communicate and whether the emergent codes resemble human language in terms of conceptual properties and human interpretability. They experiment with different sender architectures and explore the impact of incorporating supervised learning for grounding the communication.

**Reject**

My primary reason for rejection is the lack of substantial novelty and limited empirical validation of claims regarding human interpretability. While the experimental setup is sound and the exploration of different sender architectures is valuable, the results presented do not convincingly demonstrate significant progress beyond existing work in emergent communication. The core findings—agents can learn to communicate and exhibit some degree of semantic grounding—are consistent with prior studies (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016). The human evaluation, while interesting, relies on a relatively small-scale crowdsourced experiment and lacks a strong baseline for comparison.

Further, while the authors claim a connection to linguistic theories (Lewis, Crawford, etc.), the paper does not deeply engage with these theories. The discussion remains at a high level and doesn't clearly articulate how the observed phenomena contribute to or challenge existing linguistic understandings of language emergence.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, and the motivation is sound. The experimental setup is well-defined. However, the empirical results do not yet justify the claims made about the significance of the findings, especially concerning the alignment of the emergent language with human intuition. The jump from "some evidence of semantic grounding" to "grounding agents' communication in human language" feels overstated.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of emergent communication in multi-agent games has been explored previously. The primary contribution appears to be the specific experimental setup and the exploration of informed vs. agnostic senders. While this is worthwhile, the improvements over existing baselines are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance, particularly given the extensive related work. The "informed sender" architecture offers a slight improvement, but the reasons for this improvement are not deeply investigated.
2. **Weak Human Evaluation:** The human evaluation is a crucial component of the paper's claims about grounding in human language. However, the methodology feels superficial. The success rate of 68% is encouraging, but without a control group (e.g., humans guessing randomly, humans evaluating symbols generated by a baseline model) it’s difficult to assess the true significance of this result. The description of the "metonymic" links is anecdotal and lacks quantitative support. It is unclear what features of the emergent language lead to the observed level of human interpretability.
3. **Insufficient Theoretical Engagement:** The paper references important work in linguistics and game theory, but doesn’t fully leverage these frameworks to analyze the results. A deeper discussion of how the emergent communication relates to concepts like compositionality, grounding, or the evolution of linguistic structure would strengthen the paper.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Baselines:** Include strong baselines for comparison. For example, compare the performance of the informed and agnostic senders to a randomly initialized sender or a sender trained with a different learning algorithm.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to determine the contribution of different components of the experimental setup. For example, how does the vocabulary size affect the emergent language? How does the choice of image representation (fc vs. sm) influence the results?
*   **Quantitative Analysis of Symbol Usage:** Provide a more detailed quantitative analysis of symbol usage. What types of concepts are associated with specific symbols? Is there evidence of compositional structure in the emergent language?
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Assess the robustness of the results. Do the agents continue to communicate effectively when the environment changes (e.g., different images, different vocabulary size)?
*   **Human Evaluation Refinement:**  Include a stronger baseline in the human evaluation (e.g., random guessing, symbols from a simple baseline model). Provide more detailed statistics on the human judgments (e.g., inter-rater reliability). Consider gathering qualitative feedback from the human participants to better understand their reasoning.
*   **Theoretical Discussion:** Expand the theoretical discussion. How do the results relate to existing theories of language emergence? Are there any unexpected findings that challenge these theories?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How do the emergent communication protocols differ qualitatively from those observed in Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016)? What specific design choices in your setup contribute to these differences?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the human evaluation data? What percentage of the time did humans correctly identify the referred object, and what were the most common errors?
3.  You mention "metonymic" links between symbols and images. Can you provide more examples of these links and explain how they emerge from the learning process?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameters used in the training process? Did you perform a hyperparameter search?
5.  What are the computational costs associated with training the agents, and how scalable is the approach to larger vocabulary sizes and more complex environments?
