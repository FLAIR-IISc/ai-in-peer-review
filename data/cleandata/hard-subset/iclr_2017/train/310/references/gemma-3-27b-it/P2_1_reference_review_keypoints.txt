- Paper introduces a program studying emergence of communication in multi-agent referential games and what environmental features yield interpretable languages.  
- Decision: Accept (with minor revisions).  
- Demonstrates agents can learn to coordinate from scratch and shows semantic structure in emergent symbols linked to object categories.  
- Presents a referential game using ImageNet features and compares agnostic vs. informed sender architectures across vocabulary sizes.  
- Supervised grounding experiment aligns emergent symbols with human-understandable labels, improving interpretability.  
- Strong connection to prior work in linguistics, game theory, and multi-agent RL.  
- Well-motivated problem framing, likened to language acquisition.  
- Sound methodology: simple effective environment, sensible use of pre-trained CNN features, and appropriate use of Reinforce.  
- Convincing analyses: symbol purity, SVD, and t-SNE visualizations support semantic structure claims.  
- Rigorous evaluation includes chance baselines for purity and human evaluation of grounded language.  
- Clear and well-written exposition of experiments and results.  
- Suggested ablations: study effects like embedding dimensionality on performance and interpretability.  
- Recommend discussing failure cases and consistently difficult image pairs or categories.  
- Ask for more quantitative analysis of grounding (confidence intervals, per-category variation) and reporting of computational resources for reproducibility.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to chosen ImageNet subset, examples/features behind proposed metonymic links, exploiting SVD-revealed redundancy for compression/efficiency, and choice/tuning and sensitivity of temperature parameter τ.
