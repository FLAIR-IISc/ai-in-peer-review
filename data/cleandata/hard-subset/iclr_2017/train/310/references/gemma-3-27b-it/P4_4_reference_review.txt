This paper introduces a multi-agent coordination communication game to investigate the emergence of language, with a focus on creating languages interpretable by humans. The authors explore the impact of different sender architectures and environmental modifications on the properties of the developed communication protocols, demonstrating successful coordination and, with supervised learning, a degree of alignment with human semantic intuition.

**Reject** with a primary concern being a lack of sufficient novelty and a somewhat superficial exploration of the research questions. While the work is well-motivated and technically sound, the contributions feel incremental rather than groundbreaking.

The paper builds upon established ideas in the field of emergent communication (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) and Lewis signaling games. The primary contribution – combining the game-based approach with supervised learning for grounding – is a sensible direction, but the results, while positive, don’t represent a substantial leap forward. The supervised learning component feels almost as a “bolt-on” and the experiments assessing human interpretability, while showing promise (68% accuracy), lack detailed analysis regarding *what* aspects of the learned language are interpretable and *why*. Simply demonstrating that humans can guess the meaning of symbols better than chance isn't particularly illuminating. The authors acknowledge this to some extent discussing metonymic links, but a more rigorous analysis of the emergent semantics is required. Furthermore, the impact of architectural choices (agnostic vs. informed sender) is presented, but the reasons *why* the informed sender performs better remain largely unexplored beyond stating an inductive bias. The observation that even the agnostic sender develops a “living vs. non-living” distinction is interesting, but the paper doesn’t delve into the underlying mechanisms responsible for this. The purity metric, while used, feels somewhat crude and doesn’t fully capture the nuance of the emergent semantic structure.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Novelty:** The connection to Lewis's signaling game is made, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how it *extends* or significantly departs from this established framework. A stronger statement of the unique contributions is needed.
*   **Human Evaluation:** The human evaluation section would benefit from more detailed qualitative analysis. Instead of just reporting a percentage, provide examples of successful and failed interpretations, and discuss potential reasons for these outcomes. What types of images/symbols are most easily interpretable by humans? Are there systematic errors in human interpretation?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Consider more focused ablation studies. For example, what happens if you remove the supervised learning component entirely? How does the vocabulary size impact interpretability?
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper briefly mentions game-theoretic considerations (Nash equilibria), but this aspect is largely unexplored. A more thorough discussion of the game-theoretic properties of the environment and the learned communication protocols would strengthen the work.
*   **Quantitative Metrics:** Explore more sophisticated metrics for evaluating the semantic properties of the emergent language. Beyond purity, consider measures of distributional similarity or semantic coherence.
*   **Generalization:** The use of ImageNet is a positive aspect, but the generality of the findings could be improved. Would the same results hold with a different dataset or a different set of object categories?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper states that the supervised learning component "does not aim at improving agents’ coordination performance." Is this definitively true? Did you observe any changes in coordination speed or efficiency after introducing supervised learning, even if not the primary goal?
2.  Can you provide more examples of the "metonymic" links observed in the human evaluation, and explain how these links might arise from the training process?
3.  What specific aspects of the "informed sender" architecture contribute to its superior performance compared to the "agnostic sender?" Is it simply the convolutional layers, or are there other factors at play?
4.  The paper mentions potential redundancy in the informed sender’s vocabulary. How does this redundancy affect the robustness of the communication protocol?
5.  The phrase "natural-language-endowed sender" appears. What specifically constitutes a "natural-language-endowed" sender and how would you quantitatively measure this property?
