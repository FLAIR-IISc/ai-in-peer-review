This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent reinforcement learning setting, specifically focusing on referential games. The authors aim to understand how agents can develop a language from scratch, and whether features of the environment can encourage the development of more interpretable “languages” – both for other agents and for humans. They explore the impact of sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and modifications to the game setup (class-level properties vs. image-level details) and demonstrate a path towards grounding the emergent communication in human language through a combined interactive and supervised learning approach.

**Reject.** While the research direction is interesting and the initial results are promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet significant, flaws in execution and presentation. The sheer number of these issues raises concerns about the rigor of the experimental methodology and the validity of the conclusions.

The supporting arguments for this rejection are as follows:

1. **Lack of Statistical Rigor & Reporting:** The paper frequently states “significantly above chance” without providing statistical tests or p-values. For instance, the purity analysis in Section 4 lacks crucial statistical backing. Claims of improvement due to modifications to the game need to be substantiated with appropriate statistical tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA). Tables 1 & 2 are missing error bars/standard deviations, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of observed differences.
2. **Ambiguous Experimental Details & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Numerous hyperparameters are "set without tuning." While acknowledged, the absence of sensitivity analysis regarding these parameters (embedding dimensionality, filter numbers, temperature, batch size, iteration count) is a serious oversight. A change in any of these could drastically alter results. The description of the "informed sender" architecture is somewhat vague, and a clearer explanation of the convolution operation is needed.
3. **Inconsistent Notation & Terminology:** There are instances of inconsistent terminology. For example, the term “purity” is used without a clear, formal definition until Section 4. There's a confusing mix of referring to "agents" and "players" without consistent differentiation.  The use of θ to represent both the parameters and the input is confusing.
4. **Overstated Conclusions & Interpretation:** The claims of human interpretability, particularly regarding the metonymic links discovered in the supervised learning phase, are somewhat overstated. Showing examples in Figure 4 is insufficient evidence. A more rigorous user study with a larger participant pool and standardized evaluation metrics is needed.  The conclusion that the agents are developing a “natural language” is premature. They are developing a *communication protocol*, which exhibits some superficial similarities to natural language, but lacks the complexity and nuance.
5. **Minor Formatting & Typos:** Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor formatting inconsistencies, typos, and grammatical errors. For example, the references section is not formatted consistently, some sentences are awkwardly phrased, and there are missing punctuation marks. These issues, while individually small, contribute to the impression of carelessness.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

* **Provide complete statistical analysis:** Include p-values, effect sizes, and confidence intervals for all comparisons.
* **Conduct a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis:** Demonstrate the robustness of the results by varying key hyperparameters.
* **Clarify the experimental setup:** Provide a more detailed and precise description of the game environment, agent architectures, and training procedure.
* **Refine the terminology and notation:** Ensure consistency throughout the paper.
* **Moderate the claims:** Avoid overstating the conclusions and be more cautious in interpreting the results.
* **Proofread carefully:** Eliminate all typos, grammatical errors, and formatting inconsistencies.
* **Explain the choice of ImageNet:** Why ImageNet? Are there any biases inherent in this dataset that could influence the results?

Questions for the authors:

* What specific steps were taken to ensure the reproducibility of the results?
* Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the convolution operation in the informed sender architecture?
* What is the rationale behind choosing the specific hyperparameters used in the experiment?
* What metrics were used to evaluate the quality of the SVD decomposition in Figure 2?
* How sensitive are the results to the choice of the t-SNE parameters?
* Can you provide a more quantitative assessment of the human interpretability, beyond the qualitative examples in Figure 4?
