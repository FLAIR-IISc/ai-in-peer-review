- Paper studies emergence of communication in multi-agent referential games, focusing on what makes emergent language “natural” and human-interpretable.
- Explores sender architectures (agnostic vs informed), vocabulary sizes, and introduces combined RL + supervised image-classification grounding.
- Shows successful agent coordination, semantic grouping in symbols, and improved human interpretability via supervised grounding.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Strong motivation: emphasizes functional, interactive aspects of language for real-world AI agents.
- Novel contributions: informed sender architecture, RL-supervised grounding method, and experiments manipulating common knowledge.
- Rigorous evaluation: coordination metrics, semantic purity/clustering, t-SNE visualizations, and human evaluation reporting 68% accuracy.
- Well-situated in related work, including connections to Lewis’ signaling game and prior emergent-communication literature.
- Limitation: human evaluation uses a relatively small image set and participant pool — needs broader evaluation.
- Limitation: potential biases from using ImageNet images should be discussed.
- Suggestion: include ablation studies on amount/noise of supervised data to quantify grounding impact.
- Suggestion: analyze communication failure cases to understand limitations and failure modes.
- Suggestion: report computational resources and training cost for reproducibility and scalability assessment.
- Question: clarify intuition for the informed sender’s dimension-by-dimension convolutional comparison and why it aids grounding.
- Question: provide CrowdFlower participant demographics and any filtering/qualification used in the human study.
- Question: quantify relationship between vocabulary size and semantic purity and whether larger vocabularies hit diminishing returns.
- Question: discuss challenges and limitations when scaling the approach to more agents, richer protocols, and more abstract concepts.
