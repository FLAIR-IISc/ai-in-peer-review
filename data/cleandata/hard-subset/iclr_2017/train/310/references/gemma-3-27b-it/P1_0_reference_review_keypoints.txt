- Paper studies emergence of communication in multi-agent referential games and factors affecting human interpretability.  
- Introduces a framework where two agents coordinate by communicating about images, with experiments on grounding via supervised image labeling.  
- Results show agents can learn to communicate from scratch, with semantic clustering aligned to object categories and improved human interpretability after supervision.  
- Decision: Accept (with minor revisions).  
- Strong motivation and thorough literature review, grounded in signaling game theory.  
- Well-defined experimental setup using ImageNet, McRae, VGG, and controlled sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed).  
- Convincing empirical results: robust coordination, purity-based semantic clustering, and 68% human success on novel image pairs.  
- Grounding via supervised learning notably increases interpretability and alignment with human labels.  
- Clear presentation with effective figures and tables.  
- Suggest more ablation studies to isolate effects of specific design choices (e.g., informed sender conv filters).  
- Request deeper qualitative analysis of failures where humans misinterpret symbols, with example image pairs.  
- Raise scalability concerns: effects of larger vocabularies, computational complexity, and potential bottlenecks.  
- Recommend further analysis of linguistic properties such as compositionality and systematicity.  
- Ask authors to elaborate on rationale and impact of informed sender convolutional filters and their contribution to performance.  
- Ask whether human interpretations are biased by ImageNet labels during supervised grounding and whether humans project learned labels outside training distribution.  
- Ask how training time scales with vocabulary size and what techniques might mitigate computational costs.  
- Request more examples and discussion of metonymic word usage and whether it emerged spontaneously or from supervision.
