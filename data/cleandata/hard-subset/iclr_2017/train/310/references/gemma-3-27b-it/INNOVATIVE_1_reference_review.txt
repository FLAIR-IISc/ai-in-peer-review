This paper introduces a novel framework for studying the emergence of communication through multi-agent coordination games, aiming to develop agents capable of portable and human-interpretable language. The authors posit that learning language requires interaction, not just observation of existing conversations, and design a referential game where agents bootstrap communication from scratch. They explore factors influencing language development, like environmental features and agent architectures, and demonstrate initial success in aligning emergent language with human semantic intuitions through a clever combination of game-playing and supervised learning.

**Reject.** While the research direction is highly promising and the paper tackles a fundamentally important problem with a bold and innovative approach, the current presentation feels significantly underdeveloped, lacking the necessary depth of analysis to confidently support its claims. The results, while positive, are presented somewhat superficially and leave many critical questions unanswered.

The core strength of this work lies in its conceptual novelty. The framing of language emergence as a multi-agent coordination problem, inspired by linguistic and game-theoretic foundations, is compelling. The proposed game setup is a well-motivated starting point, and the exploration of informed vs. agnostic senders provides initial insights into architectural influences. The grounding attempt via combined game-playing and supervised learning is particularly intriguing. However, the experimental results, while demonstrating coordination, feel insufficiently explored. 

Specifically:

* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The comparison between informed and agnostic senders is a good start, but further ablation studies are needed. What specific components of the informed sender are crucial for improved performance and interpretability? The convolutional filters seem key, but this isn’t rigorously demonstrated.
* **Superficial Purity Analysis:** While reporting purity scores is valuable, the analysis lacks nuance. A higher purity doesn’t automatically equate to “natural” language. It’s crucial to understand *which* categories are clustered together and whether those groupings make semantic sense beyond chance. A simple example of what semantic groups emerge would bolster the claims.
* **Human Evaluation Depth:** The human evaluation, while a significant step, is presented with limited detail. What were the specific instructions given to the crowdworkers? Were there controls for potential biases? Providing examples of successful/failed human interpretations would make the results more convincing. The “metonymic” examples are compelling, but more structured analysis of such cases is required.
* **Convergence Analysis:** The paper states agents converge quickly, but the learning curves (Figure 2) are difficult to interpret. The y-axis isn’t clearly defined, and the rapid initial rise followed by plateau makes it hard to assess stability. More detailed convergence plots (e.g., reward curves, loss curves) would be helpful.
* **Theoretical Connection:** The connection to Lewis' signaling game and cheap talk theory feels somewhat underdeveloped. The paper mentions the existence of multiple Nash equilibria, but doesn't discuss how the observed emergent language relates to these theoretical possibilities. Addressing this would add significant theoretical depth.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify the Reward Function:** The reward function is simple (win/loss), but its impact on the emergent language isn't explicitly discussed. How might different reward structures influence the type of communication that emerges?
* **Explore Different Vocabulary Sizes:** The choice of vocabulary sizes (10 and 100) seems arbitrary. How does performance scale with even larger vocabularies?
* **Consider Multi-Step Communication:** Extending the game to allow for multi-step communication could lead to more complex and nuanced language.
* **Visualizations:** More visualizations of the emergent symbol representations and their relationships to image features would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide more detailed examples of the semantic groupings formed by the agents, beyond the purity scores? Specifically, what categories are consistently clustered together?
* What specific design choices in the informed sender architecture contributed most significantly to the improvement in interpretability?
* Can you elaborate on the instructions provided to the crowdworkers during the human evaluation, and what measures were taken to mitigate potential biases?
* How do the observed emergent languages relate to the different Nash equilibria predicted by cheap talk theory?
* What are the computational costs associated with scaling up this framework to larger and more complex environments?
