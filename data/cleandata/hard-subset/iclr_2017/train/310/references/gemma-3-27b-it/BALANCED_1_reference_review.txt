This paper introduces a research program investigating the emergence of language in multi-agent coordination games. The authors aim to understand how environments can be designed to foster the development of interpretable language, starting with a referential game where agents learn to communicate about visual objects. They explore the impact of sender architecture (agnostic vs. informed) and the inclusion of supervised learning on language interpretability, demonstrating successful coordination and encouraging semantic alignment with human concepts.

**Reject.** While the research direction is interesting and the experiments are well-executed, the paper lacks a compelling novel contribution to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core idea – using games to study emergent communication – is not new (citing Sukhbaatar et al. 2016 and Foerster et al. 2016), and the presented improvements (informed sender, supervised learning) feel incremental rather than transformative. The improvements in interpretability are demonstrated but not rigorously quantified beyond purity scores and a small-scale human evaluation. Furthermore, the connection to deep learning, a core tenet of ICLR, feels somewhat tenuous. The architectures used are relatively simple feedforward networks, and the focus is more on game design and linguistic insights than on novel deep learning techniques.

The arguments supporting this decision are as follows:

1. **Limited Novelty:** The paper builds upon existing work in emergent communication and does not present a fundamentally new approach or algorithm. The "informed sender" architecture, while showing improvement, is a relatively straightforward extension to existing network architectures. The supervised learning component is a well-established technique and its application here, while helpful, doesn't represent a significant advance.
2. **Weak Quantitative Evaluation of Interpretability:** While the authors demonstrate that the learned language exhibits some semantic structure (through purity scores and the human evaluation), the quantitative measures are limited. Purity scores, while helpful, can be misleading, and the human evaluation is conducted on a small scale (limited number of pairs and participants). A more rigorous evaluation of interpretability, perhaps using more sophisticated metrics or a larger-scale human study, is needed to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.
3. **Insufficient Connection to Core ICLR Themes:** ICLR emphasizes novel deep learning techniques. This paper's primary contribution lies in experimental setup and analysis of emergent communication in a game-theoretic setting. The neural network architectures used are relatively simple and do not showcase significant advancements in deep learning methodology.

Additional feedback, intended to help improve the paper:

*   **Expand the analysis of the emergent language:** Perform a more detailed analysis of the learned symbols. Are there clusters of images that consistently activate the same symbols? Can these clusters be meaningfully interpreted?  A more in-depth analysis of the symbol space would be valuable.
*   **Rigorous Quantification of Interpretability:** Conduct a larger-scale human evaluation with more diverse participants and a wider range of image pairs. Consider using metrics beyond accuracy, such as precision and recall, to better assess the quality of the human judgments. Explore automatic evaluation metrics beyond purity.
*   **Investigate the role of vocabulary size:** The results show a trade-off between vocabulary size and interpretability.  Perform a more systematic exploration of this trade-off. How does the optimal vocabulary size vary with the complexity of the environment and the task?
*   **Explore different neural network architectures:** Experiment with more sophisticated neural network architectures, such as recurrent neural networks or transformers, to see if they can lead to more complex and interpretable languages.  This could strengthen the connection to ICLR's focus on deep learning.
*   **Relate to broader linguistic theories:**  The discussion section briefly mentions linguistic theories.  A more thorough integration of these theories could help to frame the results and provide a deeper understanding of the emergent language.

Questions for the authors:

*   The human evaluation focuses on grounding existing labels to symbols. What happens if you ask humans to *guess* the intended meaning of a symbol without providing a set of predefined labels? Would they still be able to identify the underlying concepts?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and the embedding dimensionality? Have you performed a sensitivity analysis?
*   Could you provide more detail on the limitations of the current experimental setup and potential avenues for future research?
*   You mention the goal of developing practical AI. How do you envision scaling this approach to more complex environments and tasks? What are the major challenges that need to be addressed?
