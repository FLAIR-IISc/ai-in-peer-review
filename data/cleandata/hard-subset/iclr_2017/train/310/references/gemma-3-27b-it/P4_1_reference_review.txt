This paper investigates the emergence of communication in multi-agent reinforcement learning environments, specifically referential games, with the goal of understanding how to design environments that foster the development of interpretable language, even for human communication. The authors explore the impact of sender architecture (agnostic vs. informed) and environmental modifications (class-level properties vs. low-level image details) on the resulting communication protocols. They also demonstrate a method for grounding the emergent language through a combined game-playing and supervised learning approach.

**Reject** with a primary reason being a lack of sufficient novelty and depth of analysis to justify publication at ICLR. While the work is technically sound and well-executed, it largely confirms expected behaviors and builds incrementally on existing literature without presenting a truly novel contribution or a particularly insightful analysis. A secondary reason is the superficial grounding experiment, which, despite showing promising results, lacks a rigorous evaluation of the quality of the “metonymic” links and relies heavily on qualitative observations.

The approach is well-motivated, building upon a solid foundation of work in multi-agent reinforcement learning, communication games, and linguistic theory. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging related efforts from Sukhbaatar et al. (2016), Foerster et al. (2016), and others. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the use of ImageNet and pre-trained CNNs provides a reasonable basis for evaluating the emergent communication. The Reinforce algorithm is appropriately applied, and the reported results demonstrate that agents can indeed learn to coordinate and develop interpretable communication protocols. The introduction of the "informed sender" architecture is a sensible modification aimed at improving interpretability, and the subsequent experiments exploring class-level properties further contribute to understanding environmental influences.

However, the core findings – that informed senders perform better, and that using class-level information improves semantic alignment – are largely intuitive and expected. The analysis, while thorough, primarily consists of descriptive statistics and visualizations (e.g., purity scores, SVD spectra, t-SNE plots). There's a limited attempt to provide theoretical justification for the observed phenomena or to explore the limitations of the approach. The grounding experiment, while interesting, relies heavily on a crowdsourced survey and lacks a more quantitative assessment of the quality of the metonymic associations.  The authors report a 68% success rate in the human evaluation, but lack a comparative baseline (e.g., random guessing with a prior over ImageNet classes) to properly assess this performance. The reported 8% error rate feels high, and is not fully discussed.  Furthermore, the phrase "metonymic link" is used to explain the surprising success, but there is little exploration of the *types* of metonymies formed, and whether the agents are discovering any meaningful abstractions.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation studies:** Conduct more targeted ablation studies to isolate the impact of specific architectural choices or hyperparameters.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Explore potential theoretical connections between the game-theoretic framework (Lewis' signaling game) and the observed emergent behaviors. Can the authors predict the type of language that will emerge based on game parameters?
*   **Robustness:** Investigate the robustness of the emergent language to variations in the environment or communication noise.
*   **Human Evaluation Protocol:** Design a more rigorous human evaluation protocol for the grounding experiment, potentially including a more detailed annotation scheme and a comparative baseline. Consider A/B testing different grounding strategies.
*   **Scalability:** Discuss the potential scalability of this approach to more complex environments and communication scenarios.
*   **Diversity of Language:** Explore the diversity of languages that emerge from different initializations or game configurations.
*   **Explainability:** Beyond purity scores, investigate whether there are more interpretable features of the learned embeddings that correlate with semantic meaning.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  The informed sender consistently outperforms the agnostic sender. How sensitive are these results to the kernel size used in the 1D convolutions? Have you explored other inductive biases for the sender architecture?
2.  You mention that the supervised learning phase “should teach them how to effectively use this grounding.” Can you provide more details on the rationale behind this claim? What specific properties of the supervised task are expected to facilitate grounding?
3.  In the grounding experiment, you state that in 68% of cases, subjects guessed the correct image. What was the baseline performance (e.g., random guessing) for this evaluation?
4.  Can you provide examples of the types of metonymic links that emerged during the grounding experiment? Are there any patterns or commonalities in these associations?
5.  How does the vocabulary size affect the performance and interpretability of the emergent language? Have you explored vocabulary sizes beyond 10 and 100?
