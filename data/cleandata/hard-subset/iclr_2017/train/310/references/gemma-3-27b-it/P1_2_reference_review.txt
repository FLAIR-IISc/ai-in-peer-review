## ICLR 2024 Review: Emergent Communication in Multi-Agent Coordination Games

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent reinforcement learning setting. The authors present a referential game where two agents, starting as "tabula rasa", must learn to communicate to coordinate on identifying target images. The core contribution lies in exploring how the design of the environment (specifically, the level of abstraction in the input – raw pixel features vs. category-level features) impacts the characteristics of the emergent language, focusing on whether it aligns with human-interpretable semantics. They demonstrate successful coordination, evidence of conceptual grounding in the learned symbols, and a pathway to grounding the language further through a combined interactive and supervised learning approach. The authors connect their work to relevant literature in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.

This paper presents a solid and well-motivated contribution to the growing field of emergent communication. The experiments are clearly designed and executed, and the results provide valuable insights into the factors influencing the development of interpretable communication protocols. The grounding experiment with human interpretability is particularly compelling and significantly strengthens the work.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Well-Defined Problem & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of building agents that can communicate effectively with humans and positions it within the broader context of AI and multi-agent systems. The framing using the opening anecdote from Ted Chiang’s “Story of Your Life” is effective in highlighting the need for interactive communication beyond simple pattern recognition.
* **Strong Experimental Design:** The referential game setup is relatively simple but allows for a controlled exploration of the factors affecting language emergence. The comparison of ‘agnostic’ and ‘informed’ sender architectures, along with the manipulation of input features (raw vs. category-level), provides valuable comparative data. The use of ImageNet and pre-trained CNNs is sensible and practical.
* **Robust Results:** The agents consistently learn to coordinate, demonstrating the viability of the framework. The results showing alignment with conceptual categories (via purity scores) and, crucially, human interpretability are strong evidence supporting the authors’ claims.
* **Connection to Relevant Literature:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the related work in areas like multi-agent systems, linguistics, and game theory. They successfully position their contributions within these fields.
* **Novelty:** The focus on environment design to promote interpretability is a significant contribution. While emergent communication has been studied before, the attempt to actively shape the emergent language towards human-understandable concepts is a notable advancement.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarify the RL Algorithm:** While the paper mentions using the Reinforce rule, a more detailed explanation of the RL algorithm used (e.g., specific hyperparameters beyond batch size and iterations, exploration strategy) would be beneficial.
* **Ablation Studies:** A more comprehensive ablation study on the supervised learning component might be helpful. For example, what happens if the supervised learning is done on a *different* dataset than the one used for the referential game? How does the amount of supervised data affect performance?
* **Qualitative Analysis:** While quantitative results are strong, more detailed qualitative examples of the emergent language (e.g., showing examples of images consistently associated with specific symbols) would enhance the paper.  Show more examples like the dolphin/fence example from the end.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of the vocabulary size and the limited complexity of the game.  Expanding on these limitations and discussing potential avenues for future work to address them would strengthen the conclusion.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  You mention the informed sender converges faster and uses more symbols. Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of *why* the informed sender performs better? Is it simply the inductive bias towards dimension-by-dimension comparison, or is there something more nuanced happening?
*  The human evaluation on the ReferItGame data shows promising results. Did you notice any systematic biases or errors in the human judgments? Were there certain types of symbols or image pairs where humans consistently struggled?
*  You allude to potential "metonymic" links between words and images. Could you elaborate on this observation? Did you observe any recurring patterns in these metonymic associations?
*  Given the connection to Lewis’ signaling game and the potential for multiple Nash equilibria, did you explore different initializations or learning dynamics to see if the system converges to different language structures?



Overall, this is a well-written and impactful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The acceptance is recommended, contingent on addressing the minor feedback provided.
