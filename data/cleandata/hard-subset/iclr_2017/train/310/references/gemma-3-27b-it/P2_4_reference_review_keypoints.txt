- Paper investigates emergent communication in a two-player referential game, exploring architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and grounding to learn human-interpretable languages from scratch.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Novelty/Importance: Tackles emergent communication using game-theoretic learning rather than preprogrammed protocols or large supervised datasets.  
- Strong empirical results: Agents achieve robust coordination and learned protocols show semantic structure aligned with human categories and partial human interpretability, especially with supervised grounding.  
- Experimental design: Referential game isolates grounding; comparison of sender architectures and common-knowledge removal are insightful probes.  
- Evidence: Quantitative (high coordination, purity > chance) and qualitative (interpretable symbol-object mappings) results support claims; grounding improves interpretability.  
- Theory connection: Good linkage to Lewis signaling games and equilibrium analysis; common-knowledge removal is a clever robustness check.  
- Limitations acknowledged: Imperfect human interpretability and dependence on ImageNet; future work suggested.  
- Suggested improvements: Deeper analysis of failed cases and misinterpretations; test robustness to noise/distractors; discuss scalability and computational cost; clarify what is removed under “common knowledge.”  
- Questions for authors: Provide crowdsourcing details (instructions, participant confidence, qualitative feedback); explain redundancy/synonymy in informed sender vocabulary and how ambiguities are resolved; report qualitative effects of vocabulary size and feasibility of much larger vocabularies; show generalization to new images/concepts.  
- Overall: Strong, significant contribution likely to inspire further research; recommended for acceptance.
