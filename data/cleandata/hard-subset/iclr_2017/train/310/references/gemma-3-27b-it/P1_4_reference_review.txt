## Review of “Learning to Communicate”

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel investigation into the emergence of communication between artificial agents in a multi-agent coordination game. The authors posit that learning to communicate effectively requires more than simply pattern recognition from existing dialogues; it necessitates a dynamic interaction where agents bootstrap knowledge from each other. They introduce a referential game where two agents must learn to refer to objects, and analyze the resulting “language” both in terms of its ability to facilitate coordination and its alignment with human conceptual categories and interpretability. The authors explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and investigate the influence of environmental factors, such as class-level information, on language development.  Crucially, they demonstrate a path toward grounding emergent communication in human language via a combined game-playing and supervised learning approach.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a strong and well-motivated contribution to the field of multi-agent communication. The experimental setup is thoughtfully designed, the results are compelling, and the grounding experiments offer a promising direction for future research.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of purely supervised learning approaches to communication and convincingly motivates the need for interactive, emergent communication paradigms. The connection to linguistic and cognitive science principles (Chiang’s quote, references to Lewis, Austin, Clark, etc.) strengthens the intellectual foundation of the work.
* **Sound Experimental Design:** The referential game is a simple yet effective environment for studying the core principles of communication. The use of ImageNet provides realistic visual input, and the exploration of different sender architectures and environmental manipulations (class-level information) allows for a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing language emergence.
* **Compelling Results:** The findings that agents *can* learn to coordinate effectively, that the “informed” sender architecture promotes more diverse and potentially interpretable communication, and that adding a supervised learning component dramatically improves grounding in human language are all significant. The quantitative results (coordination rates, purity scores, human interpretability scores) are backed up with qualitative observations and insightful analysis.
* **Strong Related Work & Positioning:** The authors thoroughly and accurately situate their work within the broader landscape of multi-agent communication, supervised learning, and linguistic theory, clearly highlighting their contributions and differentiating their approach.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

These are suggestions to further strengthen the paper, but do not impact the acceptance decision:

* **Elaborate on the Reinforce implementation:** While the authors mention Reinforce, a brief description of the baseline and exploration strategy used would be helpful. How was the learning rate adjusted, or what steps were taken to avoid getting stuck in local optima?
* **Deeper Analysis of the "Informed" Sender:**  The paper demonstrates that the "informed" sender is superior, but more detailed ablation studies could be performed to pinpoint the specific benefit of the convolutional filters. For example, comparing to a sender with similarly complex, but randomly initialized filters might provide further insight.
* **Visualization of Learned Embeddings:** Visualizing the embeddings learned by the sender and receiver networks (e.g., using t-SNE or UMAP) could provide a more intuitive understanding of how the agents are representing the images and symbols.
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the overall results are positive, discussing examples of failures and potential limitations of the approach would enhance the paper's credibility.  What kinds of image pairs are particularly challenging?  Under what conditions does coordination break down?



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Regarding the convergence question mentioned in the introduction (regarding Nash equilibria):**  Did you observe any evidence of multiple equilibrium points emerging during training?  Were there instances where agents initially converged to suboptimal strategies before finding a more effective communication protocol?
2.  **Concerning the shared embedding layer in the supervised learning experiment:**  Was there any concern that the supervised task might “overwrite” the previously learned communication protocol?  Did you explore alternative architectures that would allow for a more gradual transfer of knowledge from the supervised task to the game-playing environment?
3.  **On the human interpretability experiments:** The 68% success rate in human guessing is encouraging.  Could you provide more detail on the distribution of accuracy across different symbols? Were some symbols far more interpretable than others?  Was there a correlation between the frequency of symbol usage and its interpretability?
4.  **Regarding the scalability of this approach:** The current experiments are limited to two agents and a relatively small vocabulary. What are the key challenges to scaling this framework to more complex scenarios with multiple agents and a larger, more diverse vocabulary?



In conclusion, this is a well-written, insightful, and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the growing field of emergent communication in AI. I strongly recommend it for acceptance.
