- Paper studies emergent communication in a referential game, testing whether agents can coordinate without pre-programmed protocols and if the emergent language shows conceptual grounding and interpretability.  
- Main finding: informed sender learns more interpretable symbols tied to broader object categories; supervised grounding further improves human interpretability.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — solid contribution with well-designed experiments and insightful results.  
- Strong motivation: addresses limits of supervised approaches and argues for functional, coordinated communication.  
- Experimental design: controlled referential game using ImageNet, comparisons of sender architectures, and manipulations of vocabulary size and supervision.  
- Results: robust convergence to successful communication across configurations; purity metrics and human studies support claims.  
- Literature grounding: situates work in multi-agent systems, linguistics, and game theory (e.g., Lewis' signaling game).  
- Request to clarify randomness: report variance across random seeds and robustness to sampling.  
- Ask for deeper failure analysis: when and why agents fail and which image pairs/concepts cause miscommunication.  
- Suggest more qualitative examples and visualizations of learned symbols and embeddings for intuitive insight.  
- Suggest reporting computational cost (training time, hardware) for completeness.  
- Recommend exploring other supervised tasks beyond object naming to further improve grounding and interpretability.
