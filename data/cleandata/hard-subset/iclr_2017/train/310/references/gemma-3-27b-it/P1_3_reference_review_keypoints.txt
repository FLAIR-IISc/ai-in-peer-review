- Paper studies emergence of communication in a referential multi-agent game, testing if agents develop human-like, semantically grounded languages.  
- Authors compare sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and add supervised grounding to improve interpretability.  
- Main findings: successful agent coordination, evidence of semantic categorization, and improved human-interpretability with supervised grounding (humans achieve ~68% accuracy).  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Strength: clear problem statement and motivation linking emergent communication to human–AI cooperation.  
- Strength: sound methodology using a Lewis-style signaling game, ImageNet inputs, varied vocabularies, and REINFORCE training.  
- Strength: convincing quantitative results (purity metrics) and strong human evaluation supporting interpretability claims.  
- Strength: thorough literature review situating the work in multi-agent and emergent communication research.  
- Suggested improvement: perform ablation studies on supervised signal strength (fewer/noisy labels) to quantify supervision effects.  
- Suggested improvement: provide more qualitative examples of symbol-to-category mappings and visualize symbol embedding spaces.  
- Suggested improvement: expand discussion of limitations (game complexity, vocabulary restrictions, human-in-the-loop scalability).  
- Suggested improvement: deepen connection to linguistic theories and analyze phenomena like metonymy observed in human evaluations.  
- Q1: Is the informed sender’s faster/better performance consistent across random seeds, and which architectural features drive it (e.g., convolutional filters)?  
- Q2: Provide details on human evaluators (demographics, native language, ImageNet familiarity) and quality-control measures for the 68% result.  
- Q3: Why switch between game and supervised learning equiprobably, and how do different mixing ratios affect outcomes?  
- Q4: Any practical limits observed for vocabulary size beyond 100, and does interpretability saturate or improve with larger vocabularies?
