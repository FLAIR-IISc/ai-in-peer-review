This paper introduces a research program investigating the emergence of communication in multi-agent coordination games. The authors aim to design environments that foster the development of a language portable to new situations and communication partners, specifically humans. They focus on referential games where agents learn to refer to objects, assessing whether agents can successfully communicate and whether emergent codes resemble human language – specifically, whether they align with conceptual properties and are interpretable by humans. The paper presents experiments with feed-forward networks in a referential game, demonstrating successful coordination and some alignment with object categories and human intuition, particularly when incorporating supervised learning for object naming.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting research direction and preliminary results, the empirical contributions feel limited and the connection to human language, while promising, is not convincingly demonstrated. The results, particularly the human evaluation, are framed cautiously, hinting at the potential rather than robust achievement of the stated goals.

The primary weakness lies in the limited scope of the empirical evaluation. The human evaluation, while innovative, relies on a relatively small number of image pairs and subjects, making it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the interpretability of the emergent language. The 68% agreement rate, while statistically significant, feels insufficient to claim substantial alignment with human language. The qualitative examples provided are compelling, but anecdotal. A more rigorous evaluation, potentially with a larger and more diverse set of images and human participants, is needed. 

Furthermore, the novelty of the approach, while present, is not fully emphasized. The connection to Lewis’ signaling game and related work is well-established, but the authors don’t sufficiently articulate *how* their approach moves beyond existing literature, beyond the focus on human interpretability. The modifications to the environment (e.g., using class-level properties) are incremental and their impact, while observed, isn't deeply analyzed in terms of the underlying mechanisms driving the observed improvements. The supervised learning component, while helpful, feels almost tacked on; the interaction between game-based learning and supervised grounding deserves more investigation. The choice of specific architectures (informed vs. agnostic sender) isn’t fully justified beyond the intuition provided.

Finally, the analysis of the “spectrum” of symbol usage (Figure 2, right) is difficult to interpret without more context. While the authors mention redundancy, the significance of the dimensionality required to summarize the language isn't clearly explained. Is a 50-dimensional representation “good” or “bad”? How does this compare to representations learned in other contexts?

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Human Evaluation:** Expand the human evaluation. Consider including a control condition where humans are shown the images and asked to provide labels directly, to establish a baseline for comparison. Explore different evaluation metrics beyond simple agreement (e.g., precision/recall).
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the system (e.g., the informed sender architecture, the supervised learning phase).
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Consider a more formal analysis of the game-theoretic properties of the environment. Can the authors provide guarantees about convergence or the types of equilibria that are likely to emerge?
*   **Visualization:** Provide more informative visualizations of the emergent language. Could the authors visualize the embedding space used by the agents? How do the embeddings of images that are associated with the same symbol cluster?
*   **Generalization:** Explore the generalization capabilities of the emergent language. Can agents trained on one set of images successfully communicate about a novel set of images?
*   **Justification of Architectures**: Provide a more compelling justification for the design choices in the sender architectures. What specific inductive biases are they intended to introduce?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of scaling up the human evaluation? What would be required to significantly increase the number of image pairs and participants?
2.  You mention the potential for "metonymic" links between symbols and images. Could you provide more examples of this phenomenon and discuss how it might be encouraged or exploited?
3.  What is the role of the temperature parameter (τ) in the Gibbs distribution? How does varying this parameter affect the learning process and the properties of the emergent language?
4.  The “informed sender” architecture seems promising. Have you explored other architectural modifications to enhance the interpretability of the emergent language, beyond the convolutional filters?
5.  In the supervised learning phase, what criteria were used to select the 100 ImageNet labels? How might the choice of labels affect the results?
