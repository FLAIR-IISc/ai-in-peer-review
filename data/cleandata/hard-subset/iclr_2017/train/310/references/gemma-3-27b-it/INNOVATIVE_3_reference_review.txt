This paper introduces a multi-agent coordination game as a novel framework for studying the emergence of communication, moving beyond supervised learning approaches that lack the interactive element crucial for truly functional language. The core idea – letting agents bootstrap a language through play – is compelling and addresses a fundamental problem in AI: enabling cooperation with humans via natural language. The authors investigate factors influencing language development, including conceptual grounding and human interpretability, presenting initial results demonstrating successful coordination and some level of semantic alignment.

**Reject.** While the ideas presented are highly innovative and tackle a crucial problem, the paper feels like a promising *start* rather than a complete contribution. The results, while positive, are preliminary and lack sufficient depth to warrant acceptance at this stage. The leap to claims about “natural language” alignment feels overstated given the limitations of the experimental setup and analysis.

The strength of this paper lies in its novelty. The proposed game-based paradigm for studying emergent communication is a genuinely fresh approach, differentiating it from both supervised learning and purely evolutionary methods. Framing the problem through the lens of Lewis’ signaling game and connecting it to linguistic theory provides a strong theoretical foundation. The exploration of “informed” vs. “agnostic” sender architectures is a particularly interesting avenue for investigation, and the initial results suggesting the informed sender’s code is more interpretable are intriguing. The move towards grounding the communication via a supervised task is a logical and potentially fruitful direction.

However, the empirical validation feels insufficient. The claim of “almost perfect coordination” needs more nuance. What does "almost perfect" mean quantitatively? The visualizations (Figure 2, Figure 3) are helpful but don’t provide statistically rigorous evidence for the claimed semantic properties. While the human evaluation shows promising results (68% accuracy), the methodology is described somewhat vaguely (e.g., how were the image pairs selected for evaluation?). The statistical significance reported for the human evaluation is encouraging, but the sample size and the specifics of the CrowdFlower setup should be detailed.  The purity metrics, while statistically significant, are not overwhelmingly high. A purity of 70% is good, but doesn’t definitively establish “natural language” alignment. Furthermore, the "metonymic" link examples presented in the discussion are post-hoc interpretations. The paper doesn’t demonstrate a systematic link between the emergent code and human semantic associations *a priori*.

The writing is generally clear, but could benefit from tightening. The numerous references to game theory (Lewis games, cheap talk games, etc.) sometimes feel tangential and could be streamlined. The discussion section is somewhat repetitive, restating findings rather than synthesizing them into broader insights.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Quantitative Results:** Provide more detailed quantitative results for all experiments. Include standard deviations, confidence intervals, and effect sizes.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components of the framework (e.g., the informed sender architecture, the supervised pre-training).
*   **Human Evaluation Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the human evaluation setup, including the number of participants, the instructions given to participants, and the method for selecting image pairs.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct an error analysis of the human evaluation to identify patterns in the types of errors made by participants. This could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the emergent communication protocol.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:**  Compare the performance of the proposed approach to relevant baselines, such as a random communication protocol or a supervised learning approach trained on a similar dataset.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   You mention the possibility of humans occasionally playing one of the roles. Have you explored this, even in a preliminary way? What were the results?
*   What are the computational costs associated with training these agents, particularly with increasing vocabulary size?
*   Could you elaborate on the specific criteria used to select the supervised labels for the grounding experiment? Why those 100 labels specifically?
*   You discuss the potential for “metonymic” links in the human evaluation. Could you provide a more systematic analysis of these links, perhaps by categorizing the types of metonymic associations observed?
