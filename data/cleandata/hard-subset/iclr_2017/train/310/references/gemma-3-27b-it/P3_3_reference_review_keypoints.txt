- Paper investigates emergence of communication in a referential game, comparing agnostic vs. informed sender architectures and showing coordination, conceptual categorization, and much-improved human interpretability with supervised grounding.

- Decision: Accept (with minor revisions).

- Clear problem and motivation: highlights limitations of purely supervised approaches and the need for interactive, emergent communication.

- Sound methodology: uses a Lewis-style referential game, ImageNet, sensible architectures, and RL; comparison of agnostic vs. informed senders reveals the role of inductive biases.

- Strong empirical results: consistent agent coordination, purity-based evidence of conceptual categories, and t-SNE visualizations linking symbols to semantic space.

- Supervised grounding dramatically improves human interpretability of the emergent language.

- Human evaluation via crowdsourcing is a major strength demonstrating real-world understandability.

- Good literature connection: situates work within multi-agent systems, language emergence, and game theory.

- Request more convergence analysis: track learning curves, vocabulary evolution, and learning-rate effects across conditions.

- Request ablation studies on supervised grounding: test smaller ImageNet subsets or alternate datasets.

- Request more qualitative examples: show successful and failed communications and typical error types.

- Ask for explicit discussion of dataset biases (e.g., ImageNet category representation) and their impact on emergent language.

- Recommend reporting computational cost/resources for reproducibility.

- Question about informed sender: justify the chosen inductive bias and its relation to human visual processing.

- Question about human evaluation: provide participant instructions, training/pre-testing, and randomization details.

- Question about metonymic links: give more examples and discuss how such shifts could be encouraged or used.

- Question about scaling: can the approach scale to larger vocabularies and more complex environments, or are there fundamental limits?
