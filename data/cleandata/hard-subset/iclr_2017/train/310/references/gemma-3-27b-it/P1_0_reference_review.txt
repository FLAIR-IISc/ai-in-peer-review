## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning to Communicate

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in multi-agent reinforcement learning settings, specifically focusing on referential games. The authors aim to understand how agents can develop a language from scratch, and what environmental factors influence the interpretability of this emergent language, particularly for humans. They introduce a framework where two agents learn to coordinate by communicating about visual stimuli, exploring both the ability of agents to successfully coordinate and the semantic properties of the resulting communication. The work further explores grounding the emergent language by combining interactive game-playing with supervised image labeling. Results show successful coordination, evidence of semantic clustering aligning with object categories, and increased interpretability through supervised learning.

**Decision:** Accept (with minor revisions)

**Key Reasons:**

This is a well-motivated and executed paper addressing a core problem in AI – enabling effective communication. The authors convincingly demonstrate that agents *can* learn to communicate from scratch, and importantly, show promising results in shaping that communication to be more aligned with human semantic understanding. The exploration of informed vs. agnostic sender architectures, and the grounding experiments, provide valuable insights into designing environments that foster interpretable emergent language.



**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation & Literature Review:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of communication for intelligent agents, placing the work in the context of multi-agent systems, linguistics, and game theory. The literature review is thorough and effectively highlights the gaps this work addresses, distinguishing it from previous approaches that rely on pre-programmed communication or heavy human intervention. The connection to Lewis’ signaling game provides a solid theoretical foundation.
* **Solid Experimental Setup:**  The experimental setup is well-defined, leveraging existing datasets (ImageNet, McRae et al.) and established architectures (VGG, feed-forward networks). The controlled experimentation with different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and vocabulary sizes allows for a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing communication success and interpretability.
* **Convincing Results:** The results demonstrating successful coordination are robust. The evidence of semantic clustering, assessed through purity measures, is encouraging, suggesting the emergence of meaningful symbol associations. Crucially, the human evaluation demonstrates that the grounded language *can* be partially understood by humans, even when applied to novel image pairs.  The improvement with the supervised learning component is particularly compelling.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the framework, experimental setup, and results.  Figures and tables are effectively used to illustrate key findings.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** While the comparison of agnostic and informed senders is valuable, it would be beneficial to see more ablation studies isolating the impact of specific design choices (e.g., the convolutional filters in the informed sender).
* **Qualitative Analysis of Failures:**  While the human evaluation shows a 68% success rate, a deeper qualitative analysis of the *failures* – where humans misinterpret the symbols – could provide further insights into the limitations of the emergent language and potential avenues for improvement.  Examples of image pairs where the meaning transfer broke down would be helpful.
* **Scalability Considerations:**  The current experiments use a vocabulary size of 10 or 100.  How would the approach scale to larger vocabularies and more complex environments? Discussing the computational complexity and potential bottlenecks would be valuable.
* **Discussion of Linguistic Properties:** While the paper touches on semantic properties, further exploration of other linguistic characteristics (e.g., compositionality, systematicity) would strengthen the connection to linguistic theories.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Regarding the informed sender's convolutional filters:** You state they introduce an inductive bias towards combining image embeddings dimension-by-dimension. Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind this design choice and provide further analysis of *how* these filters contribute to the improved performance compared to the agnostic sender?
2. **Regarding the human evaluation:** The supervised learning phase uses ImageNet class labels.  Do you observe any bias in the human interpretations towards these specific labels, even when the images depict scenes or objects outside of the training distribution? In other words, do humans *project* the learned labels onto the images?
3. **Regarding scalability:**  How does the training time scale with increasing vocabulary size?  Are there any techniques you considered or plan to explore to mitigate the computational cost of larger vocabularies?
4. **Regarding the "metonymic" links:** You mention interesting cases where the sender used words metonymically. Could you provide more examples and discuss whether this behavior emerged spontaneously or was facilitated by the supervised learning component?



Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of emergent communication.  The authors have provided a clear and compelling demonstration of the potential for agents to learn a language from scratch, and their work offers valuable insights into how to guide this process towards more interpretable and human-aligned communication systems.  Addressing the suggested feedback would further strengthen the paper.
