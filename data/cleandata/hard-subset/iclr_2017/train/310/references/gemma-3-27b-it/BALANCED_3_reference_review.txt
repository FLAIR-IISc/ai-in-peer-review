This paper introduces a research program investigating the emergence of communication in multi-agent coordination games, aiming to understand how agents can develop a language portable to new situations and human partners. The authors focus on referential games, where agents learn to refer to objects, and explore the impact of environmental features on language development, specifically examining the association of symbols with conceptual properties and human interpretability. They present experiments with both agnostic and informed sender architectures, and explore grounding the emergent language through supervised learning on image labels.

**Reject.** While the research direction is interesting and the experiments are well-designed, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and depth to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core contribution – demonstrating that agents can learn to communicate in a simplified referential game and that this communication can exhibit some degree of semantic coherence – feels incremental. The results, while positive, are largely expected given prior work (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) and the improvements achieved through the informed sender and supervised learning are not substantial enough to represent a significant breakthrough. The paper also spends a considerable amount of time reviewing related work, but doesn’t adequately differentiate its contributions beyond simply applying established techniques to a slightly different setup.

The primary issue is the lack of a compelling new insight or methodological advancement. The paper successfully replicates findings from previous work and demonstrates expected improvements with architectural changes and supervised learning. However, it doesn’t offer a novel approach to addressing the challenges of emergent communication or a particularly insightful analysis of the resulting languages. The human interpretability experiments, while encouraging, are presented somewhat superficially, and the “metonymic” link observation lacks rigorous analysis. The connection to game theory, while mentioned, doesn't appear to significantly influence the experimental design or interpretation of results. Furthermore, the reliance on a pre-trained VGG network introduces a potential bias; the emergent language may be influenced by the features learned by the network, rather than arising purely from the interaction dynamics.

Despite the rejection, the work has merit and could be improved with several revisions.

*   **Novelty:** The authors should clearly articulate what distinguishes their work from existing literature beyond the combination of existing techniques. Is there a novel aspect of the game design, the agent architecture, or the analysis that sets this work apart? A more focused research question might help.
*   **Depth of Analysis:** The human interpretability experiments need more rigorous analysis. What specific aspects of the emergent language allow for human understanding? How does this compare to baseline models or human-designed communication protocols? A quantitative measure of interpretability beyond the percentage of correctly guessed images would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Studies:** A more thorough ablation study of the informed sender architecture would be helpful. What specific aspects of the convolutional filters contribute to the improved performance?
*   **Impact of Pre-training:** Discuss the potential influence of the pre-trained VGG network on the emergent language. Consider experiments with different feature extractors or training from scratch.
*   **Game-Theoretic Connection:** If the authors intend to connect their work to game theory, they should elaborate on the implications of their results for concepts like equilibrium selection and language evolution.
*   **Scaling:** Explore the scalability of this approach. Does the performance degrade as the number of objects or agents increases?

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  How does the performance of the agnostic sender change with increased vocabulary size? Is there a point where increasing the vocabulary becomes detrimental?
2.  The paper mentions “synonymy” in the informed sender's vocabulary. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the redundancy in the symbol usage? Are there specific symbols that are used interchangeably for different objects?
3.  What is the rationale behind choosing the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments (e.g., embedding dimensionality, learning rate)? Was any hyperparameter tuning performed?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the pre-trained VGG network? Have you considered using different feature extractors or training from scratch?
5.  Can you elaborate on the specific “metonymic” links observed in the human interpretability experiments? Can you provide more examples of how humans successfully interpreted the agent’s language based on these links?
6.  You mention the potential for adding humans into the loop. Have you explored this direction, and what challenges did you encounter?
