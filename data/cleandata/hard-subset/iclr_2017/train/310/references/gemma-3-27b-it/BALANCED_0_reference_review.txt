This paper introduces a research program investigating the emergence of communication in multi-agent coordination games. The core idea is to create an environment where agents, starting as “blank slates,” develop a language through interaction, aiming for portability to new situations and, crucially, humans. The authors explore whether agents can learn to communicate and what environmental features promote the development of human-interpretable codes, focusing on referential games with image pairs. They analyze symbol usage, semantic properties, and even ground the emergent language through a supervised learning component.

**Reject** – While the research direction is promising and the paper presents a solid foundation with clear motivation and connection to relevant literature, the results, particularly those pertaining to human interpretability, feel preliminary and require substantial strengthening to justify acceptance at a competitive conference like ICLR. The leap from “purity is significantly above chance” to “the agents’ language can be thought of as ‘natural language’” feels unsubstantiated with the presented evidence.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear problem definition, compelling motivation rooted in the challenges of AI cooperation and language grounding, and its thoughtful experimental setup. The connection to Lewis’ signaling game and broader literature in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory is well established. The authors appropriately position their work relative to existing approaches like pre-programmed multi-agent systems, human-in-the-loop learning, and Wizard-of-Oz environments. The exploration of informed versus agnostic senders is a particularly interesting design choice, providing some initial insights into architectural influences on emergent communication. The incremental introduction of supervised learning to ground the language shows a thoughtful progression of the research.

However, several aspects raise concerns. First, the metrics used to assess "human interpretability" – 68% accuracy in a crowdsourced survey – while statistically significant, feels relatively low and doesn't demonstrate a robust understanding of the emergent language. It’s unclear how this performance compares to a baseline of random image selection. The claim of “metonymic” links is intriguing but lacks detailed qualitative analysis or quantitative support. The examples presented in Figure 4 are helpful, but a more systematic investigation of the types of semantic shifts observed is needed. Second, the purity metrics, while statistically significant, require more context. What is the expected purity for a random baseline given the 20 categories? What does a “good” purity score look like in this context?  Third, the influence of hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, temperature) isn't thoroughly explored, and their impact on the emergent language remains unclear. The choices appear somewhat arbitrary. Finally, the results comparing the informed and agnostic senders, while interesting, are not deeply analyzed. Does the informed sender simply learn a more complex code, or does it truly exhibit properties closer to human language?

Here are suggestions for improvement:

*   **Strengthen the Human Interpretability Analysis:** Provide a more rigorous comparison against baselines (e.g., random image selection, assigning labels randomly). Perform a more thorough qualitative analysis of the types of semantic shifts observed, providing more examples beyond those in Figure 4. Investigate the relationship between symbol usage and the underlying visual features of the images.
*   **Clarify Purity Metrics:** Provide a clear baseline for the purity metric and discuss its limitations. Explore alternative metrics that might better capture the semantic coherence of the emergent language.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters to understand their influence on the results.
*   **Deeper Analysis of Sender Architectures:**  Provide a more in-depth comparison of the informed and agnostic senders, beyond simply reporting performance differences. Analyze the learned representations and communication strategies of each architecture.
*   **Expand the Scope of Supervised Learning:** Explore alternative supervised learning approaches or datasets to further ground the emergent language. Consider incorporating feedback from human participants during the learning process.
*   **Address potential issues with the reinforcement learning implementation**: A brief description of the reward scaling and baseline subtraction used in the Reinforce algorithm would be helpful to ensure stable learning.

Questions for the authors:

*   What baseline performance was achieved in the crowdsourced survey for human interpretability, and how does 68% accuracy compare to this baseline?
*   What is the expected purity score for a random assignment of symbols to images, given the 20 categories?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as embedding dimensionality and temperature?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations of the informed and agnostic senders? What specific features do they learn to encode?
*   Beyond the examples in Figure 4, what other types of semantic shifts or metonymic links have you observed in the emergent language?
*   Could you provide more detail on the reward scaling and baseline subtraction used in the reinforcement learning implementation?
