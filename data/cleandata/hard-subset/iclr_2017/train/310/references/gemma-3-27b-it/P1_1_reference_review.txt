## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning to Communicate

**Reviewer:** AI Assistant

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a foundational exploration into the emergence of communication between artificial agents. The authors investigate whether agents can learn to coordinate and develop a language from scratch through repeated interaction in a referential game, where they must successfully identify target objects. They explore the impact of sender architecture (agnostic vs. informed) and environmental modifications (class-level properties) on the resulting language's interpretability, both by other agents and, crucially, by humans. The work builds on Lewis’ signaling game and related concepts from linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory. A key contribution is demonstrating that, with careful environmental design and a supervised grounding phase, emergent languages can achieve surprisingly high levels of human interpretability.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a compelling and well-executed study with significant contributions to the understanding of emergent communication in multi-agent systems. The work is theoretically grounded, empirically sound, and the inclusion of human evaluation is a substantial strength.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of developing genuinely communicative AI agents and positions it within the context of limitations of supervised learning approaches. The introduction, drawing from Ted Chiang, effectively illustrates the need for interactive learning.
*   **Solid Experimental Design:** The experimental setup, while relatively simple, is well-defined and allows for controlled investigation of different factors influencing language emergence. The use of ImageNet and pre-trained CNNs provides a realistic input space.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate that agents *can* learn to communicate effectively and that the “informed sender” architecture leads to more interpretable communication patterns. The improvement in human interpretability with supervised grounding is particularly impressive.
*   **Novelty & Significance:** The work goes beyond simply demonstrating emergent communication (as seen in prior work). The focus on interpretability and the exploration of environment design to facilitate this interpretability is a valuable contribution. The connection to linguistic theories and game theory adds depth and context.
*   **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors employ a variety of evaluation metrics, including coordination success rate, symbol usage analysis, and human evaluation via crowdsourcing. This multi-faceted approach strengthens the credibility of the findings.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the paper highlights successes, it would be helpful to discuss instances where communication *failed* or was particularly challenging. Analyzing these cases could provide further insights into the limitations of the current approach.
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares agnostic and informed senders, more detailed ablation studies could explore the contributions of specific components (e.g., the convolutional filters in the informed sender).
*   **Statistical Significance:** While the paper mentions statistical significance in a couple of places, more consistent reporting of statistical tests and p-values would enhance the rigor of the empirical results.
*   **Elaborate on the 'Metonymic' Links:** The observation that agents establish “metonymic” links during human evaluation is intriguing. Expanding on this phenomenon with more examples and analysis would be valuable.
*   **Computational Resources:** Briefly mentioning the computational resources used (e.g., number of GPUs, training time) would be helpful for reproducibility.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding the supervised learning phase, did you experiment with different supervised datasets beyond ImageNet labels? Would using a more complex set of annotations (e.g., referring expressions) further improve human interpretability?
*   You mention potential redundancy in the informed sender's vocabulary. Did you investigate techniques to encourage more efficient symbol usage (e.g., regularization, pruning)?
*   The paper focuses on two-agent communication. Do you anticipate that the findings would generalize to scenarios with more agents? What challenges might arise in multi-party communication?
*   You mention the convergence question in the context of cheap talk games. Did you observe any instances of non-convergence or oscillatory behavior during training? If so, what factors seemed to contribute to these issues?
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the Reinforce algorithm? Were other reinforcement learning algorithms considered, and if so, why was Reinforce selected?
