- Summary: Studies emergence of communication in a referential multi-agent RL game, examining sender architectures and environment changes, and grounding emergent symbols with supervised image labeling to improve human interpretability.

- Decision: Recommend accept with minor revisions.

- Motivation & novelty: Tackles a fundamental problem by prioritizing interpretable communication rather than just coordination — a novel and important focus.

- Methodology: Carefully designed RL signaling-game setup comparing agnostic vs. informed senders and using SVD to analyze vocabulary structure.

- Results: Agents can learn effective communication from scratch; informed sender and class-level image representations improve interpretability.

- Grounding: Combining interactive learning with supervised labeling yields higher human interpretability scores, demonstrating practical alignment with humans.

- Literature review: Comprehensive and well-contextualized across game theory, linguistics, and multi-agent systems.

- Architecture clarity: Need clearer textual description and intuition for the informed sender, especially the 2x1 convolutional filters and their impact.

- Statistical reporting: Recommend consistently reporting statistical significance (p-values) for all key metrics like purity and performance comparisons.

- Supervised ablation: Request ablation studies isolating the supervised component (e.g., shuffled labels, varying supervised data amounts) to quantify its contribution.

- Qualitative examples: Ask for more qualitative examples of emergent symbols and their meanings beyond the limited examples shown.

- Limitations: Recommend explicitly discussing limitations such as limited vocabulary size, dataset choice, and potential human evaluation bias.

- Q1 (architecture): Why choose 2x1 convolutions; were other kernel sizes tried and what were the outcomes?

- Q2 (human eval): Provide CrowdFlower participant demographics and any quality-control filters used.

- Q3 (vocabulary size): Justify vocabulary choices of 10 and 100 and report experiments with other sizes and their effects on learning and interpretability.

- Q4 (convergence): Is there analysis of learning dynamics beyond initial convergence; do agents continue refining communication over longer training?

- Q5 (future directions): Outline next steps toward conversational machines and how to scale the approach to more complex, realistic environments.
