## ICLR 2024 Review: Emergent Communication in Multi-Agent Coordination Games

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting, specifically through a referential game where two agents must learn to coordinate by developing a shared language to identify objects. The authors explore whether agents can learn to communicate successfully and whether the resulting “language” exhibits characteristics resembling human language, focusing on semantic grounding and conceptual categorization. They explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and the impact of incorporating supervised learning for grounding in human labels. The results show successful coordination, evidence of semantic categorization, and improved interpretability with supervised learning, culminating in a language that humans can understand with reasonable accuracy.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper tackles a fascinating and important problem – the emergence of communication – and presents a solid investigation with clear results. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the findings contribute valuable insights into the factors influencing the development of interpretable languages in multi-agent systems. The grounding experiments are particularly compelling, demonstrating a path towards human-understandable communication.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

*   **Clear Problem and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of enabling communication between agents and humans, grounding it within the broader AI goals of cooperation and coordination. The motivation, rooted in limitations of supervised learning for capturing the *functional* aspect of language, is well-presented.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The experimental setup, based on a variation of Lewis’ signaling game, is appropriate for the research question. The use of ImageNet images provides a rich and realistic input space. The comparison of different sender architectures and vocabulary sizes allows for a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing language emergence.  The Reinforce learning method is a sensible choice for this type of reinforcement learning problem.
*   **Convincing Results:** The authors demonstrate successful coordination between agents across different configurations. The evidence for semantic categorization, via purity metrics, is statistically significant and supports the claim that agents aren’t merely memorizing image-symbol pairings.  The human evaluation results are particularly strong, demonstrating a high degree of interpretability for the emergent language, especially when combined with supervised learning.
*   **Well-Placed in Literature:** The paper provides a thorough review of related work, correctly positioning its contribution within the fields of multi-agent systems, emergent communication, and linguistic theory.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement)**

*   **Ablation Study of Supervised Learning:** While the supervised learning experiment is compelling, a more detailed ablation study could strengthen the findings. For example, exploring different levels of supervised signal (e.g., fewer labels, noisy labels) would clarify the impact of supervision on language emergence and interpretability.
*   **Qualitative Examples:** While the paper presents some qualitative observations, providing more detailed examples of the emergent language (e.g., showing which symbols are consistently used for specific object categories) would enhance the reader’s understanding.  Visualizing the embedding space of the learned symbols would also be valuable.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The authors briefly mention scalability issues with human-in-the-loop approaches, but a more comprehensive discussion of the limitations of the current setup (e.g., the limited complexity of the game, the restricted vocabulary) would be beneficial.
*   **Connection to Linguistic Theories:** Expanding the discussion of how the emergent language aligns with or deviates from existing linguistic theories (beyond the citations) would add further depth to the analysis. For instance, discussing the observed phenomenon of "metonymy" in the human evaluation in more detail would be valuable.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Regarding the informed sender's performance:** You mention the informed sender reaches higher performance faster. Is this consistent across different initializations, or is it sensitive to the random seed? What specific architectural features of the informed sender contribute most to this improved performance (e.g., the convolutional filters)?
2.  **Regarding the human evaluation:** The 68% accuracy in the human evaluation is impressive. Could you provide more details on the demographic of the crowdworkers (e.g., native language, experience with ImageNet)? Did you perform any quality control checks to ensure the reliability of the human ratings?
3.  **Regarding the supervised learning task:**  What is the specific rationale for switching between the game-playing and supervised learning tasks equiprobably?  Have you experimented with different ratios of game playing vs. supervised learning, and how do those ratios impact the results?
4. **Regarding vocabulary size:** The results indicate the informed sender uses more symbols.  Did you observe any practical limits to the vocabulary size beyond 100?  Would increasing the vocabulary further potentially lead to even more interpretable results, or does it reach a saturation point?



I believe that addressing these questions and incorporating the suggestions for improvement would further strengthen this already compelling paper.
