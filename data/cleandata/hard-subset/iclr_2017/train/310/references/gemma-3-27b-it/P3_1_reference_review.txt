## ICLR 2024 Review: Emergent Communication in Multi-Agent Coordination Games

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in multi-agent reinforcement learning environments, specifically focusing on referential games. The authors aim to understand how agents develop a language from scratch to coordinate on identifying objects, and what environmental features influence the “naturalness” (interpretability by humans and semantic grounding) of that language. They explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed), vocabulary sizes, and introduce a method for grounding the emergent language through a combined reinforcement learning and supervised image classification task.  The authors demonstrate successful coordination, evidence of semantic grouping in the emergent symbols, and significant improvement in human interpretability through supervised grounding.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper tackles a fundamentally important problem in AI – enabling genuine communication and coordination – and makes a solid contribution by providing insights into the factors driving the emergence of interpretable language in a multi-agent setting. The experimental setup is well-designed, the results are convincing, and the connection to linguistic and game-theoretic literature is well-established.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the need for communication beyond purely statistical sequence modeling, emphasizing the functional aspects of language crucial for real-world AI agents. The framing through the lens of Ted Chiang’s “Story of Your Life” effectively highlights the importance of interactive communication.
* **Novelty and Significance:**  The work extends prior research (Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) by actively seeking to *shape* the emergent language towards human interpretability.  The introduction of the “informed sender” architecture and the combined RL-supervised learning approach are significant contributions. The experiments on manipulating common knowledge are also insightful.
* **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors employ a comprehensive evaluation strategy, including measuring coordination success, assessing semantic purity via clustering, and crucially, conducting a human evaluation to gauge interpretability. The use of t-SNE visualizations provides valuable qualitative insights.  The statistical significance reported for the human evaluation (68% accuracy) is compelling.
* **Solid Connection to Related Work:** The paper thoroughly reviews and positions itself within the relevant fields of multi-agent systems, linguistics, game theory, and reinforcement learning.  The discussion of Lewis’ signaling game and its implications for equilibrium and language emergence is particularly strong.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Limitations:**  While the human evaluation is encouraging, it is conducted on a relatively small set of image pairs. A more extensive evaluation, potentially with more diverse images and a larger participant pool, would strengthen the claims about interpretability.  Further discussion of the limitations of using ImageNet images (potential biases) would be beneficial.
* **Ablation Study of Supervised Learning:** While the paper shows a positive effect of the combined approach, a more detailed ablation study would be valuable. For example, how does the performance degrade as the amount of supervised data is reduced?  What happens if the supervised labels are noisy?
* **Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on success stories. Providing examples of communication failures and analyzing the reasons behind them could offer further insights into the limitations of the approach and suggest avenues for future research.
* **Computational Cost:** Briefly mentioning the computational resources required for training would be helpful for reproducibility and for understanding the scalability of the approach.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the informed sender:** You mention the informed sender uses convolutions to combine image embeddings "dimension-by-dimension." Could you elaborate on the intuition behind this design choice?  Why does dimension-by-dimension comparison seem particularly beneficial for grounding language?
* **Human Interpretability Experiment:** The 68% human accuracy is impressive. Could you provide more detail on the demographics of the CrowdFlower participants? Was there any filtering or qualification of participants to ensure they were familiar with the concepts depicted in the images?
* **Semantic Purity & Vocabulary Size:** You observe that the informed sender uses a larger vocabulary and achieves higher purity. Is there a correlation between vocabulary size and purity across your experiments?  Does increasing vocabulary size *always* lead to higher purity, or is there a point of diminishing returns?
* **Scaling to More Complex Environments:** This work focuses on relatively simple referential games. What are the biggest challenges to scaling this approach to more complex environments with more agents, richer communication protocols, and more abstract concepts?




In conclusion, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of emergent communication. The clear presentation, rigorous evaluation, and thoughtful discussion of related work make it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.  Addressing the minor feedback points above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
