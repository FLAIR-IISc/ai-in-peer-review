## Review of "Learning to Communicate"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent reinforcement learning setting. The authors propose a referential game where two agents must develop a language to coordinate on identifying target images. They explore how the environment (specifically, the level of abstraction in the images presented) influences the characteristics of the emergent language, examining whether it reflects general conceptual properties and human interpretability. The authors demonstrate successful communication, showing that agents can learn to coordinate, and that the resulting “language” exhibits some semantic structure, and can even be partially understood by humans with the addition of a supervised learning component for grounding.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper tackles an important problem in AI – the development of communication – with a well-designed experimental framework and insightful results. The authors clearly demonstrate the feasibility of emergent communication and provide compelling evidence that environmental factors can influence the qualities of the resulting language. The grounding experiments significantly boost the human interpretability, making a substantial contribution to the field.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of developing communicative AI and positions it within the context of limitations of purely supervised learning approaches. The motivation – to move beyond canned responses and towards agents that can truly coordinate through communication – is well-established. The opening analogy to Ted Chiang's "Story of Your Life" effectively highlights the core challenge.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The experimental setup, based on a Lewis signaling game, is a sensible choice. The authors thoughtfully explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and vocabulary sizes. The use of ImageNet and pre-trained CNNs provides a realistic input space. The reinforcement learning framework is appropriately applied.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently demonstrate successful coordination between agents. The analysis of symbol usage, including the examination of purity with respect to object categories and the analysis of the SVD spectrum, provide compelling evidence that the emergent language is not entirely random. The human evaluation is particularly strong, demonstrating a surprisingly high level of interpretability of the agent's communication.
*   **Novelty:** The investigation of environmental factors (image abstraction) on the emergence of interpretable language is a novel and valuable contribution. The combination of reinforcement learning with supervised learning for grounding is an effective approach.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Clarify the "Informed Sender":** The description of the "informed sender" architecture could be more intuitive. While the authors explain the 1D convolutions, the reasoning behind *why* this architecture leads to better performance could be strengthened. A visual example showing how the convolutions process the image embeddings would be helpful.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While the human evaluation is impressive, the authors should discuss potential biases inherent in the experimental design. For example, were the participants aware that the symbols were derived from ImageNet labels? Did this influence their judgments?
*   **Future Work Elaboration:** The "Discussion" section mentions several exciting avenues for future work. Expanding on these—specifically regarding maintaining the alignment with human language—would further strengthen the paper.
* **More details on the supervised phase**: The supervised learning phase is key to getting good results, but is described quite briefly. More details on how exactly the supervised and RL phases are interleaved, and the rationale for the chosen hyperparameters, would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding the informed sender: What is the intuition behind choosing kernel size 2x1 for the 1D convolutions? Did you experiment with other kernel sizes?
*   Regarding the human evaluation: Could you provide more details about the demographics of the crowd-sourced workers and any steps taken to ensure the quality of their annotations?
*   Regarding the interplay between RL and supervised learning: Was there any hyperparameter tuning to balance between the two objectives? If so, what strategy was used?
*   You mention "metonymic" links in the human evaluation. Could you provide more examples of these, and discuss whether this is a feature you intentionally encouraged or a surprising emergent property?



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of emergent communication. The experimental design is rigorous, the results are compelling, and the authors clearly articulate the implications of their work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
