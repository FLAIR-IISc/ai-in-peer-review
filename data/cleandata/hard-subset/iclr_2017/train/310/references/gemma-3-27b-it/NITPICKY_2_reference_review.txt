This paper introduces a multi-agent coordination communication game designed to foster the emergence of language. The authors aim to understand how environment design impacts the development of interpretable communication protocols, specifically focusing on whether agents can develop language *de novo* and whether that language reflects conceptual properties or is human-interpretable. They explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and investigate the effects of grounding the emergent language through supervised learning on image labeling.

**Reject.** While the paper tackles an interesting and important problem – the emergence of communication – the numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies throughout the paper, coupled with a lack of rigorous analysis in certain areas, lead me to recommend rejection. The experimental setup seems sound, but the presentation and justification of results are often lacking in detail and precision. The claim of achieving "perfect coordination" feels overstated given the complexity of the task and the inherent noise in neural networks.

My primary concerns stem from the following:

1. **Lack of Statistical Rigor & Overstated Claims:** The phrase “almost perfectly coordinate” is used repeatedly without sufficient quantitative backing. Table 1 shows high accuracy, but standard deviations or confidence intervals are missing. Is this performance statistically significant compared to random chance, or a naive baseline (e.g., always sending the same symbol)? The claim of "perfect coordination" in the first 1k rounds following training feels unsubstantiated and requires more robust statistical testing.
2. **Ambiguous Evaluation Metrics:** The purity metric, while standard, is not clearly justified as a sole indicator of semantic alignment. It doesn't account for partial overlaps in categories. A purity of 70% with no baseline comparison is not particularly compelling. Furthermore, the analysis of the SVD decomposition of the symbol-image matrix is brief and the interpretation (“redundancy”) feels superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the spectral properties is needed.
3. **Insufficient Detail in Human Evaluation:** The description of the human evaluation is sparse. How were participants recruited? What were the demographics of the participants? The results (68% accuracy) are presented without statistical significance testing. Was inter-rater reliability assessed? The description of the “metonymic” examples feels anecdotal without proper quantification.
4. **Inconsistencies & Minor Errors:** There are several minor inconsistencies throughout the text. For example, the definition of the referential game in Section 2 differs slightly from the description in the introduction. The formatting of the references is also not uniform (e.g., some have question marks after the year). While individually minor, these errors collectively detract from the overall professionalism of the paper.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper compares an “informed” and “agnostic” sender, but lacks a comprehensive ablation study to truly understand the contributions of the convolutional layers in the informed sender. Is it the kernel size? The number of filters? A more systematic exploration would be valuable.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Clarify the definition of “coordination”:**  Provide a precise, quantitative definition of successful coordination and report relevant statistics.
*   **Provide baseline comparisons:**  Include performance comparisons to simple baselines for all metrics.
*   **Elaborate on the human evaluation:**  Provide details on participant recruitment, demographics, and statistical analysis.
*   **Address the inconsistencies:** Carefully review the paper for inconsistencies in terminology and notation.
*   **Conduct a thorough ablation study:**  Investigate the contributions of different components of the informed sender architecture.
*   **Justify hyperparameter choices:**  Explain why specific hyperparameters were chosen, or report the results of a hyperparameter search.
*   **Expand discussion of limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the experimental setup and the generalizability of the findings.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide confidence intervals or standard deviations for the performance metrics reported in Table 1?
*   What baseline performance did you observe for the purity metric?
*   What specific measures were taken to ensure the reliability of the human evaluation results?
*   How sensitive are your results to the choice of hyperparameters?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the SVD analysis and its implications?
*   Is the “perfect coordination” reported a result of the Reinforce algorithm converging to a local optimum, or is it truly robust across different initializations?
