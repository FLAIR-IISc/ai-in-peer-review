## ICLR 2024 Review: Emergent Communication in Multi-Agent Referential Games

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting using a referential game. The authors aim to understand how agents can develop a language from scratch to coordinate on referring to objects, and what environmental factors influence the characteristics of this emergent language – specifically, whether it aligns with human-like conceptual categories and interpretability. They explore different sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and investigate the impact of grounding the communication with supervised learning on object names. The results demonstrate successful coordination, evidence of conceptual categorization in the emergent language, and significantly improved human interpretability with supervised grounding.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound investigation into a crucial problem in AI – the development of communication. The experimental setup is well-designed, the results are convincing, and the inclusion of human evaluation adds a valuable dimension to the work. While the field is nascent, this paper represents a significant step forward in understanding how agents can learn to communicate effectively and in a manner potentially understandable by humans.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of developing communication in AI agents, emphasizing the limitations of purely supervised approaches and the need for interactive learning. The motivation, drawing a parallel to human language acquisition as highlighted by the Chiang quote, is compelling.
* **Sound Methodology:** The referential game framework is a well-established approach for studying emergent communication (Lewis signaling game). The authors make sensible choices in terms of image dataset (ImageNet), neural network architectures, and reinforcement learning algorithms. The comparison between the “agnostic” and “informed” sender architectures is particularly insightful, revealing the importance of inductive biases in shaping the emergent language.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently successful coordination between agents. The evidence for conceptual categorization (through purity metrics) is encouraging, and the dramatic improvement in human interpretability with the supervised grounding task is a significant finding. The t-SNE visualizations effectively illustrate the relationship between emergent symbols and semantic space.
* **Human Evaluation:** The inclusion of a human evaluation via crowdsourcing is a crucial strength. Demonstrating that humans can understand a significant portion of the agents’ communication is a strong indicator of the language’s naturalness and potential for real-world application.
* **Connection to Relevant Literature:** The authors appropriately position their work within the broader context of multi-agent systems, language emergence, and game theory, referencing relevant prior work and highlighting their contributions.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Convergence Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates convergence, a more detailed analysis of the learning curves (beyond Figure 2 left) for different conditions would be valuable. For instance, how does the vocabulary size evolve over training? How does the learning rate affect convergence?
* **Ablation Studies:** It would be interesting to see ablation studies on the supervised learning component. For example, what happens if the supervised training is done on a smaller subset of the ImageNet categories, or on a different dataset altogether?
* **Qualitative Analysis:** Providing more qualitative examples of successful and unsuccessful communication instances would help readers better understand the nuances of the emergent language.  Show examples of what types of errors the system makes.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper acknowledges the limitations of the current setup, a more explicit discussion of potential biases in the ImageNet dataset (e.g., representation of certain object categories) and the impact on the emergent language would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** Briefly mentioning the computational resources required for the experiments would be helpful for reproducibility.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the informed sender architecture:** The paper mentions an "inductive bias" towards combining images dimension-by-dimension.  Could you elaborate on why you chose this particular inductive bias, and how it might relate to human visual processing?
* **Regarding the human evaluation:**  What specific instructions were given to the human participants? Was there any training or pre-testing to ensure they understood the task? Were the images presented in a randomized order?
* **Regarding the metonymic link observation:** You mention "metonymic" links in the human evaluation.  Could you provide more examples of these instances and discuss how these types of linguistic shifts might be encouraged or exploited in future work?
* **Regarding scaling:** Do you anticipate the approach to scale to larger vocabularies and more complex environments, or are there fundamental limitations that would need to be addressed?



Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of emergent communication in AI. The authors have conducted a thorough investigation and presented compelling evidence to support their claims.  Addressing the additional feedback would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
