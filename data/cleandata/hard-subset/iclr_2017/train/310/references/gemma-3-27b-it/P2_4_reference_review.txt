## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Learning to Communicate

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting, specifically a two-player referential game. The authors aim to understand how agents can learn a language from scratch to coordinate, and whether the features of the environment can foster the development of interpretable "languages" resembling human language. They explore different agent architectures (agnostic vs. informed senders) and training paradigms (including grounding with supervised image labeling) and demonstrate successful coordination, semantic alignment with object categories, and surprisingly, some degree of human interpretability of the emergent codes.  The work draws connections to linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, positioning itself as a step towards building AI that can effectively communicate with humans.

**Decision:** **Accept**

**Key Reasons:**

1. **Novelty and Importance:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem in AI – enabling effective communication between agents and humans. The focus on *emergent* communication in a game-theoretic setting, rather than relying on pre-programmed protocols or massive supervised datasets, is a valuable and potentially impactful approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The authors demonstrate robust coordination between agents, and provide compelling evidence that the learned communication protocols exhibit semantic structure aligned with human conceptual categories and, crucially, can be partially interpreted by humans, especially when grounded via supervised learning.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents its motivations, experimental setup, and results.  The experimental design is thoughtfully constructed. Using a referential game is a sensible approach to isolate the core challenge of grounding symbols to shared referents. The comparison between “agnostic” and “informed” senders is insightful, suggesting that architectural biases can influence the quality of the emergent language. 

The results are persuasive. The quantitative results (high coordination rates, purity scores exceeding chance) are supported by qualitative observations (examples of interpretable symbol-object mappings). The grounding experiment significantly improves interpretability and provides a clear path towards more human-aligned communication.  The connection to Lewis signaling games and the discussion of potential equilibrium outcomes demonstrate a solid understanding of the relevant theoretical background.  The exploration of common knowledge removal is a clever way to probe the robustness of the learning process.

The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., imperfect human interpretability, reliance on ImageNet) and suggest avenues for future research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

* **Deeper Analysis of Failed Cases:** While the successful communication instances are showcased, a more detailed analysis of *when* communication fails (especially in the human interpretability experiment) could offer valuable insights.  What types of image pairs and symbols lead to misinterpretations?
* **Robustness to Noise/Distraction:** How robust is the learned communication to noisy images or distractors within the scene? Exploring this would demonstrate the practicality of the approach.
* **Scalability:** While the authors address scalability in the introduction by contrasting their approach to human-in-the-loop methods, a brief discussion of the computational cost of training and the potential for scaling to more complex environments or larger vocabularies would be beneficial.
* **Clarify "Common Knowledge":** The discussion of removing "common knowledge" is interesting but could be made more precise.  What *specific* information is being removed beyond the low-level image details, and how does this affect the learning dynamics?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Human Interpretability Details:** Can you provide more details on the crowd-sourcing experiment? Specifically, what instructions were given to participants, and how confident were the participants in their responses? Was there any qualitative feedback from the participants about their reasoning?
2. **Emergent Symbol Ambiguity:** You mention redundancy in the informed sender's vocabulary (Figure 2). Could you elaborate on the nature of this redundancy? Are there clear synonymic relationships between symbols, and if so, how do agents resolve these ambiguities during communication?
3. **Impact of Vocabulary Size:**  You explored vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100.  Did you observe any qualitative differences in the types of "languages" that emerged with different vocabulary sizes beyond the quantitative results reported? Would scaling the vocabulary to even larger sizes (e.g., 1000) be feasible and potentially lead to more complex and nuanced communication?
4. **Generalization to New Images/Concepts:** Have you tested the ability of the trained agents to generalize to *new* images or concepts that were not present during training?  This would provide stronger evidence of the learned representations' abstract nature.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of emergent communication and has the potential to inspire further research in this exciting area. I recommend it for acceptance.
