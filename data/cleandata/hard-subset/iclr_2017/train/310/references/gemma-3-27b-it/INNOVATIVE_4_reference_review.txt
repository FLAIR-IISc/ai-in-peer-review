This paper introduces a fascinating research program exploring the emergence of communication in multi-agent settings, motivated by the idea that true intelligence requires not just processing information, but coordinating with others. The authors present a referential game where agents, starting as blank slates, learn to develop a language to coordinate and achieve payoffs. They investigate factors influencing the development of interpretable “languages,” examining associations with conceptual properties and human interpretability. The work is grounded in linguistic theory (Lewis’ signaling game) and builds upon recent advances in multi-agent reinforcement learning, while distinctly focusing on grounding emergent communication.

**Reject.** While conceptually exciting and demonstrating promising preliminary results, the paper feels more like a thorough exploration of a novel research *direction* than a fully realized contribution. The core idea – bootstrapping language through interaction – is compelling, and the experimental setup is well-designed, but the results, while positive, lack the depth and robustness needed for acceptance at ICLR. The gains achieved with the supervised grounding, while interesting, also feel incremental given the significant effort involved.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its novelty. The framing of communication as an emergent property arising from agent interaction, rather than a pre-programmed system, is a bold and potentially impactful approach. The investigation into whether agents develop human-interpretable codes is also a valuable contribution. However, the evidence supporting the claim of "naturalness" remains weak. The purity measures, while statistically significant, don’t convincingly demonstrate meaningful conceptual categorization. The human evaluation provides a more encouraging signal, but the methodology (CrowdFlower) and the results (68% accuracy) don’t represent a breakthrough in interpretability. The effect size is modest, and the described “metonymic” links are suggestive, but require deeper analysis.

Furthermore, the paper spends a significant amount of time establishing connections to existing literature. While important for context, this often feels disproportionate to the presentation of novel findings. The repeated references to game theory concepts (cheap talk, Nash equilibria) don’t seem to be fully leveraged, and their relevance to the practical implementation of the game remains unclear. The supervised learning component, while showing improvement, feels almost tacked on, lacking a thorough exploration of its limitations or potential for scaling. The fact that the supervision only modestly improves interpretability suggests fundamental challenges in aligning emergent communication with human language.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Deeper Analysis of Emergent Language:** Investigate the structure of the learned “languages” more thoroughly. Beyond purity measures, explore methods for visualizing the semantic space and identifying recurring patterns. Are there consistent strategies for referring to objects? Can you quantify the level of ambiguity?
*   **Robustness and Scalability:** Explore the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings, vocabulary size, and the complexity of the environment. How well does the approach scale to more agents, more complex images, or more intricate games?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more targeted ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the architecture (e.g., the informed sender’s convolutional filters).
*   **Connection to Linguistic Theory:** Strengthen the connection to relevant linguistic theories. How do the observed patterns align with existing models of language evolution and pragmatics?
*   **Human Evaluation:** Improve the rigor of the human evaluation. Consider using a more controlled experimental design and collecting more detailed data on human judgments (e.g., confidence levels, explanations for their choices).
* **Game Theoretical Analysis**: The connections to game theory are mentioned frequently, but not exploited. Could the game be analyzed more formally to predict the observed dynamics, or to suggest modifications that might lead to more desirable outcomes?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that the informed sender uses a more varied vocabulary. Can you provide a quantitative analysis of vocabulary usage for both sender architectures (e.g., entropy of the symbol distribution)?
2.  The supervised learning component improved interpretability. What would happen if you removed the supervised phase and instead used a different grounding method, such as providing agents with textual descriptions of the images?
3.  What specific aspects of the environment (beyond the simple referential game) do you believe are most crucial for fostering the emergence of interpretable languages?
4.  How do the observed dynamics change when the receiver also learns (i.e., a fully reciprocal learning environment)?
5.  The paper touches on the challenges of convergence in cheap talk games. Do you observe any evidence of non-convergence or multiple equilibria in your experiments? If so, how do you address these issues?
