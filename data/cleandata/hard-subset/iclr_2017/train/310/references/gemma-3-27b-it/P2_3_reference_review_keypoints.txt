- Paper studies emergence of communication in a multi-agent referential game where agents learn a language to identify target images.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Strong motivation: well-articulated problem and need to move beyond supervised approaches to communicative agents.
- Sound methodology: Lewis signaling-game setup, exploration of sender architectures, vocabulary sizes, ImageNet inputs, and RL training are appropriate.
- Strong empirical results: agents reliably coordinate; symbol usage shows semantic structure (purity, SVD spectrum); human evaluation shows surprising interpretability.
- Novelty: studying how environmental abstraction affects emergent language and combining RL with supervised grounding is a valuable contribution.
- Clarify the informed-sender architecture and why 1D convolutions improve performance; provide visual examples of processing.
- Discuss limitations and potential biases in human evaluation (e.g., workers knowing symbols derive from ImageNet).
- Expand the Discussion/future work on maintaining alignment with human language.
- Provide more details on the supervised phase: how supervised and RL phases are interleaved and hyperparameter choices.
- Question: why kernel size 2x1 for 1D convolutions and were other sizes tried?
- Question: provide demographics and quality-control measures for crowd-sourced human evaluators.
- Question: describe hyperparameter tuning or strategies used to balance RL and supervised objectives.
- Question: give more examples and analysis of the reported "metonymic" links and whether they were expected or emergent.
