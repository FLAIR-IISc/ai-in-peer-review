This paper investigates the emergence of communication in a multi-agent setting, specifically focusing on referential games where agents must learn to coordinate using a novel language. The authors explore factors influencing the development of interpretable languages, examining the association of symbols with conceptual properties and human interpretability through online experiments. They introduce a framework for studying language emergence and present initial results demonstrating successful coordination and some degree of semantic grounding.

**Reject.** While the paper tackles an interesting and important problem—the emergence of communication—and presents a well-motivated framework, the experimental setup and analysis suffer from a multitude of minor, yet collectively substantial, methodological flaws and inconsistencies. The claims of semantic grounding, particularly concerning human interpretability, are overstated given the experimental design and reported results.

The core issue lies in a pervasive lack of methodological rigor and attention to detail. Several points raise significant concerns:

1.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper states hyperparameters were set "without tuning," which, while potentially honest, is a significant weakness. A sensitivity analysis, even a limited one, would strengthen the results. The choice of embedding dimensionality (50), number of filters (20), and temperature (10) is presented as arbitrary. What justifies these specific values?

2.  **Reinforce Implementation:** The description of the Reinforce update rule is vague. The batch size of 32 seems small given the complexity of the network and the nature of the game. Justification for this choice is absent. Furthermore, the authors state 50k iterations were used, but do not report convergence metrics (e.g., reward curves, policy gradients) to demonstrate stability. 

3.  **Purity Metric:** While the use of purity to assess semantic grounding is reasonable, the comparison to a "random permutation" baseline feels inadequate. A more robust baseline, perhaps comparing to a distribution of symbols based on the frequency of object co-occurrence in ImageNet, would be more informative. The claim of “significantly above chance” requires statistical testing, which is absent.

4.  **Human Evaluation:** The crowdsourced experiment suffers from several critical flaws. The task – identifying the correct image given a symbol – is overly simplistic and doesn’t adequately assess the nuance of the emergent language. The number of ratings per pair (10) is insufficient to draw statistically significant conclusions.  The reported 68% accuracy, while encouraging, lacks sufficient statistical support. The wording regarding the success rate seems misleading; stating “in 68% of the cases the subjects were able to guess the right image” implies a high degree of understanding when, in fact, the chance of guessing correctly is significant. More details regarding the subject pool (e.g., demographics, language background) are needed.

5.  **Inconsistent Reporting**: The paper reports different results for the same metrics throughout the text. This is unprofessional and creates confusion. For instance, the statement about the informed sender using more symbols is contradictory to later statements about redundancy in the matrix decomposition.

6. **Formatting & References:** The references are not formatted consistently (e.g., some authors have initials, others do not). The inclusion of the review guideline at the end of the document is unusual and unnecessary.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The connection to Lewis’ signaling game and cheap talk games is valuable but underdeveloped. A more thorough discussion of the theoretical implications of the results in the context of game theory would strengthen the paper.
*   The motivation for the informed sender architecture is unclear. While the authors mention an inductive bias, the benefits over the agnostic sender are not fully justified.
*   Visualizations could be improved. The figures are small and lack clear labeling.
*   The discussion section is too brief. The authors should delve deeper into the limitations of their approach and potential avenues for future research.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What justification is there for the chosen hyperparameters? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis, even informally?
2.  Can you provide convergence plots (e.g., reward curves) for the Reinforce training process?
3.  What statistical tests were used to determine the significance of the purity metric?
4.  How was the 68% accuracy in the human evaluation calculated, and what was the expected chance level performance? What were the demographic characteristics of the crowd-sourced workers?
5. Can you elaborate on the inconsistencies in the reported results (e.g., symbol usage) and explain why these discrepancies exist?
