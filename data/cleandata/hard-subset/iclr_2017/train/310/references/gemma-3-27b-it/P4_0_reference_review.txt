This paper investigates the emergence of communication in multi-agent reinforcement learning settings, specifically focusing on referential games. The authors aim to design environments that foster the development of interpretable language, exploring the impact of environment features and supervised learning on the resulting communication protocols. They demonstrate that agents can learn to coordinate, that the learned symbols can exhibit semantic properties related to object categories, and that grounding the communication with supervised learning can significantly improve human interpretability.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound investigation, the novelty is incremental. The core findings – that agents can learn to communicate and that supervised learning aids interpretability – are broadly consistent with existing literature in the field. The improvements and specific experimental details, while valuable, do not represent a substantial advancement warranting acceptance to ICLR.

The paper is well-motivated, placing the work within the context of AI coordination and the limitations of purely supervised learning for capturing the functional aspects of communication. The approach builds upon established concepts from game theory (Lewis signaling game) and multi-agent reinforcement learning, and clearly articulates the research questions. The experimental setup is clearly described, leveraging existing datasets (ImageNet, McRae et al.’s concepts) and established architectures (VGG ConvNet, feed-forward networks). The ablation studies, comparing different sender architectures and vocabulary sizes, are commendable. The reported results demonstrate successful coordination and provide evidence for semantic grounding, supported by quantitative measures (purity, t-SNE visualization) and a human evaluation. The Reinforce algorithm is appropriately used and the training details are sufficiently provided for reproducibility. However, the presented results, while positive, do not fundamentally shift our understanding of emergent communication. The incremental nature of the improvements—particularly regarding the integration of supervised learning—feels more akin to an engineering contribution than a theoretical breakthrough. The human evaluation, while promising, is limited in scope and would benefit from more rigorous statistical analysis and a larger participant pool. The observed “metonymic” links during the human evaluation are interesting but remain largely anecdotal without more detailed analysis.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Novelty Enhancement:** The authors could strengthen the novelty by exploring more complex communication protocols beyond single-symbol transmissions. Investigating sequential communication or the emergence of compositional structures could demonstrate a more significant advance.
*   **Human Evaluation Rigor:** The human evaluation should include a control condition (e.g., random symbol-image pairings) to establish a clearer baseline. Reporting confidence intervals and effect sizes would strengthen the statistical validity of the findings. Detailed demographic information about the human subjects would be valuable.
*   **Scalability:** While the paper focuses on a relatively simple environment, discussing the potential challenges and strategies for scaling this approach to more complex scenarios would be beneficial.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While empirical results are presented, a deeper theoretical analysis of the game-theoretic properties of the environment and the resulting communication equilibria could provide further insights.
*   **Clarification of 'Common Knowledge' Removal:** The description of removing "common knowledge" is slightly vague. A more precise definition and justification of what constitutes common knowledge in this context would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions exploring different vocabulary sizes (10 and 100). How were these values chosen, and were other vocabulary sizes investigated? Did the performance or interpretability vary significantly with vocabulary size?
*   The informed sender architecture appears to perform better. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study to isolate the specific contributions of the convolutional filters to the improved performance?
*   In the human evaluation, what specific instructions were provided to the crowdworkers? Were they informed about the origin of the symbols (i.e., that they were generated by an AI)?
*   The observed “metonymic” links are intriguing. Do you have any hypotheses about why these specific associations emerged? Were there any systematic patterns in the types of metonymies observed?
*   How does the performance of your agents compare to existing state-of-the-art methods on similar referential game tasks? A comparative analysis would help contextualize your results.
