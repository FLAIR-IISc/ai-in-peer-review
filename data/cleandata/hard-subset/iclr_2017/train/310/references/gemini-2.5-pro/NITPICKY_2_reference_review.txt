### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with coordinating in a referential game. The agents must develop a communication protocol from scratch to identify a target image from a pair of images. The authors explore how architectural inductive biases (an "agnostic" vs. an "informed" sender) affect the speed of learning and the nature of the emergent language. They analyze the semantic properties of the developed protocol by measuring its "purity" with respect to human-defined object categories. Furthermore, the paper proposes a method to ground the agents' language in human language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. A final crowdsourced experiment suggests that this grounding enables a degree of human interpretability, even for objects not seen during the supervised phase, via what the authors describe as metonymic generalization.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail and Experimental Rigor:** The paper omits critical details about the experimental setup, agent architectures, and evaluation procedures. Key hyperparameters are presented as fixed "without tuning," which undermines the robustness of the findings. Claims are often based on "qualitative evaluations" or underdeveloped analyses, which is insufficient for a rigorous empirical paper.
2.  **Weaknesses in Evaluation and Unsubstantiated Claims:** The evaluation metrics, while intuitive, are not thoroughly justified or explored. The analysis of the emergent language's semantic structure relies heavily on a single purity metric and anecdotal observations from t-SNE plots. The human evaluation experiment, while a strong idea, is not described with enough detail to assess its validity or reproducibility.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

While the research direction is promising and the core ideas are interesting, the execution and presentation are marred by a significant number of minor and major flaws that collectively undermine confidence in the results.

**Methodological and Reporting Deficiencies:**
*   **Hyperparameter Justification:** The paper states key hyperparameters (embedding size, temperature) were set "without tuning." While this can sometimes demonstrate robustness, here it feels more like a lack of thoroughness. A temperature parameter of `τ=10` for the Gibbs distributions is exceptionally high, leading to a nearly uniform sampling policy. This would drastically slow down learning and seems counter-intuitive for achieving coordination. This choice is not justified or analyzed.
*   **Architectural Clarity:** The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender is not described with sufficient clarity. The claim that the "agnostic architecture nests the informed one" is mentioned in passing but not explained. The description of the 1-D convolutions in the informed sender is vague, and a more precise diagram or formulation would be necessary for reproducibility.
*   **Vague Input Specification:** In Section 2, the sender's input is described as `θS(iL, iR, t)`. This notation is confusing, as `θS` typically denotes the model's parameters, not an input function. It is unclear if the parameters themselves are part of the input, which seems unlikely.
*   **Reinforcement Learning Details:** The paper states parameters are updated via the REINFORCE rule. However, crucial details are missing. Was a baseline used to reduce variance? How were gradients from the sender and receiver combined or propagated? Given that both agents' policies contribute to the outcome, the exact learning mechanism is a critical detail.

**Analytical and Evaluative Weaknesses:**
*   **SVD Analysis:** The SVD analysis to measure vocabulary redundancy is presented without sufficient detail. The paper states "cross-validated SVD suggests 50 dimensions" but provides no information on how this cross-validation was performed. The conclusion drawn from this single number is therefore difficult to verify.
*   **Purity Metric:** While purity is a standard clustering metric, its use here has limitations. It relies on a pre-existing, hard-coded human category structure (McRae et al.) which may not be what the agents find salient in the visual data. The absolute purity scores are quite low (e.g., 23-37%), and while significantly above chance, they don't strongly support the claim of alignment with "higher level semantic meanings."
*   **Anecdotal Claims:** The paper makes a strong claim that the 2-symbol agnostic sender converges to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction, citing Caramazza & Shelton (1998). However, this is based on the authors' "qualitative evaluations," which is not a scientifically rigorous basis for such a specific claim. Similarly, the interpretation of the t-SNE plots is subjective, and the authors themselves admit "there still appears to be quite a bit of variation."
*   **Human Evaluation:** The crowdsourcing experiment is a highlight, but its description is critically lacking. There is no information on the user interface, the exact instructions given to participants, quality control measures, or participant demographics. The statistical analysis is mentioned ("logistic regression...") but not detailed. Without these details, the result (68% accuracy) is not fully interpretable or trustworthy. The examples in Figure 4 appear to be cherry-picked successes.

**Formatting and Presentation:**
*   **Bibliography:** The reference list is in a state of disarray. Many entries are incomplete, incorrectly formatted, or contain parsing errors (e.g., "? \Q1998\E"). This level of carelessness in a core academic component of the paper is concerning. For example, "Brandenburger and Dekel,?" is listed twice with different years, and several entries lack publisher or conference information.
*   **Footnote:** The footnote on page 3 referencing Jorge et al. (2016) is awkwardly placed and disrupts the flow of the text describing the general framework.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The core idea of mixing self-play with supervised grounding is very compelling. This aspect of the paper should be strengthened with more rigorous analysis and reporting.
*   Provide a dedicated appendix with a complete list of hyperparameters, detailed architectural diagrams, and the exact instructions and interface used for the crowdsourcing study.
*   The analysis would be stronger if it included other metrics besides purity. For example, one could measure the correlation between the distances in the agents' symbol-embedding space and distances in a human-derived semantic space (e.g., WordNet distance between labels).
*   The claim regarding the "living-vs-non-living" distinction needs to be substantiated. For instance, the authors could train a linear classifier on the symbol usage to predict this binary property and report its accuracy.
*   The entire bibliography needs to be meticulously reformatted and corrected. This is a non-negotiable step for a publication-ready manuscript.

**5. Questions for the Authors**
1.  Could you please justify the choice of a high temperature (`τ=10`)? Did you experiment with lower values, and if so, how did it affect convergence speed and the properties of the final communication protocol?
2.  Could you elaborate on the cross-validation procedure used for the SVD analysis that led to the conclusion of 50 effective dimensions?
3.  Beyond your qualitative assessment, what quantitative evidence do you have that the two-symbol language developed by the agnostic sender corresponds to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction?
4.  Regarding the human evaluation, what were the exact instructions given to the CrowdFlower participants? How did you ensure they were not simply guessing or choosing the more visually salient image, irrespective of the word provided? Can you provide more details about the logistic regression model?
