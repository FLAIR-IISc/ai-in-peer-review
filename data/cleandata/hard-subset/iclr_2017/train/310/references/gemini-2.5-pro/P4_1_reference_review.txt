### 1. Summary

This paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols between two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with coordinating in a cooperative referential game. Starting *tabula rasa*, the agents must develop a shared symbolic language to allow the receiver to identify a target image from a distractor image, based on a symbol sent by the sender. The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that simple feed-forward agents trained with reinforcement learning can successfully converge to a near-perfect communication protocol. Second, it analyzes the properties of this emergent language, providing evidence that the learned symbols correspond to high-level conceptual categories (e.g., semantic purity relative to human-defined categories) rather than just low-level visual features. The authors show that modifying the game's "common knowledge" (by forcing reference at the object-class level) can encourage more abstract communication. Finally, and most significantly, the paper proposes a method for grounding the emergent language in human language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. A human study confirms that this grounded protocol is partially interpretable by humans, even when referring to objects not seen during the supervised phase, demonstrating a form of compositional or metonymic generalization.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The paper presents a methodologically sound and well-executed series of experiments that convincingly demonstrate not only the emergence of communication but also provide a clear analysis of its semantic properties. The progression from basic emergence to abstract reference and finally to human-grounded language is logical and compelling.
2.  The final experiment, which combines interactive self-play with supervised learning and validates the resulting language with a human-subject study, is a novel and significant contribution. It provides strong evidence for a viable path toward creating AI agents that can develop human-interpretable communication protocols, addressing a key challenge in cooperative AI.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear in its motivation, methodology, and presentation of results. The problem of learning the functional, interactive aspects of language is well-motivated and contrasted effectively with purely supervised approaches. The referential game framework is explained precisely, and the agent architectures are simple yet sufficient for the tasks. The figures and tables are informative and support the claims made in the text.

**Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is sound. The use of a referential game is a classic and appropriate paradigm for studying the emergence of language. The agent architectures, including the distinction between an "agnostic" and an "informed" sender with a specific inductive bias, are well-reasoned. The application of the REINFORCE algorithm is standard for this type of problem involving discrete communication. The evaluation is rigorous and multifaceted, employing not only task performance (accuracy) but also intrinsic measures of linguistic structure (vocabulary usage, SVD) and semantic content (purity, t-SNE visualization). The inclusion of a crowdsourced human evaluation in the final experiment is a major strength, providing direct evidence for the central claim of human interpretability.

**Novelty and Significance:** While previous work has shown that agents can learn to communicate, this paper's main contribution lies in its deep dive into the *properties* of the emergent language and its focus on making that language interpretable. The experiment in Section 4.1, which manipulates the game environment to foster more abstract communication, is a clever and novel demonstration of how environmental pressures shape language. The hybrid training scheme in Section 5, inspired by AlphaGo but applied to cooperative communication, is a significant step forward. The finding that this approach leads to a protocol that generalizes to new objects in a human-interpretable, sometimes "metonymic," way is a compelling and important result for the field of multi-agent communication and human-AI interaction. The work successfully connects concepts from machine learning, game theory, and cognitive science.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback and do not detract from the paper's core contributions.

*   The temperature parameter (τ=10) for the Gibbs distributions is quite high, which encourages exploration. It would be interesting to see an analysis of how this parameter affects the final learned protocol. For instance, does annealing the temperature lead to a more compact or deterministic language? A brief discussion on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter would strengthen the paper.
*   The SVD analysis of the communication matrix is insightful. The interpretation could be further enhanced by relating the number of effective dimensions to other known quantities, such as the number of available vocabulary symbols or the number of ground-truth semantic categories. This could provide a clearer picture of the language's complexity and efficiency.
*   The distinction between the `sm` (softmax) and `fc` (fully-connected) layer representations is mentioned in the setup but not fully explored in the results tables. While the `fc` representation is used for most of the key results, a brief comparison or justification for this choice would be beneficial for reproducibility and understanding.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The training of two agents simultaneously with REINFORCE can suffer from non-stationarity, as each agent's policy is changing from the perspective of the other. Did you encounter any training instabilities? Was the standard REINFORCE algorithm sufficient, or were techniques such as a shared reward baseline used to reduce variance?
*   In the human evaluation experiment (Section 5), the sender was pre-trained on a supervised set of 100 ImageNet labels but then played the referential game on the ReferItGame dataset. Could you clarify the relationship between the object categories in the supervised set and those present in the ReferItGame image pairs? The strength of the generalization claim depends on the degree of conceptual overlap (or lack thereof) between these two sets.
*   The paper makes the qualitative observation that the agnostic sender, when limited to two symbols, converges to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. This is a fascinating result. Could you provide any quantitative evidence to support this claim, perhaps by evaluating cluster purity specifically against this binary partition from the McRae et al. dataset?
