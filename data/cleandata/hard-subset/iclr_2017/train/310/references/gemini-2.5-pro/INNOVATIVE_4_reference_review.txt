### Summary

This paper introduces a research program for studying the emergence of communication between AI agents. The authors design a series of referential games where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop a communication protocol from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a pair. The core contribution is not merely demonstrating that communication can emerge, but exploring how to shape the environment to make the resulting language more conceptual and, ultimately, human-interpretable.

The key findings are threefold:
1.  Simple neural agents can successfully learn to communicate in this setup. The authors show that an "informed" sender architecture with an inductive bias for comparison learns faster and develops a richer vocabulary.
2.  The emergent language captures high-level semantic properties. This is demonstrated by showing that the learned symbols cluster objects by their human-defined categories and by tweaking the game to force abstraction (object-level reference), which further improves semantic purity.
3.  Most significantly, the authors propose a novel hybrid training approach that combines interactive game-playing with a supervised image-labeling task. This method successfully grounds the emergent language in human-understandable terms. The power of this approach is demonstrated in a final experiment where humans can interpret the agent's language in a zero-shot setting on a new dataset, correctly identifying the target image 68% of the time. The analysis suggests agents learn to make creative, "metonymic" connections, a hallmark of human language.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a clear accept. The paper presents a bold and highly novel research direction that moves beyond simply demonstrating emergent communication to asking the far more interesting question of how to guide this emergence toward something that is structured, conceptual, and ultimately aligned with human language. The proposed hybrid approach of mixing self-play with supervised grounding is a significant conceptual contribution. The final experiment, demonstrating that humans can understand the agent's generalized language, provides powerful and exciting evidence for the viability of this approach.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

This paper exemplifies the kind of research ICLR should champion. It is conceptually ambitious, opens up a new avenue for investigation, and prioritizes a foundational question over incremental performance gains on an established benchmark.

1.  **High Novelty and a Compelling Vision:** The core idea of "shaping" an emergent language is a substantial step forward. While prior work has shown that agents can develop a communication protocol, these protocols are often opaque and brittle. This paper introduces concrete, simple, yet powerful mechanisms for influencing the properties of the emergent language. The idea of mixing supervised learning on a fixed dataset with interactive learning in a game environment, inspired by AlphaGo but applied to the notoriously difficult problem of language grounding, is an elegant and powerful concept. It presents a plausible and scalable path toward building agents that can learn the functional aspects of language through interaction, while still leveraging the vast amount of static data available.

2.  **Strong and Creative Experimental Validation:** The experimental design is excellent, with each experiment building logically on the last to support the central thesis. The initial experiments establish a baseline, but the "object-level reference" game (Section 4.1) is a particularly clever manipulation to force abstraction. The capstone experiment in Section 5 is the paper's strongest point. Grounding the language via supervised learning is a good idea, but testing its generalization on a completely different dataset (*ReferItGame*) and then replacing the agent receiver with human subjects is a bold and convincing way to validate the central claim. The finding that humans can interpret the agent's "metonymic" word usage (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to the sea) is a fascinating result that suggests the emergent protocol captures semantic relationships that are intuitive to humans. This is a far more meaningful result than a simple accuracy score.

3.  **Prioritizing Ideas over Perfection:** While the agent architectures are simple feed-forward networks and some hyperparameters were set without tuning, this is a feature, not a bug, in the context of this paper. The simplicity of the agents makes the emergence of complex communicative behavior even more striking. The paper's strength lies in its ideas and the clarity of its experimental questions, not in complex engineering or squeezing out state-of-the-art performance. It successfully demonstrates a proof-of-concept for a new research program, which is more valuable at this stage than a perfectly polished but less ambitious project.

### Additional Feedback

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision.

*   **Analysis of "Metonymy":** The qualitative examples of metonymic generalization are excellent. It would be fascinating to see a more systematic analysis of this phenomenon. For instance, could you use word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) to measure the semantic distance between the word the sender used (e.g., "dolphin") and the actual concepts in the target image (e.g., "water," "sea," "ocean")? This could provide quantitative evidence for the types of semantic leaps the agent is making.
*   **The Role of the Receiver:** The paper focuses heavily on the sender's training and architecture. The receiver's role is also critical for the language to emerge. It would be interesting to discuss if the receiver's architecture or learning process influences the final protocol. For example, does a more powerful receiver lead to a more compressed or abstract language from the sender?
*   **Vocabulary Size:** The experiments explore vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100. The jump in performance and interpretability with the grounded vocabulary of 100 is dramatic. It would be interesting to see an analysis of how the richness of the supervised grounding (e.g., number of labels) affects the agent's ability to generalize to new concepts. Is there a critical mass of "seed words" needed for this flexible communication to emerge?

### Questions for the Authors

*   How stable are the emergent communication protocols across different random seeds? Without the supervised grounding, do different pairs of agents converge on completely different, incompatible languages, or are there structural similarities driven by the visual input?
*   In the human evaluation, what did the failure cases look like? Were they random guesses, or were humans consistently choosing the same (wrong) image, suggesting an interpretation of the word that was systematic but different from the sender's? Analyzing these errors could be as insightful as analyzing the successes.
*   The supervised and interactive tasks are mixed with a 50/50 probability. Have you explored how sensitive the results are to this ratio? For instance, could a much smaller amount of supervision (e.g., 10%) still be sufficient to bootstrap a human-interpretable language? This would have significant implications for the scalability of this approach.
