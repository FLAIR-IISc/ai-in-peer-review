### Summary of the paper's contribution

This paper presents a compelling investigation into the emergence of communication between artificial agents. The authors propose a research program centered on multi-agent referential games, where two neural network agents (a sender and a receiver) must develop a shared communication protocol from scratch to solve a cooperative task: identifying a target image from a pair. The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that simple, tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to communicate and solve this task using reinforcement learning. Second, it systematically investigates factors that influence the nature of the emergent language, showing that architectural inductive biases (an "informed" sender that explicitly compares image features) lead to faster learning and a richer, more structured vocabulary. Third, through a clever manipulation of the game's common knowledge (showing agents different instances of the same object class), the authors encourage the agents to develop a more abstract, conceptual language. Finally, and most significantly, the paper proposes and validates a method to ground the emergent protocol in human language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. A human evaluation study confirms that the resulting language is not only more structured but also partially interpretable by humans, even when referring to objects not seen during the supervised training phase, demonstrating a form of compositional generalization and "metonymic" reasoning.

### Decision and key reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles the fundamental problem of grounding language in a functional, interactive context, moving beyond the limitations of static, supervised datasets. The approach of using multi-agent games to study the *emergence* of communication is powerful, and the paper's focus on making this emergent language structured and human-interpretable is a significant step forward for the field.

2.  **Strong and Rigorous Empirical Results:** The paper presents a series of well-designed, cumulative experiments that convincingly support its claims. The progression from establishing baseline communication, to improving it with architectural biases and game mechanics, to finally grounding it with supervision is logical and insightful. The final human evaluation experiment is a crucial piece of evidence that provides strong validation for the paper's central claim about achieving human-interpretable communication.

### Supporting arguments

The paper's strengths are numerous. The introduction provides an excellent motivation, framing the work within the broader context of AI, linguistics, and game theory, and clearly distinguishing it from related approaches. The experimental setup is clean and well-controlled.

The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures is a nice piece of experimental design. It provides a concrete example of how inductive biases can shape the learning process and the structure of the resulting communication protocol. The use of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to analyze the dimensionality of the emergent language is a clever and effective way to show that the "informed" sender is not simply using synonyms but developing a richer code.

The "object-level reference" experiment (Section 4.1) is particularly insightful. By removing low-level visual features from the agents' common knowledge, the authors force coordination on more abstract, class-level properties. This is a simple but powerful manipulation that directly addresses the question of how to foster conceptual representations and leads to measurably "purer" semantic clusters.

The paper's capstone is the hybrid supervised/interactive training experiment (Section 5). This approach, inspired by AlphaGo, is a pragmatic and effective solution to the grounding problem. The results are impressive: not only does communication efficiency remain high, but the semantic purity of the code dramatically improves. The most compelling result is the human study. Showing that humans can interpret the agent's messages on a completely different dataset (ReferItGame) at a rate of 68% is a very strong result. The qualitative analysis of "metonymic" links (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to the sea) is fascinating and suggests that the agents are learning flexible, context-dependent meanings, much like humans do. This finding has significant implications for building more robust and adaptable conversational agents.

### Additional feedback for improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to make it even better.

*   **Details of the "Informed" Sender:** The paper claims that the "agnostic architecture nests the informed one." This is not immediately obvious. While a sufficiently large MLP is a universal approximator, it's not clear that it would naturally learn the specific dimension-wise convolutional structure of the informed sender. A brief explanation or justification for this claim would be helpful.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors state that hyperparameters were set "without tuning." While this demonstrates robustness, a brief discussion on the potential effect of key parameters would be welcome. For example, the temperature parameter `τ` in the Gibbs distributions directly controls the stochasticity of the agents' policies. How does this parameter affect the exploration of the vocabulary space and the speed of convergence?
*   **Analysis of Failures:** The human evaluation showed 68% success, which is impressive but also leaves room for analysis. A brief qualitative analysis of failure cases in the human study could provide further insight. When did humans fail to understand the agent's message? Was it due to random noise, or were there systematic and interesting mismatches in the emergent semantics?
*   **Future Directions on Compositionality:** The emergent language consists of single symbols. While the metonymic use of symbols is a form of semantic flexibility, a key feature of human language is syntactic compositionality (combining words into sentences). A short discussion in the conclusion about how the current framework could be extended to encourage the emergence of multi-symbol utterances and syntactic structure would strengthen the paper's forward-looking vision.
*   **Minor Reference Formatting:** The bibliography appears to have some formatting artifacts (e.g., `\Q1998\E`). This should be corrected in the final version.

### Questions for the authors

*   Could you provide more detail on the SVD analysis? Specifically, how was the matrix of symbol usage constructed (e.g., how many image pairs were sampled) and what does the "normalized spectrum" represent?
*   In the human evaluation study, could you clarify the exact instructions given to the CrowdFlower participants? Understanding the framing of the task is important for interpreting the 68% success rate. For example, were they told the "word" came from an AI agent?
*   The agnostic sender in the initial experiments converged on a 2-symbol system that you qualitatively describe as "living-vs-non-living". Was this a consistent outcome across multiple random seeds? This is a very interesting result that aligns with findings in cognitive science, and it would be stronger if its robustness was confirmed.
