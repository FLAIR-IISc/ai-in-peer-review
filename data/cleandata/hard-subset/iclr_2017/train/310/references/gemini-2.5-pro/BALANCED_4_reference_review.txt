### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a compelling research program investigating the emergence of communication between artificial agents. The authors use a multi-agent referential game, a variant of the Lewis signaling game, where a "sender" and a "receiver" agent must coordinate to identify a target image from a pair. Starting as blank slates, the agents are trained via reinforcement learning, rewarded only for successful coordination. The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that simple feed-forward neural agents can successfully learn to communicate in this setup using a discrete vocabulary. Second, it systematically investigates how architectural inductive biases (an "informed" sender designed for comparison) and properties of the environment (removing low-level image details from common knowledge) can shape the emergent protocol, pushing it towards a more structured and semantically meaningful language. Third, through clever analysis using category purity and dimensionality reduction, the authors show that the emergent symbols correspond to high-level conceptual properties of the images rather than just low-level visual features. Finally, and most significantly, the paper proposes a novel method for grounding the agents' language in human language by mixing the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. A thoughtfully designed human study provides strong evidence that this grounding is not only effective but also generalizes, allowing humans to correctly interpret the agents' novel, metonymic use of words for objects outside the supervised training set.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that I enthusiastically recommend for acceptance. It tackles a fundamental and important problem with a clear methodology, strong results, and insightful analysis.

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an outstanding job of motivating the need for interactive, functional approaches to language learning for AI, clearly distinguishing its paradigm from purely supervised methods. The problem is framed within a simple yet powerful experimental testbed that allows for controlled and interpretable experiments.
2.  **Compelling and Rigorous Empirical Results:** The paper presents a logical progression of experiments that convincingly support its claims. The analysis goes beyond simple task accuracy to probe the semantic structure of the emergent language. The final experiment, which combines interactive learning with supervised grounding and is validated with a human study, is a standout contribution that demonstrates a concrete and promising path toward developing AI that can communicate effectively with humans.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous. The introduction is exceptionally well-written, situating the work in the broader context of AI, cognitive science, and game theory, and making a clear case for its importance. The experimental design is methodical and clean, building from a basic proof-of-concept to more sophisticated questions about the nature of the emergent language.

The analysis is multi-faceted and rigorous. The authors employ a range of techniques to understand the communication protocol, including quantitative metrics like task success and category purity, an analysis of vocabulary usage via SVD, and qualitative visualizations using t-SNE. This provides a much richer picture than a simple performance metric would.

The findings themselves are significant. The demonstration that an "informed" architecture leads to a richer vocabulary highlights the importance of inductive biases. The "object-level reference" experiment is a clever manipulation that provides direct evidence for the claim that the environment's structure shapes the emergent language.

The paper's capstone contribution is the exploration of grounding through mixed-task training. This approach is well-inspired by prior work like AlphaGo but is applied to the novel and challenging domain of communication. The resulting human study is the most compelling piece of evidence in the paper. The finding that humans can interpret the agent's metonymic generalizations (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to a picture of the sea) is a fascinating and highly encouraging result, suggesting that this method can bootstrap a flexible, human-aligned communication system.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions for improvement and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Agent Architectures:** The simplicity of the feed-forward agents is a strength, as it isolates the communication problem. However, a brief discussion on how more complex architectures (e.g., RNNs for sequences, attention mechanisms) might enable more complex, compositional language would strengthen the future work section.
*   **Scalability:** The experiments use relatively small vocabularies (10 and 100). It would be interesting to hear the authors' thoughts on how the dynamics of emergence and the effectiveness of the grounding method might change with significantly larger vocabularies (e.g., 1000s of symbols), which would be necessary for more open-ended communication.
*   **On Compositionality:** The current work focuses on referential communication with single symbols. A core feature of human language is compositionality. While beyond the scope of this paper, it would be valuable to briefly speculate on how this framework could be extended to encourage compositional language, for example, by designing tasks that require describing attributes or relations between multiple objects.
*   **Training Dynamics:** The Gibbs sampling with a temperature of 10 seems quite high, encouraging significant exploration. A comment on the sensitivity of the results to this hyperparameter and whether a curriculum (e.g., temperature annealing) was considered would be helpful.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the rebuttal period:

1.  **Equilibria and Convergence:** The introduction rightly points out that cheap talk games have multiple equilibria, including non-communicating ("babbling") ones. In your experiments, did you ever observe runs that failed to converge to a successful communication protocol, or does the REINFORCE training setup reliably find a communicating equilibrium?
2.  **SVD Dimensionality:** For the informed sender with a 100-symbol vocabulary, cross-validated SVD suggested 50 dimensions were required. How should we interpret this number in relation to the 20 ground-truth McRae categories? Does this suggest the emergent language is capturing finer-grained semantic distinctions than the provided categories?
3.  **Human Study Instructions:** Could you provide more detail on the instructions given to the human participants in the CrowdFlower experiment? Specifically, were they primed to expect non-literal or metaphorical word usage, or were they simply asked to pick the image "most related to the word"? The framing of the task could influence the degree of "charitable interpretation" from the human subjects.
