### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols between neural network agents in a cooperative, referential game setting. The core idea is to move beyond supervised learning on static conversational datasets and instead study how a functional language can be developed from scratch (*tabula rasa*) through interaction. The key contributions are:

1.  Demonstrating that simple neural agents can successfully learn to coordinate and develop an effective communication protocol in a visual referential game, using only a binary success signal for reinforcement learning.
2.  Systematically exploring how architectural inductive biases (an "informed" sender that encourages feature-wise comparison) and modifications to the game environment (forcing reference at the object-class level instead of the instance level) can guide the emergent language towards having more abstract, semantically meaningful structure.
3.  Proposing and successfully implementing a novel technique to ground the agents' emergent language in human language. This is achieved by combining the interactive referential game with a supervised image-labeling task, which encourages the agents to align their symbols with conventional human words.
4.  Providing strong evidence for the success of this grounding method through a human-subject experiment. The results show that humans can interpret the agent's communication for novel images with significantly above-chance accuracy, suggesting that the agent learns to generalize and use words in semantically plausible, even "metonymic," ways.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in AI: how to develop agents that can learn to communicate in a functional, interactive, and human-understandable way. The approach of using multi-agent cooperative games as a learning environment is well-motivated and provides a compelling alternative to purely supervised methods.
2.  The experimental design is strong, progressing logically from demonstrating basic communication emergence to carefully studying factors that influence its structure, and culminating in a novel and effective method for grounding the communication in human language. The final human evaluation experiment provides particularly convincing evidence for the paper's central claims.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main strength lies in its clear progression of experiments that build a compelling narrative. The initial experiments (Section 4) convincingly show that agents can learn to communicate effectively. More importantly, the authors don't stop there; they probe the *nature* of this communication. The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures is insightful, suggesting that even simple inductive biases can encourage a more expressive (i.e., larger vocabulary) protocol.

The "object-level reference" experiment (Section 4.1) is a clever and simple way to push the agents away from coordinating on low-level visual features towards more abstract, class-level properties. This directly addresses a key challenge in emergent communication: ensuring the learned protocol is general and conceptual rather than an overfitted solution to the specific training instances. The increase in semantic purity, while modest, supports this claim.

The highlight of the paper is the grounding experiment (Section 5). The idea of mixing interactive self-play with supervised learning, inspired by AlphaGo, is elegant and powerful. It provides a practical path towards making agent communication interpretable without requiring constant human intervention. The quantitative results are impressive: a dramatic increase in semantic purity and a 47% alignment with ground-truth labels when applicable. However, the most significant result is the human study. By testing on the ReferItGame dataset, the authors show that the learned grounding generalizes to new visual domains and that humans can understand the agent's novel word usage (e.g., "dolphin" for a sea picture). This demonstrates a form of semantic flexibility that is a hallmark of human language and is a very encouraging result for the field.

The paper is also well-written, well-situated within existing literature (connecting to multi-agent RL, game theory, and cognitive science), and the experiments are clearly described and rigorously executed.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback for strengthening the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

1.  **On the REINFORCE Algorithm and Training:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for its high variance. The chosen temperature parameter for the Gibbs distributions (τ=10) is quite high, suggesting a very soft policy is needed for stable training. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on training stability. Did the authors experiment with lower temperatures? Or with alternative policy gradient methods like A2C, which might be more sample-efficient? Adding these details would strengthen the paper's reproducibility and provide insights for future work in this area.

2.  **Analysis of the "Agnostic" Sender's Behavior:** The paper notes that the agnostic sender converges to a 2-symbol vocabulary, which qualitatively represents a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. This is a fascinating result. It would be interesting to see a more quantitative analysis of this. For example, what is the purity score if the 463 concepts are partitioned into just two meta-categories (living/non-living)? This could provide stronger evidence for this intriguing claim.

3.  **Clarity on the "Informed" Sender Architecture:** The motivation for the convolutional structure in the "informed" sender is that it has an "inductive bias towards combining the two images dimension-by-dimension." While intuitive, this could be explained more clearly. Perhaps framing it as a bias towards learning a *relational* function (e.g., identifying dimensions where the target and distractor differ) would make its advantage more explicit.

4.  **Scaling the Communication Protocol:** The current protocol is limited to a single symbol per turn. The paper briefly mentions extensions, but a short discussion in the conclusion about the challenges of scaling this framework to sequential, multi-turn dialogue would be valuable. For instance, how might the grounding approach be adapted when the "meaning" is distributed across a sequence of utterances?

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the high temperature parameter (τ=10)? How sensitive were the results (both convergence speed and final performance) to this hyperparameter? Does a lower temperature lead to faster convergence but more instability, or does it prevent convergence altogether?

2.  In the experiments without supervised grounding, the agnostic sender consistently defaults to using only 2 symbols from a vocabulary of 10 or 100. Does this suggest it gets stuck in a simple but effective local minimum? Have you tried experiments where the vocabulary size is explicitly constrained to be small (e.g., V=5) to see if this forces a more distributed use of symbols?

3.  The grounding experiment is very compelling. The supervised signal comes from single-word ImageNet labels. How do you see this approach extending to more complex scenarios where the relevant concepts aren't easily captured by a single object label (e.g., communicating about actions, relationships between objects, or abstract properties)?
