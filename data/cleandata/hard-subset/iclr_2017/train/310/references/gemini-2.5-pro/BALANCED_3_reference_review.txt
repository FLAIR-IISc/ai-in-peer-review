### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling research program into the emergence of communication between AI agents. The authors use a multi-agent cooperative referential game, a variant of the Lewis signaling game, where a "sender" agent must communicate a target image to a "receiver" agent from a pair of images. The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that simple, tabula rasa neural agents can successfully learn to coordinate and develop a communication protocol from scratch using only reinforcement learning on game success. Second, it investigates how architectural inductive biases ("informed" vs. "agnostic" senders) and game design (removing low-level common knowledge) can influence the emergent protocol, pushing it to be more abstract and conceptually grounded. The paper measures this grounding using vocabulary usage and semantic purity against human-defined categories. Finally, and most significantly, the paper proposes and validates a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, the agents develop a protocol that is not only successful but also highly interpretable to humans, as demonstrated by a crowdsourced experiment. This interpretability compellingly generalizes even to objects the agents were not explicitly taught labels for, showcasing an emergent capacity for metonymy-like semantic extension.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that tackles a fundamental and important problem: how to develop AI agents that can create and use language for coordination. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and the experiments logically build upon one another to a convincing conclusion. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The paper's primary contribution—demonstrating that combining interactive self-play with supervised learning can produce a human-interpretable communication protocol that generalizes to novel situations—is both novel and highly significant. The human evaluation provides strong evidence that this approach is a promising step towards building AI that can communicate effectively with people.
2.  **Solid Experimental Methodology:** The research is conducted rigorously. The authors systematically explore different agent architectures and game mechanics, using appropriate metrics (communication success, vocabulary size, semantic purity, SVD) to analyze the emergent languages. The progression from a basic game to a grounded communication setup is logical and effectively isolates the impact of each design choice.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

**Strengths:**
*   **Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of situating the work within the broader context of AI, drawing connections to linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory. The critique of purely supervised approaches to dialogue is well-founded, and the motivation for using interactive, multi-agent games is clear and persuasive.
*   **Incremental Experiments:** The paper builds its case effectively through a series of well-designed experiments. It starts by establishing that communication can emerge (Sec 4), then shows how to influence its properties by tweaking the environment (Sec 4.1), and finally demonstrates how to ground it in human language (Sec 5). This structure makes the paper easy to follow and the results compelling.
*   **Compelling Final Result:** The final experiment is the paper's highlight. Showing that humans can interpret the agent's language 68% of the time on out-of-domain images is a powerful result. The qualitative examples (e.g., using "dolphin" for sea) are particularly insightful, suggesting the emergence of flexible, context-dependent semantics akin to human language use. This provides a strong proof-of-concept for the proposed research direction.
*   **Clarity and Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The goals are stated clearly, the experimental setup is described well, and the results are interpreted thoughtfully.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Architectural Justification:** The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender is not fully justified. While the informed sender performs better, it feels like a somewhat arbitrary architectural choice. It's unclear why this specific 1D convolutional structure provides the right inductive bias for this task, beyond the empirical result that it works well. The claim that the agnostic architecture "nests" the informed one is also confusing and likely incorrect without further explanation.
*   **Modest Initial Results:** While statistically significant, the semantic purity scores in the initial experiments (without supervised grounding) are quite low (around 20-30%). This slightly weakens the claim that a "natural" language emerges without grounding, and instead emphasizes the critical importance of the supervised component introduced later.
*   **Simplicity of the Protocol:** The agents and the communication channel (a single discrete symbol) are intentionally simple. This is acceptable for foundational work, but it also limits the complexity of the emergent language and the tasks that can be solved. The paper could do more to acknowledge these limitations and discuss the path toward more complex, compositional language.

Despite these minor weaknesses, the paper's strengths, particularly the novelty and importance of its central contribution on grounding emergent language, are overwhelming.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to be constructive suggestions for improving the paper and for future work, and they do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

*   **Analysis of Emergent Concepts:** Before introducing supervised grounding, it would be interesting to analyze *what* concepts the agents are learning. The purity score is based on pre-defined human categories. What if the agents discover different, but equally valid, ways to partition the world? One could use methods like probing classifiers to see if the emergent symbol embeddings encode properties like color, shape, or texture.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states that hyperparameters were set without tuning. While this simplifies the experimental setup, a brief discussion or ablation study on key parameters like the Gibbs temperature (which controls the exploration/exploitation trade-off in communication) could strengthen the results' robustness.
*   **The Role of the Receiver:** The analysis focuses almost entirely on the sender's behavior. However, communication is a two-way process. An analysis of the receiver's learned representations could provide additional insights into how meaning is established between the agents.
*   **Formatting:** The bibliography appears to have some formatting errors (e.g., `\Q2002\E`). These should be corrected in the final version.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following points in their rebuttal, as it would clarify my understanding of the work.

1.  Could you please clarify the claim that "the agnostic architecture nests the informed one"? The informed sender uses a specific convolutional structure that does not seem to be a special case of the agnostic sender's fully-connected layers in a straightforward way.
2.  In the final experiment with supervised grounding, how sensitive is the model to the ratio of interactive games vs. supervised classification examples? Is a small amount of supervision sufficient to "seed" a human-aligned language, or is the 50/50 split crucial for success?
3.  The informed sender uses a larger vocabulary than the agnostic one. Have you investigated whether this is simply because its architecture is a better optimizer for this task? For instance, if the agnostic sender is trained for much longer, does its vocabulary usage eventually increase?
4.  Regarding the human evaluation, was any error analysis performed? Were there systematic cases where humans failed to understand the agent's message? This could provide valuable clues about the limits of the current grounding approach.
