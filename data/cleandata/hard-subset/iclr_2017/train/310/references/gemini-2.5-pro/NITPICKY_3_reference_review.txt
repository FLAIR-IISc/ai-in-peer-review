### **Review**

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with coordinating in a referential game. The agents, starting `tabula rasa`, must develop a communication protocol to allow the receiver to identify a target image from a pair. The authors explore whether such agents can successfully learn to communicate and whether the emergent language exhibits properties similar to human language, such as grounding in high-level conceptual categories. They introduce two sender architectures, an "agnostic" and an "informed" one with a specific inductive bias, and find the latter learns faster and uses a richer vocabulary. The paper further shows that modifying the game to obscure low-level image details encourages coordination based on more abstract, class-level properties. Finally, to align the emergent language with human language, the authors propose a hybrid training scheme that combines the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task. A crowdsourcing experiment suggests that this grounding enables humans to interpret the agents' communication to some extent, even for objects not seen during supervised training.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The experimental setup is described with insufficient rigor, particularly concerning hyperparameter choices and architectural details. The lack of justification for critical parameters (e.g., an unusually high Gibbs temperature) and the absence of sensitivity analyses call into question the robustness and reproducibility of the results.
2.  The paper is marred by a significant number of formatting errors, inconsistencies, and imprecise statements. The reference list is completely malformed, and there are discrepancies in reported training details across experiments. These issues suggest a lack of care in the paper's preparation and detract substantially from its scientific credibility.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The core ideas presented in this paper are interesting and address a significant question in AI. However, the execution and presentation are flawed to a degree that makes it unsuitable for publication at ICLR in its current form.

**Methodological Deficiencies:**

*   **Unjustified Hyperparameters:** The authors state, "We set the following hyperparameters without tuning," listing values such as an embedding dimensionality of 50 and a Gibbs temperature `τ` of 10. This is a significant weakness. The choice of `τ=10` is particularly questionable. A high temperature makes the Gibbs distribution nearly uniform, which would seem to hinder both the sender's ability to commit to a specific symbol and the receiver's ability to confidently select a target. This choice runs counter to the goal of establishing a precise communication protocol and requires a strong, explicit justification, which is absent. The paper would be much stronger with an ablation study showing the effect of these key hyperparameters on convergence and the properties of the emergent language.
*   **Vague Architectural Descriptions:** The description of the "informed sender" is difficult to follow. Phrases like "applied dimension-by-dimension" and the explanation of the filter sizes are ambiguous without a clearer diagram or a precise mathematical formulation. Furthermore, the claim that "the agnostic architecture nests the informed one" is non-obvious and is presented without any proof or detailed explanation.
*   **Insufficient Training and Evaluation Details:** The agents are trained for "50k iterations." It is unclear if this is sufficient for stable convergence. More importantly, all results appear to be from a single training run. Reporting mean and variance over multiple runs with different random seeds is standard and necessary practice to establish the robustness of RL-based findings. Additionally, the SVD analysis is intriguing but underdeveloped. The description of how "cross-validated SVD suggests 50 dimensions" is omitted, leaving the reader unable to assess the validity of this claim. The procedure for forming semantic clusters for the purity analysis is also described too briefly.

**Inconsistencies and Formatting Errors:**

*   **References:** The bibliography is in a state of complete disarray. Entries are unparsable (e.g., `Brandenburger and Dekel,? \Q1998\E`), contain stray characters, and lack standard information like publication venues. This level of formatting error is unacceptable and makes it impossible to properly check the cited literature.
*   **Inconsistent Reporting:** In Section 3, the main training regime is described as lasting for "50k iterations." However, in Section 5, the hybrid training experiment is reported to last "10k training trials." This discrepancy is unexplained. If the experimental conditions changed, this must be stated and justified.
*   **Imprecise Claims:** The paper makes several qualitative claims that lack quantitative support. For instance, the observation that the two-symbol agnostic sender converges to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is fascinating but presented as an anecdotal finding from "qualitative evaluations." Without a systematic analysis, this remains speculative.

While the research direction is promising, these numerous small-to-medium-sized flaws accumulate to create a significant deficit in scientific rigor and clarity.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Rigor is Paramount:** Please provide a comprehensive appendix with all architectural details, a complete list of hyperparameters with justifications or sensitivity analyses, and pseudo-code for the training loop.
*   **Statistical Soundness:** All key results (accuracy, purity, etc.) should be reported as the mean and standard deviation over at least 3-5 runs with different random seeds.
*   **Clarity in Writing:** Carefully revise the manuscript to eliminate ambiguity. For example, clarify the exact mechanism for the SVD analysis and the clustering process for the purity metric. Ensure the experimental setup for each result is described consistently and in full detail.
*   **Fix the Basics:** The references must be completely overhauled using a standard citation management tool (e.g., BibTeX) and a standard ICLR style file. A thorough proofread is required to catch typos and grammatical errors.
*   **Strengthen Connections:** The link to game theory ("cheap talk," equilibrium selection) is a strength. This could be reinforced by framing the supervised grounding experiment more explicitly as an equilibrium selection mechanism that favors human-interpretable solutions.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a detailed justification for using a Gibbs temperature of `τ=10`? How does performance change with lower, more conventional temperature values (e.g., `τ ≤ 1`)? Does this high temperature not encourage a nearly random policy, thereby slowing down or destabilizing learning?
2.  Can you please clarify the training length discrepancy between the experiments in Section 4 (50k iterations) and Section 5 (10k trials)?
3.  How was the subset of 100 labels for the supervised grounding task selected from the full set of 463 concepts? Was it a random subset, or was it curated to be balanced across the 20 McRae super-categories? This choice could significantly impact the generalization observed in the human evaluation experiment.
