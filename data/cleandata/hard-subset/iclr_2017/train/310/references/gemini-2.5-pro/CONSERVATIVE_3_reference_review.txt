### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols between two neural network agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," tasked with cooperating in a referential game. The agents begin as blank slates and, through reinforcement learning based on task success, develop a discrete symbolic language to identify a target image from a pair of distractors. The primary contributions are: (1) demonstrating that simple neural agents can successfully learn to communicate in this setting; (2) analyzing the properties of the emergent language, suggesting that it captures high-level semantic concepts rather than just low-level visual features; (3) showing that modifying the game's environment (e.g., by forcing reference at the object-category level) can steer the language towards greater abstraction; and (4) proposing a hybrid approach that combines interactive game-playing with a supervised classification task to ground the emergent language in human-understandable terms, leading to a protocol that humans can interpret with above-chance accuracy.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** The work introduces a novel learning paradigm but fails to situate its performance against established, non-interactive methods. The central claim is that interactive learning is superior to "canned" supervised approaches for learning the functional aspects of communication, yet this is never empirically validated. A proper baseline using a pre-trained image classifier as the sender is necessary to quantify the benefits of the proposed emergent communication framework.
2.  **Insufficiently Rigorous Evaluation of Emergent Language:** The claims about the semantic properties and superiority of the emergent language rest on analyses (e.g., purity score, SVD, t-SNE plots) that are preliminary and lack comparative depth. While interesting, these metrics do not provide conclusive evidence that the learned protocol is more structured or useful than one derived from a strong supervised model.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's premise—that interaction is key to learning functional communication—is compelling. However, the experimental validation falls short of the standards required to endorse this new approach over existing, well-understood ones.

The most significant weakness is the absence of strong baselines. The paper rightly critiques purely supervised methods but then fails to include them as a point of comparison. A straightforward and essential baseline would be to use a pre-trained network (e.g., the same VGG network used for feature extraction) as a fixed sender. In this setup, the sender would output its top-1 class prediction for the target image, and only the receiver would be trained via reinforcement learning to interpret this signal. This would directly test how well an agent can learn to utilize a powerful, human-annotated (but non-interactive) communication protocol. It would provide a performance ceiling/floor for task success and, more importantly, a crucial comparison point for the human interpretability experiment in Section 5. Without such a baseline, it is impossible to judge whether the 68% human accuracy is impressive or underwhelming. It's plausible that humans could achieve higher accuracy interpreting the direct output of a standard classifier.

The evaluation of the language's semantic structure is also not fully convincing. The "purity" metric is heavily dependent on the 20 pre-defined McRae categories. While significantly above chance, this only shows that the agents' emergent concepts partially align with one specific human-defined ontology. Agents could form a different, yet equally valid and internally consistent, conceptual space that would be unfairly penalized by this metric. The SVD analysis is mentioned only in passing, with the claim that the language "requires multiple dimensions to summarize" being asserted rather than demonstrated with sufficient detail. Similarly, t-SNE plots are useful for visualization but are not a substitute for quantitative analysis.

Furthermore, the distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures feels somewhat arbitrary. The "informed" sender, with its dimension-wise convolutional structure, seems to possess a very strong, hand-designed inductive bias towards comparing image features. While it performs better, the paper does not deeply investigate *why* this specific architecture promotes a richer vocabulary. Is this a general principle, or a coincidental finding tied to this specific task and feature representation? A more thorough ablation study would be needed to support the architectural claims.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting new direction, it feels more like a proof of concept than a method that has been rigorously validated. The claims of developing a more "natural" or "interpretable" language are not sufficiently substantiated through comparison with powerful, established methods from supervised learning.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   **Stability of REINFORCE:** The REINFORCE algorithm is known for its high variance. The learning curves in Figure 2 show a single run. Are these results representative? Reporting the mean and variance over multiple runs with different random seeds would significantly strengthen the claims about convergence speed and final performance.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Many hyperparameters (embedding dimensionality, temperature) were set without tuning. How sensitive are the results, and particularly the properties of the emergent language, to these choices? For instance, a lower temperature in the Gibbs distributions would lead to a more deterministic policy; how would this affect exploration and the final vocabulary size?
*   **Human Evaluation Baseline:** As mentioned above, the human evaluation is the most interesting result but lacks a critical baseline. How well would humans perform in the same task if the "word" they were given was the top-1 label for the target image from the pre-trained VGG network? This would help disentangle the benefit of the emergent "metonymic" communication from the general capacity of humans to link concepts.
*   **Object-Level Reference Experiment:** The motivation for this experiment is to remove "low-level common knowledge." However, by showing different image instances of the same class, you are also introducing significant noise. Is the small increase in purity a result of the agents being forced to learn abstract properties, or is it an artifact of the task becoming harder, leading to a simpler, more clustered communication strategy?
