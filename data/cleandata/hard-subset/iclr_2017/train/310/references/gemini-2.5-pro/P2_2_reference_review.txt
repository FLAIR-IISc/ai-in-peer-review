### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols between two neural agents, a sender and a receiver, in the context of a cooperative referential game. The agents, starting *tabula rasa*, must develop a shared language to identify a target image from a distractor. The core contributions are threefold: First, the paper demonstrates that simple feed-forward agents trained with reinforcement learning can successfully develop an effective communication protocol for this task using real-world images. Second, it systematically explores how environmental and architectural factors influence the properties of the emergent language. Specifically, it compares an "agnostic" versus an "informed" sender architecture and manipulates the game's "common knowledge" to encourage communication about abstract object properties rather than low-level image features. Third, and most significantly, the paper proposes and validates a novel method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language. By combining the interactive game-playing (self-play) with a supervised image-labeling task, the authors show that the agents' communication becomes significantly more interpretable and, crucially, that this interpretability generalizes to new concepts and tasks, as validated by a human study.

**2. Recommendation: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Rigorous Investigation:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem in AI—the emergence of grounded, interpretable communication—and moves beyond simply demonstrating task success. It methodically investigates the *properties* of the emergent language using well-chosen metrics (e.g., purity) and controlled experiments.
2.  **Novel and Effective Grounding Method:** The proposed technique of combining self-play with supervised learning is an elegant and effective solution to the grounding problem. The final experiment, which demonstrates that this grounding generalizes to a new dataset and is interpretable by humans, is a compelling and significant result.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, framing the work within the rich contexts of AI, linguistics, and game theory. The introduction does an excellent job of distinguishing this research program from purely supervised approaches, pre-programmed multi-agent systems, and human-in-the-loop methods, clearly carving out its unique contribution.

The experimental design is logical and progressive. The initial experiments (Section 4) establish a strong baseline, confirming that communication can emerge spontaneously. The analysis here is solid, using SVD to investigate symbol redundancy and purity scores to measure semantic coherence against human-defined categories. The finding that a simple 2-symbol language converges to a "living vs. non-living" distinction is a fascinating and plausible result that connects well to cognitive science literature. The architectural comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender provides valuable insight into how inductive biases can shape the resulting protocol.

The most compelling part of the paper is Section 5. The idea to ground the emergent language by mixing interactive RL with supervised learning is inspired by the success of hybrid approaches like AlphaGo but applied to the novel domain of communication. The results are impressive: not only does purity dramatically increase, but the agents begin to use symbols that correspond to their "correct" English labels. The final human evaluation on the ReferItGame dataset provides powerful evidence for the central claim. Showing that humans can interpret the agent's messages (68% accuracy) even when the message is not a literal description of the target (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to a sea scene) demonstrates a flexible, metonymic-like generalization. This suggests the agents have learned a grounded conceptual space that aligns with human intuition, which is a significant step toward developing agents that can naturally communicate with people. The approach is also highly scalable compared to methods that require constant human interaction during training.

The paper is clearly written, the experiments are well-described, and the claims are well-supported by the empirical results.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to improve it further. These are suggestions for refinement and do not detract from my overall positive assessment.

*   **Training Details:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for its high variance. A brief discussion of training stability would be beneficial. Were the results, especially the performance curves and final accuracies, averaged over multiple random seeds? Demonstrating robustness across runs would strengthen the empirical claims.
*   **Informed Sender Architecture:** The "informed" sender architecture, with its dimension-wise convolutions, seems specifically designed to perform comparisons between the two image vectors. While effective, it would be useful to discuss the generality of this inductive bias. Would other architectures that encourage relational reasoning (e.g., using attention mechanisms) yield similar results? This would help clarify whether the specific architecture is critical or if the general principle of encouraging comparison is the key.
*   **Magnitude of Effect in Section 4.1:** The experiment that removes low-level image details by showing different instances of the same object class is conceptually elegant. However, the reported increase in purity was quite small. It might be worth hypothesizing why this manipulation, which seems powerful in theory, did not produce a more dramatic effect on the semantic structure of the emergent language.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify if the key results (e.g., accuracy, purity scores) were averaged over multiple training runs with different initializations? Given the potential for variance in RL training, this would help assess the robustness of the findings.
2.  In the final grounding experiment (Section 5), the sender was trained on a supervised task with 100 specific object labels. How was this subset of 100 labels selected from the full set of 463 concepts? Does the choice of grounding vocabulary significantly influence the agent's ability to generalize its communication to new concepts?
3.  The receiver architecture computes dot products between the symbol embedding and the image embeddings. This seems to imply that symbols and images are mapped to a shared space. Have you analyzed this learned embedding space? For example, in the grounded experiment, do the embeddings for the symbol "dog" cluster near the embeddings of images of dogs? This could provide further insight into how grounding is achieved.
