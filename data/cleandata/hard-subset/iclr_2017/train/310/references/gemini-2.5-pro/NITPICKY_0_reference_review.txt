### Review

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," tasked with coordinating in a referential game. The agents, starting tabula rasa, must develop a communication protocol to allow the receiver to identify a target image from a pair. The paper makes several claims: (1) it demonstrates that agents can successfully learn to communicate and solve this task; (2) it explores how architectural choices (an "agnostic" vs. "informed" sender) and game design (removing low-level image features) influence the semantic properties of the emergent language, measured by vocabulary usage and cluster purity against human-defined categories; (3) it proposes a novel method for grounding the emergent language in human-understandable terms by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task; and (4) it provides evidence through a human study that this grounding generalizes, allowing humans to interpret the agents' communication even for concepts not seen during supervised training.

**2. Recommendation: Reject**

While the research program is interesting and the goal of creating human-interpretable emergent languages is important, I must recommend rejection. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Lack of Scientific Rigor and Methodological Clarity:** The paper contains numerous ambiguities, imprecise technical descriptions, and questionable methodological choices that undermine the confidence in its empirical claims. Key hyperparameters are left unjustified, statistical analyses are not fully explained, and the core learning setup is described with imprecise notation.
2.  **Poor Manuscript Preparation:** The paper is rife with formatting inconsistencies, and most critically, the provided text references numerous figures and tables (Figures 1-4, Tables 1-2) that are not included. The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted, containing errors and inconsistencies that suggest a lack of scholarly diligence. These issues make a proper assessment of the work impossible and indicate the manuscript is not ready for publication.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My recommendation is based on a meticulous examination of the paper's details:

**Methodological and Technical Flaws:**

*   **Imprecise Formulation:** The general framework in Section 2 uses sloppy notation. The sender's input is described as `θS(iL, iR, t)`, which is incorrect; `θS` represents the model parameters, not a function that takes them as input. The policy should be defined as a function of the state, parameterized by `θS`. Similarly, the loss function `-IEr̃[R(r̃)]` is imprecise. The expectation should be taken over the trajectory distribution induced by both the sender's and receiver's stochastic policies.
*   **Unjustified Hyperparameters:** The choice of a Gibbs distribution temperature `τ = 10` is highly unusual and goes without justification. Such a high temperature would make the action-selection distribution almost uniform, which seems to severely hinder learning a deterministic, effective communication protocol. This choice requires a strong rationale, which is absent. Other hyperparameters are also stated to be set "without tuning," which, while avoiding overfitting to a specific setup, can also mean the results are not robust or representative of the models' capabilities.
*   **Vague Analytical Claims:** The paper claims the "agnostic architecture nests the informed one." This is a strong claim that is not obvious and requires a formal explanation or proof, which is not provided. In Section 4, the SVD analysis relies on "cross-validated SVD," a non-standard term that is not defined, and the method for selecting 50 dimensions is not described.
*   **Superficial Evaluation:** The use of Purity as the sole cluster quality metric is insufficient; it is known to perform poorly on imbalanced clusters. More standard metrics like Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) or Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) should have been included. The claim that the 2-symbol language corresponds to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is based on "qualitative evaluations" and presented anecdotally, lacking rigorous evidence.
*   **Incomplete Human Evaluation:** The CrowdFlower experiment in Section 5 lacks crucial details for assessing its validity. There is no mention of quality control for crowd workers, no report of inter-annotator agreement, and crucially, no baseline for comparison. A random baseline is 50%, so 68% is a positive but perhaps not overwhelming result. A "human ceiling" baseline (e.g., how well a human performs the task given a word chosen by another human) is necessary to contextualize the 68% performance.

**Presentation and Formatting Issues:**

*   **Missing Figures and Tables:** The paper is fundamentally unverifiable without the figures and tables it constantly refers to. The core quantitative results (e.g., performance in Table 1, purity scores in Table 2, training curves in Figure 2, t-SNE plots in Figure 3) cannot be assessed. This is a critical omission.
*   **Bibliography:** The reference list is in a state of disarray. Many entries have formatting artifacts (e.g., `? \Q1998\E,`), are incomplete (e.g., missing publisher/venue details), and contain inconsistencies. For instance, Caramazza & Shelton is cited as (1998) in the text but listed with the year `? \Q2002\E,` in the references. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a scientific publication.
*   **General Sloppiness:** The manuscript has minor but frequent inconsistencies, such as the footnote for "Work done while at Facebook AI Research" not being attached to an author, the mixing of "data-set" and "dataset," and awkward possessive citations ("McRae et al.’s"). While individually small, their accumulation detracts from the paper's professionalism and credibility.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form, I believe the research direction is promising. I offer the following suggestions for improvement:

*   The manuscript requires a complete and thorough revision for clarity, correctness, and consistency. A standard citation manager (e.g., BibTeX) should be used to fix the bibliography.
*   All figures and tables must be included and clearly captioned.
*   The methodology section should be rewritten with precise mathematical notation. All hyperparameter choices, especially unconventional ones like `τ=10`, must be justified or ablated.
*   The empirical evaluation should be strengthened by including more robust metrics (NMI/ARI), providing necessary details for all experiments (e.g., human studies), and substantiating qualitative claims with quantitative analysis where possible.
*   The connection to game theory, while mentioned, could be more deeply integrated. For example, are the learned communication protocols Nash equilibria of the signaling game? If so, which equilibrium is selected?

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To better assess the work, I would need clarification on the following points:

1.  Could you please provide the missing figures and tables that are central to your results?
2.  Can you provide a detailed justification for using a temperature `τ=10` in the Gibbs distributions? How does this choice impact the learning dynamics and the final converged policies?
3.  Could you please elaborate on the "cross-validated SVD" method and how the number of dimensions was chosen?
4.  Regarding the human evaluation: what were the quality control measures, what was the inter-annotator agreement, and can you provide a chance and/or human-level baseline to better contextualize the 68% success rate?
5.  Can you formally explain how the agnostic sender architecture "nests" the informed one?
