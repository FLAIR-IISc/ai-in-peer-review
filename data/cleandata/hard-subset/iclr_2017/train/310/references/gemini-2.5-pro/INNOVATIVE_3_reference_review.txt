### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a compelling research program aimed at studying the emergence of communication between AI agents. The core idea is to move away from traditional supervised learning on static conversational datasets and instead use interactive, multi-agent referential games where agents must develop a communication protocol from scratch to achieve a cooperative goal. The agents, a sender and a receiver, are implemented as simple neural networks and start as blank slates. The authors investigate whether these tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to communicate, and what properties of the environment and agent architecture influence the resulting emergent language to be more "natural" or human-like.

The key contributions are:
1.  A demonstration that neural agents can successfully develop a communication protocol to solve a referential game using real-world images, learning purely from a binary success/failure reward signal.
2.  An analysis of the emergent language, showing that it develops semantic structure related to high-level conceptual categories (measured by cluster purity) rather than just low-level visual features.
3.  A novel method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language. This is achieved through a hybrid training scheme that mixes the interactive referential game with a supervised image-labeling task.
4.  A human evaluation study showing that this grounding allows humans to interpret the agents' communication with significant success, even for concepts the agents were not explicitly taught labels for, suggesting a form of compositional or metonymic generalization.

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It presents a bold and highly novel research direction that tackles a fundamental problem in AI: how to build agents that can learn the functional, goal-oriented aspects of communication through interaction. The work's primary strength is its conceptual contribution—framing language learning as an emergent phenomenon in a multi-agent system and showing a plausible path to grounding this emergent language in human terms. This prioritization of a novel, foundational paradigm over incremental improvements on existing benchmarks is precisely the kind of research that ICLR should champion.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength is its vision. The critique of purely supervised approaches to language as missing the "interactive" and "functional" aspects of communication is spot-on. The proposed framework, based on signaling games, provides a clean, minimalistic testbed to explore these ideas from first principles. While the agent architectures and the game itself are simple, this simplicity is a virtue, as it allows the focus to remain on the core dynamics of language emergence.

The experimental design is thoughtful and systematically builds the paper's argument.
*   First, the authors establish that communication can emerge successfully.
*   Second, they manipulate the agent's inductive bias (agnostic vs. informed sender) and the game's common knowledge (image-level vs. object-level reference) to show that environmental and architectural pressures can shape the emergent language toward greater abstraction. This is a clever way to probe the properties of the learned protocol.
*   Third, and most impressively, the hybrid supervised/interactive training approach in Section 5 is a genuinely novel idea. It elegantly bridges the gap between a completely arbitrary emergent code and a human-interpretable language, drawing inspiration from AlphaGo's combination of self-play and learning from human experts.

The final human evaluation provides compelling, albeit preliminary, evidence that this approach works. The finding that the sender can use words in a "metonymic" way (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to an image of the sea) and that humans can correctly interpret this usage is a fascinating result. It suggests that the agents are not just memorizing label-image pairs but are learning a flexible mapping grounded in a shared semantic space, which is a key characteristic of human linguistic creativity. This result alone is significant enough to warrant acceptance.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for my decision.

*   **Analysis of Failures:** The human study reports 68% accuracy. While impressive, an analysis of the 32% of cases where humans failed to guess the target could be very illuminating. Are these failures random, or do they reveal systematic differences in the semantic associations made by the agent versus the human participants? This could provide valuable insights for improving the grounding process.
*   **Scalability and Compositionality:** The current work uses a single symbol for communication. A natural next step is to explore more complex communication protocols involving sequences of symbols. How might this framework be extended to encourage the emergence of compositional language, where symbol meanings combine to create new meanings? A brief discussion of the potential path forward would strengthen the paper's conclusion.
*   **The "Living vs. Non-living" Hypothesis:** The claim that the 2-symbol agnostic agent converges on a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is intriguing. Could you provide more direct evidence? For example, showing the top object categories most frequently associated with each of the two symbols would make this claim more concrete.
*   **Choice of Supervised Labels:** In the hybrid training experiment, how was the subset of 100 ImageNet labels for the supervised task chosen? Was it a random sample, or curated to represent a diverse set of concepts? The nature of this set could significantly impact the generalization observed in the human study.
*   **Role of Temperature:** The temperature parameter in the Gibbs distributions was fixed at 10. This value seems high and would encourage exploration. Have you investigated the sensitivity of the learning dynamics and the final emergent protocol to this parameter? It seems plausible that a curriculum (e.g., annealing the temperature) could affect convergence speed and the nature of the learned language.
