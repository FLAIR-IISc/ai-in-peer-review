### **Summary**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between neural agents in a cooperative, multi-agent setting. The authors propose a simple referential game where a "sender" agent must transmit a symbol from a discrete vocabulary to a "receiver" agent to help it identify a target image from a pair. The central contributions are threefold: 1) Demonstrating that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn a communication protocol to solve this task using reinforcement learning. 2) Showing that specific inductive biases in the sender's architecture and modifications to the game's structure (e.g., forcing reference at the object-category level) can nudge the emergent language towards greater semantic structure, as measured by clustering purity against human-defined categories. 3) Proposing and validating a novel training paradigm that combines this interactive game-playing with a supervised image-labeling task to ground the emergent language in human-understandable terms. The most compelling result is a human study showing that this grounded language generalizes, allowing humans to interpret the agents' messages even for novel objects, often through plausible semantic extensions like metonymy.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that I recommend for acceptance. It tackles a fundamental problem in AI—the emergence of functional, grounded language—with a clear and thoughtfully designed set of experiments. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The paper moves beyond prior work in multi-agent communication (e.g., Foerster et al., 2016; Sukhbaatar et al., 2016) which primarily focused on whether agents could succeed at a task. Here, the central question is about the *nature* of the emergent language and how to align it with human semantics, a crucial step for human-AI collaboration.
2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experimental Design:** The paper presents a logical progression of experiments, starting with a baseline and then introducing controlled variations to test specific hypotheses. The object-level reference game (Section 4.1) is a simple but clever manipulation to force abstraction. The final experiment, combining interactive self-play with supervised grounding and testing with human subjects, is highly novel and provides convincing evidence for the paper's main claims.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths lie in its clear motivation, elegant experimental design, and compelling results.

*   **Motivation and Framing:** The introduction provides excellent context, positioning the work at the intersection of multi-agent RL, computational linguistics, and game theory. It correctly identifies the limitations of purely supervised approaches for learning the *functional* aspects of language and frames the research program clearly. The connection to Lewis' signaling games and the "cheap talk" literature shows a deep appreciation of the problem's history.

*   **Methodology:** The referential game is a classic, well-understood paradigm, making the results easy to interpret. The agent architectures are simple but sufficient for the task, and the distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" senders provides a nice testbed for the role of inductive bias. Using REINFORCE is a standard choice for this setup. The analyses are appropriate; using vocabulary usage statistics, SVD for measuring effective dimensionality, and cluster purity for semantic coherence are all sensible metrics that support the narrative.

*   **Key Findings and their Implications:**
    *   The discovery that the "informed" sender, with its dimension-wise comparison bias, learns faster and uses a richer vocabulary is an interesting result about architectural priors.
    *   The core contribution is the hybrid training approach in Section 5. The idea of using a supervised task not as the end goal, but as a way to inject a human-centric semantic bias into an emergent protocol is powerful. This is a practical and scalable alternative to having humans in the loop constantly.
    *   The human evaluation is the paper's strongest component. The fact that humans can achieve 68% accuracy in interpreting the agent's messages on a completely different dataset (ReferItGame) is a striking demonstration of generalization. The qualitative examples (e.g., 'dolphin' for sea, 'fence' for land) are fascinating and suggest the agents have learned a degree of semantic abstraction that mirrors human cognitive flexibility (e.g., metonymy). This result has significant implications, suggesting a path towards building AI systems that can develop their own efficient communication protocols while remaining interpretable to human partners.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are meant as constructive suggestions and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

*   **Exploration of Agent Architectures:** The sender and receiver are simple feed-forward networks. While sufficient, it would be interesting to discuss the potential role of more complex architectures. For instance, would a recurrent architecture that processes the target and distractor sequentially change the communication strategy? The current "informed" architecture imposes a specific comparison structure; a more general attention mechanism could be an interesting alternative to explore.

*   **Analysis of Failure Cases:** The human evaluation result (68% success) is strong, but the 32% failure rate is also informative. A brief analysis of common failure modes could be very insightful. Are there specific types of semantic leaps that humans consistently fail to understand? Do failures correlate with visual similarity between the target and distractor? This would add more nuance to the findings.

*   **Choice of RL Algorithm:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for high variance. While the results demonstrate its sufficiency here, a brief mention of whether training was stable or if more advanced policy gradient methods (e.g., A2C, PPO) were considered would be useful for reproducibility and context.

*   **Scalability to Compositionality:** The current vocabulary consists of atomic symbols for objects. The paper sets the stage for a research program, and a natural next step is compositionality. A short discussion in the conclusion about how this framework might be extended to encourage the emergence of syntax (e.g., describing objects by their attributes or spatial relations) would strengthen the paper's forward-looking perspective.

### **Clarifying Questions for Authors**

1.  **On the "Informed" Sender:** The informed sender's convolutional structure provides a strong inductive bias for feature-wise comparison. Could a sufficiently large "agnostic" MLP theoretically learn a similar comparison function? Is the primary benefit of the informed architecture one of sample efficiency, or does it enable learning a qualitatively different kind of protocol that the agnostic one cannot?

2.  **Role of Temperature in Gibbs Sampling:** The temperature parameter (τ=10) for sampling actions and symbols seems quite high, which would encourage exploration by making the policy more uniform. Was this value critical for learning? How sensitive are the results to this hyperparameter, and what is the effect of annealing it over time?

3.  **Details of the Human Evaluation:** The 68% accuracy is a powerful average result. Could you provide more detail on the distribution of performance? For example, were some symbols almost perfectly understood by humans while others were at chance level? Did inter-annotator agreement vary significantly across different symbol-image pairs?
