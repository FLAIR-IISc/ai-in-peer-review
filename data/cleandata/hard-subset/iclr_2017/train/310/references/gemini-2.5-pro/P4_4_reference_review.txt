### **Summary**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols between neural agents in the context of cooperative, multi-agent referential games. The primary goal is to understand how to design environments that foster the development of a language that is not only effective for coordination but is also structured in a way that resembles human language and is ultimately human-interpretable. The authors make several key contributions. First, they demonstrate that tabula rasa agents, implemented as simple feed-forward networks trained with reinforcement learning, can successfully learn to communicate to solve a referential game involving real-world images. Second, they analyze the properties of the emergent protocol, showing through cluster purity analysis and visualizations that the learned symbols correspond to high-level conceptual properties of the images rather than just low-level visual features. Third, they show that modifying the game's "common knowledge" (by showing agents different instances of the same object class) nudges the agents toward a more abstract, category-level communication system. Finally, and most significantly, the paper proposes and validates a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. A human study demonstrates that this combined training approach leads to a communication protocol that is interpretable by humans, even when generalizing to novel objects and contexts not seen during supervised training.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Systematic Investigation of Emergent Language Properties:** The paper moves beyond simply demonstrating that communication can emerge. It provides a methodical investigation into the semantic properties of the emergent protocol and explores how architectural choices (informed vs. agnostic sender) and game design (object-level reference) influence these properties. This represents a significant step toward understanding and controlling emergent communication.
2.  **Novel Grounding Method with Strong Empirical Validation:** The primary contribution is the novel method of combining interactive self-play with a supervised objective to ground the emergent language in human-understandable terms. The subsequent human evaluation is a crucial and compelling piece of evidence. It not only validates the method but also reveals insightful findings about the agents learning flexible, "metonymic" word usage, demonstrating a non-trivial generalization that is key for robust human-AI communication.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is strong across the core assessment criteria of clarity, technical soundness, and novelty.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly motivates the problem by contrasting the interactive paradigm with supervised approaches and situates the work within multiple relevant fields (multi-agent RL, game theory, cognitive science). The experimental setup, including the game framework and agent architectures, is described with sufficient detail to be understood. The progression of experiments is logical, building from basic communication emergence to the more sophisticated grounding experiments. Results are presented clearly with appropriate metrics and visualizations.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is sound and appropriate for the questions being investigated. The referential game is a standard and well-motivated paradigm for studying communication. The use of the REINFORCE algorithm is a standard choice for this non-differentiable communication setting. The evaluation is rigorous and multi-faceted, employing a good mix of quantitative metrics (communication success, vocabulary usage, SVD dimensionality, purity) and qualitative analysis (t-SNE plots, example inspection). The inclusion of a human study to test the final claim of interpretability adds significant weight and credibility to the findings.

*   **Novelty and Significance:** While prior work has shown that agents can develop communication protocols, this paper's novelty lies in its focus on the *qualities* of that communication and how to align it with human language. The central novel contribution is the combined training paradigm. This approach, inspired by AlphaGo's mix of self-play and supervised learning, is elegantly applied to the problem of language grounding. The finding that this grounding generalizes to out-of-domain images in a way that is interpretable to humans is highly significant. It suggests a scalable path toward creating agents that can learn the functional, communicative aspects of language through interaction, while leveraging supervised data to ensure the resulting language is not entirely alien to humans. The discovery of metonymic generalization is a particularly interesting result with implications for both machine learning and cognitive science.

### **Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The intuition behind the "informed sender" architecture could be elaborated upon. While the paper notes its inductive bias for dimension-by-dimension comparison, a more detailed explanation of why this specific structure would lead to a richer vocabulary (as observed empirically) would strengthen the paper's analysis of architectural influences.
*   The SVD analysis is a good method for quantifying the effective dimensionality of the language. The text mentions that "cross-validated SVD suggests 50 dimensions." A brief note in an appendix or footnote on how this cross-validation was performed would be helpful for reproducibility and clarity.
*   In the human evaluation experiment (Section 5), the choice of the 100 labels for the supervised task could potentially influence the results. It would be useful to know if these were selected randomly or based on specific criteria (e.g., frequency, visual distinctiveness).
*   The choice of a high temperature (`τ=10`) for the Gibbs/softmax distributions is somewhat unusual, as it encourages a more uniform, exploratory sampling policy. It would be interesting to comment on the sensitivity of the results to this hyperparameter. It is possible that this high temperature is beneficial for discovering a diverse vocabulary.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   In Section 5, you demonstrate that combining supervised learning with interactive playing grounds the emergent language. Is there a one-to-one mapping between the vocabulary symbols `V` and the 100 supervised labels? In other words, when the sender uses symbol `s_i`, is this always presented to the human raters as the `i`-th word from the supervised label set?
*   The human success rate of 68% on the ReferItGame experiment is very compelling. For comparison, what was the trained receiver agent's success rate on this same set of out-of-domain image pairs? This would help contextualize the human performance and understand how the "metonymic" generalizations are processed by the agent's original communication partner.
*   Could you provide more intuition on why the "informed sender" architecture, with its 1D convolutions across image embeddings, consistently develops a communication protocol with a larger vocabulary than the "agnostic" sender? Does this structure prevent the agents from finding a simpler, lower-dimensional solution (like the "living-vs-non-living" distinction)?
