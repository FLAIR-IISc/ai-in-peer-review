### Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between AI agents in a cooperative, multi-agent setting. The authors propose a framework based on referential games, a variant of Lewis' signaling games, where two neural agents—a sender and a receiver—must develop a communication protocol to successfully identify a target image from a pair. The central contributions are:
1.  A demonstration that *tabula rasa* neural agents can successfully learn to communicate and coordinate in this game, achieving near-perfect performance.
2.  An analysis of how environmental and architectural factors shape the emergent language. The paper shows that an inductive bias in the sender's architecture (the "informed" sender) leads to faster learning and a richer vocabulary. It also shows that altering the game's "common knowledge" (by showing agents different instances of the same object class) encourages the development of more abstract, conceptual symbols.
3.  A novel and effective method for grounding the emergent language in human language. By combining the interactive referential game with a supervised image-labeling task, the agents are encouraged to adopt human-interpretable symbols.
4.  Strong empirical evidence, including a human crowdsourcing experiment, that this grounding is not only successful but also generalizes to new domains and tasks. The resulting communication protocol is shown to be interpretable by humans with high accuracy, exhibiting flexible, metonymic-like word usage.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed series of experiments that systematically explore the emergence of communication. The research questions are clear, the methodology is appropriate, and the results convincingly support the claims.
2.  The proposed method of grounding agent communication by mixing interactive reinforcement learning with supervised learning is a significant and novel contribution. The subsequent validation with human subjects provides compelling evidence for the method's potential to bridge the gap between abstract agent protocols and human-understandable language.

### Supporting Arguments

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly situates the work within the broader context of AI, multi-agent systems, and game theory. The description of the referential game framework, agent architectures, and experimental progression is easy to follow. Figures and tables are used effectively to illustrate architectures, present results, and provide qualitative examples that enhance understanding.

**Technical Soundness:** The approach is methodologically sound. The use of a referential game is a standard and well-motivated paradigm for studying emergent communication. The agent models, while simple feed-forward networks, are sufficient to demonstrate the core claims. The training via the REINFORCE algorithm is a standard choice for this type of discrete-action policy gradient problem. The evaluation is rigorous and multifaceted, employing not just task performance (communication success) but also metrics designed to probe the semantic structure of the emergent language, such as vocabulary usage, SVD analysis, and cluster purity. The capstone experiment involving human evaluation provides a strong, objective measure of the ultimate goal: human-interpretable communication.

**Novelty:** While prior work has explored emergent communication in multi-agent settings, this paper makes several novel contributions. First, it provides a systematic study of how specific architectural biases and game rules influence the properties of the learned protocol. Second, and most importantly, the hybrid training scheme that integrates interactive game-playing with supervised learning (inspired by AlphaGo) is a novel approach to the grounding problem. Unlike purely supervised approaches that learn from static datasets, this method allows agents to learn the *functional* aspects of language use while ensuring the vocabulary remains aligned with human conventions. The demonstration that this grounding generalizes to a new task (ReferItGame) and enables successful human-agent communication is a significant finding.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   The hyperparameters are stated to be set "without tuning." While this is a testament to the robustness of the framework, a brief justification for some of the choices would be beneficial. For example, the Gibbs temperature (`τ=10`) is quite high, encouraging exploration. A short note on why this value was chosen or how sensitive the results are to it would add to the paper's completeness.
*   The SVD analysis in Section 4 is an interesting way to measure vocabulary complexity, but the description is brief. A more detailed explanation of how the cross-validation was performed to select the number of dimensions would strengthen this analysis.
*   The receiver's architecture is quite simple (dot product between symbol and image embeddings). It would be interesting to discuss whether a more complex receiver might alter the communication protocol that emerges, perhaps by being better able to disambiguate messages, thus placing different pressures on the sender.

### Questions for the Authors

Your responses to these questions would help clarify aspects of the work and strengthen my confidence in the assessment.
1.  The REINFORCE algorithm is known for its high variance. Given the smooth and rapid convergence shown in Figure 2, were any variance reduction techniques, such as using a baseline, employed during training? Or is the learning problem simple enough that vanilla REINFORCE is stable?
2.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind the "informed" sender's architecture? Specifically, why is applying 1D convolutions dimension-by-dimension across the two image embeddings a useful inductive bias? Does this structure explicitly encourage the network to compute a form of difference vector between the target and distractor representations?
3.  The generalization demonstrated in the human evaluation experiment is very compelling. The sender, trained on ImageNet object labels, communicates effectively about bounding boxes in the ReferItGame dataset. What do you believe is the mechanism behind this generalization? Is the sender mapping visual features present in the bounding boxes (e.g., texture, color) to the most semantically related object from its supervised training (e.g., a patch of grass becomes "fence")?
