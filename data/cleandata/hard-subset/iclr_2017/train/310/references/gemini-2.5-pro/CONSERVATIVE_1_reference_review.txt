### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," tasked with coordinating in a referential game. The agents, starting "tabula rasa," must develop a communication protocol to allow the receiver to identify a target image from a pair of images. The authors explore whether successful communication protocols emerge, what properties these protocols have, and how the game environment can be modified to encourage the development of a more "natural" and human-interpretable language. Key contributions include demonstrating that agents can successfully learn to coordinate, showing that an "informed" sender architecture with an inductive bias for comparison learns faster, and analyzing the semantic structure of the emergent symbol system using category purity. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that modifying the game to remove low-level image details as common knowledge pushes the agents toward more abstract representations. Finally, they propose a hybrid approach, inspired by AlphaGo, that combines the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task to ground the emergent symbols in human language, showing that this leads to a protocol that is partially interpretable by humans even on out-of-domain images.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper explores an interesting and ambitious research direction, the work in its current form is too preliminary and lacks the rigorous validation against established methods required for publication. The primary reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Insufficient Baselines:** The paper frames its approach as an alternative to standard supervised learning for conversational agents but provides no direct comparison to any supervised baseline, even a simple one, on the proposed task.
2.  **Overstated Claims Relative to a Simplified Setting:** The claims about developing human-understandable AI are not fully supported by the very constrained experimental setup (single-symbol communication, reliance on pre-trained visual features), which significantly simplifies the problem of language emergence.

**Supporting Arguments**

The core thesis of the paper is that interactive, multi-agent games are a promising alternative to supervised learning on "canned" conversations. This is a strong claim that requires substantial evidence. However, the paper fails to establish the merits of its approach relative to the methods it critiques. A simple and necessary baseline would be to frame the referential game in a supervised manner. For instance, a sender could be a standard image classifier that outputs a label for the target image, and a receiver could be a model that takes two images and a label and selects the image that best matches the label. Such a baseline, using established architectures, would provide a crucial performance ceiling and a point of comparison for the properties of the "emergent" language. Without this, it is impossible to judge whether the emergent protocol is more efficient, robust, or semantically richer than what could be achieved with a straightforward, well-understood method.

Secondly, the experimental setup is highly simplified, which limits the generality of the conclusions. The agents do not learn from raw pixels but from high-level feature vectors (VGG fc or sm layers) extracted from a powerful, pre-trained convolutional network. This network was itself trained via supervision on ImageNet. Therefore, the agents are not truly learning "tabula rasa"; they are learning a mapping from an already rich, semantic feature space to a set of discrete symbols. Much of the "semantic" structure identified via purity analysis likely originates from the semantic organization of the VGG feature space itself. The paper should explicitly acknowledge and analyze this significant confounding factor. The emergent "language" is also limited to a single symbol exchange, which is a far cry from the compositional and sequential nature of human language that motivates the work in the introduction. While this is a reasonable starting point, the claims should be more carefully scoped to what has actually been demonstrated: the emergence of a single-word vocabulary for a static reference task.

The analysis of the emergent protocol, while intriguing, could be more rigorous. The SVD analysis in Section 4 is used to argue that the informed sender's vocabulary is not merely redundant, but the conclusion that it "requires multiple dimensions" is qualitative. It would be more compelling to show how this dimensionality scales with task complexity or compares to the dimensionality of a supervised baseline's label space. The human evaluation in Section 5 is the strongest part of the paper, demonstrating that supervised grounding can lead to generalization. However, the 68% success rate, while significantly above chance, also indicates a substantial gap compared to human-human communication, highlighting that the emergent "metonymic" links are often not transparent.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Implement Baselines:** The most critical addition would be the implementation and thorough analysis of one or more supervised baselines for the referential game task. This would ground the paper's contributions and allow for a quantitative assessment of the proposed method's strengths and weaknesses.
*   **Ablate the Role of Pre-trained Features:** To better understand what the agents are learning, it would be valuable to see how performance and semantic purity change with different levels of feature abstraction (e.g., earlier conv layers vs. final fc layers) or with features from a randomly initialized network. This would help disentangle the learning of the communication protocol from the inherent structure of the visual features.
*   **Temper Claims:** The introduction and discussion make broad claims about developing AI that can "cooperate with others" and "thrive in a world populated by other agents." The paper should be more precise in its claims, relating them directly to the demonstrated capabilities within the single-symbol referential game.
*   **Expand the Communication Protocol:** Future work should clearly be directed towards more complex communication, involving sequences of symbols and back-and-forth dialogue, which was mentioned but not explored. This is essential for bridging the gap between the current setup and the goal of building conversational agents.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on why a direct comparison to a supervised baseline (e.g., an image classifier for the sender and a matching model for the receiver) was omitted? What challenges do you foresee in setting up such a comparison, and what results would you anticipate?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained image features? If you were to use features from a network trained on a different task (e.g., scene classification instead of object recognition), how do you believe the emergent symbol purity and human interpretability would be affected?
3.  The temperature parameter `τ=10` for the Gibbs distributions seems unusually high, which would push the policy towards uniform random sampling. Could you provide justification for this choice and comment on how sensitive the convergence and final performance are to this parameter?
