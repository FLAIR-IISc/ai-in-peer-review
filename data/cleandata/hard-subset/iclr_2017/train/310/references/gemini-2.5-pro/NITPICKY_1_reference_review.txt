### Review

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with coordinating in a referential game. The agents, starting tabula rasa, must develop a communication protocol to allow the receiver to identify a target image from a pair. The authors explore how architectural inductive biases (an "agnostic" vs. an "informed" sender) and game mechanics (e.g., forcing reference at the object-class level) influence the resulting language's semantic structure. The paper's primary contribution is a novel training paradigm that combines this interactive, game-based learning with a standard supervised image-labeling task. This hybrid approach aims to ground the emergent language in human-understandable terms. The authors demonstrate through a crowdsourced experiment that this grounding allows humans to interpret the agents' communication with significant success, even for concepts not seen during supervised training.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper is undermined by a significant number of notational inaccuracies, methodological ambiguities, and poorly justified design choices. These issues severely compromise the clarity and reproducibility of the presented framework and experiments.
2.  The manuscript exhibits a general lack of rigor and careful preparation, most notably evidenced by a completely malformed bibliography. This, combined with the other issues, suggests the paper is not yet ready for publication at a top-tier venue.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

While the research direction is promising and the human-grounding experiment is compelling, the paper is plagued by numerous flaws that, in aggregate, are too substantial to overlook.

**Methodological and Notational Flaws:**
*   **Incorrect Policy Notation:** The formalism in Section 2 is deeply flawed. The sender's input is described as `θS(iL, iR, t)`, which incorrectly treats the model parameters `θS` as an input function. The notation for the receiver's policy, `r(iL, iR, s(θS(iL, iR, t)))`, is similarly confused, as the receiver does not have access to the sender's parameters, only its output symbol. This is not a minor typographical error but a fundamental misunderstanding of standard reinforcement learning notation, which pervades the description of the core framework.
*   **Undefined Terms:** In the loss function `-IEr̃[R(r̃)]`, the term `r̃` is used without any prior definition. While one can infer its meaning from context, such imprecision is unacceptable.
*   **Confusing Architectural Descriptions:** The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" senders is not clearly motivated. The name "informed" seems arbitrary, and the claim that "the agnostic architecture nests the informed one" is non-obvious and presented without explanation. A fully-connected layer can approximate many functions, but "nesting" implies a specific structural relationship that is not apparent from the description.
*   **Lack of Hyperparameter Analysis:** The authors state that hyperparameters were set "without tuning." While this can prevent overfitting to a specific validation set, it is not a virtue. It leaves open critical questions about the robustness of the results. The Gibbs temperature `τ=10` is particularly high and could have a dramatic effect on the discreteness of the communication channel. The lack of any sensitivity analysis for crucial parameters like temperature, learning rate, or embedding dimensionality is a significant weakness.
*   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The intriguing claim that the 2-symbol agnostic sender converges to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is presented as an anecdotal finding from "qualitative evaluations." For such a specific and interesting result, quantitative evidence should be provided.

**Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
*   **Bibliography:** The reference section is in a state of complete disarray. Entries contain extraneous characters (e.g., `? \Q1998\E`), incorrect formatting, and are difficult to parse. This suggests an extreme lack of attention to detail in the manuscript's preparation. For example, the entry for Brandenburger and Dekel is listed as "Brandenburger and Dekel,? \Q1998\E, Brandenburger and Dekel," which is nonsensical. This issue is pervasive across the entire list.
*   **Misplaced Footnote:** The footnote `∗Work done while at Facebook AI Research` is attached to the end of a paragraph in the introduction, rather than being associated with the authors on the title page, which is the standard convention.
*   **Unprofessional Tone:** The paper begins with a lengthy quote from a science fiction story. While evocative, this is more suited to a blog post than a formal research publication and sets an unprofessional tone from the outset.

These issues, taken together, prevent a confident assessment of the work's validity and contribution. The core ideas are interesting, but the execution and presentation fall far short of the standards expected for ICLR.

**4. Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback for improving the manuscript.
*   The framework in Section 2 needs a complete rewrite with standard, unambiguous notation for policies, inputs, and parameters.
*   The "informed" sender architecture should be better justified. What specific prior belief about the task does a dimension-wise convolution encode, and why is this "informed"?
*   The distractor sampling strategy (always sampling from a different concept class) is a critical detail that simplifies the task. This design choice and its implications on the results should be explicitly discussed. An experiment where distractors can be from the same class would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about semantic learning.
*   The final grounding experiment is the strongest part of the paper. This should be emphasized more, perhaps by presenting it earlier or framing the paper around this contribution. The "metonymic" generalization is a fascinating result that deserves more thorough analysis.

**5. Questions for the Authors**
1.  Could you please provide the correct mathematical formulation for the sender and receiver policies, clarifying how the inputs, parameters, and outputs are defined for each agent?
2.  Could you elaborate on the claim that the agnostic architecture "nests" the informed one? Does this mean the function class of the informed sender is a strict subset of the agnostic one, and if so, how is this implemented?
3.  How robust are your findings to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the Gibbs temperature `τ`? A value of 10 seems quite high and would lead to a nearly uniform sampling policy, potentially slowing down learning. Did you experiment with lower values or an annealing schedule?
4.  How does the system perform on a more challenging version of the referential game where the target and distractor images are sampled from the same McRae category (e.g., 'cat' vs. 'dog') or even from the same base-level concept (e.g., two different instances of 'car')?
