### Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a compelling research program aimed at studying the emergence of communication between AI agents. Moving beyond standard supervised learning on static conversational datasets, the authors propose a multi-agent framework based on a simple "referential game" where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop a shared communication protocol to identify a target image from a pair. The work makes several key contributions:
1.  It demonstrates that tabula rasa agents, implemented as simple neural networks, can successfully learn to communicate and solve the referential task through reinforcement learning.
2.  It investigates how environmental and architectural choices influence the properties of the emergent language. Specifically, it shows that an "informed" sender architecture with an inductive bias for comparing inputs dimension-wise leads to a richer vocabulary.
3.  It introduces a simple but clever modification to the game (showing agents different instances of the same object class) to push the emergent language towards more abstract, conceptual representations rather than low-level visual features.
4.  Most significantly, it proposes and validates a hybrid training scheme that combines the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. This method successfully grounds the agents' emergent language in human-understandable terms, leading to a protocol that humans can interpret with significant success, even for objects not seen during the supervised phase.

### Decision
**Accept**

This is an excellent and thought-provoking paper that I am excited to see published. It tackles a fundamental problem in AI—the grounding of language in shared experience—with a bold and original research program. While the methods employed are straightforward, the conceptual framework is novel and the results, particularly the final human evaluation, are highly promising. The paper prioritizes a foundational question over incremental performance gains on an established benchmark, which is exactly the kind of ambitious research that ICLR should champion.

### Supporting Arguments
My recommendation for acceptance is based on two primary strengths of this work:

1.  **A Shift in Paradigm from Learning Language Structure to Learning Language Function:** The central thesis of the paper—that true language understanding must arise from interactive, goal-oriented use—is a powerful one. While prior work has shown that agents can learn to communicate, this paper goes a crucial step further by asking *how we can shape the emergent language to be more like our own*: conceptual, abstract, and interpretable. The experiments are not merely engineering feats; they are carefully designed probes into this question. The "object-level reference" experiment, for instance, is a simple yet brilliant way to force abstraction by removing low-level visual features from the agents' common knowledge. This focus on the *semantics* and *pragmatics* of emergent communication, rather than just its existence, represents a significant conceptual advance for the field.

2.  **The Hybrid Grounding Approach and its Compelling Validation:** The idea of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning, inspired by AlphaGo, is a fantastic contribution. It provides a clear and scalable path to aligning an agent-developed communication protocol with human language, sidestepping the brittleness of pure supervision and the inscrutability of pure emergence. The final experiment is the paper's crowning achievement. Showing that humans can interpret the agent's "words" for novel objects—and observing emergent metonymy like using "dolphin" to refer to the sea—is a striking and powerful result. It suggests that this approach doesn't just map symbols to labels, but instills a flexible, semantic common ground that can generalize. This is a bold claim, and while the validation is preliminary, the evidence provided is more than sufficient to demonstrate the immense potential of this direction.

I am willing to overlook the relative simplicity of the agent architectures and the limited hyperparameter search because the strength of the paper lies in its ideas, not its implementation details. In fact, the simplicity of the models reinforces the main point: the properties of the language emerge from the environment and the learning objectives, not from complex, pre-programmed architectural priors.

### Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement
These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   **Exploring More Complex Protocols:** The current communication channel is a single discrete symbol. A natural and exciting next step would be to allow for sequences of symbols. This would open the door to studying the emergence of more complex linguistic phenomena, such as syntax and compositionality. How would agents combine known "words" to describe novel combinations of objects or attributes?
*   **Richer Agent Architectures:** While the simplicity of the current agents is a virtue, it would be interesting to see if more sophisticated architectures (e.g., using attention mechanisms) would allow agents to ground their symbols in specific parts of the images, potentially leading to a more nuanced and descriptive language.
*   **Analysis of "Synonymy":** The SVD analysis to argue against high synonymy is a good start. This could be strengthened by a more direct analysis. For instance, one could analyze the conditional probability distributions p(symbol | target) to identify symbols that are used interchangeably for the same set of target images.
*   **The Nature of the Emergent Concepts:** The paper intriguingly mentions that the 2-symbol agnostic sender converges on a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. This is a fascinating result that deserves more prominent discussion and analysis, as it suggests the agents may be discovering fundamental semantic axes present in the data.

### Questions for the Authors
*   In the final human evaluation experiment, what did the failure cases look like? Was there a pattern to the "words" that humans systematically misinterpreted? An analysis of these errors could be very insightful for understanding the remaining gap between the agent's and humans' conceptual spaces.
*   The temperature parameter for the Gibbs distributions was set to 10, which seems quite high and would encourage a more uniform, exploratory policy. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter? Does a lower temperature lead to faster convergence but a less rich vocabulary (i.e., "language collapse")?
*   Could you provide more detail or speculation on why the "informed" sender architecture, with its dimension-wise convolutions, fosters a larger vocabulary? It's an interesting architectural bias, and a deeper intuition for why it works would be valuable.
