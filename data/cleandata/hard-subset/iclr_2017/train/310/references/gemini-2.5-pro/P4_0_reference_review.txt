### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with coordinating in a referential game. The agents must develop a communication protocol from scratch (*tabula rasa*) to identify a target image from a pair of images. The paper makes several key claims. First, it demonstrates that simple feed-forward agents trained with reinforcement learning can successfully converge to a solution, developing an effective communication protocol. Second, it provides evidence that this emergent protocol is not arbitrary but develops a semantic structure that aligns with high-level conceptual categories of the objects depicted in the images, rather than just low-level visual features. Third, the paper shows that the properties of the emergent language can be shaped by modifying the game's setup, for example, by removing instance-level details from the agents' common knowledge to encourage more abstract, class-level representations. Finally, and most significantly, the paper proposes and validates a method for grounding the emergent language in human language. By combining the interactive game-playing with a supervised image-labeling task, the agents learn a protocol that is not only successful for coordination but also partially interpretable by humans, even when communicating about objects not seen during the supervised phase.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Effective Grounding Method:** The paper introduces a compelling method for making an emergent communication protocol interpretable by humans. The hybrid approach of combining interactive reinforcement learning with a supervised classification objective is a novel and effective strategy for grounding the agents' language, moving beyond prior work that focused primarily on the emergence of ungrounded protocols.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support for Generalization:** The primary claim—that this grounding generalizes to new concepts and is understandable by humans—is supported by rigorous empirical evidence, including a well-designed crowdsourcing experiment. The finding that agents can use learned words "metonymically" to refer to novel objects, and that humans can successfully interpret these messages, is a significant and encouraging result for the field of human-AI interaction.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-situated within the relevant literature. The experimental design is a model of clarity, progressing logically from demonstrating the basic emergence of communication to exploring how its semantic properties can be controlled and, ultimately, grounded in human language.

The core strength of the paper lies in Section 5. While previous work has shown that agents can develop communication protocols, a major challenge has been the lack of interpretability. The proposed solution, inspired by the training of AlphaGo, is both simple and effective. By having the sender agent alternate between playing the referential game and performing a standard supervised image classification task with shared weights, the system is encouraged to align its emergent symbols with conventional human labels.

The results of this hybrid training are impressive. The dramatic increase in semantic purity (from ~30% to 70%) is a strong quantitative indicator of success. However, the most convincing evidence comes from the human study. The authors test the protocol's interpretability on a completely different dataset (ReferItGame), where the supervised labels are often not directly applicable. The fact that human participants could identify the correct referent 68% of the time demonstrates that the learned grounding is not brittle but generalizes in a flexible, human-like manner. This finding provides a credible pathway for developing AI agents that can bootstrap a functional and understandable language without requiring constant human intervention.

The earlier experiments, while less novel in isolation, build a solid foundation for this main contribution. The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures provides useful insight into the role of inductive biases, and the "object-level reference" experiment is a clever manipulation that cleanly demonstrates how changing the agents' common knowledge can force the emergent language to become more abstract. These experiments strengthen the paper's overall narrative and scientific rigor.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper and are not central to the decision.

*   **Analysis of Architectures:** The difference in performance and vocabulary usage between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender is interesting. A more detailed analysis or ablation study could clarify why the dimension-wise comparison mechanism of the "informed" sender is so effective. Is this specific to comparing two image vectors, or does it suggest a more general principle for communication about multiple entities?
*   **Training Stability:** The REINFORCE algorithm is known for its high variance. Reporting the variance across multiple training runs with different random seeds for the main results (e.g., success rate, purity) would add confidence in the stability and reproducibility of the findings.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The temperature parameter for the Gibbs distributions was fixed at a relatively high value (τ=10). This encourages exploration but might slow convergence or affect the final protocol. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter would be valuable. For instance, would a lower temperature lead to a more compact but less expressive language?
*   **Failure Case Analysis:** The human evaluation is a highlight of the paper. An analysis of the cases where humans failed to interpret the agent's message could be very insightful. Were these failures due to the agent making an apparently random choice, or was the intended "metonymic" link simply too abstract for a human to grasp?

### 5. Questions for the Authors

To clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  In the human evaluation experiment, you show examples of successful "metonymic" communication (e.g., 'dolphin' for sea). Could you provide examples of failure cases? What characterizes the instances where the 10 human raters consistently failed to identify the target image intended by the sender agent?
2.  The current grounding method relies on a sender-side supervised signal (image classification). Have you considered or do you have intuitions about alternative grounding mechanisms? For instance, could the receiver be provided with a supervised signal, or could grounding be achieved by occasionally substituting one of the agents with a human player who uses natural language?
3.  The vocabulary size was fixed at 10 or 100. How does the vocabulary size interact with the emergent semantics? For example, with a very large vocabulary and the hybrid training, would the sender still develop metonymic usage, or would it simply fail to use symbols for objects outside its supervised label set?
