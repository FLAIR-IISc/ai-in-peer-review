### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper addresses the fundamental problem of how to train AI agents to develop functional, cooperative communication skills. The authors argue that standard supervised learning on static conversational datasets is insufficient as it misses the interactive nature of language use. They propose a research program based on multi-agent referential games where agents, starting as blank slates, must develop a communication protocol to succeed at a cooperative task.

The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It demonstrates that simple neural network agents can successfully learn to communicate and coordinate in a referential game involving real-world images, developing a communication protocol from scratch.
2.  It investigates how environmental and architectural factors shape the emergent language. It shows that an "informed" sender architecture with an inductive bias for comparison develops a richer protocol than a generic "agnostic" one. It also shows that modifying the game to remove low-level visual features from common knowledge pushes the agents toward a more abstract, conceptual communication system.
3.  Most importantly, it introduces a novel method to ground the emergent language in human concepts. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task (inspired by AlphaGo's training), the agents develop a protocol that is not only effective for coordination but also significantly more interpretable by humans. A crowdsourcing experiment confirms that humans can understand the agent's messages, even for objects not seen during supervised training, with significantly above-chance accuracy.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and thoughtfully executed study on an important and challenging problem. The work is a valuable step towards building AI agents that can learn to communicate in a grounded and functional way. The primary reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Important Problem and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper effectively argues for moving beyond static supervised learning towards interactive, goal-driven language learning. The multi-agent game paradigm is a principled and promising direction for studying language emergence, and the paper situates itself well within related literature from AI, game theory, and cognitive science.
2.  **Compelling and Rigorous Experimental Design:** The paper presents a logical and insightful series of experiments. The progression from demonstrating basic emergence, to carefully manipulating the environment to encourage more abstract semantics (the "object-level reference" experiment), to finally grounding the language via mixed-task training is elegant. The final human evaluation provides strong, direct evidence for the central claim that this method can produce human-interpretable communication.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's claims are well-supported by its empirical results. The initial experiments clearly show that agents converge to near-perfect coordination, answering the first research question. The analysis of the emergent protocol using metrics like vocabulary usage, SVD, and purity against human-defined semantic categories provides convincing evidence that the emergent language has structure and that this structure can be influenced by architectural choices.

The experiment in Section 4.1, which modifies the game by showing agents different instances of the same object class, is particularly clever. It is a clean operationalization of the game-theoretic concept of "common knowledge" and successfully demonstrates that forcing agents to coordinate on more abstract properties leads to a more semantically coherent language, as measured by the purity metric.

The main strength of the paper lies in Section 5. The idea of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning on human labels is both novel for this domain and highly effective. The results are impressive: not only does coordination performance remain high, but the symbol purity dramatically increases, and the agent begins to use symbols corresponding to their conventional human meaning. The final human study is the capstone of the paper, directly testing the ultimate goal of human-AI communication. The finding that humans can interpret the agent's novel, "metonymic" use of words (e.g., using 'dolphin' to refer to the sea) is a fascinating and powerful result, suggesting that this training paradigm fosters a type of semantic flexibility akin to that found in human language.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.

*   **Clarity on "Informed" Sender:** The description of the "informed" sender architecture could be clearer. While the diagram helps, a more detailed explanation of how the 1D convolutions operate on the image embeddings and why this provides a useful inductive bias for comparison would strengthen the paper. The name "informed" is also a bit ambiguous; perhaps a more descriptive name like "comparative" sender could be considered.
*   **Training Details:** The paper mentions using REINFORCE. Given that this algorithm can suffer from high variance, it would be useful to know if any training instability was observed and if any variance reduction techniques (e.g., using a baseline) were necessary. Additionally, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to hyperparameters, particularly the Gibbs temperature `τ`, would be welcome.
*   **Analysis of Failures:** The human evaluation achieved 68% accuracy, which is significantly above the 50% chance baseline but still leaves room for misunderstanding. An analysis of the failure cases could be very insightful. Are there systematic patterns in the kinds of messages humans misinterpret? This could reveal limitations of the current approach and point toward important avenues for future work.
*   **Ablation on Shared Layers:** In the mixed supervision experiment, the game-embedding layer is shared between the game and the classification task. An ablation study where this layer is *not* shared would be interesting to quantify precisely how much of the grounding effect is due to this weight sharing.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   The receiver agent is never directly exposed to the supervised labels. It seems to learn to interpret the grounded symbols purely from the sender's structured behavior during the game. Could you comment on the dynamics of this process? Does the receiver's representation space also become more semantically structured as a result of interacting with the grounded sender?
*   The emergence of a "living-vs-non-living" distinction by the agnostic sender is a fascinating result that mirrors fundamental distinctions in human cognition. Was this finding robust across multiple training runs with different random seeds?
*   Have you considered extending the communication protocol beyond a single symbol? For example, allowing a sequence of symbols could enable compositional language. Do you have intuitions on how the current framework and findings might extend to a more complex, multi-symbol setting?
