### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," tasked with coordinating in a referential game. The agents must develop a communication protocol from scratch to identify a target image from a pair. The central contribution is not merely demonstrating that communication can emerge, but exploring how to shape the learning environment to foster a communication protocol that is both semantically structured and interpretable by humans.

The authors systematically build their case through a series of experiments. They first establish that simple feed-forward agents can successfully learn to communicate using a discrete vocabulary. They analyze the emergent protocol, showing that the learned symbols correspond to high-level semantic categories (measured by "purity") rather than just low-level visual features. They then demonstrate that by altering the game's "common knowledge"—forcing agents to coordinate on object-class properties rather than specific image instances—they can encourage the development of more abstract symbols.

The paper's capstone contribution is a novel training paradigm that combines the interactive, game-based reinforcement learning with a supervised image-labeling task. This hybrid approach, inspired by AlphaGo, grounds the agents' emergent language in human-provided labels. The authors show this not only dramatically improves the semantic structure of the language but, crucially, makes it interpretable to humans even when applied to novel objects not seen during supervised training. This generalization is demonstrated through a compelling crowdsourcing experiment where humans successfully interpret the agent's messages, which often rely on creative, "metonymic" associations (e.g., using the word "dolphin" to refer to an image of the sea).

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a significant contribution to the study of emergent communication and grounded language learning. The reasons for acceptance are twofold:

1.  **Strong Methodological Contribution and Execution:** The paper presents a clean, simple, and effective method for grounding emergent communication protocols in human language. The hybrid approach of mixing unsupervised multi-agent RL with a standard supervised task is both novel and demonstrably powerful. The progression of experiments is logical and rigorous, building a convincing narrative from basic communication emergence to human-interpretable generalization.

2.  **Compelling and Important Results:** The key finding—that supervised grounding enables generalization to novel concepts in a way that is understandable to humans—is both surprising and significant. The analysis of "metonymic" communication provides a fascinating glimpse into how AI agents might develop flexible, human-like semantic reasoning. This work provides a promising path forward for creating AI agents that can learn to communicate and collaborate with humans in a scalable manner.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its clear focus on a fundamental question: how do we move beyond agents that can merely *succeed* at a task to agents that can *explain* or *communicate* their reasoning in a way that aligns with human concepts?

The initial experiments (Sections 3 and 4) effectively replicate and build upon prior work (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016), but with a stronger focus on analyzing the *semantics* of the emergent language. The use of the McRae categories and the purity metric provides a quantitative handle on the abstractness of the learned symbols, and the t-SNE visualizations offer a helpful qualitative view. The experiment in Section 4.1, which manipulates common knowledge to force object-level reference, is a clever and well-motivated design choice that strengthens the claim that the environment shapes the nature of the emergent language.

The core novelty in Section 5 is expertly handled. By integrating supervised learning, the authors provide the agents with an invaluable "nudge" towards human-aligned concepts without sacrificing the benefits of interactive learning. The evaluation is particularly strong. Rather than relying solely on proxy metrics like purity, the authors conduct a direct test of human interpretability. The result that humans can guess the correct referent 68% of the time (significantly above the 50% chance baseline) on out-of-domain images is a powerful piece of evidence. The qualitative examples in Figure 4 (dolphin -> sea, fence -> land) are illuminative and support the claim that the agents are not simply memorizing labels but learning a flexible mapping within a shared semantic space. This suggests a pathway for bootstrapping communication that is far more scalable than having humans in the loop for all interactions.

The paper is also well-situated in the literature, drawing connections to linguistics, game theory, and cognitive science, which enriches the context and impact of the work.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Training Stability and Reproducibility:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for its high variance. The claims would be stronger if the authors reported results averaged over multiple random seeds, including standard deviations or error bars on the learning curves (e.g., in Figure 2). This would provide confidence that the reported results are robust and not artifacts of a fortunate initialization.
*   **"Informed" vs. "Agnostic" Sender:** The architectural difference between the "informed" and "agnostic" sender is presented as a key comparison. However, the "informed" architecture simply introduces a strong inductive bias for comparing the two image vectors element-wise. While showing this bias is helpful is a useful ablation, the finding itself is not particularly surprising. The paper could frame this more as a demonstration of the utility of architectural priors rather than a central discovery.
*   **Impact of Vocabulary Size:** The experiments use vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100. It would be interesting to see a more systematic study of how vocabulary size impacts communication success, purity, and the number of unique symbols used. Is there a "sweet spot"? Do agents with a very large vocabulary still converge to a compact, structured language?
*   **Human Evaluation Details:** The crowdsourcing experiment is excellent. To further strengthen it, it would be useful to include more details about the instructions given to the human participants. Were they told the word was generated by an AI? Was the task framed as a puzzle? This context can influence how participants approach the task.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  Could you elaborate on the training dynamics? Specifically, how stable were the results across different runs? Does the hybrid supervised/RL training in Section 5 exhibit lower variance than the pure RL setup?
2.  The emergent metonymy is one of the most exciting results. Do you have a hypothesis about the underlying mechanism? For instance, in the shared embedding space, are the representations for concepts like "dolphin" and "sea" learned to be close due to co-occurrence statistics in the pre-trained VGG model, which is then leveraged by the sender?
3.  In the human evaluation, you report 68% accuracy. Was a baseline collected where humans were given a random word (from the supervised vocabulary) instead of the one emitted by the sender? This would help calibrate the significance of the 68% figure against a non-50% baseline if any spurious correlations exist.
