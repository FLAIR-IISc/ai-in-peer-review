### Review of "Deal or No Deal? End-to-End Learning for Negotiation Dialogues"

**(Note: The provided paper is a well-known 2017 EMNLP paper, but for this exercise, I will review it as if it were a new submission to ICLR, based solely on the text provided, which seems to be an earlier draft or version of what was later published as "Learning to Communicate with Deep Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning" by Foerster et al. or a related work from that period. My review will treat the provided text as the complete submission.)**

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols between two neural network agents—“tabula rasa” learners—tasked with coordinating in a simple referential game. The agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop a shared symbolic language to identify a target image from a pair. The paper makes several key contributions: 1) It demonstrates that simple feed-forward agents trained with reinforcement learning can successfully converge to a near-perfect communication strategy. 2) It explores how architectural inductive biases (an "informed" vs. "agnostic" sender) affect the speed of convergence and the complexity of the emergent language. 3) It introduces a clever environmental modification—showing agents different instances of the same object class—to force communication to be based on more abstract, semantic properties rather than low-level visual features. 4) Most significantly, it proposes and validates a novel method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, showing that the resulting protocol is not only more semantically structured but also partially interpretable by humans in a zero-shot setting.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. Its primary strengths are:
1.  **Novel Grounding Method:** The combination of multi-agent RL for interactive communication with a standard supervised objective to ground the emergent language is a compelling and effective idea. The demonstration that this grounding generalizes to new domains (ReferItGame) and is interpretable by humans is the standout result.
2.  **Rigorous Experimental Design:** The paper systematically builds its case through a series of well-designed experiments. It starts with a foundational question (can agents learn to communicate at all?), investigates the nature of that communication, and then introduces targeted interventions to shape the emergent language toward human-like semantics. This structured approach makes the claims easy to follow and well-supported.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper tackles a fundamental problem at the intersection of multi-agent systems, language, and representation learning: how to develop agents that can learn to communicate in a functional, grounded, and ultimately human-compatible way. It correctly positions itself against purely supervised "canned dialogue" approaches, arguing for the necessity of interactive learning to capture the functional aspects of language.

The core experimental setup, a variant of the Lewis signaling game, is simple yet powerful. The initial result showing that agents can achieve high coordination success is a necessary baseline, but the paper rightly pushes further. The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures provides a nice insight into the role of inductive bias. The "informed" sender's convolutional structure, which encourages dimensional comparison between image representations, leads to faster learning and a richer vocabulary, supporting the idea that architectural choices can guide the emergence of more complex communication.

The "object-level reference" experiment (Section 4.1) is a particularly clever manipulation. By removing low-level visual features from the agents' common knowledge, the authors force coordination on more abstract class-level properties. The resulting increase in semantic purity, while modest, provides strong evidence for their hypothesis that the environment's structure dictates the nature of the emergent language.

The paper's most significant contribution is the hybrid supervised/RL training paradigm in Section 5. This approach elegantly bridges the gap between a completely arbitrary emergent protocol and human language. The results are impressive: a dramatic increase in semantic purity (70%) and, more importantly, a protocol that humans can partially understand. The finding that the agent learns to use words "metonymically" (e.g., "dolphin" for a sea picture) is fascinating. It suggests that the agent has learned a flexible mapping between its grounded vocabulary and visual concepts, a hallmark of human linguistic creativity. The human evaluation, showing 68% success, provides strong quantitative validation for this claim of interpretability and is a crucial part of the paper's success.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve it.
*   **Architectural Motivation:** The "informed" sender's architecture, using 1D convolutions for dimension-wise comparison, is presented as intuitively better. A deeper justification for why this specific bias is well-suited for a referential task would be beneficial. For instance, it could be framed as learning a "difference operator" over semantic feature dimensions.
*   **Analysis of Communication Failures:** The human evaluation achieved 68% accuracy. An analysis of the 32% failure cases would be highly informative. Are there specific types of concepts or visual relationships where the agent's "metonymic" links fail to align with human intuition? This would provide valuable insight into the limits of the current grounding method.
*   **REINFORCE and Training Stability:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for its high variance. While it clearly works here, it would be useful to briefly comment on training stability. Did the authors experience issues with convergence? Would more modern policy gradient methods like A2C or PPO be expected to perform better or more reliably?
*   **Scalability:** The current work is a proof of concept with a single-symbol message space. The paper should briefly discuss the challenges and potential paths toward scaling this framework to multi-symbol messages (i.e., sequences), which would be necessary for more complex communication.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Regarding the "informed" vs. "agnostic" senders: Is it possible that the agnostic sender, with sufficient capacity and training time, could eventually learn a strategy functionally equivalent to the dimensional comparison hard-coded into the informed sender? In other words, is the "informed" architecture merely a learning accelerant, or does it enable a qualitatively different type of solution that the agnostic one cannot find?
2.  In the hybrid training experiment (Section 5), the "game-embedding layers" are shared between the supervised and RL tasks. Could you clarify precisely which parameters are shared? How sensitive are the results to this choice? For example, what would happen if only the initial image embedding layers were shared, but the final vocabulary projection layers were separate?
3.  For the human evaluation, what were the exact instructions given to the CrowdFlower participants? Were they aware they were interpreting the output of an AI agent? This context could influence their strategy (e.g., being more open to non-literal interpretations), and clarifying it is important for interpreting the 68% success rate.
