### Paper Summary

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between *tabula rasa* neural agents within the framework of a multi-agent referential game. The primary goal is to explore how to design environments that foster the development of a communication protocol that is not only effective for coordination but also structured and interpretable by humans. The authors set up a game where a "sender" agent must transmit a symbol from a fixed vocabulary to a "receiver" agent to help it identify a target image from a pair of images.

The paper makes several key contributions through a series of well-designed experiments:
1.  It demonstrates that simple feed-forward agents trained with reinforcement learning can successfully learn to communicate and solve the referential game.
2.  It shows that architectural inductive biases (an "informed" sender that compares image features dimension-wise) can lead to faster learning and the use of a richer vocabulary compared to a generic ("agnostic") architecture.
3.  It provides evidence that the emergent communication protocol captures high-level semantic properties of the images, as measured by clustering purity against human-defined categories, and that this can be encouraged by modifying the game to force abstraction (the "object-level reference" game).
4.  Most significantly, it proposes and validates a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language by combining the interactive game with a standard supervised image classification task. A human study confirms that this hybrid training produces a code that is partially interpretable by humans, even when referring to objects not seen during the supervised training phase, through mechanisms akin to metonymy.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision:** Accept

This is a well-written and well-executed paper that presents a clear and compelling research program. The work is strongly motivated, the experiments are logically structured and build upon one another to answer the core research questions, and the results are significant. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novel and Effective Grounding Method:** The core contribution—combining interactive self-play with supervised learning to ground an emergent communication protocol—is a novel and important idea. The paper provides strong evidence, including a human-subject experiment, that this approach not only works but leads to interesting and non-trivial generalizations (e.g., metonymy). This moves the research beyond a purely theoretical simulation of language emergence and provides a concrete path toward building agents that can communicate with humans.
2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experimental Design:** The paper does an excellent job of dissecting the problem. Instead of presenting a single, monolithic result, the authors carefully construct a series of experiments to test specific hypotheses about architectural biases, game rules, and training objectives. This methodical approach adds significant weight to the conclusions and provides valuable insights for future work in this domain.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper excels across the key reviewing criteria.

**1. Well-Motivated and Positioned:** The introduction clearly frames the problem, contrasting the work with purely supervised approaches to dialogue, pre-programmed multi-agent systems, and human-in-the-loop methods. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing paradigms (lack of interactivity, scalability issues) and positions the proposed game-based framework as a promising alternative. The connections to linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory (Lewis signaling games) are well-articulated and provide a solid theoretical foundation for the work.

**2. Sound Methodology and Claims Supported by Evidence:** The experimental setup is clearly described. The use of REINFORCE is appropriate for the non-differentiable sampling of discrete symbols. The chosen metrics—communication success, vocabulary usage, SVD for dimensionality, and purity for semantic clustering—are sensible for evaluating the different facets of the emergent language.

The claims are well-supported by the results presented:
*   **Claim:** Agents can learn to communicate. **Evidence:** Table 1 and Figure 2 (left) show near-perfect communication success after a short training period.
*   **Claim:** Inductive biases matter. **Evidence:** Figure 2 shows the "informed" sender learns faster and uses more symbols, and Table 1 shows it achieves higher semantic purity.
*   **Claim:** Game design influences abstraction. **Evidence:** Table 2 and the t-SNE plot in Figure 3 (right) show that the object-level reference game increases purity, suggesting coordination on more general class properties.
*   **Claim:** Hybrid training grounds the language for human interpretation. **Evidence:** The final experiment shows a dramatic increase in purity (70%). More importantly, the human evaluation study demonstrates a 68% success rate in interpreting the agent's symbols for novel referents, which is highly significant and compelling evidence. The qualitative examples in Figure 4 are particularly insightful.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Analysis of Failures in Human Study:** The 68% success rate in the human study is impressive, but an analysis of the 32% failure cases could be very illuminating. Are there systematic patterns to the misunderstandings? For example, do humans fail when the "metonymic" link is too abstract? This could provide deeper insights into the gap between the agent's learned representations and human semantics.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The paper states that hyperparameters like the Gibbs temperature (τ=10) were set without tuning. A temperature of 10 is quite high, encouraging significant exploration. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion or ablation study on the effect of temperature. Does a lower temperature lead to faster convergence but a less diverse, "ossified" language? Understanding this trade-off would be valuable.
*   **Clarification of "Nesting" Architectures:** The paper notes that "the agnostic architecture nests the informed one." This is not immediately obvious from the descriptions and Figure 1. A clearer explanation, perhaps in an appendix, of how a generic fully-connected layer can replicate the dimension-wise convolutions of the informed sender would strengthen this point.
*   **References Formatting:** The reference list contains formatting errors (e.g., "? \Q1998\E"). This should be cleaned up before publication.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Regarding the hybrid training schedule, the sender switches "equiprobably" between the game and the supervised task. Have you experimented with different mixing ratios? Is there a sweet spot that balances grounding with the flexibility needed for the interactive game? For example, could too much supervision hinder the development of creative, emergent communication strategies?
2.  In the final human evaluation, the sender was trained on ImageNet object labels but tested on referring expressions from the ReferItGame dataset. This is a clever design. Could you elaborate on why this specific combination was chosen and whether you anticipate different results if the supervised task was, for instance, multi-label classification on datasets like COCO, which might be closer to the scene-based nature of ReferItGame?
