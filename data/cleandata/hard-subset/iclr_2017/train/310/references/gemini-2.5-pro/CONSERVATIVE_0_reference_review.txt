### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," tasked with cooperating in a referential game. The agents, starting as blank slates, must develop a communication protocol to allow the receiver to identify a target image from a pair of images. The authors explore how architectural choices (an "agnostic" vs. an "informed" sender) and game mechanics (e.g., showing agents different exemplars of the same object class) influence the properties of the emergent language. The paper evaluates the emergent protocol by measuring its semantic structure, specifically its alignment with human-defined object categories ("purity"). Finally, it proposes a method to ground the agents' language in human language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, and tests the human-interpretability of the resulting protocol via a crowdsourcing experiment.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** The paper introduces a novel learning paradigm but fails to benchmark it against established, relevant methods. The primary comparison is between two architectural variants of the proposed model, which does not adequately justify the necessity of the interactive, emergent communication framework over simpler, supervised alternatives.
2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments and Analysis:** The claims regarding the development of a "natural" and "portable" language are based on a very simple, one-shot referential game. The analysis relies heavily on interpretive metrics like cluster purity and t-SNE visualizations, which provide only indirect and qualitative evidence for the claims. The human evaluation, while a positive step, is limited in scale and lacks a proper performance ceiling for comparison.

### Supporting Arguments

**1. Absence of Strong Baselines:**

The central thesis of the paper is that interactive, multi-agent games are a more suitable paradigm for learning the functional aspects of communication than standard supervised learning on "canned" conversations. This is a strong claim that requires direct empirical support, which is currently absent. The paper only compares an "agnostic" and an "informed" sender architecture, both of which are part of the same proposed framework.

A crucial missing baseline would be a non-interactive, supervised approach to the same referential game. For example:
*   A sender could be trained as a classifier to output the ground-truth McRae category of the target image. The receiver could then be trained to use this category information to select the target. The performance of such a system would provide a floor for evaluating the benefit of an emergent protocol.
*   Alternatively, one could use a pre-trained image captioning model to generate a description of the target, and train the receiver to use this text to identify the correct image.

Without these or similar baselines, it is impossible to assess whether the complexity of the multi-agent reinforcement learning setup is justified. The paper dismisses supervised approaches in the introduction but does not provide evidence to support this dismissal. The experiment in Section 5, which incorporates supervised learning, does so as an augmentation to the proposed method rather than as a baseline, confounding the evaluation.

**2. Methodological and Empirical Weakness:**

The experimental setup is a simplified instance of communication. A two-agent, single-turn game with a discrete vocabulary and a binary success signal is a reasonable starting point, but it is a significant leap to draw broad conclusions about language emergence from this environment.

The analysis of the emergent language properties is not sufficiently rigorous.
*   **Purity Metric:** Cluster purity relative to the McRae categories is presented as a primary indicator of semantic grounding. While significantly above chance, the absolute purity scores are not exceptionally high (e.g., 29% for the informed sender in Table 1). More importantly, this metric presupposes that aligning with these specific high-level categories is the sole definition of a "natural" language, which may not be the case. The agents might be discovering other valid, but different, semantic axes.
*   **Qualitative Evidence:** The interpretation of the agnostic sender's two-symbol protocol as a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is an interesting anecdote but is presented without systematic evidence. Likewise, t-SNE plots are useful for visualization but are not a substitute for quantitative analysis and can be sensitive to hyperparameter choices.
*   **Human Evaluation:** The experiment in Section 5 is the most compelling part of the paper. However, the result that humans can guess the target image with 68% accuracy is difficult to interpret in a vacuum. The chance baseline for a binary choice is 50%. While statistically significant, this performance level is far from demonstrating a "fully understandable" language. A critical point of comparison is missing: human-human performance. What is the success rate if a human, given the same constraints (a single word from the vocabulary), tries to communicate the target to another human? This upper bound is needed to contextualize the agents' performance.

The claim that the results are "of interest to game theorists" seems overstated. The paper uses a learning algorithm (REINFORCE) to find a solution to a signaling game, but it does not engage deeply with the game-theoretic literature on equilibrium selection or convergence properties in a formal manner.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender is interesting. The "informed" architecture's inductive bias for dimensional comparison seems effective. A more in-depth analysis or ablation study to precisely isolate why this architecture performs better and encourages wider vocabulary use would strengthen the paper.
*   In Section 4.1, the experiment to enforce abstraction by showing different image instances to the sender and receiver is a clever design. However, the reported improvement in purity is marginal ("a small increase"). This finding could be explored further, as it currently weakens the claim about the effectiveness of this environmental tweak.
*   The connection to generative adversarial networks and cooperative learning is mentioned in the introduction. The current dialogue in the community has moved significantly towards large language models (LLMs). Situating this work in contrast to the capabilities of modern LLMs would be valuable. Could a small, frozen LLM act as a sender or receiver? How does this emergent protocol compare to the zero-shot communicative abilities of such models?

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on how the "living-vs-non-living" characteristic of the two-symbol language was determined? Was this based on a qualitative inspection of a few examples, or was a more systematic analysis performed across the test set?
2.  For the human evaluation in Section 5, can you clarify how the 298 image pairs were selected? You state that "for each symbol used... we randomly extracted 3 image pairs," but what was the total number of symbols used by the sender in that experiment? Was there any filtering that might have biased the sample towards more easily interpretable pairs?
3.  Given that your ultimate goal is AI that can cooperate with humans, why do you believe that bootstrapping communication from a blank slate is a more promising direction than fine-tuning large, pre-trained models that already have a robust grasp of human language? A direct comparison to a fine-tuning approach on your referential game would be a highly informative baseline.
