### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a compelling research program into the emergence of communication between artificial agents. The authors tackle the question of how to design environments that foster the development of a language that is both effective for coordination and, crucially, interpretable by humans. The core methodology involves a series of referential games, inspired by Lewis' signaling games, where a "sender" agent must communicate a target image to a "receiver" agent from a pair of images.

The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It demonstrates that simple, tabula-rasa neural agents trained with reinforcement learning can successfully develop a communication protocol to solve this referential task with high accuracy.
2.  It investigates how environmental and architectural factors influence the nature of the emergent language. Specifically, it shows that an "informed" sender architecture with an inductive bias for comparing inputs leads to a richer, more structured vocabulary than a generic "agnostic" one. It also shows that modifying the game to remove low-level image features as common knowledge nudges the agents towards a more abstract, category-level communication strategy.
3.  Most significantly, it proposes and validates a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language. By mixing the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task, the agents learn a protocol that is not only effective but also partially understandable by humans, even when communicating about objects for which they have no direct labels. This is convincingly demonstrated through a crowdsourced experiment where humans successfully interpret the agents' messages.

### **Recommendation: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Important Problem and Strong Motivation:** The paper addresses a fundamental and challenging problem in AI: moving beyond supervised learning on static datasets to understand how functional, grounded language can emerge through interaction. The introduction does an excellent job of situating this work, contrasting it clearly with existing paradigms and motivating why interactive, multi-agent scenarios are essential for developing truly communicative AI.
2.  **Elegant and Rigorous Experimental Design:** The authors present a series of well-designed, incremental experiments that build a clear and persuasive narrative. The progression from demonstrating basic communication emergence, to shaping its properties, to finally grounding it in human language is logical and insightful. The final human evaluation is a particularly strong piece of evidence that validates the paper's central claims about interpretability.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its thoughtful methodology. The initial experiments successfully establish that communication can emerge in this setup. The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures is a clever piece of experimental design, demonstrating how simple inductive biases can have a significant impact on the complexity and structure of the resulting communication protocol. The use of SVD analysis to show that the "informed" sender's vocabulary is not merely redundant is a nice touch that adds rigor to this claim.

The "object-level reference" experiment (Section 4.1) is another simple but powerful modification. By showing the sender and receiver different exemplars of the same object class, the authors create a scenario where coordination *must* happen at a more abstract level. This directly tests the hypothesis that removing low-level features from common knowledge will force the emergence of more conceptual language, which is supported by the improved purity scores.

The final set of experiments on grounding (Section 5) is the most impactful contribution. The idea of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning, inspired by AlphaGo, is cleverly adapted to the problem of language grounding. The quantitative results are impressive: a dramatic increase in semantic purity and a 47% success rate in using the correct "human" label when available. However, the most compelling result comes from the human study. By testing the protocol on a new dataset (ReferItGame) and asking humans to act as the receiver, the authors show that the grounding generalizes. The finding that humans can interpret the agents' "metonymic" use of words (e.g., "dolphin" for a picture of the sea) is fascinating and suggests that this method can create a flexible, semantically-rich communication system that shares common ground with human intuition.

The paper is well-written, the experiments are clearly explained, and the claims are well-supported by the results. It provides both a proof of concept and a clear path forward for research on emergent communication.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Analysis of the "Informed" Sender:** The paper intuits that the informed sender has a bias for "dimension-by-dimension" comparison. It would be more convincing to include a brief analysis of the learned filters. What kinds of comparative operations are actually being learned? Is it effectively learning a difference vector, or something more complex?
*   **Agent Architecture:** The agents are simple feed-forward networks. It would be useful to discuss the potential implications of using more complex, stateful architectures (e.g., LSTMs). Would memory and recurrence enable more complex communication strategies or even the emergence of sequential, multi-symbol messages?
*   **Details of Human Evaluation:** The human evaluation is a key strength, but its description is brief. Adding a few more details in an appendix (e.g., number of unique crowd-workers, exact instructions given, platform used for the interface) would enhance the reproducibility and rigor of this result.
*   **Reference Formatting:** The reference section appears to have several formatting errors (e.g., "? \Q1998\E"). These should be corrected.

### **Questions for Authors**

1.  The communication channel is limited to a single symbol per turn. How do you believe the dynamics would change if the protocol allowed for multi-symbol sequences? Do you hypothesize that a simple form of syntax or compositionality might emerge, and what changes to the game or agent architecture would be needed to encourage it?
2.  The experiments largely focus on successful convergence. Did you encounter any settings (e.g., different hyperparameters, rewards) where communication failed to emerge or converged to a sub-optimal "babbling equilibrium"? An analysis of these failure modes could be as insightful as the successful cases.
3.  In the grounding experiment, the supervised task and the referential game are sampled with equal probability. Have you explored the effect of varying this ratio? Is there a sweet spot that maximizes both coordination success and human interpretability, or does more supervision always lead to better grounding at no cost to game performance?
