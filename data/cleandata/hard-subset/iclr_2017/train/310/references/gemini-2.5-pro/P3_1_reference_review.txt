### **ICLR Review**

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols between neural agents in a cooperative, multi-agent reinforcement learning setting. The authors use a referential game where a "sender" agent must transmit a symbol from a discrete vocabulary to a "receiver" agent, enabling the receiver to identify a target image from a pair of images. The paper's core contributions are threefold: 1) It demonstrates that simple feed-forward neural agents, trained from scratch using REINFORCE, can successfully develop a communication protocol to solve this task with high accuracy. 2) It analyzes the semantic properties of the emergent language, showing that the agents' symbols correspond to high-level conceptual categories (measured by cluster purity) rather than just low-level visual features. 3) Most significantly, it proposes and validates methods to steer the emergent language towards being more abstract and human-interpretable. This is achieved first by manipulating the game's common knowledge (showing agents different instances of the same object class) and second, and more effectively, by combining the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task. The final experiment, featuring a human evaluation, compellingly shows that this hybrid training enables humans to interpret the agent's language, even when referring to objects outside the supervised training set, via plausible semantic generalizations.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. The primary reasons are:

1.  **Compelling Experimental Trajectory and Strong Results:** The paper presents a thoughtfully designed series of experiments that build upon one another, starting from the basic question of whether communication can emerge at all, and culminating in a demonstration of human-interpretable communication. The final experiment, grounding the agents' language through supervised learning and then evaluating it with human participants, is a novel and powerful demonstration of how to bridge the gap between emergent protocols and human language.
2.  **Important Research Direction:** The work tackles a fundamental problem in AI: moving beyond learning from static, "canned" datasets to learning the functional, interactive aspects of language. By framing the problem in a multi-agent setting and focusing explicitly on the *properties* of the emergent language (not just task success), the authors make a significant contribution to the development of more cooperative and understandable AI agents.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's strength lies in its clear, hypothesis-driven experimental design. The initial experiments convincingly show that agents can learn to communicate, and the comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures provides an interesting insight into how inductive biases can shape the resulting protocol (e.g., leading to a wider use of the vocabulary).

The analysis of the emergent semantics is a key differentiator from related work. While prior work had shown communication emerging, this paper goes further by asking *what* the agents are communicating about. The use of the purity metric against McRae's categories and the t-SNE visualizations provide good evidence that the learned symbols map to meaningful conceptual clusters. The "object-level reference" experiment is a particularly clever manipulation, demonstrating a deep understanding of the game-theoretic principles at play (i.e., modifying common knowledge to force more abstract coordination).

The paper's most significant contribution is the final section on grounding communication in human language. The idea of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning is inspired by successes like AlphaGo but is applied here to the novel domain of communication. The results are striking: not only does performance remain high, but purity increases dramatically, and the agents' symbols begin to align with human labels. The human evaluation provides the crucial validation. The finding that humans can interpret the agent's messages even for out-of-domain objects (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to the sea) is a fascinating result, suggesting that this training paradigm encourages the learning of flexible, metonymic-like semantic relationships that mirror those in human language. This points towards a viable path for developing AI that can bootstrap a communication system with other agents and then align it with humans without requiring constant human-in-the-loop training.

The paper is also well-situated within the literature, drawing connections to linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, and clearly distinguishing its contributions from prior work in multi-agent RL and supervised conversational AI.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, the following points could further improve its clarity and rigor:

*   **Training Details:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for high variance. Did the authors employ any variance reduction techniques, such as a baseline? The learning curves in Figure 2 appear quite stable, suggesting this might be the case. Clarifying this would strengthen the methodological description.
*   **Analysis of "Informed" Sender:** The intuition for the convolutional architecture in the "informed" sender is provided, but could be expanded. It's described as having an "inductive bias towards combining the two images dimension-by-dimension." A more detailed explanation of why this specific architecture would be expected to promote a richer vocabulary would be beneficial.
*   **Semantic Analysis:** The purity metric is a good choice, but the analysis could be deepened. For instance, instead of hard clusters, one could analyze the similarity structure of the symbol space. For example, by constructing a symbol-symbol co-occurrence matrix and comparing its structure (e.g., via RSA) to a human-derived semantic similarity matrix for the concepts, one could get a more nuanced view of the semantic alignment.
*   **Human Evaluation Details:** The crowdsourced experiment is a highlight. To further bolster its credibility, it would be helpful to report the inter-annotator agreement. Additionally, providing more detail on the instructions given to participants would help readers assess the potential for experimenter demand effects.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  In your final experiment, you demonstrate fascinating "metonymic" generalizations (e.g., `dolphin` -> `sea`, `fence` -> `land`). Do you have a hypothesis about the mechanism driving these specific generalizations? Are they driven by visual similarities in the pre-trained VGG space (e.g., sea and dolphins co-occurring in ImageNet), or is this a more abstract relationship learned during the hybrid training phase?
2.  The REINFORCE updates are applied to both the sender and receiver simultaneously based on a shared reward. Have you considered or experimented with alternative credit assignment schemes, such as training the receiver with a supervised loss (given the sender's symbol) while the sender is trained with RL, or other multi-agent learning algorithms?
3.  The vocabulary size is fixed at 10 or 100. How do you see the performance and the nature of the emergent language changing as the vocabulary size and the complexity of the visual environment (e.g., number of possible distractors) scale up significantly?
