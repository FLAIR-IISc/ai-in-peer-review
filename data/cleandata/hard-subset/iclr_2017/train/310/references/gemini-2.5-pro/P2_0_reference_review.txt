### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with coordinating in a simple referential game. The agents start *tabula rasa* and must develop their own communication protocol to successfully identify a target image from a distractor. The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It demonstrates that simple feed-forward agents trained with reinforcement learning can successfully develop a communication protocol to solve this task.
2.  It explores how inductive biases in the agent architecture (the "informed" sender) can lead to a richer, more structured emergent language, as measured by vocabulary usage and semantic clustering purity.
3.  It introduces a novel modification to the game (using different instances of the same object class for sender and receiver) to push the agents towards learning more abstract, conceptual representations rather than relying on low-level visual features.
4.  Most significantly, it proposes and validates a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, the sender learns to use symbols that align with human-understandable words. This grounded language is shown to be interpretable by human evaluators, even when referring to objects not seen during supervised training, demonstrating a form of semantic generalization.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-executed and insightful paper that addresses a fundamental question in AI: how to develop agents that can communicate effectively and, crucially, in a way that is interpretable to humans. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Significant Grounding Methodology:** The core contribution—combining interactive self-play with supervised learning to ground an emergent language—is both elegant and highly effective. The subsequent human evaluation provides strong evidence that this method enables generalization and produces a communication protocol that is not just a compact code for the agents, but one that taps into human-like semantic abstractions (e.g., the "metonymic" links). This is a significant step towards creating agents that can learn to communicate with humans without requiring constant human-in-the-loop training.
2.  **Clear Experimental Design and Rigorous Analysis:** The paper presents a series of well-designed experiments that logically build upon one another. It starts with a foundational question (Can agents learn to communicate?), investigates the properties of the emergent language, thoughtfully modifies the environment to encourage abstraction, and culminates in the grounding experiment. The claims are well-supported by both quantitative metrics (accuracy, purity, SVD analysis) and a well-designed human study with appropriate statistical analysis.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper is strong across multiple dimensions. The introduction effectively situates the work within the broader context of multi-agent AI, distinguishing it clearly from purely supervised approaches and other multi-agent communication studies. The connection to classic work in game theory (Lewis signaling games) and cognitive science provides a solid theoretical foundation.

The experimental progression is particularly compelling. The initial experiments establish a strong baseline and provide interesting insights on their own. For example, the finding that the "agnostic" sender with only two symbols converges to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is a fascinating parallel to findings in human cognition and demonstrates that the setup encourages meaningful semantic organization. The design of the "informed" sender, with its convolutional structure encouraging dimensional comparison, is a clever way to introduce an inductive bias that proves beneficial.

The final experiment is the highlight. The idea of using a separate supervised task to "anchor" the symbols to human labels, while letting the interactive game teach the agents *how* to use those symbols functionally, is a powerful paradigm. The human evaluation is the critical piece of evidence that validates this approach. Achieving 68% accuracy in a task where humans must interpret the agent's novel word usage is a strong result. The qualitative examples (e.g., "dolphin" for sea, "fence" for land) are illustrative and provide intuitive support for the claim that the agent learns flexible, context-dependent meanings, a hallmark of human language.

### Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Agent Complexity:** The agents are intentionally simple feed-forward networks. It would be interesting to discuss the potential implications of using more complex, recurrent architectures. Could recurrence enable the emergence of compositional language or multi-symbol utterances, moving beyond the single-symbol protocol used here?
*   **Analysis of Failures:** The human evaluation showed 68% success, which is impressive but also implies 32% failure. A brief analysis or discussion of the failure cases could be very insightful. Are there systematic patterns in the mistakes? Do they occur when the semantic leap required is too large, or when the images are visually ambiguous?
*   **Scalability:** The experiments use a vocabulary of up to 100 symbols. It would be valuable to include a short discussion on the potential challenges and directions for scaling this approach to much larger vocabularies and more complex environments, which would be necessary for more general-purpose conversational agents.
*   **REINFORCE Algorithm:** The paper uses the standard REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for its high variance. It would be beneficial to briefly comment on the stability of the training process. Did the authors experiment with any variance reduction techniques (e.g., baselines) or more advanced policy gradient methods, and if not, why was REINFORCE sufficient for this task?

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The temperature parameter for the Gibbs distributions is set to 10 without tuning. This parameter seems critical, as it controls the "sharpness" of the agents' policies and the degree of exploration in the communication protocol. How sensitive are the results, particularly the speed of convergence and the number of symbols used, to this hyperparameter?
2.  In the object-level reference experiment (Section 4.1), the increase in purity is noted as "small." While the t-SNE visualization is clearer, the quantitative improvement seems marginal. Do you have intuitions on how to modify the environment further to more strongly encourage this kind of abstraction?
3.  For the human evaluation, the sender produces a word that is an ImageNet label. Humans are then asked to guess the target. Have you considered the reverse experiment: providing a human-generated word for a pair of images and observing which image the *agent receiver* selects? This could provide further evidence for a shared semantic space between humans and the agents.
