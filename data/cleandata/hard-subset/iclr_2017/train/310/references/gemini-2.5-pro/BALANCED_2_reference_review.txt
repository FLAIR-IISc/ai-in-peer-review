### **Summary**

This paper presents a compelling research program on the emergence of communication in multi-agent systems. The authors use a referential game framework where two neural network agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop a shared communication protocol from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a distractor. The core contributions are threefold. First, the paper demonstrates that simple agents can successfully learn to communicate effectively in this setting using realistic image inputs. Second, it systematically investigates how different sender architectures and game mechanics (e.g., forcing reference at the object-category level) can influence the emergent protocol, pushing it towards more abstract, conceptual representations. Third, and most significantly, the paper proposes and validates a novel method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language by combining the interactive game with a standard supervised image-labeling task. A human evaluation study compellingly shows that this grounded language is partially interpretable by humans, even when used to refer to objects outside the supervised training set.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Well-motivated framework with strong execution:** The paper introduces a clean and effective experimental paradigm for studying emergent communication, grounding its approach in established concepts from game theory and cognitive science. The experiments are systematic, well-designed, and build logically towards the main claims.
2.  **Novel and impactful results on grounding:** The primary contribution—showing that combining interactive self-play with a supervised objective can produce a human-interpretable communication protocol—is a significant step towards building AI agents that can coordinate with humans. The human study provides strong, direct evidence for this claim, and the qualitative analysis of "metonymic" generalization is particularly insightful.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous. The motivation is exceptionally clear, effectively arguing for the necessity of interactive learning to capture the functional aspects of language, a limitation of purely supervised approaches. The work is well-situated in the literature, drawing connections to multi-agent RL, game theory, and linguistics, which strengthens its foundation.

The empirical investigation is thorough and convincing.
*   The initial experiments successfully establish that agents can converge to a successful communication policy, and the analysis goes beyond simple accuracy. The use of purity scores to measure semantic coherence and SVD to analyze vocabulary richness provides valuable insight into the *quality* of the emergent language.
*   The manipulation of "common knowledge" by using different image instances for the sender and receiver is a clever way to force the agents to coordinate on more abstract, class-level properties, and the results support this hypothesis (Table 2).
*   The final set of experiments on grounding is the paper's highlight. The hybrid training approach is an elegant solution to bridge the gap between an arbitrary emergent protocol and human language. The subsequent human evaluation is a critical piece of the validation, moving beyond proxy metrics to directly test the central hypothesis of human interpretability. The finding that humans can guess the correct referent 68% of the time is strong evidence that the grounding generalizes beyond the specific labels learned during supervision.

While the paper is strong, there are minor weaknesses. The agent architectures are relatively simple feed-forward networks, and the communication channel is limited to a single symbol. This is a reasonable starting point for a research program, but it simplifies the problem considerably compared to the compositional and sequential nature of human language. Furthermore, the "informed" sender architecture, while effective, introduces a strong inductive bias for comparing inputs dimension-wise, and the design feels somewhat handcrafted. However, these points do not detract from the core contributions and represent exciting avenues for future work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended to be constructive suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   The temperature parameter (τ=10) for the Gibbs distributions seems high. This encourages exploration but might also lead to more stochastic policies at test time. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be valuable.
*   In the hybrid experiment, the balance between game-playing and supervised training is set at 1:1. It would be interesting to know if this grounding effect can be achieved with less supervision (e.g., a 9:1 ratio), which would be more scalable in practice. Exploring this trade-off could provide useful insights.
*   The connection to the game-theoretic concept of "common knowledge" is a nice touch. This could be strengthened slightly by more formally discussing what information is and is not common knowledge in each experimental setup.
*   There are minor formatting issues in the bibliography (e.g., "? \Q1998\E"). A pass to clean these up would improve the final presentation.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate your thoughts on the following questions to help clarify my understanding of the work.
1.  Could you elaborate on the SVD analysis for the agnostic sender? The text states it uses a "compact 2-symbol vocabulary," but Figure 2 (right) seems to show a spectrum with more than two significant dimensions. Does this mean it primarily uses two symbols but occasionally uses others, which account for the additional dimensions?
2.  The human evaluation results are very promising. Do you have any qualitative insights into the 32% of cases where humans failed to identify the correct target? Were there common patterns in these failures, perhaps related to the sender's word choices being too abstract or ambiguous?
3.  Have you considered a symmetric grounding setup where the receiver is also trained on a supervised task (e.g., mapping symbols back to image categories)? It seems the grounding is currently only enforced on the sender's side. Would grounding both agents lead to more efficient or robust communication?
4.  The "informed" sender architecture is quite specific. Did you experiment with other architectures designed for comparing pairs of inputs, such as Siamese-style networks that process each input through shared weights before comparison?
