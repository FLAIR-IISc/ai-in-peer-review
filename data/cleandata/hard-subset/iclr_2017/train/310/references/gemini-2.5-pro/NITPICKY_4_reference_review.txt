**Summary**

The paper investigates the emergence of language in a multi-agent cooperative setting. The authors design a referential game where a "sender" agent must communicate a target image to a "receiver" agent from a pair of images. The agents, implemented as neural networks, are trained tabula rasa using reinforcement learning. The paper explores how different sender architectures and game mechanics (e.g., abstracting away from specific image instances) affect the emergent communication protocol. The authors measure success by task completion and analyze the semantic properties of the emergent language using cluster purity. A key contribution is an attempt to ground the agents' language in human language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task. A final human evaluation suggests that this grounding enables humans to interpret the agents' communication, even for concepts not seen during supervised training.

**Decision: Reject**

While the research direction is valuable and the core idea of combining interactive and supervised learning for language grounding is compelling, the paper is undermined by a significant lack of methodological rigor and an unacceptable level of carelessness in its presentation. Numerous ambiguities in the experimental setup, coupled with severe formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography, make it impossible to confidently assess the validity and reproducibility of the work. The accumulation of these flaws erodes trust in the reported results.

**Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on two primary concerns: a lack of clarity in the experimental methodology and severe presentational sloppiness.

1.  **Methodological Ambiguities and Missing Details:** The paper omits several crucial details that are necessary to understand and reproduce the experiments.
    *   **Hyperparameter Justification:** Key hyperparameters are stated as being set "without tuning," but their values are not justified. For instance, the Gibbs/softmax temperature is set to τ=10. This is a very high value that would make the policy nearly uniform, which seems counter-intuitive for effective learning. No rationale is provided for this choice.
    *   **Reinforcement Learning Implementation:** The paper states that parameters are updated via the REINFORCE rule. However, no details are given about the implementation. Vanilla REINFORCE is known for high variance; it is unclear if any standard variance reduction techniques (e.g., baselines) were employed. This is a critical detail for evaluating the stability and efficiency of the learning process.
    *   **Vague Experimental Conditions:** The description of the "object-level reference" experiment in Section 4.1 is too brief. It states that agents are shown "different pairs of images but maintaining the ImageNet class," but fails to explicitly describe the inputs for both the sender and receiver, which is essential for understanding the mechanism. Similarly, the claim that SVD dimensionality was determined via cross-validation lacks any description of the procedure.
    *   **Inconsistent or Unclear Representations:** Section 3 states that image representations are from either the VGG `sm` or `fc` layer, but it is not consistently specified which representation is used for each experiment, leaving the reader to guess.
    *   **Imprecise Notation:** The notation for the agents' policies is confusing. For example, the sender's input is described as `θS(iL, iR, t)`, incorrectly conflating the model parameters (`θS`) with the function's input. This sort of imprecision pervades the formal descriptions.

2.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of care that is unbecoming of a formal scientific publication.
    *   **Unusable Reference Section:** The most glaring issue is the bibliography, which is riddled with severe formatting errors. Entries contain raw LaTeX-like markup (e.g., `? \Q1998\E`), are incomplete, and are formatted inconsistently. For example, the entry for Brandenburger and Dekel is malformed and seemingly duplicated. A functional bibliography is a fundamental requirement for scholarly work; its absence here is a critical failure.
    *   **Misleading Architectural Claim:** The paper claims that "the agnostic architecture nests the informed one." This is factually incorrect. A fully-connected layer over concatenated inputs (agnostic) does not structurally nest an architecture using 1D convolutions across embeddings treated as channels (informed). While the former might be able to approximate the latter's function, "nesting" implies a direct structural relationship that does not exist here.
    *   **Minor Formatting Issues:** Numerous smaller issues contribute to the overall impression of carelessness. A footnote about the first author's affiliation appears on the first page, which is non-standard. The citation for Crawford (1998) is needlessly repeated in the same paragraph. Informal punctuation like the trailing ellipsis in "(e.g., cat, apple, car. . . )" is used.

While the paper tackles an important problem, the scientific process demands rigor and clarity. The sheer number of omissions, inaccuracies, and formatting errors prevents a thorough and confident review of the work's contributions.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The core idea of this paper is promising. A revised version with a meticulous approach to detail could be a strong contribution.
*   The paper would benefit from a dedicated "Related Work" section to provide a more structured overview of the literature, rather than scattering it throughout the introduction.
*   The human evaluation in Section 5 is a highlight. To strengthen it further, provide details on participant screening, instructions, and inter-annotator agreement.
*   I strongly advise a complete overhaul of the bibliography and a thorough proofreading of the entire manuscript to correct all formatting and notational errors.
*   For all experiments, please provide a complete and unambiguous description of the agent architectures, inputs, hyperparameters (with justification), and training algorithm details.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a justification for using a temperature of τ=10? How sensitive are the results to this parameter?
2.  Were any variance reduction techniques, such as a learned value function baseline, used in your REINFORCE implementation?
3.  In the object-level reference game, if the target is a "dog" and the distractor is a "cat," does the sender see (image_of_chihuahua, image_of_persian) while the receiver sees (image_of_beagle, image_of_siamese)? Please clarify the exact input structure for both agents.
4.  Regarding the test set used for evaluation: does it consist of new images of the same 463 concepts from training, or does it test generalization to images of novel concepts not seen during training?
