### **Summary**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with a cooperative referential game. The agents, starting *tabula rasa*, must develop a communication protocol to allow the receiver to identify a target image from a pair of images. The authors explore how architectural inductive biases (an "informed" vs. "agnostic" sender) affect the emergent language. They analyze the semantic properties of the developed protocol, finding that it captures high-level conceptual properties of the images. Furthermore, they demonstrate that by forcing agents to coordinate on object-level properties (by showing them different instances of the same object class), the resulting language becomes more abstract. Finally, in a compelling experiment, they show that by mixing the game with a supervised image-labeling task, the emergent language can be grounded in human language, leading to a protocol that is partially interpretable by human participants, even on out-of-domain images.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Insufficient Baselines and Contextualization:** The paper presents an interesting self-contained study but fails to situate its contributions within the existing literature through empirical comparison. While related work is cited (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), the proposed methods are not benchmarked against them. Without comparisons to established multi-agent communication methods or even simpler, non-interactive baselines, it is difficult to assess the efficacy and properties of the emergent language.
2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty and Rigor:** The core framework is a variation of the classic Lewis signaling game, and the agents are trained with the standard REINFORCE algorithm. The primary methodological contributions—the "informed" sender architecture and the mixed supervised/interactive training schedule—are interesting but incremental. The empirical analysis, while providing some insights, lacks the depth and rigor expected. For instance, claims about learned semantics (e.g., "living-vs-non-living") are based on qualitative observation, and the stability and sensitivity of the REINFORCE training procedure are not discussed.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The premise of the paper—studying emergent communication in a controlled environment—is valuable. However, the execution and evaluation fall short of the standards required for a top-tier publication.

My primary concern is the lack of comparative evaluation. The paper compares two of its own sender architectures ("agnostic" and "informed") but provides no external point of reference. The most relevant contemporary works from Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) explore similar problems of emergent communication in multi-agent RL. A comparison on a shared task, or at least a thorough discussion of the differences in emergent protocols, would be necessary to understand the unique contributions of this work. More fundamentally, a non-interactive baseline is missing. For the final human evaluation task, how would a simple supervised model perform? For instance, one could train a model to predict a distinguishing attribute for the target image. If humans could use this attribute to identify the target with similar or greater success, it would challenge the paper's core premise that interactivity is essential for developing a useful, grounded communication system.

Secondly, the technical contribution and its analysis could be strengthened. The REINFORCE algorithm is known for its high variance, yet there is no mention of training stability, learning curves beyond the first 5k steps, or the use of any variance reduction techniques (e.g., baselines). The hyperparameter settings are presented without justification; for example, a Gibbs temperature of τ=10 is quite high and would lead to a nearly uniform sampling policy, which seems counterintuitive for encouraging a precise communication protocol. The analysis of the emergent language relies on metrics like purity and post-hoc visualizations (SVD, t-SNE). While illustrative, these are not definitive. The claim that the agnostic sender develops a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is intriguing but presented as a qualitative observation. This should be supported by quantitative analysis (e.g., by computing purity against this specific binary partition).

The human evaluation experiment in Section 5 is the most promising part of the paper. However, even here, more detail is required to assess the strength of the findings. The 68% success rate is presented without a clear baseline (chance is 50%) or measures of statistical significance beyond the logistic regression. The examples provided (dolphin for sea, fence for land) are compelling, but they may represent cherry-picked successes. The paper would be more convincing if it also discussed failure modes and provided a more systematic analysis of the "metonymic links" that were successfully interpreted by humans.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The paper is well-written and the motivation is clearly articulated. The research direction is undoubtedly important for the field.
*   The experiment in Section 4.1, where agents are shown different instances of the same class, is a clever design choice that effectively forces abstraction. This is a strength of the paper.
*   The combination of supervised and reinforcement learning for grounding is a powerful idea and the results are encouraging. This approach merits further exploration.
*   To improve the paper, I would strongly recommend implementing and comparing against at least one of the cited MARL communication methods. Adding a strong, non-interactive baseline would also significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the benefits of interactive learning.
*   Provide a more thorough ablation study and analysis of hyperparameters, particularly the temperature parameter in the Gibbs distributions, to demonstrate the robustness of the learning process.
*   Quantify the qualitative claims. For example, formally test the "living-vs-non-living" hypothesis.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on why no comparisons were made to existing MARL communication frameworks like those of Foerster et al. (2016) or Sukhbaatar et al. (2016)? How do you expect your approach and the resulting language properties to differ?
2.  The REINFORCE algorithm was used for training. Did you encounter issues with training instability or high variance? Were any techniques, such as using a value function baseline, considered or implemented to mitigate this?
3.  The temperature parameter `τ` was set to 10. This seems unusually high, as it would flatten the policy distribution significantly. Could you provide the rationale for this choice and discuss how sensitive the results are to this parameter?
4.  For the human evaluation in Section 5, what was the inter-rater agreement among the 10 participants for each pair? Was the 68% success rate based on a majority vote, or is it the average accuracy across all individual ratings? Understanding the consistency of human interpretation is key to evaluating this result.
