### Summary

This paper presents a research program aimed at fostering emergent communication between AI agents through cooperative games. The authors introduce a simple referential game where a "sender" agent must communicate a single symbol to a "receiver" agent to help it identify a target image from a pair of images. The agents, implemented as simple neural networks, start as blank slates (*tabula rasa*) and are trained via reinforcement learning solely on the basis of communication success. The paper demonstrates that agents can successfully learn to communicate in this setup. More importantly, it investigates how the properties of the emergent language can be shaped. The authors show that introducing specific inductive biases into the agent architecture (the "informed" sender) leads to faster learning and a richer vocabulary. They find that the emergent symbols correspond to high-level semantic categories (measured by cluster purity) rather than just low-level visual features. By modifying the game to remove low-level image details from common knowledge (the "object-level reference" task), they successfully nudge the agents towards a more abstract, conceptual communication protocol. Finally, in a compelling set of experiments, they show that combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task effectively grounds the emergent language in human terms. A human evaluation confirms that this grounded language is interpretable and generalizes beyond the supervised labels, with agents creatively using words in a metonymic fashion that humans can understand.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Fundamental Problem and Elegant Framework:** The paper addresses the fundamental and highly challenging problem of language emergence and grounding. It does so using a clean, well-motivated experimental framework based on Lewis' signaling games, which is thoughtfully placed within a rich body of literature from AI, linguistics, and game theory.
2.  **Strong and Insightful Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and progressively build a strong case. The final experiment, which combines interactive learning with supervised grounding and is validated with a human study, is particularly compelling. It provides strong evidence that this approach can lead to a communication protocol that is not only effective between agents but also partially and flexibly interpretable by humans.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper is its clear and principled investigation into a difficult problem. The authors move beyond simply showing that agents *can* communicate, and instead focus on the more interesting question of what *kind* of language emerges and how we can guide it towards being more human-like.

The paper's experimental design is logical and effective. It starts by establishing a baseline, showing that agents can indeed solve the referential game. The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures provides a nice demonstration of how inductive biases can influence learning dynamics and the complexity of the resulting protocol. The analysis of the emergent language, using purity scores and SVD, provides quantitative evidence that the agents are learning meaningful semantic representations.

The experiment on "object-level reference" is a clever manipulation of the game's common knowledge, demonstrating that the environment's design is a powerful tool for shaping the nature of the emergent communication towards more abstract concepts.

The paper's most significant contribution is the final set of experiments on grounding the communication in human language. The idea of combining self-play (for learning the functional use of language) with supervised learning (for grounding symbols to human concepts) is powerful and analogous to the successful approach of AlphaGo. The human evaluation provides a crucial validation of this method. The finding that agents can use words metonymically (e.g., "dolphin" for an image of the sea) and that humans can correctly interpret these usages is a fascinating and encouraging result, suggesting a path towards more flexible human-AI communication. The work is well-written, the claims are well-supported by the evidence provided, and it opens up many exciting avenues for future research.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions for future work and are not critical for the acceptance of this paper.

*   **Communication Protocol Complexity:** The current work focuses on a single-symbol, single-turn communication protocol. This is an excellent starting point, but natural language is sequential and compositional. It would be interesting to see how the current framework could be extended to allow for multi-symbol messages or multi-turn dialogues, which would necessitate the use of recurrent architectures (e.g., LSTMs) for the agents.
*   **Deeper Analysis of Emergent Language:** The purity analysis is informative but relies on a pre-defined set of human categories. It would be fascinating to perform a more bottom-up analysis of the concepts the agents discover on their own. For example, visualizing the learned "game-specific" embeddings could reveal the conceptual space that the agents build. Furthermore, an analysis of failure cases (i.e., when communication breaks down) could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the learned protocol.
*   **Training and Alternatives:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm. Given the well-known high variance of this method, it would be beneficial to include a brief discussion of the training stability. Mentioning whether other techniques, such as using a baseline to reduce variance or exploring alternatives like the Gumbel-Softmax trick for passing gradients through the discrete communication channel, were considered would add valuable context for researchers building on this work.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper provides a good comparison between the two sender architectures. However, the reliance on pre-trained VGG features is a significant factor. An ablation study showing performance when training the visual module from scratch (or with less powerful features) would help clarify how much of the semantic heavy lifting is being done by the pre-trained network versus the interactive learning process.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  **Temperature Parameter:** The temperature `τ` for the Gibbs distributions was set to 10. This value seems quite high, which would push the policy towards being nearly deterministic and could limit exploration during the initial phases of learning. Could you comment on the motivation for this choice and how sensitive the results are to this hyperparameter?
2.  **Generalization to Novel Concepts:** A key goal stated is to develop a language "portable to new situations." How well does the learned communication protocol generalize to entirely new object categories that were not seen during training? For instance, in the experiments from Section 4, if the agents were trained on a subset of the McRae categories, could they still achieve above-chance communication on held-out categories?
3.  **Details of Human Evaluation:** Could you provide more detail on the instructions given to the human participants in the CrowdFlower task? Specifically, were they instructed simply to pick the image "most related" to the word, or were they primed in any way to look for abstract or non-literal connections? Understanding the framing of the task is crucial for interpreting the 68% success rate.
