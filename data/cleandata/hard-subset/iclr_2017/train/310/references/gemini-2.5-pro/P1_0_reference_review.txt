### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a compelling research program aimed at fostering emergent communication between AI agents through cooperative, multi-agent games. The core contribution is a referential game framework where two neural network agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop a communication protocol from scratch (*tabula rasa*) to identify a target image from a distractor. The paper systematically investigates several key questions: (1) whether agents can successfully learn to coordinate, (2) how environmental factors and architectural biases influence the semantic properties of the emergent language, and (3) how this emergent language can be grounded in human language to become interpretable.

The authors demonstrate that agents consistently learn to solve the coordination task. More importantly, they show that specific architectural choices (an "informed" sender with a bias for feature-wise comparison) and game setups (forcing reference at the object-category level) can nudge the emergent protocol towards having more structure and using more of the available vocabulary. The most significant contribution is a novel training paradigm that combines the interactive referential game with a supervised image-labeling task. This hybrid approach not only preserves coordination success but dramatically improves the semantic structure of the language (measured by category purity) and, crucially, grounds the agents' symbols in human-understandable terms. The authors validate this with a human study, showing that people can interpret the agent's messages to solve the referential task even for objects the agent was never explicitly taught names for, demonstrating a form of semantic generalization that is very promising for human-AI interaction.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Important Problem and Elegant Formulation:** The paper tackles the fundamental problem of how to move beyond static, supervised datasets to learn the functional, interactive aspects of language. The use of multi-agent referential games is an elegant and well-motivated framework for this problem, drawing clear connections to linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory.
2.  **Novel and Compelling Results on Grounding:** The final experiment, which combines self-play with supervised learning to ground the emergent language, is highly novel and impactful. The demonstration that this grounding generalizes, allowing humans to interpret the agent's novel "metonymic" word usage (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to a picture of the sea), is a significant finding that opens up a clear and exciting path for future research in human-AI communication.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-structured and the experiments build upon each other in a logical and convincing progression.

First, the authors establish a solid baseline, showing that simple feed-forward agents trained with reinforcement learning can solve the coordination task. They then thoughtfully investigate the *nature* of the emergent communication. The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures is insightful, providing concrete evidence that inductive biases in the model can significantly shape the complexity and structure of the learned protocol. The SVD analysis to rule out simple synonymy is a good piece of rigorous analysis.

Second, the experiment in Section 4.1, which modifies the "common knowledge" of the game by showing agents different instances of the same object class, is a clever way to force generalization. While the improvement in purity is modest, it successfully demonstrates that the game's design directly influences the level of abstraction in the emergent semantics, moving it from instance-level features to class-level properties.

Finally, the main strength of the paper lies in Section 5. The idea of using a supervised task not as the end goal, but as a "nudge" to align an agent's internal representations with human concepts during interactive learning is powerful. The results are impressive: a dramatic increase in semantic purity and, most importantly, measurable human interpretability on a novel dataset (ReferItGame). The 68% success rate in the human study is strong quantitative evidence that the agent has learned more than just simple label-object associations; it has learned a semantic space that partially overlaps with human intuition, enabling successful communication even without direct labels. The qualitative examples (Figure 4) are excellent and make the concept of learned "metonymy" very clear.

The paper is also well-situated in the literature, clearly differentiating its cooperative, emergent, and grounding-focused approach from purely supervised methods, pre-programmed multi-agent systems, and other related work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.

*   **Clarity on "Informed Sender" Intuition:** While the mechanism of the informed sender is described, the intuition for *why* a 1D convolution across the concatenated embeddings provides a bias for "dimension-by-dimension" comparison could be explained more explicitly. A little more detail here would strengthen the reader's understanding of this key architectural choice.
*   **Training Details:** The paper mentions using the REINFORCE algorithm. It is notoriously high-variance. It would be helpful to know if any variance reduction techniques (e.g., a baseline) were used, and to comment briefly on the stability of the training process.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors state that hyperparameters were set without tuning. While this is acceptable, it might be worth briefly mentioning which parameters (e.g., the Gibbs temperature `τ`) are expected to be most sensitive and what effect they might have. For instance, a very low temperature would make the policies nearly deterministic and might hinder exploration, while a very high one could prevent convergence.
*   **Human Study Details:** For completeness and replicability, it would be beneficial to include more details about the crowdsourced experiment, such as the number of participants, the exact instructions given to them, and how the 10 ratings per pair were aggregated to arrive at the 68% figure (e.g., was it majority vote, or based on the proportion of correct answers across all ratings?).

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The agnostic sender consistently collapses to using only two symbols. The paper qualitatively suggests this might correspond to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. Have you investigated why this collapse occurs? Is it a difficult-to-escape local minimum? Does the informed architecture's inductive bias primarily serve to prevent this collapse and encourage the use of a richer vocabulary?
2.  In the final human evaluation experiment, what did the failure cases look like? An error analysis could be very insightful. Were there specific types of images or metonymic leaps that humans consistently failed to understand? This could provide valuable clues for how to further improve the alignment between the agent's and humans' semantic spaces.
3.  The supervised grounding task uses 100 ImageNet labels. How was this subset of 100 chosen from the full set of 463 concepts? Was it random, or was it curated to cover a diverse set of categories? The choice of grounding vocabulary could potentially have a significant impact on the resulting generalized communication protocol.
