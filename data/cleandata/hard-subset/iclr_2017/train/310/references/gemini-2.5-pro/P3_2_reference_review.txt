### Review

**Summary of Claims and Contributions**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with coordinating in a simple referential game. The core contribution is a research program that moves beyond supervised learning on static conversational data to an interactive, game-based paradigm where agents must develop a communication protocol from scratch (`tabula rasa`). The authors systematically explore several key questions:
1.  Whether simple neural agents can successfully learn to communicate about real-world images.
2.  How architectural inductive biases (an "informed" vs. "agnostic" sender) affect the speed of convergence and the nature of the emergent language.
3.  How modifications to the game's "common knowledge" (e.g., forcing agents to refer to object categories rather than specific image instances) can push the emergent language toward greater abstraction and conceptual grounding.
4.  Most significantly, the paper proposes and validates a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task (inspired by AlphaGo's training), the authors show that the agent's communication becomes significantly more interpretable to humans, even when referring to objects not seen during the supervised phase.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a clear and compelling contribution to the field of multi-agent communication and grounded language learning. My primary reasons for acceptance are:
1.  The work tackles a fundamental problem—how to learn the functional, interactive aspects of language—with a clean, well-motivated experimental setup that builds logically from simple emergence to human-grounded communication.
2.  The experimental results are robust and persuasive. The final human evaluation experiment, which demonstrates that the grounded language generalizes to novel referents in a way that is interpretable to humans, provides powerful support for the paper's central claims and represents a significant step forward for the field.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is commendable for its clarity of purpose and methodical execution. It is well-positioned within the existing literature, correctly identifying the limitations of purely supervised approaches and building upon recent work in multi-agent RL (Foerster et al., 2016; Sukhbaatar et al., 2016) by adding a crucial focus on the *interpretability* and *semantics* of the emergent protocol.

The experimental design is a major strength. The progression is logical and each experiment provides a clear answer to a well-posed question.
- The initial experiments convincingly show that agents can solve the task, with the "informed" sender architecture providing a useful (if simple) inductive bias that speeds up learning and encourages the use of a larger vocabulary. The use of SVD to analyze vocabulary usage is a clever way to argue against simple synonymy.
- The "object-level reference" experiment (Section 4.1) is particularly elegant. By manipulating what is common knowledge between the agents, the authors force them away from coordinating on low-level visual features toward more abstract, class-level properties. This is a powerful demonstration of how the environment design can shape the emergent semantics, and the resulting increase in category purity provides strong evidence for their hypothesis.
- The final set of experiments on grounding (Section 5) is the paper's most significant contribution. The hybrid supervised-RL training paradigm is well-motivated by AlphaGo and proves highly effective. The quantitative results are impressive (70% purity, 47% use of the correct label for known objects). However, the generalization experiment on the ReferItGame dataset is the true highlight. The finding that humans can correctly interpret the agent's "metonymic" word usage (e.g., 'dolphin' for sea) 68% of the time is a striking result. It suggests that this training method doesn't just instill a rigid mapping but a flexible, semantic association that shares common ground with human intuition. This has profound implications for building AI agents that can eventually collaborate with humans.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas that could be strengthened or clarified. These points are intended as constructive feedback and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

1.  **On the REINFORCE Algorithm:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for its high variance. While it clearly works for this setup, it would be valuable to include a brief discussion of this choice. Did the authors experience training instability? Were alternatives like actor-critic methods or policy gradient methods with a baseline considered? Given the discrete communication channel, the Gumbel-Softmax trick could also be an alternative to allow for direct backpropagation, and it would be interesting to know if the authors considered it.

2.  **Sender Architecture:** The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" senders is interesting but feels somewhat preliminary. The "informed" architecture imposes a specific bias (dimension-wise comparison via convolution). It would be beneficial to discuss the intuition behind this specific choice more deeply and perhaps speculate on other architectural biases that could encourage different linguistic properties (e.g., compositionality).

3.  **Analysis of Failures:** The human evaluation result of 68% accuracy is impressive but also implies that in 32% of cases, humans failed to understand the agent. A qualitative analysis of these failure cases would be highly informative. Do they occur when the semantic link is too tenuous? Or are there systematic patterns to the agent's "mistakes"? This would provide deeper insight into the limits of the current grounding method.

4.  **Receiver's Role in Grounding:** The paper focuses on the sender's training, where the game-embedding layer is shared between the game and the supervised task. However, the receiver is never exposed to the human labels directly. It learns entirely through the reward signal from the game. This is a crucial and powerful aspect of the setup, as it means the receiver learns to "decode" the grounded symbols purely through interaction. Explicitly highlighting this point would strengthen the paper's claims about interactive learning.

**Clarifying Questions for the Authors**

1.  The temperature parameter for the Gibbs distributions was set to `τ=10` without tuning. This seems quite high, encouraging a more uniform, exploratory policy. How sensitive are the results, particularly the speed of convergence and the final performance, to this parameter? Did you experiment with a temperature annealing schedule?

2.  In the grounding experiment (Section 5), could you clarify how the receiver learns to interpret the symbols that now have a supervised grounding for the sender? Since the receiver's only learning signal is task success, it must implicitly infer the meaning of, for example, the symbol for "car". Does this slow down learning compared to the non-grounded setting?

3.  The SVD analysis suggests the emergent language requires ~50 dimensions. Given the vocabulary size of 100, this still suggests a fair degree of redundancy or structure. Do you have any intuitions about what these principal components might correspond to semantically?
