### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols between neural network agents in the context of a cooperative, referential game. The primary goal is to explore how to design learning environments and agent architectures that foster the development of a language that is not only effective for coordination but also semantically structured and, ultimately, interpretable by humans. The authors first demonstrate that tabula rasa agents, equipped with simple neural architectures, can successfully learn to communicate to identify target objects among distractors in a dataset of natural images. The work then systematically explores factors influencing the properties of the emergent language, showing that architectural inductive biases (an "informed" sender) and modifications to the game's common knowledge (forcing reference at the object-category level) can guide the language towards greater semantic abstraction and structure. The paper's most significant contribution is a novel hybrid training paradigm that combines interactive game-playing with a supervised image-labeling task. This approach is shown to successfully ground the emergent communication protocol in human-understandable concepts, dramatically increasing its semantic purity and, as validated by a human-subjects experiment, its direct interpretability to people, even when generalizing to novel objects.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Important Problem and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper tackles the fundamental challenge of moving beyond static, supervised language learning towards functional, interactive communication. The motivation is exceptionally clear, and the approach of using multi-agent referential games as a testbed is principled, well-grounded in prior work from game theory and cognitive science, and represents a promising direction for AI research.
2.  **Strong and Progressive Empirical Evidence:** The paper presents a series of logically structured experiments with convincing results. The culmination of this work—the hybrid training method for grounding communication and its rigorous validation via a human study—is a novel and impactful contribution that provides a tangible path toward developing agents that can communicate in ways aligned with human conceptual structures.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The strengths of this paper are numerous and substantial:

*   **Clarity and Vision:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction provides a compelling vision for a research program focused on emergent communication, clearly articulating the limitations of existing paradigms and situating the work within a broad and relevant literature spanning MARL, NLP, and game theory.
*   **Systematic and Insightful Experiments:** The experimental design is a key strength. The paper does not simply show that agents can learn to communicate, but methodically investigates *how* they communicate and *what* factors shape the resulting language. The comparison between "agnostic" and "informed" senders highlights the importance of inductive bias. The "object-level reference" experiment is a clever manipulation of common knowledge that successfully pushes the agents towards more abstract representations. This systematic progression builds a strong and convincing narrative.
*   **Novelty and Impact of Hybrid Training:** The method proposed in Section 5, combining interactive self-play with supervised learning, is the paper's most significant contribution. This technique provides an elegant solution to the grounding problem, encouraging the agents to align their emergent protocol with human-conventional labels without sacrificing the functional learning benefits of interaction. The demonstration that this grounding generalizes—enabling humans to interpret the agents' novel, "metonymic" word usage for unseen objects—is a powerful and exciting result.
*   **Rigorous and Multi-faceted Evaluation:** The authors go far beyond reporting simple task accuracy. The use of cluster purity to measure semantic coherence, SVD to analyze vocabulary richness, and, most importantly, a direct human evaluation of interpretability demonstrates a high degree of scientific rigor. This final human validation experiment is critical for a paper claiming to make progress on human-AI communication and adds significant weight to its conclusions.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Analysis of Hyperparameters:** The paper states that hyperparameters (e.g., Gibbs temperature `τ`, embedding dimensionality) were set without tuning. While the results are strong as is, the temperature parameter `τ` is particularly critical as it controls the hardness of the communication bottleneck. A brief discussion or ablation study on how `τ` affects the learning dynamics and the structure of the resulting language (e.g., comparing a low-`τ` deterministic protocol with a high-`τ` stochastic one) would add valuable insight.
*   **Scalability of the Hybrid Approach:** The supervised grounding experiment uses a vocabulary of 100 labels. It would be interesting to discuss the potential scalability of this approach. As the number of supervised labels grows to thousands, is there a risk that the supervised signal dominates and the interactive component becomes less important? Understanding the balance between these two learning signals is an important direction for future work.
*   **Ablation on Shared Weights:** A key implementation detail in the hybrid training is that the game-embedding layer is shared between the game and the supervised task. This is an intuitive choice, but an ablation study where these layers are kept separate would provide even stronger evidence that this weight-sharing is the specific mechanism responsible for transferring the grounding from the supervised task to the emergent protocol.

### Questions for the Authors

Your responses to these questions would help clarify some details of the work and strengthen my confidence in the assessment.

1.  **Hybrid Training Schedule:** Could you clarify the implementation of the "equiprobable" switching between the game and the supervised task in Section 5? Was this switch made on a per-batch basis (i.e., a batch was either for the game or for supervision) or a different schedule? How sensitive do you believe the results are to the relative frequency of these two task types during training?
2.  **Human Evaluation Instructions:** The human evaluation is a highlight of the paper. Could you provide more detail on the instructions given to the CrowdFlower participants? For instance, were they given any context or priming that the "words" might be used in non-literal or metaphorical ways, or were they simply asked to pick the image most related to the word?
3.  **"Object-Level Reference" Setup:** To ensure I understand the innovative setup in Section 4.1 correctly: does the sender see an image pair like (chihuahua, truck) while the receiver simultaneously sees a different pair representing the same classes, such as (Boston terrier, fire engine)? A confirmation of this mechanism would be helpful.
4.  **Living-vs-Non-Living Distinction:** The observation that the agnostic sender develops a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is fascinating and resonates with findings in cognitive science. Was this conclusion based on a qualitative inspection of which objects were assigned to each of the two primary symbols? Could you provide a few examples to illustrate this split?
