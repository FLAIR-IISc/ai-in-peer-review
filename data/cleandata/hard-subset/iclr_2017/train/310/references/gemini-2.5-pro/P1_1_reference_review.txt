### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling research program aimed at developing communication protocols between AI agents through interactive, cooperative games. The core idea is to move beyond supervised learning on static conversational datasets and instead study how language can emerge functionally, as a tool for coordination. The authors design a simple "referential game" where a sender agent must communicate to a receiver which of two images is the target. The paper makes several key contributions:

1.  It demonstrates that simple neural agents, starting *tabula rasa*, can successfully develop an effective communication protocol to solve this task.
2.  It investigates how properties of the environment and agent architecture influence the emergent language. It shows that an inductive bias in the sender's architecture (the "informed" sender) leads to a richer, more diverse vocabulary, and that manipulating "common knowledge" (by showing agents different instances of the same object class) encourages the development of more abstract, conceptual representations.
3.  Most significantly, it proposes and validates a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, the agents develop a protocol that is not only effective for coordination but also largely interpretable by humans, as validated by a crowdsourcing experiment. The paper shows this interpretability generalizes to novel images and can lead to human-like linguistic phenomena such as metonymy.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong, well-executed, and thought-provoking paper that addresses a fundamental question in AI: how to build agents that can learn to communicate functionally and cooperatively. My decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper compellingly argues for the limitations of purely supervised approaches to language and frames the problem of emergent communication in a way that is both principled (drawing from linguistics and game theory) and practical (aiming for AI that can coordinate with humans). This work represents a significant step towards functional, grounded language learning.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The paper presents a logical and clear progression of experiments that convincingly support its claims. The final experiment, which combines interactive self-play with supervised learning to ground the emergent language, is particularly novel and impactful. The successful human evaluation provides powerful evidence that the authors' approach is a viable path toward creating AI communication systems that are aligned with human understanding.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's strengths are numerous. The introduction clearly motivates the work and situates it within the relevant literature, distinguishing its goals and methods from related research in multi-agent RL, human-in-the-loop systems, and game theory.

The experimental design is excellent. The authors start by establishing a baseline result—that agents can learn to communicate at all. They then systematically investigate factors that shape this communication. The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures provides insight into the role of inductive biases. The "object-level reference" experiment (Section 4.1) is a clever use of game-theoretic concepts like common knowledge to push the agents towards more abstract representations, and the results (increased purity) nicely support the hypothesis.

The main contribution, presented in Section 5, is the grounding of the emergent language. This is a crucial step for making emergent communication useful for human-AI interaction. The idea to combine interactive learning (for function) with supervised learning (for grounding), inspired by AlphaGo, is elegant and proves to be highly effective. The results are impressive: not only does purity dramatically increase, but the agents use human-provided labels when appropriate. The subsequent human validation study on a new dataset is the paper's strongest element. Showing that humans can interpret the agent's novel word usage 68% of the time, and identifying emergent metonymy ("dolphin" for "sea"), is a fascinating and powerful result that suggests the emergent protocol captures semantics in a way that aligns with human cognition.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could help further strengthen it:

*   **Training Stability and Reproducibility:** The REINFORCE algorithm is known for its high variance. Reporting results averaged over multiple runs with different random seeds would add confidence in the stability of the findings (e.g., convergence speed, final performance, and number of symbols used).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The temperature parameter for the Gibbs sampling is set to `τ=10`, which seems unusually high and would make the sampling distribution nearly uniform. This might hinder convergence. Could the authors clarify if this is correct and comment on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter? A brief discussion on the choice of other hyperparameters (e.g., embedding size) would also be beneficial.
*   **"Informed" Sender Architecture:** The paper states that the informed sender has an inductive bias towards dimension-by-dimension comparison. While the results show this is effective, a more detailed intuition or analysis of *why* this specific bias encourages a richer vocabulary (as opposed to simply being a more powerful architecture for the task) would be welcome.
*   **SVD Analysis:** The SVD analysis to argue against high synonymy is a good idea, but the conclusion is presented very briefly. Relating the "50 dimensions" to the vocabulary size (e.g., 100) and the number of concepts (463) would provide more context for interpreting this result.

### Questions for the Authors

I have a few questions that could help clarify some aspects of the work:

1.  In the human evaluation study (Section 5), what specific instructions were given to the CrowdFlower participants? The framing of the task (e.g., "Which picture is the word referring to?" vs. "Which picture is most related to the word?") could significantly influence how participants approach the task and interpret the results.
2.  Could you provide more detail on the REINFORCE implementation? Specifically, was a baseline (e.g., a running average of rewards) used to reduce variance in the policy gradient updates?
3.  The paper focuses on a simple, one-shot referential game. How do you envision these findings and the proposed grounding methodology extending to more complex communication scenarios, such as multi-turn dialogues or tasks requiring compositional language? Does the emergence of metonymy suggest a foundation upon which more complex semantics could be built?
