### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a compelling research program aimed at studying the emergence of communication between AI agents. Moving beyond the standard supervised learning paradigm, which treats language as a static dataset to be imitated, the authors propose a framework based on interactive, cooperative referential games. In this setup, two "tabula rasa" agents—a sender and a receiver—must develop a shared communication protocol from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a pair.

The paper makes several significant contributions:
*   It demonstrates that simple neural agents can successfully learn to communicate and coordinate in this setting, developing a vocabulary to refer to a large set of real-world images.
*   It thoughtfully investigates the properties of the emergent language, showing that the agents' symbols correspond to high-level conceptual properties of the images (as measured by cluster purity) rather than just low-level visual features.
*   It introduces a clever environmental manipulation—removing low-level common knowledge by showing agents different instances of the same object class—to nudge the emergent language toward more abstract representations.
*   Most notably, it proposes a novel hybrid approach, inspired by AlphaGo, that combines interactive self-play with a supervised image-labeling task. This technique successfully grounds the agents' emergent language in human-understandable terms and, crucially, demonstrates that this grounding generalizes. A human study shows that people can interpret the agent's messages even for objects the agent was never explicitly taught the names for, suggesting the emergence of metonymic and other semantically flexible word uses characteristic of human language.

**2. Recommendation and Justification**

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent and thought-provoking paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Conceptual Novelty:** The paper champions a bold and fundamentally different approach to language in AI. By framing language learning as an emergent phenomenon from interactive, goal-driven communication, it tackles the functional aspect of language that is entirely missed by passive, supervised methods. This work represents a significant new direction for the field.
2.  **Compelling Grounding Method:** The hybrid approach of mixing self-play with supervised learning is an elegant and effective solution to the grounding problem. The final experiment, where humans successfully interpret the agent's generalized language, is a powerful proof of concept and provides the most exciting result in the paper, suggesting a viable path towards building agents that can develop communication protocols aligned with human intuition.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

This work stands out for its ambition and creativity. The central premise—that interaction, not just observation, is key to learning language—is articulated with admirable clarity and is deeply resonant. While the field has seen a proliferation of papers that achieve incremental gains on existing benchmarks using ever-larger models and datasets, this paper dares to ask a more fundamental question about the nature of communication and how it can arise in artificial agents.

The experimental progression is logical and compelling. The authors first establish that their agents can solve the basic coordination task. They then move to the more interesting questions about the *nature* of the emergent code. The use of cluster purity is a reasonable first step, but the idea to manipulate "common knowledge" to force more abstract communication is particularly insightful. This demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of the game-theoretic underpinnings of communication.

The capstone experiment on grounding is the paper's strongest point. The finding that supervised training on one set of objects can bootstrap a human-interpretable communication system that generalizes to *other* objects is a genuinely exciting result. The qualitative examples of "metonymic" links (e.g., using 'dolphin' to refer to the sea) are fascinating and suggest the agents are not merely mapping symbols to rigid categories, but are developing a more flexible, context-dependent semantic system. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-potential research that this conference should champion.

I am not overly concerned with the simplicity of the agent architectures or the fact that the validation is not exhaustive. For a paper establishing a new paradigm, these are not weaknesses but strengths. The minimalist setup allows the core ideas to be presented with clarity and makes the results all the more impressive. The work successfully lays the foundation and provides a clear roadmap for future research to build upon with more complex agents, environments, and linguistic phenomena.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback for the authors and do not detract from my strong recommendation to accept.

*   The analysis of the "informed sender" architecture's inductive bias is interesting. It would be beneficial to expand on why this dimension-by-dimension comparison is helpful. Is it that it encourages compositional representations? A deeper discussion here could provide more insight into designing agent architectures for communication.
*   The SVD analysis to test for synonymy was a nice touch. This could be explored further. For example, one could analyze the cosine similarity between the usage vectors of different symbols to more directly quantify the degree of redundancy or synonymy in the emergent vocabulary.
*   The paper mentions that the agnostic sender sometimes develops a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. This is a fascinating emergent property that aligns with findings in cognitive science. If this result is robust, it deserves more prominence and analysis in the paper, as it provides even stronger evidence that the emergent semantics are "natural."

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you elaborate on the failure modes in the human evaluation study? In the 32% of cases where humans could not guess the correct referent, were there any patterns? For instance, were the semantic leaps made by the agent simply too abstract for the human participants to follow?
*   The training combines the referential game and the supervised task with equal probability. Have you explored how this mixing ratio affects the outcome? One could imagine a curriculum where agents first develop their own protocol and are only later exposed to supervised labels, or vice-versa.
*   How sensitive are the results to the temperature parameter in the Gibbs distributions? A high temperature encourages exploration, which seems crucial for developing a rich vocabulary early on, but might prevent convergence to a stable protocol later. Do you anneal this parameter, and if not, could doing so be beneficial?
