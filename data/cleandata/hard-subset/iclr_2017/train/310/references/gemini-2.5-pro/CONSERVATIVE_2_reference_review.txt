### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—tasked with a cooperative referential game. The agents, starting as blank slates, must develop a communication protocol to allow the receiver to identify a target image from a pair. The paper's primary contributions are threefold: 1) It demonstrates that agents can successfully learn to coordinate and communicate using a discrete vocabulary learned via reinforcement learning. 2) It explores how architectural choices (an "agnostic" vs. an "informed" sender) and game mechanics (object-level vs. instance-level reference) can influence the semantic properties of the emergent language, measured by alignment with human-defined conceptual categories. 3) It proposes a hybrid training scheme, combining the interactive game with a standard supervised image labeling task, to ground the emergent language in human-understandable terms. This grounding is validated through a crowdsourcing experiment where human participants successfully interpret the agent's messages a significant portion of the time.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper motivates its approach as an alternative to supervised methods for training conversational agents. However, it fails to provide any empirical comparison against relevant supervised or even simpler rule-based baselines. Without this context, it is impossible to judge whether the complexity of the proposed multi-agent RL framework is justified or if the emergent protocol offers any practical advantage over established techniques.
2.  **Weak Evidence for "Natural" Language Emergence:** The claims that the emergent language captures "general conceptual properties" or is "semantically natural" are supported by evidence that is statistically significant but weak in absolute terms. The primary metric, purity, remains low (e.g., 26% in the best non-supervised case), and qualitative evidence from t-SNE plots is subjective and inconclusive. The paper overstates the strength of these findings.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The core premise of the paper—studying emergent language in a game-theoretic setting—is interesting and connects to a rich history of work in AI, cognitive science, and linguistics. The experiments are well-described, and the final human-in-the-loop evaluation is a commendable step towards assessing the practical goal of human-AI communication. However, the work in its current form feels more like a preliminary exploration than a rigorous scientific contribution ready for publication at ICLR.

Regarding the lack of baselines, the paper positions itself as a departure from "canned" conversations and supervised learning. This makes a direct comparison all the more critical. A simple baseline could involve training a standard classifier to predict the McRae category of the target image and using that as the "symbol." How does the success rate and semantic purity of such a system compare? Another baseline could be a supervised model trained on a small number of (image pair, symbol) examples to see how sample-efficient the interactive approach truly is. The paper also cites related multi-agent communication work (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) but does not compare its results, making it difficult to assess the novelty or improvement of the specific setup used here. Without these comparisons, the contribution remains isolated and its practical significance is unclear.

The evidence supporting the semantic quality of the emergent language is not compelling. While purity scores are "significantly above chance," a purity of 26% (Table 1, informed sender) means that for 74% of the objects, the most frequently used symbol does not correspond to the majority symbol for that object's ground-truth category. This suggests a very noisy and weakly structured protocol. The claim that the agnostic sender learns a "living-vs-non-living" distinction is an intriguing but post-hoc qualitative observation that requires more systematic validation. While the object-level reference game (Section 4.1) does increase purity, the improvement is marginal (from 26% to 29%). These results are interesting starting points but do not yet support strong claims about the emergence of a human-like conceptual system.

The final experiment grounding the language with supervision is the most promising part of the paper. The 68% human accuracy is notable. However, the analysis remains superficial. The authors invoke concepts like "metonymy" to explain successful generalization (e.g., 'dolphin' for 'sea'), but this is an interpretation applied after the fact. The paper would be stronger if it could formalize this phenomenon or analyze the properties of the learned embedding space that enable such generalizations. As it stands, the result is an interesting data point without a deep methodological insight.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Analysis of the "Informed Sender":** The paper proposes that the "informed sender" architecture has a useful inductive bias, and the results support this. However, the analysis could go deeper. What kind of filters are learned in the convolutional layer? Do they correspond to specific visual attributes that differentiate objects? A more thorough analysis of the internal workings of this architecture would strengthen the paper's contribution.
*   **Task Difficulty:** The task always involves discriminating between two images from different high-level categories. A more challenging and realistic scenario would involve fine-grained discrimination (e.g., target 'Siberian Husky', distractor 'German Shepherd'). How would the agents perform in this setting? It is likely that the emergent protocols reported here would fail, requiring a more compositional or attribute-based language.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors state that key hyperparameters like temperature were set without tuning. While this avoids overfitting to the test set, it also leaves open the question of how sensitive the results (particularly vocabulary usage and purity) are to these settings. A brief sensitivity analysis would improve the paper's rigor.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide results for a simple supervised baseline? For example, one where the sender is a classifier trained to predict the McRae category for the target image. This would provide a crucial point of comparison for the communication success and semantic purity of your emergent protocol.
2.  The human evaluation in Section 5 is based on the sender using ImageNet labels as symbols. Have you considered a human evaluation for the protocol that emerged *without* supervision (Section 4)? This would be a more direct test of whether the *tabula rasa* emergent language is in any way human-interpretable.
3.  How does the system's performance and the nature of the emergent language change as the number of distractor images increases? The current two-image setup is a significant simplification of real-world reference.
