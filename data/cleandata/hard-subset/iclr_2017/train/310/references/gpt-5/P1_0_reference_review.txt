Summary and contributions
- The paper studies how discrete communication can emerge between neural agents trained tabula rasa to solve referential games on real images. Two simple feed-forward agents (sender/receiver) are trained with REINFORCE to achieve coordination by exchanging a single symbol from a fixed vocabulary.
- It compares two sender architectures: an “agnostic” MLP and an “informed” sender with a dimension-by-dimension inductive bias (1-D convolutions across paired image embeddings). It shows both learn to communicate reliably, with the informed sender converging faster and using a richer symbol inventory.
- It analyzes what the symbols come to mean. Using cluster purity vs. human semantic categories (McRae) and visualizations (t-SNE), it argues that symbols align with higher-level semantics rather than low-level visual cues; even with two symbols, a living vs. non-living split often emerges.
- It proposes an object-level variant that removes low-level common knowledge by showing different exemplars of the same class to sender and receiver, which increases semantic purity.
- It explores grounding by interleaving self-play with supervised object naming (on a subset of ImageNet labels), which increases symbol purity and makes symbols directly interpretable. A human evaluation on ReferIt-like image pairs shows that humans can choose the intended referent given the sender’s symbol-word 68% of the time, significantly above chance.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Well-motivated, clearly framed study showing that environment design nudges emergent communication toward human-aligned semantics; (2) Inclusion of a human study and a simple but effective grounding mechanism that improves interpretability, making the work relevant beyond purely multi-agent settings.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper directly targets when and how language-like codes arise that are useful for coordination and are interpretable to humans—an important question for multi-agent communication and human-AI interaction. The setup is well-situated in the literature (signaling games, cheap talk, recent neural multi-agent communication), and the “object-level reference” tweak is an insightful application of common knowledge considerations to force abstraction.
- Methodology: The experimental design is simple and transparent. Discrete symbols are sampled (necessitating RL), and architectures are deliberately minimal. Using precomputed VGG features isolates the communication dynamics and reduces confounds.
- Results supporting claims:
  - Coordination: Near-perfect test accuracy across settings indicates the basic communication problem is solved.
  - Semantics emerge: Cluster purity above chance and the observation of broad semantic splits (e.g., living vs. non-living) support the claim that symbols align with conceptual properties. The SVD analysis suggests vocabulary is not collapsing to a trivial 1–2D code. The object-level variant further increases semantic alignment as predicted by the common knowledge argument.
  - Grounding and human interpretability: Interleaving supervised naming increases symbol usage and purity substantially and encourages use of conventional labels. Crucially, the human study shows generalization: even when images do not contain supervised categories, humans can often infer the sender’s intended referent from the word (68% vs. 50% chance), and qualitative examples show plausible metonymy.
- Novelty and potential impact: While emergent communication in referential games has antecedents, the paper’s focus on (i) architectural bias effects, (ii) environment design to promote abstraction, and (iii) practical grounding with a human study makes it a valuable, coherent contribution likely to influence follow-up work on interpretable multi-agent communication.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Rigor and reporting:
  - Provide numerical values (means, stds) and statistical tests for all purity gains, vocabulary usage, and convergence speeds; report across multiple random seeds to assess variance.
  - Clarify the SVD analysis: matrix construction, cross-validated rank selection procedure, and the quantitative rank chosen vs. baselines.
  - Include ablations on vocabulary size, temperature, and entropy regularization; report symbol entropy and mutual information between symbols and object classes.
  - Report training details affecting REINFORCE variance (reward baselines, variance reduction, temperature annealing, entropy bonuses). State whether any baseline was used.
- Baselines and controls:
  - Compare to a continuous-communication baseline (no discretization) and to differentiable relaxations (e.g., Gumbel-softmax) to assess effects on semantics and convergence.
  - Evaluate with raw pixels or earlier CNN layers to test the claim that high-level semantics drive symbol meanings (controlling for pretraining biases).
  - Add stronger semantics metrics: topographic similarity between input and message spaces, ARI/NMI for clustering, and RSA between symbol distributions and visual embeddings.
- Generalization:
  - Test zero-shot generalization to unseen object categories and to partner-swapping (train sender with receiver A, test with receiver B).
  - Increase task difficulty (more distractors, sequences of symbols, back-and-forth) and test whether trends persist.
- Human study:
  - Report per-word difficulty, inter-annotator agreement, and a stronger baseline (e.g., frequency-biased guesser). Consider a condition where symbols are anonymized to decouple from English labels to test whether learned semantics alone suffice.
- Clarity:
  - The “informed sender” architecture would benefit from a clearer diagram and mathematical description; currently the convolutional setup may be hard to reproduce.
  - Specify whether VGG features were normalized and whether sm vs. fc led to different behaviors; summarize those differences quantitatively.

Questions for the authors
- What reward baseline or variance reduction techniques (if any) were used with REINFORCE? Did you observe high variance or instability across seeds?
- How sensitive are results to the temperature parameter and to symbol sampling versus argmax at training/test time?
- How many random seeds were run for each configuration? Please report variability in coordination accuracy, purity, and number of symbols used.
- Can you quantify symbol redundancy/synonymy (e.g., by measuring mutual information or clustering symbols by usage profiles)?
- In the object-level reference setting, do agents still achieve high coordination when distractors are from the same superordinate category (hard negatives)? How does purity change?
- How does the approach generalize to unseen categories (zero-shot)? Does supervised grounding help more in that regime?
- In the human evaluation, did you test a control where the symbol is mapped to a random English word to rule out label priors? How do results change if the word is hidden and humans see only anonymized symbols plus a small lexicon-to-image legend?
- Could you release code and the human study materials to facilitate replication?
