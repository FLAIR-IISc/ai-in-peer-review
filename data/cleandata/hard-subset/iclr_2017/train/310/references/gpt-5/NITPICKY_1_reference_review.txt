1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in simple two-agent referential games over natural images. Two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed) and a receiver are trained from scratch via REINFORCE, communicating a single discrete symbol to identify a target image. The authors examine (i) whether agents learn to coordinate, (ii) how environment design influences emergent “semantics,” and (iii) how to ground symbols in human language by mixing self-play with supervised object naming. They report: near-perfect coordination; that an “informed” sender converges faster and uses more symbols; that symbol usage correlates with object categories (cluster purity above chance) and visual neighborhoods (t-SNE); that removing shared low-level visual information (showing different images of the same class to sender and receiver) nudges symbols toward class-level semantics; and that adding supervised object naming yields more interpretable symbols which humans can often use successfully in a two-choice referential test (68% accuracy on ReferIt-derived pairs). Overall, the work argues that simple environment and architectural choices shape the emergence and interpretability of communication, and that light supervision can ground agents’ symbols for human use.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient empirical rigor and reproducibility: many core claims lack comprehensive quantitative support, ablations, and statistical treatment; several critical implementation details and baselines are missing.
- Confounded evidence for “natural” semantics and human interpretability: reliance on pretrained VGG features and added supervised labels makes it unclear whether observed structure stems from emergent communication or inherited representations/supervision.

3) Supporting arguments
Rigor and completeness
- Metrics: The paper frequently references Table 1/2, spectra, purity scores, and learning curves without reporting precise values, confidence intervals, or variability across runs. Statements like “significantly above chance” are common, but except for the final human study, no formal statistical tests or effect sizes are given.
- Reproducibility: Key details are missing (optimizer, learning rates, gradient clipping, REINFORCE variance reduction/baseline, entropy regularization, temperature annealing schedule, random seeds, number of runs). Only a single batch size/dimensionality/temperature are reported as “without tuning,” offering no sensitivity analysis.
- Baselines: There are no comparisons to trivial/heuristic policies (e.g., always pick left, majority-class symbols), to non-communicating receivers, or to models trained with differentiable relaxations (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) to assess stability/variance. No tests of partner swap (train sender A with receiver A, evaluate with unseen receiver B) which is central to “portability to new communication partners.”
- Confounds: Use of VGG fc/softmax features likely encodes category structure a priori; the observed “semantic” clustering/purity may simply reflect the image representation rather than emergent language. There is no ablation with earlier CNN layers, random features, or raw pixels. Likewise, the supervised grounding experiment blurs the line between emergent semantics and direct label inheritance.

Methodology and correctness
- REINFORCE details: With discrete symbol sampling and τ=10, variance can be high; there is no mention of a baseline or variance reduction. Sampling at both sender and receiver further increases variance; rationale for high τ is not discussed.
- Architecture: The “informed” sender’s 1-D conv across embedding dimensions is interesting, but justification and comparison to simple bilinear or attention-based interactions are missing. The claim that the agnostic architecture “nests” the informed one is asserted but not demonstrated empirically.
- Training/test protocol: The text inconsistently states “10k training games,” “50k iterations,” and “1k rounds following the 10k training games,” and separately “compile a set of 10k games” at test. The exact splits and evaluation procedures are unclear.

Evaluation of semantics and interpretability
- Purity and SVD: Purity improvements and “50-dimensional” SVD claims are not quantified. Cluster purity depends on the number of clusters; how many effective symbols are used per setting? Are results robust across seeds? How does purity compare to directly clustering the VGG features?
- “Living vs non-living”: The two-symbol emergent split is anecdotal; quantitative confirmation and variance across runs are absent.
- Human study: The 68% result is promising, but design choices introduce biases: subjects see ImageNet words (some rare/technical) without screening for familiarity; no report of per-word accuracy distribution, word frequency effects, or control for visual salience. The pairs are selected only from correct agent interactions, potentially inflating performance. Chance is 50%; stronger baselines (e.g., choose the image closer to CLIP/Text embedding of the word) would contextualize the difficulty.

Positioning and novelty
- Prior work (Sukhbaatar et al.; Foerster et al.) already demonstrates emergent communication. The novelty claim here is shaping environments toward human-like semantics and partial grounding. However, without stronger ablations disentangling emergent structure from pretrained features/supervision, the incremental contribution is limited.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous reference formatting errors, incomplete entries, and typos (e.g., “Brandenburger and Dekel,? \Q1998\E”; inconsistent venues and punctuation). Terminology such as “Gibbs distribution” for softmax is non-standard in this context. Figures/tables are referenced but not included or quantified in the text.

4) Suggestions to improve (not all affect decision)
- Report full quantitative results with mean±std over multiple seeds; add confidence intervals and significance tests for purity, spectrum ranks, symbol usage entropy, and learning curves. Release code.
- Add baselines: no-communication, random-symbol sender, supervised-only labeler, Gumbel-Softmax relaxation, and CLIP-based word-to-image baselines for the human interpretability task.
- Disentangle confounds: compare feature levels (conv3/conv4/fc), random features, and fine-tuning vs frozen VGG. Show how purity varies with representation choice.
- Evaluate portability: partner swap tests; zero-shot receiver replacement; human-in-the-loop games beyond two-choice; measure calibration and robustness.
- Provide ablations: temperature schedule, entropy regularization, symbol vocabulary size, receiver capacity, supervision proportion, number of supervised labels, and overlap vs disjoint supervised classes.
- Clarify the “common knowledge” experiment: ensure sender/receiver images are different instances; test with explicit class-level supervision removed to verify reliance on higher-level cues.
- Improve human study: pre-register protocol, balance words, control for word familiarity, report per-word stats, add stronger baselines and adversarial pairs.
- Fix references, terminology, and ensure all figures/tables and numerical values are present.

Questions for the authors
- What optimizer, learning rate, baselines/variance reduction, and temperature schedules were used? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- How many random seeds were run? What is the variance in coordination accuracy, purity, and symbol usage?
- What are the exact purity numbers (and chance levels) for all settings, with confidence intervals? How does purity compare to clustering in VGG feature space alone?
- In the supervised grounding setup, are supervised classes disjoint from the game’s image concepts during play? If not, how much of the effect is direct label reuse?
- Do partner swap experiments preserve communication? Can a receiver trained from scratch with a fixed sender acquire the code quickly?
- How does performance change with different CNN feature layers, or with random features? Can emergent semantic structure arise without pretrained visual semantics?
- In the human study, what is the per-word accuracy distribution, and how does word familiarity influence results? What are results on unfiltered pairs (not only those where the agent succeeded)?
- Why choose τ=10 and sampling at both sender and receiver? Did you try entropy regularization or Gumbel-Softmax?
