Summary and contributions
- Problem: The paper studies how to design multi-agent environments that induce the emergence of communication protocols that are both effective for coordination and aligned with human-interpretable concepts, focusing on referential games over natural images.
- Approach: Two neural agents (sender/receiver) play a Lewis-style signaling game using single-symbol messages from a discrete vocabulary, trained via REINFORCE on success/failure only. Inputs are real images represented by VGG features. The paper contrasts two sender architectures (agnostic MLP vs “informed” sender with a dimension-wise comparison bias), manipulates common knowledge (same vs different image instances), and mixes self-play with a supervised object-naming task to ground symbols.
- Claims supported: (i) agents reliably learn to communicate from scratch; (ii) environment/architecture choices influence the semantics and interpretability of the emergent code; (iii) mixing self-play with supervised naming substantially increases human interpretability and category-level organization; and (iv) partial human-agent communication is possible out-of-domain.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Significance and novelty: Clear, well-motivated step toward grounded, human-aligned emergent communication using natural image inputs and a discrete channel. The “object-level reference” manipulation and the mixed supervised/self-play grounding are insightful and practically relevant.
- Evidence: Strong empirical results show robust coordination, semantic organization (above-chance cluster purity; living/non-living split), and human interpretability (68% accuracy on out-of-domain ReferIt pairs) with clear ablations (architecture, vocabulary, common knowledge).

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well-situated in multi-agent RL, emergent communication, linguistics/game theory, and contrasts with supervised-only and Wizard-of-Oz approaches. The focus on designing environments to steer interpretability is timely and well motivated.
- Methodological soundness: Training uses standard REINFORCE with discrete symbol sampling, realistic image embeddings, and simple yet revealing architectural biases. The analysis spans symbol usage statistics, SVD of usage matrices, cluster purity against established semantic categories, and t-SNE visualizations.
- Empirical support:
  - Coordination: Near-perfect success across settings; informed sender learns faster and uses more of the vocabulary.
  - Semantics: Above-chance cluster purity across all settings, higher for informed sender; emergence of a living-vs-non-living split even with two symbols is compelling.
  - Environment design: Removing low-level common knowledge (different instances per class) increases purity and yields clearer category-level structure.
  - Grounding: Joint supervised naming dramatically increases symbol usage (88/100) and purity (70%). In cases with supervised labels present, the sender uses the exact conventional label 47% of the time (vs 1% chance). Out-of-domain human evaluation shows 68% accuracy, with plausible metonymic extensions (e.g., “dolphin” for sea), suggesting pragmatically useful flexibility.
- Clarity: The paper is clear and accessible, with appropriate references and principled experimental design.

Areas for improvement (not blockers)
- Rigor and controls:
  - Report variance/CIs over multiple random seeds for success rates, purity, and human accuracies.
  - Provide stronger baselines for human study (e.g., random label, frequency-matched random label, swapped symbol mapping) and inter-annotator agreement.
  - Clarify statistical tests/intervals for purity improvements and SVD “effective dimensionality.”
- Analysis depth:
  - Context-dependence: The majority-symbol per object ignores pragmatic dependence on distractors. Consider mutual information between symbol and (target, distractor), RSA/topographic similarity between visual and symbol spaces, and communicative success conditioned on within- vs across-category distractors.
  - Cross-play: Test whether independently trained agents can communicate (co-adaptation vs conventionalization), a key desideratum for portability to new partners.
  - Robustness: Analyze sensitivity to vocabulary size, temperature, embedding dim, and different visual features (fc vs sm). Report symbol entropy and dispersion.
- Design choices:
  - The sender is told the target via input ordering; confirm no positional leakage to the receiver and that success is not due to trivial cues in VGG features.
  - Justify the high temperature choice and sampling strategy; discuss alternatives like Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through estimators and whether they change emergent codes.
  - Explore alternative schedules/ratios for supervised vs game updates; does performance/interpretablity trade off with more/less supervision?
- Scope:
  - The single-symbol channel is a strong bottleneck; a brief experiment with multi-symbol sequences or multi-round interaction would strengthen generality claims.
  - Provide code and data splits for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. Cross-play/generalization: How well do independently trained senders/receivers communicate with each other (without fine-tuning)? Does supervised grounding improve cross-play?
2. Context effects: How often does an object map to different symbols depending on the distractor? Can you quantify pragmatic flexibility (e.g., symbol divergence within-object across distractor types)?
3. Human study controls: What is subject accuracy if the label is replaced by a random supervised word or a frequency-matched distractor word? What is inter-annotator agreement?
4. Statistical reporting: Could you provide confidence intervals over multiple runs for purity, success, and symbol usage? How exactly is “cross-validated SVD suggests 50 dimensions” computed?
5. Temperature/sampling: How sensitive are results to the Gibbs temperature τ? Did you try Gumbel-Softmax or straight-through estimators; do they alter symbol usage or interpretability?
6. Supervision ratio: How does varying the supervised/self-play mix affect symbol purity, usage, and human interpretability? Does grounding only the receiver (or only the sender) help?
7. Within-category distractors: What is success and purity when distractor is from the same category as the target vs a different category? Does the code sharpen category boundaries?
8. Robustness to representation: Do results hold with different visual embeddings (e.g., ResNet, CLIP), or with grayscale/blur to suppress low-level cues?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and carefully executed paper that advances emergent communication toward human-aligned semantics using realistic inputs and offers practical insights on environment design and grounding.
