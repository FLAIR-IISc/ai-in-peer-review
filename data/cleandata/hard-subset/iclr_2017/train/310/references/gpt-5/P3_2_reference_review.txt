1) Summary and contributions
This paper studies emergent communication in multi-agent referential games using real images. Two agents (sender/receiver) start tabula rasa, observe pairs of ImageNet-derived representations, and must communicate a single discrete symbol for successful coordination. The authors (i) show convergence to near-perfect coordination with simple feed-forward networks trained with REINFORCE; (ii) compare two sender architectures (agnostic vs. an “informed” convolutional sender) and find more efficient convergence and richer symbol usage with the informed sender; (iii) analyze emergent semantics via category purity and visualization, including a variant that removes low-level common knowledge by showing different exemplars to sender and receiver to encourage class-level reference; and (iv) ground the emergent code by mixing self-play with supervised object naming, demonstrating higher purity and partial human interpretability on a ReferIt-derived human evaluation (68% accuracy in a 2AFC setup), with interesting “metonymic” usage patterns.

Overall, the paper proposes a clean framework, uses real-world visual features, and probes factors that nudge agents toward more human-aligned semantics. The joint supervised/self-play grounding is especially appealing.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The problem (emergent communication with real inputs and human-aligned interpretability) is timely and important. The approach is well-motivated and situated in the literature (cheap talk, multi-agent RL, emergent communication).
- The empirical findings are novel and informative: architectural bias matters; removing low-level common knowledge pushes higher-level semantics; combining supervised grounding with self-play improves interpretability and transfers to new images and human listeners.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper clearly positions referential games as a functional alternative to purely supervised language learning, referencing cheap talk, language evolution, and recent deep multi-agent communication work. The focus on environments that steer agents toward human-interpretable codes is well justified.
- Methodology: The game is well-defined, with discrete messages enforced via sampling from a temperature-scaled softmax and trained with REINFORCE. The two sender architectures differ by an inductive bias to compare images dimension-wise; this is a plausible, testable hypothesis borne out empirically.
- Empirical support: Communication success is robust. Analyses beyond accuracy (symbol usage distributions, SVD of usage matrices, category purity against McRae labels, t-SNE color-coding) provide converging evidence that the emergent code captures higher-level properties, especially under object-level reference. The supervised grounding experiment is a strong point: it significantly boosts purity, encourages use of conventional names, and—crucially—generalizes to a different dataset and to human listeners, with appropriate mixed-effects analysis.
- Scope: While the setup is simple (single-symbol messages, two images), the paper carefully tests design levers (architecture, representation layer, vocabulary size, object-level constraint, supervised grounding) and draws measured conclusions about moving toward human-aligned semantics.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all critical to the decision)
- Semantic analysis rigor:
  - Control for context: symbol choice depends on both target and distractor. Report mutual information between symbol and target class conditioned on distractor class; or measure stability of symbol assignment across varying distractors (context-invariance).
  - Complement purity with topographic similarity (correlation between distances in image/semantic space and distances in message space) or RSA to quantify global alignment.
  - Provide significance tests for purity above chance (e.g., permutation test distributions and CIs).
- Emergence vs. pretrained semantics: Since VGG fc/softmax features encode category-level information learned from supervised ImageNet, part of the “semanticity” might be inherited from features rather than the communication pressure. An ablation with lower-level conv features or with self-supervised features would clarify.
- Variance reduction and training details: REINFORCE high variance is a concern. Did you use a baseline/critic? Report learning curves, variance over seeds, and sample efficiency. Also justify the temperature τ=10 and explore sensitivity (including entropy regularization).
- Degeneracy and synonymy: The SVD analysis is suggestive. Also report entropy of the message distribution, effective vocabulary size, and Zipf-like behavior. Analyze whether multiple symbols are functionally synonymous by perturbing the receiver (e.g., symbol replacement tests).
- Generalization/ad-hoc team play: Test cross-play: train multiple independent sender/receiver pairs and evaluate zero-shot compatibility (important for “portable to new partners”). Also test few-shot partner adaptation.
- Human evaluation: The 2AFC on ReferIt is nice; include stronger controls (e.g., random words, shuffled-message baseline) and report per-word/per-image difficulty analyses. Consider a free-response setup or multiple-choice with semantically close foils to assess finer-grained interpretability.
- Claims: Be cautious in framing “natural language” emergence—current messages are single symbols without compositional structure. The path toward syntax/compositionality (multi-symbol sequences, back-and-forth interaction) could be outlined more concretely.

5) Questions for the authors
- Context dependence: How stable are symbol assignments across different distractors for the same target class? Can you quantify context-invariance and message consistency?
- Baselines and variance: Did you use a learned/value baseline for REINFORCE? How many seeds were run, and what is variance in convergence speed and final purity?
- Temperature and discretization: How sensitive are results to τ and to the sampling vs. argmax choice at train/test time? Did you try Gumbel-Softmax or straight-through estimators?
- Feature dependence: What happens if you use earlier conv layers or self-supervised features (e.g., to reduce category leakage)? Does purity still rise above chance?
- Cross-play: Do independently trained sender/receiver pairs understand each other zero-shot? If not, does supervised grounding improve compatibility?
- Human study controls: What is human performance with random or mismatched words? How often is the sender’s chosen word present in the ReferIt expressions for that pair when communication succeeds vs. fails?
- Object-level game: Can you report the magnitude of the purity increase and statistical significance when moving to object-level reference? How much of the gain is due to removing low-level common knowledge vs. inducing stronger class-level clustering?

In summary, this work offers a solid step toward emergent, human-aligned communication in realistic settings, with thoughtful experiments and promising grounding results. I recommend acceptance, with the above clarifications and suggested analyses to strengthen the empirical claims.
