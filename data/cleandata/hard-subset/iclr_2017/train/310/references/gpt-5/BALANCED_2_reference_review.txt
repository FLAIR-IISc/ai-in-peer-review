1. Summary and contributions
The paper investigates how communication protocols can emerge between neural agents through self-play in a referential game with natural images. It makes four main contributions: (i) a simple yet scalable multi-agent framework where sender and receiver jointly learn to communicate from reward only, reaching near-perfect coordination; (ii) an analysis of architectural inductive biases (agnostic vs informed sender) and their influence on symbol usage and speed of convergence; (iii) a manipulation of “common knowledge” (using different images of the same class for sender/receiver) that nudges agents toward more category-level semantics; and (iv) a hybrid training setup that interleaves self-play with supervised image labeling, yielding more human-interpretable symbols and positive transfer to a human-in-the-loop evaluation on ReferIt-style image pairs. Empirically, the paper measures semantic alignment via cluster purity against human-defined categories, shows qualitatively that low-dimensional “living vs non-living” distinctions can emerge, and demonstrates that light supervision substantially improves the interpretability of the learned code (68% human accuracy vs 50% chance when using words learned in supervision to guide choices in novel image pairs).

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient empirical rigor and clarity to support the paper’s central claims about “natural” semantics and portability to humans (limited ablations, weak baselines, small/underpowered human study, ad hoc analyses).
- Methodological and presentation issues (reliance on strong pretrained visual features despite “tabula rasa” framing, missing training details/variance reporting, and numerous reference formatting errors) that do not meet ICLR standards.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The question—how to design environments that yield more human-aligned emergent communication—is timely and important; connections to cheap-talk and language emergence literatures are appropriate. The emphasis on nudging interpretability via game design and weak supervision is well motivated.

- Approach placement vs prior work: Prior work (e.g., multi-agent communication without predefined protocols) is cited, and the novelty here lies in (a) environmental tweaks to favor higher-level semantics and (b) grounding via supervised labels with a human evaluation. While conceptually interesting, the technical novelty is limited; the framework, learning rule (REINFORCE), and messaging bottleneck are standard.

- Do the results support the claims?
  • Communication success: Yes; near-perfect coordination is demonstrated across setups.
  • Natural semantics: Evidence is suggestive but not fully convincing. The purity metric based on majority symbol per object is coarse; the SVD argument for nontrivial symbol space is ad hoc; no robustness across seeds, vocabulary sizes, temperatures, or alternative similarity measures is reported. The living/non-living pattern is intriguing but anecdotal.
  • Grounding and human interpretability: Interleaving with supervised labeling increases purity and symbol usage; 68% human accuracy on 298 pairs is promising. However, the study is small, not pre-registered, lacks power analysis and controls (e.g., word frequency biases, salience controls), and does not compare to simple baselines (e.g., using ImageNet classifier predictions directly as labels or using nearest-neighbor labels from fc features). It’s unclear how often humans succeed due to generic associations (e.g., “dolphin” → “sea”) versus systematic symbol grounding.

- Methodological clarity:
  • The “tabula rasa” claim is undermined by heavy reliance on VGG features; this is acceptable but should be acknowledged as a limitation, not a feature.
  • Training details are sparse: no mention of variance reduction for REINFORCE, learning rates/optimizers, seeds, confidence intervals, or convergence stability. No analysis of sample efficiency.
  • The choice of τ=10 for Gibbs sampling, sampling vs argmax at test time, and the effect on exploration/exploitation are not examined.
  • Reported purity scores and symbol counts would benefit from error bars across runs.

- Scope/limitations: The game setup is minimal (single symbol, two images). There is no study of multi-symbol messages, role rotation, more distractors, or generalization to unseen categories/pairs. These omissions limit claims about portability and human alignment.

- Presentation: The references include numerous formatting errors and placeholders; figures/tables are referenced but not fully interpretable in the text provided; some definitions (e.g., purity calculation details) could be clearer.

4. Suggestions to improve (helpful, not all part of the decision)
- Strengthen evaluation:
  • Report means and confidence intervals over multiple seeds and datasets; include ablations on vocabulary size, temperature, embedding dim, and informed vs agnostic architecture.
  • Add stronger semantic metrics: mutual information between symbols and categories; topographic correlation between distances in visual/concept space and symbol distributions; context-independence scores; clustering stability.
  • Evaluate generalization: hold-out object categories, increased number of distractors, different image sources, and robustness to visual perturbations.
  • Compare against simple baselines: (i) use an ImageNet classifier’s top label as the emitted word; (ii) nearest-neighbor labeling in fc space; (iii) a supervised-only sender without self-play.

- Clarify training:
  • Provide optimization details, variance reduction (baselines/critic), exploration schedule, and whether sampling or argmax is used at test time.
  • Analyze sample efficiency and convergence stability across seeds.

- Human study:
  • Expand and pre-register the protocol, report power analysis, inter-annotator agreement, and control for word frequency/salience.
  • Include per-word performance, confusion analyses, and examples where humans fail.
  • Test whether humans can learn the code with brief adaptation (few-shot exposure), strengthening the portability claim.

- Broaden scope:
  • Explore multi-symbol messages and bidirectional/iterated interactions.
  • Examine compositionality and role rotation to prevent idiosyncratic codes.

- Framing and writing:
  • Recast “tabula rasa” claims given the use of pretrained features; discuss the gap to end-to-end learning from pixels.
  • Fix references and polish exposition.

Questions for the authors
1) How stable are your results across random seeds, hyperparameters, and vocabulary sizes? Please report variance and confidence intervals for success rates, purity, and symbol usage.
2) What is the performance of simple baselines (ImageNet classifier label emission; nearest-neighbor labeling) on your human study?
3) Do agents generalize to unseen categories or to increased distractor counts? What happens if you rotate sender/receiver roles?
4) How sensitive are results to the temperature τ and to sampling vs argmax? Is a baseline/critic used for REINFORCE?
5) Can you quantify symbol–semantics alignment beyond purity (e.g., mutual information, topographic correlation)?
6) In the supervised + self-play setup, how much does each component contribute? Please include ablations removing supervision or removing self-play.
7) Could you release code and data splits to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and shows promising qualitative trends. With stronger experimental rigor, clearer framing, and improved evaluation (especially for human interpretability and generalization), this work could become a solid contribution.
