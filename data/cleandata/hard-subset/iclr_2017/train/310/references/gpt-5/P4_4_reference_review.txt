1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper proposes a simple multi-agent referential game framework to study the emergence of communication between neural agents grounded in natural images. Two sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) communicate a single discrete symbol to a receiver; both are trained end-to-end with REINFORCE from communication success only.
- It demonstrates that tabula rasa agents reliably converge to successful communication on a large set of real images (ImageNet instances of 463 concepts), with faster convergence and richer symbol use for the informed sender.
- It analyzes the emergent code’s semantics using category purity (relative to 20 McRae categories), symbol-usage structure (SVD), and visual-space alignment (t-SNE), reporting above-chance purity and evidence that symbols correlate with higher-level object properties (e.g., living vs. non-living).
- It introduces an “object-level reference” variant where sender and receiver see different images of the same classes to remove low-level common knowledge, yielding improved semantic purity.
- It explores grounding via mixed training: alternating the referential game with supervised object naming on a subset of ImageNet labels, substantially increasing symbol purity and interpretability. In human evaluation on ReferItGame image pairs, humans selected the intended target given the agent’s emitted word 68% of the time, indicating partial human interpretability beyond direct supervision.

2. Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (i) Clear, well-motivated framework with novel insights on shaping environments to induce more interpretable emergent communication, capped by a human evaluation; (ii) Solid evidence that combining self-play with light supervision improves alignment with human concepts, offering a practical path toward human-agent communication.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three questions:
- Problem specificity: The paper targets how to design environments and learning setups that lead tabula rasa agents to develop portable, interpretable language for reference with real images. This is specific and important for multi-agent RL and language grounding.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well-situated relative to multi-agent communication (Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), language emergence literature, and game-theoretic signaling. The focus on natural images and interpretability via environment design and weak supervision is a meaningful departure from prior synthetic tasks and fixed protocols.
- Support for claims and rigor:
  - Communication success: Robust convergence across settings is shown. Though detailed statistics (variance across seeds) are not provided in the excerpt, the qualitative and tabular statements indicate reliability.
  - Emergent semantics: Category purity above chance, the informed sender’s broader symbol usage, and SVD spectra arguing against trivial two-symbol strategies support the claim that more structured, multi-dimensional codes emerge. The object-level variant reducing low-level common knowledge appropriately increases abstraction.
  - Grounding and human interpretability: Mixed supervised/self-play training yields higher symbol purity (70%), direct label usage in 47% of supervised-target cases, and 68% human success on ReferItGame pairs, with significance testing. This triangulates that supervision helps agents use human-meaningful symbols even off-distribution.
  - Technical soundness: The discrete communication channel with REINFORCE is standard for non-differentiable sampling; the architecture choices and training scheme are reasonable and minimalistic, matching the stated goals.

Limitations (not fatal):
- Some analyses rely on heuristic visualization (t-SNE) and a single purity metric; broader, quantitative metrics (e.g., topographic similarity, information-theoretic measures) would strengthen claims about semantic structure.
- Missing details on training stability, variance reduction in REINFORCE, number of seeds, and learning rates limit reproducibility and rigor.
- The strong semantic structure of VGG features may inflate apparent abstraction; controls with less semantic features would clarify how much semantics is learned vs. inherited.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are part of the acceptance assessment)
- Reproducibility: Report optimizer, learning rates, variance-reduction baselines for REINFORCE, entropy regularization (if any), parameter counts per architecture, number of random seeds, mean ± std for key metrics, and training time/sample complexity. Consider releasing code.
- Metrics: Complement purity and SVD with topographic similarity, communication rate/entropy, mutual information between symbols and categories, and generalization to novel category pairs. Provide exact purity values, chance levels, and confidence intervals for all settings (including object-level reference).
- Controls: Evaluate with alternative visual encodings (earlier CNN layers, random features, or frozen vs. fine-tuned) to disentangle emergent semantics from pre-trained feature semantics. Test more distractors per trial and sequence-based communication to probe scalability.
- Generalization and portability: Add cross-play experiments (independently trained agents paired at test time) to evaluate protocol conventionality and portability. Test zero-shot categories and new distractor distributions.
- Human study details: Provide per-word accuracies, inter-annotator agreement, instructions, and example failures. Clarify sampling of image pairs to avoid selection bias.
- Environment design: Ablate the frequency of supervised vs. game updates, vocabulary size, temperature, and sender architecture to map how each factor affects interpretability and performance.
- Related work: Tighten connections to recent differentiable communication approaches (e.g., Gumbel-softmax) and discuss trade-offs.
- Presentation: Fix multiple reference formatting issues; ensure figures/tables include all numbers referenced in text.

Questions for the authors
- REINFORCE details: Did you use a learned baseline or other variance-reduction methods? What are the learning rates, optimizers, entropy regularization, and gradient clipping settings?
- Stability: How many random seeds were run per condition, and what is the variance of success rate, symbol purity, and symbol usage across seeds?
- Cross-play: Do independently trained agents successfully communicate when paired at test time? If not, does mixed supervision improve cross-play?
- Feature dependence: How do results change with earlier CNN layers, random projections, or non-ImageNet features? To what extent does semantic purity persist without high-level pre-trained features?
- Capacity and bias: What are parameter counts for the informed vs. agnostic sender? Could the observed differences be due to capacity rather than inductive bias?
- Vocabulary and temperature: How sensitive are results to vocabulary size and sampling temperature? Is there a trade-off between success rate and interpretability?
- Human study: What was inter-rater agreement? Can you report per-word and per-category accuracies and provide more detailed error analysis?
- Generalization: How does the protocol perform with >2 distractors or when categories at test time are unseen during both game and supervision?
- Portability: Can the learned code transfer to a new receiver architecture or a human-in-the-loop adaptation setup without retraining the sender?
