Summary and contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in two-agent referential games over real images. Agents (sender/receiver) are trained via REINFORCE to communicate a single discrete symbol so the receiver identifies a target image among two. The work compares two sender architectures (agnostic vs. an “informed” architecture with a channel-wise convolutional inductive bias), measures convergence and symbol usage, and analyzes emergent semantics via category purity (against McRae categories) and t-SNE. The paper further introduces an “object-level reference” variant that reduces low-level common knowledge by showing different instances of the same class to sender and receiver, which nudges symbols toward higher-level semantics. Finally, it combines game play with supervised object naming (100 ImageNet labels) to ground symbols in natural language, and reports larger symbol purity and a small-scale human evaluation (CrowdFlower) on ReferItGame images where humans identify the target given the sender’s word, achieving 68% accuracy.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical evidence is not sufficiently rigorous to substantiate the claims about human-interpretable, concept-level semantics; evaluation relies on ad hoc metrics, qualitative plots, and limited human studies without strong baselines or statistical robustness. (2) The novelty over prior work on emergent communication is modest; critical confounds (e.g., target/distractor ordering, heavy reliance on supervised ImageNet features) undermine core claims about “tabula rasa” emergence and conceptual grounding.

Supporting arguments
- Methodological confounds and insufficient controls: The sender always receives [target, distractor] in fixed order, enabling a relational code that depends on the particular distractor, rather than an absolute, concept-level mapping. This undermines the interpretation that symbols reflect general object semantics. A stricter test would randomize order for the sender or remove any cue to the target index. It is unclear whether results persist under such control.

- Heavy reliance on supervised visual features: All image embeddings come from a pretrained, supervised VGG network. While training of communication is RL-only, the semantics available to the agents are imbued with ImageNet supervision. This weakens the “tabula rasa” narrative and the claim that semantics “emerge” from the game; they may instead reflect pre-existing supervised structure. Experiments from raw pixels or at least self-supervised features would better support the emergence claim.

- Evaluation metrics are limited: Category “purity” is a crude measure; details on chance computation, confidence intervals, and variability across seeds are missing. t-SNE is qualitative. The SVD spectrum analysis is anecdotal. More informative and standard measures (e.g., mutual information between symbols and categories, topographic similarity, ARI/NMI of cluster assignments, symbol entropy/homonymy/synonymy statistics) are absent. The paper also lacks ablations (vocabulary size beyond 10/100, temperature, embedding size, sender architecture components) and variance reduction/baselines in REINFORCE.

- Missing strong baselines: There is no comparison to trivial or established alternatives, e.g., (i) a receiver that ignores the message and guesses by image similarity; (ii) a sender trained as a standard classifier and a receiver that matches embeddings; (iii) random-message sender; (iv) supervised referring expression templates or retrieval systems. Without these, the significance of the reported purity and human results is unclear.

- Generalization and portability are not demonstrated: The paper’s stated goal is portability to new partners and humans. However, there are no cross-play experiments (swap independently trained partners), no partner adaptation tests, and limited human evaluation. The human study (298 pairs; 10 votes each) reports 68% accuracy, but lacks controls (e.g., randomized label baseline, frequency-biased words, or a purely supervised baseline). It is unclear whether humans leverage obvious dataset regularities or whether the sender relies on metonymy in a systematic, generalizable way.

- Claims overstate contributions relative to prior work: The setting is close to prior emergent communication works (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.). Using real images and mixing supervised grounding are natural extensions but not conceptually novel, and the analyses provided are too preliminary to justify a strong claim about human-interpretable language emergence.

- Reproducibility and presentation issues: Training details omit important aspects (e.g., number of random seeds, variance, exact test-time decoding policy). References are error-prone and incomplete. No code or data splits are promised.

Constructive feedback for improvement
- Remove confounds: Randomize input order for the sender; test whether communication and purity persist. Report symbol semantics when sender cannot exploit target index.

- Robust evaluation: Run multiple seeds and report mean±std. Provide statistical tests for purity, human accuracy, and SVD findings. Use additional metrics (mutual information, topographic similarity, ARI/NMI, per-symbol entropy, homonymy/synonymy rates).

- Strong baselines: (i) Receiver ignoring messages; (ii) sender as supervised classifier + nearest-neighbor receiver; (iii) random or frequency-matched symbol assignment; (iv) supervised referring expression or retrieval system on ReferItGame. Compare human interpretability against these.

- Generalization and portability: (i) Cross-play with independently trained partners; (ii) zero-shot categories held out from both game and supervision; (iii) role-swapping; (iv) message compositionality (multi-symbol messages), noise robustness, and partner replacement.

- Grounding beyond supervised VGG: Explore self-supervised or pixel-based encoders to strengthen the emergence claim. Ablate the supervised label-mixing schedule and vocabulary size.

- Human study: Add controls (random word, unrelated word, purely supervised word-choice baseline); report item-level agreement and confidence; expand to out-of-domain images.

- Clarity and reproducibility: Fix references; provide full hyperparameters, splits, preprocessing, and release code.

Questions for the authors
1) Does communication still succeed, and do symbol semantics remain “conceptual,” if the sender’s input order is randomized or the target index is not given? Please report purity and human interpretability under this control.

2) How many random seeds were run for each condition? Provide mean±std for success rates, purity, and symbol usage.

3) What is the test-time policy (sampling vs. argmax)? How sensitive are results to temperature?

4) How does vocabulary size beyond 100 affect symbol usage, purity, and coordination?

5) Can independently trained agents communicate when paired (cross-play)? If not, how much finetuning is needed?

6) Do results hold with self-supervised encoders or when training the visual encoder end-to-end?

7) In the human study, how does performance compare to baselines (random word, frequency-matched word, supervised-only sender)?

8) Can you show zero-shot generalization to categories held out from both game play and supervised labels?
