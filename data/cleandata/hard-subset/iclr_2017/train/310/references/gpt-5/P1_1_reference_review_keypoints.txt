- Studies emergent communication in a cooperative two-agent referential game with real images; agents trained tabula rasa with pretrained image encoders via policy gradients.  
- Compares two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed with dimension-wise convolutions); informed sender converges faster and uses a richer symbol inventory.  
- Shows symbol usage correlates with higher-level semantics (category purity above chance; e.g., living vs non-living distinctions with small vocabularies).  
- SVD analysis indicates nontrivial symbol structure beyond trivial 1–2D solutions, including synonymy.  
- Introduces an “object-level reference” manipulation (sender/receiver see different images of same class) that reduces low-level common knowledge and promotes class-level semantics and higher purity.  
- Proposes a hybrid training regime alternating self-play with supervised image labeling; this improves semantic purity, increases symbol usage, and yields partial human interpretability.  
- Human study: humans pick the agent’s intended referent 68% of the time on ReferIt-derived pairs using the agent’s symbol mapped to its supervised label.  
- Decision: Weak accept—motivated by clear exploration of environment/inductive-bias effects and a pragmatic route to human-aligned communication.  
- Evidence: agents reliably reach near-perfect coordination across settings.  
- Evidence: object-level variant strengthens alignment with high-level visual similarity rather than low-level pixels.  
- Evidence: supervised grounding yields 47% exact label match for supervised targets (chance 1%), demonstrating label-symbol alignment.  
- Novelty: useful combination of real-image grounding, architectural inductive-bias analysis, environment manipulation, and human-in-the-loop evaluation.  
- Suggestion: report mean ± std over multiple random seeds for success rate, symbol usage, purity, and human accuracy.  
- Suggestion: clarify train/test splits and whether object instances/classes are held out to avoid leakage.  
- Suggestion: add baselines (e.g., argmax of pretrained VGG, continuous-communication baseline).  
- Suggestion: provide variance-reduction details for REINFORCE (baseline/critic) and learning curves with confidence intervals.  
- Metric suggestion: complement purity with NMI/AMI to control for cluster count and imbalance.  
- Metric suggestion: report topographic similarity between image-space and symbol-space distances, mutual information between symbols and categories, and symbol entropy.  
- Analysis suggestion: probe zero-shot transfer to new partners and unseen classes, with/without object-level manipulation.  
- Analysis suggestion: study effects of vocabulary size, temperature, and embedding dimensionality on code structure and interpretability.  
- Ablation suggestion: isolate components of the informed sender’s convolutional design to identify useful inductive biases.  
- Human study suggestion: detail participant recruitment, QC, compensation, per-symbol sample sizes, inter-annotator agreement, and controls for saliency or size biases.  
- Reproducibility suggestion: provide code/pseudocode, computational budget, training time, and clean up references/typos.  
- Question: how many independent runs per configuration and what is variance in coordination, purity, and symbol usage across seeds?  
- Question: how are training/test games split at image and class levels; is there a held-out-class generalization regime?  
- Question: was a baseline/critic used for REINFORCE variance reduction, and how stable was training across seeds?  
- Question: how sensitive are results to temperature and vocabulary size; do larger vocabularies consistently improve purity and human interpretability?  
- Question: can you provide ablations of convolutional kernel/channel treatment in the informed sender to pinpoint helpful biases?  
- Question: in the hybrid setting, what is the supervised/self-play update ratio and does this trade-off affect interpretability vs coordination or metonymic flexibility?  
- Question: in the human study, how often did humans choose the “wrong” image for systematic reasons, and what qualitative error categories emerged?  
- Question: have you tested partner-swapping (train sender with one receiver, test with another or with humans) to assess language portability?
