1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies how grounded communication can emerge between neural agents through cooperative referential games over real images, and how environment design influences the semantics and human-interpretability of the emergent code. The setup features two agents (sender, receiver) trained tabula rasa with REINFORCE to exchange a single discrete symbol to identify a target image among two. The authors compare two sender architectures (agnostic vs. “informed” with a simple cross-channel convolutional inductive bias), and examine:

- Whether agents reliably learn to communicate.
- Whether the induced symbols capture high-level semantics (e.g., object categories) rather than idiosyncratic low-level cues.
- How modifying common knowledge (e.g., showing different images of the same class to sender/receiver) encourages more abstract symbol meanings.
- How mixing game-based self-play with supervised object naming grounds symbols in natural language and improves human interpretability.
- A crowdsourced human evaluation on a transfer dataset (ReferItGame) showing that humans can often use the agent’s symbols to select the intended target (68% accuracy).

Overall, the paper claims: (i) simple agents can quickly learn to communicate over natural image stimuli; (ii) language-like, semantically meaningful symbol usage emerges and can be nudged through environment design; (iii) adding a light supervised naming signal substantially increases interpretability and enables partial zero-shot alignment with humans.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper addresses an important and timely question (emergent communication with real inputs and human interpretability) with a clear, simple framework and compelling empirical findings, including a human study.
- It provides actionable insights on how architectural bias and task design shape emergent semantics, and a practical path to human-aligned grounding via mixed supervised/self-play training.

3. Supporting arguments
Problem and motivation: The work is well positioned at the intersection of multi-agent RL, grounded language learning, and signaling games. It advances beyond prior emergent communication work by using real images at scale, examining interpretability, and incorporating human evaluation. The literature review is appropriate (signaling games, CommNet/DIAL contemporaries, supervised-only conversational models) and the paper’s focus on environment design and grounding is well motivated.

Correctness and rigor: 
- Learning: Agents reliably reach near-perfect coordination across setups; the “informed sender” converges faster and uses a richer symbol inventory, as expected from its inductive bias.
- Semantics: The cluster purity of symbol usage against McRae categories is consistently above chance, even with small vocabularies, suggesting alignment with broader conceptual structure. t-SNE visualizations and SVD analyses support that symbol usage is not degenerate to a trivial low-dimensional code.
- Object-level reference manipulation (disjoint images of the same class for sender/receiver) increases purity, consistent with the claim that removing low-level common knowledge encourages abstraction.
- Grounding: Mixing supervised object naming dramatically boosts symbol purity (~70%), increases vocabulary usage, and induces direct name–symbol alignment (47% exact label use when applicable). The human study on ReferItGame pairs (mostly without the supervised categories present) shows 68% success in mapping symbols to the intended target, with strong statistical significance, supporting the claim of partial human interpretability and flexible “metonymic” usage.

While some evaluations are qualitative (e.g., t-SNE) and some choices are heuristic (e.g., SVD proxy for synonymy), the empirical evidence coherently supports the central claims. The human study, though modest in scope, is a valuable addition rarely seen in this line of work.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Evaluation depth:
  - Report symbol entropy, mutual information between symbols and categories, and topographic similarity (e.g., correlation between distances in input space and symbol-space distances).
  - Provide statistical tests and confidence intervals for purity improvements across conditions, and variance across seeds.
  - Analyze generalization to unseen object classes (zero-shot) and to out-of-distribution distractors.
- Training details and stability:
  - Clarify variance-reduction techniques for REINFORCE (baselines, entropy regularization), temperature choice, and whether test-time uses sampling or argmax.
  - Report sensitivity to vocabulary size, temperature, embedding dimension, and filter counts; include ablations for the “informed” architectural bias.
- Baselines:
  - Compare against differentiable communication frameworks (e.g., continuous channels, Gumbel-Softmax) and alternate architectures (e.g., attention-based sender).
  - Include non-communicative baselines (e.g., receiver guessing using image similarity) to contextualize gains.
- Human study:
  - Discuss possible selection bias (pairs chosen where the agent succeeded). Consider a pre-registered random sample of symbol–pair instances irrespective of agent success.
  - Report per-word performance and control for word frequency/biases; consider adding synonyms or brief glosses to reduce vocabulary mismatch effects.
- Broader generalization:
  - Test partner-swapping (train with one receiver, test with another or with humans) to probe language conventionality and robustness.
  - Explore multi-symbol utterances and interactive back-and-forth, where more compositional structure might emerge.
- Reproducibility:
  - Provide code, data splits, and full hyperparameter details; specify number of runs and variance bars.

5. Questions for the authors
- REINFORCE details: Did you use a baseline or variance reduction? What was the entropy of the sender’s policy over time? Is test-time selection greedy or sampled?
- Purity metric: How many seeds were run, and what is the variance in purity and accuracy across seeds? Are purity improvements statistically significant under the object-level manipulation?
- Symbol analysis: What is the entropy of symbol usage and the mutual information between symbols and (a) ImageNet classes, (b) McRae categories? Do symbols form a topographic mapping with visual semantics?
- Generalization: How does performance change when evaluating on unseen object classes or unseen categories? Can the supervised+game-trained sender communicate with a newly initialized receiver?
- Vocabulary size: How sensitive are results to K? Does a larger K improve purity/interpretability or just increase synonymy?
- Human study design: How were the 298 pairs sampled exactly? Could you report accuracy on a random sample independent of agent success? Did you measure inter-subject agreement?
- Inductive bias: Beyond the “informed” convolution, did you try other relational inductive biases (e.g., bilinear or attention comparisons)?
- Multi-step communication: Have you explored short sequences or receiver feedback, and does this yield more compositional symbol usage?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and impactful paper that empirically demonstrates how simple design choices in multi-agent games can steer emergent communication toward semantics aligned with human concepts, and how light supervision can substantially improve human interpretability. The suggested analyses would strengthen the work further.
