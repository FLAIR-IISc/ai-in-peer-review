1) Summary of contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in a two-agent referential game using real images (ImageNet) as stimuli. Two sender architectures are compared: an “agnostic” MLP and an “informed” sender with a dimension-wise comparison inductive bias.
- Using REINFORCE and a discrete symbol bottleneck, the agents achieve high coordination accuracy. The authors analyze the emergent code via symbol usage, SVD of symbol-by-pair matrices, clustering purity against McRae semantic categories, and t-SNE visualizations.
- They modify the game to remove low-level common knowledge (sender/receiver see different exemplars of the same class) to encourage more abstract semantics, finding modest gains in semantic purity.
- They further “ground” the code by interleaving self-play with supervised image-label training on a subset of labels. This increases symbol purity and yields partial human interpretability: in a two-choice test on ReferIt-derived image pairs, crowd workers pick the intended image 68% of the time given the sender’s word.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient empirical rigor and weak baselines for the central claims about semantic alignment and human interpretability; several evaluations are qualitative or confounded, with no competitive baselines or ablations that would isolate the sources of gains.
- The “tabula rasa”/emergent claims are undermined by reliance on high-level pretrained VGG features; generalization is not convincingly established and experimental design permits shortcuts.

3) Supporting arguments
- Claims vs setup: The paper repeatedly frames the agents as “blank slates,” yet relies on pretrained VGG fc/softmax features that already encode rich semantic structure. Many reported effects (e.g., living vs non-living separation, cluster purity) plausibly reflect properties of the representation rather than emergent language. There is no control using lower-level features or raw pixels, nor analysis removing the pretrained semantics.
- Baselines and ablations: There are no baselines that:
  - Replace RL with a supervised mapping from image features to a small code optimized to help a fixed receiver (to test whether “interaction” per se is necessary).
  - Use simpler comparators (e.g., [i_target − i_distractor], bilinear forms) vs the informed sender’s 1-D conv; or compare to a Gumbel-Softmax relaxation. 
  - Evaluate a non-communicative baseline (receiver ignores symbol) and a “leaky” baseline (sender leaks an index-like cue if available).
  - Control for vocabulary size/entropy with explicit regularization.
- Possible shortcuts/degeneracy: The agnostic sender can achieve near-perfect accuracy using only two symbols. This suggests coordination on a coarse partition (e.g., animate vs inanimate), which, given random pairing across 20 categories, may be sufficient for many trials. But then how do they disambiguate when both images fall in the same coarse class? The paper does not report performance conditional on same-category vs different-category pairs, nor per-category accuracies. Without this, it is hard to assess whether high overall accuracy stems from dataset composition rather than robust semantics.
- Train/test leakage and generalization: It is unclear if exact images or pairs appear in both training and test sets, and whether concept-level splits are enforced. Testing appears to sample via “the same method,” which risks overlap or easy near-duplicates. The object-level variant improves purity slightly, but broader generalization (unseen concepts/categories, distribution shifts) is not quantified.
- Evaluation metrics: 
  - Purity is used without complementary measures (e.g., NMI/AMI) and without confidence intervals. The SVD spectrum and t-SNE plots are qualitative and not diagnostic of semantic structure.
  - The human evaluation selects pairs conditioned on sender success and receiver correctness, introducing selection bias toward easy instances. There is no comparison to baselines (random word, most frequent word, nearest ImageNet label by cosine to the target box) or to a purely supervised sender without self-play. Reporting 68% on a two-choice task is suggestive but insufficiently controlled.
- Methodological detail: RL training lacks variance reduction/entropy regularization details; temperature τ=10 is unusual and not justified; no stability statistics across runs are given. The “informed” architecture’s advantage is not disentangled from its inductive bias to compare dimensions; simpler, established comparators are not tested.
- Scope of claims: The discussion overreaches toward “conversational machines” given the narrow, static, one-shot referential setting, reliance on pretrained vision, and limited, partially biased human results.

4) Suggestions for improvement (to help the authors; not all are decision-critical)
- Rigor and controls:
  - Enforce strict splits: no image overlap; concept- and category-level holdouts; evaluate per-split and per-category. Report performance conditioned on same/different category pairs.
  - Include baselines: random symbol; nearest-label sender (no interaction); supervised-only sender; Gumbel-Softmax; simpler comparators; non-communicating receiver; limited-vocab with entropy regularization.
  - Add quantitative semantic analyses: AMI/NMI; mutual information between symbols and categories; top-k concentration; per-symbol precision/recall for categories; calibration metrics.
  - Human eval: remove selection bias (sample all trials or pre-commit sampling), compare against baselines, report inter-annotator agreement, and analyze performance by symbol type and visual confounders.
- Representation controls: Test with lower-level conv features, or modestly trained encoders, to isolate the contribution of interaction vs pretrained semantics. Consider end-to-end fine-tuning with careful regularization and controls.
- Architectural ablations: Compare informed sender to [i_t − i_d], [i_t; i_d; |i_t − i_d|; i_t ⊙ i_d], and bilinear pooling. Study effect of τ, entropy regularization, and variance reduction (baseline, critic).
- Robustness and generalization: Evaluate on unseen concepts, cross-dataset transfer beyond ReferIt (e.g., COCO referents), and out-of-domain distractors. Report multiple random seeds with mean/variance.
- Clarify claims: Avoid “tabula rasa” phrasing given pretrained VGG; scope the contribution to careful experimentation on emergent codes with real images and preliminary grounding via supervision.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact train/test protocol? Are images or concepts shared across splits? How many pairs in train vs test? Any deduplication to avoid near-duplicates?
- What fraction of test pairs are same-category vs different-category? What are accuracies for these subsets, and for same-concept different-exemplar pairs?
- How does performance and purity change with alternative feature levels (e.g., conv5) or with randomly initialized encoders?
- Can the informed sender’s gains be reproduced with simple comparators (difference, bilinear), and how sensitive are results to τ and entropy regularization?
- In the human study, what are baseline accuracies for random labels or nearest-ImageNet-label heuristics? How does accuracy change if you do not condition on receiver correctness?
- Does the agnostic sender with two symbols fail catastrophically when both images belong to the same coarse type (e.g., both animals)? Please report error breakdowns.
- Are code and data splits available to ensure reproducibility?
