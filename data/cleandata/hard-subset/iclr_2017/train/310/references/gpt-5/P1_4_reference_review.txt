Summary of contributions
- The paper studies how discrete communication protocols emerge between neural agents trained tabula rasa in a two-player referential game with real images. It asks two central questions: (i) can agents learn to communicate successfully, and (ii) what environmental/architectural factors encourage emergent codes that align with human semantics and are interpretable by humans.
- It proposes two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed, the latter biased to compare image embeddings dimension-wise) and trains sender/receiver with REINFORCE, using VGG features of ImageNet images. It enforces a one-symbol discrete bottleneck.
- It evaluates emergent semantics via: (a) category purity of symbol-to-object mappings (McRae categories), (b) visual-semantic alignment (t-SNE), (c) a “removing common knowledge” variant showing different images of the same class to sender and receiver to encourage class-level properties, and (d) grounding via a mixed regime of self-play plus supervised image labeling, yielding more interpretable symbols.
- It conducts a human study on ReferIt-derived image pairs to test whether a sender trained with mixed supervision communicates in a way humans can decode; humans select the intended target 68% of the time (above chance) and exhibit plausible metonymic interpretations.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The paper provides a clear, well-motivated framework and empirical evidence that environment/architectural choices affect both emergence and human interpretability of communication. 2) The mixed supervised/self-play grounding and accompanying human evaluation are novel and impactful steps toward human-compatible emergent language.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and positioning: The goal—emergent communication that is usable with new partners, especially humans—is central to multi-agent AI. The paper is well-situated relative to recent neural emergent communication work (Sukhbaatar et al.; Foerster et al.) and draws on relevant perspectives from game theory and cognitive science. The focus on natural images (not synthetic inputs) raises the bar on ecological validity and aligns with the “portability” desideratum.
- Technical approach: The referential game is simple yet appropriate for isolating key variables (architecture, vocabulary size, shared vs distinct visual inputs). The discrete one-symbol protocol and REINFORCE are standard and fit the problem. The “informed sender” inductive bias is a reasonable hypothesis and empirically beneficial (faster convergence, richer symbol usage).
- Evidence for claims:
  - Communication success: near-perfect coordination across settings indicates the setup is learnable and stable.
  - Semantics beyond low-level cues: Category purity significantly above chance (including with very small vocabularies) and t-SNE analyses suggest symbols track higher-level visual/conceptual properties. The “living vs non-living” split emerging with two symbols is compelling and consistent with cognitive findings.
  - Environment manipulation: The object-level variant (showing different images of the same class to the two agents) increases category purity, supporting the claim that removing low-level common knowledge pushes codes toward abstract properties.
  - Grounding for human interpretability: The mixed supervised/self-play regime substantially increases symbol purity (to ~70%), yields direct label-symbol alignment in many cases (~47% exact label use on supervised categories), and transfers partial interpretability to out-of-domain referential tasks with human partners (68% success). This is strong, novel evidence that light supervision can steer emergent codes toward human usability.
- Broader impact: The work provides concrete design levers (inductive biases, common knowledge manipulation, light supervision) to improve interpretability, a pressing challenge for emergent communication.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Baselines and ablations:
  - Provide comparisons to non-communicative or trivial strategies (e.g., nearest-neighbor to a fixed prototype, or a “cheating” common-randomness baseline) to contextualize performance/purity gains.
  - Report results with alternative discrete relaxations (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) and with/without variance-reduction baselines for REINFORCE to assess sample efficiency and stability.
  - Examine the effect of temperature and vocabulary size systematically (beyond 10/100) and whether argmax vs sampling at test changes outcomes.
- Statistical rigor:
  - Add confidence intervals or variability across multiple random seeds for coordination success, symbol usage counts, and purity metrics. The human study already includes a statistical test; similar rigor for other results would strengthen claims.
  - Clarify SVD analysis: dataset size, cross-validation protocol, and how dimensionality relates to synonymy vs polysemy.
- Dependence on pretrained vision:
  - Discuss how much of the “semantics” originates from VGG features versus the communication game. Consider an experiment with lower-level features or frozen vs finetuned encoders to tease apart sources of structure.
- Generalization tests:
  - Partner-swap experiments (new receiver with trained sender; population-level language convergence) to probe conventionalization and partner-robustness.
  - Zero-shot categories: quantify label-symbol usage and interpretability for categories unseen in the supervised set.
- Human study:
  - Report per-word and per-category breakdowns, and inter-annotator agreement.
  - Explore whether short phrases (two-symbol sequences) would boost interpretability, anticipating extensions to multi-step protocols.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results (success, purity, human interpretability) to the temperature parameter and sampling policy at test time? Would deterministic decoding alter symbol-category alignments?
2) Did you try variance-reduction techniques (baselines/critics) for REINFORCE, and did they change learning speed or symbol usage?
3) In the mixed supervised setting, do you observe catastrophic interference between labeling and game play, or does sharing the embedding layer consistently help? What happens if embeddings are not shared?
4) How consistent are the emergent codes across random seeds and runs (e.g., alignment up to permutation), and do different runs converge to similarly interpretable symbol-category mappings?
5) Can the informed sender’s advantage be replicated with other inductive biases (e.g., attention over object parts or learned similarity metrics), and do these biases differentially affect human interpretability?
6) In the object-level variant, does symbol purity improve primarily within the supervised categories or broadly across all categories? Any insight into which categories benefit most?
7) Could you release code and the exact splits used for reproducibility and to facilitate follow-up work on human interpretability?
