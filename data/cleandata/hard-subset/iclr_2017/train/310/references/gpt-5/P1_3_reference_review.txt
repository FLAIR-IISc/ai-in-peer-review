Summary of contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in a two-agent referential game using real images (ImageNet-derived), asking: (i) can tabula rasa agents learn to communicate? (ii) what properties of the environment/architecture yield more human-interpretable, concept-level meanings? (iii) can emergent codes be grounded in human language?
- Two sender architectures are explored: an agnostic MLP and an “informed” sender with an inductive bias to compare image embeddings dimension-wise. Agents are trained with REINFORCE using a discrete, single-symbol communication bottleneck.
- Empirical results show near-perfect coordination after training across settings; the informed sender converges faster and uses a richer symbol inventory. Analyses suggest symbols correlate with high-level concepts (category purity above chance; t-SNE shows visually similar objects sharing symbols; even with 2 symbols, agents discover a living/non-living split).
- Modifying “common knowledge” by showing sender/receiver different images of the same class nudges the code toward class-level semantics (higher purity).
- Adding a supervised object-naming task (100 labels) interleaved with self-play grounds the code: more symbols are used, purity increases (~70%), and when the supervised label matches the target, the sender often uses it. Human evaluation on a separate ReferIt-derived dataset shows humans pick the intended image 68% of the time given the sender’s word, suggesting partial interpretability and metonymic flexibility.

Decision
Accept (weak). Reasons: (1) The paper tackles an important, timely problem—emergent communication with an emphasis on human interpretability—and proposes a simple, compelling setup and interventions that yield informative results. (2) The empirical evidence, including a human study and a grounding mechanism via mixed supervised/self-play training, supports the central claims despite some limitations in rigor and breadth.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work is well motivated by the need for agents that communicate in natural language for coordination. It is clearly positioned relative to emergent communication (Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), classic signaling/cheap talk, and human-in-the-loop and scripted environments. The focus on interpretability/human alignment and on realistic visual inputs is a useful and somewhat distinct angle.
- Correctness and rigor: The training setup is standard and appropriate (REINFORCE on discrete symbols, precomputed CNN features). The architecture choices are reasonable; the “informed” sender’s inductive bias plausibly explains faster convergence and richer symbol usage. The interventions (removing low-level common knowledge; mixing supervised object naming) are logically motivated and the observed effects align with hypotheses (higher purity; more interpretable symbols).
- Evidence: Multiple analyses triangulate the semantics of symbols (category purity vs McRae taxonomy, SVD of symbol usage, t-SNE visualization). The human study (298 pairs, 10 judgments each; 68% accuracy with mixed-effects logistic regression) provides valuable external validation that the grounded code has partially human-understandable meanings beyond directly supervised categories. The living/non-living split with only 2 symbols is an intriguing emergent effect consistent with cognitive literature.
- Limitations: Evaluation lacks variance across seeds and stronger statistical reporting for several metrics (e.g., confidence intervals for purity; stability of symbol usage). The communication protocol is extremely limited (single symbol, no compositionality), and there is limited analysis of the topology of the learned code (e.g., topographic similarity). Comparisons to very recent concurrent work are acknowledged but empirical baselines (e.g., continuous channel, ST-Gumbel, or alternative receiver architectures) are absent. Some design choices (temperature, variance-reduction baselines) are under-specified.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Report mean±std over multiple random seeds for key metrics (accuracy, purity, number of symbols used) and learning curves to assess stability.
- Provide full results tables that are referenced (currently not visible) with fc vs softmax features and vocab size (10 vs 100).
- Quantify “interpretability” more systematically: (i) topographic similarity (correlation between distances in symbol space and semantic/visual distances), (ii) mutual information between symbols and categories, (iii) zero-shot generalization to unseen categories or new partner agents.
- Analyze the learned lexicon: confusions, synonymy/polysemy, SVD dimensionality vs vocabulary size; show a symbol–category heatmap and cluster labels post hoc.
- Partner-swap experiments: train sender with one receiver and test with a new receiver (trained only on the protocol) to probe language conventionality and portability.
- Ablations: effect of temperature, entropy regularization, REINFORCE baselines (e.g., learned critic), annealing schedules, embedding sizes, communication bandwidth (multi-symbol messages).
- Consider compositional protocols (multi-symbol sequences) and back-and-forth interaction to test whether more complex semantics emerge.
- Human study: add stronger controls (random word baselines, word frequency effects), report inter-annotator agreement, and analyze per-word performance and error types.
- References need cleanup (incomplete citations).

Questions for the authors
- How robust are results across random seeds? Please report variance for success rate, purity, symbol usage, and SVD dimensionality.
- Did you use any variance-reduction baseline for REINFORCE? If not, does adding a baseline or entropy regularization change symbol diversity or convergence?
- How sensitive are outcomes to the temperature τ and sampling vs argmax at test time?
- In the supervised grounding experiment, how are the 100 labels mapped to the 100-symbol vocabulary during self-play? Is there a one-to-one tie, or do labels and symbols share embeddings?
- Can the grounded sender communicate successfully with a newly initialized receiver trained only to imitate messages on held-out games (partner-swap)? This would test conventionalization.
- Do agents generalize to unseen object categories or to out-of-distribution images? Any evidence of overfitting to specific ImageNet classes?
- What happens with multi-symbol messages or longer interactions? Do you observe more compositional or fine-grained semantics?
- Could you report purity and human interpretability when using softmax-layer (class-probability) features vs fc features? Does low-level class bias inflate purity?

Overall, this is a clear, engaging paper that presents a clean testbed and insightful interventions to steer emergent communication toward human-interpretable semantics. With stronger evaluation and robustness analyses, it would make a solid contribution.
