1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies the emergence of communication in a two-agent referential game using real images as stimuli. The agents (sender, receiver) are trained tabula rasa with REINFORCE to select a single discrete symbol and identify a target among two images. The authors compare two sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed), show that agents reliably learn to coordinate, and analyze the emergent “language” with respect to semantic structure (cluster purity against human-defined categories, spectrum of symbol–pair usage). They further modify the game to remove low-level common knowledge (using different images of the same class for sender/receiver) to encourage higher-level semantics. Finally, they combine self-play with supervised object naming to ground symbols in human language and evaluate interpretability via a human study on ReferItGame image pairs, finding that humans can pick the intended target 68% of the time. The paper is motivated by the goal of developing communication protocols that are portable and interpretable, and it connects to literature in signaling games, multi-agent RL, and language emergence.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient empirical rigor to substantiate key claims about the emergence of human-aligned semantics and portability: limited baselines, sparse statistics, few ablations, and no tests of generalization across partners or environments beyond a small human study.
- Limited novelty relative to prior work on emergent communication in multi-agent games; the main technical components (REINFORCE with discrete symbols, referential game with image features) are incremental, and the analysis does not convincingly isolate the proposed design choices as necessary or superior.

3) Supporting arguments
- Baselines and controls: There is no comparison to simple non-communicative or heuristic strategies (e.g., receiver picks the image closest to category prototypes; or a hand-crafted sender that maps CNN clusters to symbols). It is hard to assess how much of the success/semantics stems from pre-trained VGG features vs. the learned protocol.
- Statistical rigor: Results are reported largely as point estimates. Claims such as “purity is significantly above chance” and “cross-validated SVD suggests 50 dimensions” lack details (confidence intervals, random seeds, variance across runs, exact tests). The convergence analysis does not quantify stability (e.g., fraction of seeds converging, time-to-convergence distribution).
- Scope of evaluation: The analysis mostly uses cluster purity and t-SNE visualizations. These are coarse and can be misleading. There is no topographic similarity analysis, RSA, correlation with WordNet/semantic features, or disentanglement of synonymy/polysemy. The “living vs. non-living” claim is not quantified.
- Portability/generalization: A central motivation is portability to new partners and humans. Yet there is no cross-play evaluation (agents trained independently paired at test time), partner-swap robustness, or adaptation to unseen partners. The human study is promising but limited (298 pairs, 10 judgments/pair) and conducted on a dataset distinct from training; it is unclear how robust the 68% figure is across words, categories, and runs.
- Architectural choices: The “informed sender” convolution over image-embedding channels is interesting but underspecified and not ablated. It’s unclear whether the advantage arises from inductive bias, capacity, or optimization ease. No comparison to alternative discrete communication training (e.g., straight-through estimators or Gumbel-Softmax) is provided.
- Claims vs. evidence: The paper argues that removing common knowledge pushes higher-level semantics; the reported purity gains are small and not contextualized. The supervised grounding experiment is sensible, but the linkage between supervised labels and game-time symbols is not analyzed beyond usage counts and a single aggregate human accuracy.
- Clarity/references: Some references are incomplete, and certain methodological details (e.g., symbol-temperature sensitivity, reward baselines/variance reduction, sample complexity) are missing.

4) Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Add cross-play across random seeds and architectures to assess protocol portability.
  - Report mean±std over multiple runs; include CIs for purity and human accuracy.
  - Compare against heuristic baselines (e.g., nearest-neighbor labelers, clustering-based symbol assignment).
  - Use richer metrics: topographic similarity (correlation between input similarity and message similarity), RSA with semantic spaces (WordNet path length, Glove/word2vec), AMI/ARI for clustering, and analyses of synonymy/polysemy per symbol.
  - Test for “shortcut” strategies (e.g., symbols encoding relative features of the pair rather than object properties) by varying distractors systematically and using controlled “same-class vs. different-class” and “hard negative” settings.
- Grounding and human studies:
  - Provide per-word human accuracies and difficulty analyses; assess calibration and confidence.
  - Evaluate compositional generalization by introducing multi-attribute referents or sequences of symbols; analyze whether messages systematically reflect attributes.
  - Consider a within-game human-in-the-loop test (replace receiver with human on in-domain images) to directly measure interpretability without domain shift.
- Methodology and ablations:
  - Explore vocabulary size, temperature, entropy regularization, and variance-reduction baselines (e.g., learned baselines in REINFORCE).
  - Compare informed vs. agnostic sender under equal parameter counts; test alternative inductive biases (attention over features, relational reasoning).
  - Try continuous-to-discrete training variants (Gumbel-Softmax, straight-through) and report training stability.
- Broader claims:
  - If aiming to speak to cheap-talk/game-theory, quantify convergence rates to equilibria, multiplicity across seeds, and how the environment parameters select equilibria.
  - Discuss limits of using fixed VGG features and consider end-to-end fine-tuning or self-supervised visual backbones to test robustness.
- Presentation:
  - Complete and standardize references. Clarify the cross-validated SVD procedure. Add implementation details needed for reproducibility and consider releasing code and data splits.

Questions for the authors
- How many random seeds were run per condition? What is the variance in coordination accuracy, symbol usage, and purity across seeds?
- How exactly is “significantly above chance” computed for purity? Please report absolute numbers, chance distribution, and confidence intervals.
- What is the “cross-validated SVD” setup (folds, criterion, model selection)? Can you quantify effective dimensionality with alternative criteria?
- Can you provide cross-play results (pairing independently trained senders and receivers)? How portable are protocols across runs and architectures?
- In the supervised grounding setup, what are the 100 labels, and how often do symbols align with these labels during game play on in-domain images? Do you observe systematic metonymy patterns?
- Details of the human study: demographics, quality control, inter-annotator agreement, per-word performance, and sensitivity to prompt wording. How robust is the 68% with different seeds/label sets?
- Did you use any variance-reduction baseline in REINFORCE? How sensitive are results to temperature τ and vocabulary size?
- Can you rule out pairwise “shortcut” strategies? For instance, if both images are from unseen categories but share low-level features, do messages still track category-level semantics?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem with an appealing setup and provides encouraging preliminary evidence. With stronger empirical rigor, deeper analysis, and clearer positioning against prior work, this could become a solid contribution.
