1. Summary of contributions and claims
The paper studies emergent communication in multi-agent referential games using real-world images as input. Two neural agents (sender/receiver), initialized tabula rasa and trained via REINFORCE on a discrete symbol channel, learn to coordinate by referring to a target image among distractors. The paper: (i) compares an “agnostic sender” vs an “informed sender” with an inductive bias to compare image embeddings dimension-wise; (ii) evaluates whether emergent symbols align with higher-level semantics (category purity, t-SNE organization); (iii) manipulates “common knowledge” by showing sender/receiver different instances of the same classes to encourage abstraction; and (iv) partially grounds the emergent code in human language by interleaving self-play with supervised object naming, then testing interpretability with human subjects in a human-in-the-loop receiver setting. Empirically, agents reliably learn to coordinate; the informed sender converges faster and uses a richer symbol inventory; symbol–object associations show above-chance category purity and improve under the object-level manipulation; supervised grounding increases purity and vocabulary usage, and enables humans to identify the target 68% of the time even for images outside the supervised label set. The work positions referential games as a scalable path toward human-aligned emergent communication.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited technical novelty relative to existing multi-agent emergent communication (e.g., contemporaneous work on discrete-channel communication in neural agents), with only modest architectural and training innovations.
- Empirical evaluation lacks rigor and breadth (few ablations, limited statistical analysis, unclear variability across runs, and reliance on qualitative visualization), making it hard to robustly support claims about semantic alignment and portability.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- The paper addresses whether tabula rasa agents can (a) learn to communicate in a referential game with natural images, and (b) develop symbol meanings that align with higher-level human-relevant semantics, including partial interpretability by humans.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- The motivation—interactive learning for functional language use—is clear and well contextualized across linguistics, game theory, and multi-agent RL. The setup is a natural extension of Lewis signaling games with modern neural function approximators. Related work on neural communication without pre-coded protocols is cited. However, given existing demonstrations of emergent communication and referential games, the methodological novelty is incremental (a simple inductive bias in the sender, a straightforward removal of low-level common knowledge, and interleaving with supervised naming). The paper would benefit from sharper positioning vis-à-vis nearest neural referential-game baselines and more recent grounding/interpretability work.

Do the results support the claims?
- Coordination: Clear evidence that agents reach near-perfect success across variants.
- Semantics: Above-chance cluster purity and t-SNE organization suggest some alignment with high-level categories; the “living vs. non-living” emergent dichotomy is plausible. Yet the metrics are coarse: purity depends on chosen category taxonomy; SVD spectrum analysis is suggestive but not quantified; lack of confidence intervals, multiple seeds, or effect sizes limits strength of conclusions.
- Environment shaping: The object-level manipulation yields small but consistent purity gains; this is reasonable but not deeply analyzed (e.g., disentangling visual vs. class-name priors in CNN features).
- Human interpretability: Interleaving supervised naming increases purity and symbol usage, and the human study (68% choice accuracy, significant in a mixed-effects logistic regression) is promising. Still, the protocol details are sparse (selection of pairs, word–image semantic relatedness controls, per-word difficulty), and no comparisons to alternative grounding strategies or to purely supervised baselines are provided.
- Overall, while results are encouraging, the empirical backing for portability and human alignment remains preliminary.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all affect the decision)
- Experimental rigor and reporting:
  - Report results over multiple random seeds with means, standard deviations, and learning curves; include confidence intervals for purity and usage statistics.
  - Add a variance-reduction baseline in REINFORCE and ablate temperature choices; consider Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through estimators and compare stability/sample efficiency.
  - Provide stronger baselines: e.g., a sender pre-trained only with supervised labels then frozen; a sender without the inductive-bias convolutions but matched in parameter count.
- Metrics for semantics and communication:
  - Go beyond category purity: compute mutual information between symbols and categories; top-k precision/recall for symbol–category mappings; probing with out-of-category distractors; symbol entropy and effective vocabulary size.
  - Evaluate zero-shot portability: train sender with one receiver, test with a new receiver (frozen sender); test cross-partner coordination without further training.
  - Assess generalization to unseen object classes and OOD distractors.
- Human evaluation:
  - Clarify sampling of word–pair stimuli, control for lexical frequency and concreteness, and report per-word accuracy distributions.
  - Compare against baselines: random words, nearest ImageNet label to VGG features, or a CLIP-based zero-shot word; measure whether the grounded sender outperforms these.
  - Analyze error patterns and “metonymic” shifts systematically (e.g., noun–context associations).
- Architecture and environment:
  - Detail the “informed sender” more precisely; control capacity between agnostic/informed models.
  - Explore multi-symbol messages and limited-length channels; analyze compositionality (topographic similarity, generalization to novel attribute combinations).
  - Report the effect of vocabulary size systematically (10, 50, 100, 500).
- Reproducibility:
  - Clarify train/test splits, avoidance of leakage, and mapping between McRae categories, ImageNet labels, and supervised label subset.
  - Fix and standardize the references; several entries are malformed or incomplete.

Questions for the authors
- How many runs per configuration were conducted? Please report variability across seeds for success, purity, and symbol usage.
- Did you use a baseline/critic in REINFORCE? If not, how stable is training across seeds and temperatures? Any annealing of τ?
- How is capacity matched between agnostic and informed senders? Could the observed gains be due to parameterization differences?
- How exactly is purity computed (majority assignment over objects to symbols, or per-image)? Are class imbalances controlled?
- For the object-level manipulation, are training and test concepts/images disjoint? Do results hold when testing on unseen classes?
- In the supervised grounding setup, how are the 100 labels chosen, and how are synonyms handled relative to ImageNet/ReferItGame labels?
- In the human study, how were image pairs and words sampled? Can you provide per-word accuracy, inter-annotator agreement, and examples of failure modes?
- Did you attempt zero-shot partner portability (new receiver) or human-in-the-loop training during evaluation?
- How sensitive are results to vocabulary size, message length (single vs multi-symbol), and embedding dimensionality?

Overall, the paper is clearly written and presents encouraging findings, but stronger methodological novelty and more rigorous, comprehensive evaluation are needed for ICLR.
