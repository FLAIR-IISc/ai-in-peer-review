Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a simple but compelling framework for studying emergent communication: a two-agent referential game over natural images, with discrete symbols and learning via self-play (REINFORCE). Two sender architectures (agnostic vs. “informed”) are compared.
- It analyzes how environment design affects the semantics of emergent messages: (i) standard image pairs vs. (ii) “object-level reference,” where agents see different instances of the same class to remove low-level common knowledge and encourage higher-level semantics.
- It introduces a hybrid training protocol that mixes self-play with a supervised image-labeling task, thereby grounding the sender’s symbols in human words, and evaluates interpretability with a crowdsourced human study, including transfer to a different dataset (ReferIt).
- Empirically, agents reliably learn to communicate; the “informed” sender trains faster and uses a richer code; symbol usage correlates with high-level object categories (purity > chance, improved in object-level setting); the hybrid protocol yields high symbol purity and partial human interpretability (68% accuracy in a two-choice task even when supervised words do not directly name the target).

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: The paper advances a bold and timely research direction—steering emergent communication toward human-interpretable semantics using realistic inputs and hybrid self-play/supervised grounding—and supports it with clear, positive results. While the methodology is intentionally simple and some analyses are preliminary, the conceptual novelty and demonstrated feasibility outweigh the limitations.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and scope: Prior multi-agent communication works (e.g., Sukhbaatar, Foerster) largely establish that protocols emerge; this paper goes further by (a) using large sets of real images, (b) explicitly manipulating the environment to promote higher-level semantics, and (c) combining self-play with supervised object naming to align symbols with human words and evaluating with humans. This is a meaningful step toward human-compatible agents.
- Empirical support for claims:
  - Coordination success is robust across settings and architectures.
  - Environment design matters: the object-level variant increases category-level purity and visually coherent symbol clusters, supporting the claim that removing low-level common knowledge nudges agents toward abstract semantics.
  - Hybrid grounding works: the sender reuses supervised labels during the game and achieves substantially higher symbol purity; interpretability carries over to out-of-domain ReferIt pairs, with humans correctly identifying the target 68% of the time even when direct denotations are absent—suggesting metonymic/associative semantics akin to natural language flexibility.
- Clarity of the research program: The paper clearly articulates the overarching goal (portable, human-aligned communication), shows simple but effective knobs to steer emergent codes, and provides an initial human evaluation. This is a promising foundation for further work.

Caveats (not blocking)
- Methodological details and baselines are light. There is limited analysis of sample efficiency, variance reduction in REINFORCE, sensitivity to temperature, vocabulary size, and random seeds.
- Semantics evaluation could be more rigorous. Purity is helpful but coarse; the SVD/t-SNE evidence is illustrative but not definitive. Stronger metrics (e.g., topographic similarity, mutual information, probing for category boundaries) would increase robustness.
- Human study selection may introduce bias (pairs chosen from successful agent trials); stronger controls and comparisons to supervised-only or heuristic baselines would be informative.
- The “informed” sender’s architectural bias is interesting but somewhat ad hoc; deeper discussion or ablations would clarify what inductive biases are truly beneficial.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report training stability and variability across multiple seeds; include learning curves and variance bands.
- Add baselines:
  - A purely supervised classifier used as a sender (no self-play), to compare symbol interpretability and human performance.
  - A listener trained to map supervised labels to images without self-play, to disentangle the contribution of interactive learning.
- Provide stronger semantic analyses:
  - Topographic similarity between input-space distances and message similarity.
  - Normalized mutual information between symbols and category labels; confusion matrices for living vs. non-living and other high-level dichotomies.
  - Probe whether symbol usage generalizes to unseen classes.
- Clarify the human study:
  - Pre-register or at least standardize sampling to avoid success-only bias.
  - Compare humans against simple automated baselines (e.g., choosing the image closer to the supervised label embedding, or to a CLIP-based similarity).
- Discuss partner portability: train independent pairs and then swap sender/receiver to test whether languages are shared or idiosyncratic.
- Consider brief experiments with multi-symbol messages (even 2–3 tokens) to probe compositionality; or at least analyze intra-symbol entropy and reuse patterns across contexts.
- Clean up and standardize references.

Questions for the authors
1. How stable are the findings across random seeds and hyperparameters (temperature, vocab size, embedding dim)? Do you observe mode collapse in symbol usage?
2. Do you employ any variance reduction in REINFORCE (baselines, entropy regularization)? How sensitive is coordination to these choices?
3. Can you report topographic similarity or NMI between symbols and category labels, and how these metrics change in the object-level and hybrid settings?
4. How were pairs sampled for the human study? Could you repeat the study on a uniformly sampled set (not conditioned on successful agent communication) and report differences?
5. How portable are the emergent protocols? If you swap senders and receivers trained independently, does communication succeed?
6. In the hybrid setting, how often does the sender use supervised labels for out-of-vocabulary classes via metaphor/metonymy, and can you characterize these patterns?
7. Can you compare to a supervised-only sender baseline (no self-play) to quantify the specific benefit of interactive learning for human interpretability and transfer?

Overall, this is an ambitious, forward-looking paper that takes meaningful steps toward human-interpretable emergent communication. Despite some methodological gaps, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.
