- Study of emergent discrete communication in cooperative referential games grounded in ImageNet-derived real images using two tabula rasa agents trained with REINFORCE.  
- Contribution: framework for learning to communicate on large-scale noisy visual inputs without pre-specified semantics.  
- Contribution: comparison of agnostic sender vs. informed sender with an inductive bias for dimension-wise comparison.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that emergent symbols align with higher-level semantics (category purity, t-SNE, living vs. non-living).  
- Contribution: environment tweak removing low-level common knowledge by giving sender/receiver different images of same class to encourage class-level communication.  
- Contribution: hybrid learning interleaving self-play with supervised object naming, improving interpretability and human-readability of symbols.  
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).  
- Reasoning: clear setup, concrete empirical evidence of human-aligned emergent communication, and hybrid supervision improving interpretability; human eval and common-knowledge manipulation are novel.  
- Support: motivates interactive, functional language learning beyond supervised captioning and scripted MAS setups.  
- Support: simple, reproducible game isolates discrete symbol emergence, partner co-adaptation, and environmental structure effects.  
- Support: informed sender shows faster learning, different symbol usage, and better semantic alignment than agnostic sender.  
- Support: agents reliably learn to coordinate near perfectly; category purity above chance and improved with informed sender and class-level reference.  
- Support: t-SNE and visual proximity analyses correlate visual similarity with symbol usage.  
- Support: hybrid regime yields markedly higher purity and interpretability; human eval shows 68% success on novel referents.  
- Implication: mild inductive biases and environment design can steer emergent codes toward human concepts.  
- Implication: small amounts of supervised grounding can make emergent communication more human-compatible without hurting self-play performance.  
- Concern: missing baselines such as supervised-only sender, self-play-only on ReferIt, and larger distractor sets to test scalability.  
- Concern: unclear generalization to unseen concepts vs. unseen images of seen concepts; need explicit data splits and reporting.  
- Concern: lack of cross-play evaluation to test portability of emergent languages across partners.  
- Concern: need systematic analysis of communication channel hyperparameters (vocabulary size, temperature, entropy regularization) and sensitivity to symbol collapse.  
- Concern: missing RL training details (variance reduction, optimizer, learning rate, initialization, early stopping) affecting reproducibility.  
- Concern: metrics limited to purity; should include NMI/ARI, confidence intervals, and clarify chance computations and clustering choices.  
- Concern: some analyses (SVD) are ad hoc; add metrics like topographic similarity or structure-function correlations.  
- Concern: human study selection bias (models chosen where they succeeded); need matched controls and per-word accuracy/error analysis.  
- Concern: informed sender architecture bakes in inductive bias; consider permutation-invariant designs and robustness to nuisance variation.  
- Concern: presentation issues with reference formatting and clarity on feature types (fc vs. softmax) effects.  
- Questions: do agents generalize to unseen concepts (zero-shot) or only unseen images of seen concepts?  
- Questions: can senders communicate with novel receivers (cross-play), and how does supervised grounding affect portability?  
- Questions: how do vocabulary size and temperature affect symbol collapse and purity; any phase transitions in usage diversity?  
- Questions: how does performance scale with more candidate images (4, 8-way) or multi-step communication?  
- Questions: did REINFORCE use a variance-reducing baseline; what optimizer/hyperparameters were used and how sensitive are results?  
- Questions: human-eval controls—accuracy with random labels or shuffled words, confidence intervals, and inter-annotator agreement?  
- Questions: are training and test image pairs fully disjoint at the image level and were there concept-level holdouts?
