1. Summary of claimed contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in a two-agent referential game using real images (ImageNet-derived) and discrete messages, with agents trained tabula rasa via REINFORCE. Two sender architectures are compared (agnostic vs. informed with an inductive bias to compare images dimension-wise), under a strict discrete communication bottleneck and limited vocabularies.
- It shows agents reliably learn to coordinate with high success, and that the induced symbol usage exhibits nontrivial semantic structure: symbols align with high-level conceptual categories (measured via cluster purity against McRae categories, SVD analyses, and t-SNE visualizations).
- It introduces a game variant that removes low-level common knowledge (sender and receiver view different instances from the same classes), encouraging more abstract, object-level semantics without harming coordination and improving purity.
- It proposes a simple pathway to natural language grounding by interleaving self-play with supervised object naming, sharing the sender’s game-embedding. This increases symbol purity and makes many symbols directly interpretable.
- It evaluates human interpretability on a different dataset (ReferItGame bounding boxes), showing that humans decode the sender’s symbols at 68% accuracy even when the supervised labels are not saliently present, suggesting useful semantic generalization.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The work is well-motivated, clearly positioned with respect to multi-agent RL and emergent communication, and adds novel emphasis on human interpretability and grounding with real-image inputs at scale.
- The claims are supported by consistent empirical results (successful coordination, improved semantic purity, and human decoding), and the analysis identifies environmental/design factors that nudge emergent codes toward human-like semantics.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a core AI goal—learning to communicate to coordinate—and appropriately critiques the limitations of purely supervised language learning for interactive use. It is well-situated in the literature (cheap talk, prior emergent communication in deep MARL) and articulates why interpretability and portability to humans matter.
- Technical approach: The referential game is standard yet sound; the discrete symbol bottleneck and REINFORCE training are appropriate. The informed sender’s inductive bias is a reasonable, testable design choice.
- Empirical evidence: Agents reach near-perfect coordination across settings. Symbol usage is nontrivial (not collapsed), with analyses (purity > chance, SVD spectrum not rank-1/2, t-SNE color coherence) indicating alignment with semantic structure rather than low-level artifacts. The object-level variant isolates abstraction by removing shared low-level cues, improving purity as hypothesized. The mixed supervised/self-play setup substantially boosts purity and yields directly interpretable symbols. The human evaluation on ReferIt (new domain) is the most compelling result: 68% accuracy with statistical significance demonstrates that the induced code has partially human-aligned semantics beyond the supervised label set.
- Novelty: Relative to prior MARL communication work, novelty lies in (i) using large, noisy natural images across hundreds of concepts, (ii) experimentally identifying environmental factors (common knowledge manipulation) that encourage more human-like semantics, and (iii) explicitly grounding via interleaved supervision and validating human interpretability.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The methodology and training protocol are described with key hyperparameters, and the evaluation logic is easy to follow.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not necessarily part of the accept decision)
- Rigor and reporting:
  - Provide full quantitative tables (means, standard deviations/CI across seeds) for success rates, purity, symbol usage, and SVD spectra; include statistical tests for purity gains (observed vs. randomized baseline).
  - Clarify training iterations (50k vs. “10k training games”) and test protocol (sampling vs. argmax at test; evaluation horizon).
  - Report variance reduction techniques (e.g., baselines) for REINFORCE, sample efficiency (learning curves with confidence bands), and sensitivity to temperature τ and vocabulary size.
- Task difficulty and generalization:
  - Analyze within-category distractors (fine-grained discrimination), not only across-category pairs, and report how this impacts symbol semantics and usage.
  - Test partner-swap generalization (train multiple agents independently, then pair unseen sender/receiver) to probe conventionality/portability of the emergent code.
  - Evaluate zero-shot generalization to unseen object categories and images.
- Baselines and ablations:
  - Compare to non-interactive baselines (e.g., supervised classifier-based sender trained only on labels; sender trained with pretrained word embeddings) to isolate the contribution of self-play.
  - Ablate the architectural inductive bias (e.g., replace informed convs with MLP of matched capacity), symbol sampling (Gumbel-softmax vs. REINFORCE), and supervised mixing ratio.
  - Analyze the mapping between symbols and categories with mutual information and confusion matrices to quantify synonymy and polysemy beyond SVD.
- Human evaluation:
  - Detail pair selection and guard against selection bias. Report per-word accuracies, inter-annotator agreement, and alternative prompts (e.g., free-text rationales).
  - Include a baseline where humans see only images (no word), or a random word condition, to contextualize 68%.
- Presentation:
  - Fix reference formatting issues. Include figure/table captions with precise metrics and scales. Release code/data splits for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Training details: Did you use a variance-reducing baseline or entropy regularization in REINFORCE? What is the impact of τ and message sampling vs. argmax at test on learning and purity?
- Generalization: How do agents perform with within-category distractors? Do symbols become finer-grained (subcategories) under such pressure?
- Partner portability: If you train multiple pairs independently and then cross-pair senders/receivers, how well do they coordinate without finetuning?
- Supervision mix: How sensitive are results to the ratio of supervised to self-play updates and to the size/choice of the supervised label set? Does sharing vs. not sharing the game-embedding layer change outcomes?
- Human study: How were the 298 pairs selected exactly? What was the accuracy when the supervised label was not present vs. present? Can you provide per-symbol accuracy and whether frequency correlates with interpretability?
- Symbol capacity: With larger vocabularies (e.g., 1k), does symbol usage saturate or fragment into synonyms? How does purity scale?
- Visual features: Do results hold when fine-tuning the visual encoder end-to-end, or with different backbones? Does the object-level variant still improve purity in that setting?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, technically sound study with novel empirical insights on shaping emergent communication toward human-aligned semantics, supported by both automatic analyses and human evaluation.
