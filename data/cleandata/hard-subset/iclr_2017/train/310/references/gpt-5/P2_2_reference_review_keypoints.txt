- Problem: Train tabula rasa agents to develop a communication protocol in a referential game over real images and identify factors that make emergent codes human-like and portable.
- Contribution: Show robust emergence of communication between neural agents on real images using interaction-only reinforcement with two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed).
- Contribution: Demonstrate that a simple inductive bias (informed sender’s dimension-wise comparisons) speeds convergence and yields richer vocabulary and semantic structure.
- Contribution: Show environmental design (different-image, same-class targets/distractors) reduces low-level common knowledge and nudges symbols toward higher-level semantics.
- Contribution: Propose hybrid training (self-play + supervised image naming) to ground symbols in natural language, improving category purity and human interpretability.
- Contribution: Human study: humans pick the correct target using the agent’s word at 68% accuracy on out-of-domain images, indicating meaningful transfer and pragmatic/metonymic use.
- Significance: Connects emergent communication and cheap-talk to scalable, realistic setups and proposes light supervision as a path to human interpretability.
- Decision: Weak accept — well-motivated and empirically supported work, moderate novelty but compelling combination of realistic inputs, environment tweaks, and human grounding.
- Supporting argument: Paper is well-situated in literature (emergent communication, cultural evolution, cheap-talk) and emphasizes interaction-based learning over canned supervised dialog.
- Supporting argument: Empirical results show near-perfect coordination, faster convergence and richer codes for informed sender, and multi-dimensional structure indicated by SVD.
- Supporting argument: Semantic analyses (purity, t-SNE) reveal non-trivial alignment and a constrained-vocab living vs non-living split; object-level variants probe common-knowledge effects.
- Supporting argument: Hybrid grounding raises purity to ~70% and yields 47% direct symbol-label matches on supervised categories; logistic regression supports human study significance.
- Limitation: Statistical robustness lacking—many results without variances or multiple random seeds; needs error bars and repeated runs.
- Limitation: Evaluation depth limited—purity is coarse; add quantitative metrics (AMI, V-measure, MI), symbol entropy, synonymy/homonymy, effective vocab size.
- Limitation: Scope limited to single-symbol messages; no multi-step interaction, compositionality, or partner portability (zero-shot partner-swap) tests.
- Limitation: Reproducibility details (RL stability, temperature, hyperparameter sensitivity) are thin and references formatting needs cleanup.
- Suggestions: Report means/variances over seeds and confidence intervals for key metrics and learning curves.
- Suggestions: Strengthen semantic evaluation with AMI, V-measure, MI, symbol entropy, synonymy/homonymy rates, and zero-shot generalization tests.
- Suggestions: Test partner portability (independently trained agents, role swaps) and receiver calibration on new senders.
- Suggestions: Ablations on temperature, embedding/vocab sizes, architectural components, and supervision schedule/overlap size.
- Suggestions: Expand human study reporting (Fleiss’ kappa, per-word accuracies, baselines) and show failure examples to diagnose errors.
- Suggestions: Explore multi-symbol sequences or multi-step interaction for compositionality; clarify object-level reference setup and matched low-level statistics.
- Questions for authors: Report robustness across seeds, sensitivity to τ/vocab/embedding, persistence of informed-sender advantage, partner-swap learning speed, zero-shot communication between independently trained agents.
- Questions for authors: How much supervision is minimally required for interpretability, and does hybrid training improve transfer to unseen categories/domains?
- Questions for authors: Can you quantify synonymy/homonymy and effective vocabulary size, and provide per-symbol human accuracies and inter-annotator agreement?
- Questions for authors: Could simple visual baselines explain part of the 68% human accuracy, and will code/features be released for reproducibility?
