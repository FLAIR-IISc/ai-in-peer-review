1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies how discrete communication protocols emerge between neural agents in referential games with realistic image inputs, and, crucially, how environment and training choices shape the semantics and human-interpretability of the learned codes. The authors:
- Introduce a two-agent referential game over natural images (ImageNet/VGG features), with discrete symbol messages trained via REINFORCE.
- Compare two sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed with dimension-wise comparisons) and show both achieve near-perfect coordination, with the informed sender converging faster and using a richer, less redundant symbol inventory.
- Analyze the semantics of emergent symbols using cluster purity against McRae categories and visual-space structure (t-SNE), finding above-chance alignment and, intriguingly, a living/non-living split when only two symbols are used.
- Modify “common knowledge” by showing sender/receiver different images from the same class to promote object-level semantics, yielding improved alignment.
- Combine self-play with supervised object naming to ground symbols in natural language, leading to higher purity, broader symbol usage, and improved human interpretability. In a crowdsourced test on ReferIt bounding-box pairs, human participants identify the target image given the agent’s word 68% of the time (vs. 50% chance), with qualitative evidence of metonymic use.

Overall, the paper makes a clear, concrete step toward designing environments that foster human-aligned emergent communication and demonstrates a practical path to grounding via lightweight supervision.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated, timely question: how to steer emergent communication toward human-interpretable semantics, with clear, testable design levers (architectural bias, common-knowledge manipulation, mixed supervised/self-play training).
- Empirical support is convincing and includes a human study; results go beyond mere coordination to demonstrate semantic alignment and transfer to human partners.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: The paper explicitly targets portability of emergent language to new settings and partners (especially humans) and proposes principled interventions to promote that goal.
- Positioning: Builds on multi-agent communication work (Sukhbaatar, Foerster) and classic signaling-game literature while moving to realistic image inputs and human interpretability—a meaningful advance. The “common knowledge” angle is a thoughtful bridge to game theory.
- Methodological soundness: The referential game is cleanly specified. Discrete messages and REINFORCE are appropriate. Architectural comparison isolates the role of an inductive bias. The manipulation of common knowledge is well-motivated and experimentally grounded.
- Evidence: Multiple measures (coordination success, symbol usage diversity, SVD spectrum, category-purity vs. chance, visual-space coherence) triangulate that agents aren’t merely memorizing or exploiting trivial cues. The human evaluation, although modest, is a valuable, seldom-seen component in this literature and demonstrates partial “plug-and-play” with humans.
- Scope and realism: Natural images and large concept sets increase ecological validity relative to synthetic data.

Limitations (not fatal):
- Results are largely descriptive; some key numbers (purity scores, variance across runs) are only qualitatively reported in the text excerpt. Statistical rigor (confidence intervals, seeds) could be strengthened.
- The protocol uses single-symbol messages; compositionality and longer-range interactions are not explored.
- Baselines for human interpretability (e.g., direct supervised classifier labels without interactive training; bag-of-visual-concepts) are missing.
- Generalization across partners (e.g., swapping sender/receiver or new agents) is not evaluated.
- The human study is relatively small and constrained to pairwise choices.

4) Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not central to decision)
- Report full quantitative results with variability: number of seeds, mean ± std for coordination accuracy, purity, symbol usage, and SVD rank; include chance baselines and effect sizes.
- Add ablations: temperature τ, vocabulary size, embedding dim, variance-reduction for REINFORCE, and the impact of the sender’s inductive bias.
- Include partner-robustness tests: zero-shot communication with a new receiver; cross-play between independently trained agents.
- Baseline human study: compare to words produced by a purely supervised image classifier and to random ImageNet labels matched by frequency. This would strengthen the claim that interaction, not just labeling, drives interpretability.
- Explore minimal sequence length (>1) and whether compositional patterns emerge; analyze synonymy/homonymy with information-theoretic measures.
- Analyze the “living vs. non-living” split more systematically (e.g., with external ontologies), and provide examples of learned symbol-object associations.
- Clean up and complete the references; share code and data splits for reproducibility.

5) Questions for the authors
- How many random seeds were used? What is the variability in purity, symbol usage, and convergence speed across seeds and architectures?
- Did you use any variance-reduction baseline in REINFORCE? How sensitive are results to τ and sampling vs. argmax at test time?
- Can receivers trained with one sender decode messages from a different, independently trained sender (cross-play)? Does mixed supervised training improve cross-play?
- What are the exact purity numbers (obs vs. chance) for all conditions (agnostic/informed; 10 vs. 100 symbols; image- vs. class-level; with supervision)?
- In the human study, what are the per-word accuracies and inter-annotator agreement? How does accuracy vary with the “semantic distance” between the emitted word and the target content?
- Does increasing message length or allowing multi-round interaction further improve category purity and human interpretability?
- How does performance change when scaling to more than two candidate images per game?

This paper offers a thoughtful, empirically grounded step toward human-aligned emergent communication and merits acceptance.
