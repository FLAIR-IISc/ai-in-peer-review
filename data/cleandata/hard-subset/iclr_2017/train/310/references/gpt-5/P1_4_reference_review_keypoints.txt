- Study of emergent discrete communication between neural agents in a two-player referential game with real images, asking if agents can learn to communicate and which factors yield human-aligned, interpretable codes.
- Proposes two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed) with an informed sender biased to compare image embeddings dimension-wise.
- Trains sender/receiver with REINFORCE on VGG image features and enforces a one-symbol discrete communication bottleneck.
- Evaluates emergent semantics via category purity (McRae categories), t-SNE visual-semantic alignment, an object-level “remove common knowledge” variant, and mixed supervised/self-play grounding.
- Conducts a human study (ReferIt-derived pairs) showing a mixed-supervision sender achieves 68% human-decoding accuracy and plausible metonymic interpretations.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reasons for acceptance: clear framework and empirical evidence that environment and architecture influence emergence and human interpretability.
- Main reasons for acceptance: mixed supervised/self-play grounding plus human evaluation are novel and impactful for human-compatible emergent language.
- Significance/positioning: focuses on emergent communication usable with new partners and uses natural images, increasing ecological validity and portability.
- Technical approach: simple referential game isolates key variables; discrete one-symbol protocol and REINFORCE are appropriate; informed sender speeds convergence and enriches symbol use.
- Evidence — communication success: near-perfect coordination across settings shows learnability and stability.
- Evidence — semantics beyond low-level cues: category purity significantly above chance and t-SNE analyses indicate symbols capture higher-level conceptual properties.
- Evidence — coarse semantic split: two-symbol policies reveal a living vs non-living division, aligning with cognitive findings.
- Evidence — environment manipulation: showing different images of the same class to sender and receiver increases category purity, nudging codes toward abstract properties.
- Evidence — grounding: mixed supervised/self-play increases symbol purity (~70%), yields ~47% exact label-symbol alignment on supervised categories, and partially transfers interpretability to human partners.
- Broader impact: identifies actionable design levers (inductive biases, common-knowledge manipulation, light supervision) to improve interpretability of emergent languages.
- Suggested baselines/ablations: compare to non-communicative/trivial strategies and “cheating” baselines to contextualize gains.
- Suggested method variants: report results with alternative discrete relaxations (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) and REINFORCE variance-reduction baselines to assess sample efficiency and stability.
- Suggested hyperparameter sweeps: systematically vary temperature and vocabulary size and test argmax vs sampling at test time.
- Statistical rigor: add confidence intervals and multiple random seeds for coordination, symbol usage, and purity metrics; clarify SVD analysis details.
- Dependence on pretrained vision: discuss how much semantics come from VGG features vs the game; consider experiments with lower-level features or frozen vs fine-tuned encoders.
- Generalization tests: run partner-swap experiments and population convergence tests, and quantify zero-shot category interpretability.
- Human study improvements: report per-word/per-category breakdowns, inter-annotator agreement, and test whether short multi-symbol messages boost interpretability.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to temperature and decoding policy, use of variance-reduction for REINFORCE, interference in mixed supervised setting and effects of shared vs separate embeddings.
- Questions for authors: consistency of emergent codes across random seeds and runs, and whether informed-sender benefits replicate with other inductive biases.
- Questions for authors: in the object-level variant, whether purity improvements concentrate in supervised categories or generalize broadly, and request to release code and exact splits for reproducibility.
