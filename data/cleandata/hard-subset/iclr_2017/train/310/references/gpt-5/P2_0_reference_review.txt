1) Summary of contributions
The paper investigates how natural-language-like communication can emerge from scratch between neural agents through cooperative referential games using real images. It makes three main claims: (i) tabula rasa sender/receiver agents can reliably learn to communicate to solve a 2-way reference game over thousands of ImageNet-derived images; (ii) properties of the environment and agent architecture shape the emergent “lexicon,” with evidence that symbols align with high-level conceptual categories (e.g., living vs. non-living) rather than low-level visual features; and (iii) combining self-play with supervised object labeling grounds the learned code in human language and increases interpretability, as validated by a human study showing that humans can often use the agents’ produced words to identify targets in new, out-of-domain images (ReferIt). The paper also explores an “informed sender” inductive bias that encourages dimension-wise comparison, shows that limiting common knowledge (by showing different exemplars of the same class to sender/receiver) promotes more abstract reference, and conducts analyses of symbol usage (purity vs. McRae categories, SVD of symbol–pair usage).

2) Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated task design linking emergent communication to natural images and human interpretability, with a simple but clear method to increase “semanticity” via environmental tweaks and supervised grounding.
- Empirical results convincingly show robust emergence of communication, increased category-level alignment, and some success in human-agent communication.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a central question—how to induce communicative behaviors that are portable to new situations/partners—arguing (persuasively) that interaction is necessary to capture functional aspects of language beyond supervised sequence modeling. The work is positioned against multi-agent communication without explicit protocols, Wizard-of-Oz/scripting, and human-in-the-loop approaches, and aims for scalable self-play with optional human involvement. This is well placed in the literature (Lewis signaling games, cheap talk, deep RL, emergent communication).

- Approach: The referential game is clear and minimal, enabling controlled study of what properties of the game/architecture influence the emergent code. The “informed sender” bias (dimension-wise comparisons) is a plausible inductive bias; the “object-level reference” tweak to remove low-level common knowledge is conceptually grounded in game theory; and the mixed supervised/self-play regime is an intuitive grounding mechanism inspired by AlphaGo.

- Evidence for claims:
  - Coordination: Agents quickly reach near-perfect accuracy across conditions; the informed sender converges faster and uses more symbols.
  - Semantics: Above-chance purity of symbol–object clusters vs. McRae categories; emergence of a living vs. non-living split with only two symbols; t-SNE visualizations show symbol coherence in CNN feature space; SVD suggests multi-dimensional structure beyond trivial synonymy. The object-level variant boosts purity, matching the hypothesis that removing low-level common knowledge forces more abstract reference.
  - Grounding and human interpretability: Supervised labels substantially increase purity and symbol usage; the sender often picks the exact conventional label for supervised categories and, importantly, exhibits productive “metonymic” usage on ReferIt images not containing supervised categories. In a human study, participants pick the intended target 68% of the time (vs. 50% chance), with a significant logistic regression effect.

Overall, the results are coherent and aligned with the hypotheses.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Rigor and reporting:
  - Include confidence intervals/variance across multiple random seeds for coordination accuracy, purity, symbol usage, and SVD dimensionality; the current presentation emphasizes point estimates.
  - Detail the SVD analysis (data matrix construction, cross-validation protocol, and how “50 dimensions” was selected). Consider alternative measures such as topographic similarity (correlation between distances in meaning and form spaces) and mutual information to quantify structure and ambiguity.
  - Provide more statistical detail on the human study (effect size, CI, left/right balancing, random word controls, inter-rater variability, exclusion criteria). Consider adding baselines (random word, highest-frequency word, or a non-grounded agent).
  - Clarify train/test splits for images and concepts, and whether supervised labels overlap with game concepts at test time. Analyze generalization to unseen concepts.

- Baselines and ablations:
  - Compare to non-communicative or trivial protocols (e.g., fixed symbol mapping to the receiver’s argmax of a pre-trained classifier) to isolate the benefit of emergent coordination.
  - Analyze the effect of message bottleneck size and temperature/entropy regularization; report if variance reduction (e.g., baselines) was used in REINFORCE and training stability across runs.
  - Evaluate scalability: more distractors, multi-symbol messages, or multi-round interaction. This would probe compositionality and robustness.

- Claims and scope:
  - Temper claims about “natural language resemblance”—the code is single-symbol and task-specific; the 68% human success is promising but modest. Frame as a meaningful but early step.
  - Discuss potential confounds: because image features are from VGG trained on ImageNet, category-level structure may reflect pretraining rather than purely emergent semantics. Your object-level control helps, but this point warrants explicit discussion.

- Reproducibility:
  - Provide code or detailed hyperparameters (including optimizer, learning rates, baselines, annealing schedules) and the exact 100 supervised labels.

Questions for the authors
- How stable are the emergent lexicons across random seeds? Do symbol–category associations align, up to permutation, or vary widely?
- How is purity computed exactly (micro vs. macro averaging)? What is the chance baseline and how is it estimated? Please report CIs and significance.
- What variance reduction techniques (e.g., learned baseline, reward-to-go) were used with REINFORCE, and how sensitive are results to temperature τ and embedding dimensionality?
- In the mixed supervision regime, do agents overfit to supervised classes? How do they perform when all referents at test time are unseen categories?
- How does performance and purity change with more than two distractors, or when messages are sequences? Do you observe more compositional patterns?
- Can you quantify synonymy/homonymy (e.g., entropy of symbol given object and vice versa) and topographic similarity?
- In the human study, did you include control conditions (random words, words from a non-grounded agent) and position balancing to rule out biases?

Overall, this is a timely and well-motivated step toward interactive, human-aligned emergent communication with real images. Strengthening statistical rigor, baselines, and generalization analyses would solidify the paper.
