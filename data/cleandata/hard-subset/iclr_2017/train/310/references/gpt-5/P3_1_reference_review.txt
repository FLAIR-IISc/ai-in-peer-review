Summary and contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in multi-agent referential games with real images and asks two focused questions: (i) can tabula rasa agents learn to communicate successfully; and (ii) which environmental/architectural factors nudge the emergent code toward human-interpretable, concept-level semantics. The core setup is a Lewis-style signaling game between a sender and a receiver using discrete symbols. Agents use fixed CNN features (VGG) and are trained with REINFORCE. Two sender architectures are compared (agnostic vs “informed” with a dimension-wise inductive bias). The paper proposes two interventions to encourage abstraction and interpretability: (a) “object-level reference,” where sender and receiver see different images of the same class to remove low-level common knowledge; and (b) mixed supervised training for object naming alongside self-play, aimed at grounding the code in natural language. The empirical evaluation measures coordination success, symbol usage statistics, purity of symbol-object clusters w.r.t. McRae categories, qualitative t-SNE visualizations, and a small human study on ReferIt images showing 68% human success in replacing the receiver. Overall, the paper’s positive message is that simple agents reliably learn to communicate, modest semantic structure emerges (e.g., living vs non-living), and adding supervision increases semantic purity and partial human interpretability.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons: (1) The empirical analysis supporting the central claims about semantic abstraction and human-aligned interpretability is suggestive but not sufficiently rigorous or comprehensive; (2) important experimental details and ablations are missing (training stability, variance, baselines, cross-play), limiting confidence in robustness and generality; (3) novelty relative to contemporary emergent communication work is incremental, with limited theoretical insight or stronger tasks.

Supporting arguments
- Evidence for “natural” semantics is thin. Purity vs McRae categories is above chance but still low (and depends on majority symbol per object), and t-SNE plots are anecdotal. Stronger, standard measures (e.g., topographic similarity/correlation between distances in visual space and message space, mutual information analyses) are absent. The SVD analysis of symbol–pair usage is hard to interpret as evidence of meaning dimensionality.
- Human interpretability results have potential selection bias and limited scope. The ReferIt user study samples three successful games per symbol from cases where the trained receiver already succeeded. This likely over-samples “easy” pairs. The 68% success is promising but modest, and the setup doesn’t quantify how the sender’s messages relate to human referring expressions beyond a small overlap. No comparison to plausible baselines (random word, nearest supervised label by CNN similarity, or a supervised discriminative listener) is provided.
- Generalization and portability are under-explored. The paper’s motivating question is portability to new partners and situations, yet there is no cross-play (partner swap between independently trained agents), population training, or evaluation of zero-shot generalization to unseen objects/categories. The “object-level reference” tweak is a step, but still within the same label space and with shared inductive biases.
- RL methodology is under-specified. REINFORCE without discussion of variance reduction, entropy regularization, or sensitivity to temperature suggests potential brittleness. Results are reported from single runs with no variance across seeds. It is unclear how often training fails, how quickly convergence occurs on average, or how architectural and hyperparameter choices affect stability.
- Comparisons and baselines. There is no direct comparison to concurrent differentiable communication approaches (e.g., continuous messages) or supervised discriminative models, nor to prior emergent-communication setups on synthetic inputs. The claimed novelty lies in using real images and measuring interpretability, but the analysis lacks breadth to firmly attribute improvements to the proposed interventions.
- Scope of task. The signaling game is very simple (single symbol, two images); claims about language emergence and function are necessarily limited. There is no evidence of compositionality or of systematic generalization beyond category-level associations.

Constructive feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen evaluation of semantics:
  - Report topographic similarity between visual distances and message distances; category-level confusion matrices; mutual information between symbols and semantic attributes (e.g., living/non-living, superordinate categories).
  - Replace or complement t-SNE with quantitative clustering metrics and nearest-neighbor consistency.
  - Analyze effective vocabulary size (entropy) and synonymy/homonymy explicitly.
- Improve human study rigor:
  - Avoid selection on successful trials; sample uniformly over game trials or stratify by difficulty.
  - Add baselines: random symbol, nearest supervised label by CNN features, or a supervised discriminative listener; compare against these in the human task.
  - Report inter-annotator agreement and per-word breakdown to identify systematic patterns.
- Address portability:
  - Cross-play between independently trained agents and with partner swaps mid-training.
  - Population-based training to see whether conventions stabilize and become more interpretable.
  - Generalization to unseen object classes and to new distractor distributions.
- RL details and robustness:
  - Report results over multiple seeds, with means/variances; include learning curves.
  - Describe baselines (e.g., learned value baselines), entropy bonuses, and temperature sensitivity.
  - Clarify test-time decoding (argmax vs sampling) and its impact on success.
- Ablations:
  - Compare VGG softmax vs fc features systematically; analyze the role of the “informed” inductive bias.
  - Vary vocabulary size and communication bandwidth to study pressure for abstraction.
  - Explore multi-symbol messages to probe compositional behavior.
- Presentation:
  - Clean up references and formatting; ensure all citations are complete and accurate.
  - Provide more details on dataset splits and exact counts; release code and evaluation scripts.

Questions for the authors
1. How robust are your results across random seeds and hyperparameters (temperature, embedding size, learning rate)? What is the variance in coordination success and purity?
2. Do independently trained agents coordinate with each other (cross-play)? If not, does supervised grounding improve cross-play?
3. Can you report topographic similarity and mutual information analyses to quantify alignment between visual space and message space?
4. How were the ReferIt pairs sampled for the human study, and what happens if you sample uniformly across all trials? Can you add baseline conditions?
5. Does the emergent code generalize to unseen object classes or to novel combinations of attributes? Any evidence of compositionality with multi-symbol messages?
6. What variance-reduction techniques did you use in REINFORCE? Did you test entropy regularization or Gumbel-Softmax relaxations, and how do these affect symbol usage and interpretability?

Despite the borderline decision, the problem is important, the setup is clean, and the qualitative insight that removing low-level common knowledge and mixing in supervised grounding nudges emergent semantics upward is valuable. Strengthening the empirical rigor and generalization claims could make this a solid contribution.
