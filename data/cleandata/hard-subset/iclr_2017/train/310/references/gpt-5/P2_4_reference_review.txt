Summary and contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in a two-agent referential game with real images. Agents (sender/receiver) start tabula rasa and learn a discrete, one-symbol protocol via REINFORCE to coordinate on identifying a target image among two candidates.
- Two sender architectures are compared: an “agnostic” MLP and an “informed” architecture with a per-dimension comparison bias. Agents reliably converge to high accuracy quickly; the informed sender learns faster and uses a larger portion of the vocabulary.
- The authors probe whether the emergent code aligns with human-relevant semantics by analyzing symbol–object associations: cluster purity with respect to McRae semantic categories is above chance; a living vs non-living split emerges even with only two symbols.
- A modified “object-level” variant removes low-level common knowledge by showing sender/receiver different images of the same class, nudging the code toward higher-level semantics and increasing purity.
- To ground communication in human language, the sender jointly trains on a supervised object-labeling task (subset of ImageNet labels), sharing the embedding with the game. This increases symbol purity and yields directly interpretable symbols. A crowdsourced 2AFC study shows humans can select the intended referent 68% of the time when shown the sender’s emitted “word,” even on images outside the supervised label set, suggesting meaningful generalization and metonymic use.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, well-motivated question with a simple, scalable setup using realistic inputs; (2) Evidence that environment design and light supervision push emergent codes toward human-interpretable semantics, including a human study; (3) Methodologically sound and likely to spur follow-up work on grounding and alignment.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles when and how emergent communication in cooperative AI aligns with human-understandable semantics—highly relevant to building interactive agents. It is well-situated with respect to multi-agent communication, signaling games, and language emergence, and it adds a practical grounding angle.
- Approach: The referential game is simple yet effective. The inductive bias of the informed sender is reasonable for a discrimination game. The “object-level reference” manipulation is a neat, game-theoretically motivated intervention to remove low-level common knowledge and encourage higher-level meanings.
- Claims and evidence: Claims that agents reliably learn to communicate are well supported. The semantic alignment claims are supported by multiple probes: above-chance cluster purity; t-SNE colorings correlating symbol usage with visual similarity; and the human study demonstrating interpretability in a 2AFC setup with robust significance (z = 16.75, p < 1e-4). The grounding-by-supervision intervention plausibly explains the dramatic increase in purity and human interpretability, and the generalization to ReferIt images is an important positive result.
- Rigor: Training details are provided (architectures, temperature, batch size, iterations, vocabulary sizes). The use of pre-extracted VGG features is standard and keeps the focus on communication. The human study has reasonable size (≈3k judgments) and analysis.

Limitations (do not preclude acceptance but temper claims)
- Evaluation breadth: Success is measured in 2AFC. This setting is forgiving and may overestimate language quality; compositionality and sample efficiency are not assessed. Purity is a coarse metric and does not test systematic generalization to novel concepts or compositions.
- Protocol analysis: The SVD-based synonymy analysis is heuristic; more principled information-theoretic metrics (e.g., mutual information between symbols and semantic labels; topographic similarity between symbol and concept spaces) would strengthen conclusions about structure and synonymy.
- Generalization across partners: No “partner swap” tests (zero-shot coordination with a new receiver) are reported; such tests are key to arguing that codes are stable and portable, not idiosyncratic to a paired training dynamic.
- Ablations/sensitivity: Limited exploration of sensitivity to vocabulary size, temperature, RL variance, and inductive bias (informed vs agnostic capacity). The informed sender’s advantage might partly be capacity/bias rather than a fundamentally more “word-like” code.
- Grounding scope: Grounding relies on sharing an embedding with supervised object labels; it is unclear how robust this is to label-set mismatch, polysemy, or when supervised labels are sparse. The 68% human success in 2AFC is promising but still far from natural language usability.
- Reproducibility/clarity: Some citation formatting is broken; code/data availability is not stated.

Actionable feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Add partner-swap experiments (train sender with Receiver A, test with Receiver B, and vice versa) to quantify portability and convention stabilization.
- Include more principled communication metrics: mutual information between symbol and category, topographic similarity, and message entropy; report CIs for purity and usage statistics across seeds.
- Probe compositionality: test generalization to novel category combinations or multiple-attribute discrimination; consider distractor sets that require attribute-level distinctions.
- Provide ablations: (a) capacity-matched informed vs agnostic senders; (b) vocabulary size and temperature; (c) supervision strength (fraction of labeled classes); (d) end-to-end vs fixed features; (e) training with continuous relaxations (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) as a baseline.
- Expand human evaluation: control for word frequency/familiarity, add free-text justification, and test few-shot online human–agent interaction to assess pragmatic adaptation.
- Improve presentation: fix references; detail train/val/test splits; report variance across runs; clarify whether test-time decoding uses sampling vs argmax.

Questions for the authors
- How robust are results across random seeds, especially purity and symbol usage? Please report mean±std over multiple runs.
- Do partner-swap tests maintain high coordination, and does grounding help zero-shot coordination with a new receiver?
- How sensitive are outcomes to vocabulary size and τ? Does purity peak at intermediate bottleneck strengths?
- In the object-level variant, did you control for background correlations across classes that might reintroduce low-level common knowledge?
- Can you report information-theoretic measures (MI between symbols and semantic classes) and topographic similarity between symbol space and concept embeddings?
- In the human study, how were ambiguous or polysemous supervised words handled? Do shorter/more concrete words lead to higher success?
- Does the informed sender’s advantage persist when capacity is matched, or is it primarily an inductive bias effect?
- Will you release code and the list of supervised labels to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution that advances emergent communication toward human-aligned semantics with simple, well-motivated interventions. Weak accept.
