1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in simple referential games with neural agents that start tabula rasa and learn to coordinate via RL. It introduces two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed with a dimension-wise inductive bias) and shows that agents quickly learn to communicate and achieve near-perfect coordination on real images (ImageNet/VGG features). It assesses whether emergent symbols align with human-relevant semantics through (a) cluster purity w.r.t. McRae categories, (b) t-SNE visualizations linking symbol usage to high-level visual similarity, and (c) a manipulation that removes low-level “common knowledge” by showing sender/receiver different images of the same class, nudging more abstract, class-level meanings. Finally, it proposes grounding via mixed training: combining the game with supervised image labeling. This substantially increases symbol purity and yields partial human interpretability, as shown by a small-scale crowdsourced study on ReferIt bounding boxes, where humans pick the target image correctly 68% of the time given the sender’s emitted word. Overall, the work is clear, well-motivated, and presents a simple, insightful framework for studying how environment design and weak supervision can shape emergent communication toward human interpretability.

2) Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- Empirical support for the strongest claims (portability/generalization to new partners and human interpretability) is limited and methodologically thin, with modest human success (68%) under a favorable selection and without strong baselines or ablations.
- The analysis of emergent language is informative but not rigorous enough to substantiate claims about semantic abstraction; comparisons, statistical robustness, and alternative explanations (e.g., reliance on VGG feature idiosyncrasies) are insufficiently controlled.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper seeks environments and training setups that induce agents to develop communication protocols that are portable (to new situations/partners) and interpretable by humans. This is important and well-posed.

Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed? Yes. The framing via Lewis signaling games and cheap talk, connections to emergent communication in ML/RL, and the alignment goal with human semantics are compelling. The “common knowledge” manipulation is a neat idea for encouraging abstraction. The mixed supervised/interactive training draws a sensible analogy to AlphaGo-style grounding.

Does the paper support its claims?
- Coordination: Strong. Multiple architectures reliably reach high accuracy quickly.
- Emergence of higher-level semantics: Moderately supported. Symbol–category purity above chance, t-SNE structure, and a living vs non-living split with tiny vocabularies are encouraging. However, purity is a coarse metric, SVD analysis is anecdotal, and results are presented from single runs without variance. It’s unclear how stable these phenomena are across seeds, vocab sizes, temperatures, or alternative features.
- Environment design effects: The class-level image manipulation increases purity slightly and yields crisper t-SNE plots; this supports the claim qualitatively, but a more systematic quantitative comparison would help.
- Human interpretability/portability: The supervised grounding increases purity markedly and yields interpretable symbol–label correspondences on supervised categories. The ReferIt human study is interesting, but: (i) selection included only trials where the agent receiver succeeded; (ii) there is no baseline (e.g., nearest-word-by-ImageNet classifier or a purely supervised model used as a “sender”); (iii) statistical reporting is minimal beyond a single regression; and (iv) 68% vs 50% chance, while significant, leaves uncertainty about practical communicative adequacy. Generalization to new partners (e.g., swapping in a new receiver or cross-play between independently trained agents) is not tested.

Methodological clarity and rigor: Training details omit variance reduction/baseline for REINFORCE; the effect of temperature and sampling vs argmax at test time is not reported; no confidence intervals across seeds; no ablations on the inductive bias in the informed sender. These gaps limit the strength of the empirical conclusions.

4) Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Robustness and statistics:
  - Report results over multiple random seeds with mean/variance. Include learning curves with CIs.
  - Clarify REINFORCE details (baseline/variance reduction, entropy regularization), temperature schedule, sampling vs argmax at test time, and sensitivity to vocab size and embedding dimension.
  - Provide ablations isolating the informed sender’s inductive bias (e.g., shuffle channels, remove convolution, use MLP with pairwise interactions).
- Stronger analysis of emergent codes:
  - Go beyond purity: report mutual information between symbols and categories; topographic similarity (correlation between distances in input space and symbol space); symbol usage entropy and effective vocabulary size; confusion matrices across categories.
  - Test for compositionality or systematicity (even in this simple setting), e.g., add a third image or structured distractors to see whether symbols combine or remain holistic.
  - Control for VGG feature structure: what happens with random linear projections, lower layers, or CLIP-like features?
- Environment design:
  - Evaluate cross-play: can a receiver trained independently with different seeds decode another sender? This is a strong test of “portability.”
  - Role rotation: does swapping sender/receiver or training with role alternation improve language alignment?
  - Generalization: train on some categories, test on unseen categories; test with out-of-distribution images.
- Human grounding:
  - Include baselines in the ReferIt study: (i) a purely supervised classifier that emits the nearest label; (ii) simple text or visual similarity heuristics; (iii) human upper bound with the actual referring expressions.
  - Remove selection bias: evaluate random game trials, not only successful agent-agent cases.
  - Report inter-annotator agreement, per-word performance, and error typology (e.g., metonymic vs literal).
- Clarifications:
  - How many symbols are effectively used after accounting for synonymy (e.g., via clustering on symbol co-usage)?
  - Do agents latch onto low-level cues (backgrounds) despite the class-level manipulation? Any controls (e.g., image augmentations)?
  - Does mixing supervised labels ever hurt coordination? What is the trade-off as supervised fraction varies?
  - Can you quantify the “metonymy” phenomenon (e.g., nearest-neighbor semantic similarity between emitted word and ground-truth referent)?
- Broader extensions:
  - Multi-step, bidirectional communication and sequences of symbols.
  - Occasional human-in-the-loop during training to further calibrate symbol meanings.

Overall, this is an appealing and clearly presented study with promising insights about shaping emergent communication toward human-aligned semantics. Strengthening the empirical rigor, adding cross-play/robustness tests, and providing stronger human-grounding baselines would significantly increase confidence in the central claims.
