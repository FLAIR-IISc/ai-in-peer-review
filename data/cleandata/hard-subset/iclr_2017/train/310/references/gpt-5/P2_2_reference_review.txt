1) Summary and contributions
- Problem: Train tabula rasa agents to develop a communication protocol in a referential game over natural images, and identify environmental/architectural factors that make emergent codes more human-like and portable, including to humans.
- Contributions:
  - Demonstrate robust emergence of communication between neural agents on a large set of real images using only interaction-based reinforcement signals, with two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed).
  - Show that simple inductive biases (the informed sender’s dimension-wise comparisons) accelerate convergence and increase vocabulary usage and semantic structure.
  - Provide evidence that environmental design (removing low-level common knowledge via different-image, same-class target/distractor) nudges symbols toward higher-level semantics.
  - Propose and test hybrid training (self-play + supervised image naming) to ground symbols in natural language; show that this improves category purity and significantly enhances human interpretability.
  - Human study: even when supervised labels are not present in the images, human participants can use the agent’s emitted word to select the correct target at 68% accuracy, suggesting meaningful transfer and pragmatic/metonymic usage.
- Significance: Connects cheap-talk and emergent communication to practical, scalable setups with natural images, and offers a concrete path to human interpretability via light supervision.

2) Decision
Weak accept.
Key reasons: (i) The approach is well-motivated, situated in the literature, and tackles a timely question (emergent communication with human interpretability). (ii) Empirical results, including a human study, support the core claims that architecture and environment shape semantics and that hybrid training improves interpretability. Novelty is moderate but the combination of realistic inputs, targeted environment tweaks, and human grounding is compelling.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper situates itself well relative to emergent communication (Sukhbaatar, Foerster), cultural language evolution, and cheap-talk games, and distinguishes its goals (natural images, human-aligned semantics, eventual human interaction). The move from canned supervised dialogs to interaction aligns with core arguments in linguistics/philosophy about language use.
- Correctness/rigor of results:
  - Learning to communicate: Near-perfect coordination across settings is clear and expected; informed sender converges faster and uses a richer code. The SVD analysis suggests multi-dimensional structure (though more statistics across seeds would strengthen this).
  - Semantics: Category purity vs McRae categories and t-SNE colorings suggest non-trivial semantic alignment, including a striking two-symbol living vs non-living split under constrained vocabularies. The object-level variant isolating class-level information appropriately tests the role of common knowledge.
  - Grounding: Mixing supervised naming with the game increases purity substantially (to ~70%) and yields direct symbol-label matches 47% of the time on supervised categories. The cross-dataset human study (ReferItGame boxes) demonstrating 68% target choice is the strongest evidence that the code has some human interpretability beyond direct denotations. The logistic regression with random effects supports significance.
- Limitations:
  - Statistical robustness: Many results are presented without variances or multiple random seeds; claims (e.g., purity gains, symbol usage) would benefit from error bars and repeated runs.
  - Evaluation depth: Purity is a coarse metric; additional measures (e.g., adjusted mutual information, entropy of symbol distributions, top-k category alignment, MI between symbols and categories) would solidify claims. t-SNE is illustrative but not quantitative.
  - Scope: Single-symbol messages; no back-and-forth interaction or compositional generalization analysis. Portability across partners (zero-shot partner-swap) is not tested.
  - Reproducibility details: RL stability, temperature choice, sensitivity analyses, and hyperparameter justifications are light.
  - References formatting has numerous issues; clarity would benefit from cleanup.

Despite these, the combination of environment design, architectural bias, and hybrid grounding, plus a human evaluation, meaningfully advances empirical understanding of how to nudge emergent codes toward human-interpretable semantics.

4) Suggestions to improve (helpful; not all decisive)
- Report means and variances over multiple seeds for all key metrics (success rate, purity, vocabulary usage, SVD spectrum mass), and learning curves with confidence intervals.
- Strengthen semantic evaluation:
  - Use adjusted mutual information, V-measure, and symbol-category mutual information.
  - Quantify symbol entropy, synonymy/homonymy rates, and effective vocabulary size.
  - Test generalization to unseen object categories and images (zero-shot per-concept).
- Partner portability: Evaluate communication when pairing agents trained independently, and with role swaps; test calibration on new receivers.
- Ablations:
  - Temperature sensitivity, embedding size, vocabulary size, and the informed vs agnostic architectural pieces (e.g., remove dimension-wise convs).
  - Supervision schedule and label overlap size (how much supervision is needed?).
- Human study: Report subject agreement (e.g., Fleiss’ kappa), per-word accuracies, and baselines (e.g., word frequency or nearest-neighbor in CNN space). Add examples where humans fail to understand the agent to diagnose error modes.
- Expand beyond single-symbol messages toward short sequences or multi-step interaction to probe compositionality.
- Clarify “object-level reference” setup and confirm that low-level leakage is minimized (e.g., matched low-level statistics across paired images).
- Clean and standardize references.

Questions for the authors
- How robust are the main findings across random seeds? Please report variance for success, purity, and symbol usage, and whether convergence ever fails.
- How sensitive are results to τ, vocabulary size, and embedding dimensionality? Does informed sender’s advantage persist across settings?
- Partner-swap: If a new receiver is trained from scratch with a fixed sender, how quickly does it learn? Can two independently trained agents communicate zero-shot?
- Supervision: What is the minimal amount of supervised labels needed to reach the observed human interpretability? How does interpretability scale with label overlap or with out-of-domain labels?
- Generalization: Do symbols learned with hybrid training transfer to unseen categories/visual domains better than pure self-play?
- Semantics: Can you quantify synonymy/homonymy (e.g., many-to-one or one-to-many symbol–category mappings) and effective vocabulary size?
- Human study: What were inter-annotator agreement and per-symbol accuracies? Could a simple visual baseline (e.g., mapping the word to its ImageNet visual centroid) explain part of the 68%?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and preprocessed features to facilitate replication?

Overall, the paper is well-motivated, shows credible empirical support for its claims, and makes a useful step toward human-aligned emergent communication, warranting a weak accept.
