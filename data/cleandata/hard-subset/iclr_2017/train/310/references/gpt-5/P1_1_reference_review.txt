1) Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in a cooperative, two-agent referential game grounded in real images. Agents are trained tabula rasa (with pretrained image encoders) using policy gradients, and must learn to communicate to solve the task.
- It compares two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed with dimension-wise convolutions), showing both learn to coordinate, with the informed sender converging faster and using a richer symbol inventory.
- It analyzes the emergent code: symbol usage correlates with higher-level semantics (category purity above chance; e.g., living vs non-living distinction with small vocabularies), and structure in symbol usage beyond trivial 1–2D solutions (SVD).
- It introduces a simple but insightful manipulation (“object-level reference”) that removes low-level common knowledge by showing sender and receiver different images from the same class, nudging agents toward more abstract, class-level semantics and improving purity/visual alignment.
- It proposes a hybrid training regime alternating self-play with supervised image labeling on a subset of classes, showing this improves semantic purity, increases symbol usage, and importantly yields partial human interpretability. In a human study on ReferIt-derived image pairs (mostly not containing supervised categories), humans pick the intended referent 68% of the time given the agent’s symbol mapped to its supervised label.

2) Decision
Weak accept. Key reasons: (i) clear, well-motivated exploration of how environment and inductive bias shape emergent communication with nontrivial image inputs; (ii) a pragmatic path to human interpretability by mixing supervised grounding with interactive learning, supported by a human study.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem importance and positioning: The paper addresses an active question—how to induce communication protocols that are both useful for coordination and human-aligned—bridging multi-agent RL, representation learning, and language emergence. It is well positioned relative to signaling games and recent neural multi-agent communication work, while emphasizing interpretability and human transfer.
- Methodological clarity: The referential game is specified, policies are simple feed-forward networks with a discrete bottleneck, training is via REINFORCE, and key hyperparameters are given. The “informed sender” introduces a clear inductive bias that plausibly matters for symbol formation.
- Evidence for claims:
  - Success in coordination: Agents reliably reach near-perfect accuracy across settings.
  - Semantics beyond low-level cues: Above-chance cluster purity vs McRae categories and t-SNE colorings suggest symbol usage tracks high-level visual similarity, not idiosyncratic pixels. The object-level variant strengthens this.
  - Nontrivial symbol structure: SVD analysis indicates more than a trivial 1–2 symbol system with synonyms.
  - Human interpretability via hybrid training: Supervision increases purity and leads to interpretable symbol-label alignment (47% exact label match when target label is supervised; chance 1%). Crucially, in a separate dataset with largely unseen categories (ReferIt), humans can interpret the agent’s symbols at 68% (significantly above chance). The observation of metonymic extensions is particularly interesting and linguistically plausible.
- Novelty: While related to contemporary multi-agent communication, the combination of (a) real-image grounding, (b) architectural inductive bias analysis, (c) environment design (object-level common knowledge manipulation), and (d) human-in-the-loop evaluation via hybrid supervised/self-play is a useful step forward.

4) Suggestions to improve the paper (not all impact the decision)
- Rigor and robustness:
  - Report mean ± std over multiple random seeds for success rate, symbol usage, purity, and human accuracy.
  - Provide details on train/test splits to avoid leakage across games; clarify whether object instances/classes are held out.
  - Add baselines: e.g., a sender that transmits the argmax of the pretrained VGG softmax; a receiver trained to map from that baseline to the target; or a continuous-communication baseline.
  - Include variance-reduction details for REINFORCE (baseline/critic) and learning curves with confidence intervals.
- Metrics:
  - Complement purity with normalized mutual information or adjusted mutual information to control for cluster count and imbalance.
  - Report topographic similarity between distances in image space and symbol space to quantify compositional structure.
  - Measure mutual information between symbols and categories, and entropy of symbol distribution.
- Analysis depth:
  - Probe zero-shot transfer to new partners and to unseen categories/classes, including with and without the object-level manipulation.
  - Investigate the effect of vocabulary size, temperature, and embedding dimensionality on code structure and human interpretability.
  - Provide ablations on the informed sender’s convolutional design to isolate which biases help.
- Human study:
  - Detail participant recruitment, QC, compensation, and exact interface. Provide per-symbol sample sizes and inter-annotator agreement.
  - Include chance baselines (50%), per-word performance, and controls to rule out trivial biases (e.g., saliency or object size).
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Clean up references and typos; provide a code link or pseudocode; specify computational budget and training time.

5) Questions for the authors
- How many independent runs were conducted per configuration? What is the variance in coordination accuracy, purity, and symbol usage across seeds?
- How are training and test games split at the level of images and classes? Is there a regime with held-out classes to test generalization of the learned code?
- Did you use a baseline/critic for variance reduction in REINFORCE? If not, how stable was training across seeds? If yes, please specify.
- How sensitive are the results to the temperature parameter and vocabulary size? Do larger vocabularies consistently yield higher purity/human interpretability?
- For the “agnostic vs informed” comparison, can you provide ablations of the convolutional kernel and channel treatment to pinpoint the useful inductive bias?
- In the hybrid setting, what is the ratio of supervised to self-play updates and does this trade-off affect interpretability vs. coordination? Does stronger supervision harm flexibility (e.g., metonymic use)?
- In the human study, how often did humans choose the “wrong” image for systematic reasons (e.g., consistent alternative metonymies)? Can you share qualitative error categories?
- Could you test partner-swapping (train sender with one receiver, test with a new receiver or with humans) to assess language portability beyond the human study?

Overall, this is a timely and well-motivated step toward interpretable emergent communication with real perceptual inputs. With strengthened experimental rigor and broader baselines, it would make a solid ICLR contribution.
