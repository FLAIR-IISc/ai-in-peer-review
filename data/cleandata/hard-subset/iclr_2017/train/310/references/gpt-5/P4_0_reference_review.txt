1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in multi-agent referential games using natural images. Two neural agents (sender, receiver) are trained tabula rasa with REINFORCE to coordinate by exchanging a single discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary. The setup uses real images represented by fixed VGG features, and compares two sender architectures: an agnostic MLP and an “informed” sender with a bias toward dimension-wise comparison of target and distractor embeddings. The paper investigates (a) whether agents learn to communicate reliably; (b) what game and architectural factors yield symbol usages that align with human-relevant semantic structure; and (c) how to encourage human interpretability by combining self-play with supervised object naming. Empirical findings show near-perfect coordination, faster convergence and broader symbol usage for the informed sender, symbol-category alignments above chance (via purity against McRae categories and t-SNE visualizations), improved semantic abstraction when removing low-level common knowledge by showing different exemplars of the same classes to sender and receiver, and increased human interpretability when mixing in supervised object naming (with 47% exact use of the conventional label when applicable and 68% accuracy by human subjects identifying the target given the sender’s word on a different dataset). The work positions itself among cheap-talk signaling games and recent neural emergent communication studies and emphasizes environment design to foster interpretable, human-aligned codes.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated approach with thorough experiments demonstrating both reliable emergent communication and steps toward human interpretability.
- Novel and practical emphasis on environment/inductive biases and mixed supervision to steer emergent codes toward human-relevant semantics, supported by quantitative and human evaluation.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Training conversational agents solely via supervised corpora misses interaction and function. The paper asks how to design multi-agent environments that yield portable, human-aligned communication.
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well-placed within signaling games, multi-agent RL, and emergent communication, and explicitly contrasts with related works (pre-coded protocols, Wizard-of-Oz, or pure self-play) by probing interpretability and grounding with real images and human judgments.
- Technical soundness: The referential game formulation, discrete-symbol bottleneck, and REINFORCE training are standard and appropriate. The architectural comparison isolates the effect of an inductive bias toward feature-wise comparison. Analyses include symbol usage diversity (SVD rank), cluster purity against human semantic categories, and visualization. The “object-level reference” manipulation is a principled way to remove low-level common knowledge, with empirical gains in purity. The mixed supervised/self-play setup is coherent and yields improved purity and interpretable symbol use without harming coordination.
- Empirical support and rigor: Agents reach near-perfect test coordination; symbol-category purity is consistently above chance and increases under class-level manipulation and mixed supervision; the human study on an out-of-domain dataset shows 68% target identification accuracy, statistically validated by a mixed-effects logistic regression. These results substantiate the claims that (a) communication emerges reliably, (b) environment/design choices matter, and (c) simple grounding improves human interpretability.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the decision)
- Clarify training details for reproducibility: optimizer, learning rate, gradient variance reduction (baselines), entropy regularization if any, τ selection/annealing, and whether inference uses sampling or argmax.
- Report variability across random seeds (coordination rate, purity, symbol usage). Include confidence intervals or statistical tests for purity and SVD dimensionality (e.g., permutation tests, bootstrap).
- Provide explicit numbers (means, std) for Table 1/2 in the main text; currently several claims are qualitative.
- Strengthen the “semanticity” analyses beyond purity/t-SNE: e.g., mutual information between symbols and categories, top-k label overlaps, probing for living/non-living and other semantic axes, and controlling for low-level visual similarity.
- Add baselines for the human study: performance of agents trained without the supervised phase on the same human evaluation; and comparisons between agnostic vs informed senders under mixed supervision.
- Discuss the role of fixed VGG features. The “tabula rasa” claim is qualified by leveraging pretrained visual semantics. Consider discussing to what extent the emergent code reflects the VGG representational geometry versus learning from interaction.
- Analyze vocabulary utilization and synonymy: quantify effective vocabulary size (e.g., perplexity, type-token ratio), redundancy (e.g., clustering symbols by usage), and the stability of symbol-meaning mappings across training.
- Reference formatting needs correction; several entries contain placeholders.

Questions for the authors
- Training details: Which optimizer and learning rate were used? Was a REINFORCE baseline or variance reduction employed? Was any entropy bonus used to encourage exploration? At test time, were symbols selected by argmax or sampling?
- Data splits: Are the exact image instances in the test games disjoint from training games? For the “object-level reference” setting, are sender/receiver images different instances drawn per trial and across train/test?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to vocabulary size K, temperature τ, and embedding dimensionality? Did annealing τ affect convergence or symbol stability?
- Stability and seeds: How consistent are purity and human-interpretability metrics across different random seeds?
- Supervised labels: How were the 100 supervised labels chosen? Are they balanced across super-categories? How does performance vary if supervised labels are disjoint from the McRae categories used for purity?
- Human study: What is the baseline accuracy for humans on symbols produced by agents trained without supervised grounding? How were the 298 pairs sampled (to avoid selection bias toward “easier” cases)?
- Architecture: The informed sender nests within the agnostic one, yet shows faster convergence and broader symbol usage. Can the authors quantify the inductive bias’s impact (e.g., with matched parameter counts) and provide ablations (removing the 2×1 filters or combining with residual mixing)?
- Generalization: Do symbol-meaning mappings transfer to new receivers trained from scratch or to humans without exposure? Have the authors tested zero-shot transfer to new object categories?
