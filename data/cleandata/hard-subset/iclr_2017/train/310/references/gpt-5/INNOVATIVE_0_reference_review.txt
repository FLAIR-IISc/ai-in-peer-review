1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a research program for emergent communication via multi-agent referential games with real images. The core contributions are:
- A simple yet effective two-agent “cheap talk” referential game where tabula rasa neural agents learn to communicate using a discrete vocabulary to achieve coordination on natural images (ImageNet-based).
- Architectural exploration showing that an “informed” sender with an inductive bias toward dimension-wise comparison learns faster, uses a richer code, and aligns better with human-relevant semantics than an “agnostic” sender.
- Environmental manipulations (removing low-level common knowledge by showing different instances of the same class to each agent) that nudge agents to discover higher-level, category-like semantics.
- A hybrid setup combining interactive play with supervised object naming to ground symbols in natural language; this substantially increases symbol purity and human interpretability.
- Human evaluation showing that, even when the supervised labels are not present in the images, people can pick the intended target 68% of the time from the sender’s word, suggesting meaningful, flexible (often metonymic) grounding.

I view this as a bold and timely step toward interactive, function-driven language learning that complements passive supervised learning.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Novelty and vision: A compelling, experimentally grounded program for emergent, human-aligned communication using real-world visual inputs and minimal supervision.
- Empirical evidence that environment design and light supervision shape emergent semantics toward human interpretability.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem relevance: The paper tackles how to make emergent communication both effective for coordination and aligned with human semantics—central to building cooperative agents that can interact with humans.
- Motivation and positioning: The work is well-motivated relative to supervised conversation models (which lack interactivity), Wizard-of-Oz/hand-engineered curricula, and recent neural multi-agent communication (RIAL/DIAL). The explicit focus on interpretability and transfer to humans is a valuable differentiator.
- Sound methodology: The setup is clear (REINFORCE over discrete symbols, Gibbs sampling, simple sender/receiver architectures). Choices like using VGG fc vs softmax features are reasonable; the “informed” sender’s inductive bias is well-justified.
- Evidence of success: Agents routinely reach near-perfect coordination. Symbol usage purity is above chance, improves with more informative architectures and with the object-level manipulation, and jumps markedly (to ~70%) when grounded with supervised naming. SVD analysis suggests the code is not trivially low-dimensional. t-SNE visualizations qualitatively support alignment with higher-level semantics.
- Human grounding: The crowd study is the most compelling part—68% target selection vs 50% chance across 298 pairs and 10 raters per pair, even when supervised labels aren’t present in the images. The metonymy-like usage makes the approach promising for early-stage human-agent interaction.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Stronger quantitative analysis: Report confidence intervals or statistical tests for purity gains, vocabulary entropy, and spectrum ranks; include multiple random seeds to assess stability and synonymy/homonymy.
- Baselines and ablations: Compare to continuous-channel communication (e.g., differentiable relaxations), different temperatures, and variance-reduction techniques for REINFORCE. Clarify why τ=10 and how sampling vs argmax at test time affects behavior.
- Generalization and portability: Evaluate cross-play (partner-swap between independently trained agents), zero-shot to unseen categories, and robustness to noise or symbol remapping.
- Compositionality and capacity: The single-symbol protocol is a strong bottleneck; adding multi-symbol or multi-turn interaction would test whether compositional structures emerge and improve interpretability.
- Architectural controls: Analyze why the informed sender helps—are the gains due to pairwise dimension comparisons specifically? Include an ablation that shuffles image order to ensure the sender’s target knowledge is not exploited in a trivial way by the receiver.
- Metrics: Consider topographic similarity or MI between symbolic distances and semantic distances to quantify structure in the code beyond purity.
- Human study details: Report demographics, compensation, and agreement (e.g., Fleiss’ kappa) to bolster the strength of the human-grounding claim.
- Presentation: Clean up references and minor typos; add training curves with standard deviation over seeds; clarify the choice of 100 supervised labels and overlap with the game’s categories.

Questions for the authors
- How robust are the emergent codes across random seeds (vocabulary alignment, purity, human interpretability)? Do independently trained pairs converge to isomorphic languages?
- Cross-play: If you pair a sender with a new receiver trained separately, does communication succeed? Does supervised grounding improve cross-play?
- How sensitive are results to τ and sampling strategies? Have you tried Gumbel-Softmax or variance-reduced REINFORCE?
- What is the effect of message length and multi-turn dialogs? Do you observe any compositional structure when capacity increases?
- In the “object-level reference” setup, can you quantify how much of the purity gain is due to eliminating low-level image cues vs better category abstraction?
- Can you report more details of the human study (number of unique subjects, per-word performance, inter-rater reliability) and provide examples where humans systematically fail?
- Do symbols align with known semantic dimensions (e.g., living vs non-living) across runs? Can you probe what features of the CNN space the code relies on?
- Have you tested zero-shot categories or images beyond ImageNet/ReferIt to assess portability?

Overall, this is an ambitious and promising direction with encouraging early results. Despite some limitations in scope and analysis, the novelty and forward-looking vision merit acceptance.
