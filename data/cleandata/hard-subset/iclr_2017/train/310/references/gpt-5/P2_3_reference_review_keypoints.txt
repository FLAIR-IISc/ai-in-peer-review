- Problem: Design multi-agent environments that induce emergent communication that is both effective for coordination and aligned with human-interpretable concepts in referential games over natural images.
- Approach: Two neural agents (sender/receiver) exchange single-symbol discrete messages, trained with REINFORCE using VGG image features; compare agnostic vs informed sender architectures, vary common knowledge, and mix self-play with supervised object-naming.
- Main claims: (i) agents reliably learn to communicate from scratch; (ii) environment/architecture choices shape semantics and interpretability; (iii) supervised/self-play mixing greatly increases human interpretability and category structure; (iv) partial human-agent communication generalizes out-of-domain.
- Decision: Accept.
- Significance/novelty: Advances grounded, human-aligned emergent communication using realistic inputs and discrete channels; object-level reference manipulation and supervised grounding are insightful.
- Empirical evidence: Strong coordination, semantic organization (cluster purity, living/non-living split), and human interpretability (68% on ReferIt) with clear ablations over architecture, vocabulary, and common knowledge.
- Coordination result: Near-perfect success across settings; informed sender learns faster and uses more vocabulary slots.
- Semantic structure: Above-chance cluster purity in all settings, higher for informed sender, and emergence of living-vs-non-living split even with two symbols.
- Effect of common knowledge: Removing low-level common knowledge (different instances) increases purity and clearer category-level structure.
- Grounding effect: Joint supervised naming increases symbol usage (88/100), purity (~70%), and conventional label usage (47% vs 1% chance); human eval shows pragmatic/metonymic extensions.
- Methodological soundness: Uses standard REINFORCE, realistic embeddings, and analyses including symbol statistics, SVD, cluster purity, and t-SNE.
- Clarity: Paper is clear, accessible, and well-situated in relevant literature.
- Rigor/controls needed: Report variance/confidence intervals over multiple seeds and stronger baselines for human studies (random/frequency-matched labels, inter-annotator agreement).
- Statistical clarity: Clarify statistical tests/intervals for purity improvements and how “effective dimensionality” from SVD is computed.
- Analysis depth: Address context-dependence/pragmatics (mutual information, RSA), investigate cross-play (co-adaptation vs conventionalization), and report robustness (vocab size, temperature, embedding dim, symbol entropy).
- Design-choice checks: Ensure no positional or VGG leakage, justify high temperature and sampling choices, and evaluate alternatives like Gumbel-Softmax or straight-through estimators.
- Supervision schedule: Explore different supervised/self-play ratios and whether grounding only sender or only receiver helps.
- Scope limitations: Single-symbol channel is a bottleneck—brief experiments with multi-symbol sequences or multi-round interaction would strengthen generality.
- Reproducibility: Provide code and data splits.
- Question — cross-play/generalization: Can independently trained agents communicate without fine-tuning, and does supervision improve cross-play?
- Question — context effects: How often does object-to-symbol mapping change with different distractors; can pragmatic flexibility be quantified?
- Question — human study controls: What are accuracies with random or frequency-matched labels and what is inter-annotator agreement?
- Question — statistical reporting: Provide confidence intervals for purity, success, and symbol usage; detail SVD cross-validation procedure.
- Question — temperature/sampling: How sensitive are results to Gibbs temperature τ and to using Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through estimators?
- Question — supervision ratio: How does varying supervised/self-play mix affect purity, usage, and interpretability; does grounding only one agent help?
- Question — within-category distractors: What are success and purity when distractors are from the same category vs different categories?
- Question — representation robustness: Do results hold with different visual embeddings (ResNet, CLIP) or degraded images to suppress low-level cues?
