Summary and contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in two-agent referential games built on real images. Sender and receiver are simple neural networks trained via REINFORCE with a discrete bottleneck (single symbol from a finite vocabulary).
- Two sender architectures (agnostic vs. “informed” with a bias to compare image embeddings dimension-wise) are compared. Agents quickly achieve near-perfect coordination.
- The authors analyze whether emergent symbols align with human-relevant semantics: cluster purity w.r.t. McRae categories is above chance; t-SNE suggests symbol usage correlates with high-level visual similarity. A “common-knowledge” tweak (sender/receiver see different instances of the same class) pushes symbols further toward class-level semantics.
- To improve human interpretability, they interleave game play with supervised image naming on a subset of labels, sharing embeddings between tasks. This increases symbol purity and yields partial human interpretability in a crowdsourced test (68% accuracy in picking the target given the sender’s word on non-overlapping images), with qualitative “metonymic” uses.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Insufficient scientific rigor and clarity to support the central claims (semantics beyond low-level relational cues; human interpretability; generalization). Several confounds remain, statistics are incomplete, and important training/evaluation details are missing.
2) Limited novelty and scope: messages are length-1 symbols; the approach relies on pretrained visual features (not tabula rasa) and tests mostly in-distribution; analysis lacks robustness and ablations. The presentation (references/formatting) is error-prone and below venue standards.

Supporting arguments
- Claims vs. evidence:
  - “Tabula rasa” is overstated: agents rely on VGG features, especially the high-level fc layer, which likely encodes category structure. Demonstrating that symbols reflect conceptual semantics thus requires stronger controls (e.g., lower-level features, randomized features, or end-to-end vision).
  - The claim that symbols capture “general conceptual properties” is confounded by the inherent class information in fc features and by purely relational strategies. In classic referential games, agents can succeed by encoding relative distinctions between the two images rather than absolute, context-independent meanings. The purity analysis aggregates by “most often used symbol per object,” but does not disentangle contextual symbol shifts or quantify context sensitivity.
  - The human evaluation samples only trials where the agent receiver was correct, potentially biasing toward easier cases. The 68% number lacks confidence intervals and comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., nearest-neighbor label of the target image, class-name priors, random word among those used for the pair). The number of unique symbols/words, the per-word performance, and inter-subject agreement are not reported.

- Methodological detail gaps:
  - REINFORCE details are incomplete: optimizer, learning rate, gradient variance reduction (baselines), entropy regularization, reward normalization, and number of seeds. No error bars/variability across runs; figures are described as “a sample” of test set.
  - Test-time decoding is unclear (sampling vs. argmax) and whether temperature τ=10 is used at test. No discussion of sensitivity to τ, vocabulary size, or embedding/filter choices beyond a couple of settings.
  - SVD analysis: the construction of the pair-by-symbol matrix, sampling scheme, and “cross-validated SVD suggests 50 dimensions” are underspecified. No principled model selection criterion or uncertainty is reported.
  - Purity: numeric values, variance across seeds, chance baseline computation, and significance testing procedures are missing (except a few isolated numbers in the supervised condition). The comparison across architectures/vocabularies lacks statistical rigor.

- Limited scope/novelty:
  - Communication is a single discrete token; no sequence, no compositionality analysis, no multi-step interaction. The paper does not probe classic diagnostics (productivity, systematicity, generalization to unseen attribute combinations).
  - Generalization: train/test concepts appear drawn from the same pool; no zero-shot concepts or cross-dataset generalization (except the supervised+ReferIt mix, where supervision vocabulary and game vocabulary remain the same set of symbols).
  - The informed sender’s advantage may stem from an architectural bias aligned with the feature space rather than a linguistic property; ablations to verify the claim (“agnostic nests informed”) are missing.
  - Relation to prior emergent communication work (e.g., discrete bottlenecks, referential games, human interpretability) is discussed but the paper does not clearly position unique technical advances; mixing supervised naming with emergent communication is intuitive, yet the paper does not present new algorithms or theory.

- Presentation/formatting:
  - References contain numerous formatting errors, incomplete entries, duplicated names, missing venues/pages. Several footnotes and figure/table references are awkward or unclear. This impedes verification and is below ICLR standards.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide rigorous statistical reporting: multiple seeds, means/SDs, CIs, and proper significance tests for all key metrics (success rates, purity, human eval). Release code and data splits.
- Control for relational strategies: measure mutual information between symbol and target identity conditioned on distractor; test stability of symbol assignment when swapping distractors or adding foils; evaluate context-independence.
- Strengthen generalization: zero-shot concepts, cross-dataset tests without overlap in classes, and end-to-end vision (or lower-level features) to reduce reliance on pre-encoded semantics.
- Improve baselines: supervised-only classifier as a “labeler” receiver; nearest neighbor label; randomized labels; entropy-regularized sender; bottleneck size sweeps; Gumbel-Softmax vs. sampling.
- Explore richer communication: multi-symbol messages, turn-taking, compositional tasks with controlled concept combinations.
- Clarify the informed sender architecture and show it is indeed nested in the agnostic model via ablation.
- Human eval: pre-register tasks, report per-word performance, inter-annotator agreement, and stronger baselines; avoid selection bias by including all trials or stratify by difficulty.
- Clean up references and figures; provide complete bibliographic details and improve clarity.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you ensure symbols encode absolute meanings rather than relational distinctions? What is symbol stability across varying distractors?
2) What are the exact REINFORCE training details (optimizer, LR, baselines, entropy regularization), temperature at test, and decoding policy? How many seeds and with what variance?
3) How are chance baselines and significance computed for purity and human eval? Please report CIs and per-condition statistics.
4) What is the precise cross-validated SVD procedure and criterion for selecting 50 dimensions?
5) Do you evaluate zero-shot concepts or cross-dataset generalization without label overlap? If not, can you add such experiments?
6) How sensitive are results to feature choice (softmax vs. fc vs. lower conv layers) and to vocabulary size/temperature?
7) Can you quantify context-independence (e.g., mutual information analyses) and provide confusion matrices of symbol-to-class mappings?
