1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in referential games between neural agents using real images as inputs. It introduces a simple but carefully designed two-agent, single-symbol communication game and compares two sender architectures (agnostic vs. “informed”) to examine how inductive biases and environmental design affect the emergent code. Using ImageNet-derived visual embeddings (VGG) over a broad set of 463 object concepts, the authors show: (i) agents rapidly learn to coordinate from scratch via REINFORCE; (ii) the emergent symbols correlate with higher-level semantics (e.g., broad conceptual categories) rather than low-level visual idiosyncrasies, with the “informed” sender producing more semantically aligned symbol usage; (iii) manipulating common knowledge by showing different images of the same class to the two agents increases semantic abstraction; and (iv) mixing self-play with supervised object naming helps ground the emergent code in human language, leading to substantial gains in symbol purity, broader symbol use, and partial human interpretability, including generalization to a new dataset (ReferIt) and to target concepts outside the supervised set. The paper is well-motivated, situated within multi-agent learning, signaling games, and emergent communication, and it provides both quantitative and human evaluation.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper offers a clear, compelling demonstration that architectural and environmental choices shape emergent communication on realistic data, and provides an actionable path toward human-interpretable grounding via mixed supervised/self-play training.
- The empirical evidence is convincing for the main claims (coordination success, semantic alignment trends, and human interpretability), and the study is novel and timely for ICLR.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity and motivation: The work targets how to design environments that elicit portable, interpretable emergent language. The framing via Lewis signaling games and “cheap talk” literature, and pointers to recent multi-agent communication works, are apt and well positioned.
- Empirical correctness and rigor for core claims: Coordination success is robust across settings; the “informed” sender converges faster and uses a broader, less redundant symbol inventory. The purity analyses (vs. McRae categories) and t-SNE visualizations support the claim that symbols track higher-level semantics; the “living vs non-living” split emerging with a small vocabulary is particularly telling. The manipulation of common knowledge (different-image, same-class pairing) appropriately pushes symbols toward class-level semantics. The mixed supervised/self-play approach substantially increases symbol purity and improves human interpretability (68% accuracy with crowdworkers; strong statistical significance), including transfer to data outside the supervised category set—an important step toward grounding.
- Novelty and impact: Using real image embeddings and combining self-play with supervised labels for interpretability are impactful ideas. The focus on designing environments/inductive biases to sculpt the emergent code is a valuable perspective for the community.

Caveats (not decisive): Some claims (e.g., “tabula rasa”) are softened by reliance on pretrained VGG features. Analyses such as SVD and purity are informative but could be complemented with stronger baselines and controls. Details of the RL training (e.g., variance reduction, optimization specifics) could be expanded. Generalization to unseen concepts/partners and more rigorous symbol–semantics diagnostics could be strengthened.

4) Suggestions to improve (constructive; not necessarily decision-critical)
- Baselines and controls:
  - Include non-interactive baselines: a purely supervised classifier producing labels passed to a receiver trained to map labels to targets; a nearest-neighbor or cosine-similarity baseline using VGG features and class centroids; a random symbol baseline with matched symbol frequencies.
  - Report sensitivity to the pretrained features: compare sm vs fc (partially shown), other backbones, and a control with random features to bound how much semantics comes “for free.”
- Evaluation depth:
  - Add metrics like mutual information between symbols and categories, topographic similarity between symbol distances and visual/conceptual distances, and per-run variance (stability across seeds).
  - Clarify train/test splits for images and classes to assess generalization (e.g., unseen images, unseen classes).
  - For human studies, include baselines (random, frequency-matched) and an analysis of error patterns; consider having humans produce words for targets to gauge bidirectional interpretability.
- Learning details and ablations:
  - Provide optimization specifics, learning rates, variance reduction/baselines for REINFORCE, temperature τ sensitivity, sampling vs argmax at test, and message stochasticity effects.
  - Analyze vocabulary size and communication bandwidth; test multi-symbol messages or multi-turn protocols to probe compositionality.
  - Swap partners (train with one partner, test with another) to assess portability.
- Scope and claims:
  - Temper “blank slate” wording given reliance on pretrained visual features.
  - More carefully delineate “natural-language-like” claims, while still highlighting the promising human interpretability results.

Questions for the authors
- Data splits: Are training and test images disjoint? Do you evaluate generalization to unseen classes? How does performance/purity change under such splits?
- RL details: What optimizer, learning rate, and variance reduction (e.g., baselines) are used for REINFORCE? How sensitive are results to τ and to sampling vs argmax at test?
- Stability and variance: How consistent are purity, number of symbols used, and the “living vs non-living” split across random seeds?
- Baselines: How does the human interpretability (68%) compare to a nearest-neighbor baseline using VGG features and the supervised label embeddings, or to a fully supervised sender-receiver pipeline without self-play?
- Partner portability: If you retrain only the receiver or only the sender (or swap partners across runs), does communication persist? This would support claims about portability to new partners.
- Symbol analysis: Can you report mutual information between symbols and categories, and confusion matrices (e.g., which categories share symbols)? Is there evidence of compositional reuse when vocabulary is limited?
- Multi-distractor setting: How do results scale with more than two images, or with harder distractors (same supercategory)?

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated study that makes useful conceptual and empirical contributions to emergent communication and grounding. I recommend acceptance.
