Summary and contributions
The paper studies how communication can emerge between tabula rasa neural agents playing referential games over natural images, and how the environment and training regimen shape the semantics and human-interpretability of the emergent code. Using a sender-receiver setup trained with REINFORCE and a discrete vocabulary bottleneck, the authors show: (i) agents reliably converge to successful communication; (ii) an “informed” sender architecture (with dimension-wise comparisons) learns faster and uses a richer symbol set than an “agnostic” sender; (iii) the induced symbol–referent mapping aligns with high-level conceptual structure (e.g., category-level purity, living vs. non-living), which can be amplified by removing low-level common knowledge (agents see different instances of the same class); and (iv) combining self-play with supervised object naming grounds symbols to human words, yielding a partially human-interpretable code that generalizes to new images and tasks (ReferItGame), with a promising 68% success rate in a human-in-the-loop evaluation. The work connects to cheap-talk games and emphasizes how environmental design can bias languages toward human-like semantics.

Decision
Accept. The paper takes a bold, influential step toward emergent communication grounded in realistic inputs and shows a simple, scalable route to human-interpretability by mixing self-play and supervised grounding.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and significance: While emergent communication in multi-agent RL has been explored, this paper pushes toward realistic perceptual grounding (large set of natural images), explicitly examines how environment design alters semantic alignment, and, crucially, demonstrates human interpretability without requiring humans during training. The combination of self-play and supervised naming is simple yet powerful, and the “remove common knowledge” manipulation is insightful and generative for future work.
- Motivation and placement: The problem (how to get agents to develop portable, human-aligned communication) is clearly articulated. The work is contextualized relative to multi-agent communication (Sukhbaatar, Foerster), Wizard-of-Oz curricula, and classic cheap-talk/game-theoretic analyses. The linguistic/cognitive science grounding is appropriate and motivates the evaluation criteria (category-level semantics, metonymic use).
- Empirical support: Results consistently show convergence and substantial semantic structure. The purity metrics and t-SNE visualizations, SVD analyses suggesting multi-dimensional symbol use, and the human study (68% correct choices, highly significant) support the main claims. The object-level variant (different instances per agent) is a compelling test that increases semantic abstraction. The grounding experiment shows the code’s interpretability extends beyond the directly supervised classes.

That said, there are limitations in rigor and analysis depth that I am willing to overlook given the conceptual contribution:
- The referential task is relatively easy (two images), and success ceilings make it hard to compare methods. The human study, while positive, is modest in scale and not fully controlled.
- Some analyses are qualitative (t-SNE) or use coarse metrics (purity). More robust measures (e.g., mutual information, ARI/NMI, topological alignment between symbol space and semantic distances) would strengthen the case.
- Reproducibility details (variance across seeds, confidence intervals, ablations) are limited. The “informed vs. agnostic” advantage might partly be an optimization effect.
- References and formatting need cleaning.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Evaluation
  - Report means and variance across multiple random seeds for all key metrics (success, symbol usage entropy, purity), and learning curves with CIs.
  - Add harder settings: more distractors, semantically similar distractors, and out-of-domain images.
  - Use stronger quantitative measures: NMI/ARI for clustering, MI between symbols and categories, topographic similarity between symbol distances and semantic distances, communication efficiency (bits), and robustness to channel noise.
  - Analyze partner-swap generalization (train agents separately, pair at test), role rotation, and vocabulary reinitialization to probe portability.
  - Examine compositionality (if any): referent-feature disentanglement, productivity to unseen combinations, multi-attribute references.
- Methodology
  - Provide ablations: vocabulary size, temperature, embedding dims, symbol sampling (e.g., straight-through Gumbel-Softmax), entropy or sparsity regularizers, image features (fc vs sm), and architecture components of the “informed” sender.
  - Quantify the information bottleneck (effective bits used) and symbol usage distribution.
  - Control for order effects (receiver sees random order; ensure sender isn’t encoding positional conventions).
  - Consider multi-message or interactive protocols to explore richer pragmatics.
- Grounding and human study
  - Specify details: subject demographics, quality controls, randomization, per-word accuracies, and inter-subject variability. Include example failures to characterize error modes.
  - Explore whether grounding transfers to unseen labels (zero-shot via semantic similarity) and to different human partners.
- Presentation
  - Clean up references and fix typos. Release code and data splits for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How stable is the emergent lexicon across random seeds? Do different runs converge to similar semantic partitions (up to permutation), and how often?
- Does the informed sender’s advantage persist under tighter controls (e.g., matched parameter counts, alternative optimization settings)? Could the agnostic sender catch up with more training?
- How sensitive are results to the Gibbs temperature and sampling vs. argmax at test time? Did you try straight-through Gumbel-Softmax?
- What is the effective information rate of the channel (e.g., entropy of symbol distribution) and how does it relate to success and purity?
- In the grounding experiment, what is the split between supervised labels and the referential game’s target classes? How much of the 68% human success involves metonymy vs. direct denotation?
- Did you evaluate partner-swap: pair the trained sender with a freshly trained or different receiver (and vice versa) to test language portability?
- How does performance and interpretability change with more than two images, or with semantically confusable pairs?
- Is the receiver invariant to image order? Could the sender be exploiting order conventions?

Overall, despite some evaluation and reporting gaps, the paper opens an exciting and practical direction for emergent communication with human-aligned semantics. I recommend acceptance.
