1) Summary and contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in a two-agent referential game with natural images. Two neural agents (sender/receiver), trained tabula rasa via REINFORCE on VGG image features, learn to communicate a single discrete symbol to identify a target among two images. The paper compares two sender architectures (agnostic MLP vs an “informed” architecture with dimension-wise comparisons), examines the semantics of emergent symbols (category purity against McRae categories, t-SNE visualizations, SVD analysis of symbol–pair usage), and proposes an “object-level” variant that removes low-level common knowledge by showing different instances of the same class to sender and receiver. Finally, it explores grounding by interleaving the game with supervised object naming, and evaluates human interpretability on ReferItGame pairs; humans select the correct image 68% of the time given the sender’s emitted word. The paper claims: (i) tabula rasa agents can coordinate on real images; (ii) environment design shapes symbol semantics toward higher-level properties; (iii) light supervised grounding substantially improves interpretability/human compatibility.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient novelty and rigor relative to existing emergent communication work; (2) Weak empirical methodology and analysis (limited metrics, baselines, and controls), making it hard to substantiate claims about “naturalness” and portability.

3) Supporting arguments
- Positioning and novelty: The referential game setup with neural agents and discrete messages is close to concurrent/earlier works (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.). The paper’s main differentiators—use of large natural images, an “informed” sender bias, and adding supervised grounding—are interesting but incremental. The “object-level” manipulation is conceptually neat but modest in empirical payoff and not rigorously tested.

- Methodological gaps:
  - Metrics: The core evidence for semantic “naturalness” relies on category purity vs McRae labels and t-SNE plots. These are weak proxies. Missing are stronger, standard measures (e.g., mutual information between signals and categories, topographic similarity between semantic and signal distances, speaker/listener consistency, communication rate/entropy, confusion matrices across categories).
  - Baselines/controls: No capacity-matched ablation for the informed vs agnostic sender; unclear if gains stem from inductive bias or parameterization. No analysis of temperature τ, sampling vs argmax, or variance-reduction in REINFORCE. No comparison to simple supervised baselines (e.g., using pretrained labels directly where available). No evaluation of generalization to unseen categories or partner-swap (cross-play) to test portability across trained receivers.
  - Statistical rigor: Purity improvements and SVD “dimensionality” are reported without confidence intervals or significance tests; results across random seeds are not reported. The “living vs non-living” emergence is compelling but anecdotal; quantify its accuracy.
  - Human study: Promising but potentially biased. Pairs are sampled where the agent succeeded, and words come from the supervised label set; 8% of pairs contain that label in human annotations. A stronger design would compare to (a) an ungrounded agent, (b) a purely supervised “name when possible” baseline, and (c) random-label control. Report per-word performance, image-domain shift effects, and inter-annotator agreement.

- Claims vs evidence:
  - “Portable language” and “alignment with human intuitions” are asserted, but portability is only tested via a single human choice task under favorable selection and shared supervised labels. No cross-agent generalization or zero-shot categories.
  - The “informed sender uses more symbols” could reflect synonymy or instability rather than meaningful diversification; SVD analysis is suggestive but not decisive.

- Clarity and reproducibility: Important training specifics are missing (optimizer, learning rates, baselines for REINFORCE, seeds). References have formatting issues. Some architectural details (exact parameter counts, τ meaning) are underspecified.

4) Suggestions for improvement (constructive, not all required for acceptance)
- Quantify semantics with stronger metrics:
  - Mutual information between messages and target categories; topographic similarity (correlation between distances in semantic space and in message space); speaker/listener consistency; entropy/efficiency trade-offs.
  - Confusion matrices across McRae categories; explicit “living vs non-living” classification accuracy of the induced code.
- Robustness and generalization:
  - Cross-play: train multiple agents independently and test zero-shot coordination.
  - Unseen categories and harder distractors (same supercategory).
  - Vary vocabulary sizes and temperatures; report stability over seeds and confidence intervals.
- Ablations and baselines:
  - Capacity-matched comparison between informed and agnostic senders; remove/alter conv bias to isolate its contribution.
  - Supervised baseline: always emit known object name if present; compare human interpretability and agent success.
  - Post-hoc label alignment baseline (map emergent symbols to nearest labels) vs joint supervised grounding.
- Human evaluation:
  - Include ungrounded agent and random-word controls; balance pairs irrespective of agent success; report per-word stats and agreement.
  - Analyze semantic shifts (metonymy) systematically: categorize relations (part-of, scene, affordance).
- Reporting:
  - Provide full hyperparameters, optimization details, and code. Clarify τ parameterization; include learning curves with error bands; report number of seeds and variance.

Questions for the authors
1) How many random seeds were used for each configuration? What is the variance in coordination success, purity, and symbol usage?
2) What optimizer, learning rates, and variance-reduction techniques (baselines/critics) were used for REINFORCE? How sensitive are results to τ and sampling vs argmax?
3) Are the informed and agnostic senders capacity-matched? If not, does matching parameters reduce the gap?
4) Can you report cross-play results (sender trained with receiver A, tested with unseen receiver B) to support portability?
5) Do symbols generalize to unseen object classes? What happens when test categories were held out during training?
6) For the “living vs non-living” observation, can you quantify how accurately a binary classifier trained on the emitted symbols predicts this attribute?
7) In the human study, what is the performance of (a) an ungrounded agent, and (b) a naive supervised baseline (emit label if present, else abstain/random)? How were pairs sampled to avoid bias toward easy cases?
8) How exactly are supervised labels tied to symbols during game play? Is there any parameter tying between the 100-way softmax for labels and the 100-symbol vocabulary, or is alignment emergent via shared embeddings only?
9) Can you provide significance tests and confidence intervals for purity improvements and SVD dimensionality claims?

Overall, the paper is engaging and addresses an important direction—shaping emergent communication toward human-interpretable semantics—but the current empirical rigor and comparative analysis are insufficient for acceptance.
