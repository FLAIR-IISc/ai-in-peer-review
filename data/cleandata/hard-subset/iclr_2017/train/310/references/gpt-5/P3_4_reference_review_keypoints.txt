- Paper studies emergent communication in a two-agent referential game with real images, single discrete symbol, trained with REINFORCE.
- Compares two sender architectures: agnostic MLP vs informed pairwise convolutional sender; informed sender converges faster and uses a richer symbol set.
- Analyses alignment with human semantics via cluster purity, t-SNE, an object-level variant, a grounding intervention, and a human evaluation (68% accuracy).
- Decision: Reject.
- Main reasons for rejection: contributions are incremental relative to existing work; evaluation lacks rigor, controls, ablations, and statistical support.
- Paper omits or under-engages with substantial follow-up literature on sequence protocols, compositionality metrics, population training, disentangling perceptual bias, and evaluation pitfalls.
- Methodological limitation: single-symbol, single-turn protocol prevents probing compositional structure or robust “language-like” claims.
- Training details are insufficient: no description of REINFORCE baselines/variance reduction, sensitivity to τ, batch size, embedding/filter sizes, or across-seed variance.
- The informed sender’s advantage may reflect architectural inductive bias or decision-boundary alignment rather than true semantic abstraction; ablations and capacity-matched controls are needed.
- The object-level manipulation is coarse and may still leak low-level information because VGG features of same-class images remain similar; quantitative comparisons lack statistical tests.
- Purity against McRae categories is a coarse, label-dependent metric and may reflect CNN feature structure rather than learned semantics; no confidence intervals or baseline comparisons are provided.
- SVD analysis of symbol redundancy is underdeveloped: no explained-variance thresholds, cross-validated rank estimates, or synthetic controls.
- Human evaluation is promising but hard to interpret due to likely selection bias (successful rounds only), unclear crowd QC, no per-word consistency analysis, and uncontrolled image referential clarity.
- No tests of out-of-distribution generalization (new categories/distractors), partner-swap with new receivers, or grounding durability without continued supervision.
- Missing comparisons to simple strong baselines (e.g., k-NN on VGG features, attribute-based labeling, supervised sender with learned receiver).
- Claims that agents “use higher-level semantics” and that language is “aligned with human intuitions” are overstated given limited protocol capacity and coarse analyses.
- Recommended reporting: error bars/statistical tests for symbol usage entropy, mutual information between symbols and categories, topographic similarity/RSA, and across-seed variance.
- Suggested ablations: vocabulary size, τ, embedding/filter sizes, parity of sender/receiver capacity, presence/absence of discretization bottleneck, and different feature inputs (fc vs conv vs sm).
- Suggested generalization experiments: unseen categories/combinations, adversarial within-class distractors, partner-swap/new receivers, and population training with role rotation.
- Recommend extending beyond single-symbol messages to sequences or multi-turn dialogs and evaluating compositionality with established metrics.
- Strengthen human study: preregistered protocol, balanced/randomized item sampling, inter-annotator agreement, difficulty calibration, and failure-mode analysis.
- Clarify REINFORCE implementation details and compare to alternatives (Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through); provide reproducibility artifacts (code, splits, hyperparameters, seeds).
- Investigate equilibrium selection and stability: lexicon variability across seeds, convergence behavior under perturbations, and tendency toward degenerate codes when low-level common knowledge is present.
- Concrete author questions to address: number of seeds and variability; REINFORCE baseline/variance reduction used; topographic similarity/RSA controlling for CNN similarity; effects of message length and multi-turn interaction; transferability to new receivers and required interactions; whether human trials were preselected; sensitivity to pre-trained feature choice (fc vs conv vs self-supervised); robustness of object-level manipulation under stricter leakage prevention.
