Summary and contributions
The paper studies the emergence of communication in referential games between neural agents operating over real images. Two sender architectures (agnostic vs. “informed”) communicate a single discrete symbol to a receiver to identify a target among two images. Using pretrained VGG features and REINFORCE, the authors show: (i) agents reliably achieve near-perfect coordination; (ii) the emergent symbols correlate with higher-level semantics (e.g., partial alignment with McRae categories, t-SNE visualizations); (iii) modifying common knowledge (showing different exemplars of the same class to sender/receiver) nudges agents toward more abstract, class-level reference; and (iv) adding a supervised image-labeling objective to the sender improves symbol purity and makes symbols partially interpretable by humans, as shown in a small crowd study (68% correct identification).

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) The empirical evidence is not sufficiently rigorous or comprehensive to support the claims about “natural-language-like” semantics and portability; key controls, baselines, and variance analyses are missing. (2) The contribution over prior work on emergent communication is incremental, and the architectural choices and evaluation protocols feel ad hoc, with limited validation against strong, established baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Limited baselines and controls: The work uses strong pretrained VGG features, which already encode semantic structure. Increases in cluster purity and human interpretability could stem largely from these features rather than emergent language. There is no comparison to simple, established baselines such as k-means on fc features with cluster IDs as “symbols,” nearest-neighbor template matching, or linear probes mapping features to categories. Without these, it’s difficult to attribute semantic structure to the communication protocol rather than the input representation.
- Insufficient robustness: Results are presented largely as single-run snapshots. There is no reporting of variability across random seeds, confidence intervals, or sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., temperature τ, embedding size, vocabulary size) that can critically affect discrete communication.
- Weak claims on “naturalness”: Evidence for “natural language-like” semantics relies on cluster purity (far from perfect), qualitative t-SNE plots, and a small-scale, partially curated human study. The human experiment samples 298 pairs post hoc from successful sender-receiver trials; potential selection bias and lack of controls (e.g., balanced difficulty, foil selection) limit the strength of conclusions. The 68% success rate is encouraging but not compelling without a stronger baseline (e.g., human performance given random words, words from k-means clusters, or continuous vector hints) and error analyses.
- Portability/generalization not convincingly demonstrated: There is no partner-swap evaluation (e.g., pairing trained senders with independently trained receivers), no test-time generalization to unseen concepts/classes, and no analysis of robustness when roles or the communication channel change. These are critical to claims about portability and conventionality.
- Incremental novelty relative to established literature: Communication via cheap-talk referential games is established; recent works (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) already show neural agents learning to communicate. The informed-sender 1-D conv across embedding dimensions is ad hoc and its advantage is not deeply analyzed. The supervised grounding via label prediction resembles straightforward multi-task learning; the empirical justification is modest.
- Methodological clarity: Some details are unclear or inconsistent (e.g., “1k rounds following 10k training games,” while training is 50k iterations elsewhere; how test sets are constructed; exact sampling temperatures; handling of symbol sampling at test time vs. argmax). References are also in poor shape, which impedes reproducibility.

Suggestions to improve (not central to the decision)
- Add strong baselines: k-means/mixture models over VGG features (cluster IDs as symbols), linear probing, and simple metric-learning + discretization. Compare symbol purity, downstream coordination accuracy, and human interpretability against these.
- Report robustness: Multiple seeds with mean/std; ablations on τ, embedding size, vocabulary size, sender/receiver capacity; continuous vs. discrete channels (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax).
- Test portability: Partner-swap experiments (S1 with R2), role rotation, unseen class generalization, and compositional generalization (hold out combinations or attributes).
- Strengthen human evaluation: Pre-register a sampling protocol, include controlled foils, report inter-subject variability, calibrate against baseline word assignments (random, cluster labels), and provide error typology (e.g., synonymy, metonymy, visually-driven confusions).
- Analyze representations: Control for VGG by using random or lower-level features; quantify how much emergent symbol semantics goes beyond the input representation. Measure mutual information between symbols and categories/features. Examine synonymy/homonymy systematically.
- Clarify and clean up: Resolve training/test protocol inconsistencies, detail temperatures and decision rules at test time, and fix references.

Questions for the authors
1) How many independent runs per setting did you perform? Please report variance across seeds for coordination success, symbol usage, purity, and human interpretability.
2) How do your results compare to simple baselines like k-means on fc features with cluster IDs used as symbols? Does your approach exceed these baselines in purity and human interpretability?
3) What happens if you replace VGG fc features with lower-level features or random features? Do you still observe above-chance purity and human interpretability?
4) Can you report partner-swap experiments (train S1/R1 and S2/R2 independently; test S1 with R2 and vice versa) to assess conventionality and portability?
5) In the human study, how were the 298 pairs selected, and what is the expected chance level given the foils? Can you provide a comparison to baseline word assignments and control for selection bias?
6) How sensitive are results to the temperature τ and the choice of sampling vs. argmax at test time? Does discretization help or hurt compared to a continuous channel?
7) In the object-level reference setting, do agents generalize to unseen object instances or classes? Please report held-out class performance.
8) Could you provide more rigorous analysis of the informed sender’s inductive bias (why 1-D conv across dimensions), and whether its benefit persists under capacity-matched agnostic architectures?

Overall, the paper raises important questions and presents an appealing program, but the current empirical evidence and comparisons are not yet at the level of rigor I expect for ICLR.
