1. Summary of contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in multi-agent referential games using natural images. Two sender architectures (agnostic vs. “informed” with a dimension-wise convolutional inductive bias) and a receiver learn to exchange a single discrete symbol to identify a target image among two candidates.
- Agents trained with REINFORCE achieve near-perfect coordination from scratch. The “informed” sender converges faster and uses a broader portion of the vocabulary.
- The authors analyze the emergent code: symbol–object associations show above-chance alignment with human semantic categories (cluster purity; living vs. non-living split with a small vocabulary), and t-SNE visualizations suggest alignment with visual similarity in CNN feature space.
- Modifying the game to remove low-level common knowledge (sender/receiver see different exemplars of the same classes) increases semantic alignment.
- Adding a supervised image-label task (on 100 ImageNet classes) interleaved with the game grounds symbols to human words, increases purity to 70%, and makes some symbols directly interpretable. A cross-dataset human study on ReferIt-derived image pairs shows humans pick the intended target 68% of the time given the sender’s word, suggesting partial portability to human interlocutors.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Novelty is limited relative to prior emergent communication work; the main ideas (referential games with neural agents, discrete symbol channels, policy-gradient learning) are known, and the incremental contributions (an “informed” sender, class-level variant, mixed supervised+interactive training) are not sufficiently differentiated or rigorously evaluated.
- The empirical support for claims about “natural” semantics and human interpretability is not yet scientifically rigorous: analyses rely on coarse purity metrics and visualizations, with confounds (use of pretrained VGG features; possible one-to-one mapping between supervised labels and game symbols), few ablations, and limited baselines.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
1) Specific problem: Learning communication protocols that emerge from interaction and are portable/interpretable (especially to humans). The problem is important and clearly stated.
2) Motivation and placement: The paper is well framed against multi-agent communication and language emergence literature (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.). The focus on interpretability and grounding is appropriate. However, the architectural and experimental extensions are modest and overlap with established themes (e.g., adding supervised grounding).
3) Do claims hold and are results rigorous?
- Technical soundness: The learning setup (REINFORCE with sampled discrete symbols) is standard, but key details for stability and variance control are missing (no baseline/variance reduction, no reporting across seeds, no sensitivity to temperature or vocabulary). The sender “informed” architecture is only superficially justified; ablations are limited.
- Use of pretrained VGG fc/softmax features undermines “tabula rasa” claims and may largely drive the observed semantic alignment. No experiments from raw pixels or with different encoders are reported.
- Evaluation: Communication success on a 2-image, 1-symbol task saturates quickly; stronger tasks (more distractors, role rotation, multi-step messages) are not explored. Semantic analyses use cluster purity and t-SNE, which are indirect; statistical reporting is sparse (no confidence intervals or seed variance for purity; only one p-value for the human study). No comparisons to simple non-learning or heuristic baselines (e.g., nearest-neighbor category matching from VGG) are provided.
- Grounding and human study: The supervised objective appears to tie game symbols to fixed ImageNet labels; it is unclear whether symbol indices are explicitly aligned with label indices, confounding the claim that “conventional” words emerged. The human study (68% accuracy) is promising but limited: only 10 judgments per pair, 298 pairs, unclear quality controls and error analysis, and the majority of pairs do not contain the supervised categories, making interpretation hinge on metonymic reasoning without a systematic evaluation of when/why it works.
Overall, while the direction is valuable, the evidence does not yet meet the rigor expected for ICLR.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarify the mapping between supervised labels and communication symbols. Are symbol indices hard-aligned to label indices, or does alignment emerge? Report alignment metrics and whether remapping (e.g., Hungarian assignment) improves correspondence.
- Report results across multiple random seeds with means and variances; add a baseline/critic in REINFORCE; perform sensitivity analyses (temperature τ, embedding size, vocabulary size).
- Strengthen tasks: more distractors; role rotation; partner swap/generalization to new receivers; multi-symbol messages; back-and-forth communication. Report generalization to unseen object categories/classes.
- Add baselines: a heuristic that uses VGG features to pick a symbol by nearest supervised label; a non-communicating receiver; a fixed-symbol sender; and a supervised-only sender used at test-time without interactive training.
- Improve semantic analysis: mutual information between symbols and categories; top-k category coverage per symbol; disentanglement metrics; probing classifiers on sender embeddings; control for the influence of VGG vs. emergent code.
- Human evaluation: pre-register task; add attention checks; report inter-annotator agreement; analyze per-word and per-category performance; contrast cases with and without direct category presence; compare to human baseline without the word (chance at 50%) and to a text-only baseline (word–image association without sender involvement).
- Discuss scalability and limitations more explicitly (pretrained features, simplicity of the game, single-step messages).
- Clean up references and ensure proper citation formatting.

Questions for the authors
- How exactly are game symbols tied to supervised labels? Is there a fixed 1–1 mapping by index, or does the model learn a latent alignment? If the latter, how is alignment assessed?
- How many random seeds were used? What is the variance in communication success, symbol usage, and purity across seeds?
- Did you use any variance-reduction techniques in REINFORCE (baseline, entropy regularization)? How sensitive are results to τ and to vocabulary size beyond 10/100?
- Can you report baselines where the sender selects the nearest supervised label to the target VGG feature and the receiver selects the closer image to that label embedding? How does this compare to your learned agents?
- In the object-level reference variant, how many class pairs are visually confusable? Does performance degrade as visual similarity increases or as the number of distractors grows?
- For the human study, what quality control and exclusion criteria were used? Can you provide per-word accuracy and an error taxonomy? How robust are results if you remove symbols corresponding to directly supervised labels?
- Do results hold with different visual backbones (e.g., ResNet) or from raw pixels with end-to-end training?
