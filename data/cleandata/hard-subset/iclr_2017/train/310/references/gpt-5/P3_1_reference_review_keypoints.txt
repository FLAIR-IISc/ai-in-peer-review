- Paper studies emergent communication in referential games with real images, asking if tabula rasa agents can learn to communicate and what factors promote human-interpretable semantics.  
- Setup: Lewis-style signaling game with discrete symbols, fixed VGG features, REINFORCE training, comparing agnostic vs informed sender architectures.  
- Proposed interventions: object-level reference (different images of same class) and mixed supervised training (object naming + self-play) to encourage abstraction.  
- Evaluation: coordination success, symbol usage stats, purity vs McRae categories, t-SNE visualizations, SVD of symbol–pair usage, and a small human study (68% success on ReferIt).  
- Main positive finding: agents reliably learn to communicate and modest semantic structure (e.g., living vs non-living) emerges; supervision increases semantic purity and partial human interpretability.  
- Decision: Reject (borderline).  
- Key reason 1: Empirical analysis of semantic abstraction and human-aligned interpretability is suggestive but not rigorous or comprehensive.  
- Key reason 2: Important experimental details and ablations are missing (training stability, variance, baselines, cross-play), limiting robustness and generality.  
- Key reason 3: Novelty is incremental relative to contemporary emergent communication work, with limited theoretical insight or more challenging tasks.  
- Evidence for “natural” semantics is thin: purity above chance but low, t-SNE anecdotal, missing standard measures like topographic similarity and mutual information.  
- SVD analysis of symbol–pair usage is hard to interpret as evidence of semantic dimensionality.  
- Human study has selection bias and limited scope: sampled successful games per symbol likely over-represents easy cases; 68% is modest and lacks comparison baselines.  
- Missing baselines for human tasks: random symbol, nearest supervised label, or supervised discriminative listener.  
- Generalization/portability under-explored: no cross-play, no population training, no zero-shot tests on unseen objects/categories.  
- “Object-level reference” is a step but still limited by same label space and shared inductive biases.  
- RL methodology under-specified: no discussion of variance reduction, entropy regularization, temperature sensitivity, or runs over multiple seeds.  
- Reported results appear from single runs; unclear training failure rates, convergence speed, and stability.  
- Missing comparisons to differentiable/continuous communication approaches and prior synthetic-input emergent-communication baselines.  
- Task scope is simple (single symbol, two images) so claims about language emergence, compositionality, and systematic generalization are limited.  
- Recommended semantics evaluations: topographic similarity, category confusion matrices, mutual information, quantitative clustering metrics, and explicit synonymy/homonymy analysis.  
- Human study improvements: sample uniformly/stratify by difficulty, add baselines, report inter-annotator agreement and per-word breakdown.  
- Portability experiments suggested: cross-play, partner swaps, population training, and generalization to unseen classes/distractor distributions.  
- RL robustness suggestions: report multiple seeds with means/variances, learning curves, clarify baselines (value baseline, entropy bonus), and test-time decoding policy.  
- Ablations to add: compare VGG softmax vs fc features, analyze “informed” bias, vary vocabulary size/bandwidth, and explore multi-symbol messages for compositionality.  
- Presentation fixes: clean references/formatting, provide dataset split details and counts, and release code and evaluation scripts.  
- Key author questions: robustness across seeds/hyperparameters; cross-play performance and supervised grounding effects; topographic similarity/mutual information results; ReferIt sampling and baseline comparisons; generalization/compositionality evidence; variance-reduction techniques used in REINFORCE.  
- Overall: problem is important and setup is clean; removing low-level common knowledge and adding supervised grounding seems promising but needs stronger empirical rigor to be a solid contribution.
