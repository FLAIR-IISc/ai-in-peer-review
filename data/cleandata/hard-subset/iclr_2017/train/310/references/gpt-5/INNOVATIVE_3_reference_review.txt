Summary and contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in multi-agent referential games with real images, asking (i) whether tabula rasa agents learn to communicate and (ii) what environmental/architectural choices make the emergent code more “natural” and portable to humans. Key contributions:
- A simple, scalable two-agent referential game using large-scale natural images (ImageNet-derived), with sender and receiver trained via policy gradients and a discrete, single-symbol communication bottleneck.
- Architectural comparison (agnostic vs informed sender) showing that inductive biases toward dimension-wise comparison increase symbol usage, learning speed, and semantic purity of symbol-object associations.
- A clever “object-level reference” manipulation that removes low-level common knowledge (different instances of the same class shown to sender/receiver), nudging agents toward category-level semantics.
- A grounding approach that mixes self-play with supervised image labeling, yielding a more interpretable lexicon (higher purity, many symbols aligned with object names) and human usability: crowd workers choose the intended target image 68% of the time even when the supervised label is only indirectly related to the target content.
Overall, the paper proposes a concrete, extensible program for building communicative agents that bootstrap on each other, then align to humans via lightweight supervision, with initial human validation.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper advances a bold, novel direction—shaping emergent language through environment design and minimal supervision—demonstrating promising human-interpretability and cross-dataset generalization. Despite some methodological gaps, the conceptual contribution and empirical signals merit acceptance.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and vision: The work operationalizes long-standing ideas from game theory and cognitive science in a modern deep RL setting with realistic visual inputs. The levers explored (inductive bias, common knowledge manipulation, grounding via supervised labels) are original and actionable for the community.
- Empirical support: Agents reliably learn to coordinate; informed architectures converge faster and use a richer vocabulary; symbol-object clusters have significantly above-chance purity (including a striking living vs non-living split with only two symbols); object-level reference increases semantic alignment; supervised grounding substantially boosts purity and leads to symbols usable by humans (68% correctness vs 50% chance) on a different dataset (ReferIt).
- Practical significance: The human study, though limited, indicates that minimal supervised grounding helps language transfer to human partners and to out-of-domain images via metonymic use—an encouraging property for interactive AI.
- Scope for growth: The program is clearly positioned as “first steps,” yet already reveals how environment and architecture shape emergent semantics, providing a foundation for future multi-turn/sequential communication and stronger grounding.

Suggestions to improve (non-blocking)
- Rigor and clarity
  - Report variance across random seeds (success rates, purity, number of symbols used). Is the emergent lexicon stable?
  - Detail RL training: optimizer, learning rates, gradient baselines/variance reduction, entropy regularization, and whether τ is annealed; specify test-time decoding (greedy vs sampling).
  - Provide numerical results in the main text for Tables/Figures referenced; include confidence intervals and statistical tests for purity and symbol-usage differences.
  - Clean up references (many have formatting issues).
- Analyses and ablations
  - Examine generalization to unseen object classes and partner-swap (sender trained with receiver A, tested with receiver B) to assess portability of the code.
  - Analyze homonymy/synonymy: mutual information between symbols and classes; topographic similarity between visual distances and symbol distributions; how many effective symbols after accounting for redundancy?
  - Vary vocabulary size, τ, embedding dim; compare to continuous channels and to alternative discrete relaxations (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax, straight-through).
  - Probe what features symbols latch onto (color/texture vs shape/category). Control for distractors from same vs different super-category.
- Human evaluation
  - Provide chance baselines and stronger controls (e.g., shuffled labels, frequency-matched random words).
  - Include free-text rationales or multiple-choice glosses to better understand interpretations; analyze per-word and per-image effects.
  - Clarify quality control on crowdsourcing and selection criteria for image pairs.
- Scope extensions
  - Move beyond single-symbol messages to multi-step dialogues and role rotation; test compositionality and referential efficiency under harder distractors or multiple objects.
  - Explore curriculum or environment design that induces systematicity/compositionality.
  - Discuss sample efficiency and computational cost of the approach.

Questions for the authors
1. Stability: How consistent are symbol-object mappings across seeds and training runs? Do you observe convergent “lexica,” or multiple local conventions?
2. Decoding and temperature: What is the test-time decoding policy and did you anneal τ? How sensitive are results to τ and vocabulary size?
3. Variance reduction: Did you use baselines/advantages in REINFORCE? If not, how did you manage variance; if yes, please detail.
4. Generalization: How does the system perform on unseen classes or when swapping receivers at test time? Does the grounded lexicon facilitate ad hoc coordination?
5. Semantics: Beyond purity, do nearby objects in visual space map to similar symbol distributions (e.g., topographic similarity)? Are there interpretable dimensions of variation?
6. Human study: What are the exact instructions, controls, and inter-annotator agreement? Can you report per-word accuracy and examples where humans systematically disagree with the agent?
7. Object-level reference: Can you quantify the purity increase and provide examples illustrating the shift from low-level to category-level features?

This paper is an exciting step toward practical emergent communication aligned with human semantics. With tighter analyses and expanded evaluations, it could become a touchstone for language emergence in interactive deep learning.
