Summary and contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in two-agent referential games with real images. Two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed) and a receiver are trained tabula rasa with REINFORCE to exchange a single discrete symbol per game and select the target image among two.
- The paper claims: (i) agents reliably learn to coordinate; (ii) the induced code correlates with higher-level semantics (e.g., categories) rather than low-level visual quirks; (iii) modifying common knowledge (object-level reference) nudges semantics to be more abstract; (iv) adding supervised object naming to the sender while continuing the game grounds the code in human language and improves human interpretability; (v) a crowd experiment shows humans can often pick the intended image given the sender’s symbol (mapped to a word).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons
- Methodological and reporting rigor are insufficient relative to the breadth of claims: numerous missing details, unclear or contradictory training counts, limited baselines/controls, and reliance on qualitative analyses (t-SNE, anecdotal examples) without robust, quantitative support.
- The claim of portability and human interpretability is only weakly supported; the human study and semantic analyses lack necessary controls and statistical clarity.

Supporting arguments
1) Clarity and consistency
- Training budget is inconsistent: “50k iterations (games) with batch size 32” implies 1.6M games, yet elsewhere “10k training games” and “1k rounds following.” Please reconcile. How many games does each experiment actually use for training and evaluation?
- Test-time decoding is ambiguous: you say the receiver “samples” at test; do you use sampling or argmax? This matters for coordination and purity.
- The “temperature τ=10” is unusual; is the Gibbs distribution softmax(logits/τ) or softmax(τ·logits)? The effect of τ is critical for discreteness/entropy but not analyzed.

2) Architectural controls
- The “informed sender” advantage is attributed to inductive bias, but parameter counts and capacity are not matched. Since the agnostic model “nests” the informed one, capacity and optimization differences should be controlled (e.g., equal params, depth, normalization), and results averaged over seeds with error bars.
- Receiver architecture (dot products between symbol and image embeddings) seems tailored to encourage prototype-like codes; discuss if this biases outcomes versus alternatives.

3) Optimization details
- REINFORCE is used, but no baseline/variance reduction, entropy regularization, or gradient estimator details are provided. What learning rates, optimizers, gradient clipping, reward baselines, or entropy costs? With sampling policies, these greatly affect stability and emergent codes.
- No reporting of variability across random seeds or runs. Do all runs converge? With how much variance in symbol usage and purity?

4) Semantic evaluation
- The cluster purity procedure is underspecified. If clusters are defined by symbols, purity depends strongly on the number of symbols; this can inflate purity with many small clusters. Report MI/NMI/ARI and purity under controlled numbers of clusters, and compare to label-frequency baselines. Provide statistical tests and CIs.
- The SVD “50 dimensions” result lacks detail (data size, normalization, cross-validation protocol, variance explained). It is hard to interpret vis-à-vis synonymy or polysemy.
- t-SNE plots are illustrative only; please include quantitative topographic metrics (e.g., correlation between distances in visual space and symbol distributions) and bootstrapped confidence intervals.

5) Object-level reference manipulation
- The “different images same class” manipulation is sensible, but the effect on purity is described as “small” without numbers, variability, or significance. Also, with two-image discrimination, agents can still rely on relative features. Consider evaluating with distractors from same super-category to stress absolute naming.

6) Grounding via supervised naming and human study
- The 47% exact-symbol match on supervised categories vs 1% chance is encouraging but expected; the key is whether the sender deploys words compositionally/abstractly for new concepts. The 68% human accuracy is above chance, but the study lacks critical controls:
  - No comparison to random symbols or to a model trained without the supervised task.
  - No synonym handling or normalization of label granularity; ImageNet labels can be obscure for crowd workers.
  - No pre-specified exclusion criteria, demographics, or attention checks; the logistic mixed-effects model is cited without full specification, and multiple-comparisons/selection effects aren’t addressed.
  - Only 298 symbol–pair instances and 10 judgments each; report CIs and variance across words/pairs, and analyze failure modes.
- The mapping from symbol to word relies on the supervised classifier index; is the mapping stable across training? How do you ensure that “the same symbol” corresponds to the same word?

7) Generalization and portability
- The central motivating question (portability to new partners/humans) is not directly evaluated. Missing experiments:
  - Partner swap: train multiple sender–receiver pairs independently and evaluate cross-play zero-shot.
  - New listener with frozen sender: can a new receiver learn quickly from the sender’s “language”?
  - Generalization to unseen classes/concepts and to harder distractors (same coarse category).
- The “blank slate” claim is overstated given heavy reliance on pretrained VGG features; discuss the extent to which semantics come from VGG vs emergent communication.

8) Presentation and formatting
- Many references are malformed or incomplete (dates, venues, stray tokens like “? \Q2014\E”), and several figures/tables are referenced but not included. There are typos, inconsistent notation (Gibbs vs softmax), and a dangling footnote. This significantly harms reproducibility.

Suggestions to improve (not all required for acceptance)
- Provide complete training details, seeds, error bars, and ablations (τ, vocabulary size, embedding dim, parameter counts matched). Report convergence statistics across runs.
- Strengthen semantic evaluation: MI/NMI/ARI, topographic similarity, RSA between symbol usage and semantic/category spaces; control for number of symbols.
- Add rigorous partner-swap and frozen-sender generalization tests. Evaluate on same-category distractors and on unseen classes.
- Improve human study: include baselines (random symbol; no-supervision sender), handle synonyms, preregister tasks, report CIs, attention checks, and full mixed-effects model specs.
- Clarify the supervised grounding: stability of symbol–word mapping, effect of supervision weight, and whether the receiver can be trained to interpret a pretrained sender.
- Release code and data splits; fix references and formatting.

Questions for the authors
1) How many games are actually used per experiment? Please reconcile “50k iterations with batch size 32” vs “10k training games,” and clarify test-time decoding (sampling vs argmax).
2) What are the exact REINFORCE settings (optimizer, LR, baseline, entropy)? How sensitive are results to τ, vocabulary size, and random seeds?
3) How are clusters defined for purity? What is the number of clusters vs number of categories? Please report MI/NMI and statistical significance.
4) How is “cross-validated SVD” performed? What variance is explained by the top components?
5) Are parameter counts matched across sender architectures? How many runs per condition?
6) In the human study, what controls were used (e.g., random symbol baseline, no-supervision sender), and what are the detailed statistics (CIs, per-word variance, attention checks)?
7) Does cross-play between independently trained agents succeed? Can a new receiver learn to understand a frozen sender faster than from scratch?
