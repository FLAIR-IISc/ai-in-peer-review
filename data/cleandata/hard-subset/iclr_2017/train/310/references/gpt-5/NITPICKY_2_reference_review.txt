1. Summary (claims and contributions)
- The paper studies emergent communication in two-agent referential games over real images. Agents start tabula rasa and learn a discrete symbol protocol with only the task reward via REINFORCE. Two sender architectures are compared: an “agnostic” MLP and an “informed” sender with a dimension-wise convolutional bias for comparing the two images.
- The paper investigates whether agents: (i) learn to communicate; (ii) develop symbol usages that align with higher-level human concepts. It uses cluster purity against McRae categories and t-SNE visualizations to assess semantic alignment.
- A modified “object-level reference” variant shows sender/receiver different instances of the same class to reduce low-level common knowledge, reporting a small purity increase.
- To ground symbols in human language, the sender is jointly trained (interleaved) on a supervised object-labeling task sharing the game embedding. This increases symbol usage, purity (~70%), and yields partial human interpretability in a crowd study (68% accuracy on a 2AFC task on ReferIt boxes).

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient experimental rigor/reporting to support core claims about “natural semantics” and human interpretability (unclear splits, baselines, selection biases, statistics); (2) Many methodological ambiguities and missing details in the RL setup and architectures impede reproducibility and weaken conclusions; (3) Substantial presentation/formatting issues (missing figures/tables, malformed references) that do not meet conference standards.

3. Supporting arguments
- Experimental rigor and validity
  - Train/test splits and data leakage: The main image experiments sample training/test “games” randomly from the same pool; it is unclear whether images or pairs overlap across train/test, making generalization claims ambiguous. The supervised grounding uses a subset of 100 labels but does not clarify whether images/classes in the referential game are disjoint from supervised training beyond label overlap.
  - Evaluation metrics: “Purity” maps each concept to its most frequent symbol, but the sender conditions on the distractor. This context dependence biases the per-concept majority symbol and can inflate purity even if symbols are not concept-like. No control shows purity beyond what the VGG fc space already induces; reliance on t-SNE is qualitative.
  - Human study biases: Pairs were selected only from trials where the receiver succeeded. This post-selection biases the set toward easy cases and can overestimate human interpretability. No comparison to simple baselines (e.g., nearest-neighbor class word from fc space; random left/right; majority-choice word priors). No confidence intervals are reported; 68% vs 50% chance needs a clearer effect size and robustness analysis.
  - Symbol usage claims (e.g., agnostic sender uses ~2 symbols) need robust quantification across seeds, vocab sizes, and with/without entropy regularization or mutual-information encouragement. The SVD analysis is suggestive but not tied to task performance or interpretability.
- Methodological clarity/reproducibility
  - RL details are underspecified: optimizer, learning rates, variance-reduction baselines for REINFORCE, gradient clipping, entropy regularization, exploration schedules, number of seeds/variance. Both sender and receiver sample actions stochastically even at test time; it is unclear how test success is computed and whether argmax vs sampling changes results.
  - The “informed” architecture is ambiguously specified (1-D conv across channels, kernel sizes, parameter sharing) and the claim that the agnostic architecture “nests” the informed one is not substantiated.
  - Critical hyperparameters (e.g., τ = 10) are fixed “without tuning” but there is no sensitivity analysis; emergent protocols can be highly sensitive to temperature, embedding size, and reward variance.
  - No ablations on feature type (sm vs fc), image augmentation, or role randomization; no analysis of generalization to novel concepts/distractors or out-of-distribution settings.
- Claims vs evidence
  - The central claim that agents learn “higher-level” conceptual meanings is not convincingly isolated from the inductive bias of pretrained VGG features, which already cluster semantically. The object-level variant moves in the right direction but the reported gains are small and not statistically analyzed.
  - The human interpretability claim (via supervised grounding) is promising but preliminary. Without proper controls and unbiased sampling, it does not yet support the strong conclusion that the emergent code is meaningfully grounded for humans.

- Presentation/formatting
  - Figures/tables (e.g., Table 1/2, Figure 1–4) are referenced but not included; key quantitative results are missing.
  - References are severely malformed/incomplete (years, venues, repeated author strings, “? \Q2014\E”, punctuation). This does not meet ICLR standards.
  - Several footnotes and asides are awkwardly placed; some claims repeat without precise numbers.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide complete experimental details and code. Report means, standard deviations over multiple seeds, and statistical tests for all key metrics. Include clear train/test splits preventing image leakage; evaluate generalization to unseen images and to unseen classes.
- Control analyses:
  - Baselines: nearest-neighbor labeler using VGG fc; random and majority-symbol baselines; a supervised-only sender without game interaction; entropy-regularized protocols.
  - Context dependence: measure symbol consistency conditional on distractor; evaluate mutual information between symbol and target concept vs distractor features.
  - Human study: pre-register unbiased sampling (not conditional on receiver success), provide CIs, compare to baselines, randomize left/right, and include attention checks.
- Analysis depth:
  - Examine compositionality/systematicity across distractors and with more than two images. Rotate roles and test partner swap (cross-play) to assess protocol robustness.
  - Add sensitivity studies over τ, vocab size, embedding dim, and reward baselines; report convergence stability.
  - Clarify the informed architecture; test whether its inductive bias alone (without learning) explains performance through a linear probe.
- Improve clarity/formatting:
  - Include all figures/tables with precise numbers.
  - Fix references (complete citations, consistent style).
  - Clearly specify sampling at test time vs argmax and its impact.

Questions for the authors
1. How are training and test sets separated at the image level? Do any exact images or pairs appear in both? What is the generalization gap with held-out images/classes?
2. At test time, do you use sampling or argmax for the sender/receiver? How sensitive are results to τ?
3. How many random seeds were run? What is the variance of success, purity, and symbol usage across seeds?
4. In the purity computation, how do you control for context dependence on the distractor? What is purity if you restrict to homogeneous distractor categories?
5. For the human study, why select only successful agent trials? What happens with unbiased sampling, and with a nearest-neighbor label baseline?
6. Please detail the RL optimization (optimizer, learning rates, baselines, entropy), and the exact informed-sender architecture (shapes, parameter counts).
