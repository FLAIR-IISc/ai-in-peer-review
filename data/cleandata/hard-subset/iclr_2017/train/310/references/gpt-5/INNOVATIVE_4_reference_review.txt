Summary
The paper studies how language-like communication can emerge among tabula rasa neural agents through cooperative referential games on real images, and how environment design nudges the emergent code toward human-interpretable semantics. The authors (i) show that simple sender–receiver agents trained only with REINFORCE on communication success reliably converge to near-perfect coordination; (ii) analyze two sender architectures and find that an “informed” sender with an inductive bias toward comparing image embeddings dimension-wise learns a richer, more semantically aligned symbol usage than an agnostic sender; (iii) demonstrate that removing low-level common knowledge (by showing different images of the same classes to sender/receiver) pushes symbols toward higher-level, category-like meanings; and (iv) introduce a hybrid training regime mixing self-play with supervised object naming, which markedly increases symbol purity and, crucially, yields nontrivial human interpretability in a separate dataset (68% accuracy in a human “receiver” study on ReferIt). The work is ambitious and forward-looking, offering a concrete, scalable pathway to ground emergent communication in human language.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong novelty and vision: a clean, general framework for emergent communication with explicit steps toward human alignment, including a human study; (2) Convincing empirical evidence that environment design and light supervision can steer emergent codes toward conceptual, human-interpret-able semantics.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning. The paper tackles a central AI question: how to induce communication that supports coordination and generalizes to new partners (ultimately humans). It is well motivated with respect to multi-agent RL and classic signaling games, and distinguishes itself from recent neural communication works by focusing on semantic alignment and human interpretability rather than just coordination performance. The literature coverage is appropriate and situates the contribution clearly among self-play, Wizard-of-Oz, and human-in-the-loop paradigms.

- Approach. The referential-game setup is simple, scalable, and well specified: two images, a discrete symbol bottleneck, and a receiver choice. The architectural comparison (agnostic vs informed sender) is meaningful and highlights the role of inductive bias. The “object-level reference” variant is a neat manipulation of common knowledge that cleanly tests how environmental constraints shape semantics. The hybrid training with shared embeddings across supervised labels and game symbols is elegant and minimal yet effective.

- Evidence and claims. The key empirical claims are supported: rapid convergence to successful communication; richer symbol usage and higher category purity for the informed sender; above-chance purity even with a 2-symbol code that approximates living vs non-living; further purity gains when sender/receiver see different exemplars; and substantial improvements in human interpretability under hybrid training, including generalization to images without supervised categories. The human study on ReferIt, while modest, is particularly compelling for this line of work.

- Boldness vs completeness. While some analyses are preliminary (e.g., limited ablations), the paper introduces a significant direction and demonstrates several strong, orthogonal insights: inductive biases matter; environment design can enforce higher-level semantics; and small amounts of supervised grounding can bridge to humans. This is the kind of ambitious, concept-opening contribution that merits acceptance.

Areas for improvement (not gating the decision)
- Experimental detail and robustness. Provide optimizer, learning rates, exploration/entropy regularization (if any), variance-reduction baselines for REINFORCE, and training stability across seeds. Report test-time decoding (sampling vs argmax). Include confidence intervals for purity and success rates.

- Metrics. Complement purity and t-SNE with information-theoretic measures (e.g., mutual information between symbols and category labels), topographic similarity between message distances and visual/conceptual distances, and homonymy/synonymy quantification beyond SVD.

- Baselines. Include: (a) a supervised-only sender evaluated in the game; (b) learned continuous (non-discrete) communication channel; (c) no-bias sender with matched parameter count; (d) a trivial heuristic sender (e.g., nearest ImageNet class name) to contextualize human-study results.

- Generalization analyses. Test partner swap (train with one receiver, evaluate with a new one), vocabulary size sensitivity, temperature effects, and message length (allow multi-symbol sequences).

- Human study. Report chance level, per-word performance, examples of metonymic uses and error modes, and a brief analysis of whether humans relied on object-level vs scene-level cues.

- Presentation. Clarify the informed sender architecture (kernel sizes, channel semantics) and fix reference formatting issues.

Questions for the authors
1) Training details and stability: What optimizer, learning rates, and gradient/variance-reduction techniques were used for REINFORCE? How many random seeds, and how variable are convergence speed, symbol usage, and purity?

2) Decoding policy: At test time, do you sample or take the argmax for sender and receiver? How sensitive are results to temperature and message stochasticity?

3) Purity and significance: How are chance levels computed for purity? Please include confidence intervals or permutation tests and report per-category purity to see where semantics emerge more readily.

4) Inductive bias: The informed sender nests the agnostic one in principle; is its empirical gain due to bias or capacity? Can you match parameter counts or add a linear comparator head to the agnostic sender as a control?

5) Grounding transfer: In the hybrid setup, how much does supervised pretraining alone (without self-play) enable coordination with a trained receiver? Conversely, how much does self-play alone (without labels) enable human interpretability on ReferIt?

6) Partner generalization: If you fix a trained sender and train a fresh receiver from scratch, does it learn faster? Can a receiver trained with one sender understand messages from another?

7) Message structure: Do symbols exhibit topographic similarity with visual space or WordNet distances? Any evidence of compositionality when increasing vocabulary size or allowing multi-token messages?

8) Human-in-the-loop: Could occasional human receiver rounds further boost interpretability without hurting coordination? Any preliminary observations?

Overall, this is a timely, creative, and promising step toward grounded emergent communication with humans in the loop. I recommend acceptance.
