1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in visually grounded, two-agent referential games. The key contributions are:
- A simple, scalable framework where two neural agents (sender/receiver) learn to coordinate via a discrete symbol sent by the sender to identify a target among images. Agents are trained end-to-end with REINFORCE and succeed at near-perfect coordination.
- Analysis of how architectural inductive bias influences emergent codes: an “informed” sender that compares images dimension-wise uses a richer set of symbols and converges faster than an “agnostic” sender.
- Evidence that emergent symbols reflect higher-level semantics: cluster purity against McRae categories is above chance; even with only two symbols, agents discover a coarse “living vs. non-living” split.
- A modification that reduces shared low-level “common knowledge” by presenting different images of the same class to sender/receiver, encouraging class-level meanings and improving purity.
- A practical route to human-interpretability by mixing self-play with supervised image labeling. This yields higher purity, more symbols, and human-understandable signals that generalize to new images (with a 68% human success rate on a new dataset), indicating partial grounding in natural language.

2) Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated combination of emergent communication with realistic visual inputs and human grounding, with clear empirical evidence that both coordination and partially human-interpretable semantics emerge.
- The analyses (purity, symbol usage, t-SNE visualization, human study) offer an informative picture of what is learned and how environment design affects semantic alignment.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper targets how to induce portable, human-aligned communication protocols in cooperative multi-agent settings, starting from tabula rasa agents with a discrete communication bottleneck.

Motivation and placement in literature: The motivation is strong: building agents that can coordinate with humans requires language-like communication. The work is well-placed relative to multimodal learning, emergent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), and the signaling-game/cheap-talk literature. The emphasis on environment design to nudge interpretable semantics, and on combining interactive self-play with supervised grounding, is clear and compelling.

Do the results support the claims? For coordination, yes: agents converge reliably. For semantics, cluster purity above chance, symbol usage diversity, and qualitative plots support the claim that symbols track higher-level content. The “object-level reference” tweak and the mixed supervised/self-play training provide convincing evidence that environment and auxiliary objectives shape interpretability. The human evaluation on ReferIt-derived image pairs is particularly valuable: 68% success is far above chance and suggests meaningful generalization of learned words via metonymic links.

4) Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the accept decision)
- Clarify the “tabula rasa” claim. Agents use fixed VGG features, which confers substantial prior structure. Please state explicitly whether VGG is frozen and discuss how results might change with end-to-end training or weaker visual encoders. This matters for claims about learning from scratch.
- Baselines and controls:
  - Compare cluster purity of agent symbols to unsupervised clustering of the same images (e.g., k-means on fc features with the same number of clusters) to calibrate how much additional semantic structure is induced by communication.
  - Include a random-policy or non-communicative baseline to quantify the role of the symbol channel on coordination.
  - Provide variance across multiple random seeds for coordination speed, symbol usage, and purity.
- Metrics and statistical rigor:
  - Purity: report confidence intervals, chance baselines precisely, and statistical tests versus chance and between architectures. Define how categories and symbol clusters are matched.
  - The SVD analysis is interesting but under-specified. How was cross-validated rank determined? What dataset size was used for the symbol-by-pair matrix?
  - t-SNE plots are illustrative but qualitative; consider quantitative measures (e.g., mutual information between symbols and categories, or probing classifiers predicting categories from symbol distributions).
- Environment design:
  - The sender always receives the ordered pair with the target first. Please justify this choice and discuss whether allowing role rotation or randomizing target position for the sender affects coordination and semantics.
  - Explore vocabulary size systematically and plot performance, purity, and number of effective symbols versus K. Is the 2-symbol dichotomy a stable attractor for the agnostic sender?
  - Temperature τ and sampling vs argmax: report sensitivity analyses, as these affect discreteness and exploration.
- Human study:
  - Provide more details: number of symbols tested, annotator demographics/quality control, chance level, and the distribution of per-symbol accuracies. Are results driven by a few highly interpretable symbols?
  - It would be informative to include a human-receiver baseline trained on the same supervised labels (without self-play) to disentangle the benefits of self-play in making words useful as referring expressions.
- Generalization:
  - Do agents generalize to unseen object categories or novel visual domains without supervised labels? Report transfer to held-out categories.
  - How robust is coordination when pairing agents trained separately (interoperability)? This relates directly to “portability to new partners.”
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Some references appear malformed; please fix formatting.
  - Expand on the informed-sender architecture with a clearer intuition and ablate its components (e.g., remove the dimension-wise convolution) to pinpoint what matters.

Questions for the authors
1) Are VGG features frozen? Did you try end-to-end finetuning, and how did it impact coordination and emergent semantics?
2) How many random seeds did you run, and what is the variance in coordination speed, symbol diversity, and purity?
3) How are train/test splits constructed? Are test images disjoint from training games, and do you ever present the exact same image pair in training and test?
4) Can you report purity and human-interpretability as a function of vocabulary size, temperature, and sender architecture?
5) How does performance change when pairing agents trained independently (zero-shot partner swap)?
6) In the mixed supervised/self-play setting, which fraction of the improved purity comes from reusing supervised labels verbatim versus broader semantic alignment? Any analysis on symbols not corresponding to supervised labels?
7) For the SVD analysis, please detail the data matrix construction, normalization, and how cross-validated rank ≈ 50 was determined.

Overall, this is a clear, timely study with promising results and a solid empirical exploration of how environment and auxiliary supervision shape emergent, partially human-interpretable communication.
