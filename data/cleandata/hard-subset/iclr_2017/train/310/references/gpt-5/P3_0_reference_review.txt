Summary and contributions
The paper studies the emergence of communication in cooperative multi-agent referential games grounded in real images. Two tabula rasa neural agents (sender, receiver) learn a discrete communication protocol via policy gradient (REINFORCE), with success measured by whether the receiver identifies the target image among distractors. Key contributions:
- A simple but compelling framework for learning to communicate on large-scale, noisy visual inputs (ImageNet-derived), without pre-specified semantics or protocols.
- Architectural comparison between an agnostic sender and an “informed” sender with an inductive bias for dimension-by-dimension comparison of target/distractor embeddings.
- Empirical evidence that emergent symbols align with higher-level semantics: above-chance purity with respect to 20 broad semantic categories; t-SNE analyses; and qualitative “living vs. non-living” distinctions with small vocabularies.
- An environmental tweak that removes low-level common knowledge (sender and receiver see different images of the same classes), encouraging more abstract, class-level communication.
- A hybrid learning scheme that interleaves self-play with supervised object naming, substantially improving interpretability (70% purity; 47% exact label use when applicable) and a human evaluation on ReferIt-style stimuli showing 68% success in interpreting the agent’s symbols, despite limited overlap with supervised categories and frequent metonymic use.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Reasoning: The paper presents a clear, well-motivated setup and delivers concrete empirical evidence that simple self-play agents can develop robust, somewhat human-aligned communication on realistic inputs, and that adding limited supervision significantly improves interpretability and human usability. While some analyses are ad hoc and additional ablations would strengthen the claims, the work is timely, relevant, and likely to spur further research. The human evaluation and manipulation of common knowledge are particularly novel and insightful.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The authors convincingly argue that supervised learning on canned dialogues misses the interactive, functional nature of language. Using cheap-talk inspired referential games with natural images is a well-placed and pragmatic step, differentiating from: (i) pre-scripted communications in MAS; (ii) heavy Wizard-of-Oz environments; and (iii) purely supervised captioning/translation. Connections to linguistics and game theory are apt.
- Methodology: The game is simple, reproducible in spirit, and isolates core issues: discrete symbol emergence, partner-specific co-adaptation, and the role of environmental structure. The informed vs. agnostic sender comparison is neat and reveals meaningful differences (speed, symbol usage, semantic alignment).
- Empirical support: Agents reliably learn to coordinate near perfectly. The semantic alignment claims are supported by:
  - Category purity above chance across conditions and higher purity with the informed sender and under class-level reference.
  - Visual proximity correlating with symbol usage in t-SNE plots.
  - The hybrid supervised/self-play regime markedly increasing purity and interpretability.
  - Human evaluation showing 68% success in interpreting symbols on novel, unconstrained visual referents, with qualitative evidence of metonymic mapping.
- Implications: The work demonstrates that (i) mild inductive biases and environment design can steer emergent codes toward human concepts, and (ii) small amounts of supervised grounding can make emergent communication more human-compatible without sacrificing self-play performance.

Areas for improvement and concerns
- Experimental rigor and ablations:
  - Baselines: Missing controls such as (a) supervised-only sender without self-play, (b) self-play without supervised interleaving for ReferIt images, and (c) larger distractor sets (>2 images) to assess scalability of the learned code.
  - Generalization: It is unclear whether the agents generalize to unseen concepts or only unseen images of seen concepts. Explicit splits and reporting would be valuable.
  - Cross-play: Replace partners after training (or pair senders with novel receivers) to test portability of the emergent language across partners.
  - Communication channel: Analyze the effect of vocabulary size, temperature, and entropy regularization on symbol usage and purity more systematically. The reported symbol collapse in the agnostic sender suggests sensitivity to these settings.
  - RL details: REINFORCE variance reduction (baselines), optimizer, learning rate, initialization, and early stopping are not described. These details affect stability and reproducibility.
- Metrics and analysis:
  - Purity alone is limited; include NMI/ARI and confidence intervals. Clarify how chance levels are computed and whether the number of clusters equals the number of symbols used.
  - The SVD analysis of symbol usage is interesting but ad hoc; add topographic similarity or structure-function correlations to quantify alignment between visual distances and symbol distances.
- Human study:
  - Selection bias: Pairs are chosen where the model succeeded. Include matched controls (random words, shuffled words, or random-choice baseline) to quantify absolute informativeness.
  - Report per-word accuracy distributions and error analyses to diagnose over/underextension patterns.
- Environment design:
  - The informed sender’s architecture bakes in a specific comparison inductive bias; consider permutation-invariant designs or ablations to disentangle architecture from emergent semantics.
  - Test robustness to nuisance variation (backgrounds, color shifts) to validate the claim that agents rely on higher-level properties.
- Presentation:
  - The reference list has numerous formatting issues and incomplete entries; fix for clarity and attribution.
  - Clarify whether fc vs. softmax features affect emergent semantics differently in a controlled comparison.

Questions for the authors
- Generalization: Do agents maintain performance and semantic purity on unseen concepts (zero-shot categories), not only unseen images of seen concepts?
- Cross-play: If you train multiple independent pairs, can a sender communicate with a receiver it has never trained with? How does supervised grounding affect cross-play?
- Channel capacity: How do vocabulary size and temperature influence symbol collapse and purity? Do you observe phase transitions in usage diversity?
- Scalability: How does performance change with more than two candidate images (e.g., 4, 8-way choices) or multi-step communication?
- RL stability: Did you use a variance-reducing baseline in REINFORCE? Which optimizer and hyperparameters? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Human eval controls: What is the accuracy if the provided word is replaced with a random supervised label or with a label from a different symbol used equally often? Can you report confidence intervals and inter-annotator agreement?
- Data splits: Are training and test image pairs fully disjoint at the image level? Were there any concept-level holdouts?

Overall, this is a solid and thought-provoking paper. With stronger ablations, clearer reporting, and tightened human evaluations, it would make a compelling contribution to ICLR.
