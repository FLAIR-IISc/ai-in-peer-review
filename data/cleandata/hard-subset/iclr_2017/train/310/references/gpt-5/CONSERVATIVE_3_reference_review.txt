Summary and contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in visually grounded, two-agent referential games. Agents (sender/receiver) are neural networks trained via REINFORCE, operating on high-level VGG features of real images (ImageNet). The authors compare two sender architectures (agnostic vs informed), show that agents reliably learn to communicate, and analyze symbol usage via SVD and cluster purity with respect to McRae semantic categories.
- They propose a modification (“object-level reference”) that removes low-level common knowledge by showing sender/receiver different images from the same class, nudging symbols toward higher-level semantics.
- They further “ground” the protocol by interleaving the game with a supervised image-labeling task on a subset of ImageNet classes; this increases symbol purity and yields partial human interpretability. In a human study on ReferIt-derived image pairs, humans select the sender’s intended target 68% of the time when shown the emitted word.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Claims about portability to new situations/partners and human-aligned semantics are insufficiently supported; the evaluation lacks rigorous baselines, controls, and generalization tests (e.g., cross-play) expected for such claims.
2) Methodological issues undermine the central “tabula rasa” narrative: the use of pretrained VGG features and overlapping ImageNet labels introduces strong prior knowledge and potential leakage; analyses (purity, SVD) and human evaluation exhibit selection biases and limited statistical rigor.

Supporting arguments
- Not “tabula rasa”: Agents operate on ImageNet-pretrained VGG features and, in the grounding setting, are additionally supervised with ImageNet labels. This materially weakens the claim that communication emerges from scratch and confounds whether coordination relies on pre-existing semantic structure. A control with end-to-end training from pixels or from untrained features is needed.
- Limited baselines and ablations: No comparison to simple supervised/nearest-neighbor policies that emit conventional labels without interaction, or to a non-communicative discriminative model. The “informed sender” bias is not isolated via ablations (e.g., same capacity, different inductive biases). No analysis of temperature, vocabulary size, batch size, or variance-reduction in REINFORCE.
- Statistical rigor: “Purity” is reported without confidence intervals, tests vs randomized baselines, or alternative clustering metrics (e.g., NMI/AMI) controlling for cluster size/imbalance. The SVD “50 dimensions” claim lacks details (cross-validation protocol, explained variance). Human study significance is reported (mixed-effects), but effect size estimates and CIs are missing; the dataset of pairs is constructed conditional on successful receiver choices and on symbol usage, which biases the sample toward easier cases.
- Generalization and robustness: No cross-play experiments (train multiple senders/receivers independently and test across partners) to support portability to new communication partners. No evaluation on unseen object classes, hard distractors (same super-category), or domain shift beyond ReferIt; the ReferIt test still uses words from the supervised label set, raising concerns about label leakage and background cues. Stability across random seeds and multiple runs is not reported.
- Measurement choices: Purity is a coarse proxy for semantics; living/non-living dichotomy could be induced by global visual statistics of VGG features rather than concept learning. t-SNE visualizations are anecdotal. No mutual information between symbols and semantic attributes, no probing of learned embeddings, and no causal tests (e.g., counterfactual swaps).
- Experimental reporting ambiguities: Iteration counts (10k vs 50k), evaluation using sampling vs argmax, details of reward baselines for REINFORCE, and selection of the 100 supervised labels are under-specified. Reference section has multiple formatting errors.
- Scope vs claims: The work is well aligned with prior emergent communication (Foerster, Sukhbaatar, Steels) but the novelty is incremental (natural images + a simple supervised auxiliary objective). Stronger evidence is needed for claims about human interpretability and portability.

Suggestions to improve (not required for the decision)
- Add strong baselines: supervised labeler emitting words; nearest-neighbor over VGG; random and frequency-matched symbolers. Include cross-play, zero-shot classes, hard negative distractor sets, and multi-seed aggregates with CIs.
- Control for pretrained features: end-to-end training; random features; different backbones. Ensure supervised labels do not overlap with evaluation categories; report leakage checks.
- Strengthen analyses: report AMI/NMI and bootstrapped CIs; mutual information between symbols and semantic/category attributes; probing classifiers on sender/receiver embeddings; intervention tests (swap target/distractor features while holding semantics).
- Human study: avoid selection on success; pre-register sampling; include baselines (random word, supervised-only word, human captions); report effect sizes and agreement; analyze failures and metonymy systematically.
- Clarify training details: REINFORCE variance reduction, temperature schedule, symbol sampling at test, hyperparameter sensitivity, and computational budget.
- Improve references and positioning with respect to emergent communication, pragmatic reasoning, and recent grounding literature.

Questions for the authors
1) How do results change with end-to-end training from pixels or with untrained/random CNN features? Is “living/non-living” still discovered?
2) Do independently trained agents cross-play successfully? What is the drop relative to in-pair play?
3) What are the outcomes when supervised labels are disjoint from any ImageNet categories present at test (and from ReferIt)? Any evidence of true semantic transfer beyond label overlap?
4) Can you report robust statistics (CIs) for purity, symbol usage, and human accuracy across multiple seeds and randomizations?
5) How sensitive are results to vocabulary size, temperature, and the informed-sender inductive bias? Are capacity-matched ablations available?
6) In the human study, what is the accuracy if you sample all sender emissions uniformly (including failures) rather than conditioning on correct receiver choices?
