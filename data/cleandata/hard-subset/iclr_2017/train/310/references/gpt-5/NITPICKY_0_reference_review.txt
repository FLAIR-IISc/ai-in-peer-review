Summary and contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in referential games with two neural agents (sender/receiver) trained tabula rasa via RL on natural images (VGG features). It compares two sender architectures (agnostic vs “informed” sender with per-dimension conv), shows fast convergence to near-perfect communication, and analyzes whether symbols align with high-level semantics (category purity, t-SNE, SVD of symbol usage).
- It introduces a variant that removes low-level common knowledge by showing sender and receiver different instances of the same class to encourage object-level semantics.
- It proposes a hybrid regime mixing the self-play game with supervised image labeling (100 ImageNet labels), increasing symbol “purity” and human interpretability. A crowdsourced 2AFC study on ReferIt-derived image pairs shows 68% human success in guessing the sender’s intended target from the emitted word.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Insufficient experimental rigor and reporting: critical methodological details, baselines, statistical analyses, and ablations are missing or unclear, making it hard to assess the validity and generality of the claims.
2) Numerous presentation and formatting issues (broken references, missing figures/tables, inconsistent notation) that significantly impede reproducibility and evaluation.

Supporting arguments
- Methodological clarity
  - Communication distribution: The text alternates between “sigmoid” and “Gibbs distribution with temperature τ” for symbol selection. It is unclear whether the top layer is softmax or sigmoid followed by sampling; temperature τ=10 is unusually high and not motivated; no annealing strategy is described.
  - Test-time policy: Are symbols sampled or chosen via argmax? Reported “almost perfect” coordination is sensitive to this choice.
  - RL details: REINFORCE is mentioned, but no baseline/variance-reduction, optimizer, learning rate, gradient clipping, or reward normalization details are provided. No information on random seeds, number of independent runs, or variability across runs.
  - Informed sender: The “2×1 kernel per dimension” conv on two embeddings is unusual; details (padding, bias, nonlinearity placement, parameter counts, why sigmoid rather than ReLU/tanh) are missing. The claim that the agnostic architecture “nests” the informed one needs a precise argument.
  - Training regime: 50k iterations with batch size 32 is reported, but no training curves with error bars, no validation, and no early-stopping criteria.

- Evaluation rigor
  - Core claim (semantic alignment): Purity is reported, but crucial computation details are missing: exact clustering procedure (k equals vocabulary size or number of actually used symbols?), handling of unused symbols, number of permutations for chance baselines, confidence intervals, and variability across runs. Adjusted Mutual Information or V-measure would be more appropriate than purity alone.
  - SVD analysis of synonymy: Construction of the symbol-by-pair matrix, sample size, cross-validation protocol, and quantitative criterion for “suggests 50 dimensions” are not described.
  - t-SNE visualizations are qualitative; no quantitative nearest-neighbor consistency or center-of-mass analyses are presented.
  - Object-level reference experiment: Improvements are “small”; no effect sizes or significance tests; unclear whether confounds (e.g., class imbalance, domain shift) are controlled.
  - Human study: 68% accuracy on a 2AFC task lacks baselines. At minimum: (i) a lexical association baseline using pretrained word embeddings or WordNet similarity, (ii) a supervised classifier that emits the top-1 ImageNet label for the target as a proxy sender, (iii) a random-symbol baseline with matched frequency. The authors note only 8% of pairs include the supervised word in ReferIt annotations; however, they do not stratify accuracy or provide confusion patterns conditioned on this, nor report inter-annotator agreement, quality control, or compensation. The logistic mixed model is reported with a z-score but no specification of the fixed/random structure, nor effect sizes.
  - Over-reliance on pretrained semantics: Using VGG fc and especially softmax outputs embeds strong semantic priors. The paper should quantify how much of the observed purity arises from the pretrained representation by comparing to lower-layer features or to hand-crafted features.

- Missing baselines and ablations
  - Compare to differentiable communication approaches (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through vs REINFORCE) to assess sample efficiency and stability.
  - Ablate temperature, vocabulary size, embedding size, and receiver architecture. Show sensitivity analyses.
  - Include a sender that simply communicates the argmax class from the sm layer to isolate what the game adds beyond pretrained labelers.
  - Multi-symbol messages or multi-turn protocols are discussed but not evaluated; even a small study would help positioning.

- Reproducibility and presentation
  - Multiple references are malformed or incomplete (e.g., “? \Q2016\E”), and some citations lack venues or page ranges. There are dangling footnote markers and inconsistent punctuation.
  - Figures and tables referred to (Fig. 1–4, Tables 1–2) are missing here; precise numbers (means, std, N) are not included in text.
  - Notation inconsistencies: r(iL,iR,s(…))=t is sometimes missing a parenthesis; target/distractor ordering is fixed for the sender but random for the receiver—this key design choice deserves a clearer justification and analysis of relative-vs-absolute coding strategies.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide full experimental details, code, and seeds; report mean±std over multiple runs, with confidence intervals. Clarify the top-layer activation and sampling policies, training hyperparameters, and variance-reduction tricks for REINFORCE.
- Replace purity with AMI/V-measure and report chance-adjusted metrics; give effect sizes and p-values. For SVD, report explained variance curves and a quantitative criterion.
- Add strong baselines (pretrained classifier, lexical similarity, random, and supervised-only) and ablation studies (architectures, features, temperatures, vocab sizes).
- Explicitly test for relative encoding: evaluate whether symbol usage is stable across different distractors for the same target (context-invariance test).
- For the object-level reference setting, quantify the gain and provide controls ensuring sender/receiver do not share low-level cues.
- Human study: include control conditions, inter-annotator agreement, per-word accuracies, and qualitative error analyses; ensure the analysis conditions on whether the supervised label is present or salient in the scene.
- Discuss limitations: dependence on pretrained visual semantics, narrow bandwidth (single symbol), lack of compositionality; outline a path to multi-word, multi-turn protocols.

Questions for the authors
1) What exactly is the top-layer activation and sampling procedure (sigmoid vs softmax), and what is the role of temperature τ=10? Is τ annealed? What is the test-time policy?
2) How many random seeds/runs per condition? Please report variability, confidence intervals, and significance tests for all key metrics.
3) How is purity computed (number of clusters, handling of unused symbols, permutations for chance)? Can you report AMI/V-measure and mutual information?
4) Can you quantify context-invariance: for a fixed target, how often is the same symbol used across different distractors?
5) What REINFORCE baseline and optimizer did you use? Learning rate, gradient clipping, entropy regularization?
6) For the human study, what were the instructions, QC, compensation, and inter-annotator agreement? Can you report accuracy stratified by whether the supervised word appears in the scene annotations?
7) How much of the effect remains if you use earlier conv layers (less semantic leakage) instead of fc/softmax features?
