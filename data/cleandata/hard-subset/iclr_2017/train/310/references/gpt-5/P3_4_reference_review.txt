Summary and contributions
- The paper studies emergent communication in two-agent referential games grounded in real images. Agents are trained tabula rasa with REINFORCE to communicate a single discrete symbol so the receiver can identify a target among two images.
- Two sender architectures are compared: an “agnostic” MLP and an “informed” sender with a pairwise, dimension-wise convolutional inductive bias. The informed sender converges faster and uses a richer set of symbols.
- The authors analyze whether emergent symbols align with human-interpretable semantics: (i) cluster purity against McRae’s 20 superordinate categories; (ii) t-SNE visualization; (iii) an “object-level” variant that removes low-level common knowledge by showing different images from the same class to sender/receiver; (iv) a grounding intervention mixing supervised object labeling with the game, yielding higher purity and more human-interpretable symbols; and (v) a human evaluation on ReferItGame-based pairs, where humans select the intended target at 68% accuracy given the sender’s word.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The methodological and empirical contributions are incremental relative to a substantial existing literature on emergent communication; (2) The evaluation lacks rigor and depth for ICLR: analyses are largely post-hoc and qualitative, with limited controls, ablations, and statistical support for central claims about “naturalness” and “portability.”

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and motivation
- The core problem—learning to communicate for coordination and shaping incentives/environment to bias emergent codes toward human-interpretable semantics—is important and well-motivated. The cheap-talk/game-theoretic framing is apt, and using real images is valuable.

2) Relation to prior work
- The paper cites early neural emergent communication (e.g., Foerster, Sukhbaatar) and classic signaling-game literature. However, it omits or under-engages with a large body of follow-up work on: sequence-based protocols, compositionality/topographic similarity metrics, population-based training and role rotation, disentangling perceptual bias vs semantics, and evaluation pitfalls (e.g., Havrylov & Titov 2017; Mordatch & Abbeel 2018; Lowe et al. 2019; Chaabouni et al. 2020; Kharitonov et al. 2020). As a result, novelty and positioning are not compelling for ICLR.

3) Methodology and rigor
- The communication setting is single-symbol, single-turn. This severely limits expressivity and does not probe compositional structure. Claims about “language-like” behavior should be tempered or supported with stronger evidence (e.g., topographic similarity, RSA, generalization to novel combinations).
- Training details are sparse: REINFORCE variance reduction/baselines not described; no sensitivity to τ, batch size, embedding size, or number of filters; no analysis of training variance across seeds. The choice τ=10 and pure sampling could yield high variance; it’s unclear how stability was achieved.
- The “informed” sender advantage may stem from architectural inductive bias aligned with the decision boundary rather than deeper “semantic” abstraction. Ablations (e.g., matching parameter counts, using MLPs with pairwise interactions) are needed.
- The “object-level” variant is interesting, but the manipulation is coarse. It is unclear how much low-level leakage remains (VGG fc features for different images of same class are still similar). Quantitative comparisons are small and lack statistical tests.

4) Evaluation and claims support
- “Purity” against McRae categories is a coarse, label-space-dependent measure. It can be driven by the CNN feature space rather than learned semantics. No confidence intervals, randomization/baseline comparisons beyond a single chance figure, or statistical testing are provided.
- The SVD analysis of symbol redundancy is suggestive but underdeveloped (no explained variance thresholds, cross-validated rank reliably computed, or comparisons to synthetic controls).
- Human evaluation (68% on 298 pairs with 10 judgments per pair) is promising but hard to interpret: (i) selection bias (only successful agent rounds included); (ii) crowd quality control not detailed; (iii) no analysis of per-word consistency or error patterns; (iv) referential clarity of the images not controlled; (v) 8% overlap with supervised words suggests that many trials rely on vague metonymy—useful, but it weakens the “portable to humans” claim without stronger analysis of generalization.
- No tests of out-of-distribution generalization (new categories, new distractor distributions), partner swap (new receivers), or grounding durability without ongoing supervision.
- No comparisons to simple, strong baselines: e.g., k-NN over VGG features with a fixed lexicon; attribute-based labeling; or supervised-only sender with learned receiver.

5) Scope of claims
- The paper often suggests that agents “use higher-level semantics” and that the language is “partially aligned with human intuitions.” Given the limited protocol capacity, coarse metrics, and absence of rigorous controls, these claims should be moderated.

Additional feedback and questions to improve the paper
- Report full quantitative results with error bars and statistical tests: symbol usage entropy, mutual information between symbols and categories, topographic similarity between distances in meaning space (image/class) and signal space, and across-seed variance.
- Include ablations: effect of vocabulary size, τ, sender/receiver embedding sizes, architectural capacity parity between informed/agnostic senders, presence/absence of the discretization bottleneck, and different feature inputs (sm vs fc vs lower conv layers).
- Test generalization: (i) unseen categories and category combinations; (ii) adversarial distractor sets (near misses within category); (iii) partner-swap (new receivers) and population training (multiple senders/receivers with role rotation) to assess portability.
- Move beyond single-symbol messages to sequences or multi-turn dialogs; analyze compositionality with established metrics; test whether codes scale with task complexity.
- Strengthen the human study: preregistered protocol, balanced/randomized item sampling (not only successful trials), inter-annotator agreement, calibration of difficulty, and analysis of failure modes.
- Clarify REINFORCE details: baselines/critics, variance reduction, entropy regularization, and sample efficiency. Compare to Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through estimators.
- Provide reproducibility artifacts: data splits, code, hyperparameters, seeds.
- Discuss equilibrium selection explicitly: do different seeds converge to different lexicons? How stable are conventions under perturbations? Do agents converge to degenerate codes if strong low-level common knowledge is reintroduced?

Questions for the authors
1) How many random seeds/training runs were performed, and how variable are purity, symbol usage, and convergence speed across runs?
2) Did you use a baseline/critic in REINFORCE? If so, which, and what was its impact? If not, how did you control variance?
3) Can you report topographic similarity or RSA between image/class distances and the learned symbol space? Do results hold when controlling for CNN similarity?
4) How does performance (coordination and purity) change with message length >1 and with multi-turn interaction?
5) Will the learned code transfer to a new receiver initialized from scratch? What fraction of interactions is needed for the new receiver to align?
6) In the human study, were trials preselected for success by the agent? If so, can you include a random sample of all trials to avoid selection bias?
7) How sensitive are results to the choice of pre-trained features (fc vs sm vs conv), and do you see similar trends with self-supervised features?
8) Does the “object-level” manipulation still yield gains if you strictly prevent low-level leakage (e.g., different domains, augmentations) or use more confusable distractors within a class?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem with appealing ideas, but it needs stronger experimental rigor, broader comparisons, and deeper analyses to meet the bar.
