

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces a novel research program based on multi-agent coordination communication games to develop AI agents capable of learning natural-language-based communication. The authors focus on referential games where agents must coordinate to refer to objects in images, investigating whether agents can develop a language that is semantically meaningful (aligning with human categorizations) and portable to new situations and communication partners (including humans). The work demonstrates that agents can successfully coordinate in these games, develop language with semantic properties (higher-level conceptual properties rather than low-level visual properties), and that grounding communication in natural language through supervised learning on image labels leads to a language that is partially interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in human interpretation). The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging AI, linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory to advance the development of conversational AI.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with scientifically rigorous results that effectively address the central question of whether agents can develop a language that is semantically meaningful and portable to human communication.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's central claim is well-supported by multiple lines of empirical evidence. First, the authors demonstrate that agents successfully coordinate in referential games, with the informed sender architecture converging to higher levels of coordination more quickly than the agnostic architecture. Second, they provide compelling evidence that the emergent language has semantic properties aligned with human categorizations: purity scores significantly above chance (45-70% compared to 20-30% chance) when evaluating symbol usage against McRae's 20 semantic categories. Third, and most significantly, the paper demonstrates that grounding communication through supervised learning on image labels leads to a language that is partially interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in a crowdsourced experiment), with evidence of metonymic extensions (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea). 

The methodology is scientifically rigorous, with controlled experimental variations (different sender architectures, object-level reference, grounding with supervised learning) and appropriate statistical validation (purity scores compared to chance, significance testing for human interpretation results). The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly distinguishing its approach from supervised learning (which captures statistical associations but not functional aspects of communication), human-in-the-loop approaches (which face scalability issues), and Wizard-of-Oz environments (which impose heavy engineering burdens). The connection to game theory (Lewis' signaling game) and linguistic studies strengthens the theoretical foundation.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could more explicitly articulate the specific novelty compared to prior work in multi-agent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), particularly regarding how their approach specifically advances the development of human-interpretable language.
- The claim that the informed sender uses "more varied and word-like symbols" would be strengthened with more detailed qualitative analysis of symbol usage patterns, beyond the SVD analysis presented.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the current approach, particularly regarding scalability to more complex communication tasks and robustness to real-world noise.
- The paper mentions "metonymic" extensions of language (e.g., "dolphin" for sea), but could more explicitly discuss how this relates to established linguistic theories of language acquisition and extension.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How do the authors plan to scale this approach to more complex communication tasks beyond simple referential games, such as multi-step conversations or reference to abstract concepts?
- The paper demonstrates that agents can develop metonymic links (e.g., "dolphin" for sea), but how does this compare to human language acquisition processes, and what are the implications for the long-term viability of this approach for developing truly human-like communication?
- The paper states that the informed sender uses "more varied and word-like symbols" but provides primarily quantitative evidence. Could the authors provide additional qualitative examples of symbol usage patterns to better illustrate this claim?
- How might the approach handle ambiguity in natural language (e.g., homonyms, polysemy) that would be encountered in more complex communication scenarios?
