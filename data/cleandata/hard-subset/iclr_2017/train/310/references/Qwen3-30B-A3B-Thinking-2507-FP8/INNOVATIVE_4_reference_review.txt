

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a bold and novel research program for developing AI agents that can learn natural language through multi-agent coordination games rather than supervised learning. The authors propose a framework where agents start as "blank slates" and develop language through interaction in simple environments, with the goal of creating a language that is portable to new situations and interpretable by humans. The key contribution is demonstrating that agents can learn to communicate effectively in referential games, and that the emergent language develops semantic properties resembling human language (e.g., categorizing objects by general concepts rather than low-level visual features). Most significantly, the paper introduces a novel method for grounding this emergent language in human natural language through a combination of supervised learning on image labels and interactive game play, with compelling evidence that humans can interpret the agents' communication (68% accuracy in a crowdsourced evaluation).

## 2. Decision

Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments

I recommend acceptance primarily for the paper's significant novelty and bold conceptual contribution. While the methodological approach has some limitations (e.g., the simplicity of the game environment), the paper introduces a fundamentally new direction for language learning in AI that goes beyond incremental improvements to existing supervised learning approaches. The authors make a strong case for why this approach is necessary: "Supervised approaches, which focus on the structure of language, are an excellent way to learn general statistical associations between sequences of symbols. However, they do not capture the functional aspects of communication, i.e., that humans use words to coordinate with others and make things happen."

The paper's most compelling contribution is the demonstration that emergent language can be grounded in human language through a simple combination of supervised learning and interactive game play, with humans successfully interpreting the agents' communication (68% accuracy). This is a significant step toward practical conversational AI that could potentially bypass the need for large-scale human annotation.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from related work in multi-agent systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, and Wizard-of-Oz environments. It makes a strong connection to linguistic and cognitive science research on language emergence, positioning the work as both theoretically interesting and practically relevant.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper makes a strong case for the novelty of the approach, the authors could further clarify the distinction between their method and previous work on emergent communication. Specifically, while they mention Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016), they could more explicitly explain how their grounding approach differs from these prior works.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach. For instance, how does this method scale to more complex language tasks beyond simple reference games? What are the specific constraints on the environment that make this approach work, and what would happen if those constraints were relaxed?

The human evaluation is promising but limited to 298 image pairs. A more comprehensive evaluation across a wider range of image categories would strengthen the claim that the language is interpretable by humans.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you plan to scale this approach to more complex language tasks beyond simple referential games? What specific challenges would you anticipate in scaling the method?

2. The paper demonstrates that agents can develop a language that is interpretable by humans when grounded with supervised learning. However, the current method still requires a supervised phase. How do you envision this approach evolving to require minimal or no human supervision in the long term?

3. The paper mentions that the agents develop "metonymic" links between words and images (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to a stretch of sea). How does this compare to how humans develop such semantic extensions in natural language, and what implications does this have for the naturalness of the emergent language?
