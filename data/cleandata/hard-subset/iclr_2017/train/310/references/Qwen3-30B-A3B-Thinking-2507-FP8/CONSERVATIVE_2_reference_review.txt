

# Review

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims/contribution

This paper introduces a multi-agent coordination framework for developing communication protocols through referential games, where agents learn to communicate about visual objects without pre-defined language. The authors claim that agents can develop communication protocols that associate symbols with general conceptual properties rather than low-level visual features, and that these protocols can be grounded in human language by combining the referential game with supervised image classification. They present evidence through purity metrics, t-SNE visualizations, and a human interpretability experiment showing 68% accuracy in a crowdsourced task. The paper positions itself as bridging the gap between multi-agent communication research and practical human-interpretable language development.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to support the claim that the emergent communication protocols are truly interpretable by humans or resemble natural language. The evidence presented is limited and not rigorously compared to established baselines.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The primary issue is the lack of rigorous evidence supporting the paper's central claim about human-interpretable communication. While the authors show a modest 68% accuracy in a crowdsourced human interpretation task, this is not strong evidence that the communication is truly interpretable or useful for practical human-AI interaction. In natural language processing, 68% accuracy would be considered quite low for a meaningful language understanding system, especially given the simplicity of the task (choosing between two images based on a symbol).

Furthermore, the paper fails to provide meaningful comparison to established methods. They don't benchmark against existing communication protocols or language learning approaches, making it impossible to determine if their approach is actually better than current methods. The paper claims their method "fosters the development of a language that is portable to new situations," but provides no evidence of generalization beyond the specific game setup.

The purity metrics they report (70% purity with grounding) are not particularly high, and the paper doesn't establish what level of purity would be considered "natural" language. The human interpretability experiment is limited to 298 image pairs, which is a small sample size for such a critical claim. The paper also doesn't demonstrate that the communication would be useful in complex, real-world scenarios where human-AI interaction would actually occur.

## 4. Additional feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide rigorous comparison with established communication protocols in multi-agent systems, including quantitative metrics for communication quality and human interpretability.

2. Conduct more extensive human evaluation across a wider range of scenarios, including more complex visual contexts and longer communication sequences.

3. Demonstrate practical utility of the developed communication protocol in a meaningful human-AI interaction task, not just a simple image selection experiment.

4. Provide evidence that the communication protocols generalize beyond the specific game setup to more realistic scenarios.

Questions for the authors:

1. How would you quantify "natural language" in this context, and what level of purity or interpretability would you consider sufficient for practical applications?

2. Can you provide evidence that your approach outperforms existing methods for developing interpretable communication protocols in multi-agent systems, particularly in terms of human interpretability metrics?

3. What is your plan to address the scalability of your approach to more complex communication tasks beyond the simple referential game?

4. How do you reconcile your claim that the communication is "portable to new situations" with the fact that your human interpretability experiment shows limited generalization (only 47% exact match to supervised labels for images in supervised categories)?

The paper presents an interesting research direction, but the current evidence is insufficient to support the bold claims made about human-interpretable language development. The authors would need to provide substantially more rigorous evidence and comparisons before this work could be considered for publication at ICLR.
