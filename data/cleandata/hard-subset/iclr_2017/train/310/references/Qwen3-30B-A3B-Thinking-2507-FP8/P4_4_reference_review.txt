

# Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding how natural language communication might emerge through multi-agent coordination games. The authors introduce a framework for training agents to develop communication protocols through referential games, where two agents must coordinate to identify a target image from a pair of images. The paper demonstrates that agents can learn to communicate effectively in this setup, and importantly, that the emergent communication protocols develop semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories (e.g., object categories rather than low-level visual properties). The authors further show that by combining the referential game with a supervised image classification task, the agents' communication becomes significantly more interpretable to humans, with a crowd-sourcing experiment demonstrating that humans can understand the agents' communication in 68% of cases.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field with scientifically rigorous experiments that address a well-defined problem. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper provides compelling evidence that agents can develop communication protocols with semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories, and (2) it demonstrates a practical method for grounding these protocols in human language through supervised learning, with human interpretability validated through a crowd-sourcing experiment.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a fundamental question in AI: how to develop agents that can communicate in natural language with humans. The authors correctly identify that supervised learning approaches fail to capture the functional aspects of communication, and they propose a game-theoretic framework to overcome this limitation. Their experimental design is well-suited to answer the research questions.

The paper provides strong empirical evidence for their claims. The convergence of agents to successful communication is demonstrated with clear metrics (Table 1), and they provide multiple lines of evidence for semantic alignment: cluster purity with respect to human categories (Table 1), t-SNE visualizations of semantic relationships (Figure 3), and the effect of game modifications that remove low-level visual common knowledge (Table 2). The results show that agents develop communication protocols that are significantly above chance in terms of semantic alignment, with the informed sender showing better performance than the agnostic sender.

Most importantly, the paper demonstrates a practical path to human-interpretable communication. The supervised grounding experiment is particularly compelling, showing that agents can achieve 68% human interpretability through a combination of game-based learning and supervised image labeling. The crowd-sourcing experiment with 298 image pairs and 10 ratings per pair provides a rigorous assessment of human interpretability, and the statistical significance of the results (z = 16.75, p < 0.0001) is appropriately reported.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building on relevant work in multi-agent systems (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), game theory (Lewis, 1969), and language emergence (Wagner et al., 2003; Skyrms, 2010). The authors clearly distinguish their approach from other methods, including human-in-the-loop approaches and Wizard-of-Oz environments, and provide a convincing argument for the advantages of their game-based paradigm.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors could clarify the relationship between the different agent architectures. The paper states that the informed sender "nests" the agnostic sender, but the precise relationship between these architectures and how the inductive bias affects learning would benefit from more detailed explanation.

The paper could also provide more information about the specific hyperparameters used in the supervised grounding experiment, particularly the 100 labels used for supervised training. A brief description of how these labels were selected (e.g., whether they were randomly sampled from the 463 concepts or selected based on some criteria) would help readers understand the scope of the grounding.

The authors might consider discussing the limitations of their approach more explicitly, particularly regarding the scalability of the supervised grounding method. How would this approach scale to larger vocabularies or more complex visual domains?

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses "more varied and word-like symbols" than the agnostic sender. Could the authors provide a more precise quantitative measure of this difference (e.g., entropy of symbol usage, distinct symbols used, etc.) to strengthen their claim?

2. In the supervised grounding experiment, the authors report that the sender used 88 symbols out of 100 after training. What was the specific distribution of symbol usage, and how did this relate to the supervised labels? Were certain labels consistently used more than others?

3. The crowd-sourcing experiment showed 68% human interpretability. What were the specific failure cases, and what characteristics did these cases share (e.g., were they mostly images of objects not in the supervised set, or were there specific types of semantic shifts that confused humans)?

4. The authors mention that "the language developed in referential games will be initially very limited." How do they envision scaling this approach to more complex communication tasks beyond basic object reference?
