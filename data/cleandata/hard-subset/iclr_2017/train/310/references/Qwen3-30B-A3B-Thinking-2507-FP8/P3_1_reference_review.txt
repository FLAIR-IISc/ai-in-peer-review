

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims and Contribution

This paper presents a compelling research program on developing natural-language communication protocols for AI agents through multi-agent coordination games. The authors make a strong case for moving beyond supervised learning approaches to natural language processing by proposing a framework where agents learn communication through interactive game play, rather than from pre-labeled data. Their key contributions include:

- Demonstrating that tabula rasa neural network agents can successfully develop communication protocols in referential games, achieving near-perfect coordination.
- Showing that the emergent language has semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories (e.g., object categories rather than low-level visual features).
- Proposing a novel method to ground the emergent language in human natural language by combining the referential game with a supervised image classification task.
- Providing evidence that the grounded language can be partially understood by humans through a crowdsourced study.

The paper is well-positioned at the intersection of multi-agent reinforcement learning, natural language processing, and cognitive science, and makes a significant contribution to the field by bridging theoretical work on language emergence with practical AI development.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in AI (natural language communication with humans) with a novel, well-motivated approach that advances the field beyond existing supervised learning paradigms.
2. The experimental design is rigorous and carefully executed, with multiple experiments supporting the claims about semantic properties and human interpretability.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

### Strengths

**Well-motivated research question:** The paper correctly identifies the gap between supervised learning approaches to language (which focus on statistical associations) and the functional aspects of communication (which require coordination). The authors properly position their work within the literature, distinguishing it from pre-programmed communication systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, and scripted environment approaches. Their grounding in game theory (Lewis' signaling games) and cognitive science literature is appropriate and well-executed.

**Rigorous experimental design:** The authors conducted a thorough set of experiments that systematically investigate the questions they pose. The comparison between agnostic and informed sender architectures, the modification of the game to remove low-level common knowledge, and the grounding approach with supervised learning provide a comprehensive exploration of the problem space.

**Significant results:** The paper demonstrates not only that agents can learn to communicate (reaching near-perfect coordination), but also that the communication protocol develops semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories. The human interpretability study is particularly compelling, showing that the grounded language is partially understandable to humans (68% accuracy in guessing the correct image), which directly addresses the paper's core goal of developing communication protocols that can be used with humans.

**Clear technical contributions:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the technical understanding of how language can emerge from multi-agent interactions, with the informed sender architecture providing an elegant inductive bias that leads to more natural language development.

### Minor Concerns

The paper's main limitations are somewhat acknowledged but could be more thoroughly addressed:

1. **Human study limitations:** While the human study is well-conducted, the sample size (298 image pairs) and the fact that it's crowdsourced without detailed demographic information might limit the generalizability of the results. The paper should acknowledge these limitations more explicitly.

2. **"Grounding" as a shortcut:** The approach to grounding the language in human language through supervised learning is effective but might be seen as circumventing the core problem of emergent language. The authors should more clearly discuss how this approach differs from simply pre-training on human-labeled data.

3. **Scalability concerns:** The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the approach would scale to more complex communication tasks beyond simple referential games (e.g., multi-step conversations, referring to abstract concepts).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

### Technical and Experimental Improvements

1. **Statistical rigor:** The paper mentions that results are "significantly above chance" but doesn't provide specific p-values or confidence intervals for many of the key metrics (like purity scores). Adding statistical significance testing would strengthen the claims.

2. **Human study details:** Provide more details about the human study: demographics of participants, instructions given to participants, how they were compensated, and how the baseline accuracy for random guessing was determined. This would help assess the reliability of the human interpretability results.

3. **Architecture justification:** The paper mentions that the informed sender architecture "nests" the agnostic one but doesn't clearly explain the theoretical justification for this design choice. A brief discussion of why this architecture might be expected to produce more natural language would be helpful.

4. **Limitations section:** Expand the discussion of limitations to include the following:
   - How the approach might scale to more complex communication tasks
   - Potential biases in the ImageNet dataset that might influence the emergent language
   - Whether the approach would work with more diverse input modalities (e.g., audio, video)

### Theoretical and Conceptual Improvements

1. **Game-theoretic perspective:** The paper references game theory extensively but doesn't clearly connect the results to specific game-theoretic equilibria. A brief discussion of which type of equilibrium (e.g., precise language, vague language, no language) the agents are converging to would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

2. **Comparison to recent work:** The paper cites relevant work but could include a more detailed comparison to recent multi-agent communication approaches (e.g., the work by Lazaridou et al. on multi-agent reinforcement learning for communication).

3. **"Natural language" definition:** The paper uses the phrase "natural language" but doesn't explicitly define what it means for a communication protocol to be "natural" in this context. A more precise definition would help clarify the claims.

### Questions for Authors

1. How did the authors determine the specific hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality=50, number of filters=20, temperature=10)? Were these chosen through extensive hyperparameter tuning, or are they fixed based on prior work?

2. In the human interpretability study, what was the baseline accuracy for random guessing? How did the authors ensure that participants weren't simply guessing randomly rather than understanding the language?

3. The paper states that the informed sender architecture "nests" the agnostic one. Can you clarify this relationship and explain why you chose to use this architecture specifically?

4. How do you plan to scale this approach to more complex communication tasks beyond simple referential games (e.g., multi-step conversations, referring to abstract concepts)?

5. The paper mentions that the supervised learning approach "does not aim at improving agents' coordination performance" but the results show coordination performance remains high. How do you reconcile this with the fact that the supervised learning might actually improve coordination performance?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of AI communication. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and rigorously evaluated. The results provide compelling evidence that agents can develop communication protocols that align with human semantic categories and can be partially understood by humans. While there are some limitations to address (particularly regarding scalability and the "grounding" approach), these are common challenges in this research area and don't undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-suited for ICLR and would be a valuable addition to the literature.
