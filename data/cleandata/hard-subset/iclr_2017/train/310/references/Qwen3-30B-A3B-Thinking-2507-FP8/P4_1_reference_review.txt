

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces a novel multi-agent coordination communication framework for training AI agents to develop language through game-based interaction. The authors address the central problem of how to design environments that foster the development of a language portable to new situations and communication partners, particularly humans. They demonstrate that agents can learn to communicate effectively in referential games using natural images, develop symbol meanings aligned with human semantic categories (e.g., object categories rather than low-level visual features), and show that grounding through supervised learning improves human interpretability of the emergent language. The work bridges theoretical game-theoretic models of language emergence with practical AI applications.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of language emergence in AI through well-designed experiments that address a fundamental challenge in natural language processing. The central claims are well-supported by rigorous empirical evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully answers its core question about designing environments for portable language development. The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from supervised learning approaches, pre-programmed communication systems, and human-in-the-loop methods. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation by connecting to Lewis' signaling game and evolutionary perspectives on language emergence.

The paper provides compelling empirical evidence supporting its claims. The experiments demonstrate that agents can successfully coordinate through communication (Table 1), develop symbol meanings aligned with human semantic categories (Table 1, Figure 3), and that grounding through supervised learning significantly improves human interpretability (Figure 4, crowdsourced results). The authors employ appropriate statistical measures (purity, t-SNE visualization, crowdsourced evaluation) and provide sufficient detail about experimental setup to verify reproducibility. The results are scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines, controls, and statistical analysis.

The most compelling contribution is the demonstration that supervised grounding significantly improves human interpretability (68% accuracy in crowdsourced evaluation), with the language exhibiting metonymic properties similar to natural language. This bridges the gap between theoretical language emergence and practical applications for human-AI communication.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following improvements:

1. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the "informed sender" architecture differs from the "agnostic sender" beyond the technical description. A brief qualitative example of how their different inductive biases lead to different communication patterns would enhance understanding.

2. The authors should more explicitly discuss the limitations of the current approach. For instance, how does the current framework scale to more complex communication scenarios beyond simple referential games? What are the computational costs compared to alternative approaches?

3. The paper mentions "object-level reference" as a modification to the game setup, but the experimental results in Table 2 are not as clearly presented as other results. A more detailed discussion of these results would strengthen the paper.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper shows that agents can develop a language with semantic properties, but how would the framework handle communication about abstract concepts (e.g., emotions, intentions) rather than concrete visual objects? Could this approach be extended to such scenarios?

2. The authors demonstrate that grounding through supervised learning improves human interpretability, but what are the specific limitations of this approach? For example, how does performance change when the supervised labels are sparse or when the language must generalize to novel concepts not present in the supervised data?

3. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses more dimensions to represent the language (SVD analysis), but does this correlate with better performance on tasks requiring more nuanced communication? If so, could this dimensionality be used as a metric for evaluating the "quality" of emergent languages in future work?
