

# Review of "Learning to Communicate through Multi-Agent Coordination Games"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces a novel research program for developing AI agents that can communicate through multi-agent coordination games. The authors propose using referential games where two agents (sender and receiver) must develop a language to identify target images from pairs of images. The key contributions include demonstrating that tabula rasa agents can successfully coordinate, developing a method to ground emergent communication in human language through supervised image classification, and providing evidence that the emergent language reflects higher-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features. The paper makes an interesting connection to linguistic theory and presents a compelling vision for developing AI that can communicate with humans.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's core claims about the emergence of meaningful language and human-interpretable communication are undermined by critical methodological inconsistencies and lack of statistical rigor.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary issue is a fundamental inconsistency in the experimental setup. The authors state they use "activations from either the top 1000-D softmax layer (sm) or the second-to-last 4096-D fully connected layer (fc)" (Section 3), but only report results for the fc layer throughout the paper. This creates a significant gap in the experimental evaluation. The authors should have provided a comparative analysis of both representations, as the choice of representation could substantially impact the results. The absence of any results for the softmax representation makes it impossible to evaluate whether this choice was justified or whether the results generalize to other representations.

Furthermore, the paper lacks statistical significance testing for all key claims. For example, the paper states "purity, while far from perfect, is significantly above chance in all cases" (Section 4), but provides no statistical test to support this claim. Similarly, the claim that "symbol purity dramatically increases to 70%" (Section 5) lacks a proper statistical test to confirm this is a meaningful improvement. The human interpretability results (68% accuracy) are also presented without comparison to chance (50% for binary choice) or statistical significance testing.

The paper also contains multiple formatting inconsistencies and incomplete details. The references section has inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., "Deng et al\.,? \Q2009\E, Deng et al\.,"). The paper mentions "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" but does not include these figures in the provided text. The test set construction method is described as "using the same method as for the training games" but without specific details on how this was implemented.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Report results for both image representations (softmax and fc) and analyze why one representation might be superior. This would provide a more complete evaluation of their approach.

2. Add statistical significance testing for all key claims. For example, provide t-tests or chi-square tests to confirm that purity scores are significantly above chance and that improvements after grounding are statistically meaningful.

3. Clarify the test set construction method and confirm whether training and test sets are disjoint, which is critical for valid evaluation.

4. Fix all formatting inconsistencies in the references and text, including the citation style and figure references.

5. Provide more details about the supervised image classification task, including the exact number of training examples used and how it compares to the full ImageNet dataset.

The paper's core idea is promising and well-motivated, but the current presentation does not provide sufficient evidence to support the authors' claims. The methodological inconsistencies and lack of statistical rigor are too substantial to overlook, especially for a venue like ICLR that emphasizes methodological rigor.

## Questions for Authors

1. Why was the softmax layer representation not evaluated? Was there a specific reason to exclude it, or was it an oversight?

2. Can you provide the exact statistical tests that support the claim that purity scores are "significantly above chance"?

3. How many training examples were used for the supervised image classification task, and how does this compare to the size of the full ImageNet dataset?

4. Was the test set of 10k games completely disjoint from the training set, and if not, what steps were taken to prevent data leakage?

5. How do you plan to scale this approach to more complex communication tasks beyond simple referential games, and what challenges might arise in doing so?
