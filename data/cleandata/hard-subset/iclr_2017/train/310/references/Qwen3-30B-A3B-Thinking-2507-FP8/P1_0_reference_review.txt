

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication and language learning by introducing a novel framework for developing interpretable communication protocols between AI agents that can be understood by humans. The authors address a fundamental challenge in AI: how to develop communication systems that are not only functional but also align with human language understanding. 

The paper's core contribution is a multi-agent coordination framework where agents learn to develop communication protocols through referential games. The authors demonstrate that agents can develop language-like communication that associates symbols with high-level conceptual properties rather than low-level visual features. Most importantly, they show that by incorporating supervised learning on human language data, the emergent communication becomes significantly more interpretable by humans. This bridges a critical gap between theoretical work on language emergence and practical applications for human-AI communication.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly positioned within the literature, and provides strong empirical evidence for its claims. The results are scientifically rigorous, with multiple lines of evidence supporting the main claims: successful coordination, semantic alignment with human categories, and improved human interpretability through supervised grounding.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field with well-motivated research questions, scientifically rigorous experiments, and clear evidence supporting its claims. The key strength is demonstrating a path toward human-interpretable communication protocols through a combination of multi-agent interaction and supervised grounding.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strengths justify acceptance:

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation**: The paper effectively articulates the central problem of developing language that is portable to new situations and communication partners (particularly humans). The introduction powerfully frames the problem using both theoretical (Lewis' signaling game) and practical (Ted Chiang's quote) perspectives.

2. **Strong empirical evidence**: The paper provides multiple lines of evidence supporting its claims:
   - Agents successfully coordinate in the referential game (Table 1)
   - Emergent language aligns with human semantic categories (Figure 3, Table 1)
   - Supervised grounding dramatically improves human interpretability (Section 5, Figure 4)
   - Human evaluations confirm the language's understandability (68% accuracy in the crowdsourced survey)

3. **Clear distinction from related work**: The paper effectively differentiates itself from other approaches (pre-programmed communication, Wizard-of-Oz, and "cheap talk" game theory) by focusing on developing interpretable language through multi-agent interaction combined with supervised grounding.

4. **Scientifically rigorous methodology**: The experimental design includes appropriate baselines, ablation studies, and human evaluations. The use of t-SNE visualization and purity metrics provides compelling evidence for semantic alignment.

5. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The paper connects to relevant work in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory (Lewis, Skyrms, Crawford & Sobel), while maintaining focus on practical AI applications.

The paper's main limitation is that it's a first step in a research program rather than a complete solution to the problem of human-interpretable communication, but this is acknowledged and appropriately framed as such.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Reproducibility details**: Include more specific experimental details to improve reproducibility. For example, specify the exact hyperparameters used for the different sender architectures (e.g., number of layers, activation functions), the exact random seed values used in experiments, and the specific implementation of the Gibbs distribution temperature.

2. **Statistical significance**: Add statistical significance testing for key comparisons (e.g., between informed vs. agnostic senders, with and without supervised grounding) to strengthen the claims about performance differences.

3. **Limitations discussion**: Expand the discussion of limitations. For instance, how does the approach scale to more complex communication scenarios? What are the failure modes of the supervised grounding approach? How does the language development depend on the quality of the supervised data?

4. **Practical implications**: Elaborate on how this framework could be integrated into more complex AI systems. Provide concrete examples of how this could be used in real-world applications (e.g., human-AI collaboration in medical diagnosis, customer service).

5. **Connection to natural language processing**: Strengthen the connection to current NLP approaches. How does this work relate to recent advances in language models, and what unique insights does it provide that aren't captured by large pre-trained language models?

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and provide additional evidence for my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. In the section on "Object-Level Reference," you report a small increase in purity when using class-level properties instead of image-level properties. Could you provide more detail on how the class-level properties were implemented (e.g., did you use ImageNet class labels directly, or did you use the McRae categories), and how this compares to other methods of using class-level information?

2. In the human evaluation (Section 5), you report 68% accuracy in the crowdsourced survey. What was the performance of a baseline human-to-human communication task (e.g., showing humans the same image pairs with the corresponding symbol and asking them to identify the target image)? This would help contextualize whether the 68% is good or poor relative to human performance.

3. You mention that "the language developed in referential games will be initially very limited." How do you envision this limitation being overcome in future work, particularly in terms of expanding the vocabulary and semantic scope of the communication protocol?

4. The paper states that the informed sender uses more symbols than the agnostic sender. Could you elaborate on why this is the case, given that the agnostic architecture "nests" the informed one? Is this due to the inductive bias of the informed architecture, or is there a different factor at play?

5. How does the supervised grounding approach interact with the interactive learning process? Specifically, does the supervised learning help the agents converge faster to effective communication, or does it primarily affect the interpretability of the language without affecting coordination success?
