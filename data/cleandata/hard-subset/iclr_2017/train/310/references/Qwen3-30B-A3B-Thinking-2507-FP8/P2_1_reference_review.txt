

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of AI communication by introducing a multi-agent coordination framework for developing natural language communication without relying on pre-programmed protocols or human supervision during training. The authors demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to communicate in a referential game where they must coordinate to identify target images from distractors. Crucially, they show that the communication protocols that emerge align with human semantic categories rather than low-level visual properties. The paper further introduces a novel method for grounding these emergent communication protocols in human language through a combination of supervised image labeling and interactive game play, resulting in a communication system that is partially interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in a crowdsourced interpretation task). The work bridges important gaps between AI, linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, with clear potential for practical applications in developing conversational AI.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

My key reasons for acceptance are: 
1) The paper makes a compelling contribution to a fundamental problem in AI (natural language communication) by demonstrating that agents can develop semantically meaningful communication protocols through multi-agent coordination games, with a novel method for grounding these protocols in human language.
2) The experimental design is rigorous, with appropriate metrics, controls, and statistical validation that convincingly support the claims about semantic alignment and human interpretability.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

**Strong motivation and clear problem statement:** The paper begins with a powerful analogy to Ted Chiang's "Story of Your Life" that perfectly frames the core problem: language must be learned through interaction, not from canned conversations. The authors correctly identify the limitation of supervised approaches to language learning (focusing on structure rather than function) and position their work as addressing this fundamental gap. The problem statement is clear, well-motivated, and places the work in the proper context of both AI and linguistic/cognitive science literature.

**Rigorous methodology and compelling results:** The experimental design is sound and comprehensive. The authors test different agent architectures (agnostic vs. informed senders), explore environmental modifications (using image-level vs. class-level properties), and introduce a novel method for grounding communication in human language. Their results are scientifically rigorous, with appropriate metrics (purity, t-SNE visualizations, human interpretation surveys) and statistical validation (comparing to random permutation for purity, reporting confidence intervals for human interpretation results). The finding that agents can develop communication protocols that align with human semantic categories (even when using only two symbols) is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates the emergence of meaningful conceptual properties rather than just statistical associations.

**Novel contribution to grounding language in human communication:** The paper's most significant contribution is the method for grounding agents' communication in human language through a combination of supervised learning on image labels and interactive game play. The 68% human interpretation accuracy is impressive for a system that developed communication without human supervision during training, and the qualitative examples (like the dolphin/fence metonymic examples) show the potential for flexible, human-compatible communication. This is a major step toward developing conversational AI that can interact with humans in natural ways.

**Clear connection to relevant literature:** The paper situates itself well in the literature, distinguishing from pre-programmed communication systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, and other game-based language learning work. The connection to Lewis' signaling game and the evolutionary perspective on language learning is particularly strong, and the authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of previous work while positioning their contribution.

## 4. Additional feedback to improve the paper

While the paper is strong overall, these suggestions would further strengthen it:

1. **Clarify the definition of "natural language"**: The paper claims the emergent language "resembles human language," but it doesn't explicitly define what aspects of human language it's resembling. A brief discussion of specific linguistic properties (e.g., compositionality, productivity, semantic transparency) that the emergent language exhibits would strengthen this claim.

2. **Discuss limitations of the human interpretation results**: The 68% accuracy is good, but it's still far from perfect. A more thorough discussion of why the interpretation accuracy isn't higher (e.g., whether the language is too limited, whether the human task was too difficult, whether the language has too much metonymic flexibility) would strengthen the paper.

3. **Compare with recent work**: The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison with recent work on language emergence in neural networks (e.g., Lazaridou et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2017), particularly regarding the scalability of the approach to more complex language tasks.

4. **Discuss computational costs**: The paper doesn't address the computational resources required for training these agents. Given the importance of scalability in practical AI, a brief discussion of training time, data requirements, and computational resources would be valuable.

5. **Expand on the theoretical contribution**: The paper mentions that it speaks to game theorists about convergence and equilibrium selection. A more detailed discussion of the theoretical implications of the results for game theory would strengthen the paper's contribution to that field.

## 5. Questions for the authors

1. The paper states that the agents using only two symbols converged to a "living-vs-non-living" characterization. How do you know this wasn't simply a coincidence of the specific image set used? Would this pattern hold with a different set of images?

2. In the human interpretation experiment, the paper reports 68% accuracy. How did you ensure that the human participants understood the task correctly (e.g., through training or instructions)? What was the baseline accuracy for humans guessing randomly?

3. The paper mentions that the language developed is "partially interpretable" by humans. Could you provide more examples of cases where the interpretation was incorrect, and what the most common errors were? This would help understand the limitations of the approach.

4. The paper states that the informed sender uses more symbols (88 out of 100) when grounded in human language. How does this compare to the number of symbols used in the non-grounded experiments? Is there a relationship between the number of symbols used and the quality of semantic alignment?

5. The paper mentions that the language developed through this process is limited. What specific limitations do you anticipate as the approach is scaled to more complex communication tasks, and how might these be addressed in future work?
