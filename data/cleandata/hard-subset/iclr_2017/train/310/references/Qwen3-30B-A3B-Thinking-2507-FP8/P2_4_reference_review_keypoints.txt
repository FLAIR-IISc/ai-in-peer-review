- Introduces a framework for studying emergence of natural language in multi-agent referential games where agents learn communication without pre-programmed language.

- Demonstrates emergent communication encodes high-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features.

- Proposes grounding emergent language via a simple combination of interactive game play and supervised learning on image labels.

- Empirical evaluation uses multiple complementary metrics (communication success, semantic purity, human interpretability) with careful ablations and statistical validation.

- Achieves near-perfect coordination after ~5,000 training rounds and purity scores significantly above chance.

- Supervised grounding yields strong human interpretability (68% accuracy) despite no human supervision during communication learning.

- Clearly positions the work in literature, distinguishing from pre-programmed, human-in-the-loop, and Wizard-of-Oz approaches and linking to signaling-game traditions.

- Decision: Accept — motivated, novel approach that addresses a fundamental AI problem with rigorous experiments.

- Strength: Well-articulated research question about designing environments that produce portable, partner-agnostic language.

- Strength: Well-motivated approach and thorough related-work survey situating contribution in both AI and linguistic theory.

- Strength: Qualitative examples (e.g., dolphin→sea) and varied metrics provide compelling evidence for claims.

- Limitation: Needs a more detailed discussion of scalability to complex environments, longer conversations, and computational costs.

- Limitation: Should more explicitly relate empirical findings to game-theoretic predictions about equilibrium selection in cheap-talk games.

- Limitation: Lacks direct comparison to other grounding methods (linguistic priors, human feedback).

- Question: How do ReferItGame results (agents using all 100 symbols) compare to ImageNet experiments in communication success and human interpretability?

- Question: How do observed metonymic usages relate to human semantic shifts and can the approach capture additional human-language characteristics?

- Question: What specific inductive biases enable the informed sender to use more varied symbols, and what architectural choices could further improve interpretability?
