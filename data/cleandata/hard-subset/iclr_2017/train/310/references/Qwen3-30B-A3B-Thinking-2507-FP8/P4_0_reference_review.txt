

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces a novel framework for developing AI agents that learn to communicate through multi-agent coordination games, rather than relying on supervised learning from pre-existing conversations. The authors propose referential games where two agents (sender and receiver) must coordinate on which of two images is the target by developing a communication protocol. They demonstrate that agents can learn effective communication, with the emergent language exhibiting high-level semantic properties rather than relying on low-level visual features. The paper further shows that grounding agents' communication in human language through supervised learning on image labels significantly improves human interpretability (68% accuracy in human experiments). This work makes a significant contribution to understanding how communication can emerge in AI systems while remaining aligned with human language.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with well-supported claims about how communication can emerge in multi-agent systems and be grounded in human language.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper thoroughly addresses its central question: "How do we design environments that foster the development of a language that is portable to new situations and to new communication partners (in particular humans)?" 

The approach is well-motivated, with a clear literature review that distinguishes this work from existing approaches (pre-programmed communication systems, human-in-the-loop methods, Wizard-of-Oz environments). The authors position their work within game theory (Lewis' signaling games) and linguistics, providing strong theoretical grounding for their methodology.

The paper provides rigorous empirical evidence supporting its claims. The authors demonstrate:
- Agents successfully coordinate (nearly perfect success rates after 10k training games)
- The emergent language uses high-level semantic categories (measured through purity metrics against McRae categories)
- The informed sender architecture produces more semantically natural symbols than the agnostic sender
- Supervised learning on image labels grounds communication in human language, with 68% human interpretability in a crowdsourced experiment

The methodology is sound, with appropriate baselines (agnostic vs. informed sender), multiple evaluation metrics (coordination success, semantic purity, human interpretability), and careful attention to experimental design (e.g., using t-SNE visualizations to analyze semantic relationships).

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, the authors might:
- Provide a more detailed analysis of the specific semantic shifts observed in the human interpretability experiment (e.g., how many were metonymic, metaphorical, etc.)
- Discuss the limitations of their approach more thoroughly, particularly regarding scalability to more complex communication tasks
- Include a more systematic analysis of how hyperparameters (beyond vocabulary size and image representation) affect the emergent language
- Clarify the relationship between the game-theoretic convergence results and the practical performance observed in experiments

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How does the performance scale when the number of object categories increases beyond the 20 used in the current study? Would the agents still develop meaningful semantic categories with a larger set of concepts?

2. In the human interpretability experiment, what specific types of semantic shifts (e.g., metonymic, metaphorical) were most common, and how did these affect the accuracy of human interpretation? This would help clarify the nature of the language grounding.

3. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses more symbols (88 vs. 2 in the agnostic case) after supervised training. How does this increased symbol usage relate to the semantic purity metric, and what does it suggest about the nature of the grounded language?
