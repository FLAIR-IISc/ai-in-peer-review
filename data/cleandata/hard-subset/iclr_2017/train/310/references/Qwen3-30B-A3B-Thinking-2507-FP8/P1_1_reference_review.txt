

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a compelling approach to developing AI agents that can communicate effectively using natural language through multi-agent coordination games. The authors address a fundamental limitation in current approaches to language learning for AI: supervised learning on "canned" conversations fails to capture the functional aspects of communication. Instead, they propose training agents through interactive games where they must develop a communication protocol to coordinate on simple referential tasks.

The authors make several significant contributions:
- They demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully develop communication protocols through multi-agent coordination in referential games, achieving near-perfect coordination.
- They show that the emergent communication protocols use symbols that map to general conceptual properties (e.g., object categories) rather than low-level visual features, as evidenced by semantic purity measures and t-SNE visualizations.
- They introduce a method to ground the agents' communication in human language by combining the referential game with supervised image classification, resulting in a communication protocol that is significantly more interpretable by humans (68% human accuracy in identifying target images).
- They provide theoretical connections to game theory (Lewis' signaling games) and linguistic/cognitive science literature on language emergence.

The paper is well-motivated, methodologically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of AI communication. The authors convincingly argue that their approach addresses the critical need for communication protocols that are portable to new situations and interpretable by humans.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of AI communication. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in AI language development (functional communication vs. statistical language modeling) with a novel, well-motivated approach that bridges multi-agent learning and linguistic theory.
2. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and compelling, with multiple lines of evidence supporting the claim that the emergent communication protocols have semantic properties that align with human conceptualization.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper excels in several critical areas:

**Strong theoretical foundation**: The authors provide a clear connection to Lewis' signaling games and the broader literature on language emergence. They thoughtfully position their work within the context of multi-agent systems, supervised learning, and human-in-the-loop approaches, demonstrating a deep understanding of the field.

**Rigorous methodology**: The experimental design is sound, with multiple metrics to evaluate communication success (coordination accuracy), semantic properties (purity with respect to McRae categories), and human interpretability (crowdsourced survey). The use of t-SNE visualizations to show the relationship between visual embeddings and symbol usage provides particularly compelling evidence.

**Compelling results**: The results consistently support the authors' claims. The emergence of semantically meaningful communication, the increase in purity with environmental modifications, and the significant human interpretability after grounding with supervised learning all provide strong evidence for their approach. The finding that even agents using only two symbols developed a "living vs. non-living" distinction (which aligns with human semantic systems) is particularly insightful.

**Practical significance**: The paper directly addresses a key challenge in developing conversational AI - how to create communication protocols that are interpretable by humans. The grounding approach they propose (combining game-based learning with supervised image labeling) offers a promising path toward this goal.

The paper is well-written, clear, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The results are scientifically rigorous, and the approach has clear potential for future work in developing more natural and interpretable AI communication systems.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to further enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the novelty**: While the paper positions itself well against related work, explicitly stating what makes their grounding approach distinct from previous attempts to connect multi-agent learning with natural language (e.g., compared to work that directly uses human-labeled data) would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of limitations. For example, how does the communication protocol scale to more complex environments? How robust is the protocol to noise or changes in the visual input? What are the computational costs compared to other approaches?

3. **Expand the human interpretability analysis**: The crowdsourced survey is excellent, but it would be valuable to include qualitative analysis of the cases where humans succeeded or failed to interpret the symbols. What specific features of the images or symbols contributed to successful interpretation?

4. **Consider broader implications**: The paper mentions future work on "predictive learning" and "functional facets of language." Expanding this to discuss how their approach might connect to more complex conversational tasks (beyond referential games) would strengthen the paper's significance.

5. **Improve visual presentation**: Some figures (particularly Figure 3) could benefit from clearer labels or a more detailed caption to help readers understand the relationships being shown.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me fully understand and assess the paper, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. In the grounding experiment, how does the supervised image classification task specifically influence the communication protocol during the referential game? Is it primarily about aligning the vocabulary with human labels, or does it also affect the agents' conceptual understanding?

2. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses more varied symbols, but the paper doesn't explicitly explain how the "informed" architecture is informed (what prior knowledge it incorporates). Could you clarify what the "informed" aspect refers to in terms of the agents' prior knowledge or design choices?

3. In the human interpretability experiment, you report 68% accuracy. How does this compare to a baseline where humans were simply guessing randomly (which would be 50% for a binary choice)? The paper mentions chance is 1%, but that seems to refer to the chance of matching the exact supervised label, not the chance of correctly identifying the target image.

4. The paper states that "the agents are still able to reach full coordination after 10k training trials" with the grounding approach. What was the coordination accuracy before the grounding phase, and how quickly did it improve after adding the supervised component?

5. How does the approach scale to more complex communication tasks that require multiple turns of dialogue, rather than the one-step referential game used in this paper? The paper mentions this as future work, but what initial steps would be necessary to extend to multi-turn communication?
