

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by introducing a game-based framework for training agents to develop natural-language communication protocols through interactive play rather than relying on supervised learning from human language data. The authors demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to communicate in referential games, developing symbols that correspond to high-level conceptual categories rather than low-level visual properties. Crucially, they introduce a method to ground the agents' communication in human language by combining the game with a supervised image classification task, resulting in a communication protocol that is partially interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in a crowdsourcing study). The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation grounded in game theory and linguistic research, while making practical progress toward developing AI that can communicate naturally with humans. The work represents a promising path toward the ultimate goal of developing agents that can thrive in human-centric environments through natural language communication.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field with strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The work is well-motivated, rigorously executed, and addresses a fundamental challenge in AI: developing agents that can communicate naturally with humans.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main strength lies in its clear demonstration that agents can learn meaningful communication through interactive play, rather than requiring human language supervision from the start. The authors provide multiple lines of evidence to support this claim:

First, they establish that agents can successfully coordinate in the referential game (Table 1, Figure 2), with the informed sender architecture outperforming the agnostic one. This demonstrates that communication can emerge without pre-coded protocols.

Second, they provide compelling evidence that the emergent communication has semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories (Table 1, Figure 3). The purity metric (70% vs. chance 33%) and t-SNE visualizations show that symbols correspond to high-level categories (e.g., "living vs. non-living" with 2 symbols, and more nuanced categories with larger vocabularies).

Most importantly, they demonstrate that grounding the communication with supervised learning on image labels leads to a language that is partially interpretable by humans (Section 5). The 68% accuracy in the human crowdsourcing study (with 47% of cases matching the supervised label) is a strong empirical validation that their approach creates a language that aligns with human intuition. The qualitative analysis of metonymic links (e.g., "dolphin" for a sea scene) further shows the language's flexibility, which is characteristic of natural language.

The paper is exceptionally well-placed in the literature, providing a thorough comparison with related work in multi-agent systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, and Wizard-of-Oz environments. The connection to Lewis' signaling game theory and evolutionary learning approaches is particularly strong, making the work relevant to both AI researchers and game theorists.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach. While the paper demonstrates success on simple referential games, it would be valuable to explicitly address how this approach scales to more complex language tasks (e.g., question-answering, dialogue generation).

2. A deeper analysis of why the informed sender architecture outperforms the agnostic one. While the paper notes the "inductive bias" of the informed sender, a more detailed explanation of how this architectural choice specifically contributes to better language emergence would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

3. A more thorough comparison with recent work on language emergence (e.g., the "Emergence of Grounded Language" paper by G. G. et al., 2019), to better position this work within the current landscape of language learning research.

4. A discussion of potential failure modes in the grounding approach, particularly regarding how the language might drift when interacting with humans over time (as human language conventions may differ from the emergent agent language).

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify my understanding of the work and strengthen the paper, I would like to ask the authors:

1. How do you think the current approach would scale to more complex language tasks, such as question-answering or multi-turn dialogue? What specific challenges would need to be addressed?

2. Could the metonymic linking observed in the human interpretability study (e.g., "dolphin" for a sea scene) be systematically encouraged in the training process, rather than being an emergent phenomenon? If so, how?

3. In the grounded communication experiments, how much of the human interpretability is due to the supervised labels themselves versus the interactive game play? Could the authors provide a baseline comparison of agents trained only on the supervised task without game play?

4. The paper shows that the language remains interpretable even when agents communicate about images not in the supervised category set. What specific mechanisms in the training process enable this generalization, and how might this be further improved?

5. How would you address the potential issue of language drift when agents interact with humans over time, as the human language conventions might evolve differently than the agents' communication protocol?

Overall, this is an exceptionally strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of multi-agent communication. The work is well-executed, the results are convincing, and the approach represents a promising path toward developing AI agents that can communicate naturally with humans. I strongly recommend acceptance.
