

# Review for ICLR

## Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a novel research program for developing conversational AI through multi-agent coordination communication games. The authors present a referential game framework where agents learn to communicate by coordinating on image references without pre-programmed protocols. They investigate whether agents can develop language that aligns with human semantic understanding, and propose a method to ground this language in natural human language through supervised learning. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) demonstrating agents can learn to communicate effectively in a referential game, (2) showing that the emergent language exhibits semantic properties aligned with human conceptualization, and (3) proposing a grounding approach that makes the language interpretable by humans through supervised learning on image labels.

## Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting research direction, but the methodological details are insufficiently explained, key claims lack statistical support, and there are multiple formatting inconsistencies that undermine confidence in the work.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper suffers from multiple critical issues that prevent confident assessment of its claims:

1. **Lack of statistical rigor**: The paper claims "purity, while far from perfect, is significantly above chance" and "the informed sender is producing symbols that are more semantically natural" without providing p-values or effect sizes. For a high-impact venue like ICLR, such claims require statistical validation. The paper also states "the sender uses many more symbols after training (88)" without clarifying whether this is an average or maximum count.

2. **Inconsistent reporting**: The paper claims agents "converged to perfect communication after 10k training trials" but the training details state "for a total of 50k iterations (games)". Later, it claims convergence after "15k trials" in the grounding experiment. These inconsistencies make it impossible to assess the learning dynamics.

3. **Insufficient methodological detail**: The paper states "We apply to each image a forward-pass through the pretrained VGG ConvNet" without specifying which VGG architecture was used (VGG16, VGG19, etc.). The choice of "top 1000-D softmax layer (sm) or second-to-last 4096-D fully connected layer (fc)" is not justified, and the authors don't explain why these specific layers were chosen over others.

4. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The references contain multiple formatting errors, including inconsistent capitalization ("Proceedings of CVPR" vs "In Proceedings of CVPR"), unnecessary apostrophes ("McRae et al.'s (2005)"), and inconsistent citation formats with "? \Q2018\E" markers.

5. **Architectural confusion**: The paper states "the informed sender architecture nests the agnostic one" but provides no mathematical explanation or clear diagram to clarify this relationship. The description of the informed sender's convolutional operations ("kernel size 2x1 applied dimension-by-dimension") is ambiguous and lacks technical precision.

## Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:
- Provide complete statistical validation for all claims about significance, including p-values and effect sizes.
- Clarify all methodological choices with justification (e.g., layer selection, hyperparameter choices).
- Fix all reference formatting inconsistencies and remove unnecessary apostrophes in citations.
- Provide a clear mathematical explanation of how the informed sender architecture nests the agnostic one.
- Resolve all training iteration discrepancies and clarify what "convergence" means in terms of specific performance metrics.

## Questions for Authors
1. How exactly does the informed sender architecture "nest" the agnostic one? Can you provide a mathematical expression or diagram to clarify this relationship?
2. What statistical tests were performed to determine that purity "is significantly above chance" in all cases? Please provide the specific p-values.
3. Why were the VGG softmax and fc layers specifically chosen for image representation? Were other layers tested, and if so, what were the results?
4. In the grounding experiment, the paper states convergence after "15k trials" but the training details specify "50k iterations (games)" - can you clarify this discrepancy?
5. How did you ensure that the supervised learning component didn't negatively impact communication performance in the grounding experiment? What was the learning rate for the supervised component versus the reinforcement learning component?

The paper presents an interesting research direction with potential for significant impact, but the current version lacks the methodological rigor, statistical validation, and technical precision required for publication at ICLR. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.
