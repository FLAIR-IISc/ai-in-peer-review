

# Review of "Learning to Communicate in Referential Games"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a multi-agent coordination framework for developing communication protocols in AI agents through referential games. The authors demonstrate that agents can learn to coordinate using a language that captures high-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features, and that grounding this communication with supervised learning on image labels improves human interpretability. The work makes an important contribution by connecting multi-agent learning with natural language processing, addressing the challenge of developing AI that can communicate with humans in a functional, rather than merely statistical, way. The experimental setup is well-designed, and the results on semantic properties and human interpretability are promising for the long-term goal of developing conversational agents.

## 2. Decision
Reject

Key reasons: The paper presents incremental results that don't sufficiently advance the state-of-the-art in multi-agent communication or language emergence, and the claims about "natural language" development are overstated relative to the evidence provided.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper's main contribution is incremental. While the authors build on previous work (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), they don't substantially advance the field beyond what's already known. The core claim that agents develop a language "resembling human language" is not sufficiently supported by the evidence. The purity scores (18-37% above chance) are modest, and the paper doesn't provide adequate comparison to baseline approaches or demonstrate that this level of semantic alignment is meaningfully different from what could be achieved through simpler methods.

The paper also overstates the significance of its "grounding" approach. The human interpretability results (68% accuracy) are not particularly impressive compared to standard supervised learning approaches, and the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate that this method produces better language than alternatives. The "metonymic" language examples provided (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to a stretch of sea) are intriguing but not sufficiently analyzed to support the claim that the language is "natural" or "interpretable" in a meaningful way.

Furthermore, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for why their specific environment design leads to more human-interpretable language. While they reference game theory (Lewis signaling games), they don't leverage this framework to make specific predictions about how environmental changes would affect language emergence, which would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Clarify novelty**: The paper should more precisely articulate how this work advances beyond existing multi-agent communication research. The current description of novelty is vague, and the paper would benefit from a more concrete comparison to recent work on language emergence (e.g., Lazaridou et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2017).
  
- **Strengthen claims about "natural language"**: The paper should provide more rigorous linguistic analysis of the emergent language. For example, what specific linguistic properties (beyond semantic category alignment) could be measured to demonstrate "naturalness"? The paper should avoid overstating claims about the language resembling human language without sufficient evidence.

- **Improve experimental design**: The paper could strengthen its claims by including more controlled experiments that isolate specific environmental factors. For instance, how does the degree of "common knowledge" removal affect semantic alignment? The current experiments don't systematically vary environmental factors to test specific hypotheses.

- **Address scalability**: The paper should discuss how the approach would scale to more complex communication tasks beyond simple object reference. What are the limitations of the current framework for more sophisticated language development?

## 5. Clarifying Questions
- How do you justify the claim that the language developed in your referential games "resembles human language" when purity scores are only modestly above chance (e.g., 70% purity, which is still far from perfect) and human interpretability is only 68%? What specific linguistic features would constitute "natural language" in this context?

- What specific advantages does your approach offer over simpler methods for grounding communication in natural language (e.g., fine-tuning a standard language model on image labels)? How would you measure these advantages?

- How do you plan to address the fundamental challenge that referential games, as currently designed, are limited to simple object reference tasks that don't capture the full complexity of human language?

The paper makes an interesting contribution to the field of multi-agent communication, but the current version lacks sufficient novelty and evidence to support its claims. With more rigorous analysis, clearer articulation of novelty, and better alignment between claims and evidence, this work could make a significant contribution to the field.
