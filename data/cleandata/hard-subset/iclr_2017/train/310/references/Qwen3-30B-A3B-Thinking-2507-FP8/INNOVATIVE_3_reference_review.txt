

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a bold new research direction for developing conversational AI agents through multi-agent coordination communication games. The authors convincingly argue that current approaches to training conversational agents (relying on supervised learning from "canned" conversations) fail to capture the functional aspects of communication, where humans use language to coordinate and achieve goals. Instead of relying on pre-defined communication protocols or human-in-the-loop training, the authors propose that agents can develop language through interaction with other agents in simple environments, bootstrapping knowledge from their partners. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) it demonstrates that tabula rasa agents can successfully develop communication protocols in referential games using real-world images; (2) it shows that the emergent language exhibits semantic properties aligned with human conceptualization (not just low-level visual properties); and (3) it proposes a novel method to ground the agents' communication in human natural language through a combination of supervised learning and interactive game play. The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, placing the work firmly within the context of linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory while clearly articulating how it differs from and advances existing approaches.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper presents a significant conceptual shift in how we approach training conversational agents. The authors have proposed a fundamentally new research program that could reshape how we develop AI capable of natural language communication, rather than just making incremental improvements to existing methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength is its conceptual novelty. The idea of agents developing language through multi-agent coordination games—where they start as "blank slates" and bootstrap knowledge from each other—represents a bold departure from current paradigms. This is not a minor improvement to existing methods but a new research direction with the potential to address fundamental challenges in developing AI that can communicate effectively with humans.

The authors provide compelling empirical evidence supporting their conceptual framework. Their experiments show that agents can learn to coordinate successfully in referential games using real images from ImageNet. More importantly, they demonstrate that the emergent communication protocols exhibit semantic properties that align with human conceptualization—objects from the same category tend to activate the same symbols, and the language becomes interpretable by humans after grounding through supervised learning. The human interpretability experiment (where humans were able to correctly identify target images based on the agent's symbols in 68% of cases) is particularly convincing.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. The authors carefully situate their work in relation to multi-agent systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, and evolutionary learning methods, clearly articulating how their approach differs and improves upon existing work. Their connection to linguistic and cognitive science literature on language emergence (Lewis' signaling game, etc.) is particularly strong and well-executed.

While the approach has limitations (the environment is still quite simple, vocabulary sizes are limited), these are precisely the kinds of limitations I would expect in the "first steps" of a new research program, as the authors themselves acknowledge. The paper's value lies in the conceptual direction it proposes, not in the current completeness of its implementation.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, these suggestions could further enhance its impact:

1. The paper could benefit from a clearer articulation of the specific challenges this approach solves that existing methods cannot. While the limitations of supervised learning are mentioned, a more explicit comparison of the scalability challenges between human-in-the-loop approaches and this multi-agent approach would strengthen the motivation.

2. The discussion of "common knowledge" in Section 4.1 is intriguing but could be developed more fully. How might this concept be extended to more complex communication scenarios? A brief exploration of how removing different types of common knowledge might influence language development would add depth.

3. The paper mentions future work on "ensuring that the emergent communication stays close to human natural language." A more concrete proposal for how this might be achieved (e.g., incorporating linguistic constraints or additional grounding mechanisms) would make this future work more compelling.

4. The human interpretability experiment is strong, but the authors could further validate their claims by including a comparison to a baseline method (e.g., a supervised model trained on ImageNet labels without the multi-agent component) to demonstrate that the multi-agent learning approach specifically contributes to the interpretable language.

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you envision scaling this approach to handle more complex communication scenarios beyond simple referential games? What specific challenges would need to be addressed?

2. The paper mentions that the agents' language developed metonymic properties (e.g., using "dolphin" for a stretch of sea). How might you design the game to encourage more specific semantic relationships rather than metonymic ones, and why would this be important?

3. In the human interpretability experiment, the agents achieved 68% accuracy. What do you believe are the primary factors limiting this further (e.g., vocabulary size, game complexity, training methodology), and how might they be addressed in future work?
