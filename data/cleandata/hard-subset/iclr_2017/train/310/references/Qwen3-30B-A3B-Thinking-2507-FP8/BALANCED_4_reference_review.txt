

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a research program based on multi-agent coordination communication games, where agents develop language through interaction rather than supervised learning. The authors focus on referential games where two agents must coordinate on referring to objects in images. They demonstrate that agents can learn to communicate effectively, with the emergent language capturing higher-level semantic properties rather than just low-level visual features. Crucially, they show that by combining the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task, the communication becomes more interpretable by humans. The paper makes an interesting contribution to understanding how language can emerge from interaction, with potential implications for developing AI that can communicate naturally with humans.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The main reason for rejection is that the paper presents an incremental extension of prior work without sufficient novelty or advancement of the field. While the results are interesting, they don't substantially advance the state-of-the-art in a way that justifies acceptance at ICLR.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper builds directly on prior work by Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016), who already demonstrated language emergence in neural agents. The authors acknowledge this, but their contribution is largely incremental rather than transformative. The experimental results confirm what has been shown before (agents can learn to communicate), and the human interpretability results (68% accuracy) are promising but not sufficiently strong to justify a major contribution.

The paper lacks a clear theoretical contribution or a compelling argument for why this approach is superior to other methods for developing human-interpretable communication. The authors note that "the language developed in referential games will be initially very limited," but don't provide a convincing case for why this approach is the best path forward.

Additionally, the paper does not provide a thorough comparison with the state-of-the-art. While they cite relevant work, they don't clearly position their results against the most recent approaches in the field, making it difficult to assess the significance of their contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify novelty**: The paper should more explicitly articulate what specific gap this work fills that previous approaches did not address. The connection to grounding in human language is interesting, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate this from prior work on supervised learning for language.

- **Strengthen comparison**: Include a more detailed comparison with the state-of-the-art in language emergence, particularly in terms of communication success rates and semantic interpretability. How do your results compare to the most recent approaches?

- **Expand analysis**: Provide a more detailed analysis of the emergent language. What specific linguistic properties (e.g., compositionality, syntax) do the agents develop, and how do these compare to human language?

- **Address scalability**: The paper should explore the limitations of the approach more thoroughly, particularly how performance scales with larger vocabularies, more complex environments, or different types of visual data.

- **Refine future work**: Instead of stating that future work will "study how to ensure that the emergent communication stays close to human natural language," provide a more concrete roadmap with specific hypotheses and potential methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your approach differ from the work of Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) in a way that justifies the novelty of your contribution?

2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of your results with the state-of-the-art in language emergence, particularly in terms of communication success rates and semantic interpretability?

3. How do you plan to address the scalability issues of your approach, particularly when moving from simple referential games to more complex communication scenarios?

4. What specific linguistic properties (beyond basic referential accuracy) do you believe your agents have developed, and how do you measure these properties?

5. Why do you believe that the 68% human interpretability rate is sufficient for practical applications, and what additional improvements would be necessary to make this approach viable for real-world human-AI communication?
