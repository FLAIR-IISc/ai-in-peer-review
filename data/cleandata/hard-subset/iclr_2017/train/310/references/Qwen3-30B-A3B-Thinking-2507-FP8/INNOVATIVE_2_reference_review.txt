

**1. Summary of Contribution**

This paper introduces a highly novel and compelling approach to developing natural language communication in AI agents through multi-agent coordination games. The authors present a framework where agents learn to communicate by playing referential games that require them to coordinate on object references in visual scenes. The core contribution is demonstrating that agents can develop communication protocols that not only enable successful coordination but also align with higher-level semantic concepts rather than low-level visual features. Most significantly, the paper shows a pathway to grounding these emergent communication protocols in human language through a combination of supervised learning on natural language labels and interactive game playing. The authors convincingly show that agents can develop a language that is partially interpretable by humans, even without direct human supervision during communication—a remarkable achievement that challenges traditional supervised learning paradigms in natural language processing.

**2. Decision**

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a novel paradigm for language acquisition in AI agents through multi-agent interaction. The core idea of using referential games to bootstrap communication that aligns with human semantics is bold, promising, and addresses a fundamental challenge in developing conversational AI.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's novelty is exceptional—it proposes a paradigm shift from traditional supervised learning to emergent communication through interaction. This approach directly challenges the assumption that language acquisition must be supervised, instead leveraging multi-agent interaction as a bootstrapping mechanism. The authors' framework is well-motivated by game theory, linguistics, and cognitive science, while clearly distinguishing their work from previous approaches (pre-programmed communication systems, human-in-the-loop methods, and Wizard-of-Oz environments).

The most compelling evidence is in Section 5, where the authors demonstrate that agents trained with a combination of supervised learning on ImageNet labels and interactive game playing develop a communication protocol that humans can partially interpret (68% accuracy in the human evaluation). This finding is particularly significant because it shows that agents can develop a language that aligns with human semantics without requiring direct human supervision during communication—a major step toward practical conversational AI.

The paper also provides strong evidence that the emergent language reflects higher-level semantic concepts rather than low-level visual features (Table 1, Figure 3), which is a critical step toward developing language that is portable to new situations and partners. The authors' approach to grounding communication through a combination of supervised learning and interactive game playing is particularly innovative and addresses a key limitation of previous work in emergent communication.

**4. Additional Feedback**

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
- Providing more detailed analysis of the "metonymic" language patterns (Figure 4) and their relationship to linguistic theory of metonymy.
- Discussing potential limitations and challenges of scaling this approach to more complex communication scenarios beyond simple object reference.
- Including a more comprehensive comparison with recent work on language games in reinforcement learning (e.g., Lazaridou et al. 2017, "Multi-Agent Cooperation and the Emergence of Language").

**5. Questions for Authors**

1. The paper mentions that agents develop "metonymic" language (Figure 4). Could you elaborate on how this compares to known linguistic phenomena of metonymy, and whether the agents' use of metonymy is systematic or random?

2. The human evaluation in Section 5 is limited to 298 pairs. How confident are you that this sample size is sufficient to generalize your finding that humans can interpret the agents' language (68% accuracy)? What would be the statistical power of your evaluation?

3. The authors state that the agents' language "requires multiple dimensions to summarize" (Figure 2). Could you elaborate on how this relates to linguistic theory of semantic space and dimensionality?

4. The informed sender outperforms the agnostic sender in symbol usage diversity (Figure 2). What specific inductive biases does the informed sender architecture provide, and how might these generalize to more complex communication tasks requiring nuanced distinctions?

5. How do you envision scaling this approach to handle more complex communication tasks that require not just object reference but also descriptions of actions, relationships, or abstract concepts?
