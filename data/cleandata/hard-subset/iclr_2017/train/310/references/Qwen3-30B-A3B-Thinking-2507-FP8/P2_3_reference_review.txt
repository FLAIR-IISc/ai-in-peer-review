

# Review of "Learning to Communicate via Multi-Agent Coordination Games"

## 1. Summary
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by introducing a framework for developing meaningful language through interactive coordination games. The authors demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully develop communication protocols in referential games, where they must coordinate to identify target images from distractors. Crucially, they show that the emergent language captures high-level semantic properties (e.g., object categories) rather than low-level visual features, and they propose a novel method to ground this communication in human natural language through a combination of interactive game playing and supervised learning. The work is well-motivated, builds on strong theoretical foundations from game theory and linguistics, and provides rigorous empirical evidence supporting its claims about the emergence of semantically meaningful communication.

## 2. Decision
I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a fundamental contribution to understanding how language can emerge through multi-agent interaction, and the grounding approach in Section 5 represents a promising path toward human-interpretable AI communication.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper excels in three key areas that justify acceptance:

First, the paper addresses a critical problem in AI: how to develop agents that can communicate meaningfully with humans. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches (supervised learning, human-in-the-loop, Wizard-of-Oz) and position their work as a promising alternative that doesn't require constant human intervention. The connection to Lewis' signaling game theory provides a strong theoretical foundation, and the authors effectively distinguish their work from prior multi-agent communication research (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016).

Second, the experimental design is rigorous and well-executed. The authors use multiple complementary metrics to assess language emergence: communication success (Table 1), semantic purity (Figure 3, Table 1), and human interpretability (Section 5). Their finding that agents can develop semantically meaningful communication (with purity significantly above chance) is particularly compelling. The comparison between "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures provides valuable insight into how architectural choices affect language emergence.

Third, the grounding approach (Section 5) is the paper's most significant contribution. By combining the referential game with supervised image classification, the authors demonstrate that agents can develop a communication protocol that is partially interpretable by humans (68% human accuracy in the ReferItGame experiment). This bridges a critical gap between emergent communication and practical applications, and the "metonymic" examples they provide (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to a stretch of sea) are particularly insightful about how language might generalize beyond supervised categories.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

- A more detailed discussion of limitations, particularly regarding scalability. The current approach works for basic referential games but would need significant extension to handle complex language tasks (e.g., describing relationships, actions, or abstract concepts). A brief discussion of these limitations would help readers understand the scope of the contribution.

- A more explicit comparison with the most recent related work. While the paper cites Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016), it could more clearly articulate how the grounding approach (Section 5) advances beyond these works, which focused primarily on communication emergence without human interpretability.

- A brief discussion of how the noise in the ImageNet dataset (mentioned in the abstract) might affect the learning process. The authors use ImageNet images with their labels, but the inherent noise in this dataset could influence how agents learn to categorize objects.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that agents can develop a "living vs. non-living" distinction with only two symbols. How would you expect this to scale to more complex semantic distinctions (e.g., "animal vs. vehicle" or "fruit vs. vegetable")? What environmental modifications would be needed to encourage such distinctions?

2. In Figure 3, the t-SNE visualization shows that objects close in CNN space share symbols. How would you characterize the relationship between the CNN's visual features and the semantic categories the agents learn? Is there evidence that the agents are learning to ignore visual features that correlate with category membership but are not semantically relevant?

3. The human interpretability experiment achieved 68% accuracy. How does this compare to a human-to-human baseline for the same task? For example, if you asked humans to pick the image most related to a given word from the ImageNet labels, what would their accuracy be on the ReferItGame dataset?

These questions would help clarify the scope of the contribution and the significance of the results. Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multi-agent communication and language emergence, with clear implications for developing practical AI that can communicate with humans.
