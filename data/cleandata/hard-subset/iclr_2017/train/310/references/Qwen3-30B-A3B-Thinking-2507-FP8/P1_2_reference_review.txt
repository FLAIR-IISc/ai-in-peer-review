## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of AI by addressing a fundamental challenge: how to develop agents that can learn to communicate using natural language without relying on large amounts of human-annotated data. The authors propose a novel framework based on multi-agent coordination communication games, where agents start as "blank slates" and develop a language through interaction to coordinate in simple referential games.

The paper makes several key contributions:
- It demonstrates that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to communicate in referential games, achieving near-perfect coordination
- It shows that the language developed by agents has semantic properties aligning with human conceptualizations (e.g., grouping objects by broad categories rather than low-level visual features)
- It proposes a grounding method that combines the game-based approach with supervised image classification to create a language that is partially interpretable by humans (68% human accuracy in identifying target images)
- It provides a theoretical connection to game theory, linguistics, and cognitive science, positioning the work within a rich tradition of language emergence research

The paper is well-motivated, clearly distinguishes its approach from existing work, and presents strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The authors make a compelling case for their multi-agent coordination framework as a promising path toward developing AI that can communicate effectively with humans.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The key reasons for this decision are:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in AI with a novel, well-motivated approach that makes a clear contribution to the field
2. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and provide strong evidence for the claims made

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's contribution is significant because it moves beyond traditional supervised learning approaches to language development, which have limitations in capturing the functional aspects of communication. The authors successfully demonstrate that agents can develop a functional language through multi-agent coordination, and crucially, that this language has properties resembling human language (e.g., semantic categorization).

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with careful distinction from related work in multi-agent systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, and "Wizard-of-Oz" environments. The connection to game theory (Lewis' signaling games) and linguistic/cognitive studies is particularly strong and well-justified.

The empirical methodology is sound:
- They use real ImageNet images rather than synthetic data
- They employ multiple evaluation metrics (purity, SVD decomposition, t-SNE visualizations)
- They test multiple architectures (agnostic vs. informed sender)
- They provide human evaluation of language interpretability
- They systematically modify the game environment to investigate what features foster more human-like language

The results are compelling: agents learn to communicate effectively (near 100% coordination success), develop semantic properties that align with human conceptualizations (purity significantly above chance), and the grounding approach leads to language that is partially interpretable by humans (68% accuracy). The paper's claim that "the agents' language can be thought of as 'natural language'" is supported by multiple lines of evidence.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

### Suggested Improvements

1. **Clarify the "purity" metric**: The paper uses "purity" as a key metric but doesn't provide a clear definition or reference. Adding a brief explanation of how purity is calculated (e.g., "Purity is defined as the proportion of category labels in clusters that agree with the respective cluster majority category") would help readers understand the significance of the results.

2. **Explain the 88-symbol usage**: The paper states that the grounded sender uses 88 out of 100 symbols, but doesn't explain why it didn't use all 100. A brief discussion of whether this is due to redundancy, semantic overlap, or other factors would strengthen the analysis.

3. **Add comparison to baselines**: The human evaluation shows 68% accuracy, but doesn't compare this to a baseline (e.g., random guessing would be 50% for two images). Adding this comparison would better contextualize the result.

4. **More detail on the "metonymic" examples**: The paper mentions that the language often establishes "metonymic" links (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea), but doesn't provide more examples. Adding 2-3 more specific examples would help readers understand this important phenomenon.

5. **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of limitations, particularly regarding scalability to larger vocabularies and more complex language tasks.

### Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in my assessment, I'd like to ask:

1. In Figure 3 (left), the t-SNE visualization shows some clustering by symbol. Could you provide more details on how the symbols map to specific categories (e.g., which symbols are used for "animal" vs. "vehicle" categories)?

2. The paper states that the agents reach 100% coordination success after 10k games. Could you provide more details about the training dynamics? For example, how many games did it take to reach 50% success, 75%, etc.?

3. In the grounding experiment, the paper mentions that the language generalizes beyond supervised categories. Could you provide specific examples of how this generalization works (e.g., when the supervised categories are "cat" and "dog," what does the agent say when referring to "horse")?

4. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses more symbols (88) than the agnostic sender (2). How does this affect the quality of communication? For example, does using more symbols lead to more precise communication, or is it just a different strategy that happens to work equally well?

5. The paper states that the language is "partially interpretable" by humans (68% accuracy), but doesn't compare this to other methods. How does this compare to, for example, a standard supervised language model trained on the same image labels?

These questions would help clarify the scope and significance of the results and ensure the claims are fully supported by the evidence.
