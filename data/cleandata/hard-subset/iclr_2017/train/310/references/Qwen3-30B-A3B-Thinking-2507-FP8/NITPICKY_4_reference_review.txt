

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
The paper presents a research program focused on developing AI agents that can communicate through multi-agent coordination games. The authors propose a referential game framework where agents learn to communicate about visual objects without pre-programmed language, with the goal of creating communication protocols that are interpretable by humans. They introduce two sender architectures (agnostic and informed), demonstrate that agents can learn to coordinate successfully, and show that the emergent communication protocols exhibit semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories. The paper further introduces a method to ground the agents' communication in human language through a supervised learning component. The work makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on emergent communication in multi-agent systems, particularly in its focus on human interpretability of the emergent language.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains significant methodological and technical inconsistencies that undermine the rigor of the empirical claims, despite the interesting research question and promising direction.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper fails to provide sufficient methodological detail to reproduce the experiments, which is a critical issue for a machine learning paper. For instance:
- The paper mentions using "the pretrained VGG ConvNet" but does not specify which variant (VGG16, VGG19, etc.) or whether the network was fine-tuned during training. This is essential information for reproducibility.
- The communication bottleneck uses a Gibbs distribution with temperature τ = 10, but the paper does not explain why this specific temperature was chosen or whether it was tuned. This is a critical hyperparameter that could significantly affect the results.
- The paper claims "agents almost perfectly coordinate in the 1k rounds following the 10k training games" but does not provide the actual performance metrics or show Table 1 as referenced. Without seeing the data, it's impossible to verify this claim.
- The paper states that "purity, while far from perfect, is significantly above chance in all cases" but does not provide statistical significance tests for these claims. The phrase "significantly above chance" requires proper statistical validation.

The paper also contains several technical inconsistencies:
- The paper claims "the informed sender has an inductive bias towards combining the two images dimension-by-dimension whereas the agnostic sender does not (though we note the agnostic architecture nests the informed one)" but fails to provide evidence or explanation for how the agnostic architecture nests the informed one.
- The paper mentions "the receiver does not know the target, but sees the sender's symbol and tries to guess the target image" but doesn't explain how the receiver processes the symbol (is it a one-hot vector? How is it combined with the image representations?).
- The paper states "the informed sender uses convolutions with kernel size 2x1 applied dimension-by-dimension to the two image embeddings" but doesn't explain why this dimension-by-dimension approach is better than other approaches or how it relates to the overall architecture.

Additionally, the paper contains formatting inconsistencies in the references, with some entries containing strange artifacts like "? \Q1998\E," which suggests the references were not properly formatted for submission.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide complete methodological details, including network architectures, hyperparameter choices, and implementation specifics.
2. Include statistical significance tests for all comparative claims (e.g., "significantly above chance").
3. Clarify the relationship between the agnostic and informed sender architectures.
4. Provide the actual performance metrics from Table 1 rather than just referencing it.
5. Properly format all references according to ICLR guidelines.
6. Clarify how the receiver processes the sender's symbol (is it a one-hot vector? How is it combined with image representations?).

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Why was temperature τ = 10 chosen for the Gibbs distribution? Was this value tuned, and if so, what range was explored?
2. Please provide the actual performance metrics from Table 1 to verify the claim that "agents almost perfectly coordinate in the 1k rounds following the 10k training games."
3. How exactly does the receiver process the sender's symbol? Is it treated as a one-hot vector, and if so, how is it combined with the image representations?
4. Can you provide evidence for the claim that "the agnostic architecture nests the informed one," and explain how this nesting occurs technically?
5. What was the exact VGG model used (VGG16, VGG19, etc.), and was it fine-tuned during training?
