

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by introducing a framework for developing natural-language-like communication protocols through multi-agent coordination games. The authors address the fundamental problem of how to design environments that foster the development of a language portable to new situations and communication partners (particularly humans), rather than relying on supervised learning from human-labeled data or requiring constant human intervention.

The key contributions include:
- A novel referential game framework where agents start as "blank slates" and develop communication through interaction
- Empirical evidence that agents can learn to coordinate successfully (near-perfect performance) in these games
- Demonstration that the emergent communication captures higher-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features (measured through purity metrics and t-SNE visualizations)
- A grounding approach that combines interactive game play with supervised image classification to make the emergent language interpretable by humans (achieving 68% human interpretability in a crowdsourced experiment)
- A clear distinction from related work in multi-agent systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, and Wizard-of-Oz environments

The paper is well-motivated, well-structured, and provides a compelling path toward developing conversational AI that can communicate with humans using natural language without requiring constant human supervision.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in AI (developing natural language communication without constant human supervision) with a novel, well-designed experimental approach.
2. The results are scientifically rigorous, with multiple metrics supporting the claim that the emergent language captures higher-level semantic properties and can be grounded in human language.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in its methodological approach and experimental design. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related literature, particularly by addressing the scalability issues of human-in-the-loop approaches and the limitations of supervised learning for language acquisition.

The experimental results are robust and well-supported:
- Agents consistently achieve near-perfect coordination (99.8% success rate) across multiple configurations
- Semantic purity metrics (41% vs. 2.5% chance for agnostic sender with 2 symbols) demonstrate that agents are learning higher-level semantics
- The grounding approach successfully increases symbol purity to 70% and achieves 47% direct correspondence to supervised labels
- The human evaluation (68% success rate in identifying the correct image based on the agent's symbol) provides compelling evidence for human interpretability

The paper's framework is well-placed in the literature, drawing appropriately from linguistics, cognitive science, game theory, and multi-agent systems. The authors effectively position their work as a bridge between theoretical language emergence studies and practical AI development.

The most compelling aspect of the paper is its demonstration that the agents' communication can be grounded in human language through a simple combination of game play and supervised learning, providing a clear path toward practical applications. The "metonymic" semantic shifts observed in the human evaluation (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to a stretch of sea) are particularly interesting and suggest the emergence of flexible language use.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

### Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the "portability" claim**: The paper mentions "portable to new situations and to new communication partners" but the experiments primarily test on new images within the same domain. Please clarify how the language would be tested for portability to truly novel situations or tasks (e.g., different visual domains, different types of coordination problems).

2. **Expand the grounding discussion**: The grounding approach uses only 100 labels from ImageNet. How would this approach scale to a full vocabulary of natural language? What are the limitations when applying to more complex language structures beyond basic object naming?

3. **Provide more linguistic analysis**: The paper mentions "metonymic" links but doesn't provide a detailed linguistic analysis. How does this compare to established linguistic phenomena? How does the emergent language compare to human language in terms of compositionality, syntax, or other linguistic properties?

4. **Strengthen the human evaluation**: While the 68% success rate is impressive, the human evaluation only used 298 image pairs. Consider expanding this to a larger dataset or providing more detailed analysis of the errors (e.g., why 32% of responses were incorrect).

5. **Address the limitations of the "informed" sender**: The paper shows the informed sender outperforms the agnostic sender, but doesn't fully explain why. A deeper analysis of the inductive bias introduced by the convolutional architecture would strengthen the paper.

### Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the agents developed a "living-vs-non-living" categorization when using only two symbols. How does this compare to the basic semantic categories identified in human language acquisition research (e.g., basic-level categories vs. superordinate categories)? Is this consistent with cognitive science findings?

2. The grounding approach achieves 70% purity but the human evaluation shows 68% success rate. What accounts for the discrepancy between these two metrics? How do you interpret the fact that humans can interpret the language better than the purity metric suggests?

3. The paper mentions "metonymic" links as a characteristic of natural language. In the example where "dolphin" refers to a stretch of sea, how does this differ from "synecdoche" (a related linguistic phenomenon)? How might these different types of semantic shifts affect communication reliability?

4. The paper uses a discrete communication bottleneck with Gibbs sampling. How would the results change if using a different approach to discrete communication (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax, straight-through estimators)? Would the semantic properties of the emergent language be affected?

5. The paper discusses the "common knowledge" aspect of the game. How would the emergent language change if the agents had no common knowledge about the visual domain at all (e.g., if they were trained on completely different image sets)?

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of multi-agent communication and represents an important step toward developing AI that can communicate with humans using natural language. With the minor clarifications suggested above, it will be an excellent addition to the ICLR proceedings.
