- Proposes multi-agent coordination referential games to learn communication without large human-annotated data.  
- Shows tabula rasa agents can learn to communicate, achieving near-perfect coordination.  
- Finds emergent agent language groups objects by human-like semantic categories rather than low-level features.  
- Introduces a grounding method combining games with supervised image classification yielding partially interpretable language (68% human accuracy).  
- Connects the approach theoretically to game theory, linguistics, and cognitive science.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Key reasons for accept: addresses a fundamental problem with a novel approach and provides rigorous empirical evidence.  
- Moves beyond supervised learning to capture functional aspects of communication.  
- Clearly differentiates from prior work in multi-agent systems and human-in-the-loop setups.  
- Strong theoretical placement through Lewis-style signaling games and cognitive/linguistic literature.  
- Empirical methodology is sound: uses real ImageNet images, multiple metrics (purity, SVD, t-SNE), multiple architectures, human evaluation, and systematic environment variations.  
- Results: near-100% coordination success, semantic purity above chance, and partial human interpretability after grounding.  
- Suggestion: Clarify the definition and calculation of the "purity" metric.  
- Suggestion: Explain why the grounded sender used 88 of 100 symbols rather than all symbols.  
- Suggestion: Add baseline comparisons for human evaluation (e.g., random guessing).  
- Suggestion: Provide more concrete examples of "metonymic" mappings (add 2–3 examples).  
- Suggestion: Discuss limitations more thoroughly, especially scalability to larger vocabularies and complex language tasks.  
- Question: Provide symbol-to-category mappings for t-SNE clusters (which symbols map to animals vs. vehicles?).  
- Question: Report training dynamics (when coordination reached 50%, 75%, etc., before 100% at 10k games).  
- Question: Give concrete examples of grounding generalization beyond supervised categories (e.g., what is said for "horse" when trained on "cat"/"dog").  
- Question: Explain how using more symbols (88 vs 2) affects communication quality—precision vs. alternate strategy.  
- Question: Compare the 68% human interpretability to other methods (e.g., supervised language model trained on same labels).
