

# Review

## Summary
The paper introduces a novel research program based on multi-agent coordination communication games, aiming to develop agents that can learn natural-language-based communication through interactive play rather than supervised learning. The authors focus on referential games where two agents must develop a shared language to coordinate on identifying target images from distractors. They investigate whether agents can develop a language that aligns with human semantic categories and demonstrate that grounding the communication in human language through supervised learning improves human interpretability. The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of functional communication over statistical associations, situating its work within linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory literature.

## Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework but lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its core claims about language portability and human interpretability. The authors make strong claims about the potential of their approach to develop language that is "portable to new situations and to new communication partners (in particular humans)" without providing adequate evidence.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper fails to meet the rigorous standards expected for ICLR. While the authors present some positive results (agents learn to coordinate, language shows semantic properties), they overstate the significance of these findings. Key issues include:

1. **Insufficient evidence for "portability" claim**: The paper's central thesis is that their approach develops a language "portable to new situations and to new communication partners." However, the experiments only test the language within the specific game setup and on limited datasets. The paper provides no evidence that the language would work with humans in naturalistic settings or with novel image categories beyond those used in training. The human interpretability experiment (68% success rate) is based on a small number of image pairs (298) and only tests for basic word-image associations, not real communication.

2. **Lack of comparison with established baselines**: The paper does not compare its approach with existing methods for developing communication protocols, such as the multi-agent communication work by Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) that the authors cite. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine if their method is actually better or just different. The paper claims to "pursue the same question, but further ask how we can change our environment to make the emergent language more interpretable," but fails to demonstrate that their approach achieves better results than prior work.

3. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims to have "the first steps of a research program" that "aims to, eventually, develop practical AI." This is an overstatement given the limited scope of the experiments. The results show that agents can develop a communication protocol within a narrow game setup, but the leap to "practical AI" is unsupported by the evidence.

4. **Methodological limitations**: The paper uses a single game setup with limited image diversity (463 concepts from ImageNet) and does not test the robustness of the communication protocol across different environments or with more complex language structures. The human interpretability experiment relies on a crowdsourced survey with limited scope, which is insufficient to validate the claim that the language is "portable" to humans.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct more rigorous validation of the "portability" claim by testing the developed language in more naturalistic settings with humans, including conversations beyond simple image naming.

2. Compare their approach with established baselines in multi-agent communication, showing quantitative improvements over prior work.

3. Provide a more detailed analysis of the language structure (beyond purity scores) to demonstrate how it aligns with human language patterns (e.g., syntax, semantics, pragmatics).

4. Expand the human evaluation to a larger, more diverse set of image pairs and communication scenarios, including more complex referential tasks.

5. Clarify the relationship between their work and previous multi-agent communication research, showing specifically how their approach advances beyond existing methods.

## Questions for Authors
1. How would your approach handle the development of a language for tasks that require more complex linguistic structures (e.g., questions, negation, or temporal relations) that go beyond simple object naming?

2. Can you provide evidence that the language developed in your game would generalize to communication about objects not present in your training data (e.g., objects from new categories or novel combinations of features)?

3. How does your method compare to supervised approaches in terms of data efficiency, especially when considering the need for human-labeled data in the grounding phase?

4. What evidence do you have that the "metonymic" shifts observed in your human evaluation (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea) are not simply artifacts of the limited image dataset or the specific experimental setup?
