

# Review of "Learning to Communicate with Agents"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a research program based on multi-agent coordination communication games to develop agents that can learn natural language communication. The authors propose a framework where agents learn to communicate through referential games, with the central question of how to design environments that foster the development of languages portable to new situations and communication partners (particularly humans). They demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully coordinate in these games, develop semantic meanings for symbols that align with human conceptual categories, and show that supervised learning on image labels can ground this communication in human language. The work makes a valuable contribution to the literature on language emergence by bridging theoretical game-theoretic approaches with practical AI development.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting research direction but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims about the emergence of human-like language. The methodological limitations and insufficient statistical rigor prevent me from being confident in the conclusions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The main reason for rejection is that the paper does not provide adequate evidence that the emergent communication is "natural" or "portable" as claimed. While the agents do learn to coordinate (with >95% success rate), the paper's evidence for semantic meaning is limited. The purity metric (50-70%) is significantly above chance (15-25%), but this doesn't demonstrate that the language is human-interpretable in any practical sense. The human interpretability experiment shows 68% accuracy in a crowdsourced task, but this is not sufficiently high for practical applications, and the paper lacks statistical significance tests for this result.

Another key weakness is the lack of comparison to relevant baselines. The paper claims that the "informed sender" produces more semantically natural language than the "agnostic sender," but doesn't compare to existing methods for language grounding or semantic similarity. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the results are truly novel or represent a meaningful improvement.

The paper also lacks sufficient detail on the experimental setup. For example, the hyperparameters are "set without tuning" but not explained, and the choice of 100 vs. 10 vocabulary size isn't justified. The "object-level reference" modification (Section 4.1) is presented as a way to encourage higher-level semantics, but the paper doesn't clearly explain how this differs from the original setup or why it should lead to better semantic understanding.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen this work, the authors should:

1. Provide more rigorous statistical analysis for all key results, including significance tests for the purity metrics and human interpretability results.

2. Compare their approach to relevant baselines, such as supervised learning on image captions or other language grounding methods, to demonstrate the added value of their multi-agent framework.

3. Expand the human interpretability evaluation to include a larger number of categories and more diverse image pairs, with proper control conditions.

4. Clarify the relationship between the "object-level reference" modification and the main contribution. Is this a key finding or merely an additional experiment?

5. Address the scalability question more thoroughly. How would this approach handle a full vocabulary of common words (e.g., 10,000 words) or more complex communication scenarios?

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper reports 68% accuracy in the human interpretability task. What is the expected performance of a human in the same task (i.e., picking the image most related to a word from two images)? This would help contextualize the 68% result.

- The paper states that the informed sender uses "88 symbols" out of 100 in the grounded experiment, but the vocabulary size was set to 100. Why did the sender use only 88 symbols, and how does this compare to the expected number of distinct concepts in the supervised set?

- The authors claim the emergent language "partially interpretable by humans" based on the 68% accuracy. What specific examples of human-interpretable communication do they consider most compelling, and why?

- The paper mentions "metonymic" connections between words and images (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea). How does this differ from the kind of semantic shifts that would be required for natural language understanding, and how would the authors measure this?

- How would the authors scale this approach to handle more complex communication scenarios, such as multi-step conversations or referring to abstract concepts rather than concrete objects?
