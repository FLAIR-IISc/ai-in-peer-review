

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by introducing a framework for studying the emergence of language through coordination games. The authors demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to communicate in referential games, developing symbols that correspond to high-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features. Crucially, they show that by grounding the communication in human language through a combination of supervised learning and interactive game play, the emergent language becomes partially interpretable by humans. The paper's key contributions include: (1) empirical evidence that multi-agent coordination games can foster the development of language with human-like semantic properties, (2) a method for designing game environments to encourage higher-level semantic understanding, and (3) a practical approach for grounding machine communication in human language through a combination of supervised learning and interactive game play. The results are supported by multiple lines of evidence including quantitative purity measures, SVD analysis, t-SNE visualizations, and crowdsourced human evaluations.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a strong case for its claims with scientifically rigorous empirical evidence. The authors address the central question of how to design environments that foster the development of portable language through multiple well-designed experiments. The results consistently show that agents can learn to communicate effectively, and that the language developed has semantic properties aligning with human conceptualizations. The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, clearly distinguishing its approach from related work in multi-agent systems, "Wizard-of-Oz" environments, and evolutionary approaches to language learning. 

The most compelling evidence comes from the human evaluation experiment, where 68% of human participants were able to correctly identify the target image based on the agent's symbol. This demonstrates a meaningful connection between the emergent language and human language, supporting the paper's claim that their approach can bridge the gap between machine communication and human understanding. The paper also provides a clear path to scaling this approach through the combination of supervised learning and interactive game play, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper's scientific rigor is evident in its multiple lines of evidence: quantitative purity measures showing significant deviation from chance, SVD analysis confirming the multidimensional nature of the emergent language, t-SNE visualizations demonstrating semantic clustering, and the human evaluation experiment. The authors also provide thorough ablation studies comparing different sender architectures and game configurations, strengthening the validity of their claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, the authors should consider the following improvements:

- Expand the discussion of limitations, particularly regarding the scalability of the current approach to more complex communication scenarios involving multiple objects, spatial relationships, or temporal dynamics.
- Include more detailed computational resource requirements (training time, GPU memory usage) to help readers assess the practical feasibility of the approach.
- Clarify the architectural differences between the "informed" and "agnostic" sender models, particularly regarding how the 1D convolutional filters operate on the image embeddings.
- Provide a more thorough comparison with related work on language emergence, particularly in terms of how their game setup differs from and improves upon previous approaches.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper reports that 68% of human subjects correctly identified the target image based on the agent's symbol. How does this performance compare to baseline human-human communication in a similar setting (e.g., humans using randomly assigned symbols to refer to images)?
2. In Figure 3, the t-SNE visualization shows some variation in symbol usage for visually similar objects. What factors might explain this variation, and how might the authors address it in future work to improve semantic consistency?
3. The paper mentions that the agents develop "metonymic" language. Could the authors provide more specific examples of this phenomenon and explain how it relates to linguistic theory and human language use?
4. How might the approach be extended to handle more complex referential communication, such as referring to objects in complex scenes with multiple objects and spatial relationships?
