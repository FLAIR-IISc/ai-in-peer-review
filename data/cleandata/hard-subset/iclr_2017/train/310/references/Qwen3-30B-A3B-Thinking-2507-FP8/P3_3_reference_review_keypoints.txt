- Proposes a multi-agent referential game framework for learning interpretable communication about real images through interaction, without constant human supervision.  
- Main contributions: (1) agents can learn to communicate, (2) emergent communication aligns with high-level semantic categories, (3) grounding with supervised labels improves human interpretability.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — strong contribution, rigorous methods, and compelling results.  
- Positions work clearly relative to prior multi-agent communication research and links to Lewis signaling games and cognitive science.  
- Methodology is rigorous: clear baselines (agnostic vs. informed sender), semantic purity metrics, controlled experiments, and human evaluation.  
- Results show successful communication, semantic structure well above chance, and grounding boosts direct label matching dramatically (from 1% to 47%).  
- Novel observation: agents develop metonymic mappings (e.g., using object labels for related scene elements), indicating generalization beyond supervised vocabulary.  
- Request clarification of the theoretical/motivational basis for the informed sender architecture and its inductive bias.  
- Ask for discussion of scalability and computational costs versus supervised approaches for more complex communication scenarios.  
- Recommend expanding theoretical implications, especially convergence/equilibrium questions in cheap talk games.  
- Suggest analyzing human evaluation limitations and error types (e.g., category vs. metonymic errors) for deeper insight.  
- Question: How does agent metonymy compare to human semantic shift, and is it a general mechanism or visual-domain-specific?  
- Question: How would semantic purity scale with a much larger vocabulary (e.g., 1000 symbols) — continued gains or diminishing returns?  
- Question: How to reconcile agents’ reported perfect communication with only 68% human accuracy — grounding limitation or evaluation artifact?  
- Question: How would removing low-level common knowledge scale to complex environments, and what are the trade-offs between semantic purity and instance-specific communication?
