

# Review of "Learning to Communicate via Multi-Agent Coordination Games"

## Summary
The paper proposes a multi-agent coordination framework for developing communication protocols through referential games, where agents learn to refer to objects in images without pre-defined language. The authors claim their approach leads to the emergence of "natural language" that is semantically meaningful and human-interpretable. They demonstrate that agents can coordinate successfully, develop symbols associated with high-level object categories, and show that grounding with supervised learning improves human interpretability. The work is well-motivated by relevant literature in game theory, linguistics, and multi-agent systems.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes exaggerated claims about the emergence of "natural language" without sufficient evidence, and fails to properly benchmark against established methods.

## Supporting Arguments
The primary issue is the paper's overstatement of results in claiming that agents develop "natural language." The emergent communication protocol is extremely limited (10-100 symbols), bears little resemblance to human language, and lacks syntax, semantics, or pragmatics. The authors present evidence of semantic grouping (purity scores of 70% at best), but this is far from demonstrating natural language emergence. For context, even basic supervised language models achieve much higher semantic coherence.

The paper also fails to provide a meaningful comparison against established baselines. The authors claim their approach is superior to supervised methods but provide no evidence to support this. They don't compare the interpretability of their emergent language against human-labeled data or standard supervised language models. Without this comparison, the claim that their method produces "portable language" that is "interpretable by humans" is unsubstantiated.

The human interpretability results (68% accuracy in crowdsourcing) are presented as evidence of "natural language" emergence, but this is misleading. The task is extremely simple (selecting between two images based on a symbol), and 68% is not particularly impressive compared to baseline supervised methods. The paper fails to demonstrate that their emergent language is better than existing approaches for practical communication tasks.

The paper also makes a fundamental error in claiming to develop "language" when the agents are merely learning to map symbols to object categories in a very constrained setting. The authors cite Lewis' signaling game as a foundation, but their experiments don't address the key questions about equilibrium convergence or the uniqueness of the emergent language that the game theory literature emphasizes.

The paper's most significant claim—that the language developed is "partially interpretable by humans"—is not supported by the evidence. The 68% accuracy in human interpretation is modest at best, and the paper doesn't provide any evidence that this level of interpretability would be useful in practical communication scenarios.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing against established supervised language learning methods, rather than just claiming their approach is better.

2. The authors should clarify the difference between "semantic" properties and "natural language." A symbol system that groups objects by category (e.g., "living vs. non-living") is not language, but rather a simple categorization system.

3. The paper should address the scalability issue: how would this approach scale to complex, real-world communication scenarios with thousands of concepts and nuanced meanings?

4. The authors should be more precise in their terminology. The word "language" is used repeatedly without qualification, when they are actually describing a very primitive symbol system.

5. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach, rather than overstating the significance of their results.

## Clarifying Questions
1. What specific evidence would the authors consider sufficient to demonstrate that their emergent communication protocol constitutes "natural language" rather than a simple categorization system?

2. Why is the 68% human interpretability rate considered significant, especially when compared to standard supervised approaches for similar tasks?

3. Could the authors provide a quantitative comparison between their emergent language and a standard supervised language model in terms of semantic coherence, interpretability, and task performance?

4. How does the authors' approach address the key challenges in game theory literature regarding equilibrium convergence and uniqueness in cheap talk games, which they cite as a foundation for their work?
