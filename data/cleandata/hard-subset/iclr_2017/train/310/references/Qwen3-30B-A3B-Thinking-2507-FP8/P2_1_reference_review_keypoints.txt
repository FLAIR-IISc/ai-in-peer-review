- Introduces a multi-agent, tabula rasa framework where agents learn naturalistic communication via a referential game without human supervision.
- Shows emergent communication protocols align with human semantic categories rather than low-level visual features.
- Proposes a novel grounding method combining supervised image labeling and interactive gameplay to map emergent symbols to human language.
- Reports 68% human interpretation accuracy on a crowdsourced task, indicating partial human interpretability.
- Positions the work at the intersection of AI, linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory with practical conversational AI implications.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Main reason for acceptance: compelling contribution to learning natural language through interaction, with effective grounding to human language.
- Main reason for acceptance: rigorous experimental design with appropriate metrics, controls, and statistical validation.
- Strong motivation and clear problem framing emphasizing learning language through interaction rather than supervised structure.
- Comprehensive experiments: different agent architectures, environment manipulations, and robust evaluation (purity, t-SNE, human surveys).
- Key finding: agents can develop semantically meaningful protocols even with very small symbol sets (e.g., two symbols).
- Grounding method is novel and effective, with qualitative examples showing flexible, human-compatible mappings (e.g., metonymy).
- Situates well within relevant literature and connects to Lewis' signaling game and evolutionary perspectives.
- Suggested improvement: clarify what is meant by "natural language" and which linguistic properties are exhibited.
- Suggested improvement: discuss limitations/reasons for imperfect (68%) human interpretation accuracy.
- Suggested improvement: compare more directly with recent work on emergent language (e.g., Lazaridou et al., Hu et al.) and scalability.
- Suggested improvement: report computational costs, training time, and data requirements for scalability assessment.
- Suggested improvement: expand theoretical discussion for game theorists on convergence and equilibrium selection.
- Question: could the living-vs-non-living split be an artifact of the specific image set, and would it generalize to other image corpora?
- Question: how were human participants trained/instructed in the interpretation task, and what is the random-guess baseline?
- Question: provide examples and analyses of common misinterpretations and error modes in human interpretation.
- Question: how does the number of symbols used in grounded (88/100) vs non-grounded experiments compare, and does symbol count correlate with semantic alignment?
- Question: what limitations are expected when scaling to more complex communication tasks and how might they be addressed?
