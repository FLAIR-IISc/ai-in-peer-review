- Proposes using multi-agent coordination games to learn natural language without supervised conversations.  
- Shows tabula rasa agents can learn to communicate in a referential game and converge quickly.  
- Finds emergent language maps to human conceptual categories rather than low-level visual features.  
- Introduces grounding via combining the referential game with supervised image classification to boost human interpretability.  
- Situates the work within linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, differentiating from prior approaches.  
- Well-structured experiments with attention to agent architectures and environmental factors.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant contribution with strong empirical support.  
- Strong motivation and literature placement, clarifying structural vs functional language learning.  
- Empirical rigor: controlled experiments and appropriate metrics (purity, human interpretability).  
- Grounding yields improved human interpretability (68% correct) and generalization to new images.  
- Request to clarify scalability to more complex environments and diverse interactions.  
- Ask for more precise analysis of metonymic uses of symbols and when they occur.  
- Request comparison to very recent emergent-communication papers (e.g., Lazaridou et al. 2017, Hu et al. 2017).  
- Ask for clearer theoretical justification for the "common knowledge" manipulation producing higher-level semantics.  
- Question: How to scale to multi-step conversations, ambiguity handling, or abstract-concept coordination?  
- Question: How does 68% human accuracy compare to human baselines and can interpretability approach human levels?  
- Question: Why does the informed sender architecture encourage more diverse symbol use vs the agnostic sender?  
- Question: Would grounding effects persist with different label sets (e.g., more semantically structured categories)?
