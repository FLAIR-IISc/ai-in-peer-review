- Contribution: Introduces a multi-agent referential-game framework that produces human-interpretable communication by grounding emergent symbols in supervised human language data.
- Core finding: Agents learn symbol-to-high-level-concept mappings (not low-level visuals), and supervised grounding substantially improves human interpretability.
- Empirical rigor: Multiple experiments show successful coordination, semantic alignment with human categories, and improved human understandability.
- Human evaluation: Crowdsourced survey reports 68% accuracy in humans interpreting agent symbols.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Strength: Clear problem statement and strong motivation linking signaling games to practical human-AI communication.
- Strength: Demonstrated agents coordinate effectively in referential games (Table 1).
- Strength: Emergent language aligns with human semantic categories (Figure 3, Table 1).
- Strength: Supervised grounding dramatically improves interpretability (Section 5, Figure 4).
- Strength: Clear differentiation from prior work (pre-programmed, Wizard-of-Oz, cheap talk).
- Strength: Methodologically rigorous—appropriate baselines, ablations, human evaluations, t-SNE and purity metrics.
- Strength: Strong theoretical grounding in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory.
- Limitation: Framed as an initial step toward human-interpretable communication, not a complete solution.
- Reproducibility request: Provide exact hyperparameters, random seeds, and Gibbs temperature implementation details.
- Statistical rigor request: Add significance testing for key comparisons (e.g., informed vs. agnostic, with vs. without grounding).
- Limitations expansion: Discuss scalability to complex scenarios, failure modes, and dependence on supervised data quality.
- Practical implications: Elaborate on integration into real-world systems with concrete application examples.
- NLP connection: Clarify relation to large pre-trained language models and unique insights beyond current NLP approaches.
- Q1 for authors: Specify how class-level properties were implemented and compared to other class-level methods.
- Q2 for authors: Provide a human-to-human baseline for the crowdsourced task to contextualize 68% accuracy.
- Q3 for authors: Explain plans to overcome initial language limitations and expand vocabulary/semantic scope.
- Q4 for authors: Clarify why the informed sender uses more symbols than the agnostic sender despite architectural nesting.
- Q5 for authors: Describe how supervised grounding interacts with interactive learning—speeding convergence vs. mainly improving interpretability.
