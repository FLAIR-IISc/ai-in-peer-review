- Paper proposes training AI agents via multi-agent referential games to learn functional, natural-language-like communication rather than supervised "canned" conversations.  
- Demonstrates tabula rasa agents can develop communication protocols that achieve near-perfect coordination in referential tasks.  
- Shows emergent symbols map to general conceptual properties (e.g., object categories) rather than low-level visual features, supported by semantic purity metrics and t-SNE.  
- Introduces grounding by combining the referential game with supervised image classification, producing more human-interpretable protocols (68% human accuracy).  
- Connects results to Lewis' signaling games and language emergence literature, situating work within game theory and cognitive science.  
- Decision: accept — paper addresses a core problem in AI language development with a novel, well-motivated approach.  
- Empirical results are rigorous and convincing, with multiple metrics validating semantic alignment and human interpretability.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: clear linkage to signaling-game theory and prior multi-agent and linguistic work.  
- Rigorous methodology: uses coordination accuracy, purity vs. McRae categories, crowdsourced human tests, and t-SNE visualizations.  
- Compelling empirical findings: semantic emergence, increased purity with environment changes, and interpretable grounding outcomes (including living vs. non-living binary distinction with two symbols).  
- Practical significance: grounding approach offers a promising path toward human-interpretable conversational AI.  
- Suggestion: explicitly clarify novelty relative to prior attempts connecting multi-agent learning with human language/supervised data.  
- Suggestion: discuss limitations more thoroughly—scalability, robustness to noise/visual changes, and computational costs.  
- Suggestion: expand human interpretability analysis with qualitative error analysis of when humans succeed or fail.  
- Suggestion: discuss broader implications and steps toward more complex conversational tasks beyond referential games.  
- Suggestion: improve figures (e.g., clearer labels/captions for Figure 3) for better visual presentation.  
- Question: how exactly does the supervised image classification influence the communication protocol—alignment to human labels vs. conceptual effects?  
- Question: clarify what “informed sender” means concretely—what prior knowledge or architectural choices make it "informed"?  
- Question: clarify the human interpretability baseline—does 68% compare against an appropriate chance level (paper’s reported chance numbers appear inconsistent)?  
- Question: what was coordination accuracy before grounding and how quickly did coordination improve after adding supervised learning?  
- Question: how would the approach scale to multi-turn dialogue and what initial steps are needed to extend beyond one-step referential games?
