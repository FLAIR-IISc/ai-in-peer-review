

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes a research program based on multi-agent coordination communication games, where agents develop language through interaction in simple referential environments. The authors claim that agents can learn to communicate effectively without pre-programmed protocols, and that the emergent language captures high-level conceptual properties rather than low-level visual features. They further propose that grounding the communication with supervised learning on image labels can make the language more interpretable by humans. The authors present experimental results showing coordination success, semantic clustering, and improved human interpretability after adding a supervised component.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes ambitious claims about developing a language that is "natural" and interpretable by humans, but provides insufficient evidence to support these claims. The methodology lacks thorough validation against established baselines and the human evaluation is limited in scope and rigor.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's central claim—that the emergent communication can be made "interpretable" by humans through a simple modification—is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The human evaluation consists of a small crowdsourced survey (298 image pairs) where 68% of humans correctly identified the target image based on the sender's symbol. While this is better than chance (1%), it's far from the level required for practical human-AI communication. The paper fails to compare this performance to existing methods for natural language communication or to established benchmarks in human-interpretability of AI systems.

More critically, the paper lacks rigorous comparison to established baselines in the field of emergent communication. The authors cite work by Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016), but do not demonstrate how their approach outperforms these established methods on any standard metric. The paper's experimental design does not include a proper ablation study to isolate the contribution of their specific innovations, such as the "informed sender" architecture or the grounding procedure.

The paper also overstates the significance of its findings. While it's true that agents can learn to coordinate in a referential game (a well-established result in the literature), the paper claims this leads to "a language that is portable to new situations and to new communication partners (in particular humans)." The evidence provided does not support this claim—there is no evaluation of portability to new situations or partners, and the human evaluation is limited to a small subset of the training data.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a thorough comparison with established baselines in emergent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) using standard metrics from the field. This should include a clear ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component.

2. Expand the human evaluation to a larger, more diverse set of participants and test cases, including evaluation on completely new image sets not seen during training. The current evaluation only tests on images that were part of the training set or closely related to it.

3. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the approach. The claim that the language is "natural" is particularly problematic—what does "natural" mean in this context? The paper does not define this term or provide a clear metric for measuring naturalness.

4. Address the scalability concerns raised in the introduction. The paper acknowledges that human-in-the-loop approaches have "serious scalability issues," but the proposed solution (adding humans to the game occasionally) doesn't address the core scalability problem.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your approach compare to the standard baselines in the emergent communication literature (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) on the same metrics? Can you provide a table comparing performance across these methods?

2. What specific metrics do you use to define "naturalness" of the language, and how do you measure it? The current evaluation only shows that humans can sometimes guess the correct image based on the symbol, but this doesn't demonstrate that the language has properties of natural language (e.g., compositionality, productivity).

3. How would you evaluate the portability of the language to new situations or communication partners? The paper claims this is a key goal, but provides no evidence that this is achieved.

4. The paper states that "the informed sender" outperforms the "agnostic sender," but the difference in performance is marginal (Table 1). What is the theoretical justification for this architecture choice, and how does it generalize beyond the specific experimental setup?
