

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for developing communication between AI agents through multi-agent coordination games, specifically focusing on referential games where agents must develop a language to identify target images from distractors. The authors claim that agents can successfully coordinate using a language that reflects general semantic properties rather than low-level visual features, and they demonstrate that grounding this communication in human language via supervised learning improves human interpretability. They present evidence that their agents develop a "natural" language that aligns with human semantic categories and can be partially understood by humans in a crowdsourced experiment.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes a novel contribution to the field of emergent communication, but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its core claims about the emergence of "natural language" and human-interpretable communication. The evidence provided is suggestive but lacks rigorous validation against established baselines and fails to demonstrate meaningful practical utility.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's primary claim—that agents develop communication protocols that are "natural" and human-interpretable—lacks sufficient empirical support. While the authors show that their agents can coordinate successfully and that symbol usage has some semantic structure (e.g., purity scores above chance), they do not demonstrate that their communication system approaches or exceeds the capabilities of established language models or human communication. 

The most significant weakness is the lack of comparison to established baselines. The paper does not compare its emergent communication system to:
- Traditional supervised language models trained on human language data
- Other communication protocols in multi-agent settings
- Human performance on similar referential tasks

Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether the observed properties (semantic purity, human interpretability) represent meaningful progress or simply reflect the specific constraints of their experimental setup.

The paper's evidence for "human-interpretable" communication is particularly weak. The 68% human accuracy in the crowdsourced experiment is impressive for a proof-of-concept, but it's based on a very limited dataset (298 image pairs) and shows the language is only interpretable when the sender uses "conventional" labels (47% exact match). The authors acknowledge this is a "noisy but shared common ground," but don't demonstrate how this would scale to more complex communication scenarios.

The paper also fails to address the scalability and robustness concerns that would be critical for practical applications. The approach requires agents to train together in a specific game environment, but doesn't show how this would translate to real-world scenarios where communication partners might have different training histories or contexts.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper for future submission, the authors should:

1. Add rigorous comparisons against established language models and communication baselines. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether the observed properties represent meaningful progress.

2. Conduct more comprehensive human evaluation studies with larger, more diverse datasets that better reflect real-world communication challenges.

3. Provide a clearer analysis of the limitations of their approach, including the extent to which their "natural language" is actually usable by humans in practical settings.

4. Include a more detailed discussion of how their approach would scale to more complex communication tasks beyond simple referential games.

5. Address the potential for their communication protocol to develop biases or systematic errors that might not be apparent in their current experimental setup.

**Questions for Authors:**
1. How would you demonstrate that your emergent communication system is superior to existing supervised language models for the specific task of referring to visual objects? What metrics would you use?

2. The paper claims that "the language developed in referential games will be initially very limited" but then suggests it could "suffice to establish basic communication." What evidence do you have that this limited language would scale to more complex communication scenarios?

3. The paper shows 68% human interpretability in the ReferItGame experiment, but this is on a very specific dataset. How would you expect this performance to change on more diverse or challenging datasets?

4. Given that the informed sender uses 88 symbols out of 100 with supervised grounding, while the agnostic sender uses only 2 symbols, how do you reconcile this with the claim that their communication is "natural" (which would typically involve more systematic linguistic structure than arbitrary symbol selection)?
