- Proposes multi-agent coordination communication games to learn human-interpretable protocols via interaction rather than supervised canned data.  
- Shows tabula rasa agents can develop communication protocols through interaction.  
- Emergent language encodes high-level semantic properties aligning with human conceptual categories.  
- Introduces grounding method combining interactive learning with supervised image classification to map emergent symbols to human language.  
- Human evaluation: grounded language is partially interpretable (68% accuracy vs ~1% chance).  
- Distinguishes approach from pre-programmed, human-in-the-loop, and Wizard-of-Oz systems; argues supervised methods miss communicative function.  
- Strong theoretical grounding in language emergence and signaling-game literature.  
- Rigorous experimental design using purity metrics, t-SNE clustering, and human interpretability tests.  
- Grounding experiment provides compelling evidence that supervised grounding improves interpretability significantly.  
- Claims both theoretical (cheap-talk/game convergence) and practical significance for building human-interpretable communication.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Recommend adding a scalability discussion for multi-turn dialogues, abstract references, larger vocabularies, and complex environments.  
- Recommend comparing the grounding approach to other alignment methods (e.g., large language models generating descriptions).  
- Recommend deeper analysis of grounding limitations and types of human misinterpretations.  
- Recommend reporting computational efficiency and trade-offs versus purely supervised methods.  
- Recommend exploring grounding with more natural language sources beyond ImageNet labels (e.g., ReferItGame).  
- Ask for distribution details of symbol usage in grounding (how many symbols rare vs frequent).  
- Ask how the metonymic nature of emergent language compares to human language acquisition/usage.  
- Ask for a human baseline comparison (performance on image-pair guessing without the symbol).  
- Ask what properties of the game/embedding enable generalization beyond supervised categories.  
- Ask why the informed sender’s inductive bias (1D convolutions on embeddings) yields more symbols and a more human-like language.
