

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by introducing a novel framework for studying how agents can develop interpretable communication protocols through cooperative games. The authors address a fundamental question in AI: how to design environments that foster the development of a language that is not only functional for agent coordination but also portable to human communication partners. 

The paper's main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that tabula rasa agents can successfully develop communication protocols in referential games, (2) providing evidence that these protocols capture high-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features, and (3) showing that grounding the communication in human language through supervised learning significantly improves human interpretability. The authors convincingly position their work within the broader context of linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, while making a strong case for the practical importance of this research for developing AI that can interact with humans.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a fundamental and timely question in AI with clear relevance to practical applications.
2. The experimental design is rigorous, with appropriate metrics and ablation studies that support the claims.
3. The human evaluation component provides compelling evidence that the approach leads to communication protocols that are partially interpretable by humans.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's central question—how to design environments that foster the development of a language portable to humans—is both well-motivated and important. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation by connecting to Lewis' signaling games, game theory, and linguistic studies, while clearly differentiating their work from related approaches. The paper's position in the literature is well-justified, and the authors effectively argue why their approach is superior to alternatives like supervised learning, pre-programmed communication, or human-in-the-loop systems.

The paper provides robust empirical evidence for its claims. The results in Section 4 demonstrate that agents can successfully coordinate (Table 1, Figure 2), and the semantic analysis (purity scores, t-SNE visualizations) provides strong evidence that the emergent communication protocols capture meaningful semantic distinctions. The most compelling evidence comes from Section 5, where the authors show that supervised grounding leads to a dramatic improvement in human interpretability (68% accuracy in the human evaluation), with examples of metonymic usage that align with natural language patterns.

The experimental design is scientifically rigorous. The authors systematically vary the game setup (e.g., removing low-level common knowledge), test different network architectures, and provide appropriate baselines (e.g., comparing to chance performance in purity scores). The human evaluation is particularly strong, as it goes beyond simple metrics to show that humans can understand the agents' communication in novel contexts.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, these suggestions would further strengthen it:

1. **Clarify the scalability question:** The paper focuses on a very simple two-image referential game. It would be valuable to discuss more explicitly how this approach scales to more complex communication scenarios with more objects, multiple turns of communication, or more complex environments. A brief discussion of potential challenges in scaling would be helpful.

2. **Expand the analysis of the "living vs. non-living" category:** The observation that agents converge to this basic categorization with a 2-symbol vocabulary is intriguing but underexplored. A more detailed analysis of why this happens, and how it relates to human semantic development, would add significant value.

3. **Include failure cases:** It would be helpful to see examples where the communication fails or where the human interpretability is low. This would provide a more complete picture of the approach's limitations.

4. **Clarify the relationship between the informed and agnostic sender architectures:** The paper states that the agnostic architecture "nests" the informed one, but the exact relationship between the two architectures and why the informed one performs better needs more explanation. A brief analysis of what specific inductive bias the informed sender provides would strengthen the argument.

5. **Compare to other recent works:** While the paper cites relevant literature, it could more explicitly compare its approach to recent advances in emergent communication (e.g., the work by Lazaridou et al. at ICLR 2017 or the work by Peng et al. at NeurIPS 2017).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper shows that agents can develop communication protocols with high semantic purity, but what are the specific limitations of this approach when scaling to more complex communication tasks? For example, how would the approach handle communication about abstract concepts (e.g., emotions, intentions) rather than concrete objects?

2. The paper notes that the supervised grounding approach led to 68% accuracy in human interpretation. How does this compare to other approaches for grounding agent communication? For instance, would a purely supervised approach (without the game-based component) achieve similar human interpretability?

3. The paper uses ImageNet classes for supervised grounding. What would happen if the supervised labels were more aligned with human semantic categories (e.g., from WordNet or BabelNet) rather than the more visually oriented ImageNet categories? Would this further improve human interpretability?

4. In the human evaluation, 68% accuracy was achieved. What specific types of errors did humans make (e.g., confusion between similar categories, misinterpretation of metonymic usage), and how might these be addressed in future work?

5. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses more symbols than the agnostic sender. Could you provide more insight into why the informed sender architecture leads to more diverse symbol usage, and whether this diversity directly contributes to the improved semantic purity?
