

# Review of "Learning to Communicate Through Coordination Games"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces a compelling framework for studying the emergence of natural language in multi-agent systems through referential games. The authors address a critical gap in AI research by demonstrating that agents can learn functional communication protocols through interactive coordination without pre-programmed language systems. They provide strong evidence that the emergent communication captures high-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features, and most significantly, they develop a method to ground this emergent language in human-interpretable terms through a simple combination of interactive game playing and supervised learning on image labels. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by bridging theoretical models of language emergence with practical AI applications, showing that agents can develop communication protocols that are not only functional but also partially interpretable by humans.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

**Key reasons for acceptance:** 
1. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in AI (developing functional language-capable agents) with a well-motivated, novel approach that clearly distinguishes itself from existing work.
2. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with multiple complementary metrics (communication success rates, semantic purity, human interpretability) and careful ablation studies that strongly support the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in all three key review criteria:

**First, the specific question/problem is clearly articulated and significant.** The authors correctly identify that supervised learning approaches (like those used in image captioning or machine translation) fail to capture the functional aspects of communication - that humans use words to coordinate with others and make things happen. This is a crucial insight that many papers in the field overlook. The paper's central question - "How do we design environments that foster the development of a language that is portable to new situations and to new communication partners?" - is both timely and important for the field.

**Second, the approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature.** The authors provide an excellent survey of related work, correctly distinguishing their approach from pre-programmed communication systems (Stone & Veloso, 1998), human-in-the-loop approaches (Wang et al., 2016), and Wizard-of-Oz environments (Mikolov et al., 2015). They clearly position their work within the rich tradition of linguistic and cognitive studies using signaling games (Lewis, 1969) while emphasizing their practical AI focus. The paper's motivation is particularly strong given the current limitations of purely supervised language models.

**Third, the paper strongly supports its claims with scientifically rigorous results.** The authors conduct a systematic series of experiments with clear ablation studies:
- They demonstrate that agents can successfully coordinate (near-perfect coordination in 5,000 training rounds)
- They show that the emergent language captures high-level semantic properties (purity scores significantly above chance, with informed sender outperforming agnostic sender)
- Most importantly, they demonstrate that supervised grounding significantly improves human interpretability (68% human accuracy in guessing target images)

The use of multiple metrics (purity, communication success, human interpretability) and the qualitative examples (like the dolphin example for sea) provide compelling evidence. The paper also includes appropriate statistical validation (comparing against random permutations, reporting standard deviations).

The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating that a simple combination of interactive game playing and supervised learning can produce language that is not only functional for coordination but also partially interpretable by humans. The 68% human interpretability rate is particularly impressive given the simplicity of the setup and the lack of any human supervision during the communication phase.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper is well-written and the experiments are well-designed, but there are a few areas where the authors could strengthen their contribution:

1. **Limitations discussion:** While the paper mentions future work on "ensuring that the emergent communication stays close to human natural language," a more detailed discussion of the current limitations would be valuable. For example, how does this approach scale to more complex environments or longer conversations? What are the computational costs compared to other approaches?

2. **Theoretical grounding:** The paper mentions connections to game theory (Lewis' signaling games) but could more explicitly discuss how their findings relate to theoretical predictions about equilibrium selection in cheap talk games.

3. **Comparison to other grounding methods:** A brief comparison to other approaches for grounding language (e.g., using linguistic priors or human feedback) would help situate their contribution more precisely.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In the experiment where agents communicate about ReferItGame images (which contain more complex scenes), the agents used all 100 available symbols. How does the performance (both communication success and human interpretability) compare to the ImageNet-based experiments? Does the complexity of the images affect the emergence of semantic meaning?

2. The paper shows that agents can learn to use "metonymic" references (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea). How do you think this compares to the kinds of semantic shifts that occur in human language development? Are there specific characteristics of human language that this approach could be extended to capture?

3. The authors mention that the informed sender uses more varied symbols than the agnostic sender. Could you provide more detail about the specific inductive biases that make the informed sender more effective? Are there other architectural choices that could further improve the emergence of human-interpretable language?

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of AI communication research. It provides both a compelling theoretical framework and strong empirical evidence that multi-agent coordination games can lead to functional, partially interpretable language - a critical step toward developing AI that can communicate with humans in natural, functional ways. The work is scientifically rigorous, well-motivated, and clearly addresses an important gap in the literature. I strongly recommend acceptance.
