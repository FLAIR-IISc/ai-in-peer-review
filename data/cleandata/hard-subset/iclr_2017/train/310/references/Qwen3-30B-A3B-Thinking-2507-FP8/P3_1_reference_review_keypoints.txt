- Presents a framework for agents to learn natural-language-like communication via multi-agent referential games rather than supervised data.
- Shows tabula rasa neural agents can develop communication protocols achieving near-perfect coordination.
- Finds emergent language reflects human conceptual categories rather than low-level visual features.
- Proposes grounding emergent language in human language by combining the referential game with supervised image classification.
- Provides crowdsourced evidence that grounded language is partially interpretable by humans (68% guessing accuracy).
- Positions the work at the intersection of multi-agent RL, NLP, and cognitive science, grounded in Lewis signaling games.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Key acceptance reason: tackles a fundamental AI problem with a novel, well-motivated approach moving beyond supervised paradigms.
- Key acceptance reason: rigorous experimental design with multiple experiments supporting claims about semantics and interpretability.
- Strength: clearly motivated research question distinguishing coordination-based communication from statistical supervised methods.
- Strength: thorough experiments comparing sender architectures and game modifications.
- Strength: significant empirical results demonstrating coordination and semantic alignment with human categories.
- Strength: informed sender architecture provides an effective inductive bias toward more natural language-like signals.
- Minor concern: human study sample size (298 pairs) and lack of participant demographics limit generalizability.
- Minor concern: grounding via supervised learning may be perceived as a shortcut rather than true emergent grounding.
- Minor concern: limited discussion of scalability to complex tasks (multi-step dialogue, abstract concepts).
- Technical suggestion: report statistical significance (p-values, confidence intervals) for key metrics like purity scores.
- Technical suggestion: provide detailed human study methodology (demographics, instructions, compensation, baseline).
- Technical suggestion: better explain theoretical justification for the informed sender architecture.
- Technical suggestion: expand limitations to address scalability, ImageNet biases, and other input modalities (audio/video).
- Theoretical suggestion: connect empirical results to specific game-theoretic equilibria being reached.
- Theoretical suggestion: provide a more detailed comparison to recent multi-agent communication work (e.g., Lazaridou et al.).
- Conceptual suggestion: define what is meant by “natural language” in the context of emergent protocols.
- Question: how were hyperparameters (embedding dim=50, filters=20, temperature=10) chosen—tuning or prior work?
- Question: what is the baseline random-guess accuracy and how was participant guessing controlled in the human study?
- Question: clarify how the informed sender “nests” the agnostic sender and why that architecture was chosen.
- Question: how will the approach scale to more complex communication tasks beyond referential games?
- Question: how do the authors reconcile that supervised grounding “does not aim at improving coordination” with observed high coordination performance?
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and rigorously evaluated contribution whose limitations (scalability, grounding interpretation) are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution.
