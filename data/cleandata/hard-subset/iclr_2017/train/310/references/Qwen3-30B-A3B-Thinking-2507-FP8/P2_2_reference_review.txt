

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by introducing a research program based on multi-agent coordination communication games. The authors address the critical problem of developing AI agents that can communicate using natural language in a way that is interpretable by humans. They demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn communication protocols through multi-agent interaction in referential games, where agents must coordinate about images to earn payoffs. The paper shows that the emergent communication protocols develop semantic properties that align with human conceptual categories rather than low-level visual properties. Most notably, the authors propose a method to ground the agents' communication in human language by combining the referential game with a supervised image classification task, resulting in communication that is partially interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in a crowdsourced experiment). The paper is well-motivated, provides a comprehensive literature review, and presents scientifically rigorous experiments with multiple supporting metrics and analyses.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of multi-agent communication and language emergence. The key reasons for acceptance are:

1. The paper tackles a fundamental problem in AI (developing communication protocols interpretable by humans) with a well-motivated approach that bridges game theory, linguistics, and deep learning.

2. The empirical evidence is robust, with multiple experiments showing that agents can learn meaningful communication protocols that align with human semantic categories, and that these protocols can be grounded in human language.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Specific Question/Problem Tackled

The paper clearly identifies the central problem: "How do we design environments that foster the development of a language that is portable to new situations and to new communication partners (in particular humans)?" This is a significant gap in the literature, as most existing approaches either rely on supervised learning (which doesn't capture functional aspects of communication) or require constant human intervention (which faces scalability issues). The paper makes a compelling case that multi-agent communication games offer a promising path forward.

### Approach Motivation and Literature Placement

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work across multiple fields, clearly positioning their contribution within the landscape of multi-agent systems, linguistic theory, game theory, and deep learning. They effectively contrast their approach with:
- Supervised learning methods (which miss functional aspects of communication)
- Pre-programmed communication systems (e.g., robot soccer)
- Human-in-the-loop approaches (which face scalability issues)
- Wizard-of-Oz environments (which require heavy engineering)

The connection to Lewis' signaling game theory and linguistic/cognitive studies of language emergence is particularly strong and well-explained. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the theoretical foundations while maintaining a practical AI focus.

### Supporting Evidence for Claims

The paper provides scientifically rigorous evidence for its claims through multiple complementary experiments:

1. **Basic communication success**: The agents learn to coordinate effectively in the referential game (reaching near-perfect coordination after 10k training games).

2. **Semantic properties**: The authors demonstrate that the emergent communication protocols capture general conceptual properties rather than low-level visual features, using multiple metrics:
   - Purity scores (measuring alignment with human semantic categories) significantly above chance
   - t-SNE visualizations showing semantic clustering
   - Qualitative analysis showing the emergence of "living vs. non-living" categorization even with a minimal 2-symbol vocabulary

3. **Grounding in human language**: The key innovation is showing that combining the game with supervised image classification leads to communication that is interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in a crowdsourced experiment). This is a significant step toward the ultimate goal of human-AI communication.

The paper includes careful ablation studies (comparing informed vs. agnostic sender architectures) and multiple validation methods (quantitative metrics, visualizations, human evaluation), making the evidence highly compelling.

## 4. Additional Feedback

### To Improve the Paper

1. **Clarity on "portable" language**: The paper claims the goal is developing a language "portable to new situations and to new communication partners," but the experiments primarily focus on the same image dataset. It would strengthen the paper to discuss how the authors would evaluate portability (e.g., testing on new image categories or environments).

2. **Comparison with related work**: While the paper references relevant work, it could more explicitly compare its approach with recent advances in multi-agent communication (e.g., the work by Sukhbaatar et al. 2016 and Foerster et al. 2016, which the paper cites but doesn't directly compare to its own results).

3. **Analysis of failure modes**: The paper shows positive results, but it would be valuable to include some examples where the communication failed or where the symbols didn't align with human expectations, along with an analysis of why those cases occurred.

4. **Discussion of limitations**: The paper should more thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the scalability to more complex communication scenarios (beyond simple referential games).

### Questions for the Authors

1. In the human evaluation experiment, you report 68% accuracy in guessing the correct image. Could you provide more details about the types of errors made? Were there specific categories of images where the communication was particularly effective or ineffective?

2. The paper mentions that "the agents converge to perfect communication after 15k trials" in the ReferItGame experiment. Given that this is a different dataset from the supervised phase, how much of the communication success is due to the supervised grounding versus the agents' ability to generalize the communication protocol?

3. The paper shows that using class-level properties (instead of image-level details) leads to a small increase in symbol usage purity. Could you elaborate on why this increase is relatively small, given that the agents are now only coordinating on high-level properties? What other modifications to the game setup might lead to more substantial improvements in semantic alignment?

4. You report that the agents used 88 symbols out of 100 in the grounded communication experiment. What is the relationship between the number of symbols used and the purity metric? Is there an optimal vocabulary size for achieving both high communication success and high semantic purity?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field and represents a significant step toward developing AI that can communicate with humans using natural language. The experiments are well-designed, the claims are well-supported, and the work is well-motivated within the broader literature. I believe it would be a valuable contribution to ICLR.
