

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by proposing a novel framework for developing interpretable language through multi-agent coordination games. The authors address a critical gap in current AI research: how to develop communication protocols that are not only functional for agent coordination but also semantically meaningful and potentially interpretable by humans. They introduce a referential game framework where agents learn to communicate about real-world images through interaction, rather than relying on pre-defined language or human supervision. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) demonstrating that agents can learn to communicate successfully in referential games; (2) showing that the emergent communication reflects high-level semantic categories rather than low-level visual features; and (3) proposing a grounded language approach that combines interactive game learning with supervised image labeling to improve human interpretability. The work is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and makes a significant step toward developing AI that can communicate meaningfully with humans.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, with well-motivated research questions, scientifically rigorous methodology, and compelling results that advance our understanding of how communication can emerge through multi-agent interaction. The core claim that agents can develop semantically meaningful communication protocols through interaction, and that this language can be grounded in human language through supervised learning, is well-supported by the evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's approach is well-placed in the literature. It clearly distinguishes itself from existing work on multi-agent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) by focusing on the emergence of language that is semantically meaningful and potentially interpretable by humans. The authors carefully situate their work within linguistic theory (Lewis signaling games) and cognitive science, making the connection between their experimental setup and theoretical frameworks explicit.

The methodology is scientifically rigorous. The authors establish clear baselines (agnostic vs. informed sender architectures), use appropriate metrics for semantic purity (comparing to random permutations), and include a human evaluation of the grounded language. The experimental design is well-controlled, with careful attention to how they modify the environment to encourage higher-level semantic communication. The results are convincing: agents do learn to communicate effectively, the language they develop shows semantic structure (with purity significantly above chance), and grounding through supervised learning dramatically improves human interpretability (from 1% to 47% accuracy in direct label matching).

The paper's most significant contribution is the demonstration that language grounded in human semantics can emerge from multi-agent interaction, rather than requiring constant human supervision. The finding that agents develop "metonymic" links between symbols and images (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to a stretch of sea) is particularly compelling, as it shows the language can generalize beyond the supervised vocabulary in a way that resembles human language.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the "why" behind the informed sender architecture**: The paper notes that the informed sender has an inductive bias toward dimension-by-dimension image combination, but doesn't clearly explain why this specific architecture was chosen over others. A brief discussion of the theoretical motivation for this inductive bias would strengthen the paper.

2. **Address scalability concerns**: The paper mentions that human intervention is not required at each step, but doesn't address how this approach scales to more complex communication scenarios. A brief discussion of the computational costs of training agents in this framework compared to supervised approaches would be valuable.

3. **Expand on the theoretical implications**: While the paper connects to game theory, it could more explicitly discuss how its findings relate to the theoretical questions about convergence to equilibrium in cheap talk games. The observation that agents converge to communication despite the theoretical possibility of non-convergence would be a significant contribution to this literature.

4. **Clarify the limitations of the human evaluation**: The human evaluation shows 68% accuracy, but the paper doesn't discuss the types of errors that occurred. A qualitative analysis of the errors (e.g., were they mostly category errors or metonymic misinterpretations?) would provide deeper insights.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper shows that agents can develop "metonymic" communication (e.g., using "dolphin" for a stretch of sea). How does this compare to other forms of semantic shift in human language? Could this be a general mechanism for language evolution, or is it specific to the visual domain?

2. The informed sender with the 4096-D fc embeddings achieves 70% purity with 100 symbols. How would this performance change with a larger vocabulary (e.g., 1000 symbols)? Would the purity continue to increase, or would there be diminishing returns?

3. The paper mentions that "the agents converge to perfect communication" in the grounded language experiment, but the human evaluation shows only 68% accuracy. How do you reconcile these two metrics? Does the difference reflect a fundamental limitation in the grounding approach, or is it due to the specific setup of the human evaluation?

4. The paper shows that removing low-level common knowledge (by using class-level properties instead of specific images) increases semantic purity. How would this approach scale to more complex environments where the "common knowledge" is not so easily defined? What would be the trade-offs between semantic purity and the ability to communicate about specific instances?

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The experimental results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the implications for developing AI that can communicate meaningfully with humans are substantial. With the minor revisions suggested above, this paper would be a strong candidate for publication at ICLR.
