

# Review of "Learning to Communicate in Multi-Agent Coordination Games"

## Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper presents an intriguing research program exploring how multi-agent coordination communication games can foster the emergence of language-like communication protocols in neural network agents. The authors propose a referential game framework where agents learn to communicate about visual stimuli without pre-programmed language, and they investigate how environmental design influences the semantic properties of the emergent communication. The key contributions include: (1) demonstrating that agents can learn to coordinate successfully in referential games; (2) showing that the agents' communication exhibits semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories; (3) introducing a method to ground the emergent language in human natural language through supervised learning. The work bridges multi-agent reinforcement learning, computational linguistics, and cognitive science, with potential implications for developing human-interpretable AI communication systems.

## Decision

Reject. The paper contains numerous technical and presentation flaws that undermine its scientific rigor and reproducibility.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are severely compromised by multiple critical issues:

1. **Missing Figures and Tables**: The paper references Figure 1, Figure 2, Figure 3, Figure 4, Table 1, and Table 2 throughout the text, but none of these visual elements are included in the provided text. This makes it impossible to verify the empirical claims, particularly the visualizations of symbol usage and semantic clustering. For instance, the claim that "Figure 2 (right) plots the normalized spectrum of this matrix" cannot be evaluated without seeing the figure.

2. **Inconsistent and Unclear Methodology**: The paper states "We experiment with the two architectures depicted in Figure 1" but provides no description of the architectures beyond vague references to "agnostic" and "informed" senders. The description of the "informed sender" applying "1-D convolutions" on "image embeddings by treating them as different channels" is technically ambiguous and lacks necessary implementation details for replication.

3. **Inadequate Statistical Evidence**: The paper claims "purity, while far from perfect, is significantly above chance in all cases" without providing statistical tests or p-values to support this assertion. Similarly, the claim that "68% of the cases the subjects were able to guess the right image" lacks standard error estimates or confidence intervals.

4. **Formatting and Referencing Errors**: The references section contains numerous formatting inconsistencies and errors, including "? \Q1998\E" markers, inconsistent citation styles, and missing publication details. This suggests a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's credibility.

5. **Randomly Inserted Sentence**: A sentence about "Jorge et al. (2016) explore agents playing a 'Guess Who' game" appears abruptly in Section 3, seemingly inserted by mistake. This is a clear indication of poor proofreading.

6. **Unjustified Hyperparameter Choices**: The paper states "We set the following hyperparameters without tuning" but provides no justification for these specific values. This is a significant methodological flaw in a deep learning paper.

The cumulative effect of these issues makes it impossible to assess the validity of the paper's claims or replicate the experiments. While the research question is interesting and potentially important, the paper fails to provide sufficient technical detail, evidence, or clarity to support its conclusions.

## Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide all missing figures, tables, and detailed architecture diagrams to support their claims.
2. Justify all hyperparameter choices with evidence or ablation studies.
3. Include statistical tests for all comparative claims (e.g., "significantly above chance").
4. Clarify the technical details of the "informed sender" architecture and its relationship to the "agnostic sender."
5. Fix the formatting inconsistencies in the references section.
6. Remove the randomly inserted sentence about Jorge et al. (2016) that appears out of context.
7. Provide a clear explanation of how "class-level properties" were implemented in Section 4.1.

## Clarification Questions

1. Could you please provide the missing Figure 1 that describes the two sender architectures?
2. How exactly did you implement the "class-level properties" modification in Section 4.1? Specifically, what was the input to the agents during training?
3. What statistical tests were performed to establish that the purity scores are "significantly above chance"?
4. Can you clarify the relationship between the "game-specific embedding space" and standard neural network embeddings?
5. What was the exact training procedure for the supervised image classification task (e.g., number of epochs, learning rate)?

The paper has promising ideas but is currently undermined by multiple technical and presentation flaws that prevent proper assessment. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the contribution.
