- Paper introduces multi-agent coordination communication games enabling tabula rasa agents to learn human-interpretable protocols via referential games.  
- Main claim: emergent protocols align with human conceptual categories and can be partially grounded in human language (68% human-guess accuracy).  
- Decision: Accept — strong contribution to multi-agent communication and language emergence.  
- Tackles a fundamental problem: designing environments that foster portable, human-portable communication without heavy supervision.  
- Well-motivated and well-placed in literature, linking game theory, linguistics, and deep learning.  
- Empirical evidence is robust: near-perfect coordination after ~10k games in referential tasks.  
- Emergent communication captures semantic properties (purity above chance, t-SNE clustering, living vs. non-living split).  
- Grounding via supervised image classification yields partially interpretable messages (68% crowdsourced accuracy).  
- Solid experimental methodology: ablations (informed vs. agnostic senders), multiple metrics, visualizations, and human evaluation.  
- Improvement: clarify and evaluate “portability” beyond the same image dataset (e.g., new categories/environments).  
- Improvement: more explicit comparisons to recent multi-agent communication work (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al. 2016, Foerster et al. 2016).  
- Improvement: include failure-case examples and analysis where symbols don’t align with human expectations.  
- Improvement: discuss limitations and scalability to more complex communication scenarios beyond simple referential games.  
- Question: provide details on human evaluation errors and category-specific performance (where communication succeeds or fails).  
- Question: quantify contribution of supervised grounding vs. generalization for success on different datasets (e.g., ReferItGame).  
- Question: explain why using class-level properties yields only small increases in symbol purity and what changes could boost semantic alignment.  
- Question: clarify relationship between vocabulary size used (e.g., 88/100 symbols) and purity; is there an optimal vocabulary for success and semantic purity?
