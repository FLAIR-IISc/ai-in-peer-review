- Paper introduces a framework for studying emergent, human-interpretable communication in cooperative multi-agent referential games.
- Main contributions: agents learn communication tabula rasa, protocols encode high-level semantics, supervised grounding improves human interpretability.
- Authors situate work within linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory and argue practical importance for human-AI interaction.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Key reason 1: Tackles a fundamental, timely question with clear practical relevance.
- Key reason 2: Rigorous experimental design with appropriate metrics and ablations supporting claims.
- Key reason 3: Human evaluation shows substantial interpretability gains, providing compelling evidence.
- Strong theoretical grounding connecting to Lewis signaling games and distinguishing from supervised/preprogrammed approaches.
- Robust empirical results: successful coordination, semantic analyses (purity, t-SNE) show meaningful distinctions.
- Supervised grounding yields dramatic human interpretability improvement (68% human accuracy) and naturalistic metonymic patterns.
- Experimental rigor: systematic game variations, architecture tests, baselines, and strong human evaluation protocol.
- Suggestion: Clarify scalability to more complex scenarios (more objects, turns, environments).
- Suggestion: Expand analysis of emergent "living vs. non-living" categorization with 2-symbol vocabulary.
- Suggestion: Include failure cases and examples of low human interpretability to show limitations.
- Suggestion: Clarify relationship and inductive biases between informed and agnostic sender architectures.
- Suggestion: More explicit comparison to recent emergent communication works (e.g., Lazaridou et al., Peng et al.).
- Question: How does the approach scale to communicating abstract concepts (emotions, intentions) versus concrete objects?
- Question: Would purely supervised grounding achieve similar human interpretability without the game-based component?
- Question: Would supervised labels aligned to human semantic categories (WordNet/BabelNet) improve interpretability versus ImageNet labels?
- Question: What types of errors did humans make in the 68% evaluation and how could they be mitigated?
- Question: Why does the informed sender use more symbols than the agnostic sender, and does that diversity drive higher semantic purity?
