

# Review

## Summary
The paper introduces a multi-agent coordination framework where agents learn to develop communication protocols through referential games, with the goal of creating languages that are portable to new situations and human communication partners. The authors investigate whether tabula rasa agents can successfully develop communication, and what environmental features lead to language resembling human language (e.g., associating symbols with conceptual properties rather than low-level visual properties). They demonstrate that agents can coordinate effectively, develop symbols associated with high-level semantic categories, and that grounding the communication in human language through supervised learning improves human interpretability.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution to emergent communication research but has significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting ICLR's standards. The main issues are insufficient evidence for the claimed "natural language" properties, limited experimental scope, and insufficient novelty compared to existing work.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's key claim is that the emergent language in their framework resembles human language in meaningful ways. However, the evidence supporting this claim is weak. While they show that symbols correlate with high-level semantic categories (e.g., "animal" vs. "vehicle"), they do not demonstrate that this is fundamentally different from what would be expected from simple statistical associations between visual features and symbols. The paper fails to provide evidence that the language has properties beyond simple association (e.g., compositionality, generalization to novel contexts).

The experimental design has significant limitations. The referential game is highly simplified (two agents, fixed vocabulary size, limited to ImageNet images), which does not reflect the complexity of real-world communication. The "human interpretability" results are limited to a small subset of the vocabulary (only 100 labels) and rely on crowdsourced judgments that may not reflect true language understanding. The paper also fails to sufficiently address whether the observed effects are specific to their environment design or would occur in other settings.

Most critically, the paper does not clearly articulate its novelty beyond existing work in emergent communication (Sukhbaatar et al. 2016, Foerster et al. 2016). While they mention grounding the language in human labels, this is a relatively straightforward extension of prior work, not a significant conceptual advance. The paper does not explain why their approach is superior to existing methods for developing human-interpretable communication.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide more rigorous evidence that the emergent language has properties beyond simple statistical associations (e.g., demonstrate compositionality or generalization to novel situations).
2. Expand the experimental scope to include more complex communication tasks and environments.
3. Clearly articulate the novelty compared to existing work in emergent communication, particularly regarding how their approach specifically advances the field.
4. Conduct a more thorough analysis of the limitations of their approach and how they might be addressed in future work.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your work specifically advance the field beyond existing work in emergent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al. 2016, Foerster et al. 2016)? What is the specific novelty of your approach?

2. How do you address the concern that the association between symbols and semantic categories could simply be a statistical artifact of the image dataset rather than a true linguistic phenomenon?

3. Can you provide evidence that the emergent language has properties beyond simple association (e.g., compositionality, ability to handle novel situations)?

4. How would you scale this approach to more complex communication tasks that require more sophisticated language structures?
