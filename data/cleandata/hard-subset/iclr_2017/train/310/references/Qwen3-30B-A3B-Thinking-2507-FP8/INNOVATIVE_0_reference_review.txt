

# Review of "Learning to Communicate via Multi-Agent Coordination Games"

## Summary of Contribution
This paper presents a bold and novel research program for developing AI agents capable of natural language communication through multi-agent coordination games. Rather than relying on supervised learning from human-annotated data or human-in-the-loop approaches, the authors propose training agents to develop language through interaction in simple referential games. The paper makes several significant contributions: demonstrating that tabula rasa agents can successfully develop communication protocols; showing that these protocols develop semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories; and introducing a method to ground emergent language in human natural language through a hybrid supervised-interaction approach. The work is well-positioned at the intersection of game theory, linguistics, and deep learning, with the potential to fundamentally shift how we approach language acquisition in AI.

## Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a novel and promising research direction that directly challenges the dominant paradigm of supervised language learning. While the experimental setup is relatively simple, the conceptual framework and the demonstrated ability of agents to develop semantically meaningful communication protocols represent a significant conceptual advance.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper excels in its conceptual novelty and clear articulation of a new research direction. The authors successfully demonstrate that agents can bootstrap language development through interaction, rather than requiring pre-specified communication protocols or human supervision. This is a major conceptual step beyond prior work on emergent communication (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), which focused primarily on whether communication could emerge, not whether it could develop semantic properties aligned with human intuition.

The most compelling evidence for the paper's contribution comes from the human interpretability experiments. The demonstration that agents trained with a hybrid approach (interaction + supervised language grounding) develop a communication protocol that humans can interpret at 68% accuracy is particularly significant. This moves beyond the typical "does communication emerge?" question to address the crucial practical question of whether emergent language can be made interpretable to humans—a key barrier to real-world application.

While the paper's experimental setup is relatively simple (referential games with image pairs), this simplicity is actually a strength that enables clear demonstration of the core concept. The authors make a strong case that this is the first step toward a scalable approach to language learning that doesn't require massive human annotation. The paper's focus on "portable language" that can be used with new partners and in new situations is particularly valuable and addresses a critical gap in current AI communication research.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach, particularly regarding the scalability of the referential game setup to more complex communication scenarios beyond object reference.

2. A clearer articulation of how the proposed approach differs from the "language games" literature in cognitive science (e.g., Steels & Loetzsch, 2012). While the paper references this work, it would benefit from a more precise positioning of its contribution relative to this tradition.

3. Additional analysis of the "metonymic" communication observed in the human interpretation experiments (e.g., "dolphin" referring to a stretch of sea). A more systematic exploration of this phenomenon would strengthen the claim that the emergent language has natural language-like properties.

## Clarifying Questions for Authors
1. The paper mentions that the informed sender architecture is "more natural" than the agnostic one, but the evidence for this is somewhat indirect (e.g., higher symbol usage diversity). Could the authors provide more direct evidence that the informed sender's language is more aligned with human semantic categories?

2. In the human interpretation experiment, 68% accuracy is impressive, but it's unclear how this compares to baseline performance. What would be the accuracy of humans guessing the correct image if they were simply given a random word from the vocabulary? This comparison would strengthen the claim that the emergent language is interpretable.

3. The paper mentions that "the language developed in referential games will be initially very limited." What specific limitations do the authors anticipate, and how do they plan to address these in future work to move toward more complex conversational capabilities?
