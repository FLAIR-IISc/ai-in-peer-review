

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a research program based on multi-agent coordination communication games to develop agents capable of developing a language to coordinate and earn payoffs in simple environments. The authors demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully coordinate in referential games, develop a language with semantic properties aligned with human conceptualizations, and show that supervised learning can ground this communication in human language. The paper makes a compelling case for using game-based paradigms to develop interpretable language, and the results suggest that the emergent language captures general conceptual properties rather than low-level visual features.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple methodological and presentation flaws that undermine the validity of its claims, particularly regarding statistical significance, clarity of metrics, and evidence of language portability.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes several strong claims that are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided:

- The paper claims "symbol purity dramatically increases to 70%" but provides no clear definition of "purity" or statistical significance testing for this claim. The metric is referenced but not properly defined, making it impossible to assess the validity of this claim. The paper compares to "obs-chance purity difference" but doesn't explain what this means or how it's calculated.

- The paper states "the agents are still able to reach full coordination after 10k training trials" but provides no comparison to baseline methods or previous work in referential games. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if the results are meaningful or just a baseline outcome.

- The paper claims "language is portable to new situations and to new communication partners" but provides no evidence of this. The experiments are conducted on the same dataset used for training, with no transfer testing to new situations or communication partners. This is a critical gap given the paper's main thesis.

- The reference formatting contains multiple errors throughout (e.g., "Deng et al.,? \Q2009\E" instead of "Deng et al., 2009"), indicating a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's credibility.

- The human evaluation of language interpretability states "in 68% of the cases the subjects were able to guess the right image" but provides no details about the number of participants, how they were recruited, or the statistical significance of this result. Without this information, it's impossible to assess the validity of the claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- Clarify all metrics (particularly "purity") with precise definitions and statistical significance testing for all claims.
- Provide evidence of language portability by testing the emergent language on new datasets or with new communication partners.
- Fix the reference formatting errors throughout the paper.
- Provide more details about the human evaluation methodology (number of participants, recruitment process, experimental design).
- Justify hyperparameters (e.g., temperature τ = 10) with more explanation and sensitivity analysis.
- Compare results more clearly with prior work in referential games to establish the novelty of the contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How was the "purity" metric calculated, and what is the statistical significance of the observed purity scores compared to chance?
- What evidence is there that the language developed is actually portable to new situations or new communication partners? The paper claims portability but provides no evidence of this.
- How many human participants were involved in the online experiment, and what was the methodology for recruiting them?
- Why was the temperature parameter set to 10, and how sensitive are the results to this choice?
- How does your approach to grounding communication differ from previous work on language grounding (e.g., Wang et al., 2016), and why is your method superior?
