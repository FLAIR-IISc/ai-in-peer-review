

# Review

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

This paper presents a bold and novel research program for developing AI agents capable of learning natural language through multi-agent coordination games. The authors argue that supervised learning on "canned" conversations fails to capture the functional aspects of communication, and instead propose a paradigm where agents start as blank slates and develop language through interactive coordination in simple environments. The core contribution is a framework for studying language emergence in multi-agent systems, with experiments demonstrating that agents can successfully coordinate, develop communication protocols with semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories, and that these protocols can be grounded in human language through a hybrid learning approach. The authors convincingly position their work at the intersection of linguistics, cognitive science, game theory, and AI, distinguishing their approach from existing methods that rely on pre-programmed communication, human-in-the-loop systems, or "Wizard-of-Oz" environments.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper introduces a fundamentally new direction for language learning in AI, with significant novelty and boldness that outweighs minor methodological limitations.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—using multi-agent coordination games as the basis for language learning—represents a genuinely novel approach that challenges the dominant paradigm of supervised learning for language models. The authors successfully demonstrate that agents can develop communication protocols with semantic properties (referring to high-level conceptual categories rather than low-level visual features), and that these protocols can be grounded in human language through a hybrid learning approach. 

The novelty is particularly compelling because:
- The authors move beyond simply showing that agents can communicate to investigating *how* to design environments that foster interpretable language
- They bridge the gap between theoretical work on language emergence and practical AI development
- Their grounding approach (combining supervised learning with interactive games) provides a promising path toward human-interpretable communication

The paper's claim that agents can bootstrap language development through interaction with other agents is well-supported by their empirical results, including the semantic purity of the emergent communication (70% purity with 100 symbols) and the human interpretability results (68% accuracy in crowdsourced experiments). These results are significant not because they are perfect, but because they demonstrate a viable path toward language that is both functional and interpretable.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors might consider:
- Discussing the scalability of their approach to more complex environments and communication tasks
- Providing more detailed analysis of the "metonymic" language shifts observed (e.g., dolphin for a stretch of sea), as these represent a particularly interesting aspect of the emergent language
- Including a more thorough comparison of their approach with other recent work on language emergence (e.g., the work by Lazaridou et al. on multi-agent language learning)
- Adding a brief discussion of potential limitations of their approach in handling more complex linguistic phenomena (e.g., syntax, pragmatics)

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that agents can develop language with semantic properties, but how might this approach scale to more complex linguistic structures (e.g., syntax, negation, or complex referents)? What would be the next step in the research program to address these challenges?

2. The authors show that grounding in human language through supervised learning improves human interpretability. How might the authors extend this approach to handle cases where the supervised labels are ambiguous or where multiple valid labels could apply to the same image?

3. The paper shows promising results for human interpretability (68% accuracy), but this is still significantly below perfect. What additional modifications to the game setup or learning procedure might further improve this human interpretability without compromising the agents' ability to coordinate?
