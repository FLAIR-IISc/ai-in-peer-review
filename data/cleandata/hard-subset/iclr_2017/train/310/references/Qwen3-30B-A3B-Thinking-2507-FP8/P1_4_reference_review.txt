

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a compelling research program for developing AI agents that can learn natural language communication through multi-agent coordination games, rather than relying on supervised learning with pre-defined conversations. The authors tackle the fundamental question of how to design environments that foster the development of a language that is portable to new situations and communication partners, particularly humans. 

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) It demonstrates that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to communicate through a referential game framework, showing convergence to coordination in reasonable time; (2) It provides evidence that the emergent language develops semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories rather than low-level visual features; (3) It introduces a novel method for grounding the agents' communication in human language by combining the referential game with supervised image classification, significantly improving human interpretability; and (4) It connects their work to relevant literature in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory while clearly differentiating from previous approaches.

The paper is well-structured and makes a strong case for the value of multi-agent coordination games as a paradigm for developing natural language communication in AI. The experimental design is thorough, with careful attention to both agent architectures and environmental factors that influence language emergence.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing and multi-agent systems, with strong empirical results that directly address the central problem of developing portable, human-interpretable communication protocols.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper excels in three key areas that justify acceptance:

First, the problem is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work in multi-agent communication (pre-programmed systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, Wizard-of-Oz environments), clearly identifying the gap their approach fills. They connect their work to theoretical foundations in linguistics and game theory (Lewis signaling games, cheap talk), establishing a strong conceptual basis for their methodology. The distinction between structural language learning (supervised approaches) and functional language learning (coordination-based) is particularly well-articulated.

Second, the empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The authors conduct a series of controlled experiments that systematically investigate how different environmental factors affect language emergence. Their metrics (purity with respect to conceptual categories, human interpretability in crowdsourced experiments) are appropriate and compelling. The demonstration that the language developed by agents has semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories, and that grounding through supervised learning significantly improves human interpretability (68% correct guesses in human evaluation), provides strong evidence for their claims.

Third, the paper makes a clear path toward practical applications. The authors don't just demonstrate language emergence but show how to make the emergent language more aligned with human communication. The finding that agents can generalize their communication to new image sets beyond the supervised categories (with 68% human interpretability) is particularly promising for real-world applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Clarity on scalability**: The paper mentions that the approach "does not require human intervention at each step," but doesn't address how the system would scale to more complex environments with more diverse objects and interactions. A brief discussion of potential scalability challenges and how this approach might address them would strengthen the paper.

2. **More precise analysis of the "metonymic" language**: The paper notes that agents sometimes use metonymic links (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea) and that humans can interpret these. A more detailed analysis of when and why these metonymic shifts occur, and how they might be controlled or optimized, would be valuable.

3. **Comparison to recent related work**: While the literature review is comprehensive, it doesn't discuss some very recent work in emergent communication (e.g., by Lazaridou et al. 2017, or Hu et al. 2017). A brief comparison would help situate their contribution more precisely.

4. **Clarification on the role of the "common knowledge" manipulation**: The paper removes low-level common knowledge to encourage higher-level semantics, but it's not entirely clear why this specific manipulation leads to the observed improvements. A more detailed explanation of the theoretical justification for this design choice would strengthen the paper.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me better assess the paper's contribution and potential impact, I'd like to ask the authors to clarify:

1. How do you envision scaling this approach to more complex communication tasks beyond simple referential games (e.g., multi-step conversations, handling ambiguity, or coordinating on abstract concepts)?

2. In the human evaluation experiment, you report 68% accuracy in identifying the target image. How does this compare to the performance of humans in similar referential games? Could the agents' language be improved to approach human-level interpretability?

3. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses more symbols than the agnostic sender. Could you elaborate on how the specific architecture of the informed sender (with its convolutional filters) specifically encourages this more varied symbol usage? Is there a theoretical explanation for why this architecture outperforms the agnostic one?

4. In the "grounding" experiment, you use ImageNet labels as the supervised objective. How would the approach change if you used a different set of labels (e.g., more semantically structured categories or a language with richer semantic relations)? Would the grounding effect still hold?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing and multi-agent systems. The experimental results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the work clearly addresses an important problem in AI. I believe it would be a strong addition to ICLR.
