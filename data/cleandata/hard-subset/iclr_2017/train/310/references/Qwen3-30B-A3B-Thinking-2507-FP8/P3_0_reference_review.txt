

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant and timely contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by addressing a fundamental challenge in AI: how to develop communication protocols between agents that can be understood by humans. The authors propose a novel framework based on multi-agent coordination communication games, where agents develop language through interaction rather than through supervised learning on canned conversations. The paper demonstrates that agents can learn to communicate effectively in referential games, that the emergent language captures high-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features, and most importantly, that the language can be grounded in human natural language through a combination of interactive learning and supervised learning.

The key innovations are:
1. A clear demonstration that tabula rasa agents can develop communication protocols through interaction
2. An analysis showing the semantic properties of the emergent language align with human conceptual categories
3. A practical method for grounding the emergent language in human natural language through a supervised image classification task
4. A compelling human evaluation showing that the grounded language is partially interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in identifying target images)

The work is well-motivated, well-executed, and makes a clear contribution to both the multi-agent systems and natural language processing communities. The paper effectively bridges theoretical linguistics, cognitive science, and practical AI development.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with well-designed experiments and a clear path toward human-interpretable communication. The work addresses a critical gap in current AI research by demonstrating a viable path to developing communication protocols that can be understood by humans, rather than just statistical patterns between sequences of symbols.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

**Strong theoretical foundation and well-placed in the literature:** The paper effectively situates itself within the rich tradition of linguistic and cognitive studies on language emergence (Lewis' signaling game, Crawford & Sobel, etc.) while making a compelling argument for why their approach is different from and superior to existing methods. They clearly distinguish their work from:
- Pre-programmed communication systems (Stone & Veloso, 1998)
- Human-in-the-loop approaches (Winograd, 1971; Wang et al., 2016)
- Wizard-of-Oz environments (Mikolov et al., 2015)

Their key insight that "supervised approaches do not capture the functional aspects of communication" is well-supported by the literature they cite (Austin, 1962; Clark, 1996; Wittgenstein, 1953).

**Rigorous experimental design and compelling results:** The experimental setup is carefully designed to test specific hypotheses about language emergence. The authors effectively use multiple measures to assess the quality of the emergent language:
- Purity metrics against human-defined object categories
- t-SNE visualizations showing semantic clustering
- Human evaluation of language interpretability

The most compelling evidence comes from the grounding experiment (Section 5), where they demonstrate that by combining the referential game with supervised learning on ImageNet labels, the emergent language becomes significantly more interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in identifying target images, compared to 1% chance). This is a major contribution with clear practical implications.

**Theoretical and practical significance:** The paper speaks to both game theorists (addressing convergence in cheap talk games) and practitioners (providing a path to human-interpretable communication). The grounding approach is particularly innovative and addresses a critical bottleneck in developing practical conversational AI.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these improvements would further enhance its impact:

1. **Scalability discussion:** The paper focuses on a simple referential game with a limited vocabulary (10-100 symbols). A more detailed discussion of how the approach would scale to more complex communication tasks (e.g., multi-turn dialogues, reference to abstract concepts, or more complex environments) would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

2. **Comparison to other grounding approaches:** It would be valuable to have a brief comparison of the grounding approach with other methods for aligning AI language with human language (e.g., using large language models to generate human-like descriptions).

3. **Limitations of the grounding approach:** The paper mentions that the grounded language is "partially interpretable" but doesn't thoroughly analyze the types of errors humans make. A deeper analysis of when and why humans misinterpret the agents' language would provide important insights for future work.

4. **Computational efficiency:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the grounding approach compared to purely supervised methods. A brief analysis of the trade-offs between communication success and computational resources would be helpful for practitioners.

5. **Beyond ImageNet labels:** The paper uses ImageNet labels for grounding, but these are not natural language descriptions. A discussion of how the approach might work with more natural language descriptions (e.g., from the ReferItGame dataset) would strengthen the practical relevance.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in the assessment, I'd like to ask the authors:

1. In the grounding experiment (Section 5), the authors report that the sender used 88 out of 100 symbols. Could you elaborate on the distribution of symbol usage across the vocabulary? Specifically, how many symbols were used extremely rarely versus frequently?

2. The paper mentions that the language developed in the referential game is "metonymic" (Figure 4), but doesn't discuss how this compares to human language. How do you think the metonymic nature of the emergent language relates to human language acquisition or usage patterns?

3. In the human evaluation (Section 5.2), 68% of subjects correctly identified the target image based on the sender's symbol. Given that the human subjects were shown the image pair and the symbol, how does this compare to the performance of humans in a similar task without the symbol (e.g., if humans had to guess the target based on the image pair alone)?

4. The paper shows that the grounded language generalizes beyond the supervised categories (Section 5.2), but doesn't discuss the specific mechanisms behind this generalization. What properties of the game setup or the embedding space allow for this generalization?

5. The authors mention that the informed sender uses more symbols than the agnostic sender, but doesn't fully explain why this is beneficial. Could you elaborate on how the inductive bias of the informed sender (using 1D convolutions on image embeddings) specifically contributes to developing a more human-like language?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings. The work is well-executed, well-motivated, and provides a clear path toward developing conversational AI that can communicate with humans in meaningful ways.
