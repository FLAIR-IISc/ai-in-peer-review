

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication by proposing a novel research program based on multi-agent coordination communication games. The authors argue that natural-language-based communication is essential for AI agents to cooperate with humans, and that traditional supervised learning approaches fail to capture the functional aspects of communication. Instead, they propose a framework where agents learn to communicate through interactive games without pre-coded protocols.

The paper's main contributions are:
1. Demonstrating that tabula rasa agents can successfully develop communication protocols through interaction in referential games.
2. Showing that the emergent communication protocols capture general conceptual properties rather than low-level visual features.
3. Proposing a method to ground the agents' communication in human language through a combination of interactive game playing and supervised image classification.
4. Providing evidence that this grounded language can be interpreted by humans, even for objects not seen during supervised training.

The authors conduct thorough experiments to support these claims, including analysis of semantic properties of the emergent language, human interpretability studies, and ablation studies on different game setups.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with well-motivated research questions and rigorous empirical evaluation. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in current approaches to natural language communication in AI, providing a principled alternative to supervised learning.
2. The experimental design is sound, with appropriate metrics and careful analysis of the emergent communication protocols.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and positioned within the literature. The authors correctly identify the gap between traditional supervised approaches to language learning and the functional aspects of communication. Their approach to grounding communication in human language through a combination of interactive game playing and supervised learning is particularly innovative and addresses a critical need in the field.

The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The authors show that agents can successfully coordinate in referential games, and that the communication protocols they develop capture higher-level semantic properties (as measured by purity with respect to McRae categories). The human interpretability experiments are particularly strong evidence that the approach has practical relevance for developing AI systems that can communicate with humans.

The paper's theoretical grounding in game theory is another major strength. By connecting their work to Lewis' signaling game and cheap talk literature, the authors provide a framework for understanding why certain game setups lead to more natural language emergence. The ablation studies showing that removing low-level common knowledge forces agents to coordinate on more abstract properties is particularly convincing evidence of the approach's validity.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are several areas where it could be improved:

1. **Broader Context**: The paper could better situate itself within the broader AI research landscape. While it discusses related work well, it could more explicitly connect to recent advances in language modeling (e.g., GPT-3, BERT) and multi-agent reinforcement learning.

2. **Limitations**: The paper should more explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the current approach. The experiments are conducted on a relatively small set of images (463 concepts), and the language developed is very limited. The authors should discuss how their approach might scale to more complex communication scenarios.

3. **Theoretical Analysis**: While the paper connects to game theory, it could provide more theoretical analysis of why certain game setups lead to certain types of emergent languages. A more formal analysis of the relationship between game structure and language properties would strengthen the paper.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Approaches**: The paper could benefit from a more systematic comparison to alternative approaches for grounding language in the real world (e.g., the work by Andriy et al. on grounding language in videos).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses more symbols than the agnostic sender, but it's not entirely clear why this is the case. Could you provide more intuition about how the convolutional architecture in the informed sender leads to more diverse symbol usage?

2. In the human interpretability experiment, you report 68% accuracy for humans guessing the correct image based on the sender's symbol. How does this compare to a baseline where humans are shown the actual ImageNet label for the target image (instead of the sender's symbol)? This would help contextualize the 68% figure.

3. The paper mentions that the language developed is "very limited" and that "the noisy but shared common ground might suffice to establish basic communication." Could you elaborate on what you mean by "basic communication" and how you would measure progress toward more complex communication capabilities?

4. In the section on "Object-Level Reference," you modify the game to show agents class-level properties instead of specific images. How would you expect this approach to scale to more complex visual domains where class-level properties might not be sufficient for meaningful communication?

5. The paper mentions that supervised learning on a separate dataset "provides some grounding for communication with humans, that generalizes beyond the conventional word denotations learned in the supervised phase." Could you provide more detail about how this generalization works, particularly for the metonymic examples you describe (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea)?
