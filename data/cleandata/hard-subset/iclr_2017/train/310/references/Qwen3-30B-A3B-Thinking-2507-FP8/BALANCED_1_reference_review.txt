

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper introduces a multi-agent coordination framework for studying the emergence of communication protocols between AI agents through referential games. The authors demonstrate that agents can develop symbolic communication that aligns with high-level semantic categories (rather than low-level visual features) and show that grounding this communication in natural language via supervised learning improves human interpretability. The work is well-motivated, drawing connections to game theory, linguistics, and cognitive science, and positions itself as a bridge between theoretical language evolution and practical AI development.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's main claims about language emergence and portability are overstated and not sufficiently supported by the experimental evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary weakness of the paper is its overstatement of the linguistic capabilities of the emergent communication protocol. The paper claims to develop language "portable to new situations and to new communication partners (in particular humans)" but the experiments do not demonstrate this portability. The communication protocol is developed within a highly constrained game setup using only 463 base-level concepts from ImageNet, and the human interpretability tests are limited to a small subset of categories used in the supervised phase. The paper shows that agents can develop a simple referential code for the specific visual domain they were trained on, but fails to demonstrate that this code can be meaningfully applied to new visual domains, new tasks, or new communication partners.

The paper also overstates the linguistic sophistication of the emergent communication. The authors claim to develop "language" that "resembles human language" but the emergent communication is a simple referential code with no syntax, no pragmatics, and no ability to express complex propositions. The "semantic properties" they observe are limited to basic category distinctions (e.g., living vs. non-living), which is a far cry from the rich semantic and syntactic structure of natural language.

The methodology has additional limitations. The paper uses a single game setup with very specific constraints (e.g., fixed vocabulary size, specific image representations), and doesn't adequately explore whether these constraints are necessary for the emergence of semantic communication. The ablation studies are limited to comparing two sender architectures, without exploring more fundamental design choices in the game setup.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. **Clarify the scope of "language"**: The paper should explicitly define what linguistic features they consider necessary for a communication system to be considered "language" in this context, rather than conflating simple reference with natural language.

2. **Demonstrate portability**: The most critical improvement would be to show that the emergent communication can be applied to new visual domains or tasks beyond the specific game setup used in the experiments. This could involve training on one set of visual categories and testing on a completely different set.

3. **Expand the human evaluation**: The human interpretability experiments should be extended to cover a wider range of categories and visual contexts to better assess the generalizability of the communication protocol.

4. **Include more thorough ablation studies**: The authors should systematically explore how different aspects of the game setup (e.g., vocabulary size, image representation, game rules) affect the emergence of semantic communication.

5. **Discuss limitations and scalability**: The paper should more candidly address the limitations of the current approach and discuss how it might scale to more complex communication tasks.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How would you demonstrate that the emergent communication protocol is truly "portable" to new visual domains or tasks beyond the specific game setup used in your experiments? What specific experiments would you conduct to address this?

2. The paper claims to develop communication "resembling human language," but your results only show basic category distinctions. What specific linguistic features (beyond simple reference) would you consider necessary for the communication to be considered "natural language" in your framework?

3. The paper states that the agents "develop and bootstrap knowledge on top of each others," but the experiments don't demonstrate this bootstrapping beyond the single game setup. How would you design experiments to show that the agents are truly building upon prior communication experiences across different game instances?

4. Your human evaluation shows 68% accuracy in interpreting the emergent language. How do you expect this accuracy to change when the agents communicate about more complex visual scenes or when the human participants have less context about the game setup?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding how simple communication protocols can emerge through multi-agent interaction, but it overstates the linguistic capabilities of the emergent system and fails to demonstrate the portability claims central to its contribution. With more rigorous experiments demonstrating portability and a more precise definition of "language" in this context, the work could be significantly strengthened.
