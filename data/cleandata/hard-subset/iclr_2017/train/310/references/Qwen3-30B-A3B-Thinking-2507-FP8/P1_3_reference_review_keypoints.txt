- Introduces a game-based framework for agents to learn natural-language communication through interactive play rather than supervised human language data.  
- Demonstrates tabula rasa agents can learn referential games and develop symbols tied to high-level conceptual categories.  
- Grounds agent communication with supervised image classification to produce partially human-interpretable language (68% crowdsourced accuracy).  
- Provides strong theoretical grounding in game theory and linguistics while making practical progress toward human-compatible agent communication.  
- Decision: Accept — well-motivated, rigorously executed, and supported by strong empirical evidence.  
- Shows agents successfully coordinate in referential games, with the informed sender architecture outperforming the agnostic sender.  
- Emergent communication has semantic alignment with human concepts (purity 70% vs. chance 33%; supported by t-SNE visualizations).  
- Grounding via supervised labels yields 68% human interpretability and 47% label-matching; qualitative metonymic links indicate language flexibility.  
- Situates work thoroughly in related literature, linking to multi-agent, human-in-the-loop, and signaling-game research.  
- Suggests adding a detailed discussion of limitations and scalability to complex tasks (e.g., QA, multi-turn dialogue).  
- Recommends deeper analysis of why the informed sender architecture yields better language emergence (specific inductive biases).  
- Calls for a more thorough comparison with recent language emergence work (e.g., 2019 "Emergence of Grounded Language" paper).  
- Advises discussing potential failure modes of grounding, especially language drift when interacting with humans over time.  
- Asks how the approach would scale to complex language tasks and what challenges must be addressed.  
- Asks whether metonymic linking can be systematically encouraged in training rather than left to emergence.  
- Requests a baseline separating the effect of supervised labels from interactive gameplay (agents trained only on supervised task).  
- Asks what mechanisms enable generalization to images outside the supervised category set and how to improve it.  
- Asks how to address potential language drift as agents interact with evolving human language conventions.
