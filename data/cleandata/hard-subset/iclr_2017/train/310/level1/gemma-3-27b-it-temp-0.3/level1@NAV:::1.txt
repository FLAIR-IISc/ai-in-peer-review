## Review of "A Framework for Language Learning Based on Multi-Agent Communication"

This paper presents a compelling framework for language learning grounded in multi-agent communication, specifically through referential games. The authors convincingly argue that interactive learning is crucial for developing truly intelligent agents capable of natural language communication, moving beyond the limitations of purely passive, supervised learning approaches. They demonstrate that simple neural networks can indeed learn to coordinate and develop a rudimentary “language” within this game environment, and explore methods to influence the semantic properties of this emergent language and ground it in human-understandable terms. The work is well-motivated, drawing connections to linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, and builds upon related work in multi-agent systems and communication games.

**Reject** – While the work is promising and well-executed, the paper currently lacks sufficient detail and analysis to fully convince me of the significance of the results. Specifically, the grounding experiments, while showing encouraging initial results, feel somewhat under-developed and require more rigorous evaluation.

The core strength of the paper lies in demonstrating the feasibility of emergent communication. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results showing successful coordination are positive. The exploration of environmental factors influencing semantic properties is also valuable. However, the leap from demonstrating coordination to claiming progress towards “machines that are able to communicate with humans productively” feels overstated. The grounding experiments, while a step in the right direction, rely on a relatively small-scale crowdsourcing study and the reported 68% accuracy, while statistically significant, doesn’t fully establish the practical utility of the learned language. The qualitative examples, while interesting, are anecdotal and don’t provide a comprehensive picture of the language’s interpretability.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be beneficial to include ablation studies to better understand the contribution of different components of the informed sender architecture. What specifically drives its improved performance compared to the agnostic sender?
*   **Vocabulary Size:** The choice of vocabulary size (10 vs. 100) seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of this parameter and its impact on language complexity and interpretability would be valuable.
*   **Grounding Evaluation:** The crowdsourcing experiment could be strengthened by including a control condition (e.g., human participants judging the relationship between images and randomly assigned words). A more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by human participants would also be insightful.
*   **Scalability:** While the authors acknowledge the limitations of scaling to more complex environments, a discussion of potential challenges and future directions in this regard would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters (embedding dimensionality, temperature, etc.) and whether any tuning was performed?
*   The paper mentions the possibility of adding humans into the game loop. Have you explored this, and if so, what were the results?
*   What metrics beyond purity were considered to evaluate the semantic properties of the emergent language?
*   How sensitive are the grounding results to the choice of the supervised dataset and the mapping between symbols and ImageNet labels?
