- Proposes a novel multi-agent referential game framework for emergent language learning between sender and receiver agents.  
- Shows simple neural agents can learn to coordinate and develop a language capturing conceptual object properties.  
- Introduces environment modifications and a supervised labeling method to encourage more natural, human-grounded communication.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Well-motivated approach highlighting limitations of supervised language learning and benefits of multi-agent communication.  
- Strong empirical evidence: experiments demonstrate language emergence, coordination, and interpretability.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and accessible.  
- Thorough related-work review connecting the approach to prior research.  
- Experimental results are convincing and clearly presented with helpful tables and figures.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and provide a clear roadmap for future work.  
- Limitation: current training on relatively simple referential games may not generalize to complex language tasks.  
- Requests for more detail on agent architectures and training procedures to enable replication.  
- Suggests more discussion of potential applications (e.g., human-computer interaction, child language learning).  
- Questions for authors: provide architecture/training details, plans for scaling to complex tasks, and envisioned real-world applications.
