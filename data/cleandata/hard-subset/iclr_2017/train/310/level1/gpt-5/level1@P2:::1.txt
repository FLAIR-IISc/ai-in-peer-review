Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a simple and clear framework for learning language through multi-agent interaction, instantiated as referential games over real images. Two neural agents (sender/receiver) learn, from scratch via REINFORCE, to communicate using a discrete vocabulary to identify a target among distractors. The work contributes: (i) evidence that agents with minimal architectures reliably converge to near-perfect coordination; (ii) analysis that the emergent symbols correlate with high-level semantic structure (e.g., category-level groupings) rather than low-level idiosyncrasies; (iii) an environment manipulation (“object-level reference” removing low-level common knowledge) that nudges the code toward more natural semantics; and (iv) a simple grounding mechanism that mixes self-play with supervised object naming, improving interpretability and enabling humans to use the induced “words” to select targets at above-chance rates (68%) on a different dataset. The paper is clearly written, timely, and generous in relating to prior work in emergent communication, game theory, and cognitive science.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: (1) The paper tackles an important question—how to bias emergent communication toward human-like semantics—and offers concrete, testable environment/learning design choices that produce measurable gains in interpretability. (2) The empirical package, while simple, is convincing: convergence in challenging settings (hundreds of real-world object categories), meaningful analyses of symbol semantics, and a human study showing grounding benefits.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem significance and clarity. The central question—designing environments and learning signals that produce portable, human-aligned communication—is clearly articulated and relevant to ICLR. The focus on referential games with natural images is a sensible first step toward scalable interactive learning.

2) Motivation and placement in the literature. The work is well situated with respect to multi-agent RL communication (Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), linguistic/game-theoretic perspectives (Lewis signaling, cheap talk), and prior interactive learning paradigms. The novelty relative to concurrent emergent communication work is the explicit focus on interpretability: (a) using inductive biases (informed sender) and environment changes (withheld low-level common knowledge) to encourage higher-level semantics; (b) combining self-play with supervised grounding; and (c) human evaluation. This is a meaningful and original angle for 2017.

3) Evidence and rigor. The results generally support the claims:
- Coordination: consistent near-perfect accuracy across architectures and vocab sizes on held-out games, with faster convergence for the informed sender.
- Semantics: above-chance purity of symbol-induced clusters w.r.t. McRae categories; visualization indicates local coherence in CNN space; interesting emergence of a living/non-living split with a 2-symbol code.
- Environment shaping: the object-level setup increases purity and better aligns symbols with higher-level semantics, matching the stated hypothesis.
- Grounding: mixing supervised ImageNet labels with self-play boosts symbol usage and purity, and yields human interpretability (68% correct vs 50% chance) on ReferIt-derived image regions with a significance test.

That said, some aspects weaken the rigor:
- Metrics: purity alone provides a partial view; the SVD analysis is suggestive but ad hoc. Additional statistics (e.g., NMI/ARI/V-measure, mutual information between symbols and categories/attributes, entropy of symbol usage, topographic similarity) would strengthen support for claims about semantic organization and synonymy/homonymy.
- Baselines/ablations: no comparisons to non-communicative or heuristic policies; limited analysis of alternative architectures (e.g., message length >1); no variance across random seeds. The strong claim about “nudging toward semantics” would benefit from tighter controls (e.g., same training budget, different feature layers, different vocab sizes) with confidence intervals.
- Human study details: the 68% result is promising but needs more methodological detail (participant screening, quality control, per-word/ per-category breakdown, confidence intervals). It would be useful to include a simple non-expert baseline (e.g., “always pick right image” or random word) and report effect sizes.
- Generalization: apart from the ReferIt test with human receivers, there is little analysis of out-of-distribution or zero-shot generalization of the agent-agent code.

Overall, the empirical evidence is sufficient for a weak accept given the conceptual contribution and breadth of experiments, but improving rigor and analysis depth would significantly strengthen the paper.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Report variability: multiple seeds for each configuration; convergence speed and sample efficiency (games to 90/95% accuracy) with error bars.
- Expand semantic analyses: mutual information between symbol and supercategory; symbol entropy; topographic similarity between distances in image space and message usage; confusion matrices for human study; per-category purity.
- Strengthen baselines: compare to a continuous-message baseline, entropy regularization to encourage larger vocabularies, and longer messages to probe compositionality.
- Clarify the “informed sender”: justify the 1-D conv inductive bias; test whether its advantage persists with different feature spaces (e.g., fc vs sm, or different CNNs) and temperatures; assess the claim that the agnostic architecture nests the informed one empirically.
- Grounding variants: test sequencing (pretrain supervised then self-play vs interleaving), proportion of supervised steps, and whether the receiver also benefits from weak language grounding.
- Human evaluation: provide CIs, exclusion criteria, demographics, and breakdowns (cases where the supervised word is absent vs present in the scene). Include qualitative failure cases to illuminate limits of metonymy.
- Reproducibility: release code, data splits, and trained models. Clean up reference formatting.

Questions for the authors
1) Robustness: How sensitive are results to random initialization, temperature τ, vocabulary size, and embedding dimension? Please report mean±std over multiple runs.
2) Purity and chance: How exactly is chance computed (permutation protocol)? Can you report CIs for purity and the obs−chance margin?
3) Symbol usage: Why does the agnostic sender collapse to 2 symbols? Did you try entropy bonuses or KL regularization to avoid degenerate codes?
4) Object-level setup: Are target and distractor always from different classes? How does performance/purity change if they share a supercategory?
5) Generalization: Does the learned code transfer to unseen object classes (zero-shot)? What happens when evaluating on new datasets without additional supervision?
6) Grounding: In the mixed objective, how often does the sender emit the supervised label when applicable, and how does this change across training? Does grounding improve agent-agent communication on ReferIt pairs (not just human interpretability)?
7) Human study: How were annotators filtered? What are per-word accuracies and CIs? Are successes concentrated in a small set of highly polysemous words?
8) Message length: Have you tried multi-symbol messages or interactive back-and-forth? Does compositional structure emerge under similar training budgets?

With these clarifications and strengthened analyses, the paper would make a strong and lasting contribution to emergent communication research.
