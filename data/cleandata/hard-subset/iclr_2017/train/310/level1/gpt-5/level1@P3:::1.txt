Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a simple yet compelling framework for studying emergent communication via multi-agent referential games with real images. Two neural agents (sender/receiver) learn to coordinate by exchanging a single discrete symbol to identify a target image among two candidates. The paper shows: (i) agents reliably learn to communicate from reward only using REINFORCE; (ii) the meaning of symbols shows alignment with high-level semantic structure (e.g., category-level regularities, living vs. non-living), improved by environmental tweaks that remove low-level “common knowledge” (different image instances per agent); and (iii) a simple hybrid training regime that mixes the game with supervised image labeling substantially increases semantic purity and yields partial human interpretability (68% accuracy in a crowdsourced proxy task on a different dataset). The work connects emergent communication to multimodal semantics with a focus on interpretability and human compatibility.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) Novel, timely problem framing with clear progress on emergent communication using naturalistic inputs and a human study; (2) Insightful environmental manipulations and a simple grounding strategy that demonstrably push the emergent code toward more “natural” semantics.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Well-motivated. Prior work showed learned communication in synthetic settings; here, the use of natural image features and explicit focus on interpretability and human usability is a meaningful advance.
- Methodology: The referential game is carefully designed to prevent trivial index coding (sender sees target first, receiver sees random order). The contrast between “agnostic” and “informed” sender highlights the effect of architectural inductive bias (dimension-wise comparison) on symbol use and semantic structure. The “object-level reference” manipulation appropriately tests the hypothesis that removing shared low-level details encourages category-level semantics.
- Evidence: Communication success is robust and fast. Semantic analyses include: (i) cluster purity of symbol-object associations vs. McRae categories, (ii) spectrum of symbol-by-pair usage (arguing against degenerate 1–2-d strategies), (iii) t-SNE visualization of symbol neighborhoods qualitatively aligned with visual similarity, and (iv) increased purity and symbol utilization under hybrid supervised/game training. The crowd evaluation on ReferIt-derived pairs is a notable contribution: despite minimal overlap with supervised labels, humans could often recover the sender’s intent (68%).
- Claims vs. evidence: The core claims (agents learn to communicate; environmental design and supervised grounding shape semantics; partial human interpretability) are supported. The work is exploratory but provides multiple converging analyses.

Main weaknesses
- “Tabula rasa” vs. pretrained vision: Agents are not blank slates; VGG features embody significant supervised prior aligned with ImageNet categories. Some semantic alignment may be inherited from the vision encoder. This should be acknowledged and controlled for (e.g., random features, frozen vs. finetuned, sm vs. fc comparisons reported more systematically).
- Semantics vs. pair-specific coding: Because the sender observes both images, symbols can be contrastive and ad hoc. While the object-level manipulation helps, stronger tests are needed: generalization to unseen distractors/targets, cross-pair consistency, or “listener trained with one sender tested with another” to probe conventionality.
- Evaluation rigor: Purity improvements and “significantly above chance” assertions lack detailed statistics (confidence intervals, randomization procedures). t-SNE is illustrative only. More quantitative measures (e.g., representational similarity analysis, topographic similarity between distances in vision space and symbol distributions, mutual information between symbols and categories) would strengthen claims.
- Architectural bias: The “informed sender” may predispose dimension-wise comparisons that correlate with VGG semantics; it is unclear whether better purity stems from bias or genuinely improved language formation. Additional baselines (MLP with similar capacity, different temperatures, entropy regularization) would help.
- Reproducibility/clarity: Inconsistencies (10k vs. 50k training iterations), lack of variance-reduction details for REINFORCE, and incomplete reporting of hyperparameters/sampling at test time. References are heavily garbled and should be fixed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Provide stronger generalization tests: (i) new target categories unseen during training; (ii) zero-shot distractor types; (iii) swap partners (sender A with receiver B) to assess conventionality and robustness.
- Quantify symbol usage: entropy over vocabulary; topographic similarity; stability of symbol-object mapping across random seeds and curricula; probing whether symbols capture attributes (color, animacy) vs. categories.
- Control for visual backbone: compare random features, shallower CNN features, or learned autoencoder features to estimate how much semantics comes from the encoder vs. communication pressure.
- Statistical reporting: include CIs and exact p-values for purity deltas and human study; detail randomization for chance baselines.
- Human evaluation: complement forced-choice with free-text rationales or graded relatedness; report inter-annotator agreement; analyze error patterns.
- Explore regularizers/curricula to promote conventionality (entropy constraints, KL to supervised label distributions, or partner-shuffling).
- Clarify training details (episodes, temperature annealing, test-time decoding: sample vs. argmax), and resolve iteration count inconsistencies.
- Fix and complete the bibliography.

Questions for the authors
1) How much does pretrained VGG drive semantic purity? What happens with random or early-layer features, or if you finetune the visual encoder end-to-end?
2) To what extent are symbol meanings conventional across agents? If you train multiple sender-receiver pairs independently, can a receiver from run A understand a sender from run B without joint finetuning?
3) Do symbols generalize across distractors? E.g., if a symbol is used for “dog” vs. many different distractors, does its usage remain consistent?
4) What is the exact procedure for “chance” purity: how many randomizations, and what are the variance/CI?
5) In the hybrid supervised setting, how often does the sender use a supervised label when neither image belongs to supervised categories? What drives the 68% human success—semantic relatedness, visual context, or dataset bias?
6) Can you report mutual information between symbols and (a) object categories, (b) attributes (animacy), and (c) visual cluster IDs?
7) Please clarify the discrepancy between “50k iterations” and “10k training games,” the role of temperature (and any annealing), and whether test-time messaging uses sampling or argmax.
