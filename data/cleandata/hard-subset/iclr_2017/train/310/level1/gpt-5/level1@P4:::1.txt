1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a simple but effective multi-agent framework for learning emergent communication via referential games with real images. Two neural agents (sender and receiver) learn from scratch to communicate using a discrete, arbitrary vocabulary to identify a target image among two candidates. The main claims and contributions are:
- Demonstration that tabula rasa agents trained with REINFORCE reliably and rapidly converge to successful communication in this setup.
- Analysis of how architectural biases (informed vs agnostic sender) and environmental design affect the emergent “word meanings,” showing that symbols align with higher-level semantic properties (e.g., categories, living vs non-living) rather than low-level visual correlates.
- A variant (“object-level reference”) that removes low-level common knowledge by showing different instances from the same class to sender and receiver, nudging the protocol toward more abstract semantics.
- A simple grounding strategy that interleaves the game with supervised object naming, substantially increasing symbol interpretability and enabling partial human-agent communication in a crowdsourcing study (humans identify the target 68% of the time using the agent’s symbol/label).

Overall, the work is well-motivated, clearly positioned relative to prior multi-agent communication and language emergence literature, and ties to cognitive/game-theoretic perspectives. It advances the field by using realistic visual inputs, analyzing semantic alignment, and providing an initial path toward human interpretability.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper tackles an important question (how to induce communicative agents whose symbols reflect useful, human-aligned semantics) with a simple, well-motivated framework and compelling empirical evidence.
- The combination of environmental design, architectural bias, and supervised grounding yields novel insights and measurable gains in semantic interpretability, including a human evaluation, which is rare in this line of work.

3. Supporting arguments
- Clarity: The referential game is described precisely. Architectures, training protocol (REINFORCE with discrete symbols), and dataset setup are clearly articulated. The motivation for informed vs agnostic sender and the “object-level reference” manipulation is well-explained and grounded in game theory (common knowledge).
- Technical soundness: The reinforcement learning approach (sampling a single discrete symbol; REINFORCE) is appropriate. The receiver’s scoring via dot products between symbol and image embeddings is a reasonable inductive bias. Convergence claims are consistent with the setup and priors in the literature. The use of pretrained VGG features is standard and reduces confounds from low-level perception.
- Novelty: While contemporaneous works learn communication protocols, this paper distinguishes itself by (a) using large, natural image sets; (b) quantifying semantic alignment with category purity and SVD analyses; (c) manipulating common knowledge to encourage abstraction; and (d) testing human interpretability after a simple grounding step. These are meaningful extensions toward practical, human-compatible communication.
- Empirical support: Multiple converging analyses (vocabulary usage, SVD spectrum, category purity above chance, t-SNE visualizations), plus the human experiment showing 68% success in a 2AFC setting, support the claim that the emergent code aligns with higher-level semantics and can be partially understood by humans post-grounding. The finding that a two-symbol code captures living vs non-living (a known basic semantic distinction) is particularly informative.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all are decision-critical)
- Report full quantitative results in the main text: exact communication accuracies, training curves, symbol usage counts, category purity with confidence intervals, and statistical significance for comparisons (e.g., informed vs agnostic, with/without object-level reference).
- Strengthen baselines: Compare against (i) a supervised classifier-only sender without interactive training (to isolate the benefit of interaction), (ii) a continuous communication channel, and (iii) random or hand-crafted symbol mappings. Where possible, include end-to-end variants (learn image encoders) to assess dependence on pretrained features.
- Generalization: Evaluate zero-shot generalization to unseen object classes and to out-of-distribution distractors/targets. Analyze whether symbols remain stable under changes in visual appearance (pose, background, occlusion).
- Quantify emergent semantics more rigorously: Beyond purity and t-SNE, consider metrics like adjusted mutual information between categories and symbols, clustering stability, and probing whether symbol usage correlates with semantic distances in WordNet or human feature norms.
- Communication bottleneck: Analyze sensitivity to vocabulary size, temperature τ, and sampling vs argmax at test time. Report variance across runs (REINFORCE can be high-variance).
- Interpretability and synonymy: The SVD analysis suggests multi-dimensionality; provide a clearer estimate of effective vocabulary size (e.g., entropy, type-token ratios, clustering symbols by usage). Explore whether “synonyms” are functionally redundant or context-specific.
- Human study details: Provide chance levels, interface, instructions, examples, and per-word/participant statistics. A control where the displayed label is replaced with a random label would help quantify absolute gains. Consider a calibration condition with conventional object names on in-domain images.
- Reproducibility: Include training details (learning rates, optimizers, baselines in REINFORCE, variance reduction), data splits ensuring no image leakage between train/test, and code release.

Questions for the authors
- Data splits: How are training/test games constructed to avoid leakage? Are the exact same images seen during training also present in testing, or only new combinations? Do you test on unseen object classes?
- Purity/statistics: What are the exact purity numbers (obs and chance) with confidence intervals and p-values? How consistent are they across random seeds?
- Vocabulary sensitivity: How do results vary with vocabulary sizes beyond 10 and 100? Do symbols collapse with very small K, and fragment with very large K?
- Temperature and sampling: How sensitive are convergence and interpretability to τ and to sampling vs greedy decoding at test time?
- Informed vs agnostic: Can you ablate the informed sender’s inductive bias (e.g., remove per-dimension comparisons) to pinpoint which components drive faster convergence and richer symbol use?
- Grounding transfer: In the ReferItGame transfer, how much of the 68% accuracy is attributable to true semantic links vs dataset biases? Do particular words dominate success? Please report per-word accuracies.
- Human evaluation baseline: What is the random-choice baseline (presumably 50%) and is 68% significantly above it for each symbol? How does performance vary with whether the supervised label is present vs absent in the scene?
- Robustness to visual encoders: Do results hold with different CNN feature layers or architectures (e.g., ResNet), and without pretraining (end-to-end finetuning)?
- Stability of the code: If agents are paired cross-run (sender from run A, receiver from run B), how interoperable are their languages? Does grounding via supervision improve cross-play?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically sound, and provides novel, well-motivated steps toward emergent, human-aligned communication in multi-agent settings.
