Summary of contributions
The paper studies how interactive, multi-agent communication can induce emergent language and how to nudge that language toward human-interpretable semantics. In a referential game with image pairs, simple sender/receiver networks learn to coordinate via a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary. The authors show: (i) tabula rasa agents reliably learn to communicate; (ii) an “informed” sender architecture converges faster and uses a richer, more semantically aligned code than an agnostic sender; (iii) modifying common knowledge (object-level reference) increases alignment with high-level semantics; and (iv) mixing self-play with supervised image labeling grounds symbols in natural language, improving interpretability, including a human evaluation showing 68% success in identifying the intended referent. These are clear, well-motivated steps toward interactive agents whose emergent protocols are more human-like.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Well-motivated problem and timely contribution connecting emergent communication with human interpretability/grounding; (2) Empirical evidence, including a human study, supports the core claims and offers actionable insights about environment design for semantics.

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: The paper targets when and how a communication protocol emerges and acquires human-aligned meaning in cooperative referential games—an important prerequisite for interactive AI.
- Motivation and placement: It thoughtfully situates the work in game theory (signaling/cheap talk), emergent communication, and deep RL (e.g., Foerster, Sukhbaatar), emphasizing interpretability and portability to human partners as the distinctive goal.
- Empirical support: Strong coordination is demonstrated; symbol usage correlates with semantic categories (purity above chance), increases under object-level reference, and improves substantially with supervision (higher purity, broader symbol usage). The crowdsourced study, while modest, directly tests human interpretability and yields significant above-chance performance.
- Methodological clarity: Architectures, training (REINFORCE), features (VGG), and evaluation protocols are described in enough detail to suggest reproducibility.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to my decision)
- Include stronger baselines (e.g., sender pretrained to output ImageNet labels only; non-interactive classifiers) and report variance across multiple seeds.
- Provide more rigorous statistics for purity and SVD analyses; add mutual information between symbols and categories; analyze synonymy/homonymy and stability across runs.
- Clarify train/test splits to avoid image leakage; analyze generalization to unseen categories; ablate temperature, vocabulary size, and supervision ratio.
- Discuss limits of one-symbol protocols and plans for compositional, multi-turn communication.

Questions for the authors
- How robust are results across random seeds and dataset resamplings? Please report mean ± std and failure rates.
- How exactly is purity computed (per-symbol clustering?) and is the improvement statistically significant?
- How was the 100-label supervised subset chosen, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- In the human study, were images balanced for side biases and annotator quality controlled? What is the per-word variance?
- Do independently trained agent pairs learn aligned codes, or are languages idiosyncratic without shared supervision?
