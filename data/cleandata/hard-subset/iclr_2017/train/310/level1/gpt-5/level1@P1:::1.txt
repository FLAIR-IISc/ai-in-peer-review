1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in multi-agent referential games with realistic visual inputs. Two neural agents (Sender and Receiver) are trained tabula rasa—with only a success/failure payoff—to coordinate on identifying a target image among two candidates. The Sender emits a single discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary; the Receiver guesses the target. The paper’s contributions are:
- Demonstrating successful emergence of communication (near-perfect coordination) with simple feed-forward agents trained with REINFORCE on large sets of ImageNet-derived images represented by VGG features.
- Comparing two Sender architectures: an “agnostic” sender with generic MLP composition vs. an “informed” sender with an inductive bias (per-dimension convolution across image embeddings). The informed sender converges faster and uses a richer symbol inventory.
- Providing evidence that emergent symbols correlate with intuitive semantic structure: symbol-object associations show above-chance purity with respect to 20 human categories and align with visual similarity in fc space; even with 2 symbols, agents discover a living vs. non-living split.
- Modifying the environment to remove low-level common knowledge (Sender/Receiver see different exemplars from the same class), which nudges symbols to reflect higher-level, class-level semantics (increased purity).
- Introducing a simple grounding strategy: interleaving self-play with supervised image labeling on a subset of categories. This increases symbol purity and yields directly interpretable symbols (exact match to supervised labels in 47% of applicable test pairs; chance 1%).
- Human evaluation: when playing a referential game on ReferIt-derived image patches with words learned during supervision, humans can identify the intended referent 68% of the time, indicating partial interpretability and interesting metonymic use of words.

2. Decision
Reject, primarily because:
- The technical depth and rigor are limited relative to ICLR standards: analyses rely on ad hoc metrics (purity, SVD spectrum, t-SNE visualization) without strong baselines, ablations, or principled tests of generalization/compositionality.
- Key experimental claims (emergence of semantics and human interpretability) are only partially supported; important controls and baselines are missing (e.g., comparing to purely visual clustering, degenerate strategies, cross-play).

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper asks how to design interactive environments where agents develop languages that are both effective for coordination and aligned with human semantics. This is timely and important.

Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature? Yes, the motivation to move beyond passive supervised learning is clear. The work is positioned against multi-agent communication (Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), emergent language/game-theoretic traditions, and human-in-the-loop approaches. The novelty is not in the task per se, but in combining realistic visual inputs, environmental tweaks to shape semantics, and grounding via supervised labeling with a small human evaluation.

Do the results support the claims with rigor? Partially.
- Communication success is clear. However, RL details are sparse (e.g., variance reduction/baselines, entropy bonuses, temperature annealing), and training stability across seeds is not reported.
- Semantic alignment: purity relative to McRae categories is suggestive, but no baseline is provided showing how much of this signal is already in the fc/sm features (e.g., cluster purity from K-means on visual features). The SVD spectrum and t-SNE plots are non-quantitative. “Living vs. non-living” with two symbols is intriguing but not deeply analyzed.
- The manipulation to remove low-level common knowledge is a strong idea; still, the reported purity gains are described as “small,” with no statistical testing or effect size.
- Grounding: supervised interleaving increases purity and yields some interpretability. Yet controls are missing (e.g., replacing labels with random tokens; matching performance to a simple nearest-neighbor classifier that selects a label based on visual similarity).
- Human study: 68% success is promising, but lacks baselines (random symbol-to-label mapping, human-only baseline, comparison to a visual-similarity heuristic). The sample selection (only successful Sender–Receiver trials, three pairs per symbol) may bias results.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the acceptance decision)
- Provide principled quantitative analyses:
  - Include baselines for purity: (i) random symbol assignment, (ii) clustering on VGG features (K-means/ spectral clustering) with the same number of clusters/symbols, (iii) supervised linear classifier on fc for categories to show the semantic signal in the features. Report effect sizes and confidence intervals.
  - Measure topographic similarity: correlation between distances in symbol space (e.g., KL divergence of symbol distributions per object) and distances in visual space or in semantic category space.
  - Cross-play: pair senders and receivers trained independently. If cross-play fails, the language may be idiosyncratic; if it succeeds, it indicates shared conventions.
  - Generalization: zero-shot to unseen object classes; increased number of distractors; robustness to perturbations (masking, noise).
  - Compositionality/systematicity: test whether symbols recombine productively when visual scenes contain multiple objects or attributes (even simple color/shape controls).
- RL methodology: detail REINFORCE implementation (baselines/variance reduction, entropy regularization, temperature schedule), number of seeds, variance across runs, sample complexity (are 50k iterations with batch 32 equal to 1.6M games?).
- Architecture: analyze why the informed sender helps (ablation on convolutional kernel, dimension-wise interactions, capacity-matched MLP). Report vocabulary usage entropy and mutual information between symbols and categories.
- Environment design: your “remove common knowledge” manipulation is compelling. Consider stronger manipulations (different viewpoints, occlusions, style transfer) and report how semantics shifts.
- Grounding: compare interleaved supervision against pretraining then self-play and vice versa; test whether grounded symbols retain their conventional denotations in out-of-domain images. Add baselines like nearest-neighbor label assignment. Report which words are used metonymically and when this helps or hurts.
- Human study: add controls (random label assignment; visual similarity heuristic; human-human ceiling) and report per-word and per-item accuracies.

Questions for the authors
- How many random seeds did you run for each condition? What is the variance in coordination accuracy, purity, and symbol usage?
- Do independently trained agents cross-play successfully? If not, can simple alignment (e.g., receiver fine-tuning) recover it?
- What RL variance-reduction techniques did you use (baseline networks, moving average, entropy bonus)? Was the temperature τ fixed or annealed?
- How exactly is purity computed (majority symbol per object across all contexts)? How sensitive is it to class imbalance and symbol frequency?
- What is the baseline purity when clustering the fc vectors directly (same number of clusters as effective symbols)?
- In the “different exemplars per class” setting, how do you ensure no leakage via low-level correlations (e.g., background biases)?
- In the human study, what happens if you replace the learned word with a nearest-neighbor ImageNet label based on visual similarity to the target patch? How does your 68% compare?
- Does supervised grounding persist if the supervised categories are disjoint from the categories encountered in self-play (fully disjoint vocabularies)?
- How stable is vocabulary size and symbol usage across seeds and over training? Do you observe synonymy/homonymy dynamics?

Overall, the paper is thought-provoking and makes a promising step toward emergent, human-aligned communication. With stronger baselines, analyses, and generalization tests, it could become a solid contribution.
