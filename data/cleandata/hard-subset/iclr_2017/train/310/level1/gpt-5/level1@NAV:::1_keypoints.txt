- Paper studies emergent language in a referential image-pair game and how to nudge it toward human-interpretable semantics.
- Tabula rasa sender/receiver agents reliably learn to communicate.
- An “informed” sender converges faster and produces a richer, more semantically aligned code than an agnostic sender.
- Making common knowledge object-level increases alignment with high-level semantics.
- Mixing self-play with supervised image labeling grounds symbols in natural language and improves interpretability (human eval: 68% success).
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason: Well-motivated connection between emergent communication and human interpretability/grounding.
- Key reason: Empirical evidence, including a human study, supports the core claims and provides actionable environment-design insights.
- Problem clarity: Targets when/how protocols emerge and acquire human-aligned meaning in cooperative referential games.
- Motivation/placement: Properly situated in signaling/game theory, emergent communication, and deep RL, emphasizing portability to human partners.
- Empirical support: Strong coordination, symbol usage correlates with semantic categories (purity > chance), improves with object-level reference and supervision.
- Empirical support: Crowdsourced human study, though modest, shows above-chance interpretability.
- Methodological clarity: Architectures, REINFORCE training, VGG features, and evaluation protocols described sufficiently for reproducibility.
- Feedback: Add stronger baselines (e.g., sender pretrained on ImageNet labels) and report variance across multiple seeds.
- Feedback: Provide more rigorous statistics for purity and SVD; add mutual information and analyze synonymy/homonymy and stability across runs.
- Feedback: Clarify train/test splits to avoid image leakage; evaluate generalization to unseen categories; ablate temperature, vocabulary size, and supervision ratio.
- Feedback: Discuss limits of one-symbol protocols and plans for compositional, multi-turn communication.
- Question: How robust are results across random seeds and dataset resamplings—report mean ± std and failure rates?
- Question: How exactly is purity computed and are improvements statistically significant?
- Question: How was the 100-label supervised subset chosen and how sensitive are results to that choice?
- Question: In the human study, were images balanced for side biases and was annotator quality controlled; what is per-word variance?
- Question: Do independently trained agent pairs learn aligned codes, or are languages idiosyncratic without shared supervision?
