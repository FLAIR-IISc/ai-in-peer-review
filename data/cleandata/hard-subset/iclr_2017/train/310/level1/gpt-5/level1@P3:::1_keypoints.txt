- Paper proposes a referential game framework with two neural agents exchanging a single discrete symbol to identify a real-image target among two candidates.  
- Main findings: agents learn to communicate via REINFORCE, symbols align with high-level semantic structure, removing shared low-level inputs promotes category-level semantics, and hybrid supervised/game training raises semantic purity and partial human interpretability (68%).  
- Decision: Weak Accept, justified by novel, timely framing and clear progress using naturalistic inputs plus a human study.  
- Key strengths: meaningful advance over synthetic settings by focusing on interpretability and human usability with natural image features.  
- Game design prevents trivial index coding and contrasts “agnostic” vs “informed” sender to show architectural inductive bias effects.  
- Object-level reference manipulation effectively tests whether removing shared low-level details yields category-level semantics.  
- Evidence is converging: robust/fast communication and analyses including cluster purity, symbol usage spectrum, t-SNE visualizations, and improved purity under hybrid training.  
- Human evaluation: crowdsourced proxy on ReferIt-derived pairs shows 68% recovery of sender intent despite label mismatch.  
- Core claims (communication learning, environment/supervision shaping semantics, partial human interpretability) are supported by multiple analyses.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on pretrained VGG means semantic alignment may derive from the vision encoder rather than communication pressure.  
- Weakness: sender sees both images, enabling contrastive/pair-specific coding; stronger tests for conventionality and generalization are needed.  
- Weakness: statistical rigor is limited—purity improvements and “above chance” claims lack confidence intervals and detailed significance testing.  
- Weakness: t-SNE is only illustrative; more quantitative measures (RSA, mutual information, topographic similarity) are needed.  
- Weakness: “informed sender” architectural bias may induce semantics correlated with VGG features; additional baselines are necessary.  
- Weakness: reproducibility/clarity issues (inconsistent iteration counts, missing REINFORCE variance-reduction details, incomplete hyperparameter reporting, garbled references).  
- Suggested experiments: test generalization to unseen target categories, zero-shot distractors, and partner-swapping to assess conventionality.  
- Suggested analyses: quantify symbol usage (vocabulary entropy, stability across seeds, topographic similarity, attribute vs category capture).  
- Suggested controls: compare random features, shallow CNNs, or autoencoder features and test end-to-end finetuning to isolate encoder effects.  
- Suggested reporting improvements: include confidence intervals, exact p-values, and randomization procedures for chance baselines.  
- Suggested human-eval improvements: add free-text rationales, graded relatedness, inter-annotator agreement, and error-pattern analyses.  
- Suggested training methods: explore regularizers and curricula (entropy constraints, KL to labels, partner-shuffling) to promote conventionality.  
- Suggested clarifications: report episodes, temperature/annealing, test-time decoding (sample vs argmax), and resolve iteration-count inconsistencies.  
- Requested clarifications/questions: effect of pretrained VGG vs random/early-layer features or finetuning; conventionality across independently trained agent pairs; symbol generalization across diverse distractors; procedure and variance for “chance” purity; behavior of sender in hybrid setting when images lack supervised labels and what drives the 68% human success; mutual information between symbols and categories/attributes/visual clusters; and exact training/test hyperparameter details.
