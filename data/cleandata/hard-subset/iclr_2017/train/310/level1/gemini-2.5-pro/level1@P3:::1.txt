### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes and investigates a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication, moving away from the dominant paradigm of passive learning from static text corpora. The authors tackle the fundamental problem of how a communication protocol can emerge between two agents who need to cooperate to succeed at a task. They use a simple yet effective referential game where a "sender" agent must communicate to a "receiver" agent which of two images is the target. Starting as blank slates, the agents, implemented as simple neural networks and trained with reinforcement learning, successfully develop a shared, discrete communication protocol to solve the task.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that successful communication can emerge from cooperative interaction alone. Second, it investigates how properties of the environment influence the semantics of the emergent language. The authors show that specific inductive biases in the agent architecture (the "informed" sender) and modifications to the game's common knowledge (showing agents different instances of the same object class) can guide the emergent protocol towards more abstract, categorical representations. Finally, and most significantly, the paper presents a novel method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, the authors show that the sender's protocol becomes more semantically pure and, crucially, partially interpretable by humans, even when generalizing to new types of images. The human evaluation, showing that people can interpret the agent's "metonymic" word usage, is a particularly compelling result that points towards a viable path for building communicative AI.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces an elegant and powerful framework for studying the emergence of communication, a problem of fundamental importance to AI. The methodology is simple, well-motivated, and produces clear, interpretable results.
2.  The work goes beyond merely demonstrating that communication can emerge. Its core strength lies in showing how the emergent protocol can be shaped and grounded. The final experiment, which combines interactive learning with supervised grounding and validates the result with human participants, is a novel and significant contribution that opens up a promising research direction.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, positioning itself clearly against the limitations of purely supervised approaches to language learning for interactive agents. It draws on a rich history from cognitive science, linguistics, and game theory, but uses modern deep reinforcement learning techniques to operationalize these ideas in a scalable way with complex, real-world inputs (natural images).

The experimental design is thoughtful and progresses logically. The initial experiments convincingly establish that tabula rasa agents can learn to communicate. The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" senders, while architecturally simple, provides a clear demonstration of how inductive biases can affect the nature of the learned protocol. The analysis of the emergent language using purity scores and SVD is appropriate and supports the claim that the protocol has semantic structure.

The most innovative part of the paper begins with the "object-level reference" experiment. The insight to remove low-level visual features from the agents' common knowledge by showing them different instances of the same class is a clever and effective way to force coordination on more abstract properties. This is a strong piece of evidence for the central claim that the environment's structure shapes the emergent language.

Finally, the grounding experiment is the paper's main strength. The idea of using a secondary supervised task to align the agent's emergent symbols with human language, inspired by AlphaGo's hybrid training, is highly effective. The quantitative results (dramatic increase in purity) and qualitative results (successful human interpretation on a generalization task) are very strong. The finding that agents learn to make metonymic connections (e.g., using "dolphin" for an image of the sea) is fascinating and suggests that this framework can capture some of the flexibility inherent in human language use. This provides a compelling answer to the crucial question of how an artificial communication system can be made understandable to humans without requiring constant, intensive human supervision.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.

*   **Motivation for Informed Sender:** While the "informed" sender architecture is shown to be more effective, the motivation for its specific design (1D convolutions across image embedding dimensions) could be elaborated upon. Is this meant to approximate a specific cognitive process, or is it primarily an engineering choice that was found to work well? A deeper justification for this particular inductive bias would strengthen the paper.
*   **RL Training Details:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is known for high variance. The learning curves in Figure 2 appear remarkably smooth. It would be beneficial to include details on whether any variance reduction techniques (e.g., baselines) were used. Furthermore, reporting results averaged over multiple random seeds with variance measures would increase confidence in the stability of the findings.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors state that hyperparameters were set without tuning. While this speaks to the robustness of the approach, a brief analysis of the sensitivity to key parameters, especially the Gibbs temperature `τ`, would be valuable. A temperature of 10 is quite high and encourages exploration; understanding how this choice affects the speed of convergence and the structure of the final language would be enlightening.
*   **Human Evaluation Details:** The human evaluation is a key result. To improve rigor, more details about the experimental setup could be provided. For example, what were the exact instructions given to the crowd workers? Were they given any examples or training? The chance baseline is 50%, so 68% is statistically significant, but understanding the context of the task more fully would help in interpreting this result.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   Could you comment on the different equilibria observed in your experiments? For instance, the agnostic sender seems to converge to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. Does this equilibrium appear consistently? Did you observe other qualitatively different but stable communication systems emerging across different runs?
*   The current communication protocol is a single symbol. How do you envision this framework extending to more complex, compositional language involving sequences of symbols? Would the current RL setup be sufficient, or would it require architectural changes (e.g., recurrent networks) and a modified reward structure?
*   In the grounding experiment, the sender is trained on a supervised task. Have you considered also providing some form of supervision to the receiver? It seems plausible that aligning the receiver's "understanding" of symbols with human language could further improve the emergence of a shared, grounded communication system.
