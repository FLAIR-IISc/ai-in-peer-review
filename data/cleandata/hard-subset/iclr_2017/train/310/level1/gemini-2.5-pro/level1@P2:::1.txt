### Review for ICLR_2017

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes and investigates a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication, positioning it as an alternative to the dominant paradigm of passive learning from large text corpora. The authors use a referential game, a variant of the Lewis signaling game, where two neural network agents—a sender and a receiver—must develop a communication protocol from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a pair.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that simple feed-forward neural agents can successfully learn to coordinate and communicate in this visual reference game, achieving near-perfect accuracy. Second, it explores how changes in the game's environment can shape the emergent language. Specifically, by removing low-level visual features as "common knowledge" (by showing agents different instances of the same object class), the authors show they can encourage the agents to develop a more abstract, semantic communication protocol. Finally, and most significantly, the paper presents a novel strategy to ground the agents' emergent language in human language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, the sender learns to use symbols that correspond to human-understandable words. The effectiveness of this grounding is compellingly demonstrated in a final experiment where human subjects, acting as the receiver, can correctly interpret the agent's messages with high accuracy, even in novel situations that require semantic generalization.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Strong Methodological Contribution and Experimental Design:** The paper addresses the fundamental problem of how communication can emerge and be grounded through interaction. The proposed framework is simple, elegant, and the experimental progression is exceptionally clear and logical. Each experiment builds upon the last, systematically answering key questions from basic coordination to human-level interpretability.
2.  **Compelling Human-in-the-Loop Validation:** The final experiment, where human participants successfully interpret the agent-generated language, provides powerful evidence for the paper's central claims. This moves the evaluation beyond internal metrics (like accuracy or cluster purity) to a direct, functional test of whether the learned communication is meaningful to humans, which is a crucial step for the field.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core strength lies in its clear motivation and rigorous, step-by-step investigation. The authors correctly identify a limitation in standard supervised approaches to language learning—the lack of grounding in interactive, goal-driven contexts—and propose a viable research program to address it.

The initial experiments convincingly show that agents can learn to communicate. The architectural comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender is insightful, suggesting that providing an inductive bias for feature-wise comparison accelerates learning and leads to a richer communication protocol. The use of purity scores against semantic categories and t-SNE visualizations provides good initial evidence that the emergent protocol is not arbitrary but captures semantic properties of the visual world.

The "object-level reference" experiment is a particularly clever piece of experimental design. By forcing the agents to coordinate based on abstract class properties rather than specific pixel-level details, the authors provide strong evidence that the environment's design directly influences the nature of the emergent language. This is a crucial insight for designing future multi-agent learning environments.

However, the most impactful contribution is the grounding experiment. The idea to combine interactive self-play with supervised learning, inspired by AlphaGo, is both novel in this context and highly effective. The results are impressive: not only does the agent's language become more semantically structured (as measured by a dramatic increase in purity), but it also becomes directly interpretable. The final validation with human subjects is the paper's capstone. Showing that humans can achieve 68% accuracy in interpreting the agent's messages, often by understanding "metonymic" or associative links (e.g., "dolphin" for an image of the sea), is a significant and non-obvious result. It suggests that this method does not just teach agents to parrot labels but provides a foundation for flexible, generalizable communication. This finding strongly supports the claim that the proposed framework is a promising path toward developing machines that can communicate productively with humans.

#### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Training Dynamics and Stability:** The REINFORCE algorithm is known for its high variance. The learning curves in Figure 2 appear quite smooth. Were these results averaged over multiple runs with different random seeds? Including error bars or reporting standard deviations across runs would strengthen the claims about convergence speed and final performance.
*   **Analysis of "Informed" Sender:** The "informed" sender performs better, which is attributed to its inductive bias. A more detailed analysis could be beneficial. For example, does this architecture simply learn to perform a subtraction between the image vectors and encode the difference? Visualizing the learned filters or further probing the internal representations could provide more insight into why this specific architecture is effective.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The temperature parameter for the Gibbs sampling is set to a high value (τ=10), which would make the policy nearly uniform at the start of training. Could you comment on the motivation for this choice and its sensitivity? A discussion of how this and other hyperparameters (e.g., vocabulary size, embedding dimensionality) affect the emergent language would be valuable.
*   **Quantifying "Metonymy":** The qualitative examples of metonymy are fascinating. This observation could be strengthened with a quantitative analysis. For instance, using pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe), could you measure the cosine similarity between the word the sender chose (e.g., "dolphin") and the labels of objects actually present in the image (e.g., "water," "sea")? This would provide quantitative evidence for the semantic generalization you observed.

#### Questions for the Authors:

1.  Could you clarify the training stability by providing results from multiple random seeds? How consistent were the emergent protocols and performance metrics across different runs?
2.  The emergent communication in the non-grounded experiments is analyzed for semantic structure via purity. Did you attempt to "translate" this language? For example, by mapping the most frequently used symbol for "cat" images to the word "cat" and testing this mapping on a held-out set? This could provide another measure of semantic consistency.
3.  In the final human evaluation, you show that the agent's language generalizes beyond direct denotation. Do you have a hypothesis for why the shared supervised objective fosters this type of flexible, associative reasoning rather than a more rigid use of labels? Does the interactive pressure of the game force the agent to find the "next best word" when a direct label is unavailable?
