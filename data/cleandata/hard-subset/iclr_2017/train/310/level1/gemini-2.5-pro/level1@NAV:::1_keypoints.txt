- Paper introduces a multi-agent referential game framework to study emergent language and its grounding.
- Contribution 1: Simple neural agents can learn to coordinate and develop a communication protocol from scratch.
- Contribution 2: Modifying game environment (e.g., removing low-level common knowledge) pushes emergent language toward more abstract, human-like semantics.
- Contribution 3: Novel grounding method combining interactive game-playing with supervised image-labeling yields a code interpretable by humans on novel tasks.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reasons: important research direction and strong final experimental result demonstrating interpretability and human-agent alignment.
- Crowdsourced human study shows humans can interpret agents’ emergent “metonymic” language, supporting central claims.
- Paper is well-motivated, advocating interactive, function-driven language learning over passive corpus-based learning.
- Experimental progression is logical: baseline communication → semantic analysis → grounding method.
- Results are clear, consistent, and supported by analyses using vocabulary usage and cluster purity metrics.
- Human evaluation provides robust validation of the grounding strategy.
- Improvement suggestion: include a detailed qualitative analysis of human-evaluation failure cases to understand overly abstract metonymic leaps.
- Improvement suggestion: better explain why the informed sender’s convolutional architecture promotes a richer vocabulary.
- Question 1: Can the authors provide direct analysis linking the agnostic sender’s 2-symbol language to specific object categories (e.g., living vs. non-living)?
- Question 2: In hybrid supervised/interactive training, how does performance change with the supervised-to-game-playing update ratio, and could excess supervision reduce communicative flexibility?
