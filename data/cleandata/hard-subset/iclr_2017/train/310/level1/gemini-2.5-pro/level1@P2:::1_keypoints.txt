- Proposes a multi-agent, interactive framework for language learning as an alternative to passive text-based learning.
- Uses a referential (Lewis) signaling game with sender and receiver neural agents to develop communication from scratch.
- Shows simple feed-forward agents can learn to coordinate and achieve near-perfect task accuracy.
- Demonstrates environment design (e.g., showing different object instances) shapes emergent language toward more abstract, semantic protocols.
- Introduces grounding of emergent symbols via a combined interactive game plus supervised image-labeling task.
- Empirically grounds language so humans can interpret agent messages, validated by human subjects.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: strong methodological contribution with clear, logical experimental progression from coordination to interpretability.
- Key reason 2: compelling human-in-the-loop validation that moves evaluation beyond internal metrics to functional human interpretability.
- Supports claim that supervised grounding increases semantic structure (higher purity) and interpretability of symbols.
- Architectural comparison: "informed" sender with inductive bias learns faster and produces richer protocols than "agnostic" sender.
- Clever object-level reference experiment shows forcing abstraction induces class-level semantics in communication.
- Grounding experiment (inspired by AlphaGo) is novel and effective; humans achieve ~68% accuracy and show metonymic interpretations.
- Requests stronger reporting on training dynamics: report variance across multiple random seeds and include error bars or stddevs.
- Suggests deeper analysis of the "informed" sender’s internal representations to explain why it outperforms (e.g., visualize filters).
- Asks for hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (e.g., high temperature τ=10, vocabulary size, embedding dims) and motivation for choices.
- Recommends quantitative analysis of "metonymy" using word-embedding similarity between chosen symbols and image-related labels.
- Questions for authors: provide results across random seeds and consistency of emergent protocols.
- Questions for authors: attempt explicit translation mappings (symbol→word) and test on held-out sets for semantic consistency.
- Questions for authors: hypothesize why the supervised+interactive objective fosters flexible, associative (metonymic) use of words rather than rigid labeling.
