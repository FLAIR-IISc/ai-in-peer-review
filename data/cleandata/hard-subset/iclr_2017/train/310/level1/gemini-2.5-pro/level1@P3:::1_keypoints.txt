- Proposes a multi-agent communication framework where two agents learn to communicate via a referential game rather than passive text learning.
- Shows that tabula rasa neural agents trained with reinforcement learning can develop a discrete shared communication protocol to solve the task.
- Demonstrates that successful communication can emerge purely from cooperative interaction.
- Finds that environmental and architectural factors shape the semantics of the emergent language.
- Introduces an "informed" sender architecture (1D convolutions across embedding dimensions) that biases the protocol toward more abstract representations.
- Shows that changing common knowledge (showing different instances of the same class) encourages object-level, categorical reference rather than low-level visual features.
- Presents a novel grounding method combining interactive learning with a supervised image-labeling task to align emergent symbols with natural language.
- Reports large increases in semantic purity and partial human interpretability of the emergent protocol after grounding.
- Human evaluation indicates people can interpret agents' metonymic word usage and generalize to new image types.
- Positions the work against purely supervised language learning and grounds it in cognitive science, linguistics, and game theory.
- Uses purity scores and SVD analyses to support claims about the semantic structure of emergent symbols.
- The object-level reference experiment is a clever manipulation to force abstraction in coordination.
- Grounding approach is inspired by hybrid training (e.g., AlphaGo) and is effective in making agent symbols more human-aligned.
- Decision: Accept — motivated by elegant framework, clear methodology, and significant grounding experiment validated with humans.
- Request to better justify the specific design choice behind the informed sender's architecture (cognitive vs engineering rationale).
- Ask for more RL training details (variance reduction techniques, baselines) and reporting across multiple random seeds with variance measures.
- Suggest brief sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters, especially the high Gibbs temperature (τ=10).
- Recommend more details on the human evaluation setup (instructions, training of crowd workers) to interpret the 68% success rate.
- Questions: consistency of different equilibria (e.g., living vs non-living), extension to compositional/sequential language and necessary architectural changes, and whether supervising the receiver could improve grounding.
