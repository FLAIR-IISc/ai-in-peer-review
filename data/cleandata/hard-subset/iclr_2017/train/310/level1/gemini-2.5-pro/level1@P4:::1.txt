### 1. Summary

This paper introduces a framework for studying language emergence through multi-agent communication. The authors propose a "referential game" where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must coordinate to identify a target image from a pair. The sender transmits a symbol from an arbitrary vocabulary, and both agents are rewarded only if the receiver correctly identifies the target. The paper makes three primary contributions. First, it demonstrates that simple feed-forward neural networks, trained with reinforcement learning from scratch, can successfully learn to communicate and coordinate in this game. Second, it investigates how environmental and architectural factors influence the semantics of the emergent communication protocol. The authors show that specific architectural inductive biases (an "informed" sender) and modifications to the game's common knowledge (forcing reference at the object-class level) lead to a more semantically structured and complex language. Third, the paper proposes a novel method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. A human study confirms that this grounding generalizes, enabling humans to interpret the agents' messages even for objects not seen during the supervised phase.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated paradigm for language learning that emphasizes the functional and interactive aspects of communication, providing a valuable alternative to purely passive, supervised approaches. The investigation into shaping and grounding the emergent language is a significant step beyond simply demonstrating that communication can arise.
2.  **Methodological Rigor and Strong Empirical Support:** The claims are substantiated by a series of well-designed, incremental experiments. The results are clear and convincingly demonstrate the feasibility of the proposed framework, from the initial emergence of communication to the successful grounding in natural language, validated through human evaluation.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-placed within the literature, drawing connections to multi-agent systems, cognitive science, and game theory to motivate the departure from standard supervised learning paradigms for language. The core argument—that interactive communication is essential for learning the functional aspects of language—is persuasively made.

The technical execution is sound. The use of a simple referential game provides a clean and controllable environment to study the core research questions. The agent architectures are straightforward, and the REINFORCE algorithm is an appropriate choice for training. The experimental progression is logical and effective:
1.  **Emergence of Communication:** The initial experiments clearly show that agents successfully converge to near-perfect coordination. The analysis goes beyond simple success rate, using purity scores to measure semantic coherence and SVD to analyze the complexity of the vocabulary, providing evidence that the agents learn a non-trivial communication system.
2.  **Shaping the Language:** The introduction of the "informed sender" and the "object-level reference" game are clever manipulations that demonstrate how inductive biases and the game environment can guide the emergent language toward properties that are more aligned with human semantics (i.e., higher-level, conceptual properties). These experiments provide strong support for the claim that the environment's design is crucial.
3.  **Grounding in Human Language:** This is arguably the most impactful contribution of the paper. The strategy of mixing interactive self-play with a supervised classification task is both simple and highly effective, as shown by the dramatic increase in semantic purity. The final experiment, testing human interpretability on an out-of-domain dataset (ReferItGame), is a rigorous and convincing validation. The finding that humans can correctly interpret the agents' "metonymic" use of words (e.g., 'dolphin' for 'sea') is a powerful result, suggesting that this approach can bootstrap a flexible, human-compatible communication system.

The paper is also exceptionally clear. The motivation, methods, and results are presented in a coherent and easy-to-follow narrative. The figures and tables are informative and effectively support the paper's claims.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

**Feedback:**
*   The temperature parameter for the Gibbs sampling is set to 10, which seems quite high and would result in a nearly uniform policy at the beginning of training. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be beneficial. For example, does a lower temperature accelerate convergence at the cost of vocabulary diversity?
*   The intuition behind the 1-D convolutions in the "informed sender" could be elaborated upon. While the text states it creates a bias for dimension-by-dimension comparison, a more detailed explanation of why this is a desirable bias for this task would strengthen the architectural motivation.
*   The supervised grounding experiment shows a dramatic improvement in performance. It would be interesting to see an ablation study on the amount of supervision. How does semantic purity scale with the number of supervised labels or the proportion of supervised training steps? This could provide valuable insights into the sample efficiency of the grounding process.

**Questions for the Authors:**
*   In the grounding experiment (Section 5), the game-embedding layers are shared between the game and the classification task. Have you explored alternative weight-sharing strategies? For instance, what happens if only the initial image feature extractors are shared, but the final layers that produce the vocabulary symbols are separate for each task?
*   The human evaluation is well-designed for a quantitative assessment. Did you consider a more qualitative evaluation? For example, asking subjects to guess the meaning of a symbol given several examples of its usage might provide deeper insight into the semantics being learned and the nature of the "metonymic" generalizations you observed.
*   The current framework is purely cooperative. How do you envision this model behaving in a mixed-motive game where agents might not have perfectly aligned goals? Do you hypothesize that a different, perhaps less precise or even deceptive, form of language would emerge?
