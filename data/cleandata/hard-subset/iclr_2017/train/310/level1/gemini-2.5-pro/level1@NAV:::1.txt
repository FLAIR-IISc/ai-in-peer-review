This paper presents a compelling framework for studying the emergence of language through multi-agent communication in referential games. The authors make three key contributions. First, they demonstrate that simple neural agents can successfully learn to coordinate and develop a communication protocol from scratch. Second, they thoughtfully explore how modifications to the game environment, such as removing low-level common knowledge, can nudge the emergent language towards possessing more abstract, human-like semantic properties. Finally, and most significantly, they introduce a novel method for grounding the agents' language in natural language by combining interactive game-playing with a supervised image-labeling task, showing that this results in a code that is interpretable by humans even on novel tasks.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for this decision are the paper's important research direction and the strength of its final experimental result. The work moves beyond merely achieving successful communication to tackling the more challenging and critical problem of interpretability and human-agent alignment. The final experiment, which demonstrates through a crowdsourced study that humans can successfully interpret the agent's emergent "metonymic" language, provides strong and novel evidence for the paper's central claims and is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is well-motivated, arguing persuasively for the need for interactive, function-driven language learning over passive learning from static corpora. The experimental progression is logical and builds a clear narrative, starting with establishing baseline communication, analyzing its semantic properties, and culminating in a method for grounding it. The results are clear and consistently support the authors' hypotheses. The analysis of emergent properties using metrics like vocabulary usage and cluster purity is sound, and the final human evaluation provides a robust validation of the grounding strategy.

For improvement, I would suggest the following:
*   The paper could be strengthened with a more detailed qualitative analysis of the failure cases in the human evaluation. Understanding when the agent's "metonymic" leaps were too abstract for humans would provide deeper insight.
*   While the "informed sender" architecture is shown to be superior, the intuition behind why its convolutional structure promotes a richer vocabulary could be elaborated upon for greater clarity.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  You suggest the 2-symbol language of the agnostic sender maps to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. Could you provide a more direct analysis of the object categories associated with each symbol to substantiate this intriguing claim?
2.  In the hybrid supervised/interactive training, how does performance vary with the ratio of supervised to game-playing updates? Is there a risk that too much supervision could stifle the communicative flexibility learned through interaction?
