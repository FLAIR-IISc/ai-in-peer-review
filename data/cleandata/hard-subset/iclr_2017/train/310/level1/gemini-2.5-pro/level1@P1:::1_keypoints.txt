- Introduces a referential game framework where sender and receiver agents develop a communication protocol from scratch to identify a target image from a pair.
- Shows simple feed-forward neural networks can learn to coordinate and develop an effective, discrete communication protocol.
- Emergent language exhibits semantic structure aligned with high-level conceptual properties (e.g., object categories), not just low-level visual features.
- Demonstrates that modifying the game environment (e.g., removing low-level common knowledge) can steer the emergent semantics toward more abstract, class-level properties.
- Combining interactive gameplay with supervised image-labeling grounds the agents' language in human language, producing a protocol partly interpretable by humans.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason 1 for acceptance: novel and important problem formulation shifting from passive supervised learning to active, goal-driven interactive communication.
- Reason 2 for acceptance: rigorous and convincing experimental support with quantitative analyses and human evaluation.
- Paper situates the work well within AI, cognitive science, and game theory literature and motivates the shift from supervised to interactive learning.
- The referential game is a concrete deep-learning instantiation of a Lewis signaling game.
- Experimental progression is logical: establish communication emergence, analyze semantic properties, manipulate environment to shape semantics, and ground in human language.
- Agents reach near-perfect coordination; comparison of "agnostic" vs "informed" sender shows inductive biases affect learning speed and protocol richness.
- Analysis using cluster purity, SVD, and t-SNE shows symbols map to meaningful concepts rather than memorized pairs.
- The manipulation where agents see different instances of the same class increases semantic purity, supporting abstract communication claims.
- Grounding experiment (self-play + supervised learning) substantially increases purity and yields 68% human success in crowdsourced play; shows metonymic associations.
- Use of REINFORCE is appropriate and the inclusion of human evaluation strengthens validation.
- Request for authors to elaborate intuition behind the "informed" sender's 1-D convolutional design and consider alternatives like attention mechanisms.
- Question about training stability given REINFORCE's high variance and robustness across random seeds.
- Ask for discussion on scalability to larger vocabularies and compositional language and potential challenges/directions.
- Suggest testing language portability between agents (e.g., whether a new receiver can learn an existing sender's protocol faster).
- Ask about sensitivity to the temperature parameter τ (set to 10) and whether lower temperatures affect protocol emergence.
