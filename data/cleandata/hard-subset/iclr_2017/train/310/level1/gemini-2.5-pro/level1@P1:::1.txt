### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling framework for studying the emergence of communication between artificial agents using multi-agent reinforcement learning. The central contribution is the use of a simple "referential game" where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop their own communication protocol from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a pair. The paper makes several key claims:
1.  Simple feed-forward neural networks can learn to successfully coordinate and develop an effective, discrete communication protocol in this game.
2.  The emergent "language" is not random but develops semantic structure that reflects high-level conceptual properties of the images (e.g., object categories), rather than just low-level visual features.
3.  The semantic properties of the emergent language can be shaped by modifying the game's environment, for instance, by removing low-level visual common knowledge to force coordination on more abstract, class-level properties.
4.  Finally, and most impressively, the agents' emergent language can be grounded in human language by combining the interactive game with a standard supervised image-labeling task. This leads to a protocol that is not only effective between agents but also partially interpretable by humans, who can successfully play the game with the trained agent.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong, well-executed, and thought-provoking paper that should be accepted. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles the fundamental problem of how communication can emerge and be grounded, shifting the focus from passive, supervised learning on static datasets to active, goal-driven interaction between agents. This is a crucial direction for AI and computational linguistics, and the paper provides a clear, tractable, and effective framework for exploring it.
2.  **Rigorous and Convincing Experimental Support:** The claims are backed by a series of well-designed and logical experiments. The authors do not just show that communication emerges; they carefully analyze its properties using quantitative metrics (purity, SVD) and visualizations (t-SNE), and then demonstrate how to influence these properties. The final experiment involving human subjects provides powerful evidence that the proposed grounding strategy is effective and leads to a human-interpretable communication system.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its clear motivation and methodical execution. The introduction does an excellent job of situating the work within the literature of AI, cognitive science, and game theory, clearly articulating the limitations of purely supervised approaches to language learning. The proposed referential game is a simple yet elegant instantiation of a Lewis signaling game, making a classic theoretical concept concrete within a modern deep learning context.

The experimental progression is highly logical and compelling.
*   First, the authors establish that their agents can indeed learn to communicate effectively (Section 4), reaching near-perfect coordination. The comparison between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures is insightful, suggesting that inductive biases can influence not just learning speed but also the richness of the emergent protocol.
*   Second, the analysis of the emergent semantics is a key strength. Rather than treating the communication protocol as a black box, the authors use cluster purity against known semantic categories to show that the learned symbols correspond to meaningful concepts. This provides concrete evidence that the agents are not simply memorizing image pairs.
*   Third, the experiment in Section 4.1, where agents see different instances of the same object class, is a clever manipulation to test the hypothesis that agents can be forced to communicate more abstractly. The resulting increase in semantic purity provides strong support for this claim.
*   Finally, the grounding experiment in Section 5 is the paper's highlight. The idea of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning, inspired by AlphaGo, is powerful. The results are striking: not only does purity dramatically increase, but the resulting language generalizes in a way that is understandable to humans (68% success rate in the crowdsourced experiment). The qualitative finding that the agent learns "metonymic" associations (e.g., "dolphin" for a sea picture) is a fascinating and encouraging sign of semantic flexibility.

The work is scientifically rigorous. The use of reinforcement learning (REINFORCE) is appropriate for the task. The metrics are well-chosen, and the inclusion of a human evaluation study provides a level of validation that is often missing in work on emergent communication.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

1.  **Agent Architectures:** The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender is interesting. The "informed" architecture, with its 1-D convolutions, seems designed to encourage a feature-wise comparison between the two images. Could you elaborate on the intuition behind this specific design? Have you considered other mechanisms that might encourage comparison, such as attention mechanisms, which could potentially learn which visual features are most discriminative?

2.  **Training Stability:** REINFORCE is known for its high variance. Did you encounter any training instabilities, particularly in the early stages of learning when the communication protocol has not yet emerged? Are the results stable across multiple random seeds? A brief comment on the robustness of the learning process would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Scalability of the Protocol:** The experiments use a relatively small vocabulary (10 or 100 symbols). How do you see this framework scaling to more complex scenarios requiring a larger vocabulary or compositional language (e.g., describing relationships between multiple objects)? While this is clearly future work, a brief discussion of the potential challenges and directions would be valuable.

4.  **Language Portability:** A key goal mentioned is developing a language "portable to new situations and to new communication partners." The human study demonstrates portability to a new *type* of partner, which is excellent. Have you considered testing portability between agents? For example, if you train Sender A with Receiver A, can a new, untrained Receiver B learn Sender A's protocol faster than learning a new protocol from scratch with another untrained agent? This would directly test how fixed and transferable the emergent language is.

5.  **Role of Temperature:** The temperature parameter τ in the Gibbs distributions for both the sender and receiver is fixed at 10. This seems quite high, which would encourage exploration. How sensitive are the results to this parameter? Does a lower temperature (leading to a more deterministic policy) hinder the emergence of a shared protocol?
