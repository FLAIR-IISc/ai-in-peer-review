- Introduces a referential multi-agent game where a sender emits a symbol to help a receiver identify a target image.
- Shows simple feed-forward networks trained with REINFORCE can learn to communicate and coordinate from scratch.
- Finds environmental and architectural factors (e.g., informed sender, object-level reference) shape the semantics of emergent language.
- Mixes interactive self-play with supervised image-labeling to ground agents' language in human language.
- Human study confirms grounded language generalizes and humans can interpret agents' messages for unseen objects.
- Decision: Accept — novel, significant paradigm emphasizing interactive, functional language learning over passive supervision.
- Strong methodological rigor: well-designed incremental experiments support claims across emergence, shaping, and grounding.
- Emergence results: agents converge to near-perfect coordination; purity scores and SVD analysis show non-trivial structure.
- Shaping results: informed sender and object-level reference bias the language toward higher-level, conceptual semantics.
- Grounding results: supervised mixing greatly increases semantic purity and yields interpretable, sometimes metonymic, usages.
- Paper is exceptionally clear with coherent narrative and effective figures/tables.
- Feedback: Gibbs sampling temperature set to 10 seems high—need discussion of sensitivity and trade-offs (convergence vs vocabulary diversity).
- Feedback: Explain intuition for 1-D convolutions in the informed sender and why that bias is beneficial.
- Feedback: Recommend ablation on amount of supervised labels/steps to assess sample efficiency of grounding.
- Question: Have you tried alternative weight-sharing (e.g., share early image extractors but separate vocabulary layers)?
- Question: Consider more qualitative human evaluations (e.g., show multiple examples per symbol) to probe learned semantics.
- Question: How would language emergence change in mixed-motive games—would deceptive or less precise communication arise?
