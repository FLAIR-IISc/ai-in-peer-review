This paper investigates language emergence in a multi-agent setting using a referential game. The core idea is to have two agents, a sender and a receiver, learn to communicate about images, developing a shared “language” without prior knowledge. The authors explore how environmental factors and supervised learning can influence the characteristics of this emergent language, aiming for interpretability and eventual grounding in human language.

**Reject.** While the research question is interesting and relevant to the field of AI and language learning, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and clarity to warrant acceptance at ICLR. Specifically, the empirical results, while demonstrating successful coordination, do not convincingly demonstrate significant advancements beyond prior work (Sukhbaatar et al. 2016; Foerster et al. 2016) and the connection to human interpretability feels tenuous despite the later experiments.

The primary reason for rejection stems from the limited novelty of the findings. The core concept of emergent communication through referential games is not new. The improvements—exploring environmental factors and a simple grounding strategy—feel incremental. The claim that the informed sender leads to "more natural" languages is supported by purity metrics, but these metrics are somewhat abstract and their significance is not fully established. The improvement from the supervised learning phase is more compelling but the experiment is limited in scope. 

Secondly, the clarity suffers from a lengthy and somewhat discursive introduction. While establishing the context is important, the introduction meanders and includes extensive citations of related work, often without a clear connection to the specific contributions of this paper. The experimental setup is described adequately, but the justification for specific hyperparameter choices is absent. The connection between the theoretical framing (Lewis games, cheap talk) and the practical implementation is not always apparent, making it difficult to assess the theoretical implications of the results. The qualitative observations and discussion of “metonymic” links are intriguing but not rigorously supported by quantitative analysis.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Novelty:** The authors should more clearly articulate how their work builds upon and extends existing research on emergent communication. A direct comparison to Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) with clear performance metrics would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity:** The introduction should be streamlined to focus on the core contributions of the paper. Remove tangential discussions and focus on presenting a concise and compelling motivation for the research.
*   **Metrics:** The use of purity as a measure of semantic similarity is not entirely convincing. Consider alternative metrics, such as word embedding similarity or human evaluation of semantic relatedness.
*   **Grounding Experiment:** The human evaluation experiment is a positive step, but the methodology (CrowdFlower) and the selection of image pairs should be described in more detail. A control group (e.g., human subjects guessing images without the sender's label) would provide a baseline for assessing the effectiveness of the grounding strategy. What was the agreement rate between annotators for choosing the correct image?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to evaluate the impact of specific design choices, such as the informed sender architecture or the supervised learning component.
*   **Theoretical Link:** Elaborate on the connection to Lewis' signaling game and cheap talk theory. Discuss the implications of the results for understanding equilibrium selection and language emergence in these theoretical frameworks.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How do the results quantitatively compare to those achieved by Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) using similar game environments?
2.  What is the rationale behind the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, learning rate, temperature)? Were these values tuned, or were they chosen arbitrarily?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the supervised learning component influences the emergent language? Does it primarily affect the vocabulary size, the semantic properties of the symbols, or both?
4.  What were the inter-annotator agreement rates for the human evaluation experiment?
5.  The paper mentions a "metonymic" link between symbols and images. Can you provide more examples of these links and explain how they might arise from the learning process?
