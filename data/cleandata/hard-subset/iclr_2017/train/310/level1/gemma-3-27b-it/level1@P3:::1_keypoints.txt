- Paper addresses language learning via multi-agent referential games with a well-motivated core idea and solid literature positioning.  
- Reviewer recommends rejection at this time.  
- Evidence for emergence of “natural” or “interpretable” language is weak and relies on post-hoc analyses with questionable statistical significance.  
- Grounding experiments are promising but not robustly demonstrated; human evaluation methodology is unclear and limited in scope.  
- Main strength: agents can learn to coordinate; Table 1 shows successful communication.  
- Comparison between ‘informed’ and ‘agnostic’ senders is a valuable exploration of architectural biases.  
- Claims about semantic properties are unconvincing; purity scores are only modestly above chance and lack a clear chance baseline.  
- t-SNE visualizations are suggestive but lack quantitative support and rigorous analysis.  
- SVD/sampled matrix decomposition analysis is superficial and Figure 2 (right) is not informative.  
- Claim that agents’ language resembles human language is not adequately supported by the presented evidence.  
- Grounding experiment’s 68% human agreement is encouraging but methodology is ad-hoc and underreported (e.g., number of image pairs unstated).  
- Task framing for human subjects is vague, making it unclear what criteria participants used to choose images.  
- Assertion of “metonymic” links appears to be post-hoc rationalization without further evidence.  
- Characterizing subjects as having “played the referential game” is inaccurate—subjects were guessing, not engaging in interactive communication.  
- Suggested improvements: more rigorous statistical analysis of semantic properties (beyond purity).  
- Suggested improvements: redesign grounding experiment to have humans play the referential game and collect subjective interpretability feedback.  
- Suggested improvements: discuss limitations (difficult concept types, vocabulary size effects), clarify chance baseline for purity, and fully describe crowdsourcing instructions and counts.  
- Reviewer requests clarifications on SVD singular values, expected purity under random assignment, number of image pairs per CrowdFlower participant, criteria/examples for “metonymic” links, and sensitivity to hyperparameters/tuning.
