- Paper proposes a multi-agent communication framework for language learning using referential games to induce emergent “word meanings.”
- Authors show simple neural agents can coordinate to identify target images and produce interpretable symbols.
- They explore semantic grounding by combining game play with supervised labeling and small-scale human evaluation.
- Recommendation: Reject — work is promising but lacks sufficient depth and empirical validation for a top conference.
- Main criticism: limited robustness and generalizability; experiments confined to a specific referential game setup.
- Missing sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters, broader network architectures, and variations in game environments.
- Grounding claims overstate alignment with human intuition given the small-scale and narrow human evaluation.
- Strengths: clear motivation, well-defined experimental framework, and connections to linguistic and game-theoretic ideas.
- Analysis is mostly descriptive; visualizations (t-SNE) lack quantitative measures of semantic similarity.
- The reported “living vs. non-living” symbol distinction is under-explored and its consistency/implications are unclear.
- Suggested improvements: conduct ablation studies across architectures and hyperparameters.
- Suggested improvements: add quantitative semantic analyses (e.g., symbol-category correlation metrics).
- Suggested improvements: expand and diversify human evaluations to validate grounding claims.
- Suggested improvements: include convergence and learning-dynamics analysis for agent training.
- Clarification requests: which design choices beyond the informed sender most improved semantic grounding?
- Clarification requests: how does performance scale with vocabulary size and what are limits on concept capacity?
- Clarification requests: what is the computational cost of training and how does it vary with game/environment complexity?
