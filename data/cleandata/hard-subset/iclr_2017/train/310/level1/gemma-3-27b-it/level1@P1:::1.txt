This paper explores language emergence in a multi-agent setting using referential games. The authors investigate whether agents can learn to communicate effectively from scratch, and how environmental factors influence the semantics of the emergent language, with an eye towards grounding this communication in human-understandable terms. The work is well-motivated, addressing a core challenge in AI – enabling natural language interaction – and provides a solid experimental framework.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reason for acceptance is the demonstrated success of the proposed framework in fostering communication between agents. The paper convincingly shows that agents *can* learn to coordinate through a novel communication protocol, and that this protocol isn't entirely arbitrary; it exhibits sensitivity to both object categories and can be partially aligned with human intuition. A second strong reason is the clear and systematic exploration of the environment’s influence on the emergent language, showcasing a path towards more interpretable communication.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The experimental setup is well-defined and employs established techniques (deep learning, reinforcement learning, referential games). The results are presented clearly, with tables and figures that effectively illustrate the key findings. The progression of experiments – from basic coordination to semantic grounding to human interpretability – builds a compelling narrative. Specifically, the finding that the 'informed sender' architecture leads to more interpretable language is significant, as it suggests that inductive biases can guide the emergence of more natural communication. The ablation studies (removing common knowledge, supervised learning) are well-executed and provide valuable insights. The quantitative metrics (purity, human accuracy) provide objective evidence supporting the claims. The connection to existing work in linguistics, game theory, and AI is appropriately acknowledged and contextualized. The human evaluation, while limited in scope, offers a promising indication of the potential for grounding the agent's language in human understanding.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention hyperparameters were not tuned, a brief discussion of their sensitivity would be helpful. For example, how did varying the embedding dimensionality or temperature affect performance?
*   **Vocabulary Size:** The choice of vocabulary sizes (10 and 100) is reasonable, but a more detailed justification for these choices would be beneficial. Was there a point of diminishing returns with larger vocabularies?
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The paper would benefit from more in-depth qualitative examples of the emergent language. Showing specific symbol-image pairings and analyzing the reasoning behind them could provide further insights into how the agents are learning to communicate. The examples in the grounding section are promising, and expanding on these would be valuable.
*   **Theoretical Connection:** The paper references game theory concepts (Nash equilibria, cheap talk games). While acknowledging these connections is good, exploring them in more detail – e.g., discussing the types of equilibria the agents converge to – could strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work.
*   **Receiver Architecture:** The paper focuses largely on the sender architecture. A more detailed analysis of the receiver’s learning process and its role in shaping the emergent language could provide a more complete picture.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention the possibility of multiple Nash equilibria in cheap talk games. Do you have any insights into *which* equilibrium your agents are converging to, and how the environmental factors (e.g., removing common knowledge) influence this convergence?
2.  The human evaluation focused on images with direct label correspondences. How do humans interpret the symbols used for images *without* those correspondences? A more comprehensive human evaluation, potentially including open-ended interpretation tasks, could provide a richer understanding of the emergent language.
3.  How does the performance of the 'informed sender' compare to other potential inductive biases or architectural modifications? Were there any unsuccessful attempts to guide the language emergence process?
4.  The Reinforce algorithm can be sensitive to hyperparameter settings. Did you observe any instability or variance in training, and if so, how did you address it?
5.  Could you provide more detail on the specific examples of metonymic links observed in the human evaluation, beyond the dolphin/fence example? This would help illustrate the nuances of the agent’s communication.
