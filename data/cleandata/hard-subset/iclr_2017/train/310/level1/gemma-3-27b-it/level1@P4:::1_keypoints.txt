- Decision: Reject.
- Main contribution: Studies emergent communication in a sender–receiver referential game aiming for interpretable and human-grounded languages.
- Strength: Agents successfully coordinate and a supervised phase improves alignment with human labels.
- Novelty concern: Work is incremental over prior emergent-communication studies (Sukhbaatar et al. 2016; Foerster et al. 2016).
- Empirical impact: Results do not convincingly demonstrate significant advances beyond prior work.
- Metric issues: Reliance on purity metrics to claim "more natural" languages is abstract and not fully justified.
- Supervision experiment: Supervised learning effect is more compelling but experiments are limited in scope.
- Clarity problems: Introduction is overly long and discursive, with many tangential citations and unclear linkage to contributions.
- Method details lacking: Justification for hyperparameter choices is missing.
- Theory–practice gap: Connection between Lewis signaling/cheap talk framing and practical implementation is unclear.
- Qualitative claims: Observations about “metonymic” links are intriguing but not supported by rigorous quantitative analysis.
- Recommended experiments: Add direct comparisons to prior work, alternative semantic metrics, ablation studies, and a control in human grounding tests.
- Human evaluation: Grounding experiment needs more methodological detail (CrowdFlower setup, image selection, annotator agreement rates).
- Questions for authors: Quantitative comparison to prior work; hyperparameter rationale; detailed effects of supervised component; inter-annotator agreement; more examples and explanation of metonymic links.
