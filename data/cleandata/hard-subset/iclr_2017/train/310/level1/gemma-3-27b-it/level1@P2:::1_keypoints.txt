- Positive: Presents an interesting multi-agent referential-game framework and clearly described experimental setup with encouraging coordination results.  
- Decision: Recommendation: Reject.  
- Main critique: Paper lacks rigor and clarity in presenting and interpreting results, especially for claims about "naturalness" and interpretability of emergent language.  
- Primary reason for rejection: Insufficient evidence for semantic grounding; symbols do not convincingly align with meaningful human-interpretable categories beyond coarse distinctions.  
- Evidence concerns: Purity scores are modest, t-SNE visualizations are hard to interpret, and human evaluation is limited and potentially biased.  
- Supervision caveat: Improvements from supervised learning appear to reflect vocabulary alignment with labels rather than true emergence of grounded meaning.  
- Comparison issue: No thorough head-to-head comparisons with prior emergent-communication baselines (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) or ablation studies to isolate contributions.  
- Suggested analysis 1: Provide detailed quantitative analysis of symbol usage (e.g., co-occurrence matrices, symbol assignment distributions).  
- Suggested analysis 2: Conduct ablation studies to assess contributions of architecture components (e.g., informed sender’s 1D convolutions).  
- Suggested analysis 3: Include baseline comparisons with existing emergent communication methods.  
- Human evaluation fix: Clarify protocol, instructions, metrics, and include controls (e.g., labeling without symbols) to rule out biases.  
- Limitations: Explicitly acknowledge dataset and task limitations (ImageNet reliance, simple referential game).  
- Robustness: Analyze convergence and stability of the learned protocol under environmental or vocabulary changes.  
- Questions for authors: Report purity distribution across random seeds and sensitivity to initialization.  
- Questions for authors: Quantify synonymy/redundancy (e.g., SVD analysis) in informed sender vocabulary.  
- Questions for authors: Describe controls for potential image-content biases in human evaluation.  
- Questions for authors: Compare informed vs. agnostic sender performance with equal vocabulary size.  
- Questions for authors: Report computational cost and scaling with vocabulary size and image resolution.
