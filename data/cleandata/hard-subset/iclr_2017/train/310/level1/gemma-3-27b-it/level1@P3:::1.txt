This paper tackles the problem of learning language through interaction, specifically proposing a multi-agent communication framework based on referential games. The core idea – that agents can bootstrap a communication protocol from scratch through repeated interaction – is well-motivated, drawing from linguistic theories highlighting the functional role of communication and the limitations of purely supervised learning approaches. The authors position their work within a rich landscape of related research spanning multi-agent systems, game theory, and emergent communication, and convincingly argue for the novelty of their approach in attempting to bridge the gap between theoretical frameworks and practical AI systems.

I recommend **rejection** at this time. While the paper presents a compelling idea and demonstrates initial success in a controlled setting, the evidence supporting the claims of ‘natural’ or ‘interpretable’ language emergence is weak and relies heavily on post-hoc analysis with questionable statistical significance. The grounding experiments, while promising, are also not robustly demonstrated, and the human evaluation suffers from unclear methodology and limited scope.

The paper’s main strength lies in showing that agents *can* learn to coordinate. Table 1 clearly demonstrates successful communication, and the comparison between ‘informed’ and ‘agnostic’ senders is a valuable initial exploration of architectural biases. However, the claims regarding semantic properties are less convincing. The purity scores presented in Table 1, while “significantly above chance,” are not dramatically so and are difficult to interpret without a clear understanding of the baseline chance level (how many categories would be expected to overlap purely by chance given the symbol assignment?). Furthermore, the t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) are suggestive but lack quantitative analysis. Simply observing that “objects close in CNN space are associated to the same symbol” is insufficient evidence of meaningful semantic alignment. The authors allude to a sampled matrix decomposition via SVD (mentioned in the text referencing Figure 2, right), but the figure itself isn’t particularly informative and the analysis feels superficial. The leap from these observations to the claim that the agents’ language resembles human language is a substantial one, and isn't adequately supported. 

The grounding experiment provides a more intriguing direction, but the reporting of results is also problematic. While the 68% human agreement rate is encouraging, the methodology feels ad-hoc. The number of image pairs presented to human subjects is not stated, and the framing of the task (“pick the picture that you think is most related to the word”) is vague. It’s unclear what criteria subjects were using – are they simply recalling the ImageNet label, or are they attempting to interpret the symbol *within the context of the game*? Furthermore, the assertion that “metonymic” links explain some successful cases feels like post-hoc rationalization without further evidence. The statement that subjects were able to “successfully play the referential game” with the sender, based on a 68% success rate, is a mischaracterization; subjects were only *guessing* which image the sender was referring to, not participating in an interactive communication loop.

Several points could improve the paper. First, a more rigorous statistical analysis of the semantic properties is crucial. Beyond purity, consider measures of semantic similarity between agent-generated symbol clusters and established semantic hierarchies. Second, the grounding experiment needs a more controlled design. A more direct evaluation would involve having human participants *play* the referential game with the agent sender, measuring communication success rates, and soliciting subjective feedback on the interpretability of the symbols. Third, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach. For example, what types of concepts are difficult for the agents to communicate about? How does the vocabulary size affect performance?  Fourth, clarify the baseline chance level used to determine statistical significance of the purity scores. Finally, provide a clearer description of the crowd-sourced survey, including the exact wording of the instructions presented to subjects, and the number of image pairs each subject evaluated.

I have several clarifying questions for the authors:

1.  Regarding the SVD analysis of the symbol-image matrix: Can you provide the actual values of the singular values, and a more detailed explanation of how this analysis supports the claim that the informed sender uses a more varied vocabulary than the agnostic sender?
2.  For the purity analysis, what is the expected purity score under a random assignment of symbols to images, given the category distribution in the dataset?
3.  In the grounding experiment, how many image pairs were presented to each participant in the CrowdFlower survey?
4.  Can you elaborate on the specific criteria used to identify “metonymic” links in the human evaluation, and provide examples beyond the ones given in the paper?
5.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, learning rate)? Have you performed any hyperparameter tuning?
