## Review of "A Framework for Language Learning based on Multi-Agent Communication"

This paper proposes a multi-agent communication framework for language learning, motivated by the limitations of passive learning from large text corpora for developing truly interactive AI systems. The authors focus on referential games where agents must learn to communicate to identify target images, demonstrating that simple neural networks can indeed learn to coordinate and develop interpretable “word meanings”. They further explore methods to encourage semantic grounding and demonstrate initial success in aligning the emergent language with human intuition through a combination of game-playing and supervised labeling.

**Reject.** While the research is promising and explores a compelling direction for language learning, the paper lacks sufficient depth in its analysis and empirical validation to warrant acceptance at a competitive AI conference. The results, while positive, are presented somewhat superficially, and the contributions feel incremental rather than a significant advancement.

The primary reason for rejection is the limited robustness and generalizability of the findings. While the authors show successful coordination in a specific referential game setup, the paper doesn't thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices, different network architectures (beyond the two explored), or variations in the game environment. Furthermore, the grounding experiments, although encouraging, rely on a relatively small-scale human evaluation and a specific supervised learning setup. The jump to claiming alignment with “human intuition” feels overstated given the limited scope of this evaluation.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear motivation and well-defined experimental framework. The connection to linguistic theories and game theory is also appropriately highlighted. However, the analysis remains largely descriptive. For example, the t-SNE visualizations are informative but lack quantitative measures of semantic similarity. Additionally, the discussion of the “living vs. non-living” distinction, even with two symbols, feels under-explored – is this a consistent finding, and what does it suggest about the underlying learning dynamics?

To improve the paper, I suggest the following: 
* **Ablation studies:** Systematically evaluate the impact of different architectural choices and hyperparameters.
* **Quantitative Semantic Analysis:** Use more rigorous methods for evaluating semantic coherence (e.g., calculating the correlation between symbol usage and known semantic categories).
* **Expanded Human Evaluation:** Conduct a more extensive human evaluation, possibly using a larger and more diverse participant pool.
* **Convergence Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the learning dynamics and convergence properties of the agents. 

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify:
* What specific design choices (beyond the informed sender architecture) contributed most significantly to the improved semantic grounding?
* How does the performance of the system scale with vocabulary size? Are there limitations to the number of concepts the agents can effectively represent?
* What is the computational cost of training the agents, and how does it vary with the complexity of the game environment?
