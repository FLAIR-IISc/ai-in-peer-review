This paper presents an interesting framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication in referential games. The core idea – that agents can bootstrap a communication protocol through interaction – is well-motivated and addresses a crucial challenge in AI: moving beyond passive learning of language to active, functional communication. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the initial results demonstrating successful coordination are encouraging.

**Reject.** While the work is promising, the paper lacks sufficient rigor and clarity in presenting and interpreting the results to warrant acceptance at ICLR. Specifically, the claims regarding the "naturalness" and interpretability of the emergent language are not convincingly supported. The evidence presented (purity scores, t-SNE visualizations, and human evaluation) is either weakly correlated with human intuition or relies on potentially confounded experimental designs.

The primary reason for rejection stems from a lack of convincing evidence for semantic grounding. While the authors demonstrate that agents *can* coordinate, they don’t convincingly show that the symbols they develop align with meaningful semantic categories in a way that is readily interpretable by humans, beyond a very basic "living vs. non-living" distinction. The purity scores, while statistically significant, are not overwhelmingly high, and the t-SNE visualizations are difficult to interpret without more quantitative analysis. The human evaluation, while positive, is limited in scope and susceptible to biases (e.g., participants may be inferring meaning based on the images themselves rather than the symbols).  Furthermore, the authors acknowledge that the improvements from supervised learning are largely due to aligning the agent’s vocabulary with existing labels, rather than demonstrating a true emergence of grounded meaning.

Secondly, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to baselines. While the authors reference related work (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), there's no head-to-head comparison with these approaches or an ablation study to demonstrate the specific contribution of the proposed informed sender architecture. It’s unclear whether the observed results are due to the specific architecture, the game setup, or simply the power of deep learning.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Quantitative Analysis of Symbol Usage:** Provide a more detailed analysis of symbol usage patterns. For example, compute the co-occurrence matrix of symbols and images, and analyze the distribution of symbol assignments across categories. This would provide more objective evidence for semantic grounding.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the proposed architecture (e.g., the 1D convolutions in the informed sender).
* **Baseline Comparisons:** Compare the performance of the proposed approach to existing methods for emergent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016).
* **Clarify Human Evaluation:** The description of the human evaluation is somewhat vague. Provide more details about the experimental setup, the instructions given to participants, and the metrics used to assess performance.  Consider including a control condition where participants are asked to label the images without seeing any symbols.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The authors should explicitly acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as the reliance on ImageNet images and the simplicity of the referential game.
* **Convergence Analysis:** While the paper shows convergence to coordination, it doesn’t discuss the stability of the learned communication protocol.  How robust is the learned language to changes in the environment or the addition of new objects?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The purity scores are reported, but what is the distribution of purity across different random seeds? How sensitive are these results to initialization?
2.  You mention the possibility of synonymy in the informed sender's vocabulary. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the SVD decomposition to quantify the amount of redundancy?
3.  In the human evaluation, did you control for potential biases related to image content? Could participants have simply guessed the correct image based on visual cues, rather than relying on the symbols?
4.  How does the performance of the informed sender compare to the agnostic sender when using the same vocabulary size?
5.  What is the computational cost of training the proposed model?  How does it scale with vocabulary size and image resolution?
