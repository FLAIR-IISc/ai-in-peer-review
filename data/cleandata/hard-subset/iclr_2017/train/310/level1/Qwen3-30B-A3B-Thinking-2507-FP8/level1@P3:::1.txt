

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of AI language learning by proposing a multi-agent communication framework that enables agents to develop language through interaction rather than passive learning from text corpora. The authors demonstrate that simple neural network agents can successfully coordinate in referential games, where a sender must communicate about a target image from a pair to a receiver. Crucially, they show that the emergent communication protocols develop semantic properties aligned with human conceptual categories, and they present a novel approach for grounding this communication in natural language through a combination of interactive game play and supervised learning. The paper provides both theoretical grounding in game theory and empirical evidence supporting the emergence of meaningful communication.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by demonstrating that agents can develop meaningful communication through interaction, and by providing a method to ground this communication in natural language. The approach is well-motivated, the methodology is sound, and the results are scientifically rigorous.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim that agents can develop meaningful communication through interaction rather than passive learning is well-supported. The authors demonstrate convergence to successful communication across multiple architectures and parameter settings, with the informed sender architecture achieving higher performance and more diverse symbol usage. The semantic analysis using McRae categories and t-SNE visualizations provides compelling evidence that the agents develop language aligned with human semantic categories, not just low-level visual properties.

The grounding approach is particularly innovative and well-executed. By combining interactive game play with supervised image classification, the authors successfully bridge the gap between the agents' emergent language and human natural language. The human evaluation showing 68% accuracy in interpreting the agents' communication is a strong empirical validation of their approach. This represents a crucial step toward developing machines that can communicate with humans productively.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly distinguishing its contributions from related work in multi-agent systems (Stone & Veloso, 1998), neural communication (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), and human-in-the-loop approaches (Wang et al., 2016). The authors' use of game theory as a theoretical foundation (Lewis' signaling games) and their connection to linguistic and cognitive science literature (Wagner et al., 2003; Skyrms, 2010) provide strong theoretical grounding.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, it could be enhanced in a few areas. First, the hyperparameter selection process warrants more detail. The authors state they "set the following hyperparameters without tuning," but it's unclear how they arrived at these values or whether they performed any sensitivity analysis. A brief discussion of this would strengthen the paper's methodological rigor.

Second, the comparison to Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) could be more thorough. While the authors acknowledge these works, they don't explicitly contrast their approach with these important prior results, particularly regarding the semantic properties of the emergent language.

Third, the paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the "metonymic" communication patterns observed in the human evaluation. The authors mention these as an encouraging sign of natural language-like flexibility, but a more systematic analysis would strengthen this point.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you expect the performance to scale with larger vocabulary sizes or more complex environments with multiple objects or spatial relationships?

2. What is the computational cost of the grounding procedure compared to standard supervised learning approaches for natural language processing tasks?

3. Can you provide more detail on the specific "metonymic" patterns observed in the human evaluation, and how they relate to established linguistic phenomena?

4. How might this framework be extended to handle multi-turn conversations rather than single-symbol communication, and what additional challenges would this introduce?

The paper makes a compelling case for the value of interaction-based language learning, provides strong empirical evidence for the emergence of meaningful communication, and offers a promising path toward grounding this communication in natural language. The work represents a significant step toward developing conversational agents that can communicate with humans productively, and it should be accepted for publication at ICLR 2017.
