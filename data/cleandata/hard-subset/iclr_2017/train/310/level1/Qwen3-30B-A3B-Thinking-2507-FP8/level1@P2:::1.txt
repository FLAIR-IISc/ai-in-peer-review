

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework for language learning through multi-agent communication, specifically using referential games as a training paradigm. The authors demonstrate that simple neural network agents can learn to coordinate successfully in referential games, developing a shared language to identify target images from pairs. They further investigate how modifying the game environment can encourage the emergence of language with semantic properties more aligned with human conceptualizations, and propose a grounding strategy that combines the referential game with supervised image classification to improve human interpretability of the emergent language. The paper makes three significant contributions: (1) demonstrating successful communication learning in a referential game framework, (2) showing how environmental modifications can improve semantic properties of emergent language, and (3) providing a method for grounding communication in natural language that improves human interpretability.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons: The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in natural language processing with a well-motivated approach that builds on relevant literature while providing strong empirical evidence. The results are scientifically rigorous, with multiple complementary experiments that clearly support the authors' claims about the emergence of semantically meaningful communication.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core question is well-motivated and addresses a critical limitation of current approaches to training conversational agents: the lack of interactive learning that captures the functional aspects of communication. The authors clearly position their work against existing approaches (supervised learning, human-in-the-loop, Wizard-of-Oz) and provide a strong theoretical foundation by connecting to game theory (Lewis' signaling game) and linguistic/cognitive science literature.

The empirical evidence is robust and well-designed. The authors demonstrate that agents can learn to coordinate successfully (Table 1, Figure 2), and they provide compelling evidence that the emergent language has semantic properties beyond low-level visual features (Table 1, Figure 3). The key insight that agents converge to a "living-vs-non-living" categorization even with a limited 2-symbol vocabulary (surprisingly aligning with human semantic systems) is particularly noteworthy and well-supported.

The environmental modification experiment (Section 4.1) is elegantly designed to test how removing low-level common knowledge forces agents to coordinate on higher-level properties, with results confirming the hypothesis (Table 2, Figure 3). The grounding approach (Section 5) is innovative and well-executed, demonstrating that combining the referential game with supervised image classification preserves communication success while significantly improving human interpretability (68% human accuracy in the crowdsourced test).

The paper's scientific rigor is evident in its methodological choices: using reinforcement learning for training, appropriate evaluation metrics (purity of clusters, human evaluation), and careful control of variables across experiments. The results consistently support the authors' claims, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper further, consider the following points:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of limitations. While the authors note the language is initially limited, they could explore the practical implications of this limitation for real-world conversational agents and potential paths to overcome it.

2. A brief discussion of computational requirements (training time, hardware) would help readers assess the scalability of the approach.

3. While the paper connects to game theory, it could more explicitly discuss which specific equilibrium concepts they observe (e.g., whether their emergent language corresponds to a particular type of Nash equilibrium) and why this matters for language emergence.

4. A more detailed comparison to Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) would help position the paper more clearly within the existing literature on multi-agent communication.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper notes that the informed sender uses more symbols than the agnostic sender, but doesn't fully explain the mechanism behind this difference. Could you elaborate on how the architectural differences between these sender types specifically influence the emergent language properties?

2. In the grounding section, you observe that the language generalizes beyond the supervised categories. How do you account for this generalization, and what are the specific mechanisms by which the agents extend their communication to new categories?

3. The paper mentions "metonymic" links (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea) that humans can interpret. How does this relate to established theories of semantic extension in natural language, and what implications does this have for designing future language learning systems?
