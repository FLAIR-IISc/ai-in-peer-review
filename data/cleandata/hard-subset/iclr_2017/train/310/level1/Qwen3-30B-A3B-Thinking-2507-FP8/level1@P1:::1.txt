

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework for language learning through multi-agent communication in referential games, addressing a fundamental limitation of current NLP approaches that rely on passive learning from large text corpora. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate effectively in these games, developing communication protocols that reflect high-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) it shows that agents can successfully develop communication without pre-programmed protocols; (2) it demonstrates how modifying the game environment can lead to more semantically meaningful communication; and (3) it introduces a method for grounding agents' communication in natural language through a combination of interactive learning and supervised image labeling. The results are compelling, showing that agents can develop a language that is partially interpretable by humans, with the grounded version achieving 68% human comprehension in a crowdsourced evaluation.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is significant, well-motivated, and provides strong empirical evidence supporting the claims. The key reasons for this recommendation are: (1) the paper addresses a fundamental limitation in current language learning approaches by proposing a more naturalistic, interactive framework that better captures the functional aspects of communication; and (2) the experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with clear evidence that agents can develop communication protocols that reflect semantic properties and that grounding in natural language significantly improves human interpretability.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several critical dimensions. First, it is exceptionally well-motivated, clearly positioning itself within the broader literature on language learning, multi-agent systems, and game theory. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from supervised learning methods (which focus on structural language properties), human-in-the-loop approaches (which face scalability issues), and "Wizard-of-Oz" environments (which require heavy engineering). They also ground their work in relevant theoretical frameworks, particularly Lewis' signaling games and evolutionary perspectives on language development.

Second, the paper provides robust empirical evidence for its claims. The authors conduct a series of well-designed experiments that systematically test their hypotheses about the nature of the emergent communication protocols. They demonstrate that agents can coordinate successfully (achieving near-perfect performance), that the emergent language reflects semantic properties (with purity scores significantly above chance), and that grounding in natural language through supervised learning dramatically improves human interpretability (from 1% to 68% in a human evaluation). The experiments include appropriate baselines, quantitative metrics (purity scores, coordination rates), and insightful qualitative analysis (t-SNE visualizations, human evaluation).

Third, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a practical path toward developing conversational agents that can communicate with humans. The grounding approach they propose—combining interactive game play with supervised image labeling—offers a promising solution to a key challenge in human-AI communication. The results showing that humans can understand the agents' communication even when the images don't contain supervised categories (68% accuracy) are particularly compelling and suggest the approach has real potential.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the authors:

1. Provide more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding scalability and generalization to more complex communication tasks beyond the referential game setup.

2. Include a more thorough comparison with the related work by Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016), which also explore emergent communication in neural networks. Specifically, clarify how their approach to grounding communication in natural language differs from and improves upon these prior works.

3. Expand the discussion of the "object-level reference" experiment to better explain how it differs from the main referential game setup and why it leads to improved semantic properties.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that the informed sender uses more symbols (88 out of 100) after grounding, while the agnostic sender uses only 2 symbols. Could you elaborate on why the informed architecture leads to more diverse symbol usage, and whether this diversity directly contributes to the improved semantic properties?

2. In the human evaluation, the authors found that 68% of subjects correctly identified the target image using the sender's word. How does this compare to a baseline where humans are asked to identify images based on random words? This would provide a stronger sense of how much improvement the grounding approach provides.

3. The paper mentions that the agents' language shows metonymic shifts (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea). Could the authors provide more examples of these semantic shifts and discuss their implications for the broader understanding of language learning in AI systems?

4. How do you envision scaling this approach to handle more complex communication scenarios beyond the referential game, such as multi-turn dialogues or conversations about abstract concepts?
