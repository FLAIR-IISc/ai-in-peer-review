

This paper presents a compelling approach to language learning through multi-agent communication games, addressing a critical gap in current AI systems that rely solely on passive text exposure. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can successfully coordinate in referential games to develop communication protocols, and further show how environmental modifications can induce more semantically meaningful language. Their key contributions include: (1) establishing that tabula rasa agents can learn to communicate effectively in referential games; (2) demonstrating that agents develop symbol meanings aligned with human semantic categories; and (3) introducing a grounded approach to connect agent communication with natural language through supervised learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing empirical evidence that interactive learning can produce semantically meaningful communication protocols, and their grounding strategy represents a significant step toward human-interpretable machine communication. The experimental design is rigorous, with appropriate metrics (purity, SVD analysis) and careful comparison between different sender architectures.

The paper's strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, thorough literature positioning, and convincing empirical results. The authors effectively address concerns about convergence (showing agents reach near-perfect coordination) and semantic quality (demonstrating symbol usage aligns with human conceptual categories). Their grounding approach is particularly innovative, with compelling evidence that supervised learning on natural language labels improves human interpretability of agent communication.

For improvement, the authors might more explicitly discuss the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding scalability to more complex communication scenarios. Additionally, a more detailed analysis of why the informed sender outperforms the agnostic sender would strengthen their findings.

I have two questions to clarify: First, how would the results change if the game environment included more than two agents, which would better model real-world communication scenarios? Second, what specific aspects of the supervised learning phase (beyond vocabulary size) most significantly contributed to improved human interpretability? These would help assess the generalizability of the grounding strategy.
