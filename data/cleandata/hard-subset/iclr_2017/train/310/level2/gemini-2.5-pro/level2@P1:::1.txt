### Review of "Learning to Communicate"

**1. Summary of the paper's claims and contributions**

This paper presents a novel framework for studying language emergence through multi-agent communication. The authors argue that current approaches based on passive learning from large text corpora fail to capture the functional, goal-oriented nature of language. To address this, they propose using referential games where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop a shared communication protocol from scratch to succeed at a cooperative task: identifying a target image from a pair of images.

The paper makes several key contributions:
- It demonstrates that simple neural network agents can successfully learn to communicate and coordinate in this game setup, starting as blank slates.
- It analyzes the properties of the emergent language, showing that the learned symbols correspond to high-level semantic categories of the objects depicted, rather than just low-level visual features. The authors show this through cluster purity analysis and visualization.
- It explores how modifying the game's environment—specifically, by removing low-level visual details as common knowledge—can nudge the agents toward developing a more abstract, conceptual communication system.
- Most significantly, it introduces a simple yet effective method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, the agents learn a protocol that is not only effective for their coordination but also interpretable by humans. The success of this grounding is convincingly demonstrated through a crowdsourced experiment where humans successfully interpret the agent's messages to solve the referential task, even for objects the agent was not explicitly trained to name.

**2. Decision and key reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent and thought-provoking paper that addresses a fundamental problem in artificial intelligence. It is well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a series of well-designed experiments that build a compelling narrative. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **A Compelling Framework for Interactive Learning:** The paper successfully makes the case for moving beyond passive learning and provides a simple, scalable, and effective framework for studying emergent communication. The referential game setup is an elegant testbed for exploring how functional, grounded language can arise from the need to cooperate. This work provides a strong foundation for future research in this exciting direction.
2.  **Novel and Effective Grounding Strategy:** The method of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning is a significant contribution. The resulting human evaluation experiment is a powerful demonstration that this approach can lead to a communication protocol that is not only successful between agents but is also partially understandable by humans and generalizes in non-trivial, "metonymic" ways. This is a crucial step towards building AI agents that can communicate productively with people.

**3. Supporting arguments for the decision**

The paper is strong across multiple dimensions. The motivation is clearly articulated, setting it apart from purely supervised approaches to language and situating it well within the literature of multi-agent systems, game theory, and cognitive science. The experimental progression is logical: it first establishes that communication can emerge, then analyzes its properties, then shows how to shape those properties, and finally demonstrates how to ground it in human language.

The analyses are solid. The use of cluster purity to measure semantic coherence is appropriate, and the singular value decomposition (SVD) analysis provides good evidence that the "informed" sender develops a richer vocabulary than the "agnostic" one. The t-SNE visualizations provide a helpful, intuitive view of the learned semantic space.

The final experiment in Section 5 is the highlight of the paper. Proving that humans can interpret the emergent language with 68% accuracy is a very strong result. It suggests that the grounding learned from a supervised task can be flexibly applied to new communicative situations, mirroring a key aspect of human linguistic creativity. The qualitative examples of "dolphin" for sea and "fence" for land are fascinating and convincingly support the authors' claims about the emergence of a flexible, shared common ground.

**4. Additional feedback for improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to strengthen the paper further. They do not detract from my positive assessment.

-   **Sender Architectures:** The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" senders is interesting. A little more intuition on why the dimension-wise comparison in the "informed" architecture provides a useful inductive bias would be beneficial. Is it because the pre-trained VGG features might have some semantic alignment along their dimensions?
-   **Training Details:** The paper uses the REINFORCE algorithm. It is well-known that this algorithm can suffer from high variance, potentially making training unstable. It would be helpful for reproducibility and future work if the authors could briefly comment on the stability of training or if any techniques (e.g., baselines) were used to reduce variance.
-   **Language Complexity:** The current work focuses on a single symbol from a fixed vocabulary. This is a reasonable starting point, but the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion in the conclusion about the challenges and potential paths forward for scaling this framework to more complex, compositional language (i.e., sequences of symbols).
-   **Visualizations:** The t-SNE plots in Figure 3 are useful. Their interpretability could be enhanced by, for example, using different marker shapes to denote the ground-truth McRae super-categories. This would make it easier to visually assess the alignment between the emergent symbol clusters (colors) and the human-defined semantic categories.

**5. Questions for the authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the discussion period:

1.  In the case of the agnostic sender, the paper notes that the emergent two-symbol vocabulary seems to map to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. Could you provide more direct quantitative evidence for this interesting claim, for example, by showing the distribution of McRae categories for objects assigned to each of the two symbols?
2.  In the grounding experiment (Section 5), how did the addition of the supervised loss affect the convergence speed on the primary referential game task? Did it slow down, speed up, or have no noticeable effect on the agents' ability to achieve perfect coordination?
3.  The 68% human success rate in the final experiment is compelling. Could you provide a brief qualitative analysis of the failure cases? Understanding when humans *failed* to interpret the agent's message could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the current grounding method and directions for future improvement.
