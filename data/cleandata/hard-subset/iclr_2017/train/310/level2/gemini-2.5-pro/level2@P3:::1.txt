### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for studying language emergence through multi-agent communication. The work investigates referential games where a "sender" agent and a "receiver" agent must develop a communication protocol from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a pair. The key contributions are threefold: 1) It demonstrates that simple feed-forward neural networks, trained with reinforcement learning, can successfully converge to a shared communication protocol for this task using real-world images. 2) It explores how environmental and architectural factors—specifically, an inductive bias in the sender's architecture and the removal of instance-level "common knowledge" from the game—can guide the emergent language to be more compositional and abstract, better reflecting human-like semantic categories. 3) It proposes and validates a practical method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task. This grounding is shown to produce a communication scheme that is interpretable by humans, even for concepts not seen during supervised training.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces a compelling and well-motivated research direction for learning language through interaction, providing a valuable alternative to purely supervised, passive learning paradigms. The framework is simple, elegant, and effective.
2.  The experimental investigation is thorough and insightful. The work goes beyond simply showing that agents can learn to communicate, by systematically analyzing the properties of the emergent language and demonstrating concrete strategies for shaping its semantics and grounding it in human language. The human evaluation study in the final section provides strong evidence for the central claims of the work.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper tackles the important problem of how agents can learn to communicate in a functional, goal-oriented manner. The proposed framework, based on Lewis' signaling games, is well-grounded in linguistics and game theory and is adeptly implemented using modern deep learning and reinforcement learning techniques.

The strength of the work lies in its methodical experimental design. The initial experiments convincingly show that agents can develop an effective communication protocol *tabula rasa*. The analysis of this protocol is insightful, using singular value decomposition to assess vocabulary richness and cluster purity to measure semantic coherence against human-defined categories.

Furthermore, the paper thoughtfully investigates how to make the emergent language more "natural." The introduction of the "informed sender" architecture and the modification of the game to operate at the object-class level are clever ways to inject priors that encourage more abstract representations. The results clearly support the hypothesis that these changes lead to a more semantically structured language.

The final section on grounding the agents' communication in human language is the most significant contribution. The strategy of mixing interactive self-play with a supervised classification task is inspired by recent successes like AlphaGo and is shown to be highly effective. The resulting protocol is not only more successful on the task but is also demonstrably interpretable by humans, as validated by a well-designed crowdsourcing experiment. The finding that the sender learns to use words "metonymically" for novel objects is particularly fascinating and suggests a promising path toward flexible and general human-agent communication.

The paper is clearly written, the experiments are well-motivated, and the results strongly support the conclusions. This work opens up many avenues for future research into emergent communication and represents a solid step towards building more interactive AI.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are offered to help improve the paper further and are not critical for the decision.

*   **Architectural Clarity:** The description of the "informed sender" could be clarified. While the intuition of an inductive bias for dimension-by-dimension comparison is clear, a more explicit mathematical formulation of how the 1-D convolutions and subsequent filter operate on the concatenated embeddings would enhance reproducibility.
*   **Hyperparameter Discussion:** The paper notes that hyperparameters were set without tuning. While the results are strong as they are, a brief discussion on the potential sensitivity to certain choices, particularly the temperature parameter `τ` for the Gibbs distributions, would be valuable. For example, how does temperature affect the trade-off between exploration of the vocabulary and exploitation of a working protocol during training?
*   **Scalability:** The current experiments use a relatively small vocabulary (up to 100 symbols). It would be beneficial to include a short discussion on the potential challenges and directions for scaling this framework to significantly larger vocabularies and more complex compositional language, which would be required for more sophisticated tasks.
*   **Credit Assignment in RL:** The sender and receiver are updated using the same global reward signal. This is a standard approach (REINFORCE), but it raises a credit assignment problem: when a failure occurs, it is difficult to know whether the sender sent an ambiguous message or the receiver misinterpreted a clear one. A brief comment acknowledging this challenge in the context of multi-agent communication would enrich the discussion.

### Questions for Clarification

*   In the "object-level reference" experiment (Section 4.1), are the agents trained and tested on different, unseen images of the same object classes? Clarifying this point would strengthen the claim about generalization and coordination based on abstract properties.
*   The SVD analysis is an interesting method for measuring vocabulary complexity. Could the interpretation of the results be expanded upon slightly? For instance, what is the significance of the language requiring around 50 dimensions, and how does this relate to the number of distinct concepts or semantic features in the dataset?
