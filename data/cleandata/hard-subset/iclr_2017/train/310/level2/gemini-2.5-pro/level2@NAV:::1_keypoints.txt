- Presents a multi-agent framework for language learning that moves beyond passive learning from static text corpora.  
- Core contribution: two neural agents (sender and receiver) develop a communication protocol from scratch to solve a referential game.  
- Shows that modifying the environment (e.g., removing low-level visual common knowledge) nudges emergent language toward more abstract, human-like semantics.  
- Introduces a novel grounding strategy by combining the interactive game with supervised image-labeling to align emergent language with natural language.  
- Strongly recommends acceptance.  
- Key strength 1: addresses a fundamental, timely problem with a well-motivated and elegant approach.  
- Key strength 2: claims are supported by a sequence of well-designed experiments that build on one another.  
- Experimental setup is a major strength: demonstrates coordination and analyzes emergent language using metrics like vocabulary usage and semantic purity.  
- Second experiment forces coordination on object-level properties, providing evidence that environment shapes emergent semantics.  
- Final grounding experiment is impactful: humans interpret grounded agent language with 68% accuracy, including out-of-domain images.  
- Suggestion: acknowledge limitations of using a single-symbol vocabulary and speculate on extending to compositional language.  
- Suggestion: discuss scalability to more complex tasks or environments.  
- Clarifying question 1: How was the temperature parameter (τ=10) for the Gibbs distributions selected, and how sensitive are results to this hyperparameter?  
- Clarifying question 2: How was the subset of 100 labels for supervised training chosen, and could this selection bias affect generalization in the human study?
