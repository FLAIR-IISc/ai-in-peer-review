### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication, positioning it as an alternative to the dominant paradigm of passive learning from large text corpora. The authors propose using referential games, a variant of Lewis's signaling games, where two agents (a sender and a receiver) must develop a shared communication protocol to successfully identify a target image from a pair. The paper makes three primary contributions. First, it demonstrates that simple feed-forward neural networks, acting as tabula rasa agents, can successfully learn to communicate and coordinate in this game using a discrete vocabulary. Second, it investigates methods to shape the emergent language to be more semantically meaningful, showing that architectural biases (an "informed" sender) and modifications to the game (forcing generalization across different instances of the same object class) lead to communication protocols with higher semantic purity. Third, and most significantly, the paper presents a novel strategy for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task, the sender learns to use symbols that are not only effective for the game but also interpretable by humans, showing impressive generalization to novel objects through semantic extension (e.g., metonymy).

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-written and thought-provoking paper that addresses a fundamental question in AI: how can agents learn to communicate in a functional, grounded, and interactive way? My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Compelling Framework and Strong Motivation:** The paper provides a clear and compelling argument for moving beyond passive language learning. By framing language acquisition as a problem of multi-agent coordination, it connects deep learning with a rich history of research in game theory, cognitive science, and linguistics. This interdisciplinary grounding gives the work a solid conceptual foundation.
2.  **Novel and Significant Experimental Results:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's claims. The demonstration that agents can develop a communication protocol is a solid first step. However, the final set of experiments on grounding the emergent language is the most impactful contribution. Showing that a hybrid approach (interactive game + supervised task) leads to a protocol that is human-interpretable, even when generalizing to unseen object categories, is a significant and novel result that opens up a promising avenue for future research.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths are numerous. The introduction clearly situates the work within the literature, effectively contrasting the proposed interactive paradigm with supervised, human-in-the-loop, and scripted environment approaches. The connection to Lewis signaling games and "cheap talk" from game theory is particularly valuable, as it provides a formal lens through which to understand the problem of emergent communication.

The experimental section is methodical and convincing. The initial results in Section 4 successfully establish the baseline: agents can indeed learn to communicate. The authors go beyond simple accuracy metrics, using cluster purity and SVD analysis to probe the semantic structure of the emergent language. This analysis, which suggests the "informed" sender develops a richer and more semantically coherent protocol, is a good example of the paper's thoughtful experimental design. The "object-level reference" experiment is a clever and simple modification to the game environment that successfully encourages agents to learn more abstract representations, as evidenced by the increase in semantic purity.

The paper's most significant contribution lies in Section 5. The idea of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning, inspired by work like AlphaGo, is elegantly applied to the problem of grounding language. The results are striking. Not only does this hybrid training dramatically improve semantic purity, but it also leads to a language that humans can understand. The final human evaluation on the ReferItGame dataset is the highlight of the paper. Demonstrating that humans can correctly interpret the agent's messages with 68% accuracy, even when the agent is referring to objects it was not explicitly trained to name, is a powerful demonstration of generalized, grounded communication. The qualitative examples (e.g., "dolphin" for a picture of the sea) are fascinating and suggest that the agents are learning flexible, human-like semantic associations.

### Additional Feedback

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Agent Architectures:** The description of the "informed sender" architecture is a bit dense. While the intuition is clear (encouraging dimension-wise comparison), the mechanics of the 1-D convolutions could be explained more clearly, perhaps with a more detailed diagram.
*   **REINFORCE Details:** The training uses the REINFORCE algorithm, which is notoriously high-variance. It would be helpful to know if the authors encountered stability issues and if any variance reduction techniques (e.g., using a baseline) were employed. A brief mention of this would strengthen the reproducibility of the work.
*   **Vocabulary Size:** The paper explores vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100. It would be interesting to see a brief discussion on the effect of vocabulary size. Does a larger vocabulary always lead to a more expressive or semantically pure language, or is there a point of diminishing returns?
*   **Path to Compositionality:** The current communication protocol is limited to a single symbol per turn. While this is a perfectly reasonable starting point, the work would be even more forward-looking with a brief discussion in the conclusion about potential paths toward developing more complex, compositional language (i.e., sequences of symbols).

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the architectural choice for the "informed sender"? What was the specific hypothesis behind using 1-D convolutions on the concatenated embeddings in this manner, as opposed to other ways of encouraging comparison?
2.  In the grounding experiment (Section 5), what is the sensitivity of the results to the ratio of interactive game-playing vs. supervised classification trials? Is there a sweet spot, or does more supervision always lead to better grounding at the potential cost of in-game performance?
3.  The roles of sender and receiver are fixed throughout training. Have you considered experiments where agents switch roles? Do you hypothesize this would lead to a more robust or different kind of communication protocol?
