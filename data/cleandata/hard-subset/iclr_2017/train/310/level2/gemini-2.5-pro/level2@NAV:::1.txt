This paper presents a compelling framework for language learning through multi-agent communication, moving beyond passive learning from static text corpora. The core contribution is demonstrating that two neural agents, a sender and a receiver, can successfully develop a communication protocol from scratch to solve a referential game. The authors further show that by modifying the game's environment (e.g., removing low-level visual common knowledge), the emergent language can be nudged towards more abstract, human-like semantics. Finally, they introduce a novel and effective strategy for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. My decision is based on two key strengths: 1) The work addresses a fundamental and timely problem in AI—how to develop functionally communicative agents—with a well-motivated and elegant approach. 2) The claims are supported by a series of well-designed experiments that progressively build on one another, culminating in a convincing human evaluation.

The paper’s experimental setup is a major strength. The authors start by demonstrating that agents can learn to coordinate successfully, and they thoughtfully analyze the properties of the emergent language using metrics like vocabulary usage and semantic purity. The subsequent experiment, which forces agents to coordinate on object-level properties rather than specific image features, provides strong evidence for their claim that the environment shapes the emergent semantics. The final experiment, which combines game-playing with supervised learning, is particularly impactful. The demonstration that humans can interpret the agent's grounded language with 68% accuracy, even on out-of-domain images, is a significant result that validates the practical potential of this research direction.

To further strengthen the paper, I offer the following feedback. The discussion could benefit from acknowledging the limitations of using a single-symbol vocabulary and briefly speculating on how this framework might be extended to compositional language. Additionally, while the results are impressive, the analysis could touch upon the scalability of this approach to more complex tasks or environments.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:
1.  How was the temperature parameter (τ=10) for the Gibbs distributions selected? How sensitive are the learning dynamics and the properties of the emergent language to this hyperparameter?
2.  In the final grounding experiment, how was the subset of 100 labels for supervised training chosen? Could this choice introduce a bias that affects the generalization results observed in the human study?
