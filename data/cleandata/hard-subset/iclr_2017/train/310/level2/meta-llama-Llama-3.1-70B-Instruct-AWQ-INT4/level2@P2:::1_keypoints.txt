- Proposes a multi-agent framework where agents develop an emergent language via referential games (sender/receiver identify target image).  
- Demonstrates that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate in the referential game.  
- Shows emergent language interpretability can be improved by modifying the game environment.  
- Introduces grounding of emergent code into natural language via a supervised image-labeling task.  
- Decision: accept with minor revisions.  
- Strength: well-motivated, grounded in linguistics/cognitive science/game theory, and placed in related literature.  
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation of framework, experimental setup, and results.  
- Strength: thorough analysis of agent performance, semantic properties of emergent language, and environment effects.  
- Strength: paper is well-written and free of major errors.  
- Minor issue: lacks a detailed discussion of framework limitations and future research directions.  
- Minor issue: missing information about computational resources and algorithm time complexity.  
- Minor issue: could include more visualizations of emergent language (e.g., symbol usage plots, embedding heatmaps).  
- Minor issue: needs more details on the supervised labeling task used for grounding.  
- Question: how will the framework extend to more complex language tasks like dialogue or text generation?  
- Question: how robust is the emergent language to changes in game environment or agent architectures?  
- Question: how will interpretability be evaluated in more complex scenarios involving abstract concepts or entities?
