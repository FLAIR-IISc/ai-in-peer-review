**Summary of the Paper**

The authors propose a framework for language learning through multi-agent communication, where agents develop their own language interactively out of the need to communicate. They study this learning in the context of referential games, where a sender and a receiver see a pair of images and the sender must send a message to the receiver to identify the target image. The authors show that two networks with simple configurations can learn to coordinate in the referential game and that the emergent language can be made more interpretable by humans by modifying the game environment. They also present a simple strategy for grounding the agents' code into natural language by combining the game with a supervised image labeling task.

**Decision**

I decide to accept this paper with minor revisions.

**Reasons for the Decision**

The paper tackles a specific and well-defined problem of developing a framework for language learning through multi-agent communication. The approach is well-motivated and placed in the literature, drawing on ideas from linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory. The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation of the framework, experimental setup, and results. The authors demonstrate that the proposed framework can learn to coordinate in the referential game and that the emergent language can be made more interpretable by humans.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the experimental results, including the performance of the agents in the referential game, the semantic properties of the emergent language, and the effect of modifying the game environment on the language. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the framework and its components, making it easy to follow and understand. The paper is well-written and free of major errors.

**Minor Issues and Suggestions for Improvement**

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed framework and potential avenues for future research.
2. The authors could provide more information about the computational resources required to run the experiments and the time complexity of the algorithms used.
3. The paper could include more visualizations of the emergent language, such as plots of the symbol usage or heatmaps of the image embeddings.
4. The authors could provide more details about the supervised image labeling task used to ground the agents' code into natural language.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the proposed framework to more complex language tasks, such as dialogue or text generation?
2. Can the authors provide more information about the robustness of the emergent language to changes in the game environment or the agent architectures?
3. How do the authors plan to evaluate the interpretability of the emergent language in more complex scenarios, such as when the agents are communicating about abstract concepts or entities?
