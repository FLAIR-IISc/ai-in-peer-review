**Summary**

The paper proposes a novel framework for language learning that relies on multi-agent communication, specifically in the context of referential games. The authors introduce a game-based paradigm where agents develop their own language interactively, without relying on pre-coded protocols or annotated data. The paper demonstrates that simple neural-network agents can learn to coordinate in a referential game, and that the meanings they assign to symbols capture general conceptual properties of objects. The authors also explore ways to make the emergent language more interpretable and show a path to grounding the communication in natural language by mixing the game with a supervised task.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper, with the following key reasons:

1. **Originality**: The paper proposes a novel framework for language learning that is distinct from traditional supervised approaches. The use of multi-agent communication and referential games is a fresh perspective on the problem.
2. **Technical Soundness**: The paper demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying technical concepts, including reinforcement learning, neural networks, and game theory. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Clear Problem Statement**: The paper clearly articulates the problem of developing conversational agents that can communicate with humans productively. The authors identify the limitations of traditional supervised approaches and propose a new direction.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach**: The paper provides a thorough review of related work in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying principles and justify their approach.
3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The paper presents a range of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results are convincing, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the findings.
4. **Interpretability**: The paper explores ways to make the emergent language more interpretable, which is an essential aspect of developing conversational agents that can communicate with humans.
