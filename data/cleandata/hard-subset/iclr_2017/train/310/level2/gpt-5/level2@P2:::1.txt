Summary
The paper proposes a simple yet compelling framework for learning to communicate via cooperative multi-agent referential games. Two neural agents (sender/receiver), starting tabula rasa and communicating with a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary, learn to coordinate to identify target images from pairs drawn from a large set of natural images (ImageNet). The paper contributes: (i) evidence that agents reliably and quickly develop a communication protocol with discrete messages via REINFORCE; (ii) analysis showing that the induced “word meanings” correlate with intuitive, high-level semantic properties (e.g., living vs. non-living, category structure), with stronger alignment for an “informed sender” architecture; (iii) an environmental manipulation (object-level reference with different instance views per agent) that nudges the protocol toward higher-level semantics; and (iv) a simple grounding strategy that mixes self-play with a supervised image-labeling task, improving interpretability and enabling partial human–agent communication on a separate dataset (68% human accuracy vs ~50% chance).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, timely, and novel empirical demonstration that discrete communication can emerge among neural agents on realistic visual inputs and can be steered toward human-interpretable semantics; (2) A simple, scalable grounding mechanism that measurably improves interpretability and yields promising human-in-the-loop results.

Supporting arguments
- Quality and correctness: The training setup is standard for cooperative RL (REINFORCE), and convergence to near-perfect coordination is consistently reported across architectures and vocabulary sizes. The semantic analyses (category purity vs. chance, t-SNE visualizations, SVD suggesting nontrivial symbol usage) support the claim that emergent symbols correspond to high-level concepts rather than low-level visual quirks. The object-level manipulation is principled (reducing common knowledge of low-level details) and yields the expected increase in semantic alignment. The grounding experiment is especially convincing: supervised name learning improves symbol purity (to ~70%), and in a transfer setting (ReferIt images without the supervised labels present), humans can still recover the target 68% of the time given the agent’s word, with proper statistical testing. These results collectively substantiate the core claims.
- Novelty and relation to prior work: Relative to prior emergent communication works (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), this paper (a) operates on a large, noisy set of natural images; (b) enforces a discrete bottleneck via sampling rather than differentiable continuous channels; (c) studies architectural inductive bias (informed vs. agnostic sender); and (d) focuses on controllability and interpretability of the emergent code, including human evaluation. The positioning with respect to game-theoretic signaling literature and cognitive/linguistic work is apt and strengthens the motivation.
- Significance: The simplicity and robustness of the setup make it a strong foundation for scaling to richer languages and tasks. The demonstrated steering of emergent language toward human semantics and the successful human evaluation are both important steps toward interactive agents that can communicate with people.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines for “semantic purity”: Include unsupervised baselines (e.g., k-means clustering over VGG-fc features with k = |V|; nearest-neighbor label assignment) to quantify how much the learned code improves over purely visual structure. Also report purity variance over random seeds.
- Evaluation clarity: Please specify whether test-time choices are sampled or argmax for sender/receiver; report exact communication accuracies with confidence intervals. For purity, provide mean ± std over multiple runs and random permutations. For SVD, describe the cross-validation criterion and sensitivity to sample size.
- RL details: Indicate whether you use a variance-reduction baseline, entropy regularization, or reward normalization. REINFORCE can be high-variance; clarifying these choices will aid reproducibility. Analyze sensitivity to τ and vocabulary size beyond 10/100.
- Data splits and leakage: Clarify how training/test games are constructed relative to the pool of images. Are images shared across train/test games or disjoint? Consider reporting generalization to unseen concepts or unseen instances (if not already ensured).
- Architectural analysis: The “informed sender” bias is interesting; ablate its components (e.g., simple bilinear/dot-comparison layers) to isolate what drives faster convergence and richer symbol usage. Report symbol entropy and usage distribution to complement SVD.
- Synonymy/homonymy: Go beyond spectral analysis to quantify the extent to which symbols are many-to-one (synonymy) or one-to-many (polysemy) with respect to object classes/categories. A confusion matrix between symbols and categories would help.
- Human study details: Report inter-annotator agreement and per-word performance (e.g., do certain grounded words generalize better via metonymy?). Provide examples where humans systematically fail—this can inform future grounding strategies.
- Extend object-level manipulation: Increase distractor similarity (e.g., same supercategory) to pressure finer-grained semantics; analyze whether symbols specialize hierarchically.
- References and presentation: The reference list has formatting artifacts and some missing details; please correct. Consider releasing code and preprocessed splits to facilitate follow-up work.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact test-time decoding policy (sampling vs. argmax) for sender and receiver when reporting coordination success and purity?
- Do you employ any variance-reduction techniques (baselines, entropy bonuses)? How sensitive are results to τ and to the reward estimator?
- How many random seeds were run per condition? What is the variance in coordination accuracy and purity across seeds?
- Are training and test images disjoint at the instance level? If not, can you report performance on a disjoint test set and/or on unseen object classes?
- How does performance and symbol interpretability change when distractors are semantically similar to targets (e.g., fine-grained breeds within animals)?
- Can you provide a baseline purity for clustering VGG-fc features directly (no game), to calibrate the gains from communication?
- In the grounded setup, how often does the sender use supervised labels when they are present vs. when absent? Does the sender develop systematic metonymic mappings (e.g., “dolphin”→water) that could be characterized more formally?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, carefully executed, and impactful study that pushes emergent communication toward human-interpretable language and human interaction. I recommend acceptance.
