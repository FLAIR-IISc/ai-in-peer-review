Summary and contributions: The paper proposes a multi-agent framework for language learning via referential games, demonstrating that two tabula rasa neural networks can develop a discrete communication protocol that enables near-perfect coordination over real images. Beyond showing that communication emerges, the paper claims two main contributions: (1) environmental manipulations (e.g., removing low-level common knowledge by showing different exemplars of the same class to sender/receiver) bias the emergent code toward higher-level, human-like semantics; (2) a simple grounding strategy—interleaving self-play with supervised image labeling—aligns symbols with natural language and yields measurable human interpretability in a crowdsourced task. Empirically, the authors report fast convergence, increased symbol purity with informed architectural bias and object-level reference, and 68% human accuracy (vs. 50% chance) in interpreting the sender’s symbols on a distinct dataset, suggesting partial portability to human partners.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) Novel and well-motivated focus on aligning emergent protocols with human-understandable semantics, going beyond prior work on neural communication to include environmental design and human evaluation; (2) Empirical evidence is convincing across multiple setups (architectures, vocab sizes, domain shift), including a human study.

Supporting arguments: The problem—how to induce interactive, portable, and interpretable language— is clearly posed and situated with respect to multi-agent RL and emergent communication literature (e.g., Sukhbaatar, Foerster) as well as linguistic/game-theoretic perspectives. Claims are supported with statistically relevant signals: coordination accuracy, clustering purity against McRae categories, SVD spectra to probe synonymy, t-SNE visualizations, and a preregistered-style human crowdsourcing test with mixed-effects analysis (z=16.75, p<0.0001). The supervised grounding experiment is a simple yet impactful idea that substantially improves interpretability without harming coordination. While the setting is a simplified one-shot, single-symbol game leveraging pretrained visual features, the insights about environment design and grounding are practically useful and likely to transfer to richer dialog setups.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper, not central to the decision):
- Report variability across random seeds and include confidence intervals for purity and human accuracy.
- Provide stronger baselines (e.g., non-communicative similarity-based receiver; frozen random sender; hand-crafted visual features) and cross-play between independently trained agents.
- Analyze topographic similarity/mutual information between visual/category distances and symbol space; test context-independence and compositionality.
- Clarify RL details (variance reduction, entropy regularization, temperature schedule) and ablations on vocabulary size, bottleneck temperature, and feature layers.
- Assess zero-shot generalization to new partners and multi-round/sequence communication.

Questions for authors:
- How stable are purity and human interpretability across seeds and vocab sizes?
- Does the informed sender’s advantage persist with larger vocabularies and deeper networks?
- In the grounding setup, how is leakage controlled between supervised labels and game images? Any category-wise breakdown of human accuracy?
- Can independently trained agents successfully cross-play, and how does supervision affect cross-play?
- What failure modes did the human study reveal (e.g., word-frequency biases, visually salient distractors)?
