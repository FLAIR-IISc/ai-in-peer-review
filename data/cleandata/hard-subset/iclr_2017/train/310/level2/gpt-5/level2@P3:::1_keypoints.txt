- Paper studies emergent communication in referential games with real images using discrete symbols and REINFORCE-trained sender/receiver agents.  
- Main findings: feed-forward agents reach near-perfect communication; an “informed” sender learns faster and uses richer vocabulary.  
- Induced symbol–referent mappings show semantic structure beyond low-level vision (category purity, t-SNE, SVD probe).  
- Modifying common knowledge (different instance images per class) pushes codes toward higher-level object semantics.  
- Mixing self-play with supervised labeling partially grounds symbols in natural language and yields 68% human recoverability in a crowd study.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Demonstrates on realistic images that simple design choices can steer emergent codes toward semantically meaningful and partly human-interpretable protocols.  
- Key reason 2: Framework is clear, lightweight, and reproducible, useful for further work on aligning protocols with human language.  
- Motivation: Moving from passive supervised learning to interactive, goal-driven communication is timely; referential game is a clean testbed.  
- Positioning: Well situated relative to prior emergent-communication and signaling-game work; uses large-scale natural images and human evaluation as differentiators.  
- Methodology: Policies trained with REINFORCE, discrete symbols sampled, simple architectures; informed sender’s inductive bias is reasonable and beneficial.  
- Evidence — semantic structure: Symbol category purity consistently above chance, living vs non-living split emerges with two symbols; qualitative t-SNE supports findings.  
- Evidence — steering semantics: Object-level reference variant increases symbol–category purity, aligning with common-knowledge theory.  
- Evidence — grounding/human interpretability: Mixed supervised/self-play raises purity (~70%) and humans recover targets 68% on ReferIt transfer with metonymic uses observed.  
- Limitation: Heavy reliance on pretrained VGG features means agents are not tabula rasa; visual priors likely facilitate high-level category emergence.  
- Limitation: Semantic evaluation relies mostly on purity and visualization; more rigorous metrics and controlled generalization tests are needed.  
- Limitation: Human study is conditioned on successful agent trials and samples few items per symbol, introducing potential selection bias.  
- Limitation: Stability and robustness across random seeds and partner swaps are not reported.  
- Essential: Clarify and quantify the role of pretrained features; compare sm vs fc features and include ablations with shallower or random encoders if possible.  
- Essential: Provide rigorous lexicon–semantics analyses (mutual information with categories/ImageNet classes, compare to k-means baselines, confidence intervals over runs).  
- Essential: Report variance across seeds and partner-swap experiments to assess robustness and learnability of codes by new agents.  
- Essential: Clarify human study conditioning, report unconditioned results, per-word/image difficulty, and inter-annotator agreement.  
- Nice-to-have: Explore symbol sequences or multi-round interactions to probe compositionality.  
- Nice-to-have: Quantify synonymy/homonymy and complement SVD with entropy/type–token statistics.  
- Nice-to-have: Test vocabulary sizes beyond {10,100} and effect of temperature τ on convergence/diversity.  
- Nice-to-have: Report generalization to unseen classes and different distractor distributions.  
- Question: How stable are symbol–meaning mappings across seeds — do symbols align up to permutation or drift?  
- Question: Was the human study conditioned on successful receiver choices, and how would unconditioned sampling affect results?  
- Question: Can a freshly initialized receiver learn a fixed sender’s code quickly (partner swap), and vice versa?  
- Question: Quantitatively, how do sm vs fc features differ in purity, symbol usage, and convergence speed?  
- Question: Does the informed sender’s dimension-wise comparison limit generalization or cause overfitting to feature-space idiosyncrasies?  
- Question: How does performance and purity scale with vocabulary size beyond 100, and is there evidence of compositional reuse vs one-to-one labeling?  
- Question: Are there zero-shot tests (new ImageNet classes or out-of-domain images) to evaluate transferable semantics?
