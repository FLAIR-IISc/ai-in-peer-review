Summary
The paper investigates emergent communication in multi-agent referential games using real images. Two neural agents (sender/receiver) learn to communicate via a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary so the receiver can identify a target image among distractors. The work shows: (a) simple feed-forward agents trained with REINFORCE reliably converge to near-perfect communication; (b) an “informed” sender architecture with an inductive bias toward dimension-wise comparison learns faster and uses a richer vocabulary; (c) the induced symbol–referent associations exhibit semantic structure beyond low-level vision, as evidenced by category purity and qualitative analyses; (d) modifying the game to remove low-level common knowledge (showing different instance images per class to the two agents) nudges the code toward higher-level object semantics; and (e) mixing interactive self-play with supervised image labeling partially grounds the emergent code in natural language, enabling limited human interpretability in a crowd study (68% success when humans act as receivers).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The paper demonstrates, on realistic visual inputs, that emergent communication can be steered toward semantically meaningful and partly human-interpretable codes via simple environmental and training design. (2) The framework is clear, lightweight, and reproducible enough to catalyze further work on aligning emergent protocols with human language.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The shift from passive supervised learning to interactive, goal-driven communication is timely and well motivated. The referential game is a clean testbed for studying how semantics can emerge from coordination pressure and how environmental design impacts interpretability.
- Positioning: The paper is well placed relative to multi-agent communication without pre-coded protocols (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) and to linguistic/game-theoretic signaling games. The key differentiators are the use of large-scale natural images, explicit interventions to promote human-aligned semantics, and a human evaluation of interpretability.
- Methodological soundness: Policies are trained with REINFORCE, discrete symbols are sampled, and architectures are simple. The “informed” sender’s inductive bias is reasonable and empirically helpful. Results show rapid convergence and high test accuracy across settings.
- Evidence for claims: 
  • Semantic structure: Category purity of symbol usage is consistently above chance (including with only two symbols, where a living vs. non-living split emerges), and t-SNE visualizations corroborate alignment between symbol use and high-level visual similarity. While t-SNE is qualitative, the purity analysis and SVD-based redundancy probe provide additional signal. 
  • Steering semantics: The object-level reference variant (different instance images per class per agent) increases purity, consistent with the common-knowledge argument.
  • Grounding and human interpretability: The mixed supervised/self-play setup increases symbol purity (to 70%) and reuses conventional labels in the game. When transferring to ReferIt bounding boxes, human participants recover the target 68% of the time given the emitted label, significantly above chance, with compelling qualitative metonymic uses.
- Limitations:
  • Heavy reliance on pretrained VGG features means agents are not “tabula rasa” with respect to visual semantics; this likely facilitates the emergence of high-level categories. 
  • Semantic evaluation is mostly purity and visualization; more rigorous metrics (e.g., mutual information, V-measure) and controlled generalization tests would strengthen claims.
  • The human study conditions on successful agent trials and samples few items per symbol; potential selection bias should be addressed.
  • Stability and robustness across random seeds and partners are not reported.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
Essential:
- Clarify the role of pretrained features. Report results with sm vs fc features more systematically, and discuss how much of the observed semantic clustering could be inherited from the CNN. If feasible, include an ablation with shallower or randomly initialized visual encoders to quantify dependence on visual priors.
- Provide more rigorous lexicon–semantics analyses: report mutual information between symbols and (a) coarse categories, (b) ImageNet classes; compare purity to strong baselines (e.g., k-means over fc features). Include confidence intervals over multiple runs.
- Address partner- and seed-robustness: show variance over seeds; evaluate “partner swap” (freeze a trained sender, reinitialize a receiver) to test whether the code is learnable by new agents and thus more human-like.
- Human study methodology: state whether trials were conditioned on correct agent communication; if so, add an unconditioned sample or justify the conditioning. Report per-word and per-image difficulty, and inter-annotator agreement.

Nice-to-have (optional):
- Explore sequences of symbols or multi-round interaction to assess compositionality, even briefly.
- Quantify synonymy/homonymy with clustering of symbol usage and type–token statistics; complement SVD with entropy measures.
- Analyze vocabulary size sensitivity beyond {10,100}, and the effect of temperature τ on convergence and diversity.
- Report generalization to unseen object classes and to new distractor distributions.

Questions for clarification
- How stable are the emergent symbol–meaning mappings across random seeds? Do symbols align (up to permutation) or drift substantially?
- In the human study, were the evaluated pairs preconditioned on successful receiver choices? If yes, how does performance change when sampling all emitted-symbol pairs uniformly?
- What is the effect of partner swap: can a freshly initialized receiver learn to interpret a fixed trained sender’s code quickly? Conversely, can a new sender adapt to a fixed receiver?
- How do results differ quantitatively between sm and fc features in terms of purity, symbol usage, and convergence speed?
- Does the informed sender’s dimension-wise comparison materially limit generalization or promote overfitting to idiosyncrasies of the feature space?
- How does performance and purity scale with vocabulary size beyond 100, and is there evidence of emergent compositional reuse versus one-to-one label allocation?
- Are there zero-shot generalization tests (new ImageNet classes or out-of-domain images) to assess whether symbols capture transferable semantics?

Overall, the work is a timely and solid step toward interactive, human-aligned emergent communication, with clear contributions and promising preliminary evidence of grounding. Strengthening the quantitative semantic evaluation and addressing dependence on visual pretraining would make the case even more compelling.
