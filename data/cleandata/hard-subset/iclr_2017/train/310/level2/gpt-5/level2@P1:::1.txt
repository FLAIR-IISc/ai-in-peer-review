Summary and contributions
The paper investigates how grounded communication can emerge between neural agents through interactive multi-agent referential games, and how the emergent code can be nudged toward human-interpretable semantics. Two simple sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed with a dimension-wise comparison bias) and a receiver learn, from scratch, to solve a one-symbol referential game over real images drawn from ImageNet classes (via VGG features). The study makes three contributions: (i) it demonstrates robust emergence of communication without pre-coded protocols or supervision; (ii) it analyzes the semantics of the emergent symbols, showing that—especially under an “object-level” variant that removes low-level common knowledge—symbols align with intuitive category structure (e.g., living vs. non-living, broader semantic categories), and (iii) it proposes a simple grounding strategy by jointly training the sender on supervised object labeling, leading to higher purity, direct symbol–label mappings, and partial human interpretability in a separate ReferItGame-based human study. The overall message is that both agent architecture and environment design matter for the naturalness and portability of the emergent code.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The work is timely and well-motivated, providing clear empirical evidence that interaction can yield meaningful communication and that environmental tweaks and light supervision improve semantic alignment. (2) The human evaluation showing nontrivial interpretability (68% accuracy) is valuable and rare for emergent communication papers. The insights about common knowledge and inductive biases are likely to influence follow-up research.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Training agents to communicate interactively rather than purely via passive supervised learning addresses an important gap for conversational AI and aligns with broader goals in multi-agent learning and grounding.
- Placement in literature: The work is well-positioned relative to multi-agent RL communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) and emergent language/game theory literature. Its distinctive angle is the focus on aligning emergent symbols with human-relevant semantics and demonstrating human interpretability.
- Methodological soundness: The referential game setup is clean; training with REINFORCE on discrete symbols is appropriate. The informed vs. agnostic sender comparison supports claims about inductive biases (informed sender converges faster, uses more symbols, yields higher semantic purity).
- Empirical support: Results indicate near-perfect coordination across settings, with analyses of symbol usage (SVD spectrum, cluster purity vs. McRae categories, t-SNE visualizations) supporting claims about semantic structure. The “object-level” variant convincingly manipulates common knowledge to promote abstract semantics. The mixed supervised/game training increases symbol purity and yields directly interpretable mappings; transfer to ReferIt human study provides external validation.
- Clarity and usefulness: The paper communicates a clear research program and provides actionable design insights (environmental constraints and light grounding both help).

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Reproducibility details: Please specify optimizer, learning rates, initialization, use of baselines/variance reduction in REINFORCE (e.g., learned baseline, entropy regularization), and the number of random seeds with mean/variance across runs. Some statements mix “10k” and “50k” training iterations; clarifying the exact training schedule per experiment would help.
- Evaluation breadth:
  - Report absolute numbers for coordination accuracy, symbol usage (vocabulary size actually used), and purity with confidence intervals across seeds.
  - Include a generalization test where target and distractor concepts were unseen during training to assess portability.
  - Assess “referential consistency”: does the sender use the same symbol for the same object class across diverse distractors?
  - Consider a baseline where the sender emits the top-1 ImageNet class from the softmax features; compare to emergent symbols’ purity and human interpretability.
- Architectural analysis: The informed sender’s 1-D convolution imposes a strong relational inductive bias. Adding ablations (e.g., MLP with explicit pairwise bilinear terms; Siamese encoders with difference/ratio features) could isolate which interactions matter. Also analyze sensitivity to the temperature τ and communication bottleneck strength.
- Beyond single-symbol messages: While the single-symbol game is a clean starting point, a short discussion about extending to multi-symbol sequences, role alternation, or negotiation would position the work for future compositionality studies.
- Human study details: Please report participant screening, inter-participant agreement, effect sizes, and examples of failure modes. A comparison to a text-only baseline (e.g., human guessing given the ImageNet label without seeing images first) could calibrate difficulty.
- Semantic analyses: In addition to purity wrt McRae categories, consider WordNet-based hierarchical metrics (e.g., information content, path similarity), or probing whether sender embeddings linearly separate semantic categories.

Questions for the authors
1. Training stability and variance: How consistent are convergence, symbol usage, and purity across random seeds? Do you use a baseline or entropy bonus to stabilize REINFORCE? What is the sample complexity to reach >95% accuracy?
2. Data splits and leakage: Are training and test games disjoint at the image level? For the object-level reference variant, do you ensure that the sender and receiver never see the same exact image within a game, and how often do they share the same class across train/test?
3. Purity computation: How many clusters (symbols) are considered when many symbols are unused? Do you normalize purity by effective symbol count, and can you report confidence intervals?
4. Synonymy vs. polysemy: Your SVD analysis suggests >1–2 dimensions. Can you quantify symbol entropy per object and object entropy per symbol to characterize synonymy/polysemy?
5. Grounding mechanism: When mixing supervised and game objectives, how sensitive are results to the proportion of supervised steps, the overlap of label sets, and whether the classifier head shares all vs. part of the sender embedding?
6. Human interpretability: In the ReferIt study, how were image pairs selected (balanced difficulty)? Did you analyze whether success correlates with semantic relatedness between the emitted label and target class (e.g., WordNet distance)?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and thoughtfully executed study that advances emergent communication toward human-interpretable grounding. The paper should be of broad interest to the ICLR community.
