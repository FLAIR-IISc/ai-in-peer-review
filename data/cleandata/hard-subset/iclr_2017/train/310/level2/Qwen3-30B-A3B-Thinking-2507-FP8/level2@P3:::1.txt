

The paper investigates the emergence of communication protocols through multi-agent referential games, addressing a key limitation of passive language learning by emphasizing interactive coordination. It demonstrates that simple neural networks can develop functional communication strategies without pre-defined linguistic structures, and introduces environmental modifications to steer emergent language toward human-semantic properties. The core contributions include: (1) establishing that agents converge to successful coordination using minimal architectures; (2) showing that environmental tweaks (e.g., removing low-level visual common knowledge) improve semantic alignment; and (3) proposing a grounding strategy via supervised image labeling that enhances human interpretability of the emergent language.

Accept. The work makes a substantive contribution to the emerging field of language emergence through multi-agent interaction, with strong empirical validation of its core claims. The paper’s novelty lies in its systematic exploration of *how* environmental design influences semantic properties of emergent language, moving beyond prior work that merely demonstrated communication emergence.

The paper excels in methodological rigor. It establishes clear baselines (agnostic vs. informed sender architectures), employs multiple validation approaches (semantic purity metrics, t-SNE visualizations, human evaluations), and addresses potential concerns about linguistic redundancy (via SVD analysis). The grounding experiment (Section 5) provides compelling evidence that supervised pre-training on natural language labels enables communication interpretable to humans (68% accuracy in human evaluation), directly advancing the goal of human-AI communication. The connection to game-theoretic foundations (Lewis signaling games, Crawford & Sobel) and cognitive science (McRae categories, Caramazza & Shelton) is well-integrated and strengthens the theoretical positioning.

Key strengths include the clear experimental progression: from basic coordination (Section 4) to semantic refinement (Section 4.1) to human grounding (Section 5). The human evaluation design is particularly robust—using ReferItGame for out-of-distribution testing and crowdsourcing for interpretability assessment—avoiding the scalability issues of prior human-in-the-loop approaches. The results are consistently significant (e.g., purity scores 37% above chance, 68% human accuracy) and align with theoretical expectations (e.g., agents converging on "living vs. non-living" with two symbols).

To further strengthen the paper, the authors should clarify two points. First, the paper states the informed sender "nests" the agnostic architecture but does not explicitly explain why the inductive bias (1D convolutions) leads to better semantic performance. A brief discussion of how this architectural choice facilitates dimension-wise image comparison would better justify the design choice. Second, the grounding experiment uses a 100-symbol vocabulary, yet the human evaluation only assesses 100 labels. It would be valuable to clarify whether the 100 labels were a random subset of ImageNet or selected for coverage of the McRae categories, as this affects the interpretation of the 47% direct label match rate.

Additionally, the paper could better contextualize the 68% human interpretability rate. While impressive, a comparison to baseline human performance on the ReferItGame task (e.g., "What word best describes this image?") would help assess whether the agents’ language is merely "good enough" or genuinely competitive with human usage. The authors note metonymic shifts (e.g., "dolphin" for sea), but a brief analysis of whether these shifts align with known linguistic phenomena (e.g., Pustejovsky’s generative lexicon) would deepen the theoretical contribution.

The paper’s focus on practical grounding—moving beyond "language" as a metric to human-interpretable communication—is timely and addresses a critical gap in the field. The proposed framework is scalable (no human intervention required during training) and leverages existing vision datasets effectively. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations (e.g., language initially being "very limited"), setting a clear path for future work on structural language properties.

Overall, the paper provides a rigorous, well-motivated foundation for developing communication-capable AI through multi-agent interaction, with tangible progress toward human-interpretable language. The experimental design and validation are thorough, and the grounding strategy offers a practical pathway to human-AI collaboration. The minor clarifications suggested would enhance the paper’s impact without requiring major revisions.
