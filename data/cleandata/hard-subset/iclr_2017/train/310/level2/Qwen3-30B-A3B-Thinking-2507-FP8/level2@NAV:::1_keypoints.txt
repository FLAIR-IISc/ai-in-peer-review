- Presents a multi-agent referential-game framework for language learning that yields human-interpretable communication.
- Shows simple neural networks can coordinate effectively in referential games across multiple architectures.
- Demonstrates emergent language captures meaningful, high-level semantic properties beyond low-level visual features.
- Introduces a grounding strategy that aligns agent communication with natural language via supervised learning.
- Argues this approach addresses the limitation of passive text-based NLP by enabling interactive language acquisition.
- Recommends acceptance, calling the contribution significant and well-motivated.
- Provides robust empirical evidence: coordination success (Table 1, Fig. 2) and semantic emergence (Table 1, Fig. 3).
- Shows grounding method makes communication interpretable to humans (Section 5, Fig. 4).
- Experimental design is sound with appropriate baselines, 50k training iterations, and thorough analyses.
- Uses purity metrics and t-SNE visualizations to analyze semantic properties.
- Suggests adding analysis of vocabulary size vs. semantic purity to clarify trade-offs.
- Recommends expanding limitations discussion to include potential ImageNet dataset biases.
- Advises a more thorough comparison with recent grounded language learning work to better contextualize contributions.
- Asks how results generalize to more complex referential games with multiple objects or complex scenes.
- Asks about computational costs and scalability of the grounding method versus traditional supervised approaches.
