

This paper proposes a multi-agent communication framework for language learning through referential games, where agents develop a shared language by coordinating to identify target images. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to communicate effectively (achieving near-perfect coordination), show that environmental modifications (using class-level properties instead of pixel-level details) improve semantic alignment of learned symbols, and present a grounding strategy using supervised image labeling to make the emergent language interpretable by humans (68% human accuracy in a crowdsourced task).  

Reject. The core claim that the agents develop language with "intuitive semantic properties" is overstated and not sufficiently substantiated. While the paper shows improved purity (70% vs. chance) after grounding, it fails to establish that this aligns with human semantic understanding. The purity metric (compared to random permutations) is misleading without human baselines (e.g., humans would achieve >90% purity on the same task). The grounding strategy merely maps symbols to pre-defined ImageNet labels, creating a circular dependency rather than true language grounding.  

The paper’s experimental support for "natural language" is critically weak. The semantic analysis (Section 4) claims agents use symbols reflecting "broad object categories," but the purity metric (Table 1) conflates meaningful semantic alignment with mere redundancy. For instance, the agnostic sender’s 2-symbol "living vs. non-living" strategy achieves purity significantly above chance (25.5% vs. 12.8% chance), yet this is a trivial, low-level distinction not representative of natural language. The t-SNE visualizations (Fig 3) show symbols grouping visually similar objects but with "quite a bit of variation," contradicting the paper’s claim of "semantically natural" symbols. Crucially, the paper omits key related work (e.g., Lazaridou & Baroni, 2016, which demonstrated emergent communication in similar games), undermining their "first steps" assertion.  

The grounding experiment (Section 5) is methodologically flawed: using only 100 ImageNet labels for a 100-symbol vocabulary means the sender’s "grounding" is trivially enforced by the label mapping. The 68% human interpretability rate is impressive but still far below human performance (which would approach 100% for labeled categories). The "metonymic" examples (e.g., "dolphin" for sea) reveal language that is *not* grounded in conventional semantics but relies on contextually ambiguous associations. The paper claims this "generalizes beyond supervised categories," yet the ReferItGame experiment (where 92% of cases used non-supervised categories) still achieved only 68% accuracy—far from the "productive" communication claimed in the abstract.  

**Constructive feedback:**  
1. **Clarify semantic alignment metrics:** Compare purity to human baselines (e.g., "Humans achieve 92% purity; agents achieve 70%").  
2. **Address related work:** Cite Lazaridou & Baroni (2016) and others to position contributions accurately.  
3. **Refine grounding claim:** Emphasize that grounding is achieved via label mapping, not semantic alignment. The "metonymic" examples suggest the language is *not* human-interpretable in the conventional sense.  
4. **Ablate the "informed sender":** Show whether the convolutional architecture is necessary (e.g., by comparing to a fully connected variant with identical capacity).  

**Questions for authors:**  
1. How does the 70% purity metric compare to human performance on the same clustering task (McRae categories)? Without this, the claim of "semantic properties" is unsubstantiated.  
2. In the ReferItGame experiment, why did the sender use *all* 100 symbols for non-supervised categories? Does this indicate overfitting to the supervised labels rather than genuine semantic learning?  
3. Why does the paper claim "word meanings" reflect "intuitive semantic properties" when the t-SNE plots (Fig 3) show significant symbol overlap across categories (e.g., "car" and "truck" sharing symbols)?  

The paper presents a technically sound experiment on emergent communication but overstates its contribution to "natural language" development. The human interpretability results are promising but do not validate the core claim of semantic alignment. With substantial revisions to address the metric misinterpretation and literature context, this work could be compelling. As-is, the overblown claims about language "resembling" human semantics prevent acceptance.
