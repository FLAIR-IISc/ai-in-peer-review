

This paper presents a compelling framework for language learning through multi-agent communication, specifically using referential games to develop semantic understanding and human-interpretable communication. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can successfully coordinate in referential games, develop language with meaningful semantic properties (beyond low-level visual features), and introduce a grounding strategy that aligns agent communication with natural language through supervised learning. The work addresses a critical gap in current NLP approaches by moving beyond passive text learning to interactive language acquisition.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by showing how to develop language that reflects intuitive semantic properties and by providing a practical method for grounding agent communication in natural language. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of supervised learning for communication and builds on a strong foundation of previous work in language emergence.

The evidence supporting the claims is robust. The authors demonstrate coordination success across multiple architectures (Table 1, Figure 2), show emergent language captures high-level semantic properties (Table 1, Figure 3), and provide compelling evidence that their grounding method makes communication interpretable to humans (Section 5, Figure 4). The experimental design is sound with appropriate baselines, sufficient training iterations (50k), and thorough analysis of semantic properties through purity metrics and t-SNE visualizations. The methodology clearly addresses the central question of how to develop portable, human-interpretable language through interactive learning.

To strengthen the paper, consider adding a more detailed analysis of the relationship between vocabulary size and semantic purity to better understand the trade-offs. The discussion of limitations could be expanded to include potential biases in the ImageNet dataset that might influence the semantic properties of the emergent language. Additionally, a more thorough comparison with the most recent related work (e.g., recent developments in grounded language learning) would further contextualize the contribution.

Questions for the authors: How might the results generalize to more complex referential games with multiple objects or more complex visual scenes? What are the computational costs of the grounding method compared to traditional supervised approaches, and how might this affect scalability for real-world applications?
