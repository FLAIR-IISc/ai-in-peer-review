

This paper presents a compelling exploration of language learning through multi-agent interaction in referential games, addressing a critical gap in current NLP approaches that rely on passive text exposure. The authors propose a framework where agents develop communication protocols through coordinated play, demonstrating that simple neural networks can learn to refer to visual concepts meaningfully. The work makes three key contributions: (1) establishing that agents converge to effective communication in referential games, (2) showing that emergent symbols capture high-level semantic properties (not just low-level visual features), and (3) introducing a grounded language approach that improves human interpretability. The paper is well-motivated, clearly situated within relevant literature on multi-agent communication, linguistic evolution, and reinforcement learning, and provides rigorous empirical validation.

I recommend acceptance. The core claim—that interactive multi-agent learning can produce semantically meaningful communication protocols—is strongly supported by the experiments. The semantic purity metrics (Table 1) demonstrate that agents' symbol usage aligns with human conceptual categories (e.g., "living vs. non-living" even with minimal vocabulary), and the grounding experiment (Section 5) shows a significant leap toward human-interpretable communication (68% human accuracy in guessing targets). These results are scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines, ablation studies (e.g., informed vs. agnostic sender), and careful analysis of emergent language properties.

The paper excels in its experimental design. The referential game setup is elegant and directly addresses the need for functional language learning (not just statistical pattern matching). The authors thoughtfully consider how environment design (Section 4.1) influences semantic emergence, and the grounding strategy (Section 5) provides a practical bridge to human language. The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) effectively illustrate the semantic alignment between symbols and visual concepts, while the human evaluation (Figure 4) offers compelling evidence of real-world applicability.

For further improvement, I suggest: (1) A deeper analysis of why the informed sender outperforms the agnostic sender—does its convolutional architecture specifically enable the semantic alignment observed? (2) More discussion of the computational cost of the multi-agent training versus supervised approaches, particularly regarding scalability to larger vocabularies or more complex environments. (3) Clarification on how the "living vs. non-living" categorization (with only two symbols) relates to known cognitive hierarchies (e.g., Caramazza & Shelton, 1998), as this finding is both surprising and potentially significant.

Key questions for the authors: 
1. In the minimal vocabulary case (2 symbols), the agents converge to a "living vs. non-living" split. How does this compare to theoretical predictions from game-theoretic models of language emergence (e.g., Crawford & Sobel, 1982), and why might this particular split emerge as optimal? 
2. The grounding approach relies on supervised image labeling for only 100 categories. How would the method scale if the target vocabulary expanded to cover all ImageNet classes, or if the training data lacked pre-specified labels? 
3. The human evaluation shows 68% accuracy when subjects interpret symbols for novel images (e.g., "dolphin" for sea scenes). What specific semantic mechanisms (e.g., metonymy, as noted in the paper) enable this generalization, and how might these be systematically improved?

This work makes a substantive contribution to the field, moving beyond "canned" language models toward interactive, function-driven communication systems. The results are both theoretically interesting (addressing convergence and semantic emergence in signaling games) and practically promising (demonstrating a path to human-compatible language). The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and makes a strong case for its research direction. I see no major flaws in the methodology or claims, and the results are robust across multiple experimental configurations. This is a valuable step toward the goal of developing conversational agents that communicate productively with humans.
