- Paper proposes a multi-agent referential game where simple neural nets learn to coordinate and communicate, achieving near-perfect task performance.  
- Environmental modification (class-level vs pixel-level inputs) improves semantic alignment of emergent symbols.  
- Grounding via supervised ImageNet labels yields 68% human interpretability in a crowdsourced task.  
- Recommendation: Reject — core claim that agents develop language with “intuitive semantic properties” is overstated and insufficiently supported.  
- Purity metric is misleading: comparison to random permutations is inadequate without human baselines.  
- Grounding is circular: mapping symbols to predefined ImageNet labels enforces interpretability rather than demonstrating true semantic grounding.  
- Semantic analysis is weak: high purity can reflect trivial distinctions (e.g., living vs. non-living) rather than natural-language-like categories.  
- t-SNE visualizations show substantial symbol overlap and variation, contradicting claims of semantically natural symbols.  
- Key related work (e.g., Lazaridou & Baroni 2016) is omitted, undermining novelty and positioning claims.  
- Grounding experiment is methodologically flawed: using 100 labels for a 100-symbol vocabulary trivially enforces mapping.  
- The 68% human accuracy, while promising, is far below expected human performance and does not validate conventional semantic alignment.  
- “Metonymic” examples (e.g., “dolphin” for sea) indicate contextual, ambiguous associations rather than conventional word meanings.  
- ReferItGame results do not convincingly show generalization: 92% non-supervised-category usage still yielded only 68% accuracy.  
- Evidence suggests possible overfitting to supervised labels (sender using all 100 symbols for non-supervised categories).  
- Constructive feedback: compare purity to human baselines to contextualize semantic claims.  
- Constructive feedback: cite and discuss relevant prior work (e.g., Lazaridou & Baroni 2016).  
- Constructive feedback: clarify that grounding is achieved via label mapping and analyze metonymic/ambiguous cases.  
- Constructive feedback: include ablation to test necessity of the convolutional “informed sender” vs. simpler architectures.  
- Questions for authors: How does 70% purity compare to human clustering performance?  
- Questions for authors: Why did the sender use all 100 symbols for non-supervised categories in ReferItGame—does this indicate overfitting?  
- Questions for authors: Why claim “intuitive semantic properties” when t-SNE shows substantial symbol overlap across related categories (e.g., car vs. truck)?  
- Overall: technically sound experiment but overstates contribution to natural language emergence; needs metric, grounding, and literature revisions to be compelling.
