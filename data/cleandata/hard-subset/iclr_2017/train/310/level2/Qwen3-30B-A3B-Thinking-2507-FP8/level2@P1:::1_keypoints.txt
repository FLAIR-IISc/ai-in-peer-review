- Presents a multi-agent referential game framework where agents learn communication protocols through interaction instead of passive text exposure.  
- Demonstrates simple neural networks can converge to effective communication in referential games.  
- Shows emergent symbols capture high-level semantic properties rather than only low-level visual features.  
- Introduces a grounded language approach that improves human interpretability (68% human guessing accuracy on novel images).  
- Strong empirical validation with appropriate baselines, ablation studies (informed vs. agnostic sender), and semantic purity metrics aligning symbols with human categories.  
- Elegant experimental design: environment design influences semantic emergence and the grounding strategy bridges to human language.  
- t-SNE visualizations and human evaluation convincingly illustrate semantic alignment and real-world applicability.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance; core claim is well supported and results are robust across configurations.  
- Suggested improvement: deeper analysis of why the informed sender outperforms the agnostic sender (role of convolutional architecture).  
- Suggested improvement: discuss computational cost and scalability of multi-agent training versus supervised approaches for larger vocabularies/complex environments.  
- Suggested improvement: clarify how the binary "living vs. non-living" split relates to known cognitive hierarchies (e.g., Caramazza & Shelton, 1998).  
- Key question: Why does a living vs. non-living split emerge with a 2-symbol vocabulary and how does this align with game-theoretic predictions (e.g., Crawford & Sobel)?  
- Key question: How would the grounding approach scale to all ImageNet classes or to training data without pre-specified labels?  
- Key question: What semantic mechanisms (e.g., metonymy) enable human generalization from symbols to novel images, and how can these be improved?  
- Overall: substantive contribution toward interactive, function-driven communication systems; clearly written with no major methodological flaws.
