- Paper studies emergence of communication in multi-agent referential games, emphasizing interactive coordination over passive learning.  
- Shows simple neural agents can develop functional communication without pre-defined linguistic structure.  
- Introduces environmental modifications (e.g., removing low-level visual common knowledge) to steer emergent language toward human-like semantics.  
- Core contributions: (1) agents converge to successful coordination with minimal architectures.  
- Core contributions: (2) environmental tweaks improve semantic alignment of emergent signals.  
- Core contributions: (3) supervised image-label grounding improves human interpretability of emergent language.  
- Strong empirical validation with clear baselines (agnostic vs. informed senders) and progressive experiments (coordination → semantic refinement → grounding).  
- Robust evaluation using semantic purity metrics, t-SNE visualizations, SVD analysis, and human evaluations.  
- Grounding experiment yields 68% human interpretability accuracy and 47% direct label match rate.  
- Results significant (e.g., purity scores ~37% above chance) and align with theoretical expectations (e.g., binary living/non-living split with two symbols).  
- Theoretical framing well-integrated with Lewis signaling games and cognitive science literature.  
- Human evaluation design is robust (ReferItGame for OOD testing, crowdsourcing for interpretability) and training is scalable without human-in-the-loop.  
- Requests clarification: why the informed sender’s 1D convolution inductive bias yields better semantic performance (mechanistic justification).  
- Requests clarification: how the 100-symbol vocabulary/labels were selected (random subset vs. McRae-category coverage), which affects interpreting the 47% match rate.  
- Suggests contextualizing the 68% interpretability by comparing to baseline human performance on similar description tasks.  
- Suggests brief analysis of metonymic shifts in emergent labels and relation to linguistic theories (e.g., generative lexicon).  
- Overall verdict: Accept — substantive, methodologically rigorous contribution that advances human-interpretable emergent communication with minor clarifications suggested.
