## Review of "A Framework for Language Learning Based on Multi-Agent Communication"

This paper proposes a multi-agent communication framework for language learning, motivated by the limitations of passive learning from large text corpora for developing truly interactive AI systems. The authors investigate this framework through referential games, where agents learn to coordinate by developing their own communication protocol. They demonstrate successful coordination with simple networks and explore methods to align the emergent “word meanings” with intuitive semantic properties and ground them in natural language.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The work tackles a crucial problem in AI – moving beyond statistical language modeling to genuine communicative competence – and presents a promising approach using a well-motivated multi-agent learning paradigm. The experimental results demonstrate that agents *can* learn to communicate effectively, and the exploration of environmental factors influencing semantic grounding is a valuable contribution.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, framework, and experimental setup. The results convincingly show that agents can learn to coordinate and that manipulating the game environment can influence the semantic properties of the emergent language. The grounding experiments, while preliminary, offer a compelling direction for future work, demonstrating a pathway towards human-interpretable communication. The connection to relevant literature in linguistics, game theory, and multi-agent systems is also well-established.

However, the paper could be strengthened by providing more detailed analysis of the emergent language. For example, what specific types of errors do the agents make during learning? A qualitative analysis of the learned symbol-image associations beyond purity scores would be insightful. Additionally, the impact of vocabulary size on learning dynamics could be explored further. It would also be helpful to discuss the computational cost of training and the scalability of the approach to more complex environments. 

Finally, could the authors elaborate on the choice of the VGG ConvNet features? How sensitive are the results to the choice of feature representation? What are the limitations of using pre-trained features, and how might these be addressed in future work?
