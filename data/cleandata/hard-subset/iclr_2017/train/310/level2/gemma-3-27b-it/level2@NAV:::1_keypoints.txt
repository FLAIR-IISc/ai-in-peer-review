- Proposes a novel framework for language learning using multi-agent referential communication games.  
- Shows simple neural networks can learn to coordinate in the game.  
- Emergent “word meanings” exhibit some semantic properties and partial grounding in human labels.  
- Presents an alternative, interaction-driven approach to language acquisition inspired by humans.  
- Recommends acceptance of the paper.  
- Core idea is innovative, well-motivated, and supported by promising initial experiments.  
- Grounding communication in natural language is an important and valuable direction.  
- Clearly articulates limitations of supervised learning for conversational agents and positions the framework as a remedy.  
- Experimental setup is well-defined, using a clear game structure and established image datasets.  
- Analysis employs SVD for vocabulary redundancy and purity measures for semantic alignment, yielding useful insights.  
- Authors acknowledge grounding is imperfect but view results as a positive step.  
- Needs more rigorous quantification of how environment modifications improve interpretability beyond purity scores.  
- Selection method for the 100 ImageNet labels is not described; potential selection bias and need for an ablation study.  
- Requests more qualitative examples of emergent communication, including specific “metonymic” links established by agents.
