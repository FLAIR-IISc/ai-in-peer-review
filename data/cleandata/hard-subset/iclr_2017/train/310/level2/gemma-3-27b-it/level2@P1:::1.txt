This paper tackles the problem of language emergence in multi-agent settings, specifically through referential games. The authors propose a framework where two agents learn to communicate from scratch to identify target images, and investigate how environmental changes can influence the resulting "language" towards more human-interpretable semantics. They also explore a method for grounding the learned communication in natural language via a supervised image labeling task.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the research direction is promising and the paper presents a solid experimental setup, the contributions feel incremental and the presented results, despite being positive, lack sufficient novelty and depth to warrant acceptance at ICLR. My primary concern lies with the limited insight gained beyond what has already been demonstrated in related work (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), and the lack of a compelling argument for the specific design choices made.

Here’s a more detailed breakdown:

The motivation for exploring language emergence through multi-agent games is well-established and clearly articulated. The introduction effectively frames the problem and highlights the limitations of purely supervised learning approaches. The experimental setup is also well-defined, utilizing standard architectures and datasets. The authors correctly identify the key challenge of making emergent communication interpretable, and their attempts to address this (varying game environments and grounding with supervised learning) are reasonable.

However, the core results – that agents *can* learn to coordinate and that modifying the environment influences semantic properties – are not particularly surprising, given the existing literature. The performance improvements achieved with the "informed sender" architecture are a positive step, but the explanation provided (dimension-by-dimension combination of images) feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a strong theoretical justification. Similarly, the observations regarding purity improvements with class-level properties are interesting, but don’t fundamentally change our understanding of emergent communication. 

The grounding experiment, while promising, feels somewhat tacked on and lacks sufficient analysis. While the 68% accuracy in human interpretation is encouraging, the experimental setup (CrowdFlower) isn’t thoroughly described, and it's unclear how robust this result is. The authors mention “metonymic” links, but further investigation and qualitative analysis of *why* this grounding succeeds (or fails) would be beneficial. The fact that the system successfully produces the correct label 47% of the time when the target belongs to the supervised category feels like a fairly standard supervised learning outcome and doesn't convincingly demonstrate true *grounding*.

Here are some additional points for improvement:

* **Novelty:** The authors should more clearly articulate what distinguishes their work from existing research. What specific aspect of their framework or analysis is genuinely novel? Simply demonstrating that agents can learn to communicate isn’t sufficient.
* **Ablation studies:** More detailed ablation studies are needed to understand the contribution of different components of the framework (e.g., the informed sender architecture, vocabulary size, temperature parameter).
* **Theoretical analysis:** While the paper focuses on empirical results, some theoretical analysis of the game dynamics or the emergence of communication protocols would be valuable.  For example, could the authors provide some insight into why the informed sender outperforms the agnostic sender?
* **Qualitative analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the emergent "language."  What kinds of symbols do the agents learn to use, and what do they appear to represent? Providing examples of successful and failed communication attempts would be helpful.
* **Comparison to baselines:** A comparison to other communication strategies or baseline models would strengthen the results.
* **Clarify experimental details:**  Provide more detail about the CrowdFlower experiment, including the instructions given to participants, the demographics of the participants, and the criteria used to evaluate their responses.



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  The informed sender uses convolutions with kernel size 2x1. Why this specific kernel size? Was this parameter tuned, or is there a theoretical justification for its choice?
2.  You mention that the informed sender uses more symbols. Does the performance of the informed sender degrade if the vocabulary size is reduced to match the agnostic sender?
3.  Can you provide more examples of the “metonymic” links observed in the grounding experiment?  What are some specific cases where the sender used a symbol to refer to an object that wasn’t directly represented in the supervised training data, and why do you think this worked?
4.  How sensitive is the human interpretability score to the choice of ImageNet labels used for supervised learning? Would the results be significantly different if a different set of labels were used?
5.  The paper cites Lewis’ signaling game. How does this work relate to the existing theoretical results on equilibrium selection in signaling games, particularly the possibility of multiple equilibria or non-convergence?
