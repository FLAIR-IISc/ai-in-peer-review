- Core idea strong: game-theoretic referential setup to bootstrap language from scratch is compelling and well-motivated.  
- Presentation and results: paper is clearly written and demonstrates promising initial results.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Grounding experiments: supervised grounding on ImageNet is a notable step toward human-understandable symbols.  
- Preliminary nature: results are promising but preliminary; robustness and generalizability need further investigation.  
- Experimental design strength: careful design and use of referential games (Lewis signaling game) provide a rigorous framework.  
- Emergent structure: agents learn to coordinate and produce symbols showing some semantic structure influenced by environment.  
- Architecture justification: sender/receiver network choices need more thorough justification and ablation over complexity/connectivity.  
- Quantitative analysis: semantic analysis should include stronger quantitative measures beyond current purity metrics.  
- Vocabulary scale: grounding experiments use a small vocabulary (100); scalability to larger vocabularies and complex scenes should be explored.  
- Metonymic analysis: human-evaluation of metonymic links needs more examples and a systematic investigation of cognitive mechanisms.  
- RL method question: clarify why Reinforce was used and whether alternative RL algorithms were considered.  
- Temperature sensitivity: report sensitivity analyses and tuning for the Gibbs temperature τ.  
- More symbol examples: provide additional emergent-symbol-to-interpretation examples beyond dolphin/sea and fence/land.  
- ReferItGame details: give a fuller description of the ReferItGame follow-up, including specific evaluation metrics.  
- Statistical testing: clarify whether the claim that supervised objective does not hurt communication is supported by statistical significance tests.  
- Human evaluation power: increase sample size (more than 10 ratings per pair) to obtain more robust human-evaluation results.  
- Pretraining for metonymy: explore whether pretraining agents on common metonymies improves performance.
