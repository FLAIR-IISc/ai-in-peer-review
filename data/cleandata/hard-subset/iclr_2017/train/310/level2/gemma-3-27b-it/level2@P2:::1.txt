This paper investigates language emergence in a multi-agent referential game, exploring how agents can learn to communicate and whether the resulting “language” reflects semantic properties of the images they are referencing. The authors present a framework where two agents, a sender and a receiver, interact to identify target images based on messages sent by the sender. They demonstrate successful coordination and explore the influence of environmental factors and a supervised grounding phase on the quality and interpretability of the emergent communication.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting and well-motivated line of research, and demonstrates promising initial results, I find the empirical evaluation and analysis insufficiently rigorous to support the strong claims made regarding semantic grounding and human interpretability. The core problem lies in the lack of clear baselines and controls, and the reliance on potentially biased human evaluations.

My primary concern revolves around the human evaluation presented in Section 5. While the 68% accuracy rate seems encouraging, the methodology is weak. The phrasing "subjects were able to guess the right image" is imprecise. Crucially, the experiment doesn’t control for the possibility of humans simply guessing the image based on the provided label, especially given the ImageNet labels are often descriptive. A control group shown the images *and* the ImageNet labels, asked to guess the target, is essential. Without this, it’s impossible to attribute the success rate to the emergent "language" rather than pre-existing knowledge encoded in the labels themselves. The authors acknowledge the possibility of “metonymic” links but don't provide any quantitative analysis or rigorous evidence for this phenomenon. The claim that the supervised learning leads to "some grounding" feels overly strong given this methodological weakness.

Secondly, the analysis of semantic properties in Sections 4 and 4.1 lacks depth. While showing that purity is above chance is a good starting point, it doesn't demonstrate meaningful semantic alignment.  The difference in purity between the "informed" and "agnostic" senders isn’t compellingly explained beyond architectural differences.  The t-SNE visualizations are suggestive but lack quantitative metrics. The authors state objects “are associated to the same symbol,” but don’t quantify this association or demonstrate statistical significance.  The claim that the informed sender achieves “more natural” semantics needs stronger support.

Finally, the connection to the existing literature, while broad, lacks critical engagement. The authors cite relevant work, but often without thoroughly comparing and contrasting their approach. For example, the connection to Lewis’ signaling game is stated, but the implications of differences (e.g., continuous vs. discrete signal space, realistic images vs. abstract states) are not fully explored.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Human Evaluation:** Implement a control group shown ImageNet labels alongside images to assess baseline guessing accuracy. Consider more rigorous evaluation metrics beyond simple accuracy (e.g., precision/recall for label agreement). Perform an analysis of the “metonymic” links; are there systematic patterns in the types of associations humans make?
*   **Semantic Analysis:** Develop quantitative metrics to assess semantic similarity between agent-generated symbols and human-defined categories. Explore alternative visualization techniques to the t-SNE plot that allow for more rigorous analysis. Perform statistical significance testing on the purity scores.
*   **Baselines:** Compare performance against simpler baseline strategies (e.g., random symbol selection, assigning symbols based on low-level image features). This will help establish the value of the proposed approach.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to investigate the contribution of different components of the architecture (e.g., convolutions in the informed sender).
*   **Discussion:** Strengthen the discussion section by addressing the limitations of the study and outlining clear directions for future research. Explore the implications of the findings for real-world applications.
*   **Clarification:** Clarify the meaning of "convergence" in the context of the referential game. What specific metric is used to determine convergence, and is it consistent across different experiments?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide the results of a control experiment where humans are shown images *and* the corresponding ImageNet labels and asked to identify the target image?
2.  What statistical tests were used to determine the significance of the purity scores?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, temperature)?
4.  Are there any systematic biases in the ImageNet dataset that might influence the emergent language?
5.  Can you provide more examples of the "metonymic" links observed in the human evaluation, and a quantitative analysis of their frequency?
