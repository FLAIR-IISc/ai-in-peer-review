- Paper studies emergent communication in a sender-receiver referential game and examines semantic grounding and interpretability of agent-generated symbols.
- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient empirical rigor to support strong claims about semantic grounding and human interpretability.
- Major concern: Human evaluation methodology is weak and may conflate emergent language with prior knowledge from ImageNet labels.
- Missing control: No experiment showing humans images plus ImageNet labels to assess baseline guessing accuracy.
- Lack of quantitative analysis for “metonymic” links; authors acknowledge phenomenon but provide no systematic evidence.
- Semantic analysis (purity, t-SNE) is superficial: purity above chance is insufficient and t-SNE visuals lack rigorous metrics or significance testing.
- Difference between “informed” and “agnostic” senders is not convincingly explained beyond architectural differences.
- Claims that informed sender produces “more natural” semantics are unsupported by statistical tests or quantitative association measures.
- Literature connection is broad but lacks critical engagement and detailed comparison (e.g., implications of continuous vs. discrete signals).
- Missing baselines: no comparison to simple strategies like random symbol assignment or low-level feature-based labeling.
- Missing ablation studies to isolate contributions of architectural components (e.g., informed sender convolutions).
- Methodological clarification needed: define and consistently measure “convergence” in the referential game.
- Suggested improvements: add control human studies, more rigorous evaluation metrics (precision/recall), and analysis of metonymic association patterns.
- Suggested improvements: develop quantitative semantic-similarity metrics, alternative visualizations, and perform statistical significance testing on purity.
- Questions for authors: provide control experiment with images+ImageNet labels, list statistical tests for purity, sensitivity to hyperparameters, potential ImageNet biases, and examples/quantification of metonymic links.
