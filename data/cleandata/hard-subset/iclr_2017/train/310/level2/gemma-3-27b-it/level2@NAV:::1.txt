## Review of "A Framework for Language Learning Based on Multi-Agent Communication"

This paper proposes a novel framework for language learning, moving beyond passive learning from large text corpora towards interactive language development via multi-agent communication games, specifically referential games. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate in this game and explore methods to align the emergent “word meanings” with intuitive semantic properties, even grounding them to some extent in human-understandable labels. This work is a valuable contribution, offering a compelling alternative approach to language learning inspired by how humans acquire language – through interaction.

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The core idea is innovative and well-motivated, and the initial experimental results are promising, demonstrating that agents *can* learn to communicate and that the emergent language reflects some degree of semantic understanding. Furthermore, the exploration of grounding the communication in natural language is a crucial step toward the stated goal of developing truly interactive AI systems.

The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current supervised learning approaches for conversational agents and positions the proposed framework as a solution to address these shortcomings. The experimental setup is well-defined, using a clear game structure and established image datasets. The analysis, including the use of SVD to assess vocabulary redundancy and purity measures to evaluate semantic alignment, provides valuable insights. The authors also acknowledge limitations, noting that the grounding is far from perfect but is a positive step. 

However, a few areas could benefit from further clarification. While the results show improved semantic alignment with the modifications to the game environment, the extent of this improvement could be quantified more rigorously. Specifically, how much does altering the environment impact the interpretability beyond the observed purity scores? Additionally, the method for selecting the 100 ImageNet labels for supervised learning isn't described – how were these labels chosen, and could selection bias influence the results? It would be helpful to see an ablation study demonstrating the impact of different label sets. 

Finally, providing more qualitative examples of the emergent communication, alongside the quantitative results, would help readers grasp the nuances of the learned language and better understand the grounding process. For example, what specific “metonymic” links did the agents establish?
