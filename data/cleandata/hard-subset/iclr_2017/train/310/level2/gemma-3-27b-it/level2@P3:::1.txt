This paper investigates emergent communication in multi-agent referential games, exploring conditions under which agents develop interpretable language and proposing methods for grounding this language in human-understandable terms. The core idea – using a game-theoretic setup to bootstrap language from scratch – is compelling and addresses a critical challenge in AI: moving beyond passive learning to interactive, communicative intelligence. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and demonstrates promising initial results.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel approach to language emergence and demonstrates its feasibility in a well-defined experimental setting. The grounding experiments, in particular, show a significant step toward bridging the gap between machine-generated and human-understandable language. However, the results, while promising, are still preliminary, and further investigation and clarification are needed to fully assess the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear framing of the problem, the careful experimental design, and the insightful analysis of the emergent communication protocols. The use of referential games, inspired by Lewis' signaling game and game theory, provides a rigorous framework for studying language emergence. The experiments demonstrate that agents can indeed learn to coordinate and that the resulting "language" exhibits some degree of semantic structure, particularly when influenced by environmental factors. The grounding experiments, leveraging supervised learning on ImageNet, are particularly noteworthy, showing that agents can learn to associate symbols with human-understandable labels.

However, several areas could benefit from further elaboration. First, the choice of network architecture for the sender and receiver, while reasonable, could benefit from a more thorough justification. A more detailed ablation study varying the network complexity and connectivity could provide further insight into the factors influencing language emergence. Second, the analysis of the emergent language, while insightful, could be strengthened by more quantitative measures of semantic similarity between the agent-generated symbols and human conceptual categories. The current purity metrics are useful, but may not fully capture the richness of the semantic structure. Third, the grounding experiments, while promising, rely on a relatively small vocabulary size (100). Exploring the scalability of the grounding approach to larger vocabularies and more complex scenes would be valuable. Finally, the analysis of the “metonymic” links in the human evaluation would be strengthened with more detailed examples and a more systematic investigation of the underlying cognitive mechanisms. 

Specific questions and suggestions for improvement:

*   The paper mentions the Reinforce rule for parameter updates. Were other RL algorithms considered and, if so, why was Reinforce chosen?
*   The temperature parameter τ in the Gibbs distribution plays a critical role. How sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter, and was any parameter tuning performed?
*   Could the authors provide more examples of the emergent symbols and their corresponding interpretations, beyond the "dolphin/sea" and "fence/land" examples?
*   The paper briefly mentions a follow-up experiment with ReferItGame. A more detailed description of this experiment, including the specific evaluation metrics used, would be beneficial.
*   The paper states that the supervised objective has no negative effect on communication success. Is this based on statistical significance testing? 
*   While the human evaluation is encouraging, the sample size (10 ratings per pair) seems relatively small. Could a larger sample size provide more robust results?
*   Regarding the “metonymic” link, it would be interesting to explore whether pre-training the agent with some prior knowledge of common metonymies improves performance.



Addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field. Overall, the work represents a significant step towards developing intelligent agents that can communicate effectively and naturally, and is therefore recommended for acceptance.
