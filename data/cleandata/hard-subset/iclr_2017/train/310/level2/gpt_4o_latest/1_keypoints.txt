- Introduces a novel multi-agent referential game framework where neural agents develop their own communication protocols instead of using supervised text datasets.
- Demonstrates that two neural networks can learn to communicate and that environment modifications can encourage semantically meaningful emergent language.
- Proposes a grounding strategy combining supervised learning with interactive play to better align emergent agent language with natural human language.
- Decision: paper recommended for acceptance based on novelty, rigorous experiments, and potential for human-interpretable AI communication.
- Novelty & motivation: tackles limits of supervised approaches by focusing on functional communication using referential games, grounded in linguistics and game theory.
- Experimental rigor: thorough experiments using purity metrics and t-SNE visualizations show semantic alignment of emergent communication.
- Practical implications: grounding strategy and crowdsourced evaluation suggest promise for human-agent collaboration and interpretable AI.
- Suggestion — Scalability: discuss generalization to more complex tasks, multi-step interactions, larger vocabularies, and diverse datasets.
- Suggestion — Human-agent interaction: expand on incorporating humans as players and how human feedback would shape emergent language.
- Suggestion — Limitations: explicitly address reliance on pre-trained image representations and challenges extending to real-world conversational settings.
- Suggestion — Interpretability: further methods needed to better align agent communication with human semantics beyond current grounding approach.
- Question — Ambiguity/polysemy: how does the framework handle multiple symbols referring to the same concept and ensure consistency?
- Question — Multimodal extension: can the grounding strategy be extended to audio, video, or other modalities for multi-modal communication?
- Question — Computational costs: what are the training and computational expenses of the interactive setup versus traditional supervised learning?
- Conclusion: significant and promising contribution to interactive language learning that would benefit from additional discussion of scalability and limitations.
