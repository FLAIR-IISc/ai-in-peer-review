Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a multi-agent referential game framework for learning to communicate, with the broader goal of developing interactive, language-using agents. Two neural networks (sender, receiver) learn to coordinate by exchanging a discrete symbol drawn from a fixed vocabulary to identify a target image among distractors. The contributions are: (i) showing robust emergence of successful communication from scratch across simple architectures and visual embeddings; (ii) analyzing the induced “word meanings,” including evidence that symbols align with higher-level semantics (e.g., category structure, living vs. non-living), not just low-level visual features; (iii) introducing a simple environmental tweak (removing low-level common knowledge by showing different instances of the same class to sender/receiver) that nudges the code toward more general, category-level semantics; and (iv) demonstrating a practical path to grounding in natural language by alternating game-based learning with supervised image naming, and validating partial human interpretability of the learned code with a crowdsourced study. The framework is simple, general, and invites many extensions.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) The paper presents a clear, well-motivated framework with compelling empirical evidence that agents can bootstrap communication, and that environment design can steer the emergent semantics toward human-interpretable structure. (2) The supervised grounding plus human evaluation is a concrete and promising step toward human-compatible interaction, beyond prior emergent-communication results.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? How to induce communication protocols that are useful and portable—ideally human-understandable—via interaction rather than passive learning, and how environment design biases affect emergent semantics.
- Is the approach well motivated and situated? Yes. The case against purely passive training for interactive agents is strong. The work connects to signaling games, emergent communication in multi-agent RL, and grounded language learning. Related work is cited (e.g., cheap talk, recent neural multi-agent communication), and the focus on scalability without humans-in-the-loop is well argued. I would, however, appreciate a deeper comparison with the closest recent neural emergent-communication papers to sharpen novelty claims.
- Do the results support the claims? Empirically, yes. The sender/receiver reliably converge to near-perfect accuracy across configurations. The informed sender learns faster and uses more symbols; SVD and clustering purity against McRae categories support that symbol assignments reflect higher-level semantics, sometimes coarsest-level (living vs. non-living) with very small vocabularies. The object-level manipulation (different instances per agent) modestly improves purity and is intuitively convincing. The supervised grounding significantly increases symbol purity and yields directly interpretable symbols; further, the human study on ReferIt-derived pairs shows 68% accuracy in mapping sender words to targets, indicating nontrivial human interpretability beyond directly supervised denotations. These are solid, well-aligned findings.
- Rigor and clarity: Training is described (REINFORCE, temperatures, embeddings, vocab sizes), with sensible fixed hyperparameters. Results include both success rates and semantic analyses (purity, t-SNE, SVD), plus a human evaluation with appropriate statistics (logistic regression with random effects). A few presentation issues (notably Figure 1 and parts of Section 3) hinder reproducibility and could be clarified.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarify architecture diagrams and text: Figure 1 seems to omit connections and the informed-sender convolutional setup is difficult to parse. Provide a more explicit computational graph, tensor shapes, where weights are shared (if any), and the exact operations (e.g., 2x1 kernels over concatenated embeddings).
- Report variability and stability: Show variance across multiple random seeds for convergence speed, symbol purity, and symbol usage; discuss sensitivity to temperature τ, embedding size, and vocabulary size (10 vs. 100).
- Baselines and ablations: Include stronger baselines, e.g., a sender that directly uses an ImageNet classifier output; or a “symbol = nearest class name” heuristic; or a shared-embedding matching model without learned communication. Ablate the informed-sender inductive bias versus the agnostic sender more systematically.
- Compositionality and generalization: Although single-symbol messages are a good starting point, a brief analysis of whether symbols recombine or whether meanings are stable across contexts would strengthen the discussion. Consider a small experiment with multi-symbol messages or role-rotation to probe robustness.
- Purity/statistical testing: Provide confidence intervals and significance tests for purity differences (obs-chance and across conditions). For the SVD analysis, include quantitative measures (e.g., variance explained curves) in the main text.
- Human study details: Report inter-annotator consistency, per-word performance breakdown, and examples of failure cases. Clarify how words were chosen and presented, and whether trials with supervised-category presence were filtered.
- Data and code: If possible, commit to releasing code and the list of supervised labels to facilitate follow-up work.
- References and formatting: The references section contains formatting artifacts; please clean up for camera-ready.

Questions for the authors
- How stable are the emergent symbols across random seeds? Do symbols align (up to permutation) across runs, or is there substantial drift/synonymy?
- How do vocabulary size and temperature affect (i) convergence speed, (ii) symbol purity, and (iii) human interpretability?
- Could you provide quantitative comparisons with the most closely related emergent-communication works (e.g., tasks, message bandwidth, grounding), clarifying your novel contributions?
- In the object-level manipulation, how much of the purity gain remains if you control for the number of supervised categories or the distribution of object pairs?
- How does the system perform when using sm vs. fc features, and do these choices change the nature of the emergent semantics?
- What fraction of the supervised-category targets elicit the exact corresponding symbol when there is a close distractor from the same category? Any analysis of confusions?
- Did you explore multi-symbol messages or limited back-and-forth communication? If so, does semantics become more compositional?
- How sample-efficient is learning (games to reach a given accuracy) and how does this compare to passive supervised approaches?
- In the human study, how often is success attributable to metonymy versus visual association? Any systematic categorization?

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated paper with clean experiments and a valuable step toward human-interpretable emergent communication. I recommend acceptance.
