1. Summary and contributions
The paper studies emergent communication in referential games to probe whether two interacting agents can develop interpretable symbol systems and whether these systems bear similarities to human semantic organization. Using a deliberately simple sender–receiver setup over natural images, the authors show that coordination emerges reliably, that an “informed” sender architecture uses a richer symbol inventory than an “agnostic” sender (often beyond two symbols), and that modest semantic structure (e.g., category-level regularities and a living/non-living split) appears in the learned code. They further demonstrate that mixing interactive learning with supervised object naming yields more human-interpretable symbols and some success in human–agent communication.

2. Decision: Reject (primary reasons: task reduction and insufficient evidential support for the central claims)
- The core game often collapses to image-category discrimination, making the communication protocol tantamount to encoding class labels. This substantially weakens claims about the emergence of language-like structure.
- The main findings can likely be reproduced by a supervised classifier with a discrete bottleneck (transfer learning), trained more efficiently than the proposed RL framework, yet the paper does not provide such baselines or ablations.

3. Supporting arguments
- Question/problem: Important and timely—understanding how agents develop protocols and whether these mirror human strategies. The use of simple games is a strength for interpretability.
- Motivation/related work: Well-situated in emergent communication, multi-agent RL, and game-theoretic signaling. The human-grounding angle is appealing.
- Evidence/rigor: While analyses (purity, t-SNE, SVD spectrum) suggest nontrivial structure, the setting frequently reduces to two-class image classification when images are from different categories; symbols then encode class identity. Without controls where distractor and target are from the same class (fine-grained discrimination), the presence of higher-level, human-like semantics remains ambiguous. Missing baselines (e.g., frozen VGG + shallow classifier with discrete code; supervised/IB models; straight-through/Gumbel training) limit conclusions on the necessity of RL and interaction.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Add strong baselines: supervised classifier with discrete bottleneck; kNN over CNN features; non-interactive sender pretraining; information-bottleneck objective.
- Control tasks: same-category pairs; adversarial near neighbors; out-of-category generalization; held-out classes and cross-play between independently trained agents.
- Report sample efficiency, variance across seeds, vocabulary entropy/topographic similarity, MI between symbols and class/attributes; test compositionality (multi-object scenes).
- Clarify human evaluation: stratify by in-vs-out-of supervised label set; analyze error types and metonymic uses.

Questions for authors
- What fraction of train/test pairs are same-category vs different-category, and how do results change when conditioning on same-category pairs?
- How does a supervised classifier with a discrete bottleneck perform on the game (accuracy, purity, symbol usage) compared to RL?
- Do results hold under stronger distractors (nearest neighbors in fc space) and with frozen visual features?
- How stable are symbol inventories across runs, and does cross-play succeed between independently trained agents?
- Can you quantify living/non-living structure (e.g., mutual information with that attribute) and show generalization to unseen categories?
