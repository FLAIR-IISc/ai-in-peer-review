Summary and contributions
The paper studies whether communication protocols can emerge between neural agents trained from scratch in a cooperative referential game, and how to steer the emergent “language” toward human-interpretable semantics. Two sender architectures (an “informed” sender with an inductive bias to compare images dimension-wise and an “agnostic” sender) interact with a receiver through a one-symbol discrete channel to refer to real images drawn from ImageNet. The authors show: (i) agents reliably learn to coordinate; (ii) the informed sender uses a richer symbol inventory than the agnostic sender; (iii) symbol usage partially aligns with high-level object categories and visual similarity (purity and t-SNE analyses), and this alignment is strengthened when common low-level information is removed (object-level reference condition); (iv) adding a supervised object-naming task grounds symbols to natural-language labels, improving purity and enabling human subjects to guess the intended referent 68% of the time even on images not in the supervised label set. The setup is simple and interpretable, and the paper aims at a promising direction: interactive language learning with multi-agent games plus lightweight grounding.

Decision
Reject. Key reasons: (1) The game often collapses to image classification with label transmission, so the need for emergent communication and RL is unclear; (2) Empirical support lacks strong baselines and controls to demonstrate that the proposed approach offers advantages or yields genuinely language-like structure beyond class encoding.

Supporting arguments
On the core questions:
1) What is the specific problem? The paper asks whether tabula rasa agents can develop a useful communication protocol in a visual referential game, and how environment design and weak supervision can bias the emergent code toward human-interpretable semantics.
2) Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? The motivation—learning communication functionally via interaction rather than passively—is compelling and timely. The paper situates itself with emergent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) and cognitive/game-theoretic traditions. However, the chosen task and metrics are limited in their capacity to reveal language-like properties, and the literature on baselines for label-based communication (e.g., supervised classifiers with a discrete channel) is not engaged.
3) Do results support the claims? The results show convergence and some semantic structure (purity above chance; human guessing above chance). Yet several claims are weakly supported because:
- Task reducibility: When images are from different categories, the sender can succeed by performing image classification and emitting a class token. The informed-vs-agnostic contrast may reflect architectural capacity to infer class labels rather than emergence of richer semantics. The object-level variation experiment helps but still does not clearly separate instance-level semantics from class proxies.
- Missing baselines: No comparison to a simple supervised pipeline that trains a classifier to predict ImageNet labels and a receiver to map those labels to images, nor to a frozen-pretrained classifier whose logits are discretized and sent through the channel. Such baselines might achieve higher sample efficiency and similar or better purity/human interpretability, challenging the RL-based emergence story.
- Limited analysis of the code: Purity and SVD spectrum are helpful but mostly suggestive. There is no quantification of mutual information between symbols and object classes, robustness to within-category distractors, or topographic similarity between visual and symbol spaces. The human study is small and potentially primed (words chosen from ImageNet labels), and 68% accuracy leaves ambiguity about what is being communicated (class hints vs broader semantics).
- Efficiency and rigor: The method requires many RL updates for a problem that might be solvable faster with supervised learning or imitation; there is no sample complexity analysis, no ablation on temperature, channel capacity, or alternative discretization (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through).
- Claims of “natural language-like” behavior: The single-token discrete channel and lack of compositional tests limit conclusions about language-like structure; observed “metonymic” uses could be byproducts of label correlations in visual space.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Add strong baselines: (a) a supervised classifier whose predicted label (or a learned discrete code) is sent to the receiver; (b) a retrieval baseline that sends nearest neighbor prototype IDs; (c) a continuous channel baseline with quantization at test time. Compare accuracy, purity, human interpretability, and sample efficiency.
- Hard negatives and task difficulty: Systematically vary distractor difficulty (same category, fine-grained subclasses) to ensure the task cannot be solved by coarse class codes. Report performance and symbol usage under within-category pairings.
- Code analysis: Report mutual information between symbols and object classes, symbol entropy, and topographic similarity between visual distances and symbol distances. Evaluate generalization to unseen classes and to new object pairings not seen during training.
- Grounding and human study: Expand the human evaluation with randomized or paraphrased labels, out-of-vocabulary symbols, and controls where humans do not see conventional labels. Report inter-annotator agreement and error typology.
- Architectural ablations: Compare informed vs agnostic senders under equal capacity; analyze the effect of temperature, vocabulary size, and straight-through estimators; consider multi-symbol messages to probe compositionality.
- Clarity: Clean up references and ensure all cited works are complete; add training stability details (variance across seeds, convergence criteria).

Questions for the authors
- How often are target/distractor pairs from the same high-level category during training and testing? How does performance and symbol purity change when pairs are constrained to be within the same category or fine-grained subclasses?
- What is the performance and human interpretability of a purely supervised baseline that predicts ImageNet labels and transmits them to a receiver trained to map labels to images? How does your approach compare in sample efficiency?
- Can you report mutual information between symbols and object classes, and symbol entropy over time, to quantify whether the code truly goes beyond a small set of class tokens?
- How robust are the findings across random seeds and hyperparameters (temperature τ, vocabulary size K)? Do agents converge to similar symbol semantics?
- In the supervised grounding experiment, do agents use conventional labels appropriately when both target and distractor belong to supervised classes with subtle differences (e.g., two dog breeds)?
- Have you tried multi-symbol messages or role rotation? Does that increase compositionality or interpretability?
- Can you test generalization to unseen classes and to more complex scenes (multiple objects), and report how the code adapts?
