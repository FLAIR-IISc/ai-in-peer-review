Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a multi-agent, interactive framework for language learning via referential games, arguing that interactive communication is crucial for training language-capable agents. Two neural agents (sender, receiver) learn to coordinate on image reference by exchanging a discrete symbol. The work contributes:
- A clear, general framework for multi-agent communication tasks and a concrete instantiation with real images (463 ImageNet-based object categories), simple feed-forward agents, and discrete one-symbol messages.
- Empirical evidence that agents reliably learn to communicate from scratch using only task reward, with architectures that differ in inductive bias (“agnostic” vs “informed” sender).
- Analyses suggesting emergent symbols correlate with intuitive semantic properties (e.g., category structure and a living/non-living split), strengthened by an “object-level reference” variant that removes low-level common knowledge to encourage higher-level semantics.
- A hybrid training strategy that alternates between game-based training and supervised image labeling. This grounding leads to substantially more interpretable symbols and successful human-agent communication on a separate dataset (ReferIt), even when the supervised label set does not directly match the images.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated framework and compelling empirical results that open a promising direction for interactive, multi-agent language learning; (2) The hybrid training (alternating self-play and supervised learning) is particularly fruitful and demonstrates improved interpretability and human compatibility.

Supporting arguments
1. Problem tackled: How to design interactive environments where agents develop a communication protocol that is both effective for coordination and aligned with human-understandable semantics. The paper targets the core referential use of language and examines how environment design shapes emergent meanings.
2. Motivation and literature placement: The work is well motivated by limitations of passive/supervised learning for interactive agents. It connects to signaling games and recent multi-agent RL (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), but goes further by asking how to nudge emergent codes toward human-like semantics and how to ground them in natural language. While there is related work on emergent communication, it remains relatively limited; this paper’s focus on interpretability and human studies is timely and valuable.
3. Empirical support and rigor: Results robustly show rapid convergence to successful communication across settings. Analyses of symbol usage (purity vs McRae categories, SVD spectra, t-SNE visualizations) support the claim that the codes reflect high-level semantics rather than low-level features, with clearer effects in the object-level variant. The hybrid training substantially increases symbol purity and enables human interpretability on out-of-vocabulary images (68% accuracy in a crowd study), which is strong evidence that the proposed alternation helps ground the code. Some aspects could be tightened (variance across seeds, ablations, and stronger baselines), but the main claims are convincingly supported.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not decisive for acceptance)
- Clarity of architecture and Figure 1: The diagram appears to omit key network connections and training-time vs test-time operations (sampling, temperature). Section 3’s bespoke architecture is hard to follow. Please add a detailed caption, show all modules (embeddings, conv filters for the informed sender, symbol sampling, receiver scoring), indicate dimensionalities, and clearly mark shared vs unshared parameters and the alternation between supervised and game updates.
- Training details and stability: Report variance over multiple seeds for success rate, symbol purity, and number of symbols used. Include learning curves for both sender types, and ablations on temperature τ, vocabulary size, embedding size, and message length (single vs multi-symbol).
- Baselines and controls: Compare to a supervised-only sender used in the game without interactive training, and to a continuous-message variant, to isolate the effect of discretization. Include a “random-symbol” or shuffled-code control for the human experiment.
- RL methodology: Specify optimizer, learning rates, any variance-reduction baselines for REINFORCE, and whether entropy regularization is used to prevent symbol collapse.
- Generalization: Evaluate zero-shot categories or unseen-image distributions to probe compositionality and robustness. Examine symbol stability across runs and over training.
- Human study: Provide more detail (instructions, quality control, inter-annotator variability, example failures). Consider top-1 vs top-k agreement and per-word performance.
- Related work: Expand discussion of closely related emergent communication papers and cognitive science/game-theoretic results to better situate the contribution. I understand the paper well but am not fully up-to-date on the closest literature; a richer related-work section would help readers.

Questions for the authors
- How variable are your results across random seeds (coordination success, number of symbols, purity)? Do different runs converge to different yet equally effective codes?
- How sensitive are outcomes to τ, vocabulary size, and message length? Does allowing multi-symbol messages yield more compositional or interpretable codes?
- Did you use a variance-reduction baseline or entropy bonus in REINFORCE? What is the sample efficiency and training-time breakdown for game vs supervised updates?
- In the object-level reference variant, do you observe stronger category-aligned clusters consistently? Any evidence of over-reliance on VGG semantic priors vs game incentives?
- For the hybrid setting, did you try also supervising the receiver or sharing embeddings across agents? How critical is parameter sharing between supervised and game embeddings?
- How stable is symbol meaning across time (e.g., symbol drift)? Do synonyms emerge and persist?
- Could you clarify whether Figure 1’s informed sender convolutions are truly channel-wise 1-D filters over paired dimensions, and whether the agnostic sender nests the informed sender in practice?
- In the human study, how were words chosen when the supervised label does not directly appear in ReferIt expressions? Any analysis of metonymic/associative use by semantic class?

Overall, this is a strong and promising paper: a useful framework, reasonable and interpretable results, and a compelling hybrid training path toward human-compatible emergent communication.
