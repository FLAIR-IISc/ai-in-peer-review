- Paper studies emergence of communication between neural agents in a referential game and how to steer it toward human-interpretable semantics.  
- Two sender architectures (informed vs agnostic) communicate via a one-symbol discrete channel about ImageNet images.  
- Findings: agents reliably coordinate and the informed sender uses a richer symbol inventory than the agnostic sender.  
- Findings: symbol usage partially aligns with high-level object categories and visual similarity (purity and t-SNE analyses).  
- Findings: alignment strengthens when low-level common information is removed (object-level reference condition).  
- Findings: supervised object-naming grounding improves purity and lets humans guess referents ~68% on some unseen images.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main rejection reason 1: The game can collapse to image classification + label transmission, questioning the need for emergent communication and RL.  
- Main rejection reason 2: Empirical support lacks strong baselines and controls to show advantages or genuinely language-like structure beyond class encoding.  
- Critique: Task reducibility — success may reflect class label inference rather than richer emergent semantics.  
- Critique: Missing baselines — no supervised classifier or frozen-pretrained logits discretized and sent through the channel for comparison.  
- Critique: Limited analysis — purity and SVD are suggestive but missing mutual information, robustness to within-category distractors, and topographic similarity metrics.  
- Critique: Human study concerns — small, potentially primed, and 68% leaves ambiguity about what is communicated.  
- Critique: Efficiency and rigor — many RL updates for a problem that may be solvable faster with supervised methods; no sample complexity or ablations on temperature, channel capacity, or discretization.  
- Critique: Claims of “natural language-like” behavior are unsupported by single-token channel, lack of compositionality tests, and possible label-correlation artifacts.  
- Suggested improvement: Add strong baselines (supervised classifier-label pipeline, retrieval/prototype baseline, continuous channel with quantization).  
- Suggested improvement: Vary distractor difficulty systematically (same-category and fine-grained negatives) and report performance/symbol usage.  
- Suggested improvement: Report mutual information, symbol entropy, topographic similarity, and generalization to unseen classes/pairings.  
- Suggested improvement: Expand human evaluation with randomized/paraphrased labels, out-of-vocabulary controls, inter-annotator agreement, and error analysis.  
- Suggested improvement: Provide architectural ablations (equal-capacity informed vs agnostic, temperature, vocabulary size, straight-through estimators) and try multi-symbol messages.  
- Suggested improvement: Improve clarity and reproducibility (complete references, training stability, variance across seeds, convergence criteria).  
- Key author question: How often are target/distractor pairs from the same high-level category, and how does this affect performance and purity?  
- Key author question: How does a purely supervised baseline (predict ImageNet labels → transmit) compare in performance and sample efficiency?  
- Key author question: Can you report mutual information between symbols and object classes and symbol entropy over training?  
- Key author question: How robust are findings across seeds and hyperparameters; do agents converge to similar semantics?  
- Key author question: In supervised grounding, do agents use conventional labels correctly when target and distractor are subtly different supervised classes?  
- Key author question: Have you tried multi-symbol messages or role rotation, and do these increase compositionality or interpretability?  
- Key author question: Can the method generalize to unseen classes and more complex scenes (multiple objects), and how does the code adapt?
