- Proposes a multi-agent interactive referential game framework where two neural agents learn discrete-symbol communication for image reference.
- Provides a concrete instantiation with 463 ImageNet-derived categories, simple feed-forward agents, and single-symbol discrete messages.
- Shows empirical evidence that agents learn to communicate from scratch using only task reward, comparing “agnostic” vs “informed” sender inductive biases.
- Analyses indicate emergent symbols align with high-level semantics (category structure, living/non-living split), enhanced by an object-level reference variant that removes low-level common knowledge.
- Introduces a hybrid training strategy alternating game-based self-play and supervised image labeling, producing more interpretable symbols and enabling human-agent communication (68% crowd accuracy on ReferIt).
- Decision: Accept — framework is strong and empirical results open a promising direction for interactive multi-agent language learning.
- Key reason 1: Well-motivated framework with compelling empirical results on emergent, interpretable communication.
- Key reason 2: The hybrid self-play + supervised alternation notably improves interpretability and human compatibility.
- Problem targeted: Designing interactive environments that yield effective coordination protocols aligned with human-understandable semantics.
- Motivation/literature placement: Addresses limits of passive supervised learning, connects to signaling games and multi-agent RL, and focuses on nudging emergent codes toward human-like semantics.
- Empirical rigor: Rapid convergence to communication across settings with analyses (purity, SVD, t-SNE) supporting high-level semantic coding; hybrid training boosts purity and human interpretability.
- Requested clarity: Improve Figure 1 and architecture description to show all modules, connections, dimensionalities, and training vs test operations.
- Training details/ stability: Report variance across seeds, learning curves, and ablations on τ, vocab/embedding/message size and number of symbols used.
- Baselines/controls: Add supervised-only sender baseline, continuous-message variant, and random-symbol control for human experiments.
- RL methodology: Specify optimizer, learning rates, variance-reduction baselines for REINFORCE, and any entropy regularization to avoid symbol collapse.
- Generalization: Evaluate zero-shot categories, unseen-image distributions, compositionality, and symbol stability across runs and over time.
- Human study details: Provide instructions, quality control, inter-annotator variability, example failures, top-1 vs top-k metrics, and per-word performance.
- Related work: Expand discussion of closely related emergent communication and cognitive/game-theory literature to better situate contributions.
- Question: How variable are results across random seeds for coordination success, symbol count, and purity; do distinct but equally effective codes emerge?
- Question: How sensitive are outcomes to τ, vocabulary size, and message length; does multi-symbol messaging yield more compositional/interpretable codes?
- Question: Did you use variance-reduction baselines or entropy bonuses in REINFORCE; what is sample efficiency and training-time split between game vs supervised updates?
- Question: In the object-level variant, are category-aligned clusters consistently stronger, and is there evidence of reliance on VGG semantic priors vs game incentives?
- Question: For hybrid training, did you supervise the receiver or share embeddings across agents; how critical is parameter sharing between supervised and game embeddings?
- Question: How stable is symbol meaning over time (symbol drift) and do persistent synonyms emerge?
- Question: Can you clarify the informed sender’s convolutions (channel-wise 1-D over paired dimensions) and whether the agnostic sender nests the informed sender?
- Question: In the human study, how were words chosen when supervised labels don’t appear in ReferIt, and any analysis of metonymic/associative uses by semantic class?
