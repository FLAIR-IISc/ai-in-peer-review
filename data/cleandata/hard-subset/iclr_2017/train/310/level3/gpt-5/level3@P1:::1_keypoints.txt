- Contribution (i): Feed-forward agents trained with REINFORCE can reliably establish near-perfect communication in the referential game.
- Contribution (ii): An “informed” sender that compares image embeddings dimension-wise converges faster and uses a richer symbol set than an “agnostic” sender that collapses to two symbols.
- Contribution (iii): Emergent symbols correlate with high-level semantic categories (e.g., McRae categories, living vs. non-living), not only low-level visual features.
- Contribution (iv): Showing different images from the same class to sender and receiver nudges symbols toward object-level semantics.
- Contribution (v): Grounding via mixed self-play and supervised object naming increases symbol purity and human interpretability (e.g., 68% human accuracy on ReferIt-style stimuli).
- Decision: Weak Accept.
- Reason (1): Tackles an important problem with a clear, interpretable setting advancing understanding of emergent communication and human alignment.
- Reason (2): Empirical evidence, including human studies, supports the main claims.
- Reason (3): Novel and insightful analysis of how architecture and environment shape emergent codes.
- Problem & positioning: Well-situated with multi-agent RL and language emergence literature, emphasizing interpretability and human grounding beyond prior work.
- Methodology: Simple referential game using real ImageNet-derived images and pretrained VGG features yields realism and analyzability via discrete symbols and dot-product receiver.
- Architectural bias: The informed sender’s inductive bias is justified and empirically leads to faster convergence and broader vocabulary use.
- Empirical claims: Agents achieve high coordination; symbol-category alignment evaluated via cluster purity and t-SNE shows local coherence.
- Object-level variant: The variant removing some common knowledge strengthens evidence for higher-level semantic encoding.
- Grounding experiment: Mixing supervised naming with self-play yields high purity (≈70%), many interpretable symbols, and above-chance human performance.
- Interpretability insight: Agnostic sender collapses to two symbols while informed sender uses many symbols; SVD suggests nontrivial dimensional structure.
- Qualitative findings: Metonymic symbol uses mirror natural language flexibility, supporting interpretability claims.
- Rigor: Training details provided; human study analyzed with mixed-effects logistic regression and quantitative metrics complement visualizations.
- Caveat (classification reduction): Many trials reduce to class-level discrimination, explaining strong category-level purity.
- Caveat (pretrained perception): Using VGG features imports high-level semantics; agents aren’t perceptually tabula rasa.
- Caveat (RL efficiency): For class-like protocols, supervised transfer could be more sample-efficient than REINFORCE; efficiency trade-offs are not fully quantified.
- Caveat (bandwidth): Single-symbol, one-shot communication limits conclusions about compositionality and dialogue.
- Reporting limitations: Missing variance across seeds, ablations on temperature and vocabulary, and fuller symbol-usage distributions.
- Suggestions (1): Report fraction of same- vs different-category trials and separate performance/purity for each.
- Suggestions (2): Provide learning curves and sample-efficiency comparisons for self-play, supervision-only, and mixed regimes.
- Suggestions (3): Report robustness across random seeds and hyperparameters with confidence intervals.
- Suggestions (4): Add stronger semantic analyses (AMI, clustering stability, living vs non-living tests, confusion matrices).
- Suggestions (5): Explore multi-symbol or multi-turn messages to probe compositionality and fine-grained discrimination.
- Suggestions (6): Test alternative feature extractors (earlier CNN layers or self-supervised features) to clarify pretrained perception effects.
- Questions for authors (1): What fraction of train/test pairs are same-category vs different-category, and how do performance/purity differ?
- Question (2): How sensitive are coordination success, symbol counts, and purity to random initialization and temperature τ (mean ± std)?
- Question (3): Can you quantify sample efficiency for (a) self-play only, (b) supervised naming only, and (c) mixed regimes?
- Question (4): How much do pretrained VGG features drive semantic alignment; results with earlier-layer or self-supervised features?
- Question (5): In the SVD analysis, provide explained variance curves and effective rank across settings.
- Question (6): In the human study, which words/groups drive successes and what are common error types?
- Question (7): Have you tried two-symbol messages or short sequences, and does the informed sender’s advantage and compositionality persist?
