- Proposes a multi-agent referential game where sender and receiver learn to communicate via discrete symbols to identify target images among distractors.  
- Shows robust emergence of successful communication across simple architectures and visual embeddings.  
- Analyzes induced “word meanings,” finding symbols align with higher-level semantics (e.g., categories, living vs. non-living), not only low-level features.  
- Introduces an environmental tweak (different instances per agent) that encourages category-level semantics.  
- Demonstrates grounding toward natural language by alternating game-based learning with supervised image naming and validates partial human interpretability via crowdsourcing.  
- Decision: Accept — framework is clear, well-motivated, and empirically compelling.  
- Key reason for acceptance: environment design can steer emergent semantics toward human-interpretable structure.  
- Key reason for acceptance: supervised grounding plus human evaluation is a promising step toward human-compatible interaction.  
- Problem tackled: inducing portable, human-understandable communication protocols via interaction rather than passive learning.  
- Positioning: Well situated among signaling games, emergent multi-agent RL, and grounded language learning; argues against purely passive training.  
- Empirical support: sender/receiver converge to near-perfect accuracy; informed sender learns faster and uses more symbols.  
- Semantic analyses: SVD and clustering purity indicate alignment with higher-level categories; object-level manipulation modestly improves purity.  
- Supervised grounding: increases symbol purity and yields interpretable symbols; human study shows 68% mapping accuracy.  
- Rigor/clarity: Training details and analyses provided, but some presentation issues impair reproducibility.
