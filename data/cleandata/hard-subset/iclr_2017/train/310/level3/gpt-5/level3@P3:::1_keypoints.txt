- Tackles how communication emerges between learning agents in referential games and whether induced protocols reflect human-like semantics.  
- Uses a simple, controlled two-image referential task with discrete vocabulary and success as the only reward for interpretability.  
- Compares two sender architectures: an “informed” sender (dimension-wise bias) and an “agnostic” sender, observing distinct symbol-use patterns.  
- Informed senders use many symbols; agnostic senders typically collapse to a compact 2-symbol code despite both achieving coordination.  
- Analyzes induced codes via symbol usage stats, SVD of symbol–pair matrix, cluster purity vs human categories, and t-SNE visualizations.  
- Manipulates common knowledge (showing different instances of same class) to nudge agents toward more abstract, object-level semantics.  
- Introduces supervised grounding (object-name prediction) alongside the game to align emergent symbols with natural words.  
- Human subjects interpret grounded messages above chance (reported 68% accuracy on a new dataset).  
- Overall claim: simple agents can develop communication partially reflecting high-level semantics, and light supervision improves human interpretability.  
- Decision: Accept — motivated by a clear framework and solid evidence, including human evaluation, that environment design and light supervision steer codes toward human-like semantics.  
- Supporting evidence: both architectures reach near-perfect coordination quickly and symbol–category purity is significantly above chance.  
- SVD and usage analyses suggest multi-dimensional structure beyond trivial synonymy; object-level manipulation increases semantic purity.  
- Caveat: when images are from different classes, the task often reduces to coarse class encoding, resembling transfer learning.  
- Caveat: REINFORCE on discrete symbols is sample-inefficient; supervised or Gumbel-based baselines might learn mappings faster.  
- Caveat: Purity is informative but coarse; more granular metrics (mutual information, entropy) would strengthen analysis.  
- Limitations: little evidence of systematic compositionality or robust generalization beyond coarse category codes.  
- Strength: clear, minimal task enabling interpretable post-learning analyses and methodological comparison of inductive biases.  
- Strength: careful semantic analyses and a clever common-knowledge manipulation to elicit abstract semantics.  
- Strength: practical step toward human interpretability with mixed supervised/game training and human evaluation.  
- Suggestion: strengthen task difficulty by testing same-supercategory and same-class distractors to reduce trivial category cues.  
- Suggestion: add baselines — supervised classifier-to-symbol and Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through variants to compare sample efficiency and semantics.  
- Suggestion: report variance across random seeds, vocab sizes, and sample complexity curves with confidence intervals.  
- Suggestion: deeper semantic metrics — mutual information, per-symbol image similarity, entropy/skew of symbol distribution.  
- Suggestion: explore multi-symbol messages and role rotation to test for compositional codes.  
- Questions for authors include frequency of hard pairs (same class/supercategory), supervised transfer baseline comparisons, variance across seeds, behavior when no supervised class is present, experiments with Gumbel-Softmax, sensitivity to visual features and vocab size, and symbol–class mutual information/confusion matrices.
