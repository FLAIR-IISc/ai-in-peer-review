1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a well-motivated framework for training interactive language through multi-agent referential communication games. Two neural agents (sender/receiver) learn to coordinate from scratch on real images, with evidence that their emergent symbols align with high-level semantics rather than low-level visual features. The work compares “agnostic” vs “informed” sender architectures, explores an object-level variant that removes low-level common knowledge to induce more abstract meanings, and, importantly, demonstrates a fruitful hybrid approach that alternates self-play with supervised object naming. This hybridization yields higher semantic purity and improved human interpretability, culminating in a human study showing above-chance success when humans act as receivers. The framework is simple, extensible, and invites many future variations.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) The paper offers a strong and useful framework with clear, reasonable results showing emergent communication and its semantic shaping. (ii) The alternation between game-based training and supervised learning is particularly promising for grounding and human interpretability.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: Multi-agent communication for language learning is timely and underexplored; the paper is well-motivated and cites relevant prior work (e.g., Sukhbaatar, Foerster), though a deeper positioning versus closest contemporaries would help.
- Empirical support: Results consistently show rapid coordination, increased semantic purity (especially with the informed sender and object-level manipulation), and meaningful human interpretability (68% accuracy vs. 50% chance), with appropriate statistical testing in the human study.
- Rigor and scope: The RL formulation (REINFORCE) and metrics (purity, spectrum/SVD, human study) are appropriate. While some controls and ablations could be expanded, the evidence supports the claims.

4. Suggestions to improve (helpful, not decisive)
- Clarity: Figure 1 appears to omit key connections; add a precise, self-contained diagram and caption. The Section 3 architecture description is hard to follow—consider pseudocode for the training loop and clearer notation.
- Reporting: Include error bars/variance across seeds, sensitivity to τ, embedding size, and vocabulary size; detail any variance-reduction baselines in REINFORCE.
- Analysis: Add comparisons to baselines (e.g., random or fixed codes), test role-swapping and agent-interoperability, and report topographic similarity or related measures of form-meaning alignment. Probe compositional generalization and synonymy/meronymy behavior.
- Related work: Expand discussion to situate contributions against the closest emergent-communication literature.

Questions for the authors
- Do you use a baseline/variance-reduction in REINFORCE? How sensitive are results to τ and the sampling vs. argmax choice at test time?
- How stable are results across random seeds, and how often does the “living vs. non-living” split emerge?
- Can agents trained separately interoperate (zero-shot) or after lightweight alignment?
- Did you evaluate topographic similarity between semantic distances and symbol distances?
- In the hybrid setup, how does mixing ratio (game vs. supervised) affect purity and human interpretability?
- Can you clarify the informed sender’s convolutional connections and the exact data flow omitted in Figure 1?
