This paper proposes and studies a simple but compelling framework for emergent language learning through multi-agent referential games. Two neural agents (sender/receiver) learn to communicate about real images via a discrete bottleneck, trained end-to-end with only success/failure feedback. The paper shows: (i) agents reliably and rapidly converge to successful coordination; (ii) architectural inductive bias (the “informed sender”) improves convergence and leads to richer symbol use; (iii) environment design matters—removing image-level common knowledge nudges symbol meanings toward higher-level object semantics; and (iv) mixing interactive self-play with supervised object naming grounds the learned code in human language, improving interpretability and transfer to human participants. The work is well-motivated, methodologically straightforward, and opens an attractive direction for building interactive language-capable systems without heavy human annotation.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated framework with novel insights into how environment and inductive bias shape emergent communication and its human-alignment; (2) Empirical results convincingly demonstrate both successful coordination and partial grounding in human-interpretable semantics, including a human study.

Supporting arguments:
- Problem/positioning: The paper addresses how to train interactive language agents beyond passive, supervised learning, asking specifically what game designs foster transferable, interpretable language. This is timely and important.
- Approach: The referential game is a clean instantiation of cheap talk signaling with modern vision features and discrete communication learned via REINFORCE. The two sender variants isolate the effect of architectural bias toward dimension-wise comparison. The object-level reference variant smartly reduces low-level common knowledge, pushing the code toward higher-level semantics. The combined supervised + game training is a pragmatic route to grounding, analogous in spirit to AlphaGo’s mix of supervised and self-play.
- Empirical support: Agents reach near-perfect accuracy quickly across settings. The informed sender converges faster and uses more symbols; SVD analyses suggest nontrivial signal structure beyond a few synonyms. Cluster purity with respect to human categories is consistently above chance, and improves with object-level reference and with supervised grounding (70% purity; 88/100 symbols used). Human participants identify the intended target 68% of the time on out-of-domain referential pairs, showing genuine partial interpretability and metonymic flexibility. These results substantiate the paper’s main claims.
- Scope and impact: The framework is extensible (longer messages, multi-turn, role rotation, human-in-the-loop) and should catalyze further research in emergent communication and human-AI interaction.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not central to the accept decision):
- Clarity of architecture: Figure 1 likely omits connections and the “informed” convolutional mechanism is difficult to parse from text. Please expand the diagram with clear tensor shapes and data flow, add a detailed caption, and provide a compact mathematical description of the sender scoring function in both variants.
- Experimental reporting: Include variance across multiple random seeds, learning curves with confidence bands, and sensitivity to vocabulary size and temperature. Report stability of symbol usage and purity across runs.
- Baselines and ablations: (i) Compare to continuous communication (no discretization) and to Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through estimators. (ii) Add a non-communicative baseline (e.g., receiver picks via nearest-neighbor similarity) to contextualize the gains from communication. (iii) Ablate the pre-trained vision features to discuss how much semantic structure is inherited vs. induced by the game.
- Metrics of structure: Complement purity with a topographic similarity or representational similarity analysis to quantify alignment between distances in visual space and distances in signal space.
- Human study details: Provide more protocol specifics (participant demographics, instructions, examples, quality controls), and report inter-participant agreement and effect sizes. Analyze error types to characterize where humans fail.
- Related work: Expand positioning relative to contemporary emergent communication work (e.g., multi-agent differentiable communication, cognitive science signaling games) and clarify distinctions.
- Limitations and next steps: Discuss compositionality explicitly and whether multi-symbol messages or attribute-based tasks induce more systematic generalization. Consider role rotation and multi-step negotiation to move toward conversational competence.

Questions for the authors:
1. Stability and sensitivity: How consistent are coordination accuracy, symbol usage, and purity across random seeds and different τ, learning rates, and vocabulary sizes (10 vs. 100)? Do results degrade for much larger vocabularies?
2. Discrete sampling: Did you try Gumbel-Softmax or straight-through estimators? Any empirical differences vs. REINFORCE (variance, convergence speed, symbol diversity)?
3. Sender/receiver symmetry: The sender always receives target-first while the receiver sees random order. Could this create positional shortcuts or limit generality? What happens if both see randomized orders and the target index is provided separately?
4. Purity/statistics: How is chance purity computed, and are differences statistically significant across runs? Can you report confidence intervals for purity and SVD spectrum diagnostics?
5. Object-level reference: Beyond the modest purity gains, do you observe clearer semantic clusters or improved topographic similarity? Any qualitative examples contrasting image-level vs. object-level play?
6. Grounding and generalization: In the supervised+game setting, how often does the sender use learned labels metaphorically vs. literally? Can you quantify transfer to non-supervised categories (e.g., proportion of human-correct choices conditioned on presence/absence of supervised classes)?
7. Compositionality: Have you explored multi-symbol messages or attribute-based referential games (color, shape, category) to test whether the code becomes compositional and generalizes systematically to novel combinations?
8. Reproducibility: Will you release code, trained models, and the list of supervised labels to facilitate replication and follow-up studies?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, thoughtfully executed paper that advances emergent communication toward human-aligned language use. I recommend acceptance.
