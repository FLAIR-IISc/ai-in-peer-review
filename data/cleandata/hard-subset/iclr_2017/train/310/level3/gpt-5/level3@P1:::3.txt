This paper studies language emergence via multi-agent referential games and presents three main contributions: (i) it shows that two simple neural agents, trained tabula rasa with REINFORCE over discrete symbols, rapidly learn to communicate about natural images with near-perfect accuracy; (ii) it analyzes how environmental and architectural choices shape the semantics of the learned code, providing evidence that symbols align with high-level conceptual properties (e.g., categories, living vs. non-living) rather than low-level visual features, with further gains when common low-level knowledge is removed; and (iii) it proposes a simple yet effective grounding strategy that alternates game play with supervised image labeling, yielding markedly more interpretable symbols, direct word–referent usage (47% exact label use on supervised classes), and successful human interpretability on out-of-domain images (68% accuracy).

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) Clear, well-motivated problem and a simple, compelling framework that advances interactive, function-driven language learning beyond passive supervision; (2) Strong empirical support that communication reliably emerges and that modest environmental design choices and light supervision substantially improve semantic alignment and human interpretability.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets how to induce communication protocols that are useful for coordination and potentially portable to humans. The inadequacy of passive, canned dialogue training for interactive use is well argued, and the setup connects naturally to signaling games and multi-agent learning literature.
- Placement in literature: The work relates carefully to prior neural multi-agent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) and decades of signaling-game research, and differentiates itself by (a) grounding in realistic image inputs, (b) explicit analysis of semantic alignment, and (c) a pragmatic step toward human interpretability via mixed supervised/game training and a human study. This is a meaningful and timely advance relative to contemporaneous work.
- Claims vs. evidence: Results consistently back the claims. Agents converge quickly and reliably across architectures; the “informed” sender learns faster and uses a richer effective vocabulary (SVD analysis suggests multi-dimensional symbol usage). Category-purity analyses are above chance across settings, including the intriguing emergence of a living/non-living split with only two symbols. The “object-level reference” variant appropriately removes low-level common knowledge and improves alignment. The grounding experiment is particularly convincing: supervision increases symbol purity to 70% and promotes conventional label use, while the ReferIt transfer and human evaluation (68% correct, significant by mixed-effects logistic regression) show the code supports human interpretability beyond directly supervised categories. Altogether, the methodology is simple, the ablations are sensible, and the results are compelling.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all part of my accept decision)
- Clarity: Please state explicitly how the target variable t is provided to the sender. From the text, it appears the sender receives (target, distractor) in fixed order; make this explicit in equations/architecture diagrams to avoid ambiguity.
- Reporting consistency: Table 1 vs. Table 2 scale mismatch (percent vs. proportion); also “obs–chance purity” in Table 1 appears inconsistent. Please standardize scales and verify calculations. Fix typos: “assest” → “assess”, “usufal” → “usual”.
- Stability and variance: Report results over multiple random seeds with means/SD or CIs for success rate, purity, number of symbols used, and SVD spectra. REINFORCE is high-variance—did you use a baseline or variance reduction? Sensitivity to τ (Gibbs temperature) and vocabulary size (10 vs. 100) would be valuable (learning speed, symbol entropy, purity).
- Controls for positional/shortcut strategies: Since the sender knows the target by order, add a control where input orders are randomized or occasionally swapped to rule out position-based codes. Also test games where both images are from the same class to ensure reliance on finer semantics.
- Deeper semantic analyses: Complement purity with normalized mutual information/ARI, topographic similarity (correlation between distances in visual space and symbol space), and RSA between sender embeddings and CNN feature distances. Report symbol entropy and mutual information with category labels.
- Generalization: Evaluate zero-shot generalization to unseen concepts/categories and to larger candidate sets (more than two images). Multi-symbol messages or multi-turn messages would test scalability of the approach.
- Human study details: Provide annotation UI, instructions, quality control (attention checks), and inter-annotator agreement. Consider calibrated human baselines and image–word relatedness judgments to better contextualize 68%.
- Data overlap and leakage: In the mixed supervised/game setting, clarify overlap between supervised labels and game/test images; confirm that improvements persist when supervised labels are disjoint from game categories (you partially address this with ReferIt; please quantify performance split by overlap vs. non-overlap).

Questions for the authors
1) Target encoding: Is t conveyed solely by ordering (target first) to the sender? Did you try variants where the sender does not get explicit target indication but must infer via an auxiliary cue?
2) Variability across runs: How consistent are symbol semantics and purity across seeds? Do agents converge to similar “lexicons,” or are there multiple equilibria with comparable rewards but different semantics?
3) Exploration temperature and bottleneck: How sensitive are results to τ and to vocabulary size K? Does higher τ improve semantic alignment at the cost of speed?
4) Positional bias: Can you show that the receiver’s decisions are content-driven rather than keyed to position/sender idiosyncrasies (e.g., by shuffling orders at test time)?
5) Category vs. instance information: In the object-level reference setup, what is the effect size of purity gains? Can you provide per-category analyses and examples of symbol-to-category mappings?
6) Human interpretability: In the ReferIt human study, how often were sender words semantically related via hypernymy/metonymy vs. visually associated contexts? Any error taxonomy?
7) Supervision split: If you train with supervised labels that are completely disjoint from all game categories/images, how much interpretability and purity remain?
8) Scaling up: Have you tried sequences of symbols or multi-turn interaction? Do you anticipate the same trends (faster convergence for informed architectures, better purity with object-level reference) to hold?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, carefully executed study that makes a concrete, influential step toward interactive, grounded communication. I recommend acceptance.
