Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles the timely problem of how communication emerges between learning agents and whether the resulting protocols align with human-like semantic structure. It situates this within referential games where a sender must communicate a target image to a receiver using a discrete vocabulary.
- The task design is deliberately simple and controlled, making post-hoc analysis intuitive and interpretable: two images, one symbol, one guess, with success as the only reward.
- Two sender architectures are compared: an “informed” sender with an inductive bias to compare images dimension-wise and an “agnostic” sender. Both learn to coordinate, but they exhibit strikingly different symbol-use patterns: the informed sender uses many symbols, while the agnostic sender collapses to a compact 2-symbol code.
- The paper analyzes the induced code using symbol usage statistics, SVD of a symbol–pair matrix, cluster purity relative to human semantic categories, and t-SNE visualizations. They also manipulate common knowledge by showing sender and receiver different instances of the same class (object-level reference), nudging more abstract semantics. Finally, they introduce supervised grounding (object-name prediction) alongside the game to align symbols with natural words and show human subjects can interpret the messages above chance on a new dataset.
- Overall, the work provides a clean empirical platform and evidence that simple agents can develop communication strategies that partially reflect high-level semantics, and that light supervision improves human interpretability.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) a simple, well-motivated framework yielding clear, interpretable analyses of emergent communication; (2) solid evidence (including human evaluation) that game design and light supervision can steer emergent codes toward human-like semantics.

Supporting arguments
1) What specific problem is tackled?
- How to induce communication protocols from scratch between agents via interaction, and whether the emergent code captures human-like semantic structure. Also, how environment design and minimal supervision affect the semantics and interpretability of the code.

2) Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
- Yes. The work is grounded in signaling/cheap-talk games, recent multi-agent RL communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar, Foerster), and practical neural vision–language systems. The emphasis on environment design (common knowledge manipulation) and grounding with supervised labels is thoughtful and connects to broader themes (self-play + supervision, e.g., AlphaGo).

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Coordination: Both architectures reach near-perfect accuracy quickly.
- Emergent semantics: Symbol–category purity is significantly above chance; informed senders use more symbols and show higher purity; SVD suggests multi-dimensional structure beyond trivial synonymy; object-level manipulation increases purity; human evaluation shows 68% accuracy when interpreting messages on a new dataset, significant above chance. These support claims about learnability and partial semantic alignment.
- Caveats: When the two images are from different classes, the task often collapses to coarse image classification. The observed codes frequently encode category, making the setup closely related to transfer learning. It is plausible that supervised or semi-supervised alternatives could reach similar or better outcomes faster than REINFORCE. These do not invalidate the central claims but temper the novelty of the problem difficulty and the need for RL in the basic setting.

Strengths
- Clear, minimal task enabling insightful post-learning analysis.
- Methodical comparison of inductive biases (informed vs agnostic) with robust behavioral differences in symbol usage.
- Careful semantic analyses (purity, SVD, t-SNE) and a clever manipulation of common knowledge to bias toward abstract semantics.
- Practical step toward human interpretability via mixed supervised/game training and a human subject study.
- Reproducible, scalable setting with real images and strong baselines in vision feature extraction.

Concerns and limitations (not fatal)
- Reduction to classification: With distractors from different categories, the game becomes close to supervised recognition; the symbols appear to encode class, which makes the task akin to transfer learning rather than inherently interactive language emergence.
- RL inefficiency: REINFORCE on discrete symbols is sample-inefficient; comparisons to supervised or straight-through/Gumbel relaxations are missing. A non-RL “transfer” baseline could likely learn class-to-symbol mapping faster.
- Analysis granularity: Purity is informative but coarse; more fine-grained metrics (e.g., mutual information between symbols and categories, supercategory control) would help.
- Generalization/compositionality: Little evidence of systematic generalization or compositional code (beyond coarse categories like living/non-living).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Strengthen task difficulty: Report performance and symbol analyses when target and distractor are from (a) the same supercategory, (b) the same class but different instances, to reduce trivial category cues. This will better assess whether the code goes beyond class labels.
- Add baselines: Include a supervised classifier-to-symbol baseline (sender predicts ImageNet class; receiver maps symbol to image via class prototypes) and a Gumbel-Softmax/straight-through variant to compare sample efficiency and final semantics.
- Report variance: Provide results across multiple random seeds and vocab sizes with confidence intervals; include sample complexity curves and sensitivity to temperature τ.
- Deeper semantic analysis: Measure mutual information between symbols and (super)categories, entropy/skew of symbol distribution, and per-symbol image similarity statistics.
- Explore multi-symbol messages and role rotation to assess whether longer protocols yield more compositional codes.
- Clarify fairness between architectures: Since the agnostic architecture nests the informed one, discuss optimization differences and whether the bias alone explains symbol collapse.

Questions for the authors
- How often are target/distractor pairs from the same supercategory or the same class in your main experiments? How do coordination accuracy and purity change when you enforce such “hard” pairs?
- Can you provide a supervised transfer baseline (no RL) mapping images to class symbols and the receiver to class prototypes? How does its sample efficiency and human interpretability compare?
- What is the variance across seeds for (a) speed to coordination, (b) number of symbols used, and (c) purity? Do agnostic senders always collapse to two symbols?
- In the grounding experiment, when neither image contains a supervised class, how often does the sender still use a supervised label, and what are the typical semantic relations (e.g., metonymy categories)?
- Did you try Gumbel-Softmax or other low-variance estimators for discrete communication? Any impact on learned codes and speed?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of visual features (fc vs softmax) and to vocabulary size (e.g., 10 vs 100 vs 1,000)?
- Could you quantify symbol–class mutual information and show confusion matrices to better characterize the induced lexicon?

Overall, the paper presents a clean, influential framework with convincing initial evidence and actionable analyses. Addressing the above points would further strengthen both scientific rigor and practical relevance.
