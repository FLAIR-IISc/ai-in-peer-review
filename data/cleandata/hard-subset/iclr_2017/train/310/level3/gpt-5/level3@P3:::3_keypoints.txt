- Proposes a simple referential game where a sender emits one discrete symbol to identify a target among two images and a receiver must pick the target; agents are feed-forward networks trained with REINFORCE on pretrained VGG features.
- Shows both “agnostic” and “informed” senders converge to near-perfect accuracy, with informed senders converging faster and using a richer subset of the vocabulary.
- Learned symbols correlate with semantic concepts (above-chance purity); even two symbols discover a living vs. non-living split.
- Removing low-level common knowledge (different instances for sender/receiver) increases semantic purity and interpretability.
- Alternating self-play with supervised image classification (shared embeddings) grounds symbols in natural language and yields many symbols aligned with object names.
- Human subjects identify intended referents 68% of the time in zero-shot human-agent interaction after grounding.
- Claims novelty in first learning agent-to-agent communication and then grounding it in human language, offering an alternative to supervised seq2seq grounding.
- Decision: Accept — problem is clear, framework simple and convincing, and empirical evidence is strong and diverse.
- Paper addresses learning emergent communication that supports coordination and can be nudged toward human-interpretable semantics with minimal supervision.
- Well motivated and positioned in literature; builds on signaling games and neural multi-agent work but focuses on semantic interpretability and alternating supervision.
- Results largely support claims: consistent convergence, purity and t-SNE analyses show semantic structure, and human study provides external validation.
- Clear comparison between agnostic and informed sender architectures demonstrating interpretable differences in symbol usage and learning speed.
- Careful experiments show how environmental tweaks shape emergent semantics.
- Grounding experiment via shared embeddings is simple, elegant, and practically impactful.
- Caveat: reliance on pretrained VGG features means agents are not fully tabula rasa; visual priors influence language emergence.
- Caveat: robustness claims need more evidence—report mean ± std over multiple seeds and variance-reduction details for REINFORCE.
- Caveat: semantic evaluation is limited to purity; should include other metrics like topographic similarity or normalized mutual information.
- Request clarification: explicitly state how target t is encoded (ordering vs explicit marker) and whether embedding/weights are shared across image positions.
- Request: report variance/stability (means ± std, learning curves with confidence bands) and mention any REINFORCE baselines used.
- Ablations requested: vocabulary size beyond K=100, temperature sensitivity, consistent sm vs fc feature comparisons, and sender weight-sharing variant.
- Semantic-eval requests: compute topographic similarity or MI, and control for label frequency when assessing chance purity.
- Human-eval requests: provide participant demographics, quality controls, inter-annotator agreement, failure examples, and a no-supervision baseline to quantify causal effect.
- Scope/claim edits: avoid “tabula rasa” phrasing; clarify novelty relative to contemporary emergent communication/grounding work and emphasize alternating supervision and human eval.
- Reporting issues: inconsistent table scales (percent vs fraction), likely scaling error in “obs-chance purity,” typos (“assest”→“assess”, “usufal”→“usual”), and malformed reference formatting.
- Direct questions for authors: is t encoded only by ordering and are embedding layers shared; what variance-reduction was used and sensitivity to seeds; how do performance and purity scale with large vocabularies and multi-token messages; does alternated supervision help with disjoint supervised labels (zero-shot grounding); can a fresh receiver or human pick up the convention without fine-tuning?
