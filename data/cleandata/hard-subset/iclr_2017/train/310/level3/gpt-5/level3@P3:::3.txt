1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a simple but effective referential game to study the emergence of communication between neural agents. A sender observes a target indicator t and two images, emits a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary, and a receiver must infer the target image from the symbol and the same image pair. Agents are feed-forward neural networks trained with REINFORCE on top of fixed, pretrained VGG image features. The paper shows:
- Agents reliably converge to near-optimal policies (near-perfect accuracy) with both “agnostic” and “informed” sender architectures; the latter converges faster and uses a richer subset of the vocabulary.
- The learned symbols correlate with meaningful concepts: symbol–object assignments exhibit above-chance purity w.r.t. human-defined semantic categories; even with only two symbols, agents discover a living-vs-non-living split.
- Modifying the game to remove low-level common knowledge (sender and receiver see different instances of the same classes) increases semantic “purity” and yields more interpretable symbol-to-concept mappings.
- A multi-game setup that alternates self-play with supervised image classification (shared embeddings) grounds symbols in natural language and improves human interpretability: many symbols align with object names, and human subjects select the intended referent 68% of the time in zero-shot human-agent interaction.
- The work argues that learning shared communication representations in multi-agent settings is important and challenging, and motivates starting from a minimalist task. It also claims novelty in first learning agent-to-agent communication and then grounding it in human language, offering an alternative to purely supervised seq2seq approaches.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated problem and a simple framework that convincingly demonstrates the emergence and partial semantic alignment of communication.
- Strong, diverse empirical evidence (learning dynamics, symbol usage analyses, human evaluation, and grounding via supervised alternation) that supports the paper’s claims and provides a blueprint for scalable grounding.

3. Supporting arguments
Answering the review questions:
- What problem is tackled? Learning emergent communication between agents that is useful for coordination and that can be nudged toward human-interpretable semantics; and how to ground such emergent codes in natural language with minimal supervision.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper builds on signaling games and recent neural multi-agent work (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) but distinctively focuses on semantic interpretability and grounding to human language via alternating supervision. The case for starting with a simple one-symbol, two-image game is persuasive as a controlled testbed.
- Do results support the claims? Largely yes. Convergence to high accuracy is consistent across settings; purity and t-SNE analyses indicate nontrivial semantic structure; the object-level variation manipulation increases semantic alignment; and the human study provides external validation. The supervised alternation markedly improves interpretability without sacrificing performance.

Additional strengths:
- Clear architectural comparison (agnostic vs informed sender) with interpretable differences in symbol usage and learning speed.
- Careful experimental design demonstrating how environmental tweaks (removing low-level common knowledge) shape emergent semantics.
- The grounding experiment is simple, elegant, and impactful: shared embeddings provide a practical route to human-aligned communication.

Caveats (do not change my overall assessment):
- Reliance on pretrained visual features means agents are not fully tabula rasa; the language emerges built on a rich visual prior.
- “Optimal policy” is evaluated as near-perfect accuracy; stronger evidence of robustness (multiple seeds, variance) would be helpful.
- Semantic evaluation could be expanded beyond purity (e.g., topographic similarity, mutual information controlling for label priors).

4. Feedback and questions to improve the paper
These points aim to help strengthen clarity and rigor.
- Clarify incorporation of the target variable t: From the text it appears t is encoded solely by ordering (sender always receives [target, distractor] in that order). Please make this explicit in Section 3, state whether embeddings/weights are shared across positions, and consider a brief ablation (ordering vs an explicit binary marker vs positional embeddings).
- Variance and stability: Report mean ± std over multiple random seeds for accuracy, purity, and symbol usage. Include learning curves with confidence bands. State whether any variance-reduction baseline was used for REINFORCE.
- Controls and ablations:
  - Vocabulary size: Show how purity and symbol usage scale beyond K = 100.
  - Temperature τ: Analyze sensitivity; does near-deterministic sampling (low τ) hurt exploration or semantic structure?
  - Image features: Compare sm vs fc consistently across all settings; discuss whether higher-layer features overly bias semantics.
  - Sender architecture: Explicitly report whether the agnostic sender shares embedding weights between the two image positions; if not, add a variant with shared weights to disentangle inductive biases.
- Semantic evaluation:
  - Report topographic similarity (correlation between distances in input space and symbol distributions) or mutual information between categories and symbols normalized by entropy to complement purity.
  - Control for label frequency when computing chance purity.
- Human evaluation:
  - Provide more detail on the ReferIt-based study: participant demographics, quality controls, inter-annotator agreement, and examples of failure modes.
  - Include a condition without supervised alternation to quantify the causal effect on human interpretability on the same dataset.
- Scope and claims:
  - Reconcile “tabula rasa” phrasing with the use of pretrained VGG features; “tabula rasa communicators on top of pretrained perception” would be more accurate.
  - Situate the novelty claim more precisely relative to contemporary work on emergent communication and grounding; emphasize the alternating supervision mechanism and human evaluation as distinctive contributions.

Reporting issues and typos:
- Table scales: Table 1 vs Table 2 mix percentages in [0,100] and [0,1]. Please use a single scale everywhere. The “obs-chance purity” in Table 1 likely has a scaling error.
- Typos: “assest” → “assess”; “usufal” → “usual”.
- References have several formatting artifacts (“? \Q2014\E”). Please clean these.

Questions for the authors:
- Is t encoded only by input ordering to the sender? Are embedding layers shared across the two image positions? Did you try adding an explicit binary target flag?
- What variance-reduction techniques (if any) were used in REINFORCE? How sensitive are results to random seeds?
- How does performance and purity change with larger vocabularies (e.g., 500, 1,000) and with sequences of symbols (multi-token messages)?
- Does alternated supervision help when the supervised labels are disjoint from the game’s object set (fully zero-shot grounding)?
- Can the receiver be swapped with a fresh, separately trained receiver (or a human) without fine-tuning and maintain performance, indicating shared conventions rather than idiosyncratic codes?
