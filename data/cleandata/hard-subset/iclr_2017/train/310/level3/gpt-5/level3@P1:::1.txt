Summary of contributions
The paper studies language emergence via multi-agent communication in a referential game. Two neural agents (sender and receiver) learn to coordinate on real-world images using a discrete, arbitrary symbol vocabulary, with success judged by whether the receiver identifies the target image. The work is well-motivated by the limitations of purely supervised, passive learning for interactive communication and is well-situated alongside recent multi-agent RL communication work while emphasizing interpretability and alignment with human semantics. Key contributions include: (i) demonstrating that simple feed-forward agents trained with REINFORCE can reliably establish near-perfect communication; (ii) showing that an “informed” sender architecture with an inductive bias toward comparing image embeddings dimension-wise converges faster and uses a richer symbol set than an “agnostic” sender, which tends to collapse to two symbols; (iii) evidence that emergent symbols correlate with high-level semantic categories (e.g., McRae categories, living vs non-living), not just low-level visual features; (iv) an environment tweak (removing some common knowledge by showing different images from the same class to sender and receiver) that nudges symbol meanings toward object-level semantics; and (v) a grounding strategy combining self-play with supervised object naming, yielding higher symbol purity, many directly interpretable symbols, and promising human interpretability on ReferIt-style stimuli (68% accuracy when humans replace the receiver).

Decision: Weak Accept
Reasons: (1) Important problem and clear, interpretable setting that advances understanding of emergent communication and its alignment with human semantics; (2) Empirical evidence, including human studies, supports the main claims; (3) The analysis of how architectural bias and environmental design shape emergent codes is novel and insightful.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: The paper tackles how to design environments/inductive biases so that emergent communication is effective and human-aligned—central to building interactive agents. It engages well with literature in multi-agent RL and language emergence, and it emphasizes interpretability and human grounding beyond prior work.

- Methodology: The referential game is simple yet revealing. Using real images (ImageNet-derived) and pretrained VGG features offers realism and scalability. The discrete symbol bottleneck and the receiver’s dot-product mechanism produce an analyzable communication protocol. The informed sender’s architectural bias is justified and empirically supported by faster convergence and broader symbol usage.

- Claims and evidence: The agents reach high coordination across settings. Symbol-category alignment is assessed via cluster purity against McRae categories and by t-SNE visualizations showing local coherence in CNN space. The object-level reference variant strengthens the case for higher-level semantics when low-level common knowledge is suppressed. The grounding experiment convincingly shows that mixing supervised naming with self-play greatly improves interpretability (70% purity; 47% exact production of the conventional label for supervised categories; use of 88–100 symbols) and that humans can use the sender’s symbols to perform the task above chance on out-of-domain images.

- Interpretability: The observation that the agnostic sender collapses to two symbols while the informed sender exploits many more is interesting and consistent with the inductive bias design. The SVD analysis suggests nontrivial dimensional structure, not mere synonymy. Qualitative metonymic uses (e.g., “dolphin” for sea) mirror natural language flexibility, bolstering the paper’s thesis.

- Rigor: Training details are provided, and statistics for the human study (mixed-effects logistic regression) indicate careful analysis. While some analyses rely on visualization (t-SNE), there are quantitative metrics (purity, usage counts, human success rates).

Caveats and limitations (not decision-critical)
- Reduction to classification/transfer: In many trials with different categories, the task effectively reduces to image classification, and emergent symbols often encode class identity. The environment design partially explains the strong category-level purity. Moreover, because perception uses VGG features trained on ImageNet, high-level semantics are inherited; the agents are not “tabula rasa” perceptually.

- RL efficiency vs alternatives: For protocols approximating class labels, supervised/transfer learning could learn mappings faster than REINFORCE. The paper mitigates this by including a mixed supervised/self-play regime, but the efficiency trade-off is not quantified.

- Limited communication bandwidth: Single-symbol, one-shot communication is a severe bottleneck; while purposeful here, it limits conclusions about compositionality and dialogue.

- Reporting completeness: The paper would benefit from variance across random seeds, ablations on temperature, vocabulary size, and more detailed symbol-usage distributions.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Quantify the proportion of same-category vs different-category trials and report performance/purity separately; include within-category discrimination difficulty to address “reduces to classification” concerns.
- Provide learning curves and sample efficiency comparisons between self-play only, supervision only, and mixed, ideally with matched budgets.
- Report robustness across seeds and hyperparameters (temperature τ, embedding size, vocabulary sizes 10 vs 100) and include confidence intervals.
- Add stronger semantic analyses beyond purity and t-SNE (e.g., Adjusted Mutual Information, clustering stability, relational tests like living vs non-living, artifact vs natural, and confusion matrices).
- Explore simple multi-symbol or multi-turn variants to probe whether compositional structure emerges and whether this improves fine-grained discrimination.
- Clarify the extent to which pretrained perception shapes semantics by testing alternative feature extractors or self-supervised features.

Questions for the authors
1) What fraction of training/test pairs are same-category vs different-category, and how does performance and symbol purity differ across these subsets?
2) How sensitive are your results (coordination success, symbol counts, purity) to random initialization and τ? Please report mean ± std across multiple runs.
3) Can you quantify sample efficiency comparisons among: (a) self-play only, (b) supervised naming only (then fixed sender playing the game), and (c) mixed regime?
4) To what extent do pretrained VGG features drive the observed semantic alignment? Have you tried earlier-layer features or self-supervised features, and how do purity/human interpretability change?
5) In the SVD analysis of symbol usage, can you provide the explained variance curve and effective rank across settings, to substantiate the “multi-dimensional” claim?
6) In the human study, do specific words or semantic groups account for most successes? Any analysis of error types (e.g., near-miss relations, background-driven choices)?
7) Have you tried two-symbol messages or short sequences? Does the informed sender’s advantage persist, and do you observe more compositionality?
