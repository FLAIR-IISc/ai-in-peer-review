Summary and contributions
The paper studies how communication protocols can emerge between learning agents through interaction rather than passive exposure to text. It introduces a simple but compelling referential game in which a sender views two images and a target indicator and must emit a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary for a receiver to identify the target. Both agents are neural networks trained end-to-end with REINFORCE on precomputed visual features. The paper shows:
- Agents reliably learn to coordinate to near-perfect accuracy across architectures and vocabulary sizes.
- The induced “words” correlate with meaningful visual/conceptual properties: symbols align above chance with broad semantic categories, and symbol usage respects the geometry of high-level CNN embeddings.
- Tweaking the environment to remove low-level common knowledge (showing sender/receiver different instances of the same classes) increases symbol-category alignment.
- Mixing interactive play with supervised object naming grounds symbols in natural language, substantially improving purity and enabling partial human interpretability: in a human study, subjects chose the sender-intended image 68% of the time even when the target did not literally contain the supervised label.

The work is well-motivated as an alternative to purely supervised seq2seq training for dialog, and it connects to cheap-talk signaling games and emergent communication in multi-agent RL.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-placed motivation and a simple, scalable framework that convincingly demonstrates the emergence of communication and semantically meaningful symbols.
- Strong empirical evidence, including a human evaluation, that supervision can ground emergent codes in natural language and generalize beyond the supervised categories.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? The paper asks how to design multi-agent environments in which communication emerges and whether the resulting codes are portable and human-interpretable. The referential game isolates grounded reference and measures both coordination success and semantic structure of emergent symbols.

2) Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed? Yes. The paper compellingly argues that interactive learning captures the functional nature of language absent from passive supervised training. It is situated relative to multi-agent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), linguistic/game-theoretic work on signaling games, and practical grounding via supervised labels in the spirit of AlphaGo’s blend of supervised and self-play.

3) Do the results support the claims? Largely yes.
- Convergence and performance: Agents consistently reach near-perfect accuracy; informed sender converges faster and uses more symbols.
- Semantic structure: Category purity is above chance across settings, including an emergent living/non-living split even with only two symbols. SVD analyses suggest nontrivial symbol usage beyond a degenerate few-symbol strategy; t-SNE visualizations indicate symbol neighborhoods align with visual similarity.
- Environment shaping: Showing different instances per class boosts category-level symbol alignment, consistent with the “common knowledge” manipulation.
- Grounding and human interpretability: Alternating supervised naming grows symbol repertoire (88/100 used) and raises purity (to 70%); in a separate dataset (ReferIt), human listeners pick the intended image 68% of the time based solely on the sender’s emitted word, indicating meaningful generalization.

The methodology is appropriate (policy gradients on discrete symbols, clear architectures). While some analyses are qualitative, the main claims are supported by multiple complementary evaluations.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all are decision-critical)
- Cross-play and portability: Evaluate whether independently trained speakers and listeners can understand each other (cross-play). This would directly probe portability to new partners.
- Generalization and robustness: Report variance over multiple random seeds, learning curves with confidence intervals, and sample efficiency (games to reach a fixed accuracy). Include ablations on vocabulary size, temperature/annealing, and image features (sm vs fc).
- Reporting and metrics:
  - Unify percentage scales across tables (Table 1 vs Table 2) and correct the “obs-chance purity” inconsistency in Table 1.
  - Complement purity with ARI/NMI and statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap vs random label permutations).
  - Consider reporting topographic similarity (correlation between semantic and signal distances) to further quantify groundedness.
- Human study details: Provide per-word accuracy, confidence intervals, and example failure cases. Clarify whether any pairs contained the supervised label literally and how those were handled in analysis.
- Alternative listeners: Test human-in-the-loop on the original image set (not only ReferIt) to quantify interpretability in-domain.
- Compositionality and message length: Discuss extensions to multi-symbol messages and whether symbols compose to cover novel combinations of attributes.
- Architecture clarity and fairness: More explicitly justify the “informed” sender inductive bias and discuss its generality; add an ablation showing that the agnostic sender can match semantics with more capacity or training.
- Reproducibility: Release code and data splits; detail preprocessing, train/test independence across ImageNet classes/instances, and hyperparameters (including whether temperature is annealed).

Questions for the authors
- Sender input and target signal: How exactly is the target variable t incorporated into the sender? Is it solely via fixed ordering of (target, distractor) at the sender input, or is t provided as an explicit binary input? If via ordering, did you verify that the sender does not overfit to positional biases?
- Cross-play and partner swap: If you train multiple sender/receiver pairs independently, do their protocols interoperate (speaker A with listener B)? Does supervised grounding improve cross-play?
- Stability and variance: What is the variance of final accuracy, purity, and symbol usage across runs (different seeds)? Please report mean±std with N runs.
- Vocabulary size: How do results (purity, number of symbols used, human interpretability) scale with vocabulary sizes beyond 10 and 100?
- Feature dependence: How sensitive are the results to using fc vs sm features? Do lower-layer features reduce semantic purity (supporting the “high-level semantics” claim)?
- Purity computation: How are clusters built (argmax symbol per object)? How many images per object were used, and how was chance computed (random permutations over objects or symbols)? Please include confidence intervals.
- Human evaluation: Were any trials excluded because the supervised word literally matched a visible object? Can you provide accuracy separately for “literal” vs “metonymic” uses?
- Training details: Did you use a baseline or variance reduction for REINFORCE? Any entropy regularization? Was τ fixed at 10 or annealed?
- Role randomization: The receiver sees images in random order; did you try randomizing the sender’s input order as a control, and how did it affect learning speed/semantics?

Minor edits
- Fix typos: “assest” → “assess”, “usufal” → “usual”.
- Clean up references formatting inconsistencies.

Overall, this is a timely, well-executed paper that makes a clear, empirical contribution to emergent communication and grounding.
