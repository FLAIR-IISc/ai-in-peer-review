- Decision: Reject.

- Paper proposes multi-agent referential game framework where two neural agents learn to communicate and explores environmental effects on emergent semantics and grounding to natural language.

- Main concern: Emergent “language” maps to existing semantic categories (e.g., living vs. non-living) rather than a novel or compositional language.

- Analysis shows symbol usage correlates with ImageNet categories, effectively making the task a form of transfer learning to map categories to symbols.

- Reinforcement learning appears unnecessarily complex for the observed outcome; a supervised baseline could be simpler and more efficient.

- The grounding phase admits a link to supervised learning, weakening the claim of language emergence from scratch.

- “Object-level reference” experiments (same-class images) simply reinforce category-based mapping and do not demonstrate communication about relations or complex concepts.

- The paper lacks sufficient engagement with game-theoretic framing (cheap talk), including implications for convergence and equilibrium selection.

- Need for explicit comparison to supervised baselines to justify the multi-agent RL approach.

- Recommend investigating compositional structure and communication about inter-object relationships or complex scenes.

- Suggest more thorough ablation studies (e.g., role of 1D convolutions) to isolate architectural contributions.

- Request deeper quantitative analysis of symbol usage beyond purity, including symbol distributions and overlap across categories.

- Ask for more detailed analysis of learned embeddings to identify clusters or patterns beyond ImageNet correlations.

- Questions for authors: comparisons to supervised baselines, performance when images share the same category, embedding analyses, hyperparameter sensitivity, and effect of different grounding labels or methods.
