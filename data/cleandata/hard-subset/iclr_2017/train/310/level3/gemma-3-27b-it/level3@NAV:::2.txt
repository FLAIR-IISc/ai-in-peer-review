## Review of "A Framework for Language Learning in Multi-Agent Communication Games"

This paper presents a compelling framework for investigating language emergence through multi-agent referential games. The core idea – leveraging interactive gameplay to develop communication protocols without relying solely on large, static datasets – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in AI: building truly interactive and communicative agents. The authors convincingly argue for the limitations of purely supervised learning for this purpose, highlighting the importance of the *functional* aspects of language alongside its structural properties. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the initial results demonstrate that agents can indeed learn to coordinate and develop interpretable communication signals. The addition of supervised learning to ground the emergent language is a particularly promising direction.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a challenging and important problem, proposes a sound approach, and presents reasonable results that support its claims. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The strength of this work lies in its focus on a fundamentally different learning paradigm. While prior work exists in multi-agent communication (mentioned by the authors), this paper provides a structured framework and insightful analysis of how environmental factors can influence the characteristics of the emergent language. The exploration of semantic properties – aligning symbol usage with object categories – and the subsequent grounding through supervised learning are strong contributions. The results, showing both successful coordination and a degree of semantic interpretability, are encouraging. 

**Additional Feedback:**

The description of the network architecture (Section 3) and Figure 1 are difficult to follow. A more detailed diagram, clearly labeling all connections and layers, is crucial. A caption explaining the key components and their roles would also be beneficial. While I understand the general approach, my limited familiarity with the most closely related literature makes it difficult to fully assess the novelty of the specific architectural choices. Providing a more detailed comparison to Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016), specifically outlining the differences in approach and contributions, would strengthen the paper. 

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the Reinforce algorithm for parameter updates? Were other RL algorithms considered, and if so, why was Reinforce selected?
2.  In Section 5, you mention combining game playing and supervised learning “equiprobably”. Was the 50/50 split optimized in any way, or was it chosen heuristically? How sensitive are the results to this ratio?
3.  Regarding the human evaluation in Section 5, could you provide more details about the demographics and expertise of the CrowdFlower participants? Was any pre-screening done to ensure they were familiar with the objects being depicted?
