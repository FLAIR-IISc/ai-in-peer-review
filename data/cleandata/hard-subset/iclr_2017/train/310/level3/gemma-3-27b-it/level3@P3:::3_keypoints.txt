- Proposes a novel framework for learning communication via multi-agent referential games where neural agents send symbols to identify a target image from a pair.  
- Agents reliably converge to effective communication strategies and the learned symbols exhibit semantic meaning, especially when influenced by image class information.  
- Intermittent supervised training of the sender (grounding) further improves interpretability and alignment with human concepts.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Presents a strong contribution to multi-agent learning and emergent communication as an alternative to supervised language learning.  
- Experimental setup is rigorous: uses pre-trained VGG features, REINFORCE, and explores different sender architectures and vocabulary sizes.  
- Results show successful convergence, purity metrics aligning symbols with object categories, and improved purity/interpretability with supervised grounding; human evaluation achieves 68% accuracy.  
- Situates work well within related literature (Lewis signaling game, multi-agent communication, linguistics, game theory) and highlights novelty in agent-to-agent learning then grounding.  
- Requests clarification on how the target variable t is encoded/used by the sender network.  
- Flags inconsistent percentage values between Table 1 and Table 2 and questions the "obs-chance purity" value in Table 1.  
- Minor typos to correct (e.g., "assest"→"assess", "usufal"→"usual").  
- Suggests additional visualizations (UMAP/t-SNE of symbol embeddings) to reveal semantic structure of emergent language.  
- Recommends a more detailed ablation study on hyperparameters (embedding dimensionality, learning rate, temperature) and their effects on communication and semantic purity.  
- Asks authors to (1) elaborate on t incorporation, (2) confirm obs-chance purity calculation, (3) report any biases in which semantic properties emerge (color, shape, etc.), and (4) consider exploring/metapragmatically leveraging observed metonymic links.
