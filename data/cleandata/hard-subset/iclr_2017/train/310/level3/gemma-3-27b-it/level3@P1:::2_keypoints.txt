- Introduces a framework for language learning via multi-agent referential games, emphasizing interactive learning over passive supervised approaches.

- Decision: Accept.

- Shows simple neural networks can learn to coordinate in referential games and develop an emergent language.

- Demonstrates that environment design (e.g., focusing on object categories vs low-level features) shapes the semantic properties of the emergent language.

- Presents a combined interactive + supervised grounding approach that substantially improves human interpretability (68% human interpretation accuracy).

- Strength: Well-motivated approach addressing limitations of static datasets with a clean experimental referential-game setup.

- Figure 1 unclear: diagram missing connection details—needs clearer visualization and caption explaining data flow, convolutions, embeddings, and vocabulary layers.

- Related work section cursory: needs deeper comparison to prior multi-agent communication work (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) and discussion of relative strengths/weaknesses.

- Ablation study recommended to quantify the relative contributions of game-based learning vs supervised grounding (e.g., supervised-only and game-only baselines).

- Analyze “failed” cases where humans misinterpret symbols to reveal limitations and guide future improvements.

- Clarify the role of "cheap talk" games and Nash equilibrium discussion or remove it if not clearly connected to empirical results.

- Questions for authors include requests to (1) clarify network architecture/figure, (2) compare to prior work in learning dynamics and emergent language, (3) justify hyperparameter choices and sensitivity, (4) elaborate on observed metonymic links and patterns, and (5) discuss challenges of integrating human players into the game.
