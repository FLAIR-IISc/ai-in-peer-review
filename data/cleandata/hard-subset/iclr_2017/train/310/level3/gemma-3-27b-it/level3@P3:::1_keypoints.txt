- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Paper investigates emergence of communication in a referential game to see if agent strategies align with human language development.
- Framework: agents learn to communicate through interaction without pre-defined protocols; referential game with ImageNet images for realism.
- Main empirical findings: successful coordination between agents and informed senders use a richer symbol vocabulary than agnostic senders.
- Evidence of semantic grounding via cluster purity and t-SNE visualizations; supervised grounding shows promise for human interpretability.
- Strength: clear motivation that interactive learning captures functional aspects of communication better than pure supervision.
- Strength: well-designed experimental setup that situates itself within multi-agent communication, linguistics, and game theory literature.
- Critique: task can reduce to image classification when images are from different categories; implications need deeper discussion (fundamental limitation vs. mitigations).
- Critique: resemblance to transfer learning—expand on why pre-trained representations speed learning and how RL framework could incorporate pre-training to improve efficiency.
- Critique: justify the informed sender’s 1D convolutional filter architecture and consider alternative architectures for dimension-wise combination.
- Critique: symbol redundancy/SVD hints at synonymy but requires more detailed analysis of interchangeable symbols and their characteristics.
- Critique: human interpretability experiment (CrowdFlower) needs survey design details and examples of failed communications to analyze limitations.
- Questions for authors: ways to make the game less like simple classification (noise, ambiguity), experience with further pre-trained representations, participant framing in human experiments, sensitivity and rationale for temperature τ, and mechanisms behind supervised-generalization to unsupervised categories.
