## Review of “The current mainstream approach to train natural language systems is to expose them to large amounts of text…”

This paper tackles the important problem of understanding how communication emerges between agents, and whether the strategies employed in this emergent communication resemble human language development. The authors propose a framework based on multi-agent referential games, where agents learn to communicate to identify target images. The use of a relatively simple task is a strength, as it allows for more intuitive post-learning analysis of the learned “language”. The finding that informed agents utilize multiple symbols while agnostic agents rely on fewer is interesting, suggesting the architecture influences communicative complexity. 

I recommend **Reject**ion, primarily due to concerns about the task's effective reduction to image classification and its relationship to transfer learning. While the emergent communication is interesting, the setup appears to shortcut the complexities of genuine language emergence.

The core issue is that, particularly when images are drawn from different categories, the task effectively becomes an image classification problem. The sender essentially needs to encode which *category* the target image belongs to, rather than engaging in nuanced reference. This significantly simplifies the challenge. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge the underlying image representations come from a pre-trained CNN (VGG). This feels closer to a transfer learning problem – leveraging existing visual knowledge – rather than a purely emergent communication system built from scratch via reinforcement learning. The reinforcement learning component seems almost unnecessary in this setup, as a supervised approach targeting image classification might achieve similar or better performance far more efficiently. The results, while demonstrating learning, don't convincingly demonstrate *language* emergence, but rather efficient encoding of pre-existing visual categories.

Additional feedback: 

*   **Clarification on task difficulty:** The authors should address the point about the task reducing to image classification. How do the results change if images within the *same* category are used? This would necessitate more subtle communication and provide stronger evidence for emergent language.
*   **Comparison to baselines:** How does the performance of these agents compare to a simple baseline that directly maps image categories to symbols? This would help establish the value of the learned communication protocol beyond a simple lookup table.
*   **Symbol interpretability beyond category:** The analysis of symbol purity focuses on category alignment. While relevant, it doesn’t explore whether symbols capture finer-grained visual features *within* a category.
*   **Impact of vocabulary size:** The paper explores vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100. What happens with even larger vocabulary sizes? Does the complexity of the learned “language” increase appropriately?



I would like to understand how sensitive the reported results are to the choice of image representation (fc vs. sm layers). Does switching image representations significantly alter the learning dynamics or the interpretability of the emergent language? Additionally, what is the computational cost of training compared to a supervised classification baseline for this task?
