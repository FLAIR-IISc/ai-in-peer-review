This paper introduces a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication in the context of referential games. The authors demonstrate that two simple neural networks can learn to coordinate and communicate, and further explore how environmental changes can influence the semantics of the emergent language. They also present a method for grounding the agents’ code in natural language through a combined game-playing and supervised learning approach.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper addresses an interesting and important problem and presents a well-defined experimental setup, the results, as presented, do not demonstrate the emergence of a truly novel language but rather a mapping to existing semantic categories, and the reinforcement learning approach appears unnecessarily complex for the demonstrated outcome.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary concern lies in the interpretation of the emergent “language.” The authors acknowledge that, even with two symbols, the agnostic sender converges to a "living-vs-non-living" characterization. This suggests the learned communication is not truly novel, but rather a mapping to pre-existing, high-level distinctions. While the informed sender uses more symbols, the analysis reveals that these symbols correlate with the underlying ImageNet categories. This effectively transforms the problem into a form of transfer learning, where the agent learns to map image categories to symbols, rather than developing a compositional language. The authors themselves acknowledge the link to supervised learning when they introduce the grounding phase. 

Furthermore, the choice of reinforcement learning seems excessive given the observed outcome. If the goal is primarily to associate symbols with image categories, a supervised learning approach directly targeting this task would likely be more efficient and simpler. The computational cost and complexity of reinforcement learning do not seem justified by the achieved results. The authors briefly mention this is akin to a cheap talk game, but do not delve into the theoretical implications or limitations within the established game theory framework. 

The improvements observed with the “object-level reference” modification, where agents are presented with images from the same class, further support the critique. Reducing the task to distinguishing instances within a category does not demonstrate the ability to communicate about more complex concepts or relationships. It simply reinforces the category-based mapping.

Finally, while the grounding experiment shows promising results in human interpretability, it relies heavily on the pre-existing labels from ImageNet. The improvement in interpretability is largely due to the agents learning to use the existing names, rather than developing a truly grounded language from scratch.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the novelty:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this work differs from existing transfer learning approaches. Explicitly comparing the results to a supervised baseline would strengthen the argument for the value of the proposed multi-agent framework.
*   **Investigate compositional structure:** The current experiments focus on referential communication about individual objects. It would be valuable to explore whether the agents can learn to communicate about relationships *between* objects or more complex scenes.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** Elaborate on the connection to cheap talk games and the implications of the observed results within this theoretical framework. Address potential issues related to convergence and equilibrium selection.
*   **Ablation studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the architecture, such as the 1D convolutions in the informed sender.
*   **Quantitative analysis of symbol usage:** Beyond purity, provide a more detailed quantitative analysis of symbol usage patterns. For instance, analyze the distribution of symbols across different image categories and assess the degree of overlap.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Have you compared the performance of the reinforcement learning agents to a supervised learning baseline trained directly on the image-to-symbol mapping task? If so, what were the results?
2.  The task seems to be easier when the two images are of different categories. Does the performance degrade when the two images belong to the same category? If so, how?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned embeddings? Are there any meaningful clusters or patterns that emerge in the embedding space beyond the correlation with ImageNet categories?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the embedding dimensionality, the number of filters, and the temperature parameter?
5.  The grounding experiment relies on ImageNet labels. How would the results change if you used a different set of labels or a different grounding method?
