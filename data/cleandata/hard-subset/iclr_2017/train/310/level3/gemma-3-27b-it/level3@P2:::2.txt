This paper introduces a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication, specifically within the context of referential games. The authors demonstrate that two neural networks, acting as sender and receiver, can learn to coordinate and develop a communication protocol from scratch. Further, they explore modifications to the game environment to encourage the emergence of more interpretable “word meanings” and present a strategy to ground this emergent code in natural language via a combined interactive/supervised learning approach.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper tackles an important problem – moving beyond passive learning for natural language systems to develop truly interactive agents – and presents a compelling approach with promising results. The experimental setup is well-designed, the results are positive and insightful, and the combination of game-based and supervised learning shows particular promise.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for this work is clear and well-articulated. The paper convincingly argues that supervised learning from static datasets falls short of capturing the functional aspects of communication—its role in coordination and action. The proposed framework, utilizing referential games, provides a principled way to address this limitation by allowing agents to bootstrap language through interaction. 

The experimental results are convincing. The agents demonstrably learn to coordinate, and the modifications to the game environment, such as removing low-level visual details, demonstrably influence the characteristics of the learned communication protocol, nudging it towards more semantically meaningful representations. The grounding of the agent’s code into natural language through a combination of game-playing and supervised classification is a significant achievement, showing a clear path towards creating agents capable of interacting with humans. The human evaluation (Section 5) provides strong evidence that the emergent language, while not perfect, exhibits a degree of interpretability. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The connections to relevant work in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory are thoroughly discussed, demonstrating a strong understanding of the broader research landscape. While the reviewer admits limited familiarity with all related work (as noted in the provided key points), the authors do a good job of positioning their contribution within the existing literature. The inclusion of references to classic papers in the field is particularly commendable.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is generally strong, there are a few areas where clarification or expansion would be beneficial. 

*   **Figure 1:** This figure is crucial for understanding the network architectures but is difficult to parse. The omission of connections between layers and the lack of a detailed caption severely hinders comprehension. A revised figure with clearly labeled connections and a comprehensive caption explaining the role of each component would greatly improve clarity.
*   **Agnostic vs. Informed Sender:** While the paper discusses the differences in performance between these two architectures, a more detailed explanation of *why* the informed sender converges faster and produces more interpretable symbols would be valuable. Is it solely the inductive bias of the convolutional filters, or are there other factors at play?
*   **Vocabulary Size:** The choice of vocabulary sizes (10 and 100) seems somewhat arbitrary. A brief discussion of how the performance and characteristics of the emergent language change with different vocabulary sizes could provide further insights.
*   **Reward Function:** The current reward function is binary (win/loss). Exploring alternative reward structures (e.g., proportional to accuracy or complexity of communication) might lead to different outcomes and provide a deeper understanding of the learning dynamics.
*   **Scalability:** While the framework demonstrates success with relatively simple images, a discussion of the challenges of scaling this approach to more complex visual scenes and more sophisticated communication tasks would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the specific architectures chosen for the sender and receiver networks? Were other architectures considered, and if so, why were these selected?
2.  In Section 5, you mention a “metonymic” link between symbols and image content. Could you provide more examples of these metonymic mappings observed in your experiments? This would help illustrate this interesting phenomenon.
3.  How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, batch size, and temperature parameter? Did you perform any hyperparameter optimization, and if so, what were the key findings?
4.  Given the success of grounding the communication through supervised learning, do you foresee any potential drawbacks or limitations of this approach? For example, could it lead to a bias towards human-centric language and hinder the emergence of truly novel communication strategies?
