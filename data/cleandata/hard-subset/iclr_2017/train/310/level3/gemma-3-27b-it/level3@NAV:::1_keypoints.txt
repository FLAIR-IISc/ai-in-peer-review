- Studies emergence of communication via multi-agent referential games; simple task facilitates post-learning analysis.  
- Strength: finding that informed agents use multiple symbols while agnostic agents use fewer, suggesting architecture affects communicative complexity.  
- Recommendation: Reject, mainly because the task appears reducible to image classification, undermining claims of language emergence.  
- Core concern: when images come from different categories, the sender can simply encode the image category, making the task a category-classification problem.  
- Use of pre-trained VGG features suggests a transfer-learning setup rather than communication emerging from scratch.  
- Reinforcement learning may be unnecessary here; a supervised classification approach could likely achieve similar or better results more efficiently.  
- Results demonstrate efficient encoding of pre-existing visual categories, not convincing evidence of genuine language emergence.  
- Suggestion: evaluate with images within the same category to require subtler, fine-grained communication.  
- Request baseline comparisons, e.g., a direct category-to-symbol mapping or supervised classifiers, to establish the learned protocol’s added value.  
- Symbol purity analysis is limited to category alignment; authors should examine whether symbols capture intra-category visual features.  
- Explore impact of larger vocabulary sizes beyond 10 and 100 to see if communicative complexity scales appropriately.  
- Ask for sensitivity analysis to image representation choice (fc vs. softmax layers) and its effect on learning dynamics and interpretability.  
- Request comparison of computational cost of training versus a supervised classification baseline.
