## Review of "Learning to Communicate"

This paper proposes a novel framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication within a referential game. The authors demonstrate that two neural networks, initialized randomly, can learn to coordinate and develop a communication protocol to identify target images from a pair. Further, they show that manipulating the game environment—specifically, focusing on object categories rather than individual images—can induce emergent “word meanings” that align better with intuitive semantic properties. Finally, they present a strategy to ground this agent-generated communication in natural language using a combined training regime of interactive games and supervised image classification. This work represents a compelling first step towards developing truly interactive AI systems capable of communicating effectively, and I **recommend acceptance** based on the novelty and promising results.

My recommendation stems from two primary reasons. First, the approach offers a compelling alternative to purely supervised learning methods for natural language processing. By framing language learning as a coordination problem, the authors sidestep the limitations of relying on large, pre-labeled datasets. Second, the demonstrated ability to guide the emergent language towards semantic coherence through environmental manipulation and grounding offers a concrete path toward developing more interpretable and human-compatible AI communication systems. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly positioned within the broader landscape of multi-agent learning and language emergence. The authors appropriately cite relevant work in areas such as referential games (Lewis, 1969), multi-agent reinforcement learning (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), and the limitations of purely supervised approaches (Vinyals & Le, 2015). The connection to linguistic and game-theoretic concepts (Skyrms, 2010; Crawford, 1998) provides a strong theoretical foundation. The experimental setup, while relatively simple, is well-defined and allows for clear evaluation of the proposed approach. The results, particularly the convergence to successful communication and the evidence of semantic alignment through purity metrics and human evaluation, are convincing. The incremental addition of supervised learning to ground the communication protocol is a significant contribution and the resulting improved interpretability is highly encouraging.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. I would appreciate clarification on how the target variable *t* is incorporated into the sender network’s input. The description in Section 2 states the sender receives “iL, iR, t,” but the specifics of how this information is used are not fully detailed. A diagram showing the input processing flow would be helpful. 

Furthermore, there appears to be an inconsistency in the reporting of percentage values in Tables 1 and 2. Table 1 reports "Purity" as a percentage in the range [0, 100], while Table 2 reports it as a value in the range [0, 1]. This is likely a minor error, but should be corrected to ensure consistency and avoid confusion. Finally, there are a few minor typos that should be addressed, such as "assest" should be "assess" and "usufal" should be "usual".

I have a few additional questions for the authors. The paper mentions that the informed sender reaches higher performance more quickly than the agnostic sender. Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for this difference? Is it simply due to the inductive bias introduced by the convolutions, or are there other factors at play?  Also, while the human evaluation is a positive result, the sample size appears relatively small (25 image pairs).  Would a larger-scale evaluation confirm these findings? Finally, the authors mention "metonymic" links in the human evaluation section. Could you provide more examples of this phenomenon and discuss the potential implications for developing more robust and flexible communication systems? 

Addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field. Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that presents a promising new direction for language learning in AI.
