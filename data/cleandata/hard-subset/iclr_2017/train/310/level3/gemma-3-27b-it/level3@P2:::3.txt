This paper introduces a novel framework for learning language through multi-agent communication, specifically in the context of referential games. The authors demonstrate that two simple neural networks, acting as a sender and receiver, can learn to coordinate and develop a communication protocol from scratch, without pre-defined language structures. Furthermore, they explore methods to influence the emergent language to better align with intuitive semantic properties and successfully ground the agents’ code into natural language through a combined game-playing and supervised learning approach.

**Decision: Accept** with high confidence. The paper tackles a challenging and important problem – learning communication – with a well-motivated and rigorously evaluated approach. The results are compelling and demonstrate a significant step towards developing more interactive and intelligent agents.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper convincingly demonstrates the feasibility of learning communication from first principles. The experimental setup, based on referential games, is well-defined and allows for controlled evaluation of the learning process. The authors successfully show that agents can achieve near-perfect coordination, indicating the emergence of a functional language. Crucially, they go beyond merely demonstrating coordination and investigate the *semantic* properties of the learned language. The results indicating alignment with object categories and human interpretability are particularly strong and demonstrate a promising path towards building truly communicative agents.

The grounding experiments, combining the referential game with supervised image classification, are a key contribution. This approach bridges the gap between the agents’ internal communication code and human-understandable language, showcasing a viable strategy for enabling interaction between agents and humans. The reported 68% accuracy in human subject evaluations is a very encouraging result.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and findings. The connection to relevant literature in multi-agent systems, linguistics, and game theory is thoughtfully presented, positioning the work within a broader research context. The authors also acknowledge limitations and suggest promising avenues for future research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

While the paper is strong, a few minor improvements could enhance its clarity and impact. 

1. **Target Variable Incorporation:** The paper states the sender receives input θS(iL, iR, t). It would be helpful to explicitly describe *how* the target variable ‘t’ is incorporated into the sender network’s input. Is it a one-hot encoding, a separate input channel, or another mechanism? A diagram or brief explanation would clarify this.

2. **Table Consistency:** There is an inconsistency in the reporting of percentage values in Table 1 and Table 2. Table 1 reports purity as a percentage (e.g., 57%), while Table 2 presents values between 0 and 1. The "obs-chance purity" difference in Table 1 also appears off, given the purity values presented. Please ensure consistent formatting and accuracy in these tables.

3. **Minor Typos:** There are a couple of minor typographical errors: "assest" should be "assess" and "usufal" should be "usual".

4. **Ablation Study:** While the supervised grounding experiment is compelling, an ablation study examining the relative contribution of the game-playing and supervised learning components could provide further insights. For example, training *only* on the supervised task as a baseline, or varying the proportion of time spent on each task.

5. **Qualitative Examples:** Providing more qualitative examples of the emergent communication, beyond those in Figure 4, would be beneficial. Showing specific symbol-image pairings and explaining the reasoning behind the agent’s choices could help readers better understand the learned representations.



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Beyond the supervised learning approach, do you foresee other techniques for grounding the agent’s communication in human language, such as incorporating human feedback directly into the game-playing loop?
2.  The paper mentions that the informed sender reaches higher levels of performance more quickly. Can you elaborate on the specific architectural advantages of the informed sender that contribute to this faster learning?
3.  Are there any specific cases where the emergent language significantly *diverges* from human intuition, and what factors might contribute to these divergences?
4.  The discussion mentions the potential for scaling this framework to more complex environments. What are the biggest challenges you anticipate in scaling this approach, and how do you plan to address them?
