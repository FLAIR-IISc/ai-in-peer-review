- Proposes a novel multi-agent referential game framework for emergent language learning.
- Shows two randomly initialized neural agents can coordinate and develop a communication protocol to identify target images.
- Demonstrates environmental manipulation (focusing on object categories) induces emergent “word meanings” that align with semantic properties.
- Introduces grounding of agent communication in natural language via combined interactive-game and supervised image classification training.
- Recommends acceptance based on novelty and promising results.
- Argues the approach is a compelling alternative to purely supervised NLP, avoiding reliance on large labeled datasets by framing learning as coordination.
- Shows ability to steer emergent language toward semantic coherence and interpretability through environment design and grounding.
- Paper is well-motivated, situates itself with relevant literature in referential games, multi-agent RL, and linguistic/game-theoretic foundations.
- Experimental setup is simple, well-defined, and yields convincing results (convergence, purity metrics, human evaluation).
- Incremental supervised grounding is a significant contribution that improves interpretability.
- Requests clarification on how the target variable t is incorporated into the sender’s input and suggests adding a processing-flow diagram.
- Notes inconsistency in reporting “Purity” (Table 1 uses [0,100]%, Table 2 uses [0,1]) that should be corrected.
- Points out minor typos ("assest" → "assess", "usufal" → "usual") to fix.
- Asks why the informed sender converges faster—whether due solely to convolutional inductive bias or other factors.
- Flags the small human-evaluation sample size (25 image pairs) and asks if larger-scale evaluation would confirm results.
- Requests more examples and discussion of observed “metonymic” links and their implications for robust, flexible communication systems.
