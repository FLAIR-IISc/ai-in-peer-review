## Review of "A Framework for Language Learning in Multi-Agent Communication Games"

This paper presents a compelling framework for emergent language learning through a referential game played between two neural network agents. The core idea—allowing agents to bootstrap communication through interaction rather than relying on passively learned data—is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in the development of truly interactive AI systems. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results demonstrating convergence to optimal policies and the emergence of meaningful communication tokens are promising. The subsequent experiments exploring environmental variations to influence semantic content, and grounding the communication in human language, represent valuable extensions and move the field forward. The authors correctly position their work within the broader literature on multi-agent communication, game theory, and linguistics, highlighting the novelty of their approach—learning agent-to-agent communication *then* grounding it in human language. 

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, and the presented results offer a significant contribution to the field of emergent communication. The approach of utilizing a simple game environment to foster language learning is a sensible starting point and justifies the initial focus on a relatively basic task. 

My assessment rests on three key points. First, the paper tackles a clearly defined and important problem: how to develop agents that can communicate effectively and learn language through interaction. Second, the approach is well-motivated, building on existing work in multi-agent systems and linguistics while offering a novel perspective. Finally, the paper provides strong empirical support for its claims, demonstrating both successful communication and the ability to influence the semantic content of the learned language.

However, a few points could further strengthen the paper. The mechanism by which the target variable *t* is incorporated into the sender network remains unclear, and clarification on this aspect would be helpful. Additionally, there appears to be an inconsistency in the reporting of percentage values in Table 1 and Table 2; the “obs-chance purity” value in Table 1 likely contains an error. Minor typos such as "assest" to "assess" and "usufal" to "usual" should also be corrected.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you please elaborate on how the target variable *t* is fed into the sender network architecture? Is it simply concatenated with the image representations, or is there a more complex mechanism at play?
*   Regarding the purity scores reported in Table 1 and Table 2, could you double-check the calculation of “obs-chance purity” and confirm its correct representation?
*   Do you have any qualitative examples of the types of “metonymic” links observed during the human evaluation, beyond the examples provided in the paper?
