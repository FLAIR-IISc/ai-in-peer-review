- Introduces a multi-agent language learning framework using referential games where sender and receiver neural networks develop a communication protocol from scratch.  
- Demonstrates agents can coordinate successfully and learn meaningful communication through interaction.  
- Modifications to the game environment (e.g., removing low-level visual details) encourage more semantically interpretable emergent “word meanings.”  
- Proposes grounding the emergent code in natural language via a combined interactive and supervised learning approach.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Tackles the important problem of moving beyond passive supervised learning toward interactive agents capable of communicative coordination.  
- Experimental setup is well-designed and produces convincing, insightful results.  
- Combination of game-based learning and supervised grounding shows promise for human-interpretable communication.  
- Human evaluation indicates the emergent language exhibits a degree of interpretability, though not perfect.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and situates contributions within linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory.  
- Figure 1 is hard to parse; needs clearer connections between layers and a more detailed caption.  
- More explanation is needed on why the informed sender converges faster and yields more interpretable symbols beyond convolutional inductive biases.  
- Choice of vocabulary sizes (10 and 100) seems arbitrary; discussion of effects across sizes is missing.  
- Reward function is binary (win/loss); exploring alternative reward structures could yield different learning dynamics.  
- Scalability to more complex visual scenes and communication tasks is not addressed and should be discussed.  
- Questions: rationale for chosen sender/receiver architectures and whether alternatives were considered.  
- Questions: request for more examples of the reported “metonymic” symbol-to-image mappings.  
- Questions: sensitivity of results to hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, temperature) and whether hyperparameter searches were performed.  
- Questions: potential drawbacks of supervised grounding, such as bias toward human-centric language that might limit novel communication strategies.
