## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper introduces a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication within the context of referential games. The authors investigate whether agents can develop a language from scratch through interaction, and explore how environmental factors can influence the semantic properties of the emergent code. They demonstrate successful coordination between two neural networks playing a referential game, and show that modifications to the game can lead to more interpretable "word meanings" reflecting intuitive semantic properties, and even ground this code into natural language. This work represents a valuable step toward developing interactive AI agents capable of productive communication, particularly with humans.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents interesting preliminary results and explores a compelling research direction, the core contribution feels incremental and the empirical validation lacks the robustness required for acceptance at ICLR. The primary concern is the limited novelty of the findings given the relatively straightforward task setup and the strong connection to existing supervised learning paradigms.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper’s core contribution – demonstrating that agents can learn to communicate in a referential game – is not entirely novel. Prior work (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) has already shown the emergence of communication in similar game settings. The key addition of exploring environment modifications to influence semantic properties is a valid direction, but the observed effects (e.g., the informed sender utilizing more symbols) are not particularly surprising or impactful. The results, especially concerning the improvement in semantic alignment with object categories, largely appear as a consequence of leveraging the underlying structure already present in the ImageNet dataset and the convolutional features extracted from it. 

The observation that the informed sender outperforms the agnostic sender is also logical given the architecture's explicit encoding of inter-image relationships. However, the analysis doesn't fully address whether this difference stems from genuine emergent linguistic principles, or simply a more efficient exploitation of the given task structure. The claim that this is an improvement in interpretable semantics feels weakened by the fact that the emergent “language” still heavily relies on the pre-trained feature space.

Furthermore, the grounding experiment, while promising, feels more like a transfer learning exercise than true language grounding. Supervised training on image labels undoubtedly aids performance, but the question remains whether the agents learn a compositional or systematic representation of language, or merely memorize label associations. The human evaluation, while positive, only demonstrates interpretability on a limited subset of images within the supervised vocabulary.  The connection between emergent symbol usage and human intuition feels tenuous and requires further investigation.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the experimental setup and results. The inclusion of related work provides adequate context. However, several areas could be strengthened to improve the contribution:

*   **Robustness to Input Diversity:** The authors acknowledge that the game is simplified. Explore the sensitivity of the results to variations in image quality, noise, or even different datasets. Would the emergent language still exhibit similar properties with more realistic or challenging inputs?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Provide a more thorough analysis of the informed sender architecture. What is the contribution of the 1D convolutions? How does vocabulary size impact the learning process?
*   **Quantitative Analysis of Semantic Similarity:** The purity metric is a helpful starting point, but more sophisticated measures of semantic similarity between agent-generated symbols and human-defined categories would be valuable.
*   **Compositionality:** Explore whether the emergent communication can handle compositional elements (e.g., describing complex scenes with combinations of symbols).
*   **Generalization to Novel Concepts:**  Test the ability of the learned communication protocol to generalize to new objects or concepts not seen during training.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The task appears to reduce to image classification when target and distractor images come from different classes. How do you address the concern that the emergent “language” is simply leveraging pre-existing class distinctions within ImageNet, rather than genuinely learning to refer?
2.  The supervised learning phase substantially improves grounding in human language. How do you differentiate between genuine language grounding and a transfer learning effect, where the agent is simply memorizing the association between symbols and labels?
3.  The human evaluation only focuses on images within the supervised category set. How would the human interpretability of the emergent language fare on images from categories *not* included in the supervised training data?
4.  What strategies are being considered to move beyond the limitations of relying on a pre-trained feature extractor? Would incorporating an end-to-end trainable system enhance the learning of more grounded and interpretable symbols?
