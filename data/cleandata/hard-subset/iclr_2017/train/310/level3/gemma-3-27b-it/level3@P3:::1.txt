## Review of “Learning to Communicate: Emergent Language in Referential Games”

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two agents in a referential game setting, aiming to understand if and how machine problem-solving strategies align with human language development. The authors present a framework where agents learn to communicate through interaction, without pre-defined protocols, and explore factors influencing the interpretability of the resulting “language.” They demonstrate successful coordination between agents and show that informed agents utilize more diverse symbols than agnostic ones, with evidence suggesting some semantic grounding related to object categories and even potential for human interpretability through supervised learning.

**Decision: Accept with minor revisions.** The paper tackles a compelling and important research question – understanding the foundations of communication and the emergence of language in artificial agents – and provides a well-designed experimental setup to address it. The results are interesting and demonstrate the potential for learning interpretable communication strategies.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's strength lies in its clear and well-motivated approach. The problem of creating interactive, communicative AI systems is critical, and the authors convincingly argue that purely supervised learning falls short in capturing the functional aspects of communication. The referential game provides a simplified yet insightful environment to study this problem. The choice of using ImageNet images adds realism and allows for analysis of visual semantics. 

The empirical results are supportive of the claims. The demonstration of successful coordination is a key achievement, and the observation that informed agents employ a richer vocabulary than agnostic agents is a significant finding. The exploration of semantic grounding through cluster purity and t-SNE visualization provides valuable insights into the “meaning” of the emergent symbols. The grounding experiment with supervised learning, while preliminary, is a promising step towards bridging the gap between machine and human communication. The paper also does a good job of situating its work within the broader literature on multi-agent communication, linguistics, and game theory.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is generally strong, several areas could benefit from further clarification and elaboration:

*   **Image Classification Critique:** The authors acknowledge the critique that the task reduces to image classification when images are from different categories. While this is valid, the paper could more thoroughly discuss the implications. Is this a fundamental limitation of the setup, or can it be mitigated through more complex environments or task structures? What does it *mean* that it simplifies to image classification?
*   **Transfer Learning Critique:** The authors’ acknowledgment of the task resembling transfer learning is also important. Expanding on *why* it might be faster through transfer learning (e.g., pre-trained representations, existing knowledge) would strengthen the discussion. Could the RL framework be modified to incorporate pre-training or other techniques to accelerate learning and more directly address the efficiency concerns?
*   **Informed Sender Architecture:** The justification for the 1D convolutional filters in the informed sender architecture could be more explicit. Why is dimension-by-dimension combination thought to be particularly helpful? Are there alternative architectures that could achieve similar results?
*   **Symbol Redundancy:** While the SVD analysis hints at synonymy, a more detailed analysis of the vocabulary usage would be beneficial. Are certain symbols consistently used interchangeably? What are the characteristics of these redundant symbols?
*   **Human Interpretability Experiment:** The CrowdFlower experiment is exciting, but the details of the survey design (e.g., instructions to participants, examples provided) could be included in the appendix. Also, providing a few examples of image pairs where communication *failed* could offer insights into the limitations of the learned language. What types of images/concepts were most often misinterpreted?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the image classification simplification, do you foresee ways to modify the game environment (e.g., adding noise, introducing ambiguity in image categories) to make the communication task more challenging and less reliant on simple classification?
2.  Given the observation that the setup bears resemblance to transfer learning, have you explored incorporating pre-trained representations (beyond the initial VGG features) into the agents' architectures? If so, what impact did it have on learning speed and the quality of the emergent language?
3.  In the human interpretability experiment, were participants aware that the "words" were generated by an AI, or were they presented as existing words? Could the framing of the task have influenced their interpretations?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the temperature parameter (τ) in the Gibbs distributions? How sensitive are the results to different values of τ, and what is the rationale for the chosen value?
5.  The paper mentions that the supervised learning phase improved the interpretability of the code even for images *not* in the supervised category. Could you elaborate on the mechanisms behind this generalization? Are the agents leveraging learned relationships between categories, or is something else happening?
