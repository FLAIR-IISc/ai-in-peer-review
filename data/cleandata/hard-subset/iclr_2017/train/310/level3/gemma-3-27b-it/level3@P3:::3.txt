## Review of "Referential Games"

This paper proposes a novel framework for learning communication through multi-agent referential games. Agents, implemented as neural networks, learn to coordinate by sending symbols to identify a target image from a pair. The authors demonstrate that these agents converge to effective communication strategies, and that the resulting symbols exhibit semantic meaning, particularly when influenced by image class information. Further grounding is achieved by intermittently training the sender on a supervised image classification task. The work presents a compelling alternative to traditional, supervised approaches to language learning and offers valuable insights into the emergence of communication.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a strong contribution to the field of multi-agent learning and emergent communication. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the feasibility of learning communication *from scratch* and provide evidence that the learned symbols capture meaningful concepts. The grounding component further strengthens the work and moves it closer to practical applications involving human-machine interaction.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in AI – how to develop agents that can effectively communicate. The proposed approach, leveraging referential games and reinforcement learning, is well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of supervised learning for interactive communication and position their work as a necessary step towards developing truly intelligent agents. 

The experimental setup is rigorous. The use of pre-trained image features from VGG provides a strong visual foundation, while the REINFORCE algorithm allows for effective learning in the game environment. The authors explore different sender architectures (agnostic and informed) and vocabulary sizes, providing valuable insights into the factors influencing communication quality and semantic meaning. 

The results are compelling. The observed convergence to successful communication, combined with the purity metrics demonstrating semantic alignment with object categories, strongly supports the claim that the agents learn meaningful symbols. The improvement in purity and interpretability when incorporating supervised learning is a significant finding, highlighting the benefits of grounding the emergent communication in human-understandable concepts. The human evaluation, showing a 68% accuracy in interpreting the learned symbols, further validates the approach.

The connection to related work (Lewis’ signaling game, previous multi-agent communication efforts, and work in linguistics and game theory) is well-established. The authors clearly differentiate their work and highlight its novelty – specifically, learning agent-to-agent communication and then grounding it in human language.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few minor points could be addressed to improve clarity and impact.

*   **Target Variable Incorporation:** The reviewer requested clarification on how the target variable `t` is incorporated into the sender network. While the paper states the target is presented alongside the distractor, a more detailed explanation of how this information is encoded and used by the sender network (e.g., is it concatenated to the feature vectors, used as a mask, etc.) would be beneficial.
*   **Table Consistency:** The issue regarding inconsistent percentage values in Table 1 and Table 2 should be addressed. The “obs-chance purity” value in Table 1 seems suspect. Double-checking these values and ensuring consistency in reporting is crucial.
*   **Typos:** The minor typos ("assest" → "assess", "usufal" → "usual") should be corrected.
*   **Visualizations:** While Figure 3 is informative, exploring visualizations of the learned symbol embeddings could further illuminate the semantic structure of the emergent language. A UMAP or t-SNE plot of the symbol embeddings themselves might reveal clusters representing semantic categories.
*   **Ablation Study:**  A more detailed ablation study investigating the impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, learning rate, temperature) on communication performance and semantic purity could provide further insights.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific implementation details of how the target variable `t` is incorporated into the sender network’s input?
2.  Could you confirm the accuracy of the "obs-chance purity" value in Table 1 and explain the calculation method?
3.  Do you observe any consistent biases or limitations in the types of semantic relationships that emerge in the learned language? For example, are certain object properties (e.g., color, shape) more readily encoded in the symbols than others?
4.  Considering the “metonymic” links observed in the human evaluation, do you believe further exploration of this phenomenon – consciously encouraging or leveraging it – could lead to more interpretable and effective communication systems?
