## Review of “Learning to Communicate”

This paper introduces a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication within referential games. The authors argue that interactive learning is crucial for developing truly communicative agents, moving beyond the limitations of purely passive, supervised approaches. They demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate in these games, and explore how modifying the game environment influences the characteristics of the emergent language. Finally, they present a strategy to ground the agents’ code in human-understandable language through a combined interactive and supervised learning approach.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem in AI – building agents that can genuinely communicate. The experimental results are encouraging and the combination of game-based learning with supervised grounding is a particularly promising direction. While there are areas for improvement (detailed below), the novelty and potential impact of this work justify acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of learning through interaction, inspired by the observation that humans learn language by communicating with others, is a strong and intuitive one. The paper convincingly argues that this approach addresses a key weakness of current language learning paradigms, which rely heavily on large static datasets and fail to capture the dynamic, functional aspects of communication.  The experimental setup using referential games provides a clean and controlled environment for studying emergent communication. The authors demonstrate that agents *can* indeed learn to coordinate and develop a language from scratch.

The exploration of environmental factors that influence the emergent language is insightful. The results showing that altering the game to focus on object categories, rather than low-level visual features, leads to more semantically meaningful symbol assignments highlights the importance of environment design in shaping language development. 

The most significant contribution, in my view, is the demonstration that grounding the agents’ communication through a combined interactive-supervised learning approach significantly improves the interpretability of the emergent language. Achieving 68% accuracy in human interpretation of the agent's symbols is a remarkable result, suggesting that the agents are learning to associate symbols with concepts in a way that aligns with human understanding.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is generally strong, several points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact.

*   **Figure 1 Clarity:** The diagram depicting the sender architectures (Figure 1) is difficult to follow. It appears to be missing crucial connection details. A clearer diagram with a detailed caption explaining the data flow and network components would greatly enhance understanding. Specifically, showing how the image representations are fed into the network, the function of the convolutions in the “informed sender,” and the relationship between the embedding and vocabulary layers is essential.
*   **Related Work Depth:** The related work section covers relevant areas, but it feels somewhat cursory. The authors acknowledge other work in multi-agent communication (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016), but a more in-depth comparison of their approach to these existing methods, and a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of each, would be beneficial. The reviewer admits a lack of familiarity with these fields, so a more thorough contextualization would be welcome.
*   **Ablation Study for Grounding:** The grounding results (Section 5) are very impressive. However, it would be valuable to include an ablation study to determine the relative contribution of the game-based learning versus the supervised learning. For example, what performance is achieved with only supervised learning, or with game-based learning but without the supervised pre-training?
*   **Analysis of “Failed” Cases:** The paper highlights successful examples of grounding, but it would be informative to analyze cases where the human subjects failed to correctly interpret the agent's symbols. This could provide insights into the limitations of the current approach and suggest directions for future research.
*  **Explain “Cheap Talk”**: The discussion of "cheap talk" games and their Nash equilibria feels somewhat disconnected from the main results. While the connection is theoretically interesting, the authors should either better explain how these concepts relate to their empirical findings or consider removing this section for conciseness.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the network architecture in Figure 1, including a clearer diagram?
2.  How does your approach differ from, and potentially build upon, the work of Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) in terms of the learning dynamics and the characteristics of the emergent language?
3.  What were the primary reasons for the specific hyperparameters chosen (e.g., embedding dimensionality, temperature)? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis?
4.  Can you elaborate on the types of “metonymic” links observed in the human interpretation of the agent’s communication? Were there recurring patterns or specific types of semantic shifts that were particularly common?
5.  The paper discusses the potential for extending the framework to include human players. What challenges do you anticipate in integrating humans into the game, and how would you address them?
