This paper introduces a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication games, specifically referential games where agents must learn to communicate to identify target images. The core idea – that interactive games can provide a strong signal for learning communication, moving beyond passive supervised learning – is compelling and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can indeed learn to coordinate in this setting, and explore how to modify the environment to encourage the development of more semantically meaningful “languages.” They also present a promising strategy for grounding the learned communication in human language through a combination of game-based training and supervised learning.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper tackles an important problem in AI – building truly interactive agents capable of natural language communication – and presents a novel and promising approach. While some aspects require clarification (discussed below), the core contributions are sound, the results are reasonable, and the work opens up exciting avenues for future research.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for this work is strong. The limitations of purely supervised learning for interactive tasks are well articulated, and the proposed game-based framework offers a compelling alternative. The experimental setup, while relatively simple, is clearly described and provides a good baseline for evaluating the approach. The results demonstrating successful coordination between agents are positive, and the exploration of different sender architectures and environmental modifications is valuable. 

The combination of game-based learning with supervised learning (Section 5) is particularly noteworthy. The results showing improved semantic grounding and human interpretability suggest that this is a fruitful direction for future work. The paper provides evidence that the emergent “languages” aren’t purely arbitrary but can reflect underlying semantic properties of the images, especially when the environment is designed to encourage this. The inclusion of a human evaluation (crowdsourcing) to assess the interpretability of the learned communication is a strong point. 

The framework itself (Section 2) appears robust and adaptable, and the authors rightly point out numerous possible extensions. The discussion section correctly frames the work within broader theoretical contexts, including game theory and linguistic studies.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Figure 1 Clarity:** The primary weakness of the paper is the clarity of Figure 1. The description of the network architectures is difficult to follow, and the diagram itself appears incomplete (as the authors acknowledge). A more detailed and visually clear diagram, with all network connections explicitly shown and a comprehensive caption explaining each component, is crucial. Specifically, it is unclear how the target/distractor image representations are processed within each architecture.
* **Related Work Depth:** While the authors acknowledge related work, the review indicates a potential lack of familiarity with the most closely related literature. A more thorough discussion of existing approaches to emergent communication in multi-agent systems, particularly those using similar game-theoretic setups, would strengthen the paper.  It would be useful to explicitly compare and contrast this work with other approaches in terms of architectural choices, learning algorithms, and environmental designs.
* **Vocabulary Size Impact:** The paper explores vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100. It would be helpful to discuss the trade-offs involved in choosing a larger vocabulary. Does increasing the vocabulary lead to more expressive communication, or does it simply increase the complexity of learning?
* **Ablation Studies:** While the results on different sender architectures are presented, further ablation studies could be conducted to isolate the effects of specific design choices. For example, what is the impact of the 1D convolutions in the informed sender architecture?
* **Human Evaluation Details:** More details about the crowdsourced human evaluation would be valuable. What instructions were given to the participants? What was the demographic profile of the participants?  Providing the exact phrasing of the questions asked in the survey would be useful for assessing the validity of the results.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding Figure 1, could you provide a more detailed diagram with all network connections clearly labelled? Could you walk through a single example of how an image pair and target designation would be processed by each sender architecture, step-by-step?
2.  Can you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, learning rate, temperature parameter)? Was any hyperparameter tuning performed?
3.  Were any attempts made to analyze the learned representations within the sender and receiver networks? For example, could you visualize the embeddings to see if they exhibit any interpretable structure?
4.  In the combined game/supervised learning setup, what was the relative proportion of time spent in each task? Did varying this proportion significantly impact the results?
5.  You mention that the supervised learning provides “basic grounding”. Are there plans to explore more sophisticated grounding techniques, such as using external knowledge bases or incorporating human feedback more directly into the learning process?
