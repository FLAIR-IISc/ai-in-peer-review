- Proposes an interactive multi-agent framework using a referential game where a sender sends one discrete symbol to help a receiver pick a target image from a pair.  
- Agents are simple neural nets trained from scratch with reinforcement learning based on communication success.  
- Demonstrates agents can develop an emergent communication protocol.  
- Shows semantic shaping of the emergent language by altering the game's "common knowledge" (e.g., showing different instances of the same object class).  
- Main contribution: grounding emergent agent language in human language by alternating game-based training with supervised image-labeling.  
- The combined game+supervision approach produces a protocol much more interpretable to humans, including on objects unseen during supervised training.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Two primary reasons for acceptance: provides an interactive alternative to passive supervised learning, and introduces a novel, effective hybrid grounding method.  
- Strong motivation and clear Lewis-style signaling-game framework that is valuable for the community.  
- Convincing experiments: agents learn communication from a blank slate; semantic purity and t-SNE analyses show symbols capture meaningful image properties.  
- Section 5 hybrid training is the highlight—yields dramatic alignment with human language and 68% human accuracy on novel/metonymic uses.  
- Overall assessment: well-executed study with strong standalone contribution and promising future directions.  
- Weakness: agent architecture descriptions and Figure 1 are unclear, lack caption/labels, and omit tensor-shape/operation details.  
- Questions for authors: clarify differences from Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016); provide analysis supporting the two-symbol "living vs. non-living" hypothesis (e.g., accuracy on same-supercategory pairs); and precisely describe the informed sender's convolutional input tensor shape.
