### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the emergence of communication between two AI agents, a sender and a receiver, within the framework of a referential game. The core contribution is demonstrating that two neural network agents, starting as blank slates, can successfully develop a shared communication protocol to solve a cooperative task: identifying a target image from a pair. The work tackles the important and challenging problem of moving beyond passive language learning from static text corpora towards interactive, goal-driven communication.

The authors use a simple and intuitive task, which allows for clear post-hoc analysis of the emergent language. Key findings include:
- Agents consistently learn to coordinate successfully.
- An "informed" sender architecture, which has an inductive bias for comparing image features, develops a richer vocabulary, using multiple symbols to communicate. In contrast, an "agnostic" sender converges to a minimal two-symbol language, often corresponding to a high-level semantic distinction like "living vs. non-living".
- The emergent symbols show semantic coherence, clustering objects from similar conceptual categories (e.g., animals, vehicles) together.
- The paper demonstrates a method for grounding the agents' arbitrary code in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. This not only makes the code interpretable but also shows that the agents can generalize this grounded knowledge to refer to novel objects via semantic association (e.g., using the word "dolphin" to refer to an image of the sea).

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The experimental setup, particularly when drawing the target and distractor images from different high-level categories, reduces the complex problem of communication to a much simpler, non-interactive task. The emergent protocol appears to be a set of learned class labels rather than a truly compositional or referential language.
2.  The reinforcement learning (RL) framework is an inefficient and unnecessarily complex method for the problem being solved. The task is more naturally framed as a supervised or transfer learning problem, which would likely converge faster and provide a stronger baseline.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary weakness of this work lies in the formulation of the referential game. The paper states that target/distractor pairs are created by randomly sampling two concepts from McRae et al.'s set. Since this set spans 20 general categories, the vast majority of pairs will consist of images from different categories (e.g., a "cat" and a "car"). In this scenario, the task does not require genuine communication or negotiation of meaning. Instead, it can be solved by two decoupled classification systems:

-   The **sender** receives the target image (e.g., a cat) and learns to perform image classification, outputting a unique symbol for the "cat" class.
-   The **receiver** receives two images (cat, car) and the symbol for "cat". It independently classifies both images and simply selects the one whose predicted class matches the received symbol.

There is no need for the sender to consider the distractor or for the receiver to learn the sender's intent beyond this simple label-matching. The "language" that emerges is just a vocabulary of class labels. The finding that the "informed" sender uses more symbols supports this interpretation: it is better at learning a multi-class classifier than the "agnostic" sender, which only learns a binary classifier (e.g., living/non-living).

This leads to the second major concern: the choice of methodology. Reinforcement learning is well-suited for problems involving sequential decision-making and credit assignment over time. However, the task as designed here is not truly interactive in a meaningful way. A much more direct and efficient approach would be to use a pre-trained image classifier (like the VGG network the authors already use for feature extraction) as a foundation. The sender's task would be to transmit a compressed representation of the target's class, and the receiver's task would be to match it. This framing as a transfer learning problem would likely achieve near-perfect accuracy much faster than the 10k RL iterations reported. The success of the final grounding experiment, which explicitly introduces a supervised classification objective, further reinforces the idea that the underlying problem is one of classification, not complex communication. The impressive results from this experiment are less about grounding an emergent language and more about demonstrating that a classifier, when trained with labels, can use those labels effectively.

**4. Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

While I recommend rejection in its current form, the paper explores a fascinating and important research direction. The following suggestions are offered to help strengthen the work:

-   **Increase Task Difficulty:** The core claims about emergent communication would be much more convincing if the task could not be solved by simple classification. The authors should design experiments where the target and distractor are always from the *same* base-level category (e.g., two different dogs, two different cars). This would force the agents to communicate about finer-grained visual attributes (e.g., color, orientation, shape) rather than just the object class. This setup would truly necessitate a more nuanced and compositional communication protocol.
-   **Stronger Baselines:** To justify the use of RL, the paper needs to compare against strong, non-communicative, or supervised baselines. A simple baseline would be a system where the sender is a trained classifier that passes its predicted label (or logits) to a receiver that also classifies its inputs and performs a match. Showing that the RL-based communication approach outperforms such a baseline, especially on a more difficult task, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.
-   **Expand on the Grounding Experiment:** The final experiment on grounding is the most compelling part of the paper. The observation of "metonymic" generalization is very interesting. This section feels rushed but could be the basis of a very strong paper on its own. It would be beneficial to analyze these generalizations more systematically. For example, can the semantic distance (e.g., using WordNet or embedding similarity) between the supervised word and the target object predict human or agent success?

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  What is the performance of your agents in a setting where the target and distractor images are guaranteed to be from the same McRae category (e.g., "mammal") or even the same base-level concept (e.g., two different images of a "dog")?
2.  Have you compared your RL-based approach to a supervised baseline where the sender is explicitly trained as a classifier on the image categories and the receiver is a simple matching module? How do they compare in terms of convergence speed and final accuracy?
3.  The architectural difference between the "informed" and "agnostic" sender is interesting but the motivation for the 1D convolution in the informed sender is not fully clear. Could you elaborate on the hypothesis behind this design and why this specific inductive bias is expected to encourage a richer vocabulary over other architectures that also process both image representations jointly?
