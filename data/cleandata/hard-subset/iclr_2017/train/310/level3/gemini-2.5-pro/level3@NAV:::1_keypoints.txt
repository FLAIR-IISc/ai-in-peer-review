- Paper studies emergent communication in a referential game where a sender transmits a message to a receiver to identify a target image from a pair.
- Main contribution: shows simple neural agents can learn to communicate from scratch and analyzes the emergent language.
- Finding: an "informed" sender develops a richer multi-symbol vocabulary, while an "agnostic" sender collapses to a minimal two-symbol system.
- Authors also explore grounding the agents' language in human-understandable concepts.
- Decision: Reject — reviewers find the paper promising but flawed in its current form.
- Core criticism 1: The referential task effectively reduces to simple image classification when target and distractor are from different high-level categories.
- Core criticism 2: The emergent protocol appears to be a learned encoding of image classes, making the use of reinforcement learning unnecessarily complex.
- Supporting argument: If labels separate targets and distractors by category, the sender only needs to signal category and the receiver performs straightforward classification.
- Methodological critique: The problem could be more efficiently solved via transfer learning or supervised approaches, so RL does not clearly demonstrate added value.
- Suggested improvement 1: Increase task difficulty by using same-category pairs (e.g., two dog breeds) to force communication about finer visual distinctions.
- Suggested improvement 2: Include a non-RL supervised baseline (e.g., pre-trained sender classifier + receiver interpreting logits) for direct comparison.
- Questions for authors: what was the distribution of same- vs different-category pairs in training/test, and how did performance and vocabulary size change when restricted to same-category pairs?
- Question for authors: can you justify the choice of reinforcement learning over simpler supervised frameworks given the apparent mapping to existing image classes?
