### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a compelling framework for studying the emergence of language through multi-agent communication. Instead of relying on passive learning from large, static text corpora, the authors propose training agents interactively within the context of referential games. In these games, a "sender" and a "receiver" agent, both implemented as simple neural networks, must collaboratively develop their own communication protocol from scratch to succeed at a task: identifying a target image from a pair of images.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to coordinate and develop a communication system that achieves near-perfect accuracy in the referential game. Second, it investigates the semantic properties of this emergent language, showing that the symbols learned by the agents correspond to higher-level conceptual properties of the images rather than just low-level visual features. The authors show that modifying the game's environment—for instance, by forcing agents to coordinate on object-level properties rather than specific image instances—can further encourage the development of more abstract semantics. Finally, and perhaps most significantly, the paper proposes and validates a hybrid training strategy that combines interactive game-playing with a supervised image-labeling task. This approach successfully grounds the agents' emergent language in natural language, producing a communication protocol that is demonstrably interpretable by humans, even when applied to novel objects.

### **Decision and Justification**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-motivated and thoughtfully executed paper that explores a promising research direction for developing more interactive and functional AI agents. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Strong and Generalizable Framework:** The paper introduces a clean and powerful framework based on referential games to study emergent communication. This approach provides a valuable alternative to standard supervised methods and opens up many avenues for future research into how agents can learn to communicate and coordinate.
2.  **Promising Results in Grounding Language:** The experiments, particularly the hybrid approach combining interactive learning with supervised classification, offer a compelling and practical path toward bridging the gap between an arbitrary, emergent agent language and human language. The successful human evaluation in Section 5 is strong evidence that this method can produce an interpretable and generalizable communication system.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, arguing effectively that interactive learning is crucial for capturing the functional aspects of communication that are missed by passive, supervised approaches. The connection to Lewis’ signaling games and cognitive science provides a solid theoretical foundation.

The empirical results convincingly support the paper's claims. The demonstration that agents can converge to a successful communication protocol (Section 4) is a solid baseline result. The analysis goes further by investigating the *nature* of the emergent language. Using cluster purity with respect to semantic categories and t-SNE visualizations provides insightful, albeit preliminary, evidence that the learned symbols have a semantic basis.

The most impactful contribution is the exploration of grounding in Section 5. By interleaving the referential game with a supervised labeling task, the authors show that the sender agent learns to use symbols that align with conventional human labels. The human evaluation experiment is a crucial step that validates this claim, showing that people can interpret the agent’s novel "metonymic" use of words to refer to related concepts (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to the sea). This finding is very encouraging, as it suggests a scalable way to guide emergent communication toward human-understandable language without requiring constant human intervention in the game itself. This hybrid self-play and supervised learning model, reminiscent of the strategy used by AlphaGo, appears to be a very fruitful direction for future work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Clarity of Agent Architectures:** The description of the sender architectures in Section 3, particularly the "informed sender," is difficult to understand from the text and Figure 1. Figure 1 lacks a caption and seems to omit key connections, making it hard to visualize the data flow. A clearer diagram and a more detailed textual explanation of how the 1-D convolutions are applied to the concatenated image embeddings would significantly improve the paper's reproducibility and clarity.
*   **Contextualization with Related Work:** The paper does a good job of setting up its motivation against the broader backdrop of NLP. However, a more direct and detailed comparison with the most closely related contemporary work on multi-agent communication (e.g., Foerster et al., 2016; Sukhbaatar et al., 2016) would be beneficial. Explicitly articulating the specific novelties of this paper's framework, experimental design, and analysis in relation to these other works would help readers better situate its contribution within this emerging subfield.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points:

1.  Could you please elaborate on the "informed sender" architecture? A more detailed walkthrough of how the two image embeddings are processed by the convolutional filters would be very helpful for understanding the intended inductive bias.
2.  The results from the hybrid training experiment are very interesting. Did you observe any tension or trade-off between the agents' performance on the supervised classification task and their success rate in the referential game, especially when dealing with objects not present in the supervised label set?
3.  In your analysis of the emergent language, you found that the informed sender used a wider vocabulary. Have you considered analyzing the emergent language for signs of compositionality? For example, do certain symbols consistently refer to specific attributes (like color or shape) that appear across different object classes?
