**Review of "Emergent Communication in Multi-Agent Referential Games"**

This paper investigates the important and challenging problem of how a communication protocol can emerge between two AI agents tasked with coordinating on a goal. The authors propose a framework based on referential games where a "sender" agent must transmit a message to a "receiver" agent to identify a target image from a pair. The paper's core contribution is demonstrating that simple neural agents can successfully learn to communicate from scratch. Furthermore, the authors analyze the emergent language, finding that an architecturally "informed" sender develops a richer, multi-symbol vocabulary, while an "agnostic" sender relies on a minimal two-symbol system. The work also commendably explores methods for grounding the agents' language in human-understandable concepts.

**Decision: Reject**

My recommendation is to reject this paper in its current form. While the research direction is promising, the experimental setup has two fundamental weaknesses that undermine the central claims. First, the task effectively reduces to a simple image classification problem. Second, the emergent communication protocol appears to be little more than a learned encoding for image classes, making the reinforcement learning approach unnecessarily complex for the problem being solved.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary issue is the construction of the referential game. When the target and distractor images are sampled from different high-level categories (e.g., an apple vs. a car), the sender's task is not to develop a nuanced language but simply to signal the category of the target. The receiver then performs a straightforward classification task based on this signal. The problem is thus not one of emergent communication in a complex environment, but rather a distributed classification task. The finding that the agnostic agent learns a two-symbol system (e.g., "living" vs. "non-living") strongly supports this interpretation.

This simplification leads to the second issue: the methodology feels mismatched to the problem. The challenge as framed could be solved more efficiently as a transfer learning problem. A sender could be pre-trained to output class embeddings, which the receiver then uses for identification. The use of reinforcement learning to converge on this simple solution does not sufficiently demonstrate the power of interactive learning for developing complex, compositional language.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

To strengthen the paper, I suggest the authors revise the experimental design to necessitate more complex communication.
1.  **Increase Task Difficulty:** The core experiments should focus on scenarios where the target and distractor images are from the *same* semantic category (e.g., two different types of dogs). This would force agents to communicate about distinguishing visual features rather than broad class labels, making the emergent language far more interesting.
2.  **Comparative Baseline:** A direct comparison against a non-RL, supervised baseline would be valuable. For instance, training the sender as a classifier and the receiver to interpret its logits would clarify what, if anything, the interactive RL paradigm adds.

**Questions for the Authors**
*   What was the distribution of same-category versus different-category pairs in your training and test sets? How did agent performance and vocabulary size change when restricted to only same-category pairs?
*   Could you justify the choice of reinforcement learning over a simpler supervised framework, given that the emergent protocol seems to map directly to pre-existing image classes?
