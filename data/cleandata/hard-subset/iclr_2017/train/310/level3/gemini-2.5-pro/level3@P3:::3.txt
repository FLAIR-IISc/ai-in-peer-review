### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a compelling framework for studying the emergence of communication between AI agents. The authors propose a simple yet effective referential game where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must coordinate to identify a target image from a pair. The sender, knowing the target, transmits a single symbol from an arbitrary vocabulary to the receiver, who must then guess the target. Both agents are rewarded for successful coordination.

The agents, implemented as simple neural networks using pretrained image features, are trained via reinforcement learning (REINFORCE) and successfully converge to optimal policies. The core contribution lies in the analysis of the emergent communication protocol. The authors demonstrate that the learned symbols are not arbitrary but capture meaningful semantic concepts, as measured by clustering purity against human-defined categories.

The paper thoughtfully explores how environmental changes can shape the emergent language. By forcing agents to coordinate on object-class properties rather than specific image instances, they induce even more semantically abstract and meaningful communication. The most novel contribution is a multi-game setup where the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task. This method successfully grounds the agent-developed symbols in natural language labels, making the communication protocol interpretable to humans. The authors show this grounding generalizes, allowing the sender to creatively use words in a metonymic fashion to refer to unseen objects, a behavior humans can understand. This work is presented as a valuable first step toward building interactive agents that learn the functional, goal-oriented aspects of language, offering an alternative to passive, sequence-to-sequence learning paradigms.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  The paper presents a clear, well-motivated, and elegant framework for studying the emergence of grounded communication, a fundamental and challenging problem in artificial intelligence. The use of a simple referential game is an excellent choice for isolating the core challenges of developing a shared communication protocol.
2.  The novel experiment combining interactive game-playing with supervised learning to ground the emergent language in human-understandable terms is a significant contribution. It provides a practical and promising path toward making multi-agent communication systems transparent and interoperable with humans.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The problem of learning shared, grounded communication is of great interest to the community. This paper tackles it from first principles, starting with a simple task that is justified by the complexity of the problem. The authors rightly argue that supervised learning on "canned" conversations fails to capture the interactive and functional nature of language. Their proposed multi-agent referential game provides a clean testbed to explore these functional aspects. The finding that simple agents can converge and develop a protocol with semantic structure is a strong foundational result.

The paper's main strength is the progression of experiments. The initial experiment demonstrates successful learning. The second experiment cleverly tweaks the game's "common knowledge" to encourage more abstract representations, showing that the environment's design is key to the nature of the emergent language. The final and most impactful experiment addresses the critical issue of interpretability. By jointly training on the game and a supervised classification task, the authors show how to bridge the gap between an arbitrary agent-developed code and human language. The finding that this grounding generalizes, enabling "metonymic" word use that humans can successfully interpret, is particularly exciting and suggests a path toward more flexible and human-like communication in machines. This work effectively pioneers a method to learn agent-to-agent communication and then connect it to human language, providing a compelling alternative to purely supervised approaches.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the final version of the paper and are not critical to my decision.

**Clarification Question:**
*   Could the authors please clarify precisely how the target variable `t` is incorporated into the sender network? The text states the sender receives `(iL, iR, t)`, and the caption for Figure 1 mentions that the target and distractor representations are fed "always in this order". Is the target identity provided *only* by fixing the input order of the two image feature vectors? If so, this should be stated more explicitly in the main text for clarity.

**Reporting Issues:**
*   There appears to be an inconsistency in how percentages are reported. In Table 1, purity values are in the [0, 100] range, while in Table 2, they are in the [0, 1] range. This should be made consistent.
*   Related to the above, the "obs-chance purity" value of 37% reported in Section 5 for the supervised experiment seems unusually high if other chance-corrected values in Table 1 are in the single digits. This might be a typo or reflect a different calculation that would benefit from clarification.

**Minor Typos:**
*   In Section 4, "assest" should be "assess".
*   In a reference, "usufal" should be "usual" (this may be a parsing error in the provided text).
