This paper proposes a framework for language learning based on multi-agent interactive referential communication games. The authors demonstrate that tabula rasa agents can successfully develop an emergent communication protocol to solve a cooperative image identification task. Furthermore, the paper explores methods to align this emergent language with human semantics, first by modifying the game's common knowledge and, more compellingly, by combining game-based reinforcement learning with supervised learning on natural language labels. This hybrid approach not only improves semantic purity but also results in a code that is demonstrably interpretable by humans, even for novel objects.

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The work is well-motivated, tackling the important problem of grounding language through interaction, and the proposed framework of multi-agent referential games is a promising research direction. The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating not only the emergence of communication but also a novel and effective method for grounding this emergent language in natural language, making it interpretable to humans.

The paper successfully situates itself within the literature, noting the limitations of purely supervised approaches for interactive agents. The general framework presented in Section 2 is strong, adaptable, and invites interesting future variations. The experimental results support the main claims; the agents consistently learn to coordinate, and the reported purity metrics suggest the emergent language captures meaningful semantic properties. The experiment in Section 5, which alternates between game-based training and supervised learning, is particularly compelling. The subsequent human evaluation provides strong evidence for the viability of this hybrid approach. While I am not deeply familiar with the most closely related literature on emergent communication, the contributions appear solid and the direction valuable.

The following points are offered to help improve the paper for the final version and are not critical to my decision:
The primary area for improvement is clarity in the experimental setup. The description of the agent architectures in Section 3 is difficult to parse. This is exacerbated by Figure 1, which appears to be missing key network connections and lacks a descriptive caption. A revised, more detailed diagram with a proper caption would significantly enhance the reader's understanding of the model's mechanics and the distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" senders.

Could the authors clarify the "informed sender" architecture? Specifically, how do the 1-D convolutions operate on the two image embeddings, and what is the structure of the subsequent filter that produces vocabulary scores? A revised Figure 1 would likely answer this. Additionally, the temperature parameter (τ=10) for the Gibbs distributions seems high. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the results to this hyperparameter?
