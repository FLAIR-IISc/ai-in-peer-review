### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a framework for studying the emergence of language through multi-agent communication. The authors introduce a referential game where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must coordinate to identify a target image from a pair. The sender, knowing the target, sends a single symbol from a discrete vocabulary to the receiver, who must then identify the correct image. The agents are implemented as neural networks and are trained from scratch using reinforcement learning (REINFORCE), with communication success as the only reward signal.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that simple agents can successfully learn to communicate and solve the task, converging to near-perfect coordination. Second, it investigates the semantic properties of the emergent language, showing that the learned symbols correspond to meaningful conceptual categories (e.g., living vs. non-living things) rather than just low-level visual features. The authors show that by modifying the game to remove low-level visual common knowledge (by showing agents different instances of the same object class), the emergent language becomes even more semantically abstract and general. Finally, and most significantly, the paper presents a novel strategy for grounding the agents' language in human language. By training the sender in a multi-task setting that alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task, the resulting communication protocol becomes significantly more interpretable to humans, even when referring to objects not seen during supervised training.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a well-executed and interesting paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of grounded language learning from an interactive, multi-agent perspective. This is a refreshing and well-motivated departure from purely supervised, passive learning paradigms (e.g., standard image captioning), and the simple yet effective referential game setup provides a strong foundation for future work in this direction.
2.  The proposed method for grounding the emergent communication in natural language by combining interactive self-play with supervised learning is novel and compelling. The experiments, particularly the human evaluation, provide strong evidence that this approach can produce a communication system that is not only effective between agents but also partially understandable by humans, a crucial step towards building interactive AI.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper is well-motivated, positioning itself clearly in the literature and highlighting the limitations of existing supervised approaches for learning the functional, interactive aspects of language. The use of a Lewis-style signaling game is a principled choice, connecting the work to a rich history in game theory and cognitive science. The decision to start with a simple task—referring to one of two images with a single symbol—is methodologically sound, as it allows for a clear and controlled investigation of the core research questions.

The empirical support for the claims is strong. The experiments systematically demonstrate that (a) agents can converge to a successful communication protocol, (b) the protocol has emergent semantic structure, and (c) this structure can be shaped by modifying the game environment. The use of purity scores and SVD analysis to analyze the semantics of the emergent language provides quantitative evidence for these claims.

The final set of experiments on grounding is the paper's strongest contribution. The idea of using a supervised task to "nudge" the emergent language towards human conventions, inspired by work like AlphaGo, is elegant and effective. The results are impressive: not only does performance in the game remain high, but symbol purity increases dramatically, and the sender learns to use conventional labels. The human evaluation experiment is particularly convincing. It shows that the grounding generalizes beyond the supervised labels, allowing humans to interpret the sender's messages even for novel objects through "metonymic" or other semantic links. This demonstrates a flexible, robust form of communication that is a significant step beyond simply memorizing labels.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are meant as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Reporting Consistency:** There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the reporting of purity scores. In Table 1, the "obs-chance purity" values (0.023, 0.109) seem to be on a [0, 1] scale, while the other purity values appear to be percentages on a [0, 100] scale. Please check and correct this for clarity. If the values are indeed correct, a footnote explaining the different scales would be helpful.
*   **Architectural Discussion:** The agent architectures are intentionally simple, which is appropriate for this foundational work. However, a brief paragraph in the discussion could touch upon how this framework might extend to more complex architectures (e.g., using LSTMs for sender and receiver) or richer communication protocols (e.g., allowing for multi-symbol messages or a back-and-forth dialogue). This would help frame the current work and its future potential more broadly.
*   **Typos:** I noticed a few minor typos that should be corrected, for example, "assess" is misspelled as "assest" and "usual" as "usufal" in the version I read.
