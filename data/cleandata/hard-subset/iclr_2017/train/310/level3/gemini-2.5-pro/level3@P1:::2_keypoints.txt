- Introduces a referential-game framework where sender and receiver agents learn communication from scratch to identify target images.
- Demonstrates tabula rasa agents can learn to communicate and solve the task with high accuracy.
- Shows the emergent communication protocol develops semantic structure aligned with conceptual image properties (measured by clustering purity).
- Finds that manipulating common knowledge (e.g., showing different instances of the same class) encourages more abstract and general language.
- Proposes a hybrid training method (alternating interactive game and supervised image-labeling) to ground emergent symbols in natural language.
- Crowdsourced evaluation shows the hybrid approach yields symbols that are interpretable by humans and generalize to unseen objects.
- Decision: Accept — paper is well-motivated, elegant, and experimentally solid.
- Positions interactive, goal-oriented language learning as a valuable alternative to passive supervised learning, grounded in relevant literature.
- The "informed" sender architecture exhibits a useful inductive bias that aids coordination.
- Analysis (cluster purity, t-SNE) supports that learned symbols capture meaningful visual/conceptual properties rather than low-level features.
- Mixed-training yields improved semantic purity and human interpretability; agents display metonymic usages (e.g., “dolphin” → sea).
- Suggests clearer descriptions/diagrams of agent architectures (especially the informed sender) and better explanation/visualization of 1-D convolution usage.
- Notes minor bibliographic formatting errors to correct.
- Asks authors to clarify differences from prior multi-agent communication work (Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.) to better situate contributions.
- Requests intuition for the informed sender’s architectural inductive bias and asks about sensitivity to vocabulary size and potential for compositionality.
