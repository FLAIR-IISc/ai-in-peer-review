### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling framework for studying the emergence of language through multi-agent communication. The authors propose a "referential game" where a sender and a receiver, implemented as simple neural networks, must develop a shared communication protocol from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a pair. The work makes several key contributions:
1.  It demonstrates that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to coordinate and communicate using a discrete, arbitrary vocabulary, solving the referential game with high accuracy.
2.  It introduces and compares two sender architectures, "agnostic" and "informed," showing that an inductive bias towards comparing image features (the informed sender) leads to faster convergence and the use of a richer, more expressive vocabulary.
3.  The paper provides evidence that the emergent language is not arbitrary but develops semantic structure that aligns with human-conceptual categories (e.g., the McRae categories), as measured by cluster purity.
4.  It shows that by modifying the game's environment (e.g., forcing agents to coordinate on object-level properties), the emergent protocol can be guided towards more abstract, general meanings.
5.  Finally, it proposes a practical method for grounding the agents' language in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task. This results in a communication protocol that is partially interpretable and usable by humans, even for concepts not seen during supervised training.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The experimental setup of the referential game, where target and distractor images are drawn from a large set of distinct concepts, often reduces the complex problem of communication to a simpler image classification task.
2.  Consequently, the emergent communication protocol appears to primarily function as a set of learned class labels, which questions the novelty and necessity of the multi-agent reinforcement learning framework compared to more direct supervised or transfer learning approaches.

### Supporting Arguments

The core weakness of the current work lies in the construction of the referential game. The images are sampled from 463 distinct base-level concepts. When the sender and receiver are presented with a pair of images from different concepts (e.g., a "cat" and a "car"), the sender's task is not to describe the specific cat but simply to transmit a symbol that means "cat." The receiver's task is then to recognize which of the two images is a cat. This effectively turns the sender into a classifier and the communication channel into a medium for transmitting the class label. The high success rates are therefore not surprising, as modern neural networks are excellent classifiers.

This simplification has significant implications for the paper's claims. The finding that the "informed" sender uses more symbols than the "agnostic" one (which defaults to a two-symbol system) is interesting, but within this classification framework, it may simply indicate that one architecture is better at learning a multi-class classifier while the other learns a simpler binary one (e.g., "living vs. non-living," as the authors note). The communication aspect feels secondary to the classification challenge.

This leads to the second key reason for rejection. If the core task is to associate images with discrete symbols that represent their class, the problem is highly analogous to standard classification or transfer learning. One could train a classifier on the VGG features to predict McRae categories and use the category index as the "symbol." This would likely be far more sample-efficient than the proposed reinforcement learning approach. While the paper aims to show that communication can *emerge* from interaction, the chosen task is not challenging enough to necessitate a truly descriptive or compositional language. The success of the final grounding experiment, which explicitly mixes in a supervised classification objective, further reinforces the idea that the underlying problem being solved is fundamentally one of classification. The paper would be much stronger if the task required communication that goes beyond simple labeling.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to strengthen the paper and are not central to the decision.
1.  **Increase Task Difficulty:** The most significant improvement would be to design a game where simple classification is insufficient. For instance, the target and distractor could always be drawn from the *same* base-level concept (e.g., two different images of dogs, or two different cars). This would force the agents to develop a more nuanced, descriptive language to communicate distinguishing features ("the brown dog," "the dog running") rather than just the category label.
2.  **Analyze the "Informed" Sender's Advantage:** The architectural difference between the informed and agnostic senders is a nice element of the paper. A deeper analysis of *why* the dimension-wise comparison helps would be valuable. Does it allow the sender to more easily isolate distinguishing features between the two images? Visualizing the learned filters or the intermediate representations could provide valuable insight.
3.  **Expand on the Human Evaluation:** The experiment in Section 5, showing that humans can interpret the agent's grounded language, is the most exciting result in the paper. This demonstrates a non-trivial generalization. Expanding on this would be very valuable. For example, how does human performance correlate with the semantic distance between the supervised word and the target content? Can the agents' "metonymic" links be predicted or systematized? This part of the work points toward a truly interactive AI and deserves more focus.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  In the experiments in Section 4, what percentage of the image pairs presented to the agents consist of a target and distractor from the same McRae category? If this percentage is low, does this not confirm that the task is predominantly one of inter-class discrimination (i.e., classification)?
2.  Given that the emergent symbols seem to correspond to object categories, could you comment on the advantages of your multi-agent RL approach over a more direct method, such as training a standard classifier on the image features and using its predictions as the communication symbols?
3.  In the final human evaluation experiment, the agents were trained with supervision on 100 ImageNet labels but tested on ReferItGame images. Was there any overlap in the object categories between the supervised training set and the objects present in the ReferItGame test set? The ability to generalize to truly unseen concepts is a critical measure of success for this grounding strategy.
