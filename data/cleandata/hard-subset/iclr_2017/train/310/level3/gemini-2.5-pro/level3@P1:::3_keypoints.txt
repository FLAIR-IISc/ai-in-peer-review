- Proposed a referential game framework where a sender sends a single discrete symbol to a receiver to identify a target image, with agents trained from scratch via REINFORCE using only communication success as reward.  
- Demonstrated simple neural agents can learn to communicate and achieve near-perfect coordination on the task.  
- Showed emergent symbols align with meaningful conceptual categories (e.g., living vs. non-living) rather than only low-level visual features.  
- Found that removing low-level visual common knowledge (different instances of same class) yields more abstract, general emergent language.  
- Introduced a multi-task grounding strategy: alternating the referential game with supervised image classification to align emergent symbols with human language.  
- Grounding method produced communication protocols that are more interpretable to humans and generalize to objects outside supervised labels.  
- Decision: Accept — tackles grounded, interactive language learning and offers a novel, compelling grounding approach.  
- Methodologically strong: well-motivated, connects to Lewis signaling games, and uses a simple controlled task suitable for the research questions.  
- Empirical support is systematic: agents converge, languages show semantic structure, and environment manipulations shape semantics; analyses include purity scores and SVD.  
- Grounding experiments are the paper’s strongest contribution, with supervised nudging increasing symbol purity and human evaluations showing robust interpretability and generalization.  
- Reporting inconsistency: check purity score scales (Table 1 obs-chance purity values vs. other purity values) or add explanatory footnote.  
- Suggest adding discussion on extending architectures/protocols (e.g., LSTMs, multi-symbol messages, dialogue) to frame future work.  
- Minor typos noted (e.g., "assest" vs. "assess", "usufal" vs. "usual").  
- Clarification requested: explicitly describe how target t is provided to the sender (e.g., are target and distractor feature vectors concatenated in a fixed order?).
