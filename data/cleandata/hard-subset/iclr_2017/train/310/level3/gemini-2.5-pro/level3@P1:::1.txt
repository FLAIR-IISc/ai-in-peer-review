### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework for studying the emergence of language through multi-agent communication. The authors propose a "referential game" where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must coordinate to identify a target image from a pair of images. Starting as blank slates, the agents develop a shared communication protocol using a discrete vocabulary, trained via reinforcement learning based on task success. The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It demonstrates that simple feed-forward networks can successfully learn to communicate and coordinate in this game, achieving near-perfect accuracy.
2.  It investigates how architectural inductive biases (the "informed" vs. "agnostic" sender) affect the emergent language, showing that the informed architecture leads to a richer, more multi-faceted vocabulary.
3.  It explores how modifying the game environment (e.g., showing agents different instances of the same object class) can encourage the development of more abstract, conceptual symbol meanings.
4.  Finally, it presents a compelling method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task. This leads to a communication protocol that is partially interpretable and generalizable to humans, even on novel objects.

The work is well-motivated, drawing connections to linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, and tackles the important and challenging problem of moving beyond passive language learning towards interactive, functional communication. The experimental setup is simple, elegant, and allows for intuitive analysis of the emergent communication protocols.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

My decision is based on two primary concerns that challenge the central claims about the emergence of a communicative language:

1.  **Task Simplification:** The experimental setup, where target and distractor images are typically drawn from different high-level categories, often reduces the communication task to a simple distributed classification problem, rather than a more nuanced referential act.
2.  **Methodological Justification:** The emergent protocol appears to primarily encode object class information. In this context, reinforcement learning seems to be an inefficient method for what is effectively a transfer learning problem, which could be solved more directly with supervised methods.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

1.  **The "Communication" as Distributed Classification:**
    The core of the referential game involves a sender communicating information to a receiver to distinguish a target from a distractor. The paper's dataset is constructed by sampling two concepts (e.g., 'cat', 'car') and then one image for each. In the vast majority of these games, the sender's optimal strategy is not to describe the specific visual properties of the target image, but simply to identify its category. For instance, if the target is a cat and the distractor is a car, the sender only needs to learn a symbol for "cat" and the receiver only needs to learn to associate that symbol with the cat image.

    This setup does not necessitate the kind of compositional or descriptive language the introduction alludes to. Instead, it becomes a task where the sender acts as a classifier, and the communication channel is merely a bottleneck transmitting the predicted class label. The receiver then performs a simple matching task. The paper's own "purity" analysis supports this interpretation: the fact that the emergent symbols cluster well according to the McRae semantic categories suggests that the symbols are functioning as stand-ins for these categories. While this is a form of coordination, it oversimplifies the problem of communication and may not generalize to more complex scenarios where fine-grained distinctions are required.

2.  **Inefficiency of RL for This Task Formulation:**
    Following from the first point, if the task is primarily about transmitting a class label, then framing it as a reinforcement learning problem solved with REINFORCE seems unnecessarily complex and sample-inefficient. A more direct approach would be to train the sender as a standard supervised image classifier on the McRae categories. The receiver could then be trained to use the sender's (fixed) output to select the correct image. This would likely converge much faster and more reliably.

    The claim that agents "bootstrap knowledge on top of each other" through interaction is weakened if the interaction is limited to this simple signal-response loop. The current setup does not convincingly demonstrate that the interactive, multi-agent RL framework is essential for solving the task or that it produces a richer outcome than a more straightforward supervised pipeline would. The success of the agents seems less about the emergence of a flexible language and more about their ability to learn a shared, discrete code for pre-existing object categories.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

Despite the critical points above, I believe this is a thought-provoking paper with a promising research direction. The following suggestions are intended to help strengthen the core claims and experimental design.

*   **Harder Referential Tasks:** The most significant improvement would be to design a version of the game where the simple classification strategy is insufficient. The most obvious way to do this is to ensure that the target and distractor are frequently from the *same* semantic category (e.g., two different dogs, two different chairs). In this "hard mode," the sender would be forced to communicate distinguishing features (e.g., "sitting" vs. "running," "red" vs. "blue") rather than just the object class. Success in such a setup would provide much stronger evidence for the emergence of a descriptive, functional language.

*   **Analysis of the Informed Sender:** The result that the "informed" sender develops a richer vocabulary is one of the most interesting findings. The paper intuits that this is due to its inductive bias for dimension-by-dimension comparison. It would be valuable to analyze this further. What kind of feature differences is this architecture learning to exploit? Visualizing the learned filters or analyzing which dimensions are most influential could provide deeper insight into how this architecture facilitates more complex signaling.

*   **Strengthening the Grounding Experiment:** The grounding experiment in Section 5 is the most compelling part of the paper, particularly the "metonymic" generalizations (e.g., 'dolphin' for a sea picture). This demonstrates a flexible, human-like use of symbols. This result is very strong and could be the primary focus of the paper. It would be interesting to analyze the failures as well. When do humans fail to understand the agent's signal? This could reveal limitations in the learned protocol and suggest avenues for future work.

### Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and assess the work, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  What is the performance of the agents on a version of the test set where the target and distractor images are guaranteed to be from the same McRae category? If this experiment has not been run, what would you hypothesize the outcome would be for the "agnostic" and "informed" senders?

2.  Could you provide more analysis or intuition as to *why* the convolutional structure of the "informed" sender encourages the use of a wider vocabulary, while the "agnostic" sender collapses to a two-symbol system? Does it learn to encode the *differences* between the image representations?

3.  Have you considered a baseline where the sender is first trained as a supervised classifier on the image categories, and its weights are then frozen? A second receiver agent could then be trained (either via RL or supervision) to interpret the sender's output. How would the sample efficiency and final performance of such a decoupled system compare to your end-to-end interactive training?
