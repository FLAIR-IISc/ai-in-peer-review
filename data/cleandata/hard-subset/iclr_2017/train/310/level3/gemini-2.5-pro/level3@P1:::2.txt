### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a compelling framework for studying language emergence through multi-agent communication. The authors move away from traditional passive learning on static text corpora and instead propose using interactive, cooperative referential games to train agents to develop a functional communication protocol from scratch. In their setup, a "sender" agent and a "receiver" agent, both implemented as simple neural networks, must coordinate to identify a target image from a pair of images.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that tabula rasa agents can successfully learn to communicate and solve the task with high accuracy. Second, it provides evidence that the emergent communication protocol is not arbitrary but develops a semantic structure that reflects conceptual properties of the images, as measured by clustering purity against human-provided categories. Third, the authors show that by manipulating the "common knowledge" within the game (e.g., by showing agents different instances of the same object class), they can encourage the emergent language to be more abstract and general. Finally, and perhaps most significantly, the paper presents a novel and effective method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural human language. By alternating the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, the agents learn to use symbols that are not only effective for the game but are also interpretable by humans, a claim robustly supported by a crowdsourced experiment. This hybrid approach appears to be a very promising direction for developing agents that can eventually communicate productively with people.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I am happy to recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two primary reasons:
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant framework for studying language learning as an interactive, goal-oriented process. This is a refreshing and important departure from the dominant supervised learning paradigm and opens up many avenues for future research.
2.  The experimental results are solid and progressively build a convincing narrative. The final experiment, which demonstrates that a hybrid of interactive and supervised learning can ground the emergent communication in human-interpretable language, is a particularly novel and impactful contribution.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's core premise—that language is best learned through interactive use rather than passive observation—is persuasively argued and well-placed in the literature, citing foundational ideas from linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory. The referential game is a clean and simple instantiation of this principle, allowing for controlled experiments on language emergence. The general framework outlined in Section 2 is clear and provides a strong foundation for the work.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The initial experiments successfully establish that agents can learn to coordinate, with the "informed" sender architecture showing a particularly interesting inductive bias. The subsequent analysis of the emergent protocol's semantic properties using cluster purity and t-SNE visualizations provides convincing evidence that the learned symbols correspond to meaningful visual and conceptual properties, rather than just overfitting to low-level image features.

The most exciting contribution is the mixed-training paradigm presented in Section 5. The idea of using supervised learning on object labels not as the end goal, but as a way to "nudge" the emergent language towards one that is shared with humans, is insightful. The results are impressive: not only does this improve semantic purity, but the human evaluation study confirms that the resulting language is significantly more interpretable, even when referring to objects not seen during the supervised phase. The discovery that agents make "metonymic" links (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to the sea) is a fascinating finding that suggests this approach can capture some of the flexibility inherent in human language. The overall work is a valuable step towards creating AI that can learn to communicate in a grounded and functional way.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Clarity of Agent Architectures:** The description of the agent architectures in Section 3, particularly the "informed sender," is difficult to follow. The accompanying Figure 1 is schematic to the point of being ambiguous and lacks a caption. A more detailed diagram that clearly shows the inputs, layers, and connections for both sender architectures would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and reproducibility. The explanation of applying 1-D convolutions by treating image embeddings as channels is non-standard and would benefit from a more thorough description or visualization.
*   **Minor Edits:** The bibliography appears to have some formatting errors (e.g., "? \Q1998\E"), which should be corrected in the final version.

### Questions for the Authors

I have a few questions that could help clarify certain aspects of the work and its context:

1.  The introduction positions the work against other multi-agent communication papers (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) by stating that you further investigate how to make the emergent language more interpretable. Could you elaborate on the key differences in the task setup or methodology between your work and theirs that enable this investigation? A more direct comparison would help to better situate your specific contributions.
2.  What was the primary intuition behind the specific architecture of the "informed sender"? While it clearly performs well, the motivation for its inductive bias (combining image embeddings dimension-by-dimension) is not fully spelled out. Why is this a particularly suitable bias for a referential game?
3.  The experiments use vocabularies of size 10 and 100. How sensitive are your findings, particularly regarding the semantic structure (purity) and symbol usage, to the vocabulary size? Would you expect a different qualitative outcome (e.g., the emergence of compositionality) with a significantly larger vocabulary available to the agents?
