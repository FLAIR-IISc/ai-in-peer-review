### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel framework for studying language emergence through multi-agent communication. The authors propose a simple yet effective "referential game" where two neural network agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop their own communication protocol to successfully identify a target image from a pair. Starting as blank slates and trained with reinforcement learning, the agents learn to coordinate and solve the task with near-perfect accuracy. The work's primary contributions are showing that (1) simple neural agents can successfully develop a communication protocol from scratch in this interactive setting; (2) the emergent "language" captures meaningful, high-level semantic properties of the images, rather than just low-level visual features; and (3) this emergent language can be effectively grounded in natural language by combining the interactive game with a standard supervised image-labeling task. This grounding step is shown to make the agents' communication significantly more interpretable to humans, even on out-of-domain tasks.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles the important challenge of moving beyond passive, supervised learning for language. By framing language acquisition as an interactive, goal-oriented coordination problem between agents, it opens up a promising research direction for developing AI that can learn the functional aspects of communication.
2.  **Elegant and Effective Grounding Strategy:** The proposed method of first allowing a communication protocol to emerge through self-play and then grounding it in human language via a separate supervised task is both simple and powerful. The final human evaluation experiment provides compelling evidence that this approach can produce an agent language that is not only effective for agent-agent coordination but also partially understandable by humans, demonstrating a clear path towards human-AI communication.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-structured, the claims are clear, and the experiments are thoughtfully designed to support them.

**1. Well-motivated problem and approach:** The paper frames its central question clearly: How can we design environments that foster the development of a language useful for coordination with other agents, including humans? The approach is well-motivated by drawing parallels to foundational work in linguistics and game theory (e.g., Lewis' signaling games). It is also well-placed within the current literature, effectively contrasting its interactive, cooperative paradigm with supervised seq2seq models, complex "Wizard-of-Oz" environments, and purely human-in-the-loop systems. The choice of a simple referential game is an excellent starting point for this ambitious research program.

**2. Strong Empirical Support for Claims:** The paper systematically validates its claims through a series of experiments.
*   **Learning to Communicate:** The agents' ability to converge to near-perfect coordination (Table 1) robustly demonstrates that the proposed RL framework is effective. The comparison between "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures provides interesting insights into how inductive biases can affect the speed of convergence and the nature of the learned protocol.
*   **Semantic Structure of Emergent Language:** The authors go beyond simply measuring task success and analyze the properties of the emergent language. The cluster purity analysis and the t-SNE visualizations provide convincing evidence that the learned symbols correspond to high-level semantic categories (e.g., "living-vs-non-living") rather than just instance-level visual features. The experiment in Section 4.1, which forces agents to coordinate on class-level properties, further strengthens this claim.
*   **Grounding in Human Language:** The final experiment is a key strength of the paper. By mixing the game with supervised image classification, the authors show a dramatic increase in the semantic purity of the emergent code (from ~17% to 70%). Most impressively, the crowdsourced experiment demonstrates that humans can interpret the agent's communication with 68% accuracy, even when the agent uses its vocabulary "metonymically" for concepts it was not explicitly trained on. This is a powerful result that validates the overall approach.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions for improving the paper's clarity and are not critical to the decision.
*   There appears to be an inconsistency in how percentages are reported in Table 1 and Table 2. Table 2 reports purity values as proportions (e.g., 0.198), while Table 1 seems to use a 0-100 scale. Furthermore, the "obs-chance purity" values in Table 1 (e.g., 116.7) seem incorrect, as purity is typically a measure between 0 and 100. This might be a typo and should likely be reported as the simple difference (e.g., 16.7). Please clarify this.
*   A quick proofread could catch a few minor typos throughout the text (e.g., "assest" -> "assess", "usufal" -> "usual").

### **Questions for Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following point to ensure I have fully understood the model's implementation:
*   The paper states that the sender is informed of the target because it receives the target and distractor representations "always in this order". Could you please confirm that this fixed ordering is the sole mechanism by which the sender network is informed of the target `t`? For example, is the input to the sender a concatenation of `[feature_vector(target_image), feature_vector(distractor_image)]`? A brief clarification of this input structure would be helpful.
