This paper proposes a framework for training agents to communicate through interactive, cooperative multi-agent games. Specifically, it explores a referential game where a "sender" agent must send a symbol from a discrete vocabulary to a "receiver" agent to help it identify a target image from a pair. The agents, implemented as simple neural networks, are trained from scratch using reinforcement learning based on the success of their communication. The paper demonstrates that agents can successfully develop a communication protocol this way. It further investigates how to shape the emergent language to be more semantically meaningful by altering the game's "common knowledge" (e.g., by showing agents different instances of the same object class). The most significant contribution is a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in human language by alternating game-based training with a supervised image-labeling task. This combined approach is shown to produce a communication protocol that is significantly more interpretable to humans, even when referring to objects not seen during supervised training.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that explores a well-motivated and promising direction for language learning. My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:
1.  The work compellingly argues for and implements an interactive, game-based framework for learning communication, which is a valuable alternative to the dominant paradigm of passive supervised learning on static text corpora.
2.  The proposed method of combining interactive learning with supervised grounding (Section 5) is both novel and effective, yielding impressive results in human interpretability and suggesting a practical path forward for developing agents that can communicate with people.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's core motivation is excellent. Moving beyond learning from "canned" conversations towards learning the functional aspects of language through goal-oriented interaction is a critical step for AI. The Lewis-style signaling game is a simple, elegant, and effective environment for studying this problem. The general framework presented in Section 2 is clear and provides a solid foundation for the community to build upon.

The experimental results are convincing and well-presented. The initial experiments (Section 4) successfully demonstrate that agents can learn to communicate effectively from a blank slate. The analysis of the emergent protocol using semantic purity and t-SNE visualizations provides good evidence that the learned symbols capture meaningful conceptual properties of the images.

The highlight of the paper is the experiment in Section 5, which combines the referential game with a supervised classification task. This idea, inspired by AlphaGo's training regimen, is powerful. The results are striking: not only does communication success remain high, but the resulting protocol becomes dramatically more aligned with human language. The finding that humans could correctly interpret the agent's novel, "metonymic" use of words for unseen objects (68% accuracy) is a fantastic result that underscores the potential of this hybrid approach. It demonstrates a form of compositional generalization that is often lacking in purely supervised systems.

Overall, this is a well-executed study that is both a strong standalone contribution and a source of inspiration for many interesting future research directions.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

The agent architecture descriptions in Section 3 and the accompanying Figure 1 are difficult to parse. Figure 1 lacks a caption, labels for data flow, and appears to omit connections, making it hard to fully understand the model. Specifically, the "informed sender" architecture is described as having an inductive bias for dimension-by-dimension comparison via 1-D convolutions, but the diagram and text do not make it clear how this is implemented. A more detailed diagram with a clear caption explaining the tensor shapes and operations at each step would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors**

Your answers to these questions would help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper.

1.  Could you elaborate on the key distinctions between your framework and the contemporary works by Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016)? While your work focuses on making the emergent language interpretable, a more direct comparison of the methods and task environments would help better situate your contributions within the emerging field of multi-agent communication.
2.  The "agnostic" sender achieves near-perfect performance while using only two symbols. You suggest this might correspond to a "living vs. non-living" distinction. This is a fascinating result. Do you have any further analysis to support this hypothesis? For instance, what is the communication accuracy on pairs where both images are from the same "living" or "non-living" super-category?
3.  Regarding the "informed sender," could you provide a more precise description of the convolutional operation? Is the input a tensor of shape `[2, embedding_dim]` which is treated as `[height=2, width=1, channels=embedding_dim]`, or is it treated differently? Clarifying this would be very helpful.
