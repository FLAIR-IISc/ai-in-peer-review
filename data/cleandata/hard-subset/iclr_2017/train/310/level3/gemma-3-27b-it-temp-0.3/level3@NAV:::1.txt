## Review of "Learning to Communicate"

This paper tackles the important problem of understanding how communication emerges between agents and whether the strategies they develop resemble human communication. The authors propose a multi-agent referential game framework where agents learn to communicate from scratch, and investigate factors influencing the development of interpretable “languages”. The use of a relatively simple task is a strength, as it allows for more intuitive post-learning analysis of the emergent communication protocols. The findings regarding informed agents utilizing multiple symbols while agnostic agents rely on fewer are interesting and suggest a link between architectural choices and communication complexity.

I recommend **rejection** at this time. While the work is promising, the core task appears to fundamentally reduce to an image classification problem when the two images presented to the agents are from different categories. This significantly weakens the claim of emergent *communication* as it suggests the agents are primarily learning to classify rather than exchange information. 

My primary concern stems from the observation that the task, as presented, strongly incentivizes encoding image class rather than nuanced differences *within* a class. This is further supported by the authors’ own acknowledgement that the setup resembles a transfer learning problem, where the agents could potentially achieve similar results more efficiently through supervised learning. The reinforcement learning approach, while valid, feels unnecessarily complex given the apparent simplicity of the underlying task. The reported improvements in semantic purity, while positive, are not compelling enough to overcome this fundamental issue. The grounding experiment, while a step in the right direction, doesn’t fully address the core problem of whether genuine communication is occurring.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the task. It would be helpful to explore how the results change when the two images are from the *same* category, forcing the agents to communicate more specific information. Furthermore, a comparison to a baseline image classification model trained on the same data would provide valuable context for evaluating the performance of the agents. 

I have the following questions for the authors: 

1.  How do the results change when the two images presented to the agents are drawn from the same ImageNet category? Does this necessitate a more complex communication strategy?
2.  Have you compared the performance of the agents to a standard image classification model trained to distinguish between the two images presented in each game? If so, what were the results?
3.  Could you elaborate on the specific mechanisms by which the supervised learning phase improves the interpretability of the emergent language, beyond simply associating symbols with ImageNet labels? Does it influence the *structure* of the communication protocol?
