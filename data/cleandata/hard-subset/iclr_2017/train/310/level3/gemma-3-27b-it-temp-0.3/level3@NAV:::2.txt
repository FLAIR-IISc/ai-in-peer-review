## Review of "Multi-Agent Communication in Referential Games"

This paper presents a framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication in referential games. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate and develop a communication protocol from scratch, and explore how manipulating the game environment can influence the semantic properties of the emergent language. They further propose a strategy for grounding this learned communication in natural language through a combination of game-based training and supervised learning. The work is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in AI – moving beyond passive learning to interactive, communicative agents. The exploration of environment design to encourage interpretable language is particularly insightful.

I recommend **Acceptance** of this paper. The approach is novel and promising, and the results are reasonable given the complexity of the problem. The combination of game-based learning with supervised grounding appears to be a particularly fruitful direction, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

The strength of this work lies in its clear framing of the problem and the demonstration of emergent communication. The authors convincingly argue for the limitations of purely supervised learning and present a compelling alternative. The results showing the influence of the game environment on semantic properties are also noteworthy. The grounding strategy, while preliminary, shows encouraging signs of aligning the agent’s language with human intuition. 

However, the paper’s architecture (Figure 1) is difficult to follow. The description in Section 3 is insufficient to fully understand the network connections and the purpose of specific components. A clearer diagram with a detailed caption is crucial. Additionally, while the authors acknowledge related work, I lack sufficient familiarity with the most closely related literature (specifically, the nuances within the multi-agent communication field) to fully assess the novelty and context of their approach. 

I would appreciate clarification on the following:

*   Could the authors elaborate on the choice of the specific network architectures used? What design principles guided these choices, and were alternative architectures considered?
*   What is the computational cost of training these agents? How does this scale with vocabulary size and the complexity of the image dataset?
*   Regarding the supervised learning phase, what criteria were used to select the 100 labels used for grounding? Would using a larger or different set of labels significantly impact the results?
*   Could the authors provide more examples of the “metonymic” links observed in the human evaluation, as described in Section 5?
