## Review of "A Framework for Language Learning via Multi-Agent Communication"

This paper presents a compelling framework for emergent language learning through referential games played between two neural network agents. The authors successfully demonstrate that these agents can converge to effective communication policies and that the resulting “symbols” exhibit semantic properties related to the underlying image content. The subsequent grounding of these symbols in human language via a combined game-playing and supervised learning approach is a particularly exciting and novel contribution. The work is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in AI – moving beyond passive learning of language to interactive communication – and builds upon relevant work in multi-agent systems, linguistics, and game theory. 

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The core results convincingly demonstrate the feasibility of learning communication protocols from scratch, and the grounding experiment represents a significant step towards bridging the gap between machine and human language. The experimental setup is clear, and the results are presented with sufficient detail to allow for replication. 

The strength of this work lies in its elegant simplicity and the clear demonstration of emergent behavior. The authors effectively show that, given a suitable environment, agents can develop meaningful communication strategies without explicit linguistic supervision. The multi-game setup, alternating between the referential game and image classification, is a clever way to improve the grounding of the learned symbols. The analysis of symbol purity and the visualization of semantic relationships further strengthen the claims.

However, a clarification regarding the incorporation of the target variable *t* into the sender network would be beneficial. Specifically, how is this binary signal represented and processed within the sender’s architecture? Additionally, there appears to be an inconsistency in the reporting of percentage values in Table 1 and Table 2. Table 1 reports values between 0 and 100, while Table 2 reports values between 0 and 1. This likely indicates an error in the “obs-chance purity” values in Table 1. Finally, minor typos should be addressed: “assest” should be “assess”, and “usufal” should be “usual”.



I believe addressing these points will further enhance the clarity and rigor of this already strong contribution. I also wonder if the authors have explored the impact of different vocabulary sizes on the quality of the emergent language. Would a larger vocabulary lead to more nuanced communication, or does it introduce unnecessary complexity?
