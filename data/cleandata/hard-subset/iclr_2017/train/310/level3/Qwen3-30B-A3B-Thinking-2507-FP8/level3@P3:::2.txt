

# Review of "Learning to Communicate with Multi-Agent Referential Games" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a compelling framework for training natural language systems through multi-agent interactive referential communication games. The authors address a fundamental limitation in current NLP approaches by demonstrating that agents can develop functional communication protocols through interactive gameplay rather than passive text exposure. They show that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate in referential games, developing symbol meanings that align with human semantic categories. The paper makes a particularly significant contribution by proposing a hybrid approach that combines game-based learning with supervised image labeling to ground communication in natural language, demonstrating that agents can develop a language interpretable by humans. The work is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and opens promising new directions for developing conversational AI.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a well-designed methodology, scientifically rigorous results, and meaningful insights into language emergence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

1. **Problem Definition and Motivation**: The paper clearly identifies the critical problem of training language systems for interactive communication rather than just statistical pattern matching. The authors effectively motivate their approach by contrasting supervised learning with the functional aspects of communication, citing relevant linguistic and cognitive science literature. The framework is well-placed in the literature, with thoughtful discussion of related work from multi-agent systems, game theory, and language evolution.

2. **Scientific Rigor**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence that simple neural networks can learn to communicate effectively in referential games, with clear metrics showing coordination success and semantic alignment. The authors go beyond basic communication to investigate how to make emergent languages more interpretable, using purity metrics, SVD analysis, and human evaluation. The hybrid approach combining game-based training with supervised learning to ground language in natural vocabulary is particularly compelling and well-supported.

3. **Novelty and Impact**: While prior work exists on multi-agent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al. 2016; Foerster et al. 2016), this paper makes a significant contribution by investigating how to shape the emergent language to better reflect human semantic understanding and by proposing a concrete path to grounding communication in natural language. The results demonstrate that agents can develop communication protocols that are partially interpretable by humans, a critical step toward developing practical conversational agents.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- Figure 1's architecture diagram is difficult to interpret as it omits key connections between network components. A clearer diagram with labeled connections would greatly improve understanding of the sender and receiver architectures, especially the "informed sender" design.

- The description of the "informed sender" architecture (Section 3) would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the 1D convolutions work with the image embeddings. The statement that "the agnostic architecture nests the informed one" is confusing without a clearer explanation of the architectural relationship.

- The paper mentions in Section 6 that they suggest alternating between game-based training and supervised learning, but this strategy isn't elaborated on in the main text. A more detailed explanation of this approach would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the agnostic sender architecture "nests" the informed one, but it's unclear how this is achieved. Could you clarify the architectural relationship between these two models?

2. In the grounded language experiment (Section 5), you use 100 labels from your dataset for supervised training. How were these specific labels selected, and how representative are they of the broader semantic space of the images?

3. The paper describes "metonymic" links between symbols and images (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea). Could you elaborate on how these differ from standard semantic shifts in human language, and whether these metonymic patterns are consistent across multiple runs or agents?
