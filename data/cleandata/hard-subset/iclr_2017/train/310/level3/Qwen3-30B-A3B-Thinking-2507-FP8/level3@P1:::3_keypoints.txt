- Presents a multi-agent referential game framework where sender and receiver coordinate with arbitrary symbols to identify target images.  
- Shows that minimal feedforward agents can reach near-perfect coordination within 10k training rounds.  
- Demonstrates that removing low-level visual common knowledge induces more semantically meaningful symbol use aligned with human conceptual categories.  
- Introduces a grounding strategy alternating sender training between the referential game and supervised ImageNet classification to improve interpretability.  
- Grounding yields strong human interpretability (68% crowd identification vs. 1% chance) and 70% semantic purity vs. 33% for non-grounded setups.  
- Empirical evaluations include SVD decomposition, purity metrics, and t-SNE visualizations, showing robust semantic structure.  
- Generalizability validated by results on the ReferItGame dataset beyond the training domain.  
- Recommends acceptance because the paradigm is scalable, avoids heavy supervision, and aligns emergent communication with human language.  
- Strengths: clean experimental design, rigorous semantic evaluation, and an innovative, scalable grounding method using existing supervised datasets.  
- Improvement needed: clarify how the target variable t is incorporated into the sender network (binary mask, one-hot, or image ordering).  
- Fix inconsistencies in percentage reporting in Tables 1–2 (e.g., "obs-chance purity" scaling) and correct minor typos.  
- Ask authors to elaborate whether the agnostic sender’s living-vs-non-living split reflects architectural bias or game dynamics.
