

This paper presents a compelling framework for learning language through multi-agent interaction in referential games, addressing a critical gap in training conversational agents. The authors propose a simple yet effective setup where a sender and receiver agents coordinate to identify target images from pairs, using arbitrary symbols to communicate. Crucially, they demonstrate that agents with minimal architectures (feedforward networks) can converge to near-perfect coordination within 10k training rounds. The work further advances the field by showing how environmental modifications—such as removing low-level visual common knowledge—induce more semantically meaningful symbol usage, aligning with human-level conceptual categories. Most significantly, they introduce a novel grounding strategy: alternating the sender's training between the referential game and supervised image classification (using ImageNet labels), which dramatically improves symbol interpretability by humans (68% correct identification in crowdsourced tests vs. 1% chance). This bridges the gap between emergent agent communication and human-understandable language, offering a scalable alternative to human-in-the-loop methods.

I recommend **acceptance** for two key reasons. First, the paper establishes a novel and scalable paradigm for language learning that avoids data-hungry supervised approaches by leveraging multi-agent bootstrapping—a critical step toward interactive AI. Second, the grounding strategy successfully aligns emergent communication with human language without requiring continuous human supervision, directly addressing a core challenge in conversational AI. The empirical results are robust: semantic purity (measured against McRae categories) significantly exceeds chance in all configurations, and the grounding method achieves 70% purity (vs. 33% in non-grounded setups) while enabling human interpretable communication (68% accuracy in crowdsourcing). This work not only advances the theoretical understanding of language emergence but also provides a practical pathway to human-compatible agents.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clean experimental design, rigorous evaluation of semantic properties (via SVD decomposition, purity metrics, and t-SNE visualizations), and the convincing demonstration that simple neural agents can develop functional communication. The grounding approach is particularly innovative—it repurposes existing supervised datasets (ImageNet) to provide semantic anchors, avoiding the scalability issues of prior human-in-the-loop methods. The authors also thoughtfully engage with related work (e.g., distinguishing from "Wizard-of-Oz" setups and multi-agent game theory literature), positioning their contribution as both theoretically grounded and practically oriented. The results on the ReferItGame dataset further validate the generalizability of the grounded language beyond the training domain.

For improvement, the authors should: (1) clarify the incorporation of the target variable *t* into the sender network (e.g., is it a binary mask, a one-hot vector, or implicitly handled via image ordering?), as this is critical for reproducibility; (2) fix inconsistent percentage reporting in Tables 1–2 (e.g., "obs-chance purity" values in [0,1] vs. [0,100]); (3) correct minor typos ("assest" → "assess," "usufal" → "usual"). These are easily addressable and do not undermine the core contribution.

To further validate the semantic alignment claim, I ask: How does the target variable *t* specifically influence the sender’s policy? The paper states the sender "receives input θ_S(i_L, i_R, t)" but does not detail how *t* is encoded (e.g., as a binary flag, or via the image ordering). Clarifying this would strengthen the methodological transparency. Additionally, the observation that the agnostic sender (using only 2 symbols) still achieves semantic purity via a "living-vs-non-living" split (citing Caramazza & Shelton, 1998) is intriguing—could the authors elaborate on whether this reflects an inherent bias in the architecture or the game dynamics? This would deepen the theoretical discussion of language emergence.
