- Paper studies emergent interpretable communication in referential games where sender and receiver identify a target image using a limited vocabulary.  
- Authors show focusing the game on object categories yields protocols with better semantic alignment and propose grounding these in natural language via supervised learning.  
- Reviewer recommends rejection.  
- Major criticism 1: When image pairs are from different categories, the task reduces to trivial image classification, undermining the communication claim.  
- Major criticism 2: Learned symbols simply encode image class, making the setup akin to transfer learning rather than a genuine communication protocol.  
- Claim that agents develop human-like language is unsupported; observed symbol usage likely reflects the classification task, not functional communication.  
- Informed vs. agnostic agent differences reflect architectural classification performance, not meaningful linguistic properties.  
- Authors fail to demonstrate that communication is necessary for coordination.  
- Suggested fixes: design tasks where communication is essential, show communication properties beyond classification, and clarify differences from existing image-classification work.  
- Reviewer questions: How would the game be modified so communication is truly necessary, and what evidence shows symbol usage reflects functional communication rather than classification patterns?
