

**Review**

This paper presents an innovative approach to developing machine communication through multi-agent referential games, addressing the critical gap between supervised language learning and interactive language acquisition. The authors demonstrate that neural agents can learn to coordinate using symbolic communication in a referential game setting, where a sender identifies a target image among two options using a discrete vocabulary. Crucially, they show that by incorporating supervised image classification into the training regimen, the agents' communication becomes grounded in human language, with symbols aligning meaningfully with object categories and even generalizing to human interpretable concepts. The work makes a compelling case for an alternative to sequence-to-sequence approaches by focusing on interactive language emergence, and provides strong empirical evidence that agents develop semantically meaningful communication protocols.

I recommend acceptance. The paper tackles a fundamental question in AI: how to develop communication systems that emerge from interaction rather than relying solely on human-annotated data. The approach is well-motivated, building on game-theoretic foundations while addressing key limitations of prior work in multi-agent communication. The results are scientifically rigorous, with clear convergence evidence, semantic analysis of symbol meaning, and successful grounding in human language through supervised fine-tuning.

The paper's key strengths include the elegant multi-game setup that progressively refines symbol meaning, the thorough analysis of semantic purity across different architectures, and the convincing human evaluation showing 68% accuracy in human interpretation of the agents' communication. The authors make a strong case for the novelty of their approach as the first to learn agent-to-agent communication and then ground it in human language.

For improvement, please clarify how the target variable t is incorporated into the sender network architecture (this is crucial for understanding the information flow). Additionally, please correct the inconsistent percentage reporting in Tables 1 and 2 (one uses [0,100], the other [0,1]) and fix the minor typos ("assest" → "assess", "usufal" → "usual"). These are straightforward fixes that would enhance clarity without affecting the core contributions.
