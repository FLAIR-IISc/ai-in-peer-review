

# Review for ICLR 2017 Paper

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for language learning through multi-agent communication in referential games. The authors demonstrate that simple neural network agents can learn to coordinate effectively without any pre-defined communication protocol, developing their own "language" to identify target images from a pair of visual inputs. The paper makes several significant contributions: it establishes that agents can develop communication protocols reflecting semantic properties of images (rather than just low-level visual features), introduces a method for grounding these protocols in natural language through supervised learning, and provides interpretability through human evaluation of the emergent communication. The work is well-motivated and builds upon relevant literature in multi-agent systems, game theory, and cognitive science.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's core claims are undermined by fundamental issues with the experimental setup that render the main findings trivial and uninformative about language emergence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper claims to study "the emergence of language" through multi-agent communication, but the experimental setup effectively reduces to image classification when the two images are from different categories (which constitutes the vast majority of cases). This is a critical flaw that invalidates the paper's central contribution.

Specifically, the task reduces to image classification because the sender only needs to transmit the category of the target image (e.g., "cat" or "car"), and the receiver can simply identify which image matches that category. This is not language emergence—it's a simple transfer learning problem where the communication protocol merely encodes image class labels. The authors' own results confirm this: the agnostic sender uses only two symbols (representing "living" vs. "non-living" categories), while the informed sender uses more symbols but still essentially encodes image class information.

The paper also fails to distinguish between communication and classification. When the two images are from different categories (which happens most of the time), the problem is fundamentally not about communication but about classification. The authors acknowledge this by noting that "the task effectively reduces to image classification when the two images are from different categories," yet they proceed to claim that their framework demonstrates language emergence.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper would be significantly improved by:
- Explicitly acknowledging that the current experimental setup does not demonstrate language emergence but rather image classification with a communication channel
- Proposing a more challenging experimental design where the target and distractor images are from the same category, requiring more sophisticated communication strategies beyond simple class labeling
- Providing a more nuanced comparison with related work that has already tackled similar problems, such as Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016), which demonstrate more sophisticated communication protocols
- Clarifying the relationship between their "grounding" approach and the fundamental issue that their communication protocol is simply a way to transmit image class labels

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your framework truly differ from simply training a classifier to identify the target image, given that the task reduces to image classification when the two images are from different categories? What specific communication challenges does your approach address that aren't already solved by standard classification?

2. In the cases where the target and distractor images are from the same category (which you acknowledge is a limitation of your current setup), how would your agents' communication protocols differ from the current approach? What specific challenges would they face that require a more sophisticated language?

3. How do you reconcile your claim of "language emergence" with the fact that your symbols merely encode image class, which is essentially a transfer learning problem that could be solved without any communication protocol at all?
