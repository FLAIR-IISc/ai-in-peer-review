- Proposes a multi-agent referential game framework where sender and receiver develop language through interactive coordination, addressing passive text-exposure limits.
- Agents learn to identify target images from pairs via emergent communication.
- Shows simple neural networks can learn effective communication protocols.
- Demonstrates environmental modifications can induce more semantically intuitive "word meanings."
- Introduces a grounding strategy combining game-based learning with supervised image labeling to produce human-interpretable communication.
- Argues interactive language learning is more functionally grounded than passive supervised approaches.
- Recommendation: accept—problem is well-motivated and approach is scientifically rigorous with thorough experiments.
- Strength: robust experimental design comparing informed vs. agnostic sender architectures and their effect on communication.
- Strength: agents develop meaningful protocols without prior linguistic knowledge, using only coordination feedback.
- Evidence: emergent communication captures higher-level semantics (purity metrics, t-SNE visualizations).
- Strength: grounding experiment yields 68% human interpretation accuracy, indicating interpretability by humans.
- Literature placement: situates work relative to Sukhbaatar et al. (2016), Foerster et al. (2016), Lewis' signaling game, and cognitive science foundations.
- Revision request: clarify network architecture in Figure 1 and Section 3 with explicit information-flow diagrams and captions.
- Revision request: explain why the informed sender's convolutional structure yields better semantic generalization than the agnostic architecture.
- Revision request: contextualize the 68% human accuracy against baselines (random or supervised-only) to show significance.
- Revision request: discuss scalability of the approach to more complex communication tasks beyond referential games.
- Revision request: relate and compare to Wizard-of-Oz approaches (Mikolov et al. 2015), SHRLDU (Winograd 1971), and Wang et al. (2016) for clarity on novelty.
- Overall: well-written, replicable, statistically rigorous, limitations acknowledged, and makes a significant contribution toward human-AI communication.
