

This paper investigates the emergence of interpretable communication protocols in multi-agent systems through referential games. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate effectively in a game where a sender and receiver must identify a target image from a pair, with the sender using a limited vocabulary to communicate. They further show that modifying the game environment to focus on object categories rather than specific images leads to communication protocols with better semantic alignment to human concepts, and introduce a method to ground this communication in natural language through supervised learning.

I recommend rejection. The paper's key contribution is undermined by two critical issues. First, the task effectively reduces to image classification when the two images are from different categories, making the communication problem trivial rather than meaningful. Second, the symbols merely encode image class, rendering the setup more akin to a transfer learning problem than a genuine communication challenge. The agents' "language" is simply a compressed representation of image categories, not a functional communication protocol.

The paper's claim that agents develop language resembling human strategies is unsupported. The observed "symbol usage" is merely a byproduct of the image classification task, not evidence of functional communication. The informed vs. agnostic agent comparison shows architectural differences in classification performance, not meaningful linguistic properties. The authors fail to demonstrate that communication is necessary for coordination, which is central to their claim.

For improvement, the authors should: 1) Design a task where communication is genuinely necessary for coordination, not just a means to classify images; 2) Provide evidence that the agents' communication has properties beyond simple image classification; 3) Better articulate how their approach differs from existing image classification work.

To clarify my assessment: How would you modify the game to ensure communication is truly necessary for coordination, rather than merely serving as a classification shortcut? What evidence do you have that the agents' symbol usage reflects functional communication properties rather than just image classification patterns?
