

# Review of "Learning to Communicate" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a novel framework for learning agent-to-agent communication through multi-agent referential games. The authors propose a simple yet powerful setup where a sender, given a binary target and two images, sends a symbol to a receiver who must identify the target. Both agents are rewarded for correct prediction, creating a reinforcement learning problem where communication emerges naturally. The paper demonstrates that neural network agents can learn effective communication protocols that capture meaningful semantic concepts, not just low-level visual features. Crucially, the authors introduce a multi-game setup that alternates between the referential game and supervised image classification, effectively grounding the agents' communication in human language. The paper provides compelling evidence that the emergent communication can be interpreted by humans, with 68% accuracy in a crowdsourced human evaluation. This work represents a significant step toward developing AI systems that can communicate with humans productively, offering a promising alternative to traditional seq2seq approaches.

## 2. Decision
Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of multi-agent learning and language acquisition. The authors clearly articulate the problem of developing interactive language systems that go beyond passive supervised learning, and they provide a well-motivated framework for addressing it. Their approach is well-placed in the literature, building on Lewis' signaling game while introducing novel elements like the multi-game setup for grounding communication.

The paper provides robust empirical evidence supporting its claims. The experiments demonstrate that agents converge to optimal policies (with near-perfect coordination), that learned communication tokens capture meaningful semantic concepts (evidenced by higher purity scores and t-SNE visualizations), and that grounding through supervised learning improves human interpretability (with 68% human accuracy in a follow-up experiment). The multi-game setup is particularly compelling as it provides a scalable solution to the grounding problem without requiring constant human intervention.

The paper's novelty is well-justified: it's the first to learn agent-to-agent communication and then ground it in human language, addressing a key limitation of previous work. The authors' approach to grounding through a multi-game setup is both elegant and practical, offering a clear path toward human-interpretable communication.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the target variable t is incorporated into the sender network. The abstract states the sender is "given a binary target t," but the detailed description in Section 3 suggests the target is implicitly known through the ordering of images (iL and iR), which is different from a true "given" target. Please clarify this in the revision.
- There's an inconsistency in Table 1 and Table 2 regarding percentage reporting (one in [0,100], the other in [0,1]). The "obs-chance purity" in Table 1 is likely a typo and should be reported consistently with Table 2.
- Please fix minor typos: "assest" → "assess", "usufal" → "usual" (in the abstract).
- Consider adding a brief discussion of how the results might scale to more complex communication tasks or larger vocabularies.
- The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the "metonymic" communication patterns observed in the human evaluation (Figure 4).

## Clarification Questions
1. How exactly is the target variable t incorporated into the sender network? The paper mentions the sender is "given a binary target t" but the architecture description suggests the target is indicated through image ordering (iL or iR), which is different from having t as an explicit input.
2. The "obs-chance purity" in Table 1 appears to be inconsistent with the reporting in Table 2. Please clarify whether this is a reporting error and provide consistent values across both tables.
3. The paper states that the informed sender uses 88 symbols after grounding, but the vocabulary size was 100. How many symbols were unused, and why?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field, with a well-designed experiment that provides compelling evidence for the emergence of meaningful communication. The grounding approach is particularly promising for future work on human-AI communication. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's strong contribution. I recommend acceptance.
