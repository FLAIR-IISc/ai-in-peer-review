- Proposes a multi-agent referential game framework to train language through interaction rather than passive text exposure.  
- Shows simple neural nets can learn to coordinate in referential games and develop symbol meanings aligned with human semantic categories.  
- Introduces a hybrid approach combining game-based learning with supervised image labeling to ground emergent communication in natural language.  
- Well-motivated, rigorously evaluated work that opens promising directions for conversational AI.  
- Recommendation: Accept — strong contribution with sound methodology and meaningful insights.  
- Problem framing clearly contrasts functional interactive communication with standard supervised/statistical NLP approaches and situates work in relevant literature.  
- Empirical rigor: provides coordination/semantic alignment metrics, SVD analysis, purity measures, and human evaluation to support claims.  
- Shows how supervised grounding improves interpretability of emergent languages, with convincing experimental support.  
- Novelty: advances beyond prior multi-agent work by shaping emergent language toward human semantics and proposing grounding methods.  
- Figure 1 is unclear — missing key connections and would benefit from a clearer, labeled architecture diagram.  
- The "informed sender" architecture and its 1D convolutions need a more detailed, clearer explanation and its relation to the agnostic sender.  
- The suggested alternating game-based and supervised training strategy (Section 6) is mentioned but not elaborated in the main text.  
- Request clarification: how exactly does the agnostic sender "nest" the informed sender architecturally?  
- Request clarification: how were the 100 labels for supervised grounding chosen and how representative are they of the dataset’s semantic space?  
- Request clarification: explain the reported "metonymic" symbol-image links, how they compare to human semantic shifts, and whether these patterns are consistent across runs/agents.
