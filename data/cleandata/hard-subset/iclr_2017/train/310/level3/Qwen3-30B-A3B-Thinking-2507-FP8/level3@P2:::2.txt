

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of natural language processing by introducing a novel framework for language learning through multi-agent communication games. The authors propose using referential games where two agents (a sender and receiver) must coordinate to identify target images from a pair, leading to the emergence of a communication protocol without pre-defined language. The paper demonstrates that simple neural networks can successfully develop communication strategies that reflect semantic properties of images, and further shows how to ground this emergent language in natural language through a combination of interactive game-based learning and supervised learning. This work addresses a significant limitation of current language models that rely on passive learning from large text corpora, and offers a promising path toward developing interactive language systems that can communicate with humans.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong case for its contribution to the field. First, it tackles a meaningful problem: how to develop language systems that can interactively learn communication rather than passively absorbing pre-existing language. The authors clearly articulate how current supervised approaches fail to capture the functional aspects of communication, making their interactive framework well-motivated and timely.

Second, the approach is well-placed in the literature. The paper connects to relevant work in linguistics, cognitive science, game theory, and recent advances in multi-agent reinforcement learning. The authors properly situate their contribution relative to Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016), while highlighting their novel focus on making emergent language interpretable by humans.

Third, the paper provides scientifically rigorous evidence for its claims. The experiments are well-designed to test whether agents can learn to communicate (Table 1), whether the emergent language reflects semantic properties (Figure 3), and whether grounding in natural language improves human interpretability (Section 5). The results consistently support the authors' claims, with appropriate statistical analysis and comparison to baseline approaches.

The most significant contribution appears to be the demonstration of how to ground emergent communication in natural language through a combination of game-based learning and supervised learning (Section 5), which addresses a critical barrier to practical applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper makes a strong contribution, there are a few areas that could be improved to strengthen it further:

1. Figure 1 requires significant clarification. The network architecture description in Section 3 is difficult to follow without a clear visual representation. The figure should include all connections and clearly label the input, processing, and output layers. A detailed caption explaining the diagram would also be helpful.

2. The description of the "informed sender" architecture in Section 3 could be made more accessible. The current explanation is quite technical and would benefit from a more intuitive description of what the convolutional filters are doing and why they're advantageous.

3. The related work section could be strengthened by more thoroughly comparing with the closest literature, particularly regarding the specific differences between their approach and the work by Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016).

## 5. Clarifying Questions

To better understand the paper and ensure confidence in my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. How does your approach specifically differ from the multi-agent communication work by Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) in terms of the communication protocols learned? The paper mentions these works but doesn't clearly articulate the key differences.

2. The paper mentions that the agents can develop a language that generalizes beyond the supervised categories (Section 5). What specific mechanisms enable this generalization, and how might this be further improved or analyzed?

3. The results in Figure 3 (right) show improved semantic clustering when using class-level properties rather than image-level properties. How would this approach scale to more complex visual domains with greater semantic diversity?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating a promising path toward developing language systems that can communicate with humans through interactive learning rather than passive learning from text. The framework is well-motivated, the results are scientifically rigorous, and the potential for future applications is significant. With minor revisions to improve clarity, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
