

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper proposes an innovative framework for training language-capable agents through multi-agent communication games, specifically focusing on referential games where a sender must communicate to a receiver about a target image among two options. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate effectively in this setting, developing communication protocols that capture meaningful semantic properties rather than just low-level visual features. They further show that by grounding the communication in natural language through a supervised image classification task, the emergent language becomes interpretable by humans. The work makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between multi-agent communication learning and human-interpretable language, offering a promising alternative to traditional supervised sequence-to-sequence approaches.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a critical problem in natural language processing: developing agents that can communicate functionally rather than just statistically. The authors provide strong motivation by clearly distinguishing their approach from supervised learning methods that fail to capture the interactive nature of communication. The experimental design is well-justified, with appropriate baselines and careful analysis of the semantic properties of the emergent language.

The paper's key strength lies in its experimental validation. The authors demonstrate that agents converge to successful communication (Table 1), and crucially, that the communication captures meaningful semantic properties (Figures 2-3). The human evaluation in Section 5 provides compelling evidence that grounding the communication through supervised learning leads to language that humans can interpret (68% accuracy in the crowdsourced task). This represents a significant step toward developing machines that can communicate productively with humans.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly differentiating itself from related work in multi-agent systems (e.g., pre-programmed communication), human-in-the-loop approaches (e.g., Wizard-of-Oz), and supervised learning. The connection to game theory and linguistic evolution provides theoretical grounding for their empirical findings.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from more detailed explanation of how the target variable t is incorporated into the sender network architecture (mentioned as a key clarification point).
- There is an inconsistency in Table 1 and Table 2 regarding percentage reporting (one in [0,100], the other in [0,1]), particularly with the "obs-chance purity" metric in Table 1. This should be standardized to avoid confusion.
- Please correct minor typos: "assest" should be "assess," "usufal" should be "usual."
- Consider adding a brief discussion of the computational cost of the approach compared to traditional supervised methods.
- The authors might want to explore the limits of their approach - what types of language features cannot be captured through this framework?

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How is the target variable t specifically incorporated into the sender network's input? The paper states that "the sender receives input θS(iL, iR, t)" but doesn't detail how t is encoded in the network architecture. This is critical for understanding the learning process.

2. The "obs-chance purity" in Table 1 is reported in the [0,1] range while other purity values in Table 1 and Table 2 are in [0,100]. This inconsistency needs clarification. Is the "obs-chance purity" in Table 1 meant to be a percentage or a fraction?

3. In the grounding experiment, the authors report 47% accuracy when the sender produces the exact supervised label for target images. Could you provide more detail on the cases where this accuracy was not 100%? What types of errors occurred?

This paper makes an important contribution to the field by demonstrating how multi-agent communication games can lead to meaningful language development that can be grounded in human language. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, the approach is well-motivated, and the paper represents a significant step toward developing machines that can communicate with humans productively. With the minor revisions noted above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
