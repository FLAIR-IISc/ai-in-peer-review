- Proposes a framework for training language-capable agents via multi-agent referential games where a sender communicates a target image to a receiver.  
- Shows simple neural networks can coordinate and develop communication protocols that capture semantic, not just low-level visual, properties.  
- Demonstrates grounding emergent communication in supervised image classification produces human-interpretable language.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Strength: Addresses the need for functionally interactive agent communication and contrasts clearly with supervised seq2seq approaches.  
- Strength: Experimental design is well-justified with appropriate baselines and semantic analysis.  
- Empirical results: agents converge to successful communication (Table 1) and protocols exhibit semantic structure (Figures 2–3).  
- Human evaluation: grounding yields interpretable language with 68% accuracy in crowdsourced tasks.  
- Literature placement: distinguishes from pre-programmed, human-in-the-loop, and supervised methods; connects to game theory and linguistic evolution.  
- Request clarification: how the target variable t is incorporated into the sender network input.  
- Reporting issue: standardize percentage vs fraction reporting (obs-chance purity inconsistency between Table 1 and Table 2).  
- Minor edits: fix typos ("assest"→"assess", "usufal"→"usual") and discuss computational cost relative to supervised methods.  
- Suggestion: explore limits of the framework—what language features cannot be learned via this approach.  
- Question: provide details on the 47% grounding accuracy cases—what types of errors occurred when the sender produced the supervised label.
