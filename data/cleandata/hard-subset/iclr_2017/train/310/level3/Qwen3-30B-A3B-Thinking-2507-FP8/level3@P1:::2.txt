

This paper presents a compelling approach to language learning through multi-agent communication in referential games, addressing a critical limitation of current natural language systems that rely on passive text exposure. The authors propose a framework where two agents (sender and receiver) develop their own language through interactive coordination in a referential game, where they must identify target images from pairs. The key contributions include demonstrating that simple neural networks can learn effective communication protocols, exploring how environmental modifications can induce more semantically intuitive "word meanings," and proposing a grounding strategy that combines game-based learning with supervised image labeling to create human-interpretable communication. The paper makes a strong case for the importance of interactive language learning over passive supervised approaches, citing the functional aspects of communication as central to human interaction.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The central problem of developing portable, interpretable communication protocols through multi-agent interaction is well-motivated and addresses a significant gap in current natural language processing. The approach is scientifically rigorous, with thorough experiments that demonstrate both the feasibility of the method and its alignment with human semantic intuitions.

The paper's strengths are several. First, the experimental design is robust and well-justified, with careful analysis of how different agent architectures (informed vs. agnostic senders) affect communication quality and semantic richness. The demonstration that agents can develop meaningful communication protocols without prior linguistic knowledge—using only coordination feedback—is impressive. Second, the authors provide compelling evidence that the emergent communication reflects higher-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features, as shown through purity metrics across McRae categories and t-SNE visualizations. Third, the grounding approach in Section 5 is particularly innovative, demonstrating that combining game-based learning with supervised image classification leads to communication protocols that humans can interpret (68% accuracy in human interpretation tasks), which is a crucial step toward human-AI communication.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, acknowledging relevant prior work on multi-agent communication (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) while clearly differentiating its focus on developing interpretable communication rather than merely demonstrating communication emergence. The connection to linguistic theory (Lewis' signaling game) and cognitive science provides strong theoretical grounding for the experimental design.

For additional improvement, the authors should clarify the network architecture in Figure 1, which appears to omit key connections between layers. The description in Section 3 is difficult to follow without a more detailed diagram, and the caption should explicitly explain the information flow. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the informed sender's convolutional structure specifically enables better semantic generalization compared to the agnostic architecture, as this appears to be a key insight.

I would also appreciate clarification on whether the 68% human interpretation accuracy in the grounding experiment represents a significant improvement over a baseline (e.g., random guessing or a simple supervised-only baseline), as this would better contextualize the contribution. Additionally, the authors might consider discussing how the approach scales to more complex communication tasks beyond referential games, as this would strengthen the argument for the framework's broader applicability.

The paper's claim that agents can develop communication protocols that reflect human semantic intuitions is supported by multiple lines of evidence: the purity metrics, the t-SNE visualizations, and the human interpretation experiments. The grounding approach is particularly compelling as it provides a path toward human-interpretable communication without requiring extensive human annotation during the learning phase. The fact that agents can generalize their communication to novel images beyond the supervised categories (e.g., using "metonymic" relationships) is especially promising for real-world applications.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and sufficient technical detail to replicate the experiments. The results are presented with appropriate statistical rigor, and the limitations are honestly acknowledged. The authors have made a significant contribution to understanding how language can emerge from multi-agent coordination, with clear implications for developing more natural human-AI communication systems.

To address the reviewers' concerns about the related literature, I would appreciate a brief discussion of how this work relates to the "Wizard-of-Oz" approaches (Mikolov et al., 2015) and the SHRLDU program (Winograd, 1971), particularly regarding the scalability advantages of the proposed framework. Also, the paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison with the recent work by Wang et al. (2016) on learning language games through interaction, to further clarify the novelty of the approach.

The authors' suggestion to combine game-based learning with supervised learning (Section 5) is particularly promising and represents a significant step toward practical applications. The fact that this approach maintains high coordination performance while improving human interpretability demonstrates a careful balance between the functional and structural aspects of language learning that is often overlooked in current approaches.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of language learning through multi-agent interaction, with compelling evidence that simple neural networks can develop communication protocols that align with human semantic intuition, and a practical path toward grounding these protocols in natural language. The work is well-executed, well-motivated, and has clear implications for future research in human-AI communication.
