- Paper proposes a framework for emergent communication using referential games where neural senders and receivers learn to identify target images, and shows environment modifications and supervised grounding can yield more human-like semantics.

- Decision: recommend reject — claims of language emergence are not sufficiently supported because the task effectively reduces to image classification.

- Core critique: when target and distractor are from different categories, the task becomes simple category classification, not coordination-dependent communication.

- Empirical evidence: the agnostic sender collapses to two symbols (likely living vs non-living), while the informed sender uses more symbols, indicating minimal classification-driven encoding.

- Modification results (class-level properties) increase purity, consistent with simpler classification rather than genuine emergent communication.

- Grounding via supervised learning is interesting but does not resolve that agents are mapping categories to words rather than developing coordination strategies.

- Authors’ claim about progress toward human-productive communication is overstated given the experimental limitations.

- Suggested analysis: more thoroughly analyze why the communication task differs from standard image classification.

- Suggested experiment: create scenarios forcing coordination beyond classification (e.g., both images from the same category) to test for nuanced communication.

- Suggested discussion: better situate the work in emergent communication literature and clarify the distinction between “language” and “communication.”

- Questions for authors: clarify how the referential game differs from classification and the role of communication; how to force agents beyond basic category distinctions; what allows generalization beyond supervised categories; how to modify the game to require true coordination (e.g., distinguishing same-category images).
