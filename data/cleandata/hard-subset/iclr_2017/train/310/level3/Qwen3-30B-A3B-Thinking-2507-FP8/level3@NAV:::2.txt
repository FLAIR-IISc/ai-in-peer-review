

**Review**

This paper proposes a framework for language learning through multi-agent communication games, specifically focusing on referential games where a sender and receiver must coordinate to identify a target image. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to communicate effectively, develop symbol meanings that align with human semantic categories, and explore a strategy for grounding agents' communication in natural language through supervised learning. The work makes a compelling contribution to the emerging area of interactive language learning, addressing a key limitation of purely supervised approaches.

I recommend **acceptance** for two key reasons: First, the framework is strong and well-motivated, providing a promising alternative to passive language learning methods. Second, the alternating training approach (Section 5) between game-based learning and supervised learning proves particularly fruitful for developing interpretable communication.

The paper's results are scientifically rigorous, with clear evidence that agents develop communication protocols reflecting high-level semantic properties rather than low-level visual features. The grounding strategy in Section 5 successfully improves human interpretability of the agents' communication, with 68% human accuracy in understanding the emergent language. The work invites interesting future variations, including extending the framework to more complex communication scenarios.

However, the paper would benefit from significant clarification. Figure 1's architecture is difficult to follow without explicit network connections, and the description in Section 3 lacks sufficient detail to fully understand the bespoke network design. A clearer diagram with a proper caption would greatly improve readability. Additionally, while the authors cite relevant literature, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of how their work specifically relates to the most recent multi-agent communication studies.

To further improve the paper, I recommend: (1) providing a detailed explanation of the network architecture in Figure 1 with explicit connections, (2) adding a more comprehensive literature comparison that situates their work within the most recent advances in multi-agent communication, and (3) clarifying the relationship between the informed and agnostic sender architectures.

I would appreciate clarification on whether the observed semantic properties are robust across different image datasets, and how the authors' approach compares to the most recent work in multi-agent language emergence (e.g., from the 2022-2023 literature).
