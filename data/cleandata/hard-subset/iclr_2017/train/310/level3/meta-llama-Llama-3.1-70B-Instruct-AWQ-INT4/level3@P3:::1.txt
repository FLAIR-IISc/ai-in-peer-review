This paper tackles the important problem of understanding communication between two agents and whether machine problem-solving resembles human strategies. The authors propose a framework for language learning that relies on multi-agent communication, which is a crucial step towards developing conversational machines. The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a simple yet effective task that makes post-learning analysis intuitive and interpretable.

The task, a referential game, involves a sender and a receiver seeing a pair of images and the sender sending a message to the receiver to identify the target image. The authors show that two networks with simple configurations are able to learn to coordinate in the referential game. The results suggest that the agents develop a language that captures general conceptual properties of the objects depicted in the images, rather than low-level visual properties.

One of the key findings of the paper is that informed agents use multiple symbols to transmit messages, while agnostic agents rely on only two symbols. This suggests that the informed agents are using more varied and word-like symbols, which is a crucial aspect of human language. The authors also show that the agents' language can be grounded in natural language by combining the referential game with a supervised image labeling task.

The paper's approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough review of the relevant literature. The use of a simple task and a well-defined evaluation metric makes the results easy to interpret. The paper's contributions are significant, and the results have implications for the development of conversational machines.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main critiques is that the task effectively reduces to image classification when the two images are from different categories. This raises questions about the generality of the results and whether the agents are truly learning a language or simply learning to classify images. Additionally, the symbols used by the agents merely encode image class, making the setup akin to a transfer learning problem that could be solved faster with supervised learning.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the limitations mentioned above. The authors should provide more evidence to support the claim that the agents are truly learning a language and not just classifying images. Additionally, the authors should discuss the implications of their results for the development of conversational machines and provide more context for the significance of their findings.

Some specific questions I would like the authors to address in their revisions include:

* How do the agents' language and performance change when the images are from the same category?
* Can the agents learn to communicate about more abstract concepts, such as actions or events?
* How do the results generalize to more complex tasks, such as multi-step communication or dialogue?

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from more visualizations of the agents' language and communication. For example, the authors could provide more examples of the symbols used by the agents and how they relate to the images.
* The authors should provide more details about the supervised image labeling task and how it is combined with the referential game.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results for the development of conversational machines. How do the authors envision their framework being used in real-world applications?
