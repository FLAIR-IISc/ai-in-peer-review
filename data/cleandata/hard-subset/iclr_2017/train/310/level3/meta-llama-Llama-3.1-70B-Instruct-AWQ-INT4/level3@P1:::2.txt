This paper introduces a novel framework for training natural language systems through multi-agent interactive referential communication games. The authors propose a well-motivated approach to develop conversational machines that can communicate with humans productively. The framework is based on a simple yet effective game setup, where two agents, a sender and a receiver, interact to identify a target image from a pair of images. The agents develop their own language to coordinate and earn payoffs, and the authors demonstrate that this approach can lead to the emergence of a language that captures general conceptual properties of objects.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the framework, including the game setup, agent architectures, and training details. The authors also present several experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of their approach, including the ability of agents to coordinate, the semantic properties of the emergent language, and the impact of tweaking the game setup to encourage high-level semantics. The results show that the agents can learn to coordinate and develop a language that is partially interpretable by humans.

One of the key contributions of this paper is the demonstration that alternating between game-based training and supervised learning can be a fruitful approach to training natural language systems. The authors show that this approach can lead to improved communication success and increased interpretability of the emergent language.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The framework and results are not thoroughly contextualized within the existing literature on multi-agent communication and natural language processing. While the authors mention some related work, a more comprehensive review of the literature would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the bespoke architecture and training details in Section 3 could be clarified with additional diagrams and explanations.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing and multi-agent communication. The framework and results presented in the paper are well-motivated and well-executed, and the authors provide a clear and compelling vision for future research in this area.
