**Summary**

This paper proposes a framework for language learning that relies on multi-agent communication. The authors study this learning in the context of referential games, where a sender and a receiver see a pair of images and the sender must communicate the target image to the receiver. The authors show that two networks with simple configurations can learn to coordinate in the referential game and that the agents' language can be made more interpretable by changing the game environment. They also present a simple strategy for grounding the agents' code into natural language.

**Decision**

I decide to **accept** this paper with the following key reasons:

1. The paper tackles an important problem in the field of artificial intelligence, namely understanding communication between two agents and whether machine problem-solving resembles human strategies.
2. The authors use a simple task that makes post-learning analysis intuitive and interpretable, allowing for a clear understanding of the results.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a framework for language learning that relies on multi-agent communication. The authors demonstrate that two networks with simple configurations can learn to coordinate in the referential game, and that the agents' language can be made more interpretable by changing the game environment. The results show that informed agents use multiple symbols to transmit messages, while agnostic agents rely on only two symbols. The authors also present a simple strategy for grounding the agents' code into natural language, which is a crucial step towards developing conversational machines.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One critique is that the task effectively reduces to image classification when the two images are from different categories. This simplification may not fully capture the complexity of human communication. Another critique is that the symbols merely encode image class, making the setup akin to a transfer learning problem that could be solved faster than with reinforcement learning.

**Additional Feedback**

To improve the paper, the authors could consider the following suggestions:

* Provide more details on the architecture of the sender and receiver networks, including the number of layers, the number of units in each layer, and the activation functions used.
* Discuss the implications of the results in the context of human communication and language learning. How do the findings relate to human strategies for communication, and what insights can be gained from the comparison?
* Consider using more complex tasks or environments to evaluate the proposed framework. This could include tasks that require the agents to communicate about more abstract concepts or to use more nuanced language.
* Provide more analysis on the interpretability of the agents' language. While the authors show that the symbols can be made more interpretable by changing the game environment, it is unclear how well the agents' language generalizes to new situations or to human language.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you provide more details on the training process, including the number of iterations, the batch size, and the learning rate?
* How do you plan to extend the proposed framework to more complex tasks or environments?
* Can you discuss the relationship between the proposed framework and other approaches to language learning, such as supervised learning or unsupervised learning?
* How do you plan to evaluate the proposed framework in more realistic scenarios, such as human-agent communication or multi-agent communication?
