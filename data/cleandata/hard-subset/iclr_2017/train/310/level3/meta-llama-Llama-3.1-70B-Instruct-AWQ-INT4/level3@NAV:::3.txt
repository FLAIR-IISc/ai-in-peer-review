**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to language learning through multi-agent communication in a referential game setup. The game involves a sender and a receiver, both neural networks, that learn to communicate about images by developing their own language. The authors show that the agents can converge to optimal policies and learn meaningful communication tokens that capture general conceptual properties of the objects depicted in the images. They also demonstrate that the agents' language can be grounded in human language by combining the game with a supervised image classification task. The results suggest that this approach can lead to the development of conversational machines that can communicate with humans productively.

**Decision**

Based on the review, I recommend acceptance of the paper. The paper tackles a specific and well-motivated problem, namely, the development of conversational machines through multi-agent communication. The approach is well-placed in the literature, and the results are scientifically rigorous and support the claims made by the authors.

**Questions for the Authors**

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify some aspects of the paper:

1. How is the target variable t incorporated into the sender network? The paper mentions that the sender receives input θS(iL, iR, t), but it is not clear how the target variable t is used in the sender network.
2. Can the authors provide more details on the supervised image classification task used to ground the agents' language in human language? How does the supervised task affect the emergence of meaningful communication tokens?
3. Can the authors provide more insights into the interpretability of the agents' language? How do the agents' symbols relate to human language, and what are the implications of this for the development of conversational machines?
