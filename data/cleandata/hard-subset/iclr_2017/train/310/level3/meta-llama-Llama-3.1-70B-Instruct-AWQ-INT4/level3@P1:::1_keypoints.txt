- Proposes a novel multi-agent framework for language learning studied via referential games (sender/receiver communicating target image).  
- Agents develop their own interactive emergent language; authors explore environment changes to shape semantic properties.  
- Well-motivated approach drawing on linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory to build conversational machines.  
- Significant contribution to multi-agent communication with a clear experimental framework.  
- Experiments show two simple networks can learn to coordinate and produce interpretable emergent language.  
- Results indicate the emergent language can reflect intuitive image semantics beyond trivial solutions.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance based on importance of problem and strength of contributions.  
- Critique: task can reduce to image classification when images come from different categories, limiting communicative complexity.  
- Critique: symbols may merely encode image class, making the setup similar to transfer learning rather than genuine language emergence.  
- Authors acknowledge these limitations and present evidence that the language is more nuanced than simple classification.  
- Suggested improvements: deeper analysis of emergent language structure and syntax.  
- Suggested experiments: study language evolution over time and effects of varying game environments/parameters.  
- Requested comparisons to other learning paradigms (supervised learning, transfer learning) to contextualize results.
