This paper proposes a novel framework for language learning that relies on multi-agent communication. The authors study this learning in the context of referential games, where a sender and a receiver see a pair of images and the sender must communicate the target image to the receiver. The agents develop their own language interactively, and the authors investigate how to make changes to the game environment to cause the emergent language to better reflect intuitive semantic properties of the images.

The authors' approach is well-motivated, as they aim to develop conversational machines that can communicate with humans productively. They draw inspiration from linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory, and their work is a significant contribution to the field of multi-agent communication.

The paper supports its claims through a series of experiments, where the authors show that two networks with simple configurations can learn to coordinate in the referential game. They also explore how to make changes to the game environment to cause the emergent language to better reflect intuitive semantic properties of the images. The authors' results are promising, as they show that the agents can develop a language that is interpretable by humans.
