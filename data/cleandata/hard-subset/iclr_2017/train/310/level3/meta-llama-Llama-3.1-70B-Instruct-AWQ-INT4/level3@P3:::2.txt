This paper introduces a novel approach to training natural language systems by leveraging multi-agent interactive referential communication games. The authors propose a framework for language learning that relies on agents developing their own language interactively to communicate about images. The paper explores the emergence of language in these games and investigates how to make the induced language more interpretable and aligned with human intuitions.

I decide to accept this paper, with the main reasons being the novelty and promise of the proposed approach, as well as the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation.

The paper provides a well-motivated and well-placed approach in the literature, building upon ideas from linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory. The authors demonstrate that their framework can lead to the emergence of a language that captures general conceptual properties of objects, rather than low-level visual properties. The results also show that the induced language can be made more interpretable by humans by combining the game with a supervised task.

The paper supports its claims with a thorough experimental evaluation, including a comparison of different sender architectures, an analysis of the semantic properties of the emergent communication protocol, and a demonstration of the effectiveness of grounding the agents' communication in human language.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of clarity in the description of the bespoke architecture and the experimental setup. Figure 1, in particular, appears to omit network connections, and the caption could be improved to provide a clearer explanation of the architecture.

To improve the paper, I would suggest providing more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameter tuning process and the specific implementation of the reinforcement learning algorithm. Additionally, it would be helpful to include more qualitative examples of the emergent language and its interpretation by humans.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer to clarify my understanding of the paper include:

* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the informed sender architecture and how it differs from the agnostic sender architecture?
* How did you select the hyperparameters for the experiments, and did you perform any hyperparameter tuning?
* Can you provide more examples of the emergent language and its interpretation by humans, particularly in the context of the supervised task?
* How do you plan to extend this work to more complex communication scenarios, such as multi-step communication or communication with multiple agents?

Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to training natural language systems and provides a thorough experimental evaluation of its effectiveness. With some minor revisions to address the above comments, I believe this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
