- Introduces a novel, well-motivated framework training NLP systems via multi-agent referential communication games.  
- Uses a simple sender–receiver game where agents identify a target image from a pair to develop an emergent language.  
- Shows agents can coordinate and develop a language that captures general conceptual properties of objects.  
- Provides a thorough analysis of game setup, agent architectures, and training details.  
- Presents experiments evaluating coordination success, semantic properties of emergent language, and effects of game tweaks encouraging high-level semantics.  
- Demonstrates alternating between game-based training and supervised learning improves communication success and interpretability.  
- Finds the emergent language is partially interpretable by humans.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons: novel framework and comprehensive experimental analysis.  
- Limitation: insufficient contextualization within existing multi-agent communication and NLP literature.  
- Limitation: bespoke architecture and training details (Section 3) need clearer diagrams and explanations.  
- Suggestion: explore other game setups and agent architectures to further validate effectiveness.  
- Questions for authors: clarify relation to prior multi-agent/NLP work and how this approach differs.  
- Questions for authors: plan for extending to multi-turn dialogue and more nuanced semantics?  
- Questions for authors: provide more details on the supervised component and how it grounds emergent language in natural language.
