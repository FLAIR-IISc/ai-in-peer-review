### Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for learning a communication protocol between two neural agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," within the context of an interactive referential game. The core task requires the agents to develop a shared language from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a pair of images. The authors make several key contributions:
1.  They demonstrate that simple feed-forward networks can successfully learn to communicate and coordinate to solve this task, developing their own symbolic language *tabula rasa*.
2.  They investigate how modifying the game's environment—specifically by removing low-level visual features from the agents' "common knowledge"—can nudge the emergent language towards more abstract, categorical representations that align better with human semantic concepts.
3.  Most compellingly, they propose and validate a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language. By interleaving the cooperative game with a standard supervised image labeling task, the sender learns to use human-understandable words in a functional, communicative context. This grounded language proves to be partially interpretable by humans, even when used to refer to objects outside the supervised vocabulary, exhibiting interesting and flexible semantic generalizations.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and thoughtfully executed study on emergent communication. My decision is based on two key strengths:

1.  **A Compelling Framework for Functional Language Learning:** The work effectively argues for and demonstrates the value of interactive, goal-driven learning over passive, supervised methods for developing functional communication skills in agents. The referential game setup is simple, elegant, and provides a clean environment for studying language emergence.
2.  **Novel and Promising Grounding Technique:** The method of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning to ground the emergent language is a significant contribution. The results, particularly the successful human interpretation experiment and the qualitative examples of "metonymic" generalization, provide strong evidence that this is a fruitful path towards building agents that can communicate productively with humans.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are well-supported by a series of carefully designed experiments. The initial experiments convincingly show that agents can learn to coordinate, and the analysis goes beyond simple task accuracy. By measuring vocabulary usage, performing SVD on the symbol-pair matrix, and calculating cluster purity against human-defined semantic categories, the authors provide a nuanced view of the properties of the emergent language. The finding that the "informed" sender architecture learns faster and develops a richer code supports the idea that architectural biases can play a key role.

The experiment in Section 4.1, where agents are forced to coordinate on class-level properties rather than instance-level details, is a clever demonstration of how environmental constraints can shape the resulting language towards greater semantic abstraction. This strengthens the central thesis that the environment is a critical component in fostering a desired type of language.

The final set of experiments in Section 5 provides the strongest support for the paper's claims. The dramatic increase in semantic purity (to 70%) after introducing the supervised task is a clear quantitative win. More importantly, the crowdsourced experiment provides qualitative and quantitative evidence for the primary goal: making the agents' communication understandable to humans. The fact that humans could correctly identify the target image 68% of the time, often by interpreting flexible, non-literal word usage (e.g., "dolphin" for a sea image), is a powerful and exciting result. It suggests that this hybrid training approach provides a foundation for a shared, flexible common ground between agents and humans.

While I am not an expert in the most closely related literature on multi-agent communication, the paper seems well-positioned, drawing connections to cognitive science, game theory, and recent work in deep reinforcement learning.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions aimed at improving the paper's clarity and are not critical to my assessment.

1.  **Clarity of Agent Architectures:** The description of the sender and receiver architectures in Section 3 is quite dense, and the diagram in Figure 1, while aesthetically clean, is too abstract to fully clarify the data flow. Specifically, the "informed sender" architecture is non-standard and difficult to fully grasp from the text alone. I would strongly recommend adding a detailed caption to Figure 1 that explains each component, the connections, and the dimensionalities of the tensors at each step. This would greatly aid reader comprehension.

2.  **Positioning Relative to Concurrent Work:** The paper cites other recent work on emergent communication (Foerster et al., 2016; Sukhbaatar et al., 2016). A slightly more detailed discussion in the introduction or related work section that explicitly contrasts this paper's referential game setup and grounding methodology with the approaches in that work could help readers better appreciate this paper's unique contributions.

### Questions for Authors

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The temperature parameter (τ=10) for the Gibbs distributions seems quite high, which would encourage a more stochastic policy. Have you experimented with different temperature values or an annealing schedule? How does this parameter affect the convergence of the communication protocol and the diversity of the symbols used in the final language?

2.  In the grounding experiment (Section 5), the supervised task used 100 labels from the initial ImageNet-derived dataset, but the referential game was played on a dataset derived from ReferItGame. Could you provide more detail on the relationship between these two datasets? For instance, how much overlap is there in the object categories present? Understanding this domain shift would help in assessing the impressive generalization shown in the human-subject experiment.

3.  The current framework uses a fixed, predefined vocabulary size. Do you have thoughts on how this approach could be extended to an open-ended vocabulary, where agents might invent new symbols as needed?
