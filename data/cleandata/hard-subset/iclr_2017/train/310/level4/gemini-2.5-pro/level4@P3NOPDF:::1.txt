This paper investigates the problem of emergent communication between two artificial agents in a cooperative referential game. The work addresses fundamental questions about how a shared, symbolic protocol can arise to solve a task, and whether such machine-generated communication bears any resemblance to human problem-solving. The authors' findings, particularly regarding the influence of prior knowledge on the complexity of the learned protocol, are intriguing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Fundamental Research Question:** The paper tackles the crucial and timely problem of understanding emergent communication. As multi-agent reinforcement learning systems become more prevalent, investigating how agents learn to cooperate and communicate is of significant scientific interest. The work contributes to the foundational understanding of whether machine intelligence develops communication strategies analogous to human ones.
*   **Clarity of Experimental Design:** The chosen task is simple and well-defined, which is a major strength of the paper. This simplicity allows for a clear and intuitive analysis of the communication protocols that emerge after learning. It makes the agents' strategies and the meaning of their symbols transparent and easy to interpret.
*   **Interesting Empirical Finding:** A key contribution is the observation regarding the difference in protocol complexity between "informed" and "agnostic" agents. The finding that informed agents, aware of the underlying class structure, develop a more expressive protocol using multiple symbols, while agnostic agents converge on a minimal two-symbol system, is a compelling result that highlights how prior knowledge shapes communication.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Task Simplification and Alternative Formulations:** The primary concern with this work lies in the formulation of the experimental task. In the case where the two images presented to the agents are from different categories, the task effectively reduces to a supervised image classification problem. The first agent's role becomes identifying the category of the target image and encoding it as a symbol. The second agent then learns to map this symbol back to the correct image, which is essentially a classification task.

    This observation suggests that the problem could be framed as a transfer learning challenge rather than one requiring a full reinforcement learning setup. A more direct approach would be to train a classifier to identify the image categories and then use the class labels as the communication symbols. It is plausible that such a supervised approach would converge much faster and more reliably than the proposed RL algorithm. The current framework may be an unnecessarily complex tool for the problem it is solving, which raises questions about the necessity and efficiency of the emergent communication approach in this specific context. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of this alternative framing and, ideally, a baseline comparison against a more direct supervised learning method. This would help to justify the choice of reinforcement learning and better isolate the unique contributions of the emergent communication paradigm.

**Summary:**

Overall, this is an interesting paper with a well-designed experiment that yields a clear and notable result. However, the work's impact is limited by the concern that the chosen task might not be complex enough to necessitate an emergent communication approach, and could potentially be solved more efficiently by simpler, well-established methods like supervised classification. Addressing this limitation by comparing the proposed method against such a baseline would substantially improve the paper and clarify the specific advantages of the RL-based approach for this problem.
