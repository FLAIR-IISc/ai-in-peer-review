### **Summary**

This paper proposes a referential game between two agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," to study the emergence of communication. Both agents observe a pair of images. The sender is also given a binary target variable `t`, indicating which of the two is the target image. The sender's task is to transmit a single symbol (a message) to the receiver, who must then use this message to identify the target image. A shared reward is given to both agents upon successful identification.

The authors parameterize the agents as neural networks that process pre-trained image feature vectors and train them end-to-end using the REINFORCE algorithm. The experiments show that the agents successfully converge to optimal policies. More importantly, the learned communication protocol—the mapping from image pairs to symbols—is shown to capture meaningful visual concepts.

The paper further investigates two extensions. First, in a variant of the game grounded on distinct image classes, the agents learn even more interpretable concepts. Second, a multi-task setup is introduced where the sender alternates between playing the referential game and performing a supervised image classification task. The results demonstrate that anchoring the communication to a supervised task leads to the emergence of an even more semantically meaningful symbolic code.

### **Evaluation and Recommendation**

This paper addresses the interesting and challenging problem of learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setting. This is a significantly more complex task than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which rightly justifies the authors' choice to begin with a relatively simple and well-defined task.

The primary contribution of this work is its novel approach to studying language. The framework allows agents to first develop their own communication protocol from scratch to solve a collaborative task, and then grounds this emergent language in human-understandable concepts. This presents a compelling alternative paradigm to conventional sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to focus on capturing the statistical properties of existing human language rather than its functional aspects. As the authors remark, their method prioritizes how language can emerge to serve a purpose.

The proposed task, the learning framework, and the insightful analysis and visualization of what the communicated tokens represent are valuable contributions. I believe this work serves as a useful stepping stone for future research in emergent communication and multi-agent learning. For these reasons, I recommend that the paper be accepted.
