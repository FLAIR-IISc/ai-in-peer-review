This paper proposes a multi-agent framework for studying emergent language, centered on a referential game. In this game, a "sender" agent must communicate a symbol to a "receiver" agent to enable it to identify a target image from a pair. The agents, parameterized as neural networks using pre-trained image features, receive a shared reward for success and are trained via reinforcement learning (REINFORCE). The work demonstrates that agents successfully converge to optimal policies, developing a symbolic code that reflects meaningful semantic concepts. The paper further shows that modifying the game to be grounded on object classes rather than specific image instances enhances the semantic structure of the emergent language. Finally, the authors introduce a multi-task setup where the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task, which effectively grounds the learned symbols in natural language, making them more interpretable.

I recommend accepting this paper. My decision is based on the novelty of the paradigm, which explores learning communication from first principles and then grounding it in human language, and the quality of the analysis demonstrating the emergence of a meaningful communication protocol.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by exploring an alternative to standard supervised sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to learn the statistical properties of language rather than its functional aspects. By framing language acquisition as a multi-agent coordination problem, the work investigates how communication can emerge from the need to cooperate. The approach of first fostering an emergent language and then aligning it with human language through a supervised objective is a novel and promising direction. The experiments are well-designed, and the analysis effectively demonstrates that the learned communication protocol is not arbitrary but captures semantic properties of the visual world. This work represents a solid and insightful stepping stone for future research into developing interactive, communicative agents.
