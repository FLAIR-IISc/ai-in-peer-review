This paper proposes a referential game between two agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," to study the emergence of communication. Both agents observe two images. The sender is also given a binary variable `t` that designates one of the images as the target. The sender's task is to transmit a symbol (a message from a discrete vocabulary) to the receiver, who must then use this message to identify the target image. Both agents receive a shared reward if the receiver's prediction is correct.

The agents are parameterized as neural networks that process pre-trained image feature vectors, and they are trained end-to-end using the REINFORCE algorithm. The authors demonstrate that under this setup, the agents converge to optimal policies. More importantly, the analysis shows that the learned communication protocol—the symbolic code transmitted between agents—develops meaningful, disentangled concepts related to image properties.

The paper extends this initial setup with two insightful variations. First, in a game grounded on distinct image classes, the agents are shown to learn even more interpretable concepts. Second, a multi-game setup is introduced where the sender alternates between playing the referential game and performing a standard supervised image classification task. The authors show that anchoring the agent's representations to this supervised task results in an emergent communication protocol with even clearer semantic structure.

**Assessment**

Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setting is an interesting and important research direction. The authors rightly point out that this is a considerably more challenging task than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which justifies their use of a simple and well-defined game as a starting point.

To the best of my knowledge, the proposed methodology of first learning a communication protocol between agents from scratch and then grounding this communication in human-understandable concepts is novel. As the authors remark, this may serve as an alternative paradigm to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to focus on the statistical properties of language rather than its functional, goal-oriented aspects.

The contributions of the proposed task and framework, along with the insightful analysis and visualization of the communicated tokens, represent a valuable stepping stone for future work in this area. For these reasons, I recommend that the paper be accepted.
