This paper proposes a referential game between two agents, a "sender" and a "receiver." Both agents observe a pair of images. The sender is also given a binary target variable indicating which of the two images is the target. Its task is to send a discrete symbol (a message) to the receiver, which must then use this message to identify the target image. Both agents receive a shared reward if the receiver's prediction is correct. The agents are parameterized as neural networks that take pre-trained image features as input and are trained using the REINFORCE algorithm.

The authors demonstrate that in this setting, the agents converge to optimal policies. Furthermore, their analysis shows that the learned communication protocol—the symbolic code transmitted from sender to receiver—encodes meaningful visual concepts. The paper extends this finding with experiments on a game variant where the images are drawn from different classes, which encourages the agents to learn even more semantically meaningful concepts. Finally, a multi-game setup is introduced where the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task. As expected, anchoring the communication in this supervised task leads to the emergence of symbols with even clearer semantic meaning.

Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setting is a compelling and important research direction. This task is significantly more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which justifies the paper's focus on a relatively simple, well-defined problem. To my knowledge, the approach of first having agents develop a communication protocol from scratch and then grounding this protocol in human-interpretable concepts (via the classification task) is novel. As the authors note, this paradigm offers an alternative to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which often prioritize learning the statistical properties of language over its functional, communicative aspects. I believe the paper's contributions—the proposed task, the learning framework, and the insightful analysis and visualization of the emergent communication protocol—represent a valuable stepping stone for future research in this area. For these reasons, I recommend that the paper be accepted.
