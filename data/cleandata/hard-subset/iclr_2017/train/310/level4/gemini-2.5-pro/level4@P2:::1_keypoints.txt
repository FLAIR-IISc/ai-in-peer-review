- Paper proposes a multi-agent referential game framework to study emergent language and shows agents can coordinate from scratch.  
- Core contributions: environment manipulations induce more semantically natural protocols and a method to ground emergent symbols in human language.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason: Experimental setup reduces to a supervised classification-like task, limiting claims about emergent language.  
- Coordination can be achieved by learning discriminative class labels, so the protocol may not reflect descriptive or compositional language.  
- In the setup, target and distractor from different object concepts encourage one-to-one symbol→category mappings rather than descriptive signals.  
- Evidence: "Agnostic" sender collapses to two symbols (e.g., living vs non-living), indicating high-level classification rather than language.  
- "Informed" sender’s architecture biases it toward dimension-wise comparisons, favoring discrimination over descriptive structure.  
- Stronger claims would require tasks forcing nuanced communication (e.g., same-category distinctions or multiple distractors).  
- Suggested experiments: intra-category target/distractor pairs to force attribute-based messages and increasing the number of distractors to raise precision demands.  
- Suggested analyses: allow multi-symbol messages to test compositionality and assess symbol consistency across contexts to probe systematicity.  
- Grounding experiment is promising; questions about design choice to supervise only the sender and whether supervising the receiver was considered.  
- Recommend quantifying metonymic links (e.g., semantic similarity via WordNet or embeddings) observed in grounding results.  
- Clarification requests: Were same-category (McRae) experiments run, and how did success and symbol usage change?  
- Request for elaboration on which functional aspects of communication (beyond discrimination) the authors believe the current setup captures.
