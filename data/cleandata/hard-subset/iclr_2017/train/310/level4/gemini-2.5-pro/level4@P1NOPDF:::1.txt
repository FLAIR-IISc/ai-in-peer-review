This paper presents an interesting study on emergent communication between two agents. As reinforcement learning finds broader application, studies like this that investigate the fundamental nature of machine intelligence and its potential parallels with human cognition are particularly valuable.

**Pros**

*   The paper addresses the important and timely problem of understanding how communication protocols can emerge between artificial agents. It revisits the fundamental question of whether the problem-solving strategies developed by machines are analogous to those used by humans.
*   The authors have designed a task that is simple enough to permit an intuitive and straightforward analysis of the resulting communication protocols learned by the agents.
*   One of the more interesting findings is the difference in the complexity of the learned communication: informed agents learned to utilize multiple symbols to convey information, whereas agnostic agents converged on a minimal, two-symbol system.

**Cons**

*   My main criticism is that the experimental task can be simplified to a standard image classification problem, particularly in the case where the two images presented to the agent are from different categories.
*   In this setting, the communicated symbols effectively serve as class labels, and the second agent’s task is merely to learn the correct association between a symbol and an image.
*   This framing suggests that the problem is analogous to a transfer learning setup, where one agent generates a feature representation (the symbol) that is then used by a second agent. It seems likely that a supervised transfer learning approach could solve this task far more efficiently than the reinforcement learning method presented.
