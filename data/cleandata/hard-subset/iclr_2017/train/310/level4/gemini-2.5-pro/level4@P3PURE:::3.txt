This paper proposes a referential game between two agents, a "sender" and a "receiver." Both agents observe two images. The sender is also given a binary target variable, `t`, and must send a single symbol (a message) to the receiver. The receiver's goal is to use this message to infer the target `t`. Both agents receive a shared reward if the receiver correctly predicts the target. The agents are parameterized as neural networks, which take pretrained image feature vectors as input, and are trained end-to-end using the REINFORCE algorithm.

The authors show that in this setting, the agents converge to optimal policies. Furthermore, their analysis indicates that the learned communication protocol—the symbolic code transmitted from sender to receiver—encodes meaningful visual concepts. The paper also presents experiments on a variant of the game where the referential task is grounded in distinct image classes, which appears to encourage the emergence of even more semantically meaningful concepts. Finally, a multi-game setup is proposed where the sender alternates between playing the referential game and performing a supervised image classification task. As might be expected, when the representations are anchored by the supervised task, the learned communication protocol becomes more interpretable and aligned with human-understandable concepts.

The problem of learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setup is an important and interesting research direction. This task is considerably more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which justifies the paper's focus on a relatively simple, well-defined problem. To the best of my knowledge, the proposed approach of first allowing agents to develop a communication protocol from scratch and then grounding this emergent language in human-understandable concepts is novel. As the authors remark, this presents an alternative paradigm to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which often focus on capturing the statistical properties of language rather than its functional, communicative aspects. The paper's contributions—the proposed task and framework, along with the analysis and visualization of the emergent communication protocol—serve as a valuable stepping stone for future work in this area. For these reasons, I recommend that the paper be accepted.
