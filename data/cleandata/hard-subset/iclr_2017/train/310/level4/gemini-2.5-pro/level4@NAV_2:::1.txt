This paper presents a compelling framework for studying emergent language by situating two neural network agents, a sender and a receiver, in a cooperative referential game. The authors demonstrate that these agents, starting as blank slates, can successfully develop a communication protocol to identify a target image from a pair. The work further explores how architectural biases (informed vs. agnostic senders) and modifications to the game's common knowledge affect the semantic properties of the emergent language. A key contribution is a method for grounding this agent-developed code in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image labeling task, showing that the resulting communication is partially interpretable by humans, even on novel objects.

I recommend **Reject**. My decision is based on two primary concerns:
1.  The experimental setup often simplifies the communication task into a discriminative labeling or classification problem, rather than a true referential challenge.
2.  Consequently, the framework may not be learning the functional, interactive aspects of communication as claimed, but rather a less efficient form of transfer learning for image categorization.

The core task requires the sender to produce a symbol that allows the receiver to identify a target image from a distractor. Since the target and distractor images are created by sampling two random concepts from the 463 base-level concepts (e.g., cat, apple), it is highly probable that in any given game, the two images are from distinct semantic categories. In this scenario, the sender does not need to develop a compositional or descriptive language to distinguish subtle features. Instead, it only needs to learn a unique symbol for the target's category (e.g., a symbol for "cat"), which the receiver then learns to associate with the corresponding image. This reduces the rich problem of referential communication to a much simpler classification task where symbols effectively become class labels.

This concern is amplified by the final experiment, which explicitly introduces a supervised classification objective to ground the language. While the results on human interpretability are interesting, this design choice suggests that the core problem can be effectively modeled as one of transfer learning. An agent could be pre-trained on classification and then use its learned representations or labels to "communicate." The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate what is gained by the more complex and sample-inefficient reinforcement learning setup over a more direct classification or transfer learning approach, thus weakening the central claim about learning the *interactive* and *functional* aspects of language.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper for a future submission. They are not part of the core assessment for the decision.

*   The research direction is fundamentally important. As AI systems become more autonomous and interactive, understanding how they can develop communication from first principles is a crucial area of inquiry.
*   The use of a simple, analyzable task is a strength. The analysis of the "informed" sender using a wider vocabulary compared to the "agnostic" sender's two-symbol strategy is an insightful result that highlights the impact of inductive biases.
*   To better support the claims about emergent communication, the experimental setup could be made more challenging. For instance, experiments where the target and distractor are always drawn from the same semantic super-category (e.g., two different types of dogs, or two different types of vehicles) would force the agents to develop a more nuanced communication protocol that cannot be reduced to simple category labeling.

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
1.  Could you provide statistics on the semantic similarity of the target/distractor pairs used during training? Specifically, how frequently are both images from the same McRae et al. (2005) super-category? Does communication accuracy degrade significantly in these harder, within-category trials?
2.  For the agnostic sender that develops a two-symbol language, you offer the intriguing qualitative interpretation of a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. Could you elaborate on the analysis that led to this conclusion? Is it possible the agents learned a simpler, non-semantic strategy (e.g., one symbol for "image on the left" and another for "image on the right") that would also achieve high accuracy?
3.  In the final grounding experiment, how well does the sender communicate about image pairs where *both* the target and distractor belong to categories that were not part of the 100-label supervised training set? This would provide stronger evidence for the generalization of the "metonymic" communication you describe.
