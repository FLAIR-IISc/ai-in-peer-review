- Paper summary: proposes a sender–receiver referential game where neural agents develop a communication protocol; studies architectural biases and common-knowledge manipulations; grounds emergent code via supervised image labeling producing partially human-interpretable signals.

- Recommendation: Reject.

- Main concern 1: The task is often a discriminative labeling/classification problem rather than a true referential challenge.

- Main concern 2: The framework may be learning transfer of image categorization features rather than the interactive, functional aspects of communication claimed.

- Sampling critique: Because target/distractor pairs are usually from distinct base-level categories, a single category symbol suffices, obviating compositional or descriptive language.

- Grounding critique: Adding a supervised classification objective suggests the result could be achieved via pretraining/transfer learning, weakening the need for complex RL-based interactive learning.

- Methodological gap: The paper does not show what is gained by the reinforcement-learning interactive setup over direct classification or transfer-learning baselines.

- Positive: The research direction (emergent communication from first principles) is important for autonomous, interactive AI.

- Positive: The simple, analyzable task is a strength; the difference between informed and agnostic senders (wider vocabulary vs. two-symbol strategy) is an insightful result about inductive biases.

- Recommended improvement: Make the task harder (e.g., always sample target and distractor from the same semantic super-category) to force nuanced, non-class-label communication.

- Question 1: Provide statistics on semantic similarity of training pairs and how often pairs share the same McRae et al. (2005) super-category; report accuracy on within-category trials.

- Question 2: Elaborate the analysis supporting the agnostic sender’s “living vs. non-living” split and rule out trivial non-semantic strategies (e.g., left/right mapping).

- Question 3: In the grounding experiment, how well does the sender communicate when both target and distractor belong to categories outside the 100-label supervised set (tests generalization/metonymy)?
