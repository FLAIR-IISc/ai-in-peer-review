- Framework: agents (sender/receiver) learn a communication protocol from scratch in an interactive referential game to identify a target image from a pair.  
- Contribution: shows simple feed-forward networks can develop a symbolic language tabula rasa.  
- Contribution: removing low-level visual features from common knowledge pushes emergent language toward abstract, categorical representations aligned with human semantics.  
- Contribution: grounding via interleaving supervised image labeling with the game yields human-understandable words and flexible generalization beyond supervised vocabulary.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Strength: compelling, goal-driven referential game framework demonstrating value of interactive learning for functional communication.  
- Strength: novel and promising grounding technique combining self-play and supervised learning with evidence of human interpretability and metonymic generalization.  
- Experiments: careful analyses (vocabulary usage, SVD, cluster purity) substantiate claims; informed sender architecture yields faster learning and richer codes.  
- Environmental manipulation: forcing class-level coordination shows how task constraints shape semantic abstraction in emergent language.  
- Grounding results: supervised grounding raises semantic purity (~70%) and humans correctly identified targets ~68%, often via non-literal cues.  
- Positioning: paper connects to cognitive science, game theory, and deep RL, though reviewer not expert on closest related work.  
- Feedback: clarify agent architectures and expand Figure 1 caption to explain components, connections, and tensor dimensionalities.  
- Feedback: more explicitly contrast this setup and grounding method with recent emergent-communication work (e.g., Foerster et al., Sukhbaatar et al.).  
- Question: have you tested different temperature (τ) values or annealing schedules and their effects on convergence and symbol diversity?  
- Question: please clarify overlap and domain shift between the 100-label ImageNet-derived supervised set and the ReferItGame dataset used for the referential game.  
- Question: how might the fixed vocabulary approach be extended to an open-ended vocabulary where agents invent new symbols?
