### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a compelling framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication. The authors propose a "referential game" where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop their own communication protocol from scratch to successfully identify a target image from a pair. The work makes several key contributions:
1.  It demonstrates that simple neural network agents can successfully learn to coordinate and establish a communication system in this interactive setting, starting *tabula rasa*.
2.  It investigates how environmental modifications—specifically, altering what constitutes "common knowledge" in the game—can influence the emergent language to better align with high-level, human-intuitive semantic concepts rather than low-level visual features.
3.  Crucially, it introduces a practical method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language. By combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task, the authors show that the agents' communication can become partially interpretable by humans, even when referring to objects not seen during the supervised phase.

The approach of using interactive games to teach the functional aspects of communication is well-motivated and presents a promising alternative to purely passive learning from static text corpora.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear and valuable contribution. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Framework:** The paper effectively argues for the limitations of supervised-only approaches for training interactive agents and proposes a well-designed, game-based framework to study the emergence of functional communication. The grounding in cognitive science and game theory provides a solid conceptual foundation.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Support:** The experiments systematically support the paper's claims. The results not only show that communication emerges, but also provide quantitative and qualitative evidence that the emergent protocol has semantic structure. The final experiment, which grounds the agents' language and tests it with human participants, is particularly convincing and demonstrates a clear path toward building agents that can communicate productively with humans.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The authors correctly identify that communication is not just about statistical pattern recognition but about functional coordination. The referential game setup is an elegant and minimal environment to study this phenomenon. The experiments are well-designed and build on each other logically.

In Section 4, the authors establish that agents can solve the coordination task. The analysis goes beyond simple success rate, using SVD to show that the "informed" sender develops a communication protocol with non-trivial dimensionality, and using the purity metric to demonstrate that the emergent symbols align significantly with human-defined semantic categories.

The experiment in Section 4.1 is a clever demonstration of how to guide the emergent language. By forcing agents to coordinate on class-level properties rather than specific image instances, they successfully increased the semantic purity of the symbol usage. This highlights a key advantage of the game-based approach: the learning environment itself becomes a tool for shaping the desired linguistic behavior.

The most significant contribution is arguably in Section 5. The hybrid approach of mixing interactive self-play with supervised learning, inspired by AlphaGo, is a pragmatic and powerful strategy. The results are striking: not only does communication success remain high, but the protocol becomes directly interpretable. The human evaluation, showing that people could correctly interpret the agent's novel, "metonymic" word usage 68% of the time, provides strong evidence that this method successfully grounds the communication in a way that generalizes beyond the supervised training data. This is a crucial step towards creating AI that can flexibly communicate with humans.

While I am not an expert in the most closely related multi-agent communication literature, the paper appears to position itself well, differentiating its focus on interpretability and grounding from prior work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at improving the paper's clarity and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Clarity of Agent Architectures:** The agent architectures, particularly as depicted in Figure 1, are difficult to fully understand. The diagram is highly schematic, and the accompanying description in Section 3 is dense. I would strongly recommend adding a detailed caption to Figure 1 that walks the reader through the data flow, including tensor dimensions at each step. Clarifying the intuition behind the 1-D convolutions in the "informed" sender (i.e., that they act as dimension-wise comparison operators) in the caption would also be very helpful.
*   **Strengthening the Game Theory Connection:** The introduction mentions connections to game theory, such as cheap talk games and Nash equilibria. The paper could be strengthened by briefly revisiting this in the discussion. For instance, could the authors speculate on which type of equilibrium (e.g., a pooling or separating equilibrium) their agents are converging to? A slightly deeper engagement with this theoretical framework could broaden the paper's appeal to that community.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Answering these questions would help clarify some details and strengthen my confidence in the assessment.

1.  The introduction mentions that this work differs from Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) by focusing on making the emergent language interpretable. Could you please elaborate further on the key methodological differences that enable this focus, beyond the hybrid training introduced in Section 5?
2.  In the hybrid training experiment (Section 5), the sender switches between game-playing and supervised classification with equal probability. How sensitive is the model's performance—both in terms of communication success and human interpretability—to this ratio? It seems this trade-off would be critical for practical applications of this method.
