- Introduces a referential game where sender and receiver learn a communication protocol from scratch.
- Demonstrates simple neural agents can coordinate and develop communication starting tabula rasa.
- Shows changing "common knowledge" in the environment steers emergent language toward high-level, human-intuitive semantics.
- Presents a hybrid method combining interactive play with supervised image-labeling to ground emergent symbols in natural language.
- Grounding yields partial human interpretability that generalizes to objects not seen during supervised training.
- Argues interactive games teach functional communication better than passive text-only learning.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason 1: Novel, well-motivated game-based framework grounded in cognitive science and game theory.
- Reason 2: Strong empirical support with systematic experiments and convincing quantitative/qualitative analyses.
- Experiments: agents reliably solve the coordination task; SVD shows non-trivial protocol dimensionality; purity metric shows symbol alignment with semantic categories.
- Forcing class-level coordination (Section 4.1) increases semantic purity, illustrating environment-as-tool for shaping language.
- Hybrid training (Section 5) keeps communication success high and makes protocols interpretable; human eval shows 68% correct interpretation of novel usages.
- Paper reasonably differentiates itself from prior multi-agent work by focusing on interpretability and grounding.
- Suggestion: clarify agent architectures and Figure 1 (detailed caption, tensor dimensions, explain 1-D conv intuition).
- Suggestion: deepen engagement with game theory (e.g., speculate on pooling vs. separating equilibria).
- Question: clarify methodological differences from Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) beyond the hybrid training.
- Question: how sensitive are communication success and human interpretability to the sender’s game-play vs. supervised training ratio?
