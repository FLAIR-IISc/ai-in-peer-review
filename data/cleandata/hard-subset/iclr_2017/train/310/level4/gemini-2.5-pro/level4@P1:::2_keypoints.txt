- Presents a framework where sender and receiver agents learn an emergent communication protocol via an interactive referential game.  
- Demonstrates agents can develop a communication system from scratch that successfully solves the task.  
- Shows the emergent language captures meaningful, high-level semantic properties of images.  
- Altering the game environment (e.g., removing low-level visual features) encourages agents to form more abstract, general communication.  
- Introduces a hybrid method combining interactive self-play with supervised image-labeling to ground emergent language in natural language.  
- The hybrid approach produces communication protocols that are significantly more interpretable to humans.  
- Experiments are well-designed and progress logically from basic coordination to semantic analysis and grounding.  
- Analysis using cluster purity and t-SNE supports the claim that language structure reflects semantic categories.  
- The modification in Section 4.1 effectively pushes agents toward class-level, abstract representations.  
- Human evaluation shows people can interpret the agents’ “metonymic” uses, highlighting practical human-facing potential.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept.  
- Figure 1 (architectural diagram) is hard to interpret and should be more detailed or have a clearer, explanatory caption.  
- Authors should clarify the precise processing/combination of 1-D convolution feature maps in the “informed sender” architecture.  
- Reviewer caveat: their expertise in multi-agent communication is limited, so novelty relative to closest work may be incompletely assessed.
