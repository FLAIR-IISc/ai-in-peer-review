This paper presents a referential game between a sender and a receiver agent. Both agents observe a pair of images, but only the sender receives a binary target variable, `t`. The sender's objective is to transmit a symbol (a message) that enables the receiver to correctly identify this target. Successful prediction by the receiver results in a shared reward for both agents. The paper implements the agents as neural networks that process pre-trained image feature vectors and are trained via the REINFORCE algorithm.

The core findings demonstrate that the agents successfully converge to optimal policies. Critically, the symbolic communication protocol that emerges is shown to encode meaningful concepts. The authors further investigate this by grounding the game in different image classes, a setting where the learned concepts become even more distinct. A final experiment introduces a multi-game setup, in which the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task. As expected, anchoring the agent's representations to this supervised task results in a communication protocol with even greater semantic meaning.

The problem of learning shared representations for multi-agent communication is a compelling and important research avenue. The complexity of this task, which significantly exceeds that of standard supervised or single-agent reinforcement learning, justifies the paper's focus on a relatively simple initial setup. The approach of first learning an emergent communication protocol and subsequently grounding it in human-interpretable concepts appears to be a novel contribution. As the authors suggest, this paradigm offers a compelling alternative to conventional sequence-to-sequence models, which often prioritize statistical properties over the functional aspects of language. The paper's contributions—the task formulation, the framework itself, and the insightful analysis of the learned communication—provide a solid foundation for future work. For these reasons, I support the paper's acceptance.
