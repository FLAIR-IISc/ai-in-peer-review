### Summary

This paper presents a framework for studying the emergence of language through multi-agent communication, departing from the standard paradigm of passive learning from large text corpora. The authors propose a "referential game" where a "sender" agent must communicate the identity of a target image to a "receiver" agent from a pair of images, using a vocabulary of arbitrary symbols. Both agents, implemented as simple neural networks, start as blank slates and are trained via reinforcement learning based on successful communication.

The core contributions are threefold:
1.  The paper demonstrates that agents can successfully learn to coordinate and develop a communication protocol from scratch in this interactive setting.
2.  It investigates how environmental changes—specifically, forcing agents to refer to object classes rather than specific image instances—can nudge the emergent language to be more abstract and semantically structured, as measured by clustering purity against human-defined categories.
3.  The authors propose and validate a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. This hybrid approach results in a communication protocol that is significantly more interpretable to humans, even when referring to objects not seen during supervised training.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This paper is a valuable contribution that tackles the fundamental problem of how communication can emerge functionally rather than being learned passively. The experimental setup is simple, elegant, and allows for clear analysis, representing a promising step towards building more interactive and grounded language agents.

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1.  **Strong Motivation and Novel Framework:** The paper compellingly argues for the need to move beyond supervised learning on static datasets to study the functional and interactive aspects of language. The referential game provides a clean and effective testbed for this research program, and the results convincingly show that agents can develop a shared communication protocol.
2.  **Insightful Analysis and Grounding Strategy:** The analysis of the emergent language, using metrics like symbol purity and visualizations, provides solid evidence for the authors' claims. More importantly, the strategy of combining interactive self-play with supervised learning to ground the agents' language in human-understandable terms is both novel and effective, as demonstrated by the impressive results of the human evaluation experiment.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear formulation of a foundational problem and the rigorous set of experiments designed to explore it. The authors successfully establish that even simple feed-forward networks can converge to a successful communication strategy using only a shared reward signal. The distinction between the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures provides an interesting insight into how inductive biases can affect the nature of the emergent language, with the informed sender developing a richer vocabulary.

The experiments in Section 4.1, where agents are shown different instances of the same object class, are particularly clever. By removing low-level visual features from the agents' "common knowledge," the authors force the communication protocol to become more abstract and align better with high-level semantic categories. This demonstrates a clear path for controlling the properties of the emergent language by carefully designing the environment.

The final set of experiments on grounding the agents' language is the paper's most significant contribution. The idea of jointly training on an interactive game and a supervised task is inspired by recent successes like AlphaGo but is applied here to the novel problem of making an emergent agent-to-agent protocol understandable to humans. The results are compelling: not only does the sender adopt conventional labels for familiar objects, but it also learns to use them metaphorically or metonymically for novel objects (e.g., using "dolphin" to refer to the sea). The fact that human subjects could interpret these novel usages with high accuracy (68%) strongly supports the claim that this method provides a robust form of grounding that generalizes beyond the supervised data.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the acceptance decision. The work is very promising, and these suggestions are aimed at strengthening its claims and exploring future directions.

1.  **Communication vs. Distributed Classification:** A central question is whether the agents are learning to "communicate" in a rich sense or if they are solving a distributed classification task. In the current setup, where target and distractor are typically from different semantic categories (e.g., a "car" vs. a "dog"), the optimal strategy is simply for the sender to output a symbol corresponding to the target's class, and for the receiver to map that symbol to the correct image. The communication channel effectively becomes a one-hot vector of emergent class labels. This is interesting, but it may not require a "language" with compositional or descriptive properties.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would strengthen the paper to include experiments where discrimination is more challenging. What happens if the sender and receiver must distinguish between two objects from the *same* class (e.g., a "red car" and a "blue car")? This would force the agents to develop symbols for attributes (e.g., "red," "blue") rather than just object types, representing a step towards a more compositional language.

2.  **Analysis of the Agnostic Sender's Language:** The paper notes that the agnostic sender converges to a 2-symbol vocabulary that roughly corresponds to a "living-vs-non-living" distinction. This is a fascinating result that mirrors fundamental distinctions in human cognition. It would be beneficial to include a more detailed analysis of this. For instance, could you provide the purity score specifically for this 2-cluster solution or show examples of how different objects are categorized?

3.  **Experimental Details for Reproducibility:**
    *   Could you clarify if the results reported are averaged over multiple training runs with different random seeds? Given the potential for high variance in reinforcement learning with the REINFORCE algorithm, reporting means and standard deviations would increase confidence in the stability of the results.
    *   The temperature parameter for the Gibbs distributions was set to 10 without tuning. This seems quite high, which would make the policy nearly uniform at the beginning of training. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the learning process to this parameter? Did you observe any training instability?

Overall, this is an excellent and thought-provoking paper that introduces a valuable research direction and provides a strong foundation for future work in emergent communication.
