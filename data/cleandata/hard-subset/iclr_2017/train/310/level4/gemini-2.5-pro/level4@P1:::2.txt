### **Summary**

This paper presents a compelling framework for studying emergent language by training two neural agents—a sender and a receiver—to communicate in an interactive, cooperative referential game. The core contribution is to demonstrate that agents can successfully develop a communication protocol from scratch to solve this task. The authors go further by investigating the semantic properties of the emergent language, showing that it captures meaningful conceptual properties of the images being described. They also demonstrate that by altering the game's environment (e.g., by removing low-level visual features from common knowledge), the agents can be encouraged to develop a more abstract and general communication scheme. Finally, and perhaps most promisingly, the paper introduces a method for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task. This hybrid approach is shown to produce a communication protocol that is significantly more interpretable to humans.

### **Decision**

I recommend **Accepting** this paper.

1.  The work introduces a well-motivated and flexible framework for exploring emergent communication, tackling the important problem of how agents can learn the functional, interactive aspects of language, moving beyond passive learning from static datasets.
2.  The experiments are thoughtfully designed and provide convincing evidence for the paper's claims. The progression from demonstrating basic communication to analyzing its semantic structure and finally grounding it in human language is logical and impactful.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its clear and effective demonstration of a research program for language learning based on multi-agent communication. The referential game setup is simple yet powerful enough to elicit complex communicative behaviors. The results in Section 4 convincingly show that not only can agents learn to coordinate, but the structure of their emergent language reflects high-level semantic categories, which is a non-trivial outcome. The analysis using cluster purity and t-SNE visualizations provides solid support for these claims.

The experiments in Sections 4.1 and 5 are particularly strong. Modifying the game to encourage coordination based on class-level properties (Section 4.1) is a clever way to push the agents toward more abstract representations. The hybrid approach in Section 5, which combines interactive self-play with supervised learning, is a key contribution. It offers a practical and scalable path toward developing agents that can communicate effectively with humans, sidestepping the need for constant human intervention. The final human evaluation, showing that people could interpret the agent's "metonymic" use of words, is a fascinating result that highlights the potential of this approach for creating more flexible and human-like communicative AI.

### **Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

The architectural diagram in Figure 1 is difficult to interpret. While it has likely been simplified for clarity, the omission of connections—combined with the detailed and novel architecture described in Section 3—makes it challenging to map the text to the visual representation. This is particularly true for the "informed sender" model. I would strongly recommend either making the diagram more detailed to accurately reflect all connections or, at a minimum, adding a comprehensive caption that explicitly walks the reader through the components and data flow for both sender architectures. This would significantly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the proposed models.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Could the authors please clarify the precise connections in the "informed sender" architecture? Specifically, after the 1-D convolutions are applied to the image embeddings, how are the resulting feature maps combined and processed before producing the final scores for the vocabulary symbols? A more detailed explanation or a clearer diagram would help in fully understanding the inductive biases of this model.

**A final note on my assessment:** While I am confident in my understanding of the paper's methodology and contributions, my expertise is not specialized in the multi-agent communication literature. Therefore, my evaluation of the work's novelty relative to its closest neighbors may be incomplete.
