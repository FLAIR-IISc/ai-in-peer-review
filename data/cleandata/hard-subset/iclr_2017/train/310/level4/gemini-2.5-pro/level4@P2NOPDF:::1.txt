This paper addresses the fundamental and important problem of understanding emergent communication between artificial agents. As reinforcement learning finds broader application, this work revisits a foundational question: do machines develop problem-solving and communication strategies analogous to those of humans? The investigation into this area is both timely and valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper's primary contribution is its focus on the emergence of communication protocols in a cooperative, multi-agent setting. It tackles a crucial question regarding the nature of learned representations and their use in information transfer.
*   The experimental task is well-designed in its simplicity, which is a significant strength. This allows for a clear and intuitive post-hoc analysis of the learned communication strategies, making the results easy to interpret and understand.
*   A particularly compelling finding is the divergence in communication strategies between "informed" and "agnostic" agents. The observation that informed agents developed a protocol using multiple symbols to convey more nuanced information, whereas agnostic agents converged on a minimal two-symbol system, provides a concrete and interesting insight into how prior knowledge shapes the evolution of a communication language.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The main weakness of the current setup is that the proposed task can be reframed as a simpler machine learning problem, which may limit the generalizability of the conclusions about emergent communication. Specifically, in the case where the two images presented to the first agent are from different categories, the task reduces to a supervised image classification problem.
*   In this scenario, the communication symbols essentially function as labels for the image categories. The first agent's role is to classify the target image, and the second agent's task is to learn a mapping from this symbolic label back to the corresponding image. This structure is highly analogous to a standard transfer learning pipeline, where one model generates features (or, in this case, a discrete label) that a second model uses for a downstream task.
*   This raises the question of whether reinforcement learning is the most efficient framework for this specific problem. A supervised learning approach could likely solve the same classification-and-matching task with significantly greater sample efficiency. While the RL framework is more general, its application here seems to obscure the fact that the underlying problem has a more direct, and potentially simpler, solution. The authors should discuss why an RL approach is necessary and what it reveals that a more direct supervised or transfer learning formulation would not.
