Training natural language systems by situating multiple agents in an interactive referential communication game is a compelling idea. As the authors note, while there is some prior work on using multi-agent games to learn communication, this area remains relatively underexplored and is a direction worth pursuing. Furthermore, the proposed approach of alternating between these games and supervised learning, as demonstrated in the experiments (Section 5) and suggested for future work (Section 6), seems particularly promising.

Regarding clarity, I believe some network connections in Figure 1 may have been omitted for simplification. However, given the highly customized architecture and the somewhat difficult-to-follow description in Section 3, this simplified diagram unfortunately adds to the confusion rather than alleviating it. The diagram could be improved, but at a minimum, a detailed caption is essential to help readers interpret the figure correctly.

Overall, the proposed framework (Section 2) is well-conceived and appears both effective and broadly applicable. The results are solid, and the paper opens up several promising avenues for future variations on this work.

Caveat: While I am confident in my understanding of the paper's contributions, I am not sufficiently familiar with the most closely related literature to definitively assess the work's novelty and positioning within the field.
