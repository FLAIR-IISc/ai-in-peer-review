This paper proposes a referential game between two agents, a "sender" and a "receiver," to study the emergence of communication. In the game, both agents observe two images. The sender is also given a binary target variable, *t*, indicating which of the two images is the target. The sender's task is to transmit a symbolic message to the receiver, enabling the receiver to identify the correct image. Both agents receive a shared reward if the receiver's prediction is successful. The agents are parameterized as neural networks, which use pre-trained image feature vectors as input, and are trained end-to-end using REINFORCE.

The authors show that in this setting, the agents converge to optimal policies and that their learned communication protocol corresponds to meaningful visual concepts. The paper also presents experiments on a variant of the game where the two images are drawn from different classes. In this setting, the agents appear to learn even more clearly interpretable concepts. Finally, a multi-game setup is proposed in which the sender alternates between playing the referential game and performing a standard supervised image classification task. As might be expected, when the sender's representations are anchored by this secondary task, the emergent communication corresponds to even more clearly defined visual concepts.

Learning to communicate shared representations in a multi-agent setting is an interesting and important research direction. This is a considerably more challenging problem than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which justifies the paper's focus on a relatively simple task. To the best of my knowledge, the approach of first learning a communication protocol between agents and then grounding this emergent language in human-interpretable concepts is novel. As the authors remark, this may offer an alternative paradigm to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to focus on the statistical properties of language rather than its functional and grounded aspects. I believe the paper's contributions—the novel framework, the detailed analysis, and the insightful visualizations of the emergent communication—provide a valuable stepping stone for future research in this area. For these reasons, I recommend that the paper be accepted.
