### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for studying language emergence through multi-agent communication. The authors introduce a referential game where two agents, a sender and a receiver, must develop a communication protocol to successfully identify a target image from a pair. The agents are modeled as neural networks and trained via reinforcement learning, using only the game's success signal as a reward. The key contributions are:

1.  A demonstration that tabula rasa agents can successfully converge to an optimal policy in this game, effectively creating their own symbolic language.
2.  An analysis showing that the emergent communication protocol captures meaningful semantic concepts from the images, rather than relying solely on low-level visual features.
3.  An exploration of how modifying the game's environment—specifically, by removing low-level image details and forcing coordination at the object-class level—can steer the emergent language towards more abstract and generalizable concepts.
4.  A simple yet effective strategy for grounding the agents' emergent language in natural language by combining the interactive game with a supervised image classification task. This grounded language is shown to be partially interpretable by humans, even when used to describe novel objects.

### Recommendation and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and novel approach for learning communication protocols from scratch. It provides a compelling alternative to standard supervised learning paradigms by focusing on the functional, goal-driven aspects of language.
2.  The experiments are thoughtfully designed and provide strong empirical support for the claims. The analysis of the emergent language is insightful and the proposed method for grounding the communication in natural language is a valuable contribution.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The primary strength of this work lies in its framing of language learning as a problem of cooperative, multi-agent coordination. This is a much harder and arguably more realistic setting than standard supervised tasks like image captioning, which learn statistical correlations from static datasets. The authors correctly argue that such supervised methods often miss the interactive and functional nature of language. By successfully showing that agents can develop a communication system from first principles in a referential game, this paper provides a valuable proof of concept for this alternative paradigm.

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors not only show that the agents learn to succeed at the game but also conduct a series of insightful analyses to understand the nature of the emergent language. The use of purity scores to measure semantic clustering and SVD to analyze symbol usage provides quantitative evidence that the learned protocol is non-trivial and semantically structured. The subsequent experiments, which demonstrate that environmental constraints (object-level reference) and a supervised grounding signal can shape the emergent language to be more abstract and human-interpretable, are particularly compelling. The final human evaluation experiment provides strong evidence that the grounding technique generalizes beyond the supervised training data, a crucial step towards creating agents that can communicate effectively with people.

To my knowledge, the approach of first allowing a communication protocol to emerge between agents and then grounding it in natural language is novel. This work serves as a useful stepping stone for future research into more complex forms of emergent communication and human-AI interaction.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Clarity on Sender Architecture:** The paper states that the sender receives input `θS(iL, iR, t)`. However, the text also mentions that the target and distractor representations are fed in a fixed order, "so that they are implicitly informed of which image is the target." It would be beneficial to explicitly clarify whether the target variable `t` is used as a distinct input or if the ordering of image vectors is the sole mechanism for conveying this information.
*   **Agnostic vs. Informed Sender:** The "informed" sender architecture consistently outperforms the "agnostic" one, developing a richer vocabulary. The paper attributes this to an inductive bias towards dimension-by-dimension comparison. A slightly more detailed discussion of why this particular bias is so effective in this task could further strengthen the architectural contribution of the paper.
*   **Minor Typos:** A final proofread would be helpful to catch minor typos, such as "assess" misspelled as "assest" in the introduction and "usual" as "usufal" in Section 5.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you please clarify the formatting and values in the results tables? In Table 1, percentages appear to be in the range [0, 100], while in Table 2 they seem to be in [0, 1]. Furthermore, the "obs-chance purity" values in Table 1 are extremely small. Is this a typo, or is the difference between observed and chance purity indeed this small?
*   How sensitive is the learning process to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the temperature `τ` of the Gibbs distributions? This parameter controls the exploration-exploitation trade-off in the agents' policies, and understanding its impact could provide further insight into the dynamics of the learning process.
