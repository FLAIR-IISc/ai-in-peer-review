This paper proposes an interesting and valuable framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication in the context of referential games. The core idea is that agents can develop their own communication protocol interactively, driven by the need to coordinate to solve a task. The authors show that simple neural network agents can successfully learn to communicate in this setting. More significantly, they investigate methods to shape the emergent language to better align with human semantics, culminating in a particularly fruitful experiment that combines the interactive game with a supervised image-labeling task to ground the agents' communication in natural language.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are that it presents a compelling and well-motivated framework for studying emergent communication, and it provides strong empirical support for a novel hybrid approach that grounds this emergent language in a way that is interpretable to humans.

The paper’s motivation for moving beyond passive, supervised learning towards interactive, function-driven communication is very convincing. The proposed referential game framework is an elegant and effective testbed for these ideas. The experiments are well-designed and the results clearly demonstrate that agents can converge on a successful communication strategy from a blank slate. The analyses exploring the semantic properties of the emergent language, using metrics like cluster purity and visualizations, are thorough and insightful. The key contribution lies in Section 5, where the authors introduce a method for grounding the agents' code in natural language by interleaving game-playing with a standard supervised classification task. This demonstration that the resulting communication protocol is not only successful but also partially understandable by humans—even on novel concepts—is a significant and promising step towards creating agents that can productively communicate with people.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the manuscript and are not central to my decision.
*   The architectural diagrams in Figure 1 are difficult to fully understand. It appears some connections may have been omitted for the sake of clarity, but given the customized nature of the architectures, this abstraction makes it challenging to follow the precise data flow described in Section 3. The paper would be significantly clearer if the diagrams were revised or, at a minimum, augmented with a detailed caption explaining each component and connection for both the agnostic and informed sender models.

I have one question for the authors that would help clarify my understanding of the agent architectures:
*   Could you please elaborate on the inductive bias of the "informed" sender? The text states it has a bias for combining image representations dimension-by-dimension via 1-D convolutions. How does this practically differ from the "agnostic" sender, which could theoretically learn a similar function with its fully-connected layers? A more detailed explanation of this architectural choice would strengthen the paper.
