This paper presents a compelling approach for training natural language systems through a multi-agent, interactive referential communication game. The authors correctly position their work in the context of prior research on multi-agent learning, and their proposed direction is a worthwhile and promising one. A particularly strong aspect of the methodology is the integration of the communication game with standard supervised learning, as demonstrated in the experiments (Section 5) and suggested as a future direction (Section 6). This hybrid approach appears to be a fruitful avenue for research.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its presentation, specifically regarding the model architecture in Figure 1. While the diagram seems to omit certain network connections, likely for the sake of simplicity, this abstraction hinders comprehension rather than aiding it. Given the highly customized architecture and the somewhat complex description in Section 3, the simplified figure makes it difficult for the reader to fully grasp the model's design. To address this, the figure should be revised for clarity. At a minimum, a comprehensive caption must be added to explain each component, the flow of information, and the logic behind the diagram's design.

Overall, the paper introduces a valuable and effective framework with broad applicability. The results are solid and support the viability of the proposed method. This work provides a strong foundation for future research, and I expect it will inspire many interesting variations.

One caveat to my review is that my expertise does not extend to the full breadth of literature on multi-agent communication. Therefore, while I am confident in my understanding of the paper's proposed method and its empirical results, my assessment of its novelty relative to the most closely related prior work may be incomplete.
