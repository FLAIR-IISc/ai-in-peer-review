- Proposes a valuable multi-agent referential-game framework where agents develop communication protocols interactively to coordinate on tasks.
- Demonstrates that simple neural-network agents can learn to communicate from scratch in this setting.
- Introduces a hybrid approach that grounds emergent agent communication in human semantics by interleaving game play with supervised image-labeling.
- Shows strong empirical support that the grounded protocol is successful and partially interpretable by humans, even on novel concepts.
- Motivation to move beyond passive supervised learning toward interactive, function-driven communication is convincing and well-argued.
- Experiments are well-designed and analyses (cluster purity, visualizations) provide thorough insight into the semantic properties of the emergent language.
- Section 5 is the key contribution, presenting the grounding method that makes emergent codes more human-interpretable.
- Minor critique: architectural diagrams in Figure 1 are unclear/omissive and should be revised or accompanied by detailed captions to clarify data flow.
- Question for authors: clarify the inductive bias of the "informed" sender and how it practically differs from the "agnostic" sender despite the latter's capacity to learn similar functions.
