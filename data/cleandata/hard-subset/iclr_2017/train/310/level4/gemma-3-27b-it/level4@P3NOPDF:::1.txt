This paper investigates the emergent communication strategies of reinforcement learning agents tasked with a simple information transmission game. The work addresses a fundamental question increasingly relevant as RL applications expand: how do machine problem-solving approaches compare to human communication? This is a crucial problem, and the paper’s exploration is timely and valuable.

The task design is a significant strength. Its simplicity facilitates intuitive post-hoc analysis of the learned communication protocols. The authors demonstrate an interesting divergence in strategy between “informed” and “agnostic” agents. Specifically, informed agents utilize a richer symbol set, employing multiple symbols to convey information, while agnostic agents converge on a solution reliant on only two symbols. This observation offers a compelling starting point for understanding how access to information shapes communication complexity.

However, the core limitation of this work lies in the nature of the task and its resultant trivialization. The communication problem, in effect, reduces to an image classification task when the two presented images originate from different categories. In this scenario, the learned “symbols” essentially map directly to image classes, and the receiving agent learns to classify an image based on the transmitted symbol – effectively assigning it to the category indicated by the sender. Consequently, the entire process resembles a transfer learning problem. A supervised learning approach, pre-trained on image classification, could likely achieve comparable or superior performance with considerably less computational expense than the employed reinforcement learning algorithm. 

This framing as transfer learning diminishes the significance of the emergent communication aspect. While the agents *are* communicating, the content of that communication isn’t complex; it's a direct label transfer. The paper doesn’t adequately address this point or explore whether more complex image relationships or less categorical distinctions would lead to genuinely emergent and more nuanced communication strategies. 

Further clarification on the following would be beneficial:

* **Computational Cost:** A comparison of training time and resource utilization against a similarly performing supervised learning baseline would help contextualize the advantages (if any) of the RL approach.
* **Generalizability:** How well do the learned communication protocols generalize to slightly modified image sets or task variations? Does the reliance on image class labels limit adaptability?
* **Symbol Grounding:** The paper touches on symbol grounding, but a more thorough discussion of how the agents' internal representations relate to the visual input would be valuable.



In summary, while this paper raises an important question and presents an interesting empirical observation regarding the influence of information access on communication strategy, the inherent simplicity of the task ultimately limits the novelty and impact of the findings. The reduction to a transfer learning problem weakens the claim of emergent communication. The paper is a worthwhile starting point for further investigation, but significant revisions addressing the points above are necessary to elevate its contribution.
