To train natural language systems by establishing interactive referential communication games between multiple agents is a compelling approach. As the authors note, prior work leveraging multi-agent games for communication is limited, making this a valuable direction for research. The strategy of alternating between these games and supervised learning, demonstrated in Section 5 and proposed for future work in Section 6, appears particularly promising. 

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper, primarily due to the novelty of the proposed framework and the encouraging initial results demonstrating the emergence of interpretable communication strategies. Additionally, the exploration of environmental factors influencing language development, and the attempt to ground the emergent language, represent significant contributions.

The paper effectively introduces a research program focused on multi-agent coordination and communication games. The framework, detailed in Section 2, is well-defined and provides a clear structure for investigating the development of language. The experimental results (Sections 4 & 5) demonstrate that agents can indeed learn to coordinate and that the resulting communication protocols exhibit semantic properties related to object categories and, with supervised grounding, align with human language. The authors successfully demonstrate learning in a referential game and provide initial steps towards developing interpretable communication signals. The inclusion of both ablations (informed vs. agnostic sender) and modifications to the game environment (object-level reference) strengthens the analysis.

To further improve the paper, I offer the following suggestions. The description of the network architectures (Section 3) could be made more accessible. While the figure (Fig 1) provides a visual overview, it is somewhat difficult to fully understand the connections and operations without a more detailed caption or elaboration in the text. Specifically, clarifying the role of the 1D convolutions in the informed sender would be beneficial. Additionally, while the human evaluation in Section 5 is a strong result, further details on the crowd-sourcing procedure (e.g., participant demographics, instructions given) would enhance the reproducibility and interpretation of the findings.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors. First, could you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters (Section 3)? Were these values selected through experimentation or based on prior work? Second, the paper mentions potential redundancy in the informed sender’s vocabulary (Figure 2). Could you provide more details on the nature of this redundancy and whether it impacts the interpretability of the learned symbols? Finally, in Section 5, you mention “metonymic” links between symbols and image content. Could you provide further examples of these links and discuss the implications for the development of more robust and generalizable communication systems?
