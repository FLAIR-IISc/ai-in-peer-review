This paper introduces a referential game played between two agents. Both agents are presented with two images, and the first agent – the sender – receives a binary target variable and communicates a symbol to the second agent, the receiver, with the goal of enabling accurate target prediction. Both agents receive a reward based on the receiver's prediction accuracy. The authors propose implementing the agents as neural networks, leveraging pre-trained image representations as feature vectors, and training them using the REINFORCE algorithm. Results demonstrate convergence to optimal policies, with the learned communication – the symbolic code exchanged between agents – exhibiting meaningful concepts. The paper further presents experiments on a variant of the game utilizing different image classes, which appears to promote the learning of even more interpretable concepts. Finally, a multi-game setup is explored, alternating the sender agent between the referential game and a supervised image classification task. As expected, grounding the communication in the supervised task yields symbolic representations with enhanced semantic meaning. 

The exploration of learning shared representations for communication within a multi-agent framework is a valuable research direction, particularly given its increased complexity compared to standard supervised or single-agent reinforcement learning. The proposed approach of initially establishing communication between two agents before grounding it in human-interpretable concepts is, to the best of my knowledge, a novel contribution. As highlighted by the authors, this work potentially offers a complementary paradigm to traditional sequence-to-sequence models, emphasizing functional aspects of language over purely statistical properties. I view the contributions of the proposed task, framework, and the subsequent analysis and visualization of the communicated tokens as a significant step forward and a strong foundation for future research. Therefore, I recommend acceptance of this paper.
