- Investigates emergent communication in RL agents for an information transmission game; timely and valuable question.  
- Task design is a strength: simplicity enables intuitive post-hoc analysis of learned protocols.  
- Key empirical finding: informed agents use a richer symbol set, while agnostic agents converge on just two symbols.  
- Suggests that access to information influences communication complexity.  
- Core limitation: the task effectively reduces to image classification when images are from different categories.  
- Learned symbols map directly to image classes, so the receiver is essentially performing classification based on a transmitted label.  
- This framing resembles transfer learning and undermines the novelty of emergent communication.  
- A supervised, pre-trained image classifier could likely match or exceed performance with far less computational cost than RL.  
- The paper fails to explore whether more complex image relationships or less categorical distinctions would produce genuinely nuanced communication.  
- Clarification needed on computational cost: compare RL training time and resources to a supervised baseline.  
- Clarification needed on generalizability: do protocols transfer to modified image sets or task variations?  
- Clarification needed on symbol grounding: deeper analysis of agents’ internal representations and their relation to visual input.  
- Overall: important question and interesting observation, but task simplicity and reduction to transfer learning limit novelty; significant revisions required.
