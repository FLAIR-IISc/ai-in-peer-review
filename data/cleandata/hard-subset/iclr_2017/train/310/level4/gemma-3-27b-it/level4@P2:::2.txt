This paper proposes a novel framework for training natural language systems through multi-agent communication in a referential game. The core idea – enabling agents to develop language interactively through the need to communicate – is compelling and aligns with the challenge of building truly interactive AI. The approach builds upon, and extends, existing work in multi-agent systems and game theory, and the authors demonstrate promising initial results, particularly when combining the game-based learning with supervised learning for grounding. This combination appears particularly fruitful for developing communication that is more readily interpretable by humans.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to resubmit. While the work is promising and the ideas are well-motivated, the paper suffers from presentation issues that hinder clear understanding and assessment of its contribution. Specifically, the architecture descriptions and figure captions are insufficiently detailed, making it difficult to fully grasp the experimental setup and evaluate the significance of the architectural choices.

The paper effectively summarizes the limitations of current passive learning approaches to natural language processing and positions the proposed multi-agent framework as a solution. The motivation, drawing on insights from linguistics and cognitive science, is strong and well-articulated. The experimental setup, based on referential games, seems appropriate for investigating the emergence of communication. The results demonstrate that agents can learn to coordinate and that environmental manipulations can influence the semantic properties of the developed language. The grounding experiment in Section 5, combining game-play with supervised learning, is a particularly valuable contribution, showing a pathway toward human-interpretable communication. However, the clarity of presentation is a significant drawback. The network architectures described in Section 3 and depicted in Figure 1 are difficult to follow without more detailed explanations. The figure lacks sufficient detail (e.g. dimensions of layers) and a clarifying caption. This makes it challenging to assess the novelty and impact of the “informed sender” architecture. Moreover, the paper’s connection to the broader landscape of related work feels somewhat superficial, and a more in-depth discussion of existing approaches (particularly those most closely aligned with this work) would strengthen the paper’s position. The caveat expressed by the reviewer regarding familiarity with closely related literature is also a concern, suggesting a potentially missing contextualization of the work’s significance. 

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

*   **Expand Architectural Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the neural network architectures, including layer sizes, activation functions, and other relevant parameters. Revise Figure 1 to be more informative, adding a comprehensive caption explaining all components.
*   **Clarify Terminology:** Ensure consistent and precise use of terminology throughout the paper.
*   **Strengthen Related Work Discussion:** Provide a more thorough review of related work, specifically focusing on the most closely related approaches to multi-agent communication and language emergence. Discuss how this work differentiates itself and contributes uniquely to the field.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of specific components of the proposed framework, such as the informed sender architecture or the supervised grounding phase.
*   **Error Analysis:** Present a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the agents, providing insights into the limitations of the current approach.
*   **Quantitative Analysis of Grounding:** Present more quantitative metrics related to the human evaluation of the grounded language.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the specific ImageNet classes used in the experiment? Was this choice motivated by any particular characteristics of the classes?
*   What is the computational cost of training the agents in the referential game? How does this scale with vocabulary size and image resolution?
*   How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameters used during training (e.g., learning rate, batch size, temperature)?
*   Regarding the supervised learning phase, how did you select the 100 labels? Did you observe any differences in performance when using a different set of labels?
*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the “informed sender” architecture outperforms the “agnostic sender” architecture? What specific inductive biases does it introduce?
