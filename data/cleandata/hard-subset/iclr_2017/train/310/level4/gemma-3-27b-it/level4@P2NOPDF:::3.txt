This paper introduces a referential game played between two agents designed to explore the emergence of communication. Each agent observes two images, and the first agent – the sender – receives a binary target variable and must transmit a symbol to the second agent – the receiver – enabling the receiver to predict the target. Both agents receive a reward for correct target prediction. The authors propose implementing the agents as neural networks, leveraging pre-trained image representations as feature vectors, and training them using the REINFORCE algorithm. Through this setup, they demonstrate that the agents converge to optimal policies and develop symbolic communication – the transmitted code – that represents meaningful concepts. Further experiments are conducted with a game variant based on different image classes, leading to the emergence of even more interpretable concepts. Finally, a multi-game setup is explored where the sender alternates between playing the referential game and performing a supervised image classification task. As expected, grounding the communication in the supervised task results in even more semantically rich symbolic representations.

The research direction of learning shared representations for communication within a multi-agent framework is compelling. This represents a significant challenge compared to traditional supervised or single-agent reinforcement learning, justifying the choice of a relatively simple initial task. To the best of my knowledge, the approach of first establishing communication between agents and then grounding it in concepts understandable to humans is novel. The authors rightly point out that this offers a potentially valuable alternative to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which often prioritize statistical properties of language over its functional aspects. I believe the contributions of the proposed task and framework, alongside the thorough analysis and visualization of the communicated tokens, represent a valuable contribution and a solid foundation for future research. Therefore, I recommend this paper for acceptance.
