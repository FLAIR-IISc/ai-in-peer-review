This paper tackles a crucial problem in the development of truly intelligent agents: how to learn communication grounded in interaction, rather than solely through passive observation of data. The authors propose a multi-agent communication framework based on referential games, where agents learn to coordinate by developing their own language. This is a compelling approach, particularly in the context of building conversational agents and moving beyond purely supervised learning paradigms. The simplicity of the game setup allows for intuitive analysis of the emergent communication strategies, and the initial results demonstrating successful coordination are promising.

**Reject**

My primary concern is that the emergent communication, while successful, appears strongly tied to implicit image classification, diminishing the novelty of the learned “language”. The observed differences between informed and agnostic senders, while interesting, seem to stem from differing inductive biases rather than fundamentally different communication strategies. Additionally, while the paper presents intriguing initial steps toward grounding the communication in human language, the findings regarding interpretability require further scrutiny and evidence.

The paper effectively frames the problem and motivates the multi-agent approach well, drawing connections to relevant work in linguistics, cognitive science, and game theory. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on emergent communication, contrasting it with pre-programmed systems, human-in-the-loop approaches, and “Wizard-of-Oz” environments. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the choice of the referential game as a starting point is sensible. The authors demonstrate that agents can indeed learn to coordinate and achieve high levels of success in the game. The observation that informed senders utilize a more diverse vocabulary than agnostic senders is interesting, and the analysis of symbol purity suggests some level of semantic organization in the emergent language.

However, the core issue is the inherent link to image classification. When the presented images represent different object categories, the task effectively becomes learning to identify the category of the target image, and the “symbols” simply map to these categories. While the reinforcement learning framework allows the agents to discover this mapping, it’s not clear that this constitutes the emergence of a truly novel language. It appears more akin to transfer learning, where the agents leverage pre-existing knowledge about object categories (implicitly encoded in the image embeddings) to solve a new task. The claim that the agents are developing a language *de novo* seems overstated given this reliance on pre-existing semantic information.

Regarding the grounding experiments, the reported 68% success rate in human interpretability is encouraging, but requires more detailed analysis. What types of images and symbols yielded successful interpretations? What was the nature of the “metonymic” links observed? A more rigorous evaluation, perhaps involving a larger and more diverse set of human participants, would be necessary to confidently assess the interpretability of the emergent language. The reliance on ImageNet labels during training also raises concerns about the extent to which the learned communication is genuinely grounded in semantic understanding versus simply replicating existing label associations. 

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following:

*   **Ablation studies:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the choice of image embeddings. Could the observed behavior be reproduced using random embeddings, or embeddings derived from a different dataset?
*   **Analysis of Symbol Usage:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the symbols used by the agents. Visualize the images associated with each symbol to better understand their meaning.
*   **Comparison to Baseline:** Compare the performance of the proposed approach to a simple baseline that directly maps images to symbols using a supervised learning algorithm. This would help to quantify the added value of the reinforcement learning framework.
*   **Clarify the Notion of “Language”:** Refine the definition of "language" used in the paper. Acknowledge the limitations of the emergent communication and discuss how it differs from natural human language.
*   **Human Interpretability Details:** Provide more detailed information about the human evaluation experiments, including the demographics of the participants, the instructions given, and examples of successful and unsuccessful interpretations.



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  How do the results change when the images shown to the sender and receiver are visually similar (e.g., different instances of the same object)? Would this reduce the reliance on implicit image classification?
2.  Can you provide examples of symbols that are consistently used to refer to images that do *not* belong to the same semantic category? What features of these images might be driving the symbol assignment?
3.  In the grounding experiments, what percentage of the symbols were directly associated with ImageNet labels? How did the performance differ for those symbols compared to the others?
4.  Have you explored alternative reward structures or game dynamics that might encourage the development of more complex and nuanced communication strategies?
5.  How does the performance scale with the vocabulary size? Are there diminishing returns as the vocabulary increases?
