This paper presents an interesting investigation into communication between reinforcement learning agents. The core question it addresses – whether machine problem-solving strategies align with human communication – is increasingly relevant as RL applications become more widespread. This work thoughtfully returns to this fundamental issue.

A significant strength of this paper lies in the simplicity of the designed task, which facilitates intuitive post-learning analysis. The clarity of the environment allows for easier interpretation of the learned communication strategies. 

The observation regarding the differing communication strategies employed by informed versus agnostic agents is particularly insightful. Specifically, the finding that informed agents utilize a richer symbolic repertoire while agnostic agents converge on a limited set of symbols (two in this case) provides a compelling point for discussion and further study. This highlights a potential link between the agent’s understanding of the task and the complexity of its communication.

However, a key concern is the extent to which the communication task reduces to a problem of image classification. When the two images presented to the agents belong to distinct categories, the learned symbols effectively correspond to these image classes, with the receiving agent learning to map them. Consequently, the entire process can be reinterpreted as a transfer learning problem. A classifier could potentially be trained to directly map images to classes far more efficiently than the proposed reinforcement learning framework. This raises questions about the necessity and efficiency of the RL approach in this specific setting.
