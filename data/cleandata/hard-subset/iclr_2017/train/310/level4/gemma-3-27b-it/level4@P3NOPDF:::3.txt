This paper introduces a referential game played between two agents—a sender and a receiver—designed to investigate the emergence of meaningful communication. Both agents observe two images, and the sender, given a binary target variable *t*, must transmit a symbol (message) to the receiver to enable accurate prediction of *t*. Both agents receive a reward based on the receiver’s success in predicting the target. The authors parametrize the agents as neural networks, leveraging pre-trained image representations as feature vectors, and train them using the REINFORCE algorithm. The results demonstrate that the agents converge to optimal policies and, crucially, that the learned communication—the symbolic code exchanged—develops meaningful concepts. Experiments extend this to a variant of the game grounded in specific image classes, revealing even more interpretable concepts. Furthermore, the authors propose a multi-game setup where the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task. As expected, anchoring the communication through the supervised task yields even more semantically coherent symbolic representations.

Learning shared representations for communication within a multi-agent framework represents a compelling and challenging research direction, exceeding the complexity of standard supervised or single-agent reinforcement learning. The choice to begin with a relatively simple task is well-justified. To the best of my knowledge, the approach of initially establishing communication between two agents and subsequently grounding this communication in human-understandable concepts is novel. This work offers a potentially valuable alternative to the dominant paradigm of sequence-to-sequence models, which often prioritize statistical regularities of language over its functional aspects. I believe the contributions of the proposed task and framework, alongside the insightful analysis and visualization of the communicated tokens, constitute a useful foundation for future research in this area. For these reasons, I recommend this paper for acceptance.

However, I have a few minor comments that could improve the clarity and rigor of the presentation:

*   **Target Incorporation:** Please clarify how the target variable (*t*) is incorporated into the sender network’s input. A more explicit description of this process would be beneficial.

*   **Table Consistency:** Tables 1 and 2 present percentage values (%) inconsistently. In Table 1, the percentages appear to range between 0 and 100, while in Table 2, they range between 0 and 1. Please ensure consistency in the reporting of these values. Furthermore, the “obs-chance purity” column in Table 1 contains extremely small values; I suspect this might be an error.

*   **Typographical Errors:** There are a couple of typographical errors that should be corrected: “assest” should be “assess,” and “usufal” should be “usual.”
