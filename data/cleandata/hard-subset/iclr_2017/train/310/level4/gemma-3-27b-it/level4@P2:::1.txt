This paper investigates a crucial problem in AI: how to develop communication between agents, moving beyond purely reactive systems towards more human-like interaction. The authors propose a multi-agent communication framework based on referential games, where agents learn to coordinate by developing their own language. This is a valuable direction for research, particularly as reinforcement learning expands into more complex applications where interaction and communication are essential. The simplicity of the experimental setup – a referential game with images – is a strength, allowing for intuitive analysis of the learned communication strategies. The observation that informed agents utilized a more diverse vocabulary than agnostic agents was particularly insightful.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting approach, the core result feels more like a sophisticated transfer learning problem than a demonstration of emergent communication. The observed learning appears to largely rely on classifying images into pre-defined categories, effectively mapping symbols to existing image classes.

The paper demonstrates that two agents can learn to coordinate in a referential game, and that the resulting “language” exhibits some semantic properties, aligning with object categories. However, the core issue lies in the nature of the task. If the two presented images represent different object categories, the task fundamentally becomes one of image classification. The learned symbols then represent those categories, and the process can be framed as a form of transfer learning where the agents leverage pre-existing knowledge encoded in the image features (derived from a pre-trained CNN) to quickly learn this mapping. This bypasses the challenge of truly *emergent* communication—creating meaning from scratch without pre-defined categories. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by referencing work on supervised learning, but the framing of the work as discovering a language feels misleading given this reliance on pre-existing semantic labels. While the reinforcement learning setup is well-defined and implemented, it feels somewhat unnecessary given the nature of the task. A supervised learning approach directly optimized for classification could likely achieve similar, if not better, results with greater efficiency. The argument that agents "bootstrap knowledge on top of each other" feels less compelling when the initial knowledge is largely provided by the pre-trained CNN. 

Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Clarify the novelty:** The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of how this work goes beyond standard transfer learning. More emphasis on the cases where the two images *belong* to the same category would strengthen the argument for emergent communication.
*   **Ablation studies:** Conduct experiments to isolate the contribution of the reinforcement learning framework. How do results compare to a direct supervised learning approach trained on the same data and with the same architecture?
*   **Analysis of symbol usage:** A more in-depth analysis of the symbols used in the game is warranted. The authors mention redundancy, but a more detailed analysis of the learned embeddings and their relation to the image features would be beneficial.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** The authors should more explicitly discuss the limitations of the current setup, particularly the reliance on pre-defined object categories. 
*   **Explore alternative environments:** Could a more challenging environment, where the distinction between images is more subtle or relies on relationships rather than categories, lead to more truly emergent communication?



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  How do the results change when the two images presented to the agents belong to the same object category? Is performance maintained, and if so, how does the symbol usage differ?
2.  Have you compared the performance of your reinforcement learning approach to a direct supervised classification approach trained on the same data, using a similar network architecture? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a visualization of the learned embeddings for the symbols used by the agents? This would help to understand how the symbols are related to each other and to the image features.
4.  The paper touches on the convergence properties of the game. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate and the temperature parameter?
5.  You mention the possibility of adding humans to the loop. Have you explored this, and if so, what were the challenges and benefits?
