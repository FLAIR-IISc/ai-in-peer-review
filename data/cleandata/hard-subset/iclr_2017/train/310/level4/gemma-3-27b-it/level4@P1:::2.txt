To train natural language systems by placing multiple agents within an interactive referential communication game is a promising approach. As the authors mention, prior work using multi-agent games to teach communication is limited, making this a worthwhile direction to pursue. Moreover, the strategy of switching between these games and supervised learning, demonstrated in Section 5 and discussed in Section 6, appears particularly fruitful.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core idea is compelling, the experimental setup is well-designed, and the results demonstrate the potential of the proposed framework. The paper successfully shows that agents can learn to coordinate and develop interpretable communication protocols, and explores how environmental factors influence the emergence of language-like behavior.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear articulation of the problem – how to design environments that foster the development of a language portable to new situations and communication partners – and its rigorous exploration of this problem through referential games. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that agents can learn to communicate effectively, and that modifying the game environment can encourage the development of more semantically meaningful communication codes. The grounding experiments in Section 5 further showcase the potential for aligning emergent language with human understanding. 

However, several points could enhance the paper. Regarding Figure 1, the network connections appear incomplete, and the diagram, coupled with the detailed description in Section 3, is somewhat confusing. A more comprehensive diagram with a clarifying caption would improve understanding. Additionally, while the paper references related work, it would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how this work builds upon and distinguishes itself from existing approaches, particularly Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016). The authors acknowledge these works, but a more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   In Section 3, you mention hyperparameters were set "without tuning". Could you briefly discuss the sensitivity of your results to these hyperparameters, or any preliminary experiments you conducted to justify their selection?
*   The observation in Section 4 that the informed sender uses a more varied vocabulary is interesting. Could you elaborate on why you believe the informed sender’s architecture encourages this behavior, and whether you observed any trade-offs between vocabulary size and communication efficiency?
*   In Section 5, you note the emergence of “metonymic” links between symbols and image content. Could you provide more examples of these links, and discuss the implications of this phenomenon for grounding emergent language in human understanding?
*   Regarding the supervised learning phase, did you consider different strategies for integrating it with the game-playing environment? For example, would interleaving the two tasks at different frequencies, or using a different supervised objective, have yielded different results?
