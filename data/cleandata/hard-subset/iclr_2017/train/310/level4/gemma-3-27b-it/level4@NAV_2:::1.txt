This paper tackles a crucial problem in machine learning: understanding communication between agents. As reinforcement learning applications expand, this work returns to a fundamental question – whether machine problem-solving mirrors human approaches. The chosen task is sufficiently simple to facilitate intuitive post-learning analysis. It was particularly interesting to observe that informed agents utilized a wider vocabulary, while agnostic agents converged to a limited two-symbol system.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a valuable exploration, the experimental setup, as currently described, appears to reduce the communication task to a form of image classification, particularly when the presented images belong to distinct categories. This significantly limits the novelty of the emergent communication.

The core issue lies in the task’s inherent structure. When presented with images from different classes, the learned symbols effectively become class labels, and the receiver’s task becomes one of assigning images to these pre-defined categories. This transforms the problem into a transfer learning scenario, where the agent learns to classify images based on pre-existing knowledge (ImageNet labels), rather than developing a truly emergent language. The reinforcement learning framework, in this context, may be unnecessarily complex and potentially slower than directly training a classifier. The reported improvements with the informed sender, while interesting, may stem from a more efficient exploration of the existing class-based information. The authors demonstrate successful coordination, but the nature of that coordination needs further scrutiny to ascertain whether it constitutes genuine emergent communication or a sophisticated form of classification.

Further feedback to improve the paper:

*   **Clarify the novelty of emergent communication:** The authors should more explicitly address how this work differentiates itself from existing transfer learning techniques. A more rigorous analysis of the learned symbols – beyond purity metrics related to pre-defined categories – is needed to demonstrate genuine emergent properties.
*   **Explore scenarios with more subtle differences:** Conducting experiments with images from the *same* category but with differing attributes (e.g., two different dog breeds) would provide a stronger test of the agents’ ability to develop a truly emergent and nuanced communication system.
*   **Quantify the complexity of the communication:** Metrics beyond coordination success (e.g., information-theoretic measures of mutual information between sender symbols and image features) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the complexity and efficiency of the emergent communication protocol.
*   **Investigate the impact of vocabulary size:** A more systematic exploration of vocabulary size and its effect on the quality and interpretability of the emergent language would be valuable.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned symbol representations (e.g., visualizations of the embedding space) to demonstrate whether they capture information *beyond* pre-defined image categories?
*   What is the computational cost of the reinforcement learning approach compared to training a supervised classifier directly on the same task?
*   How do the results change when the target and distractor images are chosen from the same object category, presenting more subtle differences?
*   What is the effect of the temperature parameter τ on the diversity of the communication protocol?
