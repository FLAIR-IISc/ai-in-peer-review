- Training NL systems via multi-agent referential games is a promising and worthwhile direction given limited prior work.  
- Alternating between game play and supervised learning (Section 5/6) is a particularly fruitful strategy.  
- Recommendation: accept — core idea is compelling and contribution is strong.  
- Experimental setup is well-designed and results show agents can coordinate and develop interpretable communication protocols.  
- The paper explores how environmental factors affect the emergence of language-like behavior and portability to new partners/situations.  
- Strength: clear problem articulation and rigorous exploration using referential games.  
- Experiments demonstrate that modifying the game environment can encourage more semantically meaningful communication codes.  
- Grounding experiments (Section 5) show potential for aligning emergent language with human understanding.  
- Weakness: Figure 1’s network diagram appears incomplete/confusing and needs a more comprehensive figure and clarifying caption.  
- Weakness: related-work discussion should more deeply compare and distinguish this work from Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016).  
- Question: clarify hyperparameter sensitivity and any preliminary tuning or robustness checks beyond “without tuning.”  
- Question: explain why the informed sender uses a more varied vocabulary, and whether there are trade-offs between vocabulary size and communication efficiency.  
- Question: provide more examples of the observed “metonymic” links and discuss implications for grounding in human understanding.  
- Question: discuss alternative strategies for integrating supervised learning with the game (e.g., different interleaving frequencies or supervised objectives) and their potential effects.
