- Paper studies emergent communication between agents in RL; informed agents used a larger vocabulary while agnostic agents converged to a two-symbol system.
- Recommendation: Reject — experimental setup appears to reduce the task to image classification when images are from distinct categories, limiting novelty of emergent communication.
- Core critique: learned symbols act like class labels, turning the problem into transfer learning from ImageNet labels rather than genuine language emergence.
- Methodological concern: RL may be unnecessarily complex and slower than supervised classifiers for this task; reported gains with informed sender may reflect better use of class information.
- Need clearer differentiation from transfer learning and deeper analysis of whether learned symbols truly exhibit emergent properties beyond class labels.
- Suggest experiments with more subtle within-category differences (e.g., different breeds) to better test emergent, nuanced communication.
- Recommend quantifying communication complexity using additional metrics (e.g., mutual information between symbols and image features) beyond coordination success.
- Advise systematic study of vocabulary size effects on the quality and interpretability of the emergent language.
- Ask for detailed analysis/visualizations of learned symbol representations to show information beyond pre-defined categories.
- Request comparison of computational cost between the RL approach and a supervised classifier trained on the same task.
- Ask how results change when target and distractors are from the same object category (more subtle differences).
- Ask how the temperature parameter τ affects diversity of the communication protocol.
