This paper introduces a novel framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication within a referential game. Two agents, a sender and a receiver, are presented with pairs of images; the sender must transmit a symbol to the receiver to identify a designated target image, receiving a reward for successful identification. The authors parametrize these agents as neural networks trained using reinforcement learning (REINFORCE) and demonstrate that they can learn to coordinate effectively, developing a communicatory code with discernible semantic properties. The work further explores modifications to the game environment to encourage the emergence of more intuitively meaningful “word meanings” and presents a strategy for grounding the agent’s code in natural language via a combined game-playing and supervised learning setup.

I recommend this paper for acceptance. The approach of fostering communication through agent interaction, rather than relying on large, passively-observed datasets, is a compelling and potentially valuable direction for AI research, particularly for building agents capable of genuine interaction. The grounding experiments are particularly encouraging, suggesting a pathway towards communication interpretable by humans.

The core contribution lies in the demonstration that agents can, from scratch, learn to communicate effectively and develop symbol meanings that align with conceptual properties of the images. This is a significant step towards understanding how language might emerge and is distinct from traditional sequence-to-sequence learning approaches that primarily capture statistical relationships. The novel multi-game setup, alternating between referential games and supervised classification, offers a promising technique for grounding the emergent language and increasing its interpretability. The visualizations and analysis presented provide a compelling case for the meaningfulness of the learned communication.

However, several points could be addressed to strengthen the paper:

*   **Target Incorporation:** The method by which the target variable (t) is incorporated into the sender network’s input could be clarified. Specifically, how is this binary signal represented and utilized within the network architecture?
*   **Table Consistency:** There appears to be an inconsistency in the presentation of percentage values in Tables 1 and 2. Table 1 uses percentages in the range [0, 100], while Table 2 uses values in the range [0, 1]. Please standardize this for clarity. Furthermore, the values in the “obs-chance purity” column of Table 1 seem unusually small; please verify their accuracy.
*   **Typos:** Minor typographical errors, such as “assest” (should be “assess”) and “usufal” (should be “usual”), should be corrected.
*   **Ablation Study:** While the combined game-playing and supervised learning approach is promising, an ablation study investigating the contribution of each component (game-playing alone vs. supervised learning alone) would provide further insights into their individual effects and synergistic benefits.
*   **Vocabulary Size Impact:** The paper explores vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100. A more detailed analysis of how performance and interpretability scale with vocabulary size might be valuable. Are there diminishing returns to increasing vocabulary size, or does a larger vocabulary consistently lead to better results?
*   **Qualitative Examples:** More detailed qualitative examples of the emergent communication, perhaps illustrating specific symbol-image mappings, could further strengthen the paper’s arguments and provide a more intuitive understanding of the learned language.



I hope these suggestions are helpful.
