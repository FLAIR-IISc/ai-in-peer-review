This paper proposes a referential game between two agents, where one agent (the sender) must communicate information about a target image to the other (the receiver) through a learned symbolic code. The agents are parameterized as neural networks and trained using reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate that agents can learn to coordinate effectively, and that the emergent communication exhibits semantic properties related to object categories. Further experiments explore grounding the communication in human language through a combined game-playing and supervised learning approach. 

I recommend this paper for **acceptance**. The work addresses an important problem – learning communication in a multi-agent setting – and offers a novel approach that moves beyond purely statistical language modeling towards understanding the functional aspects of communication. The experimental results are promising and provide initial evidence that the learned communication codes are interpretable and can be aligned with human semantic understanding.

The core contribution lies in establishing a framework for studying emergent communication where agents develop their own language through interaction, rather than relying on pre-defined protocols or large, labeled datasets. This framework is well-motivated by the challenges of building truly interactive AI systems, such as conversational agents. The paper’s exploration of how environmental factors (e.g., focusing on object classes) influence the properties of the emergent language is particularly insightful. The grounding experiments, combining reinforcement learning with supervised learning, are a significant step towards making the learned communication more accessible to humans. The analysis presented, including the use of SVD and t-SNE, effectively illustrates the semantic structure of the learned codes.

While the paper is generally strong, several minor points could enhance its clarity and impact. 

*   **Clarification of Target Input:** Please clarify how the target variable (*t*) is incorporated into the sender networks. Is it provided as a separate input, or is it encoded through the image presentation order as mentioned? A more explicit description would be helpful.
*   **Table Consistency:** Tables 1 and 2 present percentage values differently (ranging from 0-100 in Table 1 and seemingly 0-1 in Table 2). Please ensure consistent formatting for clarity. Also, the “obs-chance purity” values in Table 1 appear very small; please verify their accuracy.
*   **Typographical Errors:** Correct the typographical errors “assest” to “assess” and “usufal” to “usual.”
*   **Ablation Study:** It would be beneficial to see an ablation study examining the impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, vocabulary size) on the performance and interpretability of the learned communication.
*   **Qualitative Examples:** Including a few more qualitative examples of successful communication pairs (image pairs and corresponding symbols) alongside their interpretations would further strengthen the paper's narrative and aid understanding.
* **Human Evaluation Details:** Could you please elaborate on the demographics of the CrowdFlower participants and any quality control measures taken to ensure reliable annotations during the human evaluation?

I would appreciate the authors addressing these points. However, these suggestions are primarily aimed at improving the presentation and completeness of the work and do not detract from my overall positive assessment. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of emergent communication and deserves to be accepted for publication.
