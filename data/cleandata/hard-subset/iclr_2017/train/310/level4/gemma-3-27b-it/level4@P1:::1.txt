This paper tackles a crucial problem: understanding communication between two agents. As more applications of reinforcement learning are explored, this work returns to a fundamental question: is machine problem-solving analogous to that of humans? The task is simple enough to allow for intuitive post-learning analysis. It was particularly interesting to observe that informed agents utilized a diverse vocabulary, while agnostic agents relied on a more limited set of only two symbols.

**Decision: Reject** with key reasons being a lack of novelty and a somewhat limited scope given the framing of the problem. While the experimental results are solid, the approach ultimately feels more akin to a transfer learning problem than a novel exploration of emergent communication.

Here are supporting arguments for this decision. The core of the task, particularly when the two presented images belong to different categories, effectively reduces to image classification. The learned symbols can be readily interpreted as image class labels, and the receiver learns to assign these labels to either of the images. This suggests the agents are primarily leveraging pre-existing knowledge embedded in the image features (VGG network) rather than developing a truly emergent communication system *de novo*. The reinforcement learning framework, while valid, appears to be used to fine-tune a classifier rather than to bootstrap a language from scratch. The authors acknowledge the connection to transfer learning, but do not adequately address how their approach differs significantly or offers unique insights beyond standard transfer learning techniques. Furthermore, the environments remain relatively simple. While scaling to more complex scenarios is mentioned as future work, the current setting does not fully capture the richness and ambiguity inherent in natural language communication.

Additional feedback, intended to help improve the paper, is provided below. 

*   **Clarify the novelty:** The authors should more explicitly address how this work extends beyond existing transfer learning methods or supervised learning approaches. What specific aspects of the multi-agent game environment contribute to a truly emergent communication process, as opposed to simply learning to map images to existing class labels?
*   **Explore more complex environments:** The use of paired images from distinct categories simplifies the problem. Would the agents still converge to meaningful communication if the images were more similar, requiring more nuanced descriptions? Exploring more challenging environments would demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
*   **Quantitative comparison to baseline:** It would strengthen the paper to include a quantitative comparison to a standard image classification baseline trained on the same data. This would help to demonstrate the added benefit of the multi-agent learning framework.
*   **Analysis of symbol usage:** The analysis of symbol usage is a good start, but could be expanded. What kinds of visual features are most strongly correlated with the learned symbols? Are there patterns in the errors that reveal insights into the agents' understanding of the images?
*   **Impact of vocabulary size:** The results show a difference between vocabulary sizes of 10 and 100. Was any investigation undertaken to find an optimal vocabulary size, or to see if the results hold as vocabulary sizes increase significantly?

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   How does the performance of the informed sender compare to a standard classifier trained directly on the image features, with the labels being the symbols learned by the agent?
*   Were any attempts made to regularize the learned embeddings to encourage more interpretable or human-like symbol assignments?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained image features (VGG)? Would different feature representations lead to different emergent languages?
*   Could you elaborate on the limitations of using the referential game as a proxy for natural language communication, and how future work might address these limitations?
