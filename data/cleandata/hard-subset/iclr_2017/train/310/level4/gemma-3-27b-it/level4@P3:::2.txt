This paper presents a novel framework for language learning based on multi-agent communication within a referential game. The approach, where agents develop language through interaction rather than passive exposure to text, is a promising direction, particularly for building interactive AI systems like conversational agents. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate in this game, and explore methods for aligning the emergent language with human semantic intuitions, including grounding it through supervised learning. This work builds upon, and contributes to, the growing body of research leveraging multi-agent games for communication and coordination.

**Reject**

While the paper demonstrates a compelling framework and promising initial results, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and detail to fully assess the contribution and scientific rigor. Furthermore, the connection to the broader literature, while acknowledged, is not deeply explored, leaving me uncertain about the novelty of the approach.

The paper’s strengths lie in its intuitive framework (Section 2) and the demonstration that agents *can* learn to coordinate (Section 4). The experimental setup, while complex, is clearly laid out, and the initial results showing both coordination and some semantic alignment are encouraging. The strategy of combining the game with supervised learning (Section 5) appears particularly fruitful and positions the work well for future exploration of grounding agents’ communication in human language. However, several issues hinder a positive assessment. 

Firstly, the description of the network architectures (Section 3, Figure 1) is difficult to follow. The figure itself is not fully informative, and the textual explanation lacks sufficient detail, particularly regarding the dimensions and operations within the networks. The comment regarding potentially omitted connections is valid – a clearer, more complete diagram with a detailed caption is essential. The lack of clarity makes it difficult to evaluate the architectural choices and their impact on performance.

Secondly, the discussion of results lacks depth. While the authors demonstrate that the informed sender outperforms the agnostic sender, the *why* behind this difference is not thoroughly explored. Similarly, the analysis of symbol usage (e.g., dimensionality of the vocabulary matrix) feels superficial. More in-depth analysis of the learned representations and communication strategies would strengthen the claims.

Thirdly, while the paper acknowledges related work, the positioning of the contribution within the broader field feels somewhat weak. The discussion of Lewis games and related linguistic research is extensive, but the authors could more explicitly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods. Specifically, addressing how their work overcomes the limitations of previous approaches (e.g., scalability of human-in-the-loop methods, engineering burden of “Wizard-of-Oz” environments) would be beneficial.

Finally, the grounding experiment (Section 5) is promising but limited. While the results showing human interpretability are encouraging, the experiment's setup and the interpretation of the 68% accuracy require further scrutiny. A more rigorous evaluation of the generalization ability of the grounded language would strengthen the findings.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Clarify Network Architectures:** Provide a more detailed description of the network architectures, including all relevant dimensions and operations. A more informative figure with a clear caption is essential.
*   **In-Depth Analysis:** Conduct a more in-depth analysis of the learned representations and communication strategies. Visualize the learned embeddings and explore the relationship between symbol usage and image features.
*   **Literature Review:** Strengthen the positioning of the contribution within the broader literature. Explicitly address how the proposed approach differs from and improves upon existing methods.
*   **Grounding Experiment:** Provide more details about the crowdsourced evaluation. Include information about the demographics of the participants and the specific instructions they received. Investigate the cases where human interpretations differed from the intended meaning.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Consider performing ablation studies to assess the impact of different components of the framework (e.g., the supervised learning phase, the informed sender architecture).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the network architectures, including a complete diagram with a clear caption?
2.  Why does the informed sender consistently outperform the agnostic sender? What specific architectural features contribute to this improvement?
3.  The paper mentions redundancy in the vocabulary matrix. Can you elaborate on the nature of this redundancy and its implications for the learned language?
4.  In the grounding experiment, what criteria were used to select the image pairs for human evaluation? How might the results differ if a different selection strategy were used?
5.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters? Did you perform any hyperparameter tuning?
