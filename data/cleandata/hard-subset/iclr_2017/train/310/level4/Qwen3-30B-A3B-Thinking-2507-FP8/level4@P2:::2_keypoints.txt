- Introduces a novel framework for training language-capable agents via multi-agent referential games without pre-defined language.  
- Shows simple neural networks can learn effective communication protocols through interactive coordination.  
- Demonstrates that modifying the environment (e.g., using class-level properties) improves semantic interpretability of emergent language.  
- Proposes a grounding strategy combining interactive gameplay with supervised image labeling, achieving 68% human comprehension.  
- Decision: Accept — paper makes a clear, significant contribution with robust experimental support.  
- Problem statement: Addresses moving beyond passive supervised learning to interactive, functional language acquisition using referential games.  
- Motivation & literature placement: Well-situated within linguistics, cognitive science, and multi-agent systems; contrasts with prior supervised and human-in-the-loop methods.  
- Empirical claim support: Agents attain near-perfect coordination; informed sender uses larger vocabulary (88 vs 2) and higher semantic purity (~70%).  
- Semantic analysis: SVD and t-SNE show the emergent language is high-dimensional and aligns with high-level object categories.  
- Human grounding evidence: Supervised grounding transfers to humans (68% comprehension) while authors acknowledge remaining noise.  
- Figure feedback: Architecture diagram (Fig.1) is confusing—add a clearer caption and simplified sender/receiver differences.  
- Statistical feedback: Section 4.1 claims about general property coordination need stronger statistical justification (e.g., p-values for purity increases).  
- Limitation note: Grounding relies on ImageNet labels, which may not capture nuanced human semantics (example: "dolphin" for sea).  
- Question 1 for authors: How is "word-likeness" quantified beyond vocabulary size, and can humans distinguish informed sender symbols from noise?  
- Question 2 for authors: What are the most common human misinterpretations in ReferItGame (semantic drift, visual confusion) and how to reduce them?  
- Question 3 for authors: How sensitive is communication success to the ratio of supervised vs. game training steps?
