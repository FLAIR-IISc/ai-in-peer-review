

This paper introduces a novel framework for training language-capable agents through multi-agent referential games, where senders and receivers develop communication protocols via interactive coordination without pre-defined language. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn effective communication in these games, and further show how modifying the environment (e.g., using class-level properties instead of raw images) improves the semantic interpretability of the emergent language. Crucially, they propose a grounding strategy combining interactive game play with supervised image labeling, enabling agents to use human-interpretable labels and achieving 68% human comprehension in follow-up tests.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the development of interactive language agents, and the experimental evidence robustly supports its core claims.

**Supporting Arguments:**  
1. **Problem Statement:** The paper directly addresses the critical gap in training conversational agents—moving beyond passive supervised learning to interactive, functional language acquisition. The authors clearly frame their work as a step toward "machines that can communicate with humans productively," distinguishing it from prior approaches like Wizard-of-Oz (which requires constant human input) or pre-programmed protocols (e.g., Stone & Veloso, 1998). The focus on referential games as a minimal, scalable environment for language emergence is well-motivated and addresses a key challenge in AI communication.  

2. **Motivation & Literature Placement:** The work is strongly situated within linguistics (Lewis signaling games), cognitive science, and multi-agent systems. The authors thoughtfully contrast their approach with related work (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) and justify their focus on *practical* AI development—using realistic image data and aligning with human semantic intuitions—rather than purely theoretical or human-in-the-loop paradigms. The connection to game theory (e.g., convergence to equilibria in cheap-talk games) adds theoretical depth without overclaiming.  

3. **Claim Support:** Results are rigorously validated through multiple complementary methods:  
   - **Empirical:** Agents achieve near-perfect coordination (Table 1), with the "informed sender" using a richer vocabulary (88 symbols vs. 2 in agnostic version) and higher semantic purity (70% vs. baseline).  
   - **Semantic Analysis:** SVD decomposition confirms the language requires >50 dimensions (not just synonymy), and t-SNE visualizations show symbols align with high-level object categories (e.g., "living vs. non-living").  
   - **Human Grounding:** The supervised grounding strategy yields 68% human comprehension in ReferItGame experiments—a compelling demonstration of transferability beyond the training set. Crucially, the paper avoids overclaiming: it acknowledges the language remains "noisy" but shows *progress* toward human-interpretable communication.  

**Additional Feedback:**  
- **Figure 1:** The architecture diagram is confusing due to omitted connections and lack of caption. Please add a clear caption explaining the sender/receiver input flows and the role of the "game-specific embedding" layer. A simplified diagram focusing on the key differences between agnostic/informed senders would greatly improve readability.  
- **Section 4.1:** The claim that "agents can coordinate on general properties when low-level common knowledge is removed" (Table 2) requires stronger statistical justification (e.g., p-values for purity increases).  
- **Limitations:** Briefly acknowledge that the current grounding strategy relies on ImageNet labels, which may not capture nuanced human semantics (e.g., "dolphin" for sea in Fig 4).  

**Questions for Authors:**  
1. The paper states the informed sender uses "more varied and word-like symbols" (Section 4). How do you quantify "word-likeness" beyond vocabulary size? Could a human distinguish the informed sender’s symbols from random noise (e.g., via a simple classification task)?  
2. In the ReferItGame experiment (Section 5), 68% human comprehension is impressive but still leaves room for error. What are the *most common* misinterpretations (e.g., semantic drift, visual confusion), and how might the framework be adjusted to reduce these?  
3. The grounding strategy shares the game-embedding layer between supervised and game phases. How sensitive is the communication success to the *ratio* of supervised vs. game training steps?  

This work is a compelling step toward functional language agents. The grounding approach is particularly promising for real-world deployment, and the experimental design thoroughly validates the core claims. With minor clarifications to the figures and additional statistical detail for key results, this paper will significantly advance the field.
