Summary: The paper presents a compelling framework for developing language-capable agents through multi-agent referential games, where sender-receiver pairs learn to coordinate using arbitrary symbols without pre-defined linguistic structures. It demonstrates that simple neural networks can develop semantically meaningful communication protocols aligned with human conceptual categories, and introduces a novel grounding strategy by integrating supervised image labeling with interactive game play. The work makes significant progress toward enabling machines to communicate with humans using interpretable language.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper successfully demonstrates that agents can develop functional communication protocols that reflect high-level semantic properties (Section 4), and the grounding approach via supervised learning (Section 5) provides a clear path to human-interpretable language.

3. Supporting arguments: 
   - The agents achieve near-perfect coordination (Table 1) while developing symbols with semantic purity significantly above chance (e.g., 70% purity after grounding), demonstrating that communication emerges with meaningful conceptual alignment.
   - The grounding strategy (Section 5) is particularly compelling: it enables agents to use 88 symbols (vs. 2 in baseline) with 68% human interpretability in crowdsourcing tests, showing that supervised learning on image labels directly improves human-agent communication without compromising coordination success.
   - The environmental modifications (Section 4.1) that shift communication toward class-level semantics (e.g., "living vs. non-living" categories) provide strong evidence that game design can steer emergent language toward human-intuitive properties.

4. Additional feedback: 
   - The figure caption for Figure 1 is currently insufficient to interpret the network architectures described in Section 3. Adding explicit labels for the "aggressive" vs. "informed" sender components (e.g., indicating where convolutions are applied) would greatly improve clarity.
   - The paper could strengthen its connection to linguistic theory by briefly discussing how the emergent "living vs. non-living" cluster (Section 4) relates to established semantic typologies (e.g., Caramazza & Shelton, 1998).

5. Questions for authors:
   - The "living vs. non-living" cluster achieved with only two symbols (Section 4) is intriguing but unexpected. Could the authors elaborate on whether this reflects a fundamental semantic bias in the learning process, or whether it would persist with larger vocabulary sizes?
   - The 68% human interpretability rate (Section 5) is impressive but lacks statistical significance testing. Could the authors provide confidence intervals or p-values for this result to confirm it's not due to chance?
