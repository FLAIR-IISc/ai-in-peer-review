

This paper introduces a referential game between two agents operating on image inputs. The sender observes two images and a binary target variable \(t\), then transmits a symbol (message) to the receiver. The receiver uses this message to predict \(t\), and both agents receive a shared reward upon successful prediction. The authors parameterize the agents as neural networks with pretrained image features and train them via REINFORCE. Results demonstrate that agents converge to near-optimal policies, and the learned symbolic communications encode interpretable visual concepts. The authors extend this to a variant using distinct image classes, where agents learn even more semantically meaningful representations. Furthermore, a multi-game setup is proposed: the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task. When anchored to classification, the symbolic communications exhibit heightened interpretability.

The work addresses a significant challenge—learning shared communication protocols in multi-agent reinforcement learning—which is inherently more complex than standard supervised or single-agent RL. The authors' approach of first establishing communication protocols and then grounding them in human language represents a novel paradigm, potentially offering a functional alternative to sequence-to-sequence models that emphasize statistical regularities over semantic content. The paper’s contributions—proposing a new task framework, analyzing learned symbols, and visualizing their semantic content—are valuable for advancing research in grounded communication. This constitutes a meaningful stepping stone for future work, warranting acceptance.
