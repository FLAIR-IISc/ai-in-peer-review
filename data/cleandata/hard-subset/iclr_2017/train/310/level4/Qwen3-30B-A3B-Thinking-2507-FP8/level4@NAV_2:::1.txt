

This paper makes an important contribution to understanding how language can emerge through interactive communication between agents. The authors propose a framework based on multi-agent communication games, specifically focusing on referential games where a sender and receiver must coordinate to identify target images. The paper demonstrates that simple neural networks can learn to develop communication protocols through this interactive process, and further explores how to design game environments to encourage the emergence of more semantically meaningful language. The authors also present a novel strategy for grounding agents' communication in natural language through a combination of interactive game play and supervised learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that agents can develop language-like communication through interaction rather than supervised learning, and it provides concrete evidence that the emergent communication protocols capture high-level semantic properties of the visual input.

The paper successfully addresses the central questions of whether agents can learn to coordinate through interactive communication and whether the resulting communication protocols reflect intuitive semantic properties. The experiments show that agents converge to successful coordination, and the analysis of symbol usage reveals that the communication protocols capture broader conceptual categories rather than low-level visual features. The grounding strategy using a combination of supervised learning and interactive game play is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates how agents can develop communication protocols that are partially interpretable by humans.

The work is well-motivated by the limitations of current supervised learning approaches to natural language processing, and it places itself appropriately within the literature on language emergence, multi-agent systems, and game theory. The experimental setup is clear and well-executed, with appropriate baselines and thorough analysis of the emergent communication protocols.

To strengthen the paper, consider providing more detail on the training dynamics, particularly the convergence rates of the different architectures. The paper could also benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of the current approach, especially regarding the scalability of the communication protocols to more complex environments.

Could you clarify how the informed sender's use of convolutional layers specifically contributes to the emergence of more semantically meaningful symbols, compared to the agnostic sender? Additionally, what evidence do you have that the observed semantic properties of the emergent language would generalize to more complex real-world communication scenarios beyond the referential game setup?
