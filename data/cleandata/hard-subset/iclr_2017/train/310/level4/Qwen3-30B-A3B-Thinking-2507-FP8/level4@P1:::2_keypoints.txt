- Introduces a multi-agent referential game framework showing neural agents can develop a shared communication protocol that captures meaningful semantic properties of images.  
- Proposes grounding emergent communication in natural language via combined interactive gameplay and supervised image-label training to enable human-interpretable signals.  
- Argues the work bridges theoretical linguistics, game theory, and practical AI with an experimental roadmap toward human-AI conversational agents.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—significant contribution demonstrating emergent, human-compatible communication.  
- Core problem is clearly motivated: supervised "canned" conversations are insufficient, and multi-agent interaction is needed to learn functional communication.  
- Approach is well-situated in prior work (Lewis signaling games, language evolution, multi-agent systems) and clearly differentiates from supervised and Wizard-of-Oz methods.  
- Strong empirical support: coordination across architectures (informed vs. agnostic senders), semantic structure in emergent language (purity, t-SNE), and supervised grounding improving human interpretability (68% human accuracy).  
- Ablation studies (removing low-level common knowledge) support claims about environmental design encouraging abstract reference.  
- Suggest clarifying Figure 1 (show omitted connections, add detailed caption and explanation of informed sender architecture).  
- Request clearer definition of "common knowledge" in Section 4.1—does it mean shared exact image features or a broader concept?  
- Ask for deeper discussion in Section 5 on why supervised labels generalize to unseen categories (e.g., role of CNN embeddings vs. other factors).  
- Recommend analyzing human evaluation errors (which image types are ambiguous or misinterpreted) to strengthen conclusions.  
- Suggest explicitly quantifying differences from Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) with metrics showing how the emergent language is "more natural."  
- Key question: in grounding experiments, when senders use supervised labels for semantically shifted targets (e.g., "dolphin" used for sea images), is this metonymic usage a learned strategy from joint training or an emergent behavior—clarify mechanism and generalization implications.
