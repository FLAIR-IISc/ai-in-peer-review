

Thank you for submitting this work. The paper addresses a timely and important question regarding multi-agent communication in reinforcement learning, which is increasingly relevant as RL applications expand. Below is my assessment.

**Strengths**  
- The paper tackles a foundational challenge: understanding how agents communicate to solve tasks, thereby revisiting a core question about whether machine problem-solving strategies align with human cognition. This focus is highly relevant as RL systems grow more complex.  
- The experimental design is sufficiently simple to enable intuitive post-hoc analysis of communication dynamics, making the results accessible and interpretable.  
- The findings reveal a compelling contrast: informed agents (aware of the communication context) leverage multiple symbols to encode nuanced information, while agnostic agents (unaware of context) default to a binary symbol system. This divergence highlights how agent awareness shapes communication efficiency.  

**Weaknesses**  
The primary concern is that the task reduces to a standard image classification problem, undermining the paper’s reinforcement learning framing. Specifically, when two distinct images are transmitted, the symbols used by the sender function as direct class labels for the receiver. For instance, if the sender transmits images from different categories, the receiver learns to associate symbols with these categories—effectively solving a classification task. This means the core challenge is not inherently a communication problem requiring RL, but rather a transfer learning task where supervised methods could achieve comparable or superior results with far fewer samples and computational resources. The RL approach thus appears unnecessarily complex for the problem as defined, and the paper does not sufficiently justify why RL is necessary over simpler alternatives.  

**Recommendation**  
While the paper raises valuable questions about agent communication, its technical contribution is limited by the task’s reduction to classification. To strengthen the work, the authors should either: (1) redefine the task to avoid this reduction (e.g., by introducing ambiguity or dynamic environments where symbols cannot map directly to static classes), or (2) explicitly compare RL against supervised baselines to demonstrate RL’s added value. Without such revisions, the paper risks conflating a standard ML problem with a novel RL challenge.
