- Introduces a multi-agent referential-game framework where sender and receiver learn symbolic communication without a pre-defined language.  
- Uses simple neural networks trained with REINFORCE and pretrained VGG image features to converge on communication protocols.  
- Emergent symbols align with high-level semantic categories (e.g., "animal" vs. "vehicle") rather than low-level visual features.  
- Grounds emergent language in human natural language by combining the game with supervised image classification.  
- Grounding yields human-interpretable labels: crowdworkers interpret agent symbols with 68% accuracy (vs. 1% chance).  
- Paper’s two main contributions: a scalable paradigm for emergent language via interaction and a systematic method to ground it in human semantics.  
- Strong empirical results: near-perfect coordination (98–100% success) across architectures and vocab sizes; informed sender uses many more distinct symbols (88/100) than agnostic sender (2).  
- Semantic analyses support claims: McRae category purity (70% vs. 33% chance), t-SNE visualizations, and human interpretability tests show high-level conceptual structure.  
- Grounding experiment demonstrates a bridge between emergent symbols and human language and reveals flexible metonymic usages (e.g., "dolphin" for sea scenes).  
- Framework is well-motivated by game theory and cognitive science and addresses design of environments that foster portable, human-compatible language.
