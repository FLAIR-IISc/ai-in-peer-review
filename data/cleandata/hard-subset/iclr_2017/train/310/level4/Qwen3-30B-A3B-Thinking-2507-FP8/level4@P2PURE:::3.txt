

This paper introduces a referential game between two agents, each observing two images. The sender agent receives a binary target variable (t) and transmits a symbolic message to the receiver agent, whose task is to predict the target. Both agents receive a reward when the receiver correctly predicts the target. The authors parameterize the agents as neural networks using pretrained image representations as feature vectors, training them via REINFORCE. The results demonstrate that the agents converge to optimal policies, and their learned communications—such as the symbolic codes transmitted from sender to receiver—encode meaningful concepts. Additionally, experiments on a variant grounded in distinct image classes reveal that agents learn even more semantically meaningful concepts. The paper further proposes a multi-task setup where the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task. As expected, grounding the communication in the supervised learning task leads to symbolic messages with enhanced meaningfulness.

Learning shared communication protocols in a multi-agent context represents a compelling research direction. This task is significantly more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, justifying the choice of a relatively simple initial setup. To the best of my knowledge, the approach of first developing communication between agents and subsequently grounding it in human language is novel. As the authors note, this offers an alternative paradigm to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which typically emphasize statistical language properties over functional semantics. The proposed task framework, along with the analysis and visualization of communicated token semantics, constitutes a valuable foundation for future work. Consequently, I recommend acceptance.
