

This paper introduces a novel framework for interactive language learning through multi-agent referential games, where two agents develop a shared communication protocol by coordinating to identify target images. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn effective communication strategies without pre-defined linguistic structures, and further show how environmental modifications—such as shifting from pixel-level to class-level image representations—and hybrid training with supervised image classification can steer the emergent language toward more human-interpretable semantics. The work makes a compelling case for interactive learning as a complement to traditional supervised approaches, with strong empirical evidence that the agents' symbolic communication encodes meaningful conceptual properties rather than low-level visual features. The grounding experiment, where agents learn to map symbols to human language labels while maintaining coordination, represents a significant step toward human-AI communication.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by establishing a viable paradigm for functional language acquisition through multi-agent interaction, addressing a critical gap in current approaches that prioritize statistical language patterns over communication utility. The core evidence supporting this claim—convergence to coordinated communication, semantic alignment with human conceptual categories, and successful grounding in natural language—is robust and well-validated.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper successfully demonstrates that agents can bootstrap language learning through interaction alone. Both the "agnostic" and "informed" sender architectures achieve near-perfect coordination (Table 1), with the informed sender showing faster convergence and richer symbol usage (Figure 2). Crucially, the authors rigorously assess whether the emergent language reflects human semantics: (1) Purity metrics for symbol-object clusters significantly exceed chance (Table 1), and (2) t-SNE visualizations reveal that semantically similar objects activate similar symbols (Figure 3). The "object-level reference" modification (Section 4.1) further strengthens this by showing that removing low-level visual common knowledge forces agents to use higher-level semantic categories, increasing purity by 15% (Table 2).  

The grounding strategy (Section 5) is particularly compelling. By training the sender on both the referential game and supervised image classification (using ImageNet labels), the authors achieve 70% symbol purity and 68% human interpretability in crowdsourced trials—far exceeding chance (1%). The qualitative analysis of "metonymic" symbol usage (e.g., "dolphin" for sea, "fence" for land) demonstrates that the language generalizes meaningfully beyond the supervised vocabulary, a key step toward human-AI communication. This approach avoids the scalability issues of human-in-the-loop methods (e.g., Wizard-of-Oz) and the engineering burden of scripted environments (e.g., Mikolov et al., 2015), while providing a clear path to aligning machine communication with human intuition.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
1. **Clarify target input**: The paper states the sender receives "t ∈ {L,R}" but does not specify how this target is encoded. Is it visually marked (e.g., a green square in the image pair, as suggested in Figure 1), or provided as a separate binary input? Please explicitly state this in Section 3 to avoid ambiguity.  
2. **Table formatting**: Table 1 reports "obs-chance purity" as small absolute values (e.g., 0.005), while Table 2 uses percentages. Standardize to percentages (e.g., "0.5%" instead of "0.005") for consistency with the text. The values in Table 1 appear correct (e.g., 0.005 difference from chance), but the formatting may mislead readers.  
3. **Terminology precision**: Replace "symbolic code" with "emergent language" or "symbolic communication" for accuracy (the symbols are part of a learned protocol, not a fixed code).  
4. **Human interpretability details**: The 68% human accuracy is impressive, but clarify whether this was measured *only* on test pairs where the target image belonged to a supervised category (as in the first experiment) or *including* out-of-vocabulary images (as in the ReferItGame extension). The latter is more significant for generalizability.  

**Key Questions for Authors**  
1. How does the visual marking of the target (e.g., green square) affect the sender’s learning compared to a purely symbolic input? Could the agents coordinate without this explicit cue, and if so, how would that impact semantic alignment?  
2. In the grounding experiment, the sender uses 88 symbols after training (vs. 2 in the agnostic sender). How does this expansion correlate with the diversity of supervised labels (100 total vs. 100 labels used)?  
3. The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) show clusters of similar symbols. Could the authors provide a qualitative example of a symbol that successfully generalizes to novel image pairs (e.g., "dolphin" for sea), highlighting the metonymic reasoning?  

This work establishes a rigorous foundation for interactive language acquisition and offers a practical pathway to grounding machine communication in human semantics. The experimental design, analysis, and incremental improvements (from basic coordination to human-interpretable grounding) are exceptionally well-executed. I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to the field.
