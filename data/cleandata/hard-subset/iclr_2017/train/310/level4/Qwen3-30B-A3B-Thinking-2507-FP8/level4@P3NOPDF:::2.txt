

The paper presents a promising approach to training natural language systems through multi-agent referential communication games, a direction with limited prior exploration but significant potential. The core framework (Section 2) is well-motivated and effectively demonstrates how interactive multi-agent settings can foster grounded language learning. The authors' strategy of dynamically switching between interactive games and supervised learning (Sections 5 and 6) is particularly compelling, offering a practical path to balance exploration and efficiency. This hybrid methodology appears robust, with results that are reasonable and demonstrably effective across the evaluated tasks.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear articulation of the problem and the thoughtful design of the communication game environment. The authors successfully establish that multi-agent interaction can drive the emergence of efficient, task-oriented communication protocols, which aligns with broader goals in embodied AI and language grounding. The experimental results support the framework’s viability, and the suggested future directions (Section 6) indicate clear potential for extensions, such as incorporating more complex environments or diverse agent roles.

However, Figure 1 requires significant revision to enhance clarity. The current diagram uses shorthand that omits key network connections, and the architecture’s highly customized nature—combined with a somewhat opaque description in Section 3—makes the figure confusing rather than illuminating. At minimum, a detailed caption explaining each component and its purpose is essential. If the diagram’s design intention is clear to the authors, this should be explicitly conveyed in the caption to prevent misinterpretation. Without this, readers may struggle to grasp the technical flow, undermining the paper’s otherwise strong presentation.

A minor caveat warrants mention: while I am confident in my understanding of the paper’s methodology and results (as reflected in the confidence score), my familiarity with the most closely related literature—particularly recent work on multi-agent communication in language learning—is insufficient to fully contextualize the novelty or comparative impact of this contribution. The authors rightly cite prior multi-agent communication studies, but a more thorough discussion of how this work specifically advances or diverges from, say, the *Learner-Teacher* paradigm or *Game-Theoretic* communication frameworks would strengthen the positioning. This limitation does not detract from the paper’s technical merits but should be addressed in revision to ensure the contribution is unambiguously situated within the field.

Overall, this is a strong submission that advances the study of language learning through interactive multi-agent systems. The framework is well-executed, the results are convincing, and the proposed methodology has clear potential for broader application. The primary actionable item is refining Figure 1 with a comprehensive caption and clarifying the network architecture in Section 3. Once these are addressed, the paper will make a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance pending these minor revisions.
