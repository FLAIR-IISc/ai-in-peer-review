

This paper introduces a novel framework for language learning through multi-agent communication games, specifically focusing on referential games where a sender and receiver must coordinate to identify a target image from a pair. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn effective communication protocols through reinforcement learning, developing symbolic codes that capture semantic properties of the images rather than low-level visual features. They further show that modifying the game environment to remove low-level common knowledge encourages agents to develop more abstract, human-like semantic representations. Most significantly, they propose a grounding strategy where the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task, resulting in communication that is more interpretable to humans. The paper makes a valuable contribution by shifting the focus from statistical language modeling to functional communication, providing a promising pathway toward developing conversational agents that can interact with humans productively.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of current language learning approaches, which primarily focus on statistical associations rather than the functional aspects of communication. The authors provide compelling evidence that agents can develop meaningful communication protocols through interaction, and their grounding strategy represents a significant step toward human-interpretable machine communication.

The paper's contributions are well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly position their work within the context of multi-agent systems, linguistic evolution, and cognitive science, while distinguishing their approach from existing methods. They build upon seminal work on signaling games (Lewis, 1969) while addressing practical AI challenges that previous literature has not fully considered. The framework is well-designed to explore how environmental modifications influence language emergence, and the grounding strategy is particularly innovative.

The results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The authors demonstrate that agents converge to near-perfect coordination across multiple experimental configurations. Their semantic analysis using purity metrics and t-SNE visualizations provides strong evidence that the agents develop meaningful symbol meanings aligned with human conceptual categories. The grounding experiment is especially convincing, showing that supervised learning on a subset of image labels significantly improves human interpretability (68% accuracy in a crowdsourced survey, compared to 1% chance). The paper carefully addresses potential limitations, such as the fact that agents still require human supervision for grounding, and the language developed is initially quite limited.

The paper's clarity and organization are excellent. The methodology is described in sufficient detail to be reproducible, and the results are presented with appropriate statistical rigor. The authors effectively connect their findings to broader theoretical frameworks in game theory, linguistics, and cognitive science, while maintaining a clear focus on practical AI applications. Their analysis of how different agent architectures (informed vs. agnostic senders) affect language emergence provides valuable insights into the relationship between inductive bias and communication quality.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
- Clarify how the target variable (t) is incorporated into the sender network. The current description is ambiguous about whether the sender receives explicit information about which image is the target, which would significantly affect the learning dynamics.
- Standardize the percentage formatting in Tables 1 and 2. The first table uses [0, 100] while the second uses [0, 1], creating confusion. The "obs-chance purity" values in Table 1 appear to be extremely small (likely a formatting issue) and should be corrected.
- Correct minor typos: "assest" should be "assess" and "usufal" should be "usual."
- Consider adding a brief discussion about the scalability of the approach to larger vocabularies and more complex environments, as this is an important consideration for practical applications.

Key questions for authors:
1. How do you expect the performance to scale with larger vocabulary sizes (e.g., 1,000+ symbols) and more complex visual inputs?
2. In the grounding experiment, you report 68% accuracy in human interpretation. What specific semantic patterns (e.g., metonymic links) did human subjects rely on to interpret the symbols, and how might these be systematically improved?
3. The paper mentions that "the informed sender is using more varied and word-like symbols." Could you provide more quantitative evidence to support this claim, perhaps through additional linguistic analysis of the emergent code?

This work makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that functional language learning can emerge through multi-agent communication games, and that this approach can be grounded in human language through a simple yet effective strategy. The paper is well-written, methodologically sound, and addresses a critical gap in current language learning approaches. It provides a strong foundation for future work on developing conversational agents that can communicate with humans in a natural and meaningful way.
