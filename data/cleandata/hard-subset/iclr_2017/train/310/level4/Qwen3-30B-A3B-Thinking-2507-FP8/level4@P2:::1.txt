

Summary: This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in AI—developing agents capable of interactive communication—by introducing a multi-agent framework grounded in referential games. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can learn to coordinate via emergent language through reinforcement learning, without pre-defined protocols. Crucially, they show how modifying the game environment (e.g., removing low-level visual common knowledge) encourages symbols to align with high-level semantic categories (e.g., "animal" vs. specific breeds), and they propose a novel grounding strategy using supervised learning to connect emergent symbols with human language. The work makes a significant step toward interpretable, human-compatible communication in AI.

Decision: Weak Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of language emergence in multi-agent systems, with strong empirical evidence supporting its core claims. However, the scope is limited to simplified referential games, and the human-grounding results, while promising, require deeper validation.

Supporting Arguments:  
The paper successfully answers its core questions. First, it demonstrates that tabula rasa agents *can* develop coordinated communication (Table 1, Fig. 2), with the informed sender achieving higher semantic purity (70% vs. 40% for agnostic) and using 88 symbols vs. 2. This addresses the central problem of whether agents bootstrap language through interaction. Second, the motivation is well-grounded in linguistics, game theory, and prior work (e.g., Lewis signaling games), while clearly differentiating from human-in-the-loop or pre-programmed approaches. The experiments rigorously test semantic properties: (a) symbol clusters align with McRae categories (purity > chance), (b) t-SNE visualizations show symbols map to high-level visual semantics (Fig. 3), and (c) the grounding strategy (supervised image labeling + referential game) significantly improves human interpretability (68% accuracy in crowdsourcing vs. 1% chance). The evidence is scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines, statistical validation (purity vs. random permutation), and ablation studies (e.g., object-level reference in Section 4.1).  

Key Weaknesses:  
The primary limitation is the gap between the artificial referential game and real-world conversational AI. While the grounding approach is innovative, the human interpretability results (68% accuracy) remain modest, and the paper does not address how this scales to complex dialogues or noisy real-world inputs (e.g., natural language queries). The claim that the task "boils down to image classification" misrepresents the work: the agents learn *symbolic communication* for *high-level categories* (e.g., "animal" for diverse images), not class labels. The informed sender’s 88-symbol usage (vs. agnostic’s 2) proves it transcends simple classification. However, the vocabulary size (100 symbols) is small for natural language, and the paper does not explore how to handle ambiguity or open-ended references beyond object-level pairs.  

Additional Feedback:  
1. **Human Grounding Validation**: The crowdsourcing experiment is promising but lacks analysis of *why* 32% of cases failed. Include qualitative examples of misinterpretations (e.g., "dolphin" for sea) to clarify limitations.  
2. **Scalability**: Discuss how the approach might extend to multi-turn dialogue or larger vocabularies (e.g., 1,000+ symbols).  
3. **Baseline Comparison**: Compare against a strong RL baseline (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016) to quantify the *efficiency* gains from the grounding strategy.  
4. **Semantic Analysis**: Add a quantitative metric for "semantic naturalness" (e.g., human-rated symbol coherence) beyond purity.  

Clarifying Questions:  
1. The paper states the informed sender uses "more varied and word-like symbols" (Section 4). How did you ensure the symbol usage isn’t merely high synonymy (e.g., multiple symbols for "dog")? The SVD analysis suggests dimensionality >1, but could you provide a concrete example of a symbol used for distinct high-level categories (e.g., "vehicle" for car/boat)?  
2. In the grounded experiment (Section 5), the sender used 100 symbols across ReferItGame images (which contain diverse objects not in the supervised set). How did the agent generalize to *unseen* categories (e.g., "giraffe" when trained on "cat/dog")? Did it rely on metonymy (as in Fig. 4), and if so, how might this be refined?  
3. The human interpretability score (68%) is notable but not "productive" for real-world use. What additional steps would you propose to reach >90% accuracy?  

This work is a compelling first step toward human-grounded communication in AI. With minor clarifications on scalability and human evaluation, it will significantly advance the field.
