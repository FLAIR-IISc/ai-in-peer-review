

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**1. Summary of Contribution**  
This paper introduces a compelling framework for language learning through multi-agent communication games, specifically focused on referential games where agents develop a shared language to coordinate in identifying target images. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can successfully learn to communicate without pre-defined protocols, showing that the emergent language captures meaningful semantic properties rather than merely low-level visual features. Crucially, they propose a practical approach to ground this communication in natural language through a combination of interactive game play and supervised learning on image labels, enabling human-interpretable communication. The work bridges theoretical linguistics, game theory, and practical AI development with a clear experimental roadmap toward human-AI conversational agents.

**2. Decision**  
I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that natural language communication can emerge through cooperative multi-agent interactions, and it provides a promising path toward grounding this communication in human language.

**3. Supporting Arguments**  
The paper successfully addresses the three key questions for evaluation:

First, the core problem—designing environments that foster portable, human-compatible language development—is clearly articulated and well-motivated. The authors convincingly argue that supervised learning on "canned" conversations fails to capture the functional aspects of communication, and they position their multi-agent approach as a necessary complement to existing methods.

Second, the approach is thoroughly grounded in the literature. The paper effectively situates its work within game theory (Lewis signaling games), linguistic evolution studies, and multi-agent systems, while clearly differentiating its contribution from related work in supervised learning, human-in-the-loop systems, and Wizard-of-Oz environments. The experimental design draws appropriately from these foundations.

Third, the paper provides robust empirical support for its claims. The authors demonstrate successful coordination across multiple architectures (informed vs. agnostic senders), show that the emergent language captures high-level semantic properties (via purity metrics and t-SNE visualizations), and provide compelling evidence that supervised grounding significantly improves human interpretability (with 68% accuracy in human evaluations). The ablation studies (e.g., removing low-level common knowledge to encourage abstract reference) further strengthen the claims about environmental design.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions**  
The paper is well-written and technically sound overall. To further improve clarity and impact, I suggest the following:

- **Figure 1**: The architecture diagram is currently confusing due to omitted connections and lack of caption. A more detailed caption explaining the key components (especially the convolutional structure of the informed sender) would greatly aid understanding. If the diagram is simplified, consider adding a brief textual explanation in the caption.

- **Section 4.1**: The claim that "agents can only coordinate on general properties of object classes" after removing low-level common knowledge is important but could be strengthened with a more precise explanation of what "common knowledge" means in this context. Could you clarify whether this refers specifically to the shared knowledge of the exact image features, or something more general?

- **Section 5**: The supervised grounding experiment is compelling, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of why the supervised labels generalize to unseen categories (e.g., the dolphin/fence example in Figure 4). Is this due to the nature of the CNN embeddings, or are there other factors at play?

- **Human Evaluation**: The 68% accuracy in the human evaluation is impressive, but it would be valuable to understand the distribution of errors. What types of images are most difficult for humans to interpret (e.g., ambiguous objects, similar categories)? A brief qualitative analysis of common error cases would strengthen the conclusion.

- **Comparison to Prior Work**: While the paper mentions related work, it would be helpful to explicitly quantify how this approach differs from Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) in terms of linguistic properties of the emergent language. Specifically, what metrics would you use to demonstrate that your language is "more natural" than theirs?

Finally, I have one key question to help clarify the work: In the grounding experiment (Section 5), when the sender uses a symbol corresponding to a supervised label (e.g., "dolphin"), but the target image is of a sea stretch rather than a dolphin, how does the model handle this semantic shift? Is this "metonymic" usage a learned strategy, or does it emerge naturally from the joint training objective? Understanding this would help assess whether the approach generalizes well to real-world language use.
