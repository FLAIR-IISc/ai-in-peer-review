

This paper introduces a referential game between two agents operating on image observations. The sender agent receives a binary target variable \( t \) and transmits a symbolic message to the receiver agent, whose goal is to predict \( t \). Both agents receive a reward if the receiver successfully predicts the target. The authors parameterize the agents as neural networks using pretrained image feature vectors and train them via REINFORCE. The results demonstrate that the agents converge to optimal policies, and their learned symbolic communications exhibit meaningful concepts. The paper further evaluates a variant grounded on distinct image classes, where agents learn even more interpretable concepts. Additionally, a multi-game setup is proposed where the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task. When anchored to classification, the symbolic communications become more semantically meaningful.

The work addresses an important research direction: learning shared communication protocols in multi-agent settings, which is significantly more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning. The simplicity of the task justifies the experimental approach. To the best of my knowledge, the framework—first learning agent communication and then grounding it in human language—represents a novel paradigm. This contrasts with standard sequence-to-sequence models, which typically emphasize statistical language properties over functional semantics. The contributions of the proposed task, framework, and the analysis/visualization of communicated tokens constitute a valuable stepping stone for future research. I believe this paper should be accepted.
