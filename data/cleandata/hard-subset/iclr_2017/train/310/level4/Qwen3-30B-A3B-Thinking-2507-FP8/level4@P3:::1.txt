

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper presents an innovative framework for studying language emergence through multi-agent communication in referential games. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can successfully develop communication protocols when trained through interactive play, without pre-defined language structures. The work makes significant contributions by showing how to engineer game environments to encourage the emergence of semantic properties in the agents' communication and by proposing a novel strategy for grounding this communication in natural language through supervised learning. The paper is well-motivated by both AI and linguistic literature, and the experimental design is rigorous and carefully executed.

## Decision
Accept. This paper makes a strong contribution to understanding how communication can emerge from interaction, with clear pathways toward developing more human-like language capabilities in AI systems.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in AI: how to move beyond passive language learning to interactive communication. The authors successfully demonstrate that agents can learn to coordinate through a simple reinforcement learning framework, with the informed sender architecture achieving more diverse symbol usage than the agnostic variant. Crucially, the paper goes beyond basic coordination to investigate semantic properties of the emergent language, showing that symbols correlate with high-level conceptual categories rather than low-level visual features. This finding is significant as it suggests the language development process may align with human semantic organization.

The grounding approach is particularly compelling. By combining the referential game with supervised image classification, the authors show that agents can develop communication protocols that are partially interpretable by humans, even when communicating about novel image categories. The human evaluation showing 68% accuracy in identifying target images based on the agents' symbols is a strong empirical validation of their approach. This work provides a valuable bridge between theoretical language emergence studies and practical applications in human-AI communication.

The paper is well-placed within the literature, building on game-theoretic approaches to language emergence while making novel contributions to the AI community through practical implementation and evaluation. The experimental design effectively addresses the core question of how to foster interpretable language development in multi-agent systems.

## Additional Feedback
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of why the informed sender architecture outperforms the agnostic variant. While the paper notes the informed sender's inductive bias toward dimension-by-dimension combination of image representations, a more thorough analysis of how this bias affects the learning dynamics would strengthen the argument.

The authors might also consider exploring how the communication protocol evolves over time during training, rather than just reporting final performance metrics. A time-series analysis of symbol usage and semantic purity could provide deeper insights into the learning process.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the agents' language "reflects intuitive semantic properties," but the evidence for this is primarily correlational. Could you elaborate on how the paper distinguishes between language that reflects intuitive semantics versus language that merely correlates with visual features due to the training data distribution?

2. In the grounding experiment, the authors achieve 68% human interpretability. What factors do you believe limit this further, and what specific modifications to the training procedure might most effectively increase human interpretability without compromising coordination performance?

3. The paper mentions that agents use "metonymic" links in their communication (e.g., "dolphin" for a stretch of sea). How generalizable is this phenomenon, and could the authors provide additional examples of such semantic shifts in their data?

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of language learning in multi-agent systems, with clear pathways toward more human-like communication capabilities in AI. The experimental results are robust, the methodology is sound, and the implications for future work are significant. I recommend acceptance.
