

Review:  

This paper introduces a compelling framework for developing interactive language capabilities in AI through multi-agent referential games. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks, trained via reinforcement learning in a two-player coordination task, can spontaneously develop a shared communication protocol that captures high-level semantic properties of visual inputs. Crucially, they further show how modifying the game environment—by removing low-level visual common knowledge—and integrating supervised image classification can ground this emergent language in human-understandable terms. The work addresses a fundamental limitation of current language models (which rely on static text corpora) by emphasizing the functional, interactive nature of communication. The contributions are significant: (1) a novel paradigm for bootstrapping language via multi-agent interaction, (2) empirical evidence that such protocols align with human semantic intuition, and (3) a scalable path to human-interpretable communication.  

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper should be accepted for its conceptual novelty and rigorous empirical validation. The core innovation—using referential games to *emerge* language through agent collaboration rather than supervising on static text—directly addresses a key gap in AI’s ability to develop functional communication. Furthermore, the authors provide robust evidence that the emergent language reflects meaningful semantic structure, not merely low-level visual features.  

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper excels in two critical areas. First, it rigorously validates the core hypothesis that tabula rasa agents can converge to effective communication. The authors show near-perfect coordination (99%+ accuracy) across multiple architectures and vocabulary sizes within 10k training rounds (Table 1). Crucially, they demonstrate that the *semantic quality* of the language improves with environmental modifications: (a) removing low-level visual common knowledge (Section 4.1) increases symbol purity by 12% (Table 2), and (b) grounding via supervised image classification (Section 5) yields symbols interpretable by humans (68% accuracy in crowdsourced tests). These results are not merely correlational; they are systematically tied to design choices (e.g., informed vs. agnostic senders, class-level vs. image-level inputs), strengthening causal claims.  

Second, the work is exceptionally well-positioned in the literature. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from three key paradigms: (i) pre-programmed communication (e.g., robot soccer), (ii) human-in-the-loop systems (e.g., Wizard-of-Oz), and (iii) scripted environments (e.g., SHRLDU). By leveraging game-theoretic foundations (Lewis signaling games) and cognitive science insights, they establish a principled bridge between emergent communication research and practical AI development. The comparison to related work (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., 2016; Foerster et al., 2016) is particularly strong, highlighting how their framework advances beyond *whether* communication emerges to *how* it can be steered toward human usability.  

**Additional Feedback**  
To further strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
1. **Clarify the target (t) input to the sender**: The draft mentions the sender receives "t" but does not specify how it is encoded (e.g., as a binary indicator, a one-hot vector, or integrated into image embeddings). A brief sentence in Section 2 or 3 would resolve ambiguity.  
2. **Standardize table formatting**: Table 1 reports "obs-chance purity" as small decimals (e.g., 0.007), while Table 2 uses percentages (e.g., 37%). Consistency is essential—please report all values in [0,1] or [0,100] with clear labels.  
3. **Refine the "purity" metric description**: The paper states purity "reaches 100% for perfect clustering" but does not clarify how the random baseline is computed (e.g., via permutation of labels). A sentence citing Zhao & Karypis (2003) would suffice.  
4. **Expand on the "aggressive" vocabulary usage**: The informed sender uses 88 symbols (vs. agnostic’s 2) with 70% purity. A short discussion of *why* this matters—e.g., how it enables richer semantic generalization—would bolster the argument.  

**Clarifying Questions**  
To fully assess the paper’s rigor, I request clarification on two points:  
1. **Convergence stability**: The paper notes "agents converge to coordination" (Section 4), but does this hold across multiple random seeds? If not, a brief note on variance (e.g., standard deviation in test accuracy) would be valuable.  
2. **Human interpretability scope**: The crowdsourced test (Section 5) shows 68% accuracy *when the target image belongs to a supervised category*. How does this generalize to *unseen* categories (e.g., a "dolphin" symbol used for a sea image with no prior dolphin supervision)? A single sentence on this would strengthen the "generalization" claim.  

**Conclusion**  
This work makes a substantial contribution to the development of AI systems capable of functional communication. The framework is elegantly simple yet powerful, the experiments are scientifically rigorous, and the path toward human-interpretable language is both novel and practical. The minor revisions requested above would elevate an already strong paper. I recommend acceptance.
