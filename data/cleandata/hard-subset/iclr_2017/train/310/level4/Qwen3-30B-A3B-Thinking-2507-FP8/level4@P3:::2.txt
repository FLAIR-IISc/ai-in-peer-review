

This paper presents a compelling framework for training language models through multi-agent referential games, addressing a critical limitation of current passive learning approaches. The authors demonstrate that simple neural networks can develop effective communication protocols through interactive coordination in referential games, where senders and receivers must identify target images from pairs using arbitrary symbols. Crucially, they show that environmental modifications (e.g., removing low-level visual common knowledge) and grounding via supervised image labeling significantly improve the semantic coherence and human interpretability of the emergent language. The work bridges theoretical linguistics, game theory, and practical AI development with clear empirical validation.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper makes a strong contribution to interactive language learning, with two key strengths supporting acceptance: (1) it provides the first rigorous demonstration that *tabula rasa* agents can develop communication protocols aligned with high-level semantic properties without pre-coded language, and (2) its grounding strategy via supervised image labeling successfully bridges emergent agent language to human-interpretable terms, as validated through crowdsourced human evaluation.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper directly addresses the core challenge of functional language learning by moving beyond supervised data to interactive multi-agent environments. The referential game setup effectively models the *purpose* of language (coordination), not just its structure. Key evidence includes:  
- Agents achieve near-perfect coordination within 10k training rounds (Table 1), with the informed sender outperforming the agnostic variant in symbol diversity and semantic purity (Figure 2, Table 1).  
- Semantic analysis via SVD (Figure 2, right) and t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) confirms that symbols correlate with high-level categories (e.g., "living vs. non-living" even with a 2-symbol vocabulary), significantly exceeding chance purity (70% vs. 33% chance).  
- The grounding approach (Section 5) is particularly impactful: supervised training on ImageNet labels increases symbol purity to 70% and enables human interpretation (68% accuracy in crowdsourcing), with agents generalizing to novel images via metonymic reasoning (Figure 4). This addresses the paper’s central goal of "developing machines that communicate productively with humans."

The methodology is scientifically rigorous, with careful ablation studies (e.g., object-level reference in Section 4.1), appropriate baselines (chance purity comparisons), and transparent hyperparameters. The work is well-placed in the literature, extending Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) and Foerster et al. (2016) by focusing on *semantic alignment* rather than just coordination. While the paper acknowledges related work (e.g., "Wizard-of-Oz" approaches), it clearly distinguishes its contribution: a scalable paradigm where humans are *not* required for every interaction.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
1. **Figure 1 Clarity**: The architecture diagram (Figure 1) requires a detailed caption explaining the "game-specific embedding" layers, filter operations (especially for the informed sender), and how image order is handled. The current description (Section 3) is sufficient, but the figure needs to explicitly map these components to the text.  
2. **SVD Analysis**: The claim that "the language still requires multiple dimensions to summarize" (Figure 2, right) needs stronger justification. Please clarify how the cross-validated SVD dimensionality (50) was determined and why this implies *non-redundant* symbol usage (vs. synonymy).  
3. **Human Evaluation**: The crowdsourcing protocol (Section 5) should specify how "human interpretability" was measured beyond 68% accuracy (e.g., inter-rater reliability, error analysis for misclassifications).  

**Clarifying Questions for Authors**  
1. In the grounding experiment (Section 5), how does the shared embedding layer *specifically* transfer semantic knowledge from supervised classification to the referential game? Does the agent ever use supervised labels *during* game play, or only via the shared embedding?  
2. The paper notes that agents using only two symbols still achieve "living vs. non-living" categorization. How does this align with the authors’ claim that "symbols are assigned to meanings that make intuitive sense"? Is this a limitation or a feature of the approach?  
3. For the ReferItGame extension (Section 5), the paper states agents "used all 100 available symbols." Was this due to the larger image set, or was the vocabulary size adjusted?  

This work provides a vital step toward interactive language learning and makes a compelling case for multi-agent games as a foundation for human-compatible communication. The grounding strategy is particularly promising for future applications. With minor clarifications to the methodology and analysis, this paper will be a valuable contribution to the field.
