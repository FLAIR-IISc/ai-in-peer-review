- Proposes a multi-agent referential game where a sender sends a discrete symbol about an image pair and a receiver must pick the target, learning communication from scratch.
- Compares an “agnostic” sender to an “informed” sender with a per-dimension inductive bias; both learn communication but the informed sender converges faster and uses a richer vocabulary.
- Emergent symbols correlate with higher-level semantic properties (measured via cluster purity against human categories and SVD of symbol–pair usage).
- Showing sender and receiver different instances of the same class reduces reliance on low-level cues and pushes symbols toward object-level semantics, improving purity.
- Mixing self-play with supervised object naming partially grounds symbols, improving interpretability and yielding some success with human partners on out-of-domain images.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Addresses an important question with a clean, well-motivated setup and consistent empirical support.
- Key reason 2: Analyses and environment manipulations show how to bias emergent codes toward human-interpretable semantics and how minimal supervision aids grounding.
- Problem significance: Positions work at the intersection of multi-agent RL, language emergence, and grounding, arguing supervised passive learning is insufficient for interactive language.
- Methodological clarity: Experimental setup, architectures, and training are clearly described; informed vs agnostic comparison is insightful.
- Empirical rigor: Agents reach near-perfect coordination and the analytic methods (purity, SVD) are sensible probes of code structure and redundancy.
- Grounding/human eval: Combining self-play with supervised naming increases interpretability and produces nontrivial human–agent performance.
- Main limitation: When targets and distractors are from different categories, task can collapse to image classification—communication may simply encode classes—and could be more sample-efficiently trained than with REINFORCE.
- Partial mitigation: Authors use same-class trials and grounding/human evaluation, but stronger controls (non-communicative baselines) are needed.
- Suggestion: Include supervised/discriminative baselines (e.g., pair classifier or matching network) and report accuracy and sample complexity against RL.
- Suggestion: Report proportion of same-class vs different-class games and analyze performance and symbol usage separately for each regime.
- Suggestion: Provide variance across random seeds for convergence speed, vocabulary size, and purity; clarify cross-validated SVD rank stability.
- Suggestion: Report confidence intervals/significance for purity improvements and consider additional metrics (NMI/ARI) or category-level confusion analyses.
- Suggestion: Probe code semantics beyond majority clustering—analyze synonymy, polysemy, symbol co-usage, and context sensitivity with different distractors.
- Suggestion: Expand human study details: participant instructions, inter-rater variability, error analyses, and examples of systematic failures.
- Suggestion: Discuss extensions to multi-symbol messages or multi-turn interaction to address classification-like collapse and enable compositional references.
- Question 1: What fraction of training/testing pairs have target and distractor from the same semantic category, and can you break down accuracy/purity by this split?
- Question 2: How sensitive are convergence speed, vocabulary size, and purity to temperature τ and vocabulary size, and do seeds show collapses to few symbols?
- Question 3: What exact procedure was used for cross-validated rank selection in the SVD and how stable is inferred dimensionality across runs?
- Question 4: Can you provide comparisons to non-communicative baselines (pairwise classifier or fixed codebook) for accuracy and sample efficiency?
- Question 5: In the object-level reference setting, do symbols align more with ImageNet classes or with broader visual similarity clusters—any confusion matrices?
- Question 6: In human eval, did success rely on metonymic/contextual associations vs direct denotations, and which symbol types generalize better to ReferItGame images?
