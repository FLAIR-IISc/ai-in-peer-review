1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a framework for learning language through multi-agent interaction in referential games, where two neural agents must communicate to identify a target image among distractors. The core contributions are:
- Demonstrating that two simple networks trained with REINFORCE can reliably develop a communication protocol from scratch to solve referential games with real images.
- Comparing two sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed), showing faster convergence and richer symbol use with an inductive bias for dimension-wise comparisons.
- Providing evidence that emergent symbols correlate with higher-level object semantics (e.g., category-level structure, living vs non-living), not only low-level visual features.
- Introducing a manipulation of “common knowledge” (showing different instances of the same class to sender and receiver) that nudges the emergent code toward more abstract, class-level meanings.
- Grounding the emergent code in natural language by interleaving the game with a supervised image-labeling task, yielding much higher symbol purity and partial interpretability by humans.
- Conducting a crowd study indicating that, even when supervised labels are only indirectly related to the images, humans can identify the intended referents 68% of the time based on the sender’s symbol, suggesting promising bridges to human communication.

2. Decision
Accept. The work offers a clear, well-motivated, and empirically supported step toward interactive, language-using agents. The integration of learning-by-interaction with light supervision for grounding, the use of realistic image inputs, and the human evaluation together make this a timely and impactful contribution.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses how agents can learn to communicate in service of a task, and how to shape environments so that emergent symbols align with human-interpretable semantics and, ultimately, with natural language.
- Is the approach well motivated and situated in the literature? The motivation—learning language as a tool for coordination rather than purely from passive text—is compelling. The paper situates itself relative to multi-agent communication (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al.; Foerster et al.), emergent language/game-theoretic signaling, and supervised NLP/vision. The added focus on interpretability and human grounding is a differentiating strength.
- Are the claims supported? Empirical results are consistent and rigorous within scope:
  - Convergence: Agents achieve near-perfect coordination across architectures and vocab sizes.
  - Semantics: Symbol–object associations show above-chance purity with respect to 20 semantic categories, and t-SNE plots qualitatively support clustering by higher-level visual/semantic similarity. The “living vs. non-living” split with only two symbols is particularly intriguing.
  - Environment shaping: Removing low-level common knowledge increases symbol purity, as hypothesized.
  - Grounding: Interleaved supervised labeling substantially increases symbol purity (to ~70%), leads the sender to use many more symbols, and yields partial human interpretability (47% exact match on supervised classes and 68% success on a separate dataset where labels are often only metonymically related).
- Methodological soundness: The training setup (REINFORCE with Gibbs sampling, VGG features, careful train/test split of games) is appropriate. Analyses (SVD for redundancy, purity for cluster quality, logistic mixed-effects for crowd study) are reasonable and generally align with the claims.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarity of architecture and Figure 1: The sender architectures are nonstandard and the current diagram omits key connections. Please add a precise, self-contained caption and ensure the figure matches the textual description (e.g., how the 1-D convolutions operate over the two image embeddings, kernel sizes, channel organization, and how scores over symbols are produced). A compact pseudocode block for both senders and the receiver would greatly help reproducibility.
- “Tabula rasa” vs. pretrained perception: While the communication channel is learned from scratch, visual inputs come from a pretrained VGG. Please clarify this distinction more explicitly and discuss its implications for claims about learning “from scratch.”
- Evaluation metrics: Alongside purity, consider reporting normalized mutual information or adjusted Rand index; also report confidence intervals or variability across random seeds. For the SVD analysis, provide more detail (matrix construction, normalization, rank selection criterion).
- Baselines and ablations: Include a no-communication or random-symbol baseline (already implied by chance) and ablations on vocabulary size, temperature, and sampling vs. argmax at test time. Learning curves with variance across seeds would strengthen the robustness claims.
- Generalization and conventionality: Test cross-play (agents trained independently asked to communicate without joint fine-tuning) to assess whether the emergent code converges to a shared convention. Evaluate generalization to unseen object classes and out-of-distribution images.
- Human study details: Report more on the crowd experiment—participant demographics/filters, control conditions (e.g., shuffled words), per-word performance variance, and example failure cases—to help interpret the 68% success rate.
- Related work positioning: Expand the comparison to prior emergent communication work (including differences in input realism, grounding, and human evaluation) and to contemporary referential game studies.

5. Questions for the authors
- Architecture
  - For the informed sender, can you formalize the convolutional operation and explain why a 2×1 kernel across embeddings is preferable to, e.g., bilinear terms or attention over dimensions?
  - How sensitive are results to the number of filters, embedding dimensionality, and temperature τ?
- Training and evaluation
  - Do you sample or take argmax at test time for the sender/receiver? How does this choice affect success and symbol usage?
  - How stable are symbol purity and usage across random seeds? Please provide mean ± std over multiple runs.
  - Can you report cross-play performance between independently trained agents?
- Object-level reference experiment
  - Please clarify the setup: do sender and receiver see different instances for both target and distractor classes in every game? Are class labels strictly matched or only approximately (e.g., via WordNet synsets)?
  - Does the manipulation reduce reliance on low-level features as intended, or do agents exploit other shared biases (e.g., background cues)?
- Grounding with supervision
  - How much does performance depend on the overlap between supervised labels and game concepts? What happens with fewer labels or noisy labels?
  - In the ReferIt-based study, did you include control trials with mismatched or random words to calibrate the 68% human success rate?
  - Can you quantify “metonymic” uses (e.g., word–image embedding similarity) and provide systematic examples?
- Future directions
  - Have you tried multi-symbol messages or interactive back-and-forth protocols? Do these increase compositionality or human interpretability?
  - Do agents exhibit compositional behavior (e.g., reusing symbols across combinations of attributes) in any setting?

Overall, this is a strong and promising paper that meaningfully advances interactive, grounded language learning. The suggestions above are intended to improve clarity and deepen the empirical evaluation, not to detract from the main contributions.
