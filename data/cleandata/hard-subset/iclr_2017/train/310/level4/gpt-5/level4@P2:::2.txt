Summary
The paper proposes a framework for learning language through multi-agent communication, instantiated as referential games between a sender and a receiver. Agents start tabula rasa and must coordinate to identify a target image using a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary. The paper shows: (i) two simple neural architectures can reliably learn to communicate; (ii) environmental design (e.g., using different images of the same class for sender and receiver) nudges the emergent code toward higher-level, human-like semantics; and (iii) mixing interactive self-play with supervised image labeling grounds the agents’ symbols in natural language and improves human interpretability. Empirically, agents reach near-perfect coordination, symbol usage partially aligns with semantic categories, and supervised grounding substantially increases symbol purity and enables humans to use the sender’s words to pick the correct image at above-chance rates. This is a clear, promising step toward interactive language learning and human-AI communication.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Convincing empirical evidence that simple agents can develop effective communication protocols, and that environment design and light supervision substantially improve semantic alignment and interpretability.
- The framework is timely and general, with strong potential for future extensions to richer interactive language settings.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles how to induce communication protocols that are functional (not just structural) by learning through interaction, addressing a core limitation of purely supervised approaches to conversational agents. The focus on portability to new partners and human interpretability is well motivated.
- Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself within multi-agent communication and signaling games and acknowledges recent neural approaches without pre-coded protocols. While not exhaustive, the positioning is sensible and highlights the novel emphasis on steering emergent codes toward human semantics and grounding via mixed objectives.
- Claims and evidence: Results show rapid convergence to high coordination across architectures and settings. The informed sender uses a richer symbol inventory and yields higher cluster purity, suggesting more semantically coherent codes. The object-level setup (distinct exemplars per class for sender/receiver) appropriately removes low-level common knowledge and increases semantic alignment. The combined supervised/game training boosts symbol purity markedly and improves human interpretability (68% correct selection on ReferIt-derived pairs even when direct category matches are rare), supporting the claim that light supervised grounding aids human communication. Analyses (SVD of symbol usage, t-SNE visualizations) further support non-trivial structure in the learned code.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Clarity of architecture and Figure 1: The current diagram omits connections and, together with the dense description, is hard to parse. Please add a detailed caption, explicitly indicate inputs/embeddings, convolution shapes, and where sampling occurs. A schematic for both sender variants and the receiver would help.
- Training details and stability: Provide variance across random seeds, learning curves with confidence intervals, and any variance-reduction techniques for REINFORCE (e.g., baselines). Justify the choice of temperature τ=10 and discuss sensitivity to τ and vocabulary size.
- Baselines and ablations: Include baselines such as continuous communication (no discretization), supervised-only sender, or pre-trained semantic embeddings, and ablations on the informed sender (e.g., remove per-dimension convolutions) to isolate what drives performance and purity.
- Quantify symbol usage: Report effective vocabulary size over time, synonymy/polysemy measures, and mutual information between symbols and categories. Complement SVD with explicit dimensionality estimates and redundancy metrics.
- Generalization: Report performance and interpretability on unseen concepts/classes and out-of-domain images more systematically. Clarify whether supervised labels overlap with the game’s concept set and how this affects generalization.
- Human study details: Add more details on instructions, quality control, and inter-annotator variability. Consider alternative prompts (e.g., free-text paraphrases) to probe what humans think the sender’s words mean.
- Literature: Expand the discussion of related neural communication work and classic signaling-game equilibria, relating observed behaviors (e.g., two-symbol “living vs. non-living” split) to theory.

Questions for the authors
- Architecture and sampling:
  - What variance-reduction methods were used for REINFORCE? Any learned baseline or entropy regularization?
  - How sensitive are results to the temperature τ and vocabulary size K? Do larger vocabularies consistently improve purity/interpretability?
  - In the receiver, do you always sample at training and take argmax at test, and does this gap affect performance?
- Informed vs. agnostic sender:
  - Can you quantify the benefit of the per-dimension convolutional inductive bias beyond symbol count (e.g., purity gains controlling for effective vocab size)?
  - How robust is the informed sender advantage across different visual features (sm vs. fc) and seeds?
- Object-level reference:
  - In the “different exemplars” setup, do you ensure no leakage via low-level cues (e.g., image borders or positional hints)? How do you confirm coordination is not based on spurious cues?
- Supervised grounding:
  - What is the overlap between supervised labels and game categories? Do you test generalization when labels are disjoint from game classes?
  - How often does the sender use supervised labels metonymically vs. denotationally, and can you characterize common shift types?
- Human evaluation:
  - Did you analyze per-word performance (which words generalize best)? Any correlation with visual concreteness or frequency?
  - Could a human-in-the-loop receiver learn rapidly from a few interactions with the sender? Any preliminary evidence?
- Reproducibility:
  - Will you release code, trained models, and the exact image splits/pairs to facilitate replication?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and impactful contribution that demonstrates interactive emergence of communication and a practical path toward human interpretability.
