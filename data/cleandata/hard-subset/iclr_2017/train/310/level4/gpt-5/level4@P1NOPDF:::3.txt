This paper proposes a referential game between two agents. Both agents observe two images. The first agent (the sender) receives a binary target variable t and must transmit a discrete symbol (a message) to the second agent (the receiver) so that the receiver can recover the target. Both agents receive a reward if the receiver predicts the target correctly. The agents are parameterized as neural networks that consume pretrained visual features of the images, and they are trained end-to-end with REINFORCE. In this setting, the agents are shown to converge to optimal policies, and the learned communication protocol (i.e., the symbolic code emitted by the sender and interpreted by the receiver) captures meaningful concepts. The paper also presents experiments on a variant of the game grounded in different image classes; in this setting the agents appear to learn even more meaningful concepts. Finally, the authors study a multi-game setup in which the sender alternates between playing the referential game and a supervised image-classification task. As expected, when the communication is anchored to the supervised task, the learned symbols align even more closely with meaningful concepts.

Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setting is an interesting direction. It is substantially more difficult than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which justifies beginning with a relatively simple task. To the best of my knowledge, the idea of first inducing communication between two agents and then grounding it in human language is novel. As the authors note, this may offer an alternative to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to emphasize the statistical regularities of language rather than its functional use. I find the proposed task and framework, along with the analyses and visualizations of what the messages represent, to be a useful stepping stone for future work. For these reasons, I recommend acceptance.
