- Paper studies emergence of discrete communication between neural agents via a referential game with real images and REINFORCE-trained senders/receivers.
- Experimental setup: sender sees two images + target indicator, emits one symbol from a fixed vocabulary, receiver picks the target.
- Main empirical findings: agents reliably coordinate without a pre-specified code.
- Finding: an “informed” sender with a comparator bias converges faster and uses a richer symbol subset than an “agnostic” sender.
- Finding: induced symbols show non-trivial semantic structure, align with intuitive categories above chance, and cluster in CNN visual space.
- Finding: showing different exemplars to sender and receiver (modifying common knowledge) pushes codes toward category-level semantics.
- Finding: mixing self-play with supervised object naming grounds symbols in natural language and improves human interpretability (human accuracy ~68%).
- Overall verdict: clear, timely contribution with thoughtful empirical analysis and useful human evaluation — weak accept.
- Key reason: novel, well-motivated empirical demonstration of emergent discrete communication on real images with insightful semantic and human-grounding analyses.
- Key reason: hybrid self-play + supervised grounding is simple yet compelling and improves interpretability.
- Methodological strength: clean training setup using discrete sampling and REINFORCE, principled environment manipulations to encourage abstraction.
- Empirical rigor: analyses include coordination accuracy, symbol usage diversity, SVD of symbol–pair matrices, category purity, and t-SNE visualizations.
- Addressed concerns: “object-level reference” variant reduces reliance on low-level image idiosyncrasies; grounding experiment increases purity and enables human interpretation beyond supervised labels.
- Impact and clarity: task simplicity aids analysis and findings are likely to stimulate work on compositionality, grounding, and human–AI communication.
- Caveat: task can reduce to classification when target/distractor are different classes, making RL potentially inefficient versus supervised baselines.
- Caveat: limited probing of generalization and compositionality—no zero-shot, multi-step dialogs, or compositionality tests reported.
- Suggested improvement: report symbol usage stats across seeds/vocab sizes, entropy, mutual information, and type–token distributions.
- Suggested baseline: include supervised sender + learned receiver and continuous-communication baselines to quantify interactive training benefits.
- Suggested generalization tests: harder distractors (same superordinate/fine-grained siblings), out-of-distribution pairs, and same-class attribute differences.
- Suggested human study improvements: provide CIs, effect sizes, sampling details, per-word success/failure analysis, and correlation with embedding similarity.
- Suggested training analyses: report variance over seeds, learning curves, sensitivity to temperature, embedding size, vocab size, training stability, and runtime.
- Suggested architecture ablation: further isolate what makes the “informed” sender effective (e.g., comparator variants).
- Reproducibility request: release code and data splits and standardize references.
- Questions: how do performance, symbol purity, and interpretability vary with vocabulary size (e.g., 5–500)?
- Questions: do symbols encode attributes when both images are same class but differ in attributes (color, pose, parts)?
- Questions: how robust are results across seeds and alternative CNN features/layers/backbones?
- Questions: can you quantitatively compare against a supervised sender + learned receiver baseline?
- Questions: does supervised/self-play grounding cause systematic meaning drift or metonymy beyond conventional denotations?
- Questions: have you tried multi-symbol or multi-turn communication to elicit compositionality under stronger bottlenecks?
- Questions: how do you prevent degenerate equilibria tied to dataset idiosyncrasies, and is the different-exemplar trick sufficient?
