- Paper proposes training language-capable agents via referential multi-agent games where a sender sends one discrete symbol to a receiver to identify a target image.  
- Simple neural agents (agnostic and informed senders) learn to communicate from scratch; the informed sender converges faster and uses more of the vocabulary.  
- Emergent symbols align above chance with intuitive conceptual categories (e.g., object classes, living vs. non-living).  
- Changing game common knowledge from image-level to object-level nudges agents toward more abstract, category-level semantics.  
- Interleaving self-play with supervised image labeling grounds symbols, increasing symbol purity and enabling many symbols to map to conventional labels.  
- Initial human evaluation shows humans can often recover the sender’s intended referent from produced words, indicating promising human-agent compatibility.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: clear framework demonstrating emergent communication on real images, and a practical grounding mechanism that improves interpretability and human compatibility.  
- Agents achieve high coordination accuracy across architectures; informed sender converges faster and uses a broader vocabulary.  
- Symbol-category purity is reliably above chance; emergence of living vs. non-living distinction is cognitively interesting.  
- Object-level game design reduces reliance on low-level image cues and increases semantic abstraction, supported by clustering and t-SNE.  
- Interleaved supervised naming boosts symbol purity, increases symbol usage, and yields direct interpretability.  
- Crowdsourced evaluation (68% success; significant logistic regression) indicates learned code carries semantics exploitable by humans beyond supervised categories.  
- Contribution is timely: combines realistic visual inputs, controlled manipulations for semantics, and a simple grounding approach missing in prior work.
