Summary:
This paper studies emergent communication in a simple referential game between two agents. Both agents observe two images; the sender receives a binary target variable t and sends a discrete symbol to the receiver, who must infer t. Agents are parameterized as neural networks operating on pretrained image features and are trained end-to-end with REINFORCE. The authors analyze the learned communication protocol and show that symbols correlate with semantically meaningful concepts. They further introduce a variant of the game grounded in image classes, which yields symbols with clearer semantic structure, and a multi-task setting in which the sender alternates between the referential game and supervised image classification. Grounding the sender in supervised labels leads to even more interpretable symbols.

Strengths:
- Timely and interesting problem: learning to communicate in multi-agent settings is a compelling direction, and starting from a controlled referential game is appropriate.
- Novel angle: learning communication first and then grounding it in human language differs from standard sequence-to-sequence approaches and highlights functional aspects of communication.
- Empirical evidence: across variants, the learned discrete codes exhibit nontrivial structure and align with high-level visual concepts, supporting the central claim.
- Clarity of the setup: the referential game is well-motivated, easy to understand, and amenable to analysis.
- Potential impact: the framework and analyses offer a useful stepping stone for future work on emergent communication and its alignment with human language.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Methodological clarity: Some implementation details are underspecified and limit reproducibility and interpretability.
  - How exactly is the binary target t incorporated into the sender? Is it concatenated to the sender’s visual embedding, injected via a gating mechanism, or used to select between two encoders? Please specify the architecture, the point of injection, and any normalization used.
  - What is the size of the symbol vocabulary and the message length? Are messages single tokens only, or can they be sequences? If sequences, what is the decoding/training protocol (e.g., straight-through, Gumbel-Softmax, sampling with REINFORCE)?
  - For REINFORCE, what variance-reduction strategies are used (baselines, entropy regularization)? Reporting these would help contextualize stability and convergence.
- Claims of optimality: The paper states that agents converge to optimal policies. Given the stochastic optimization and finite training time, this seems too strong. “Effective” or “near-optimal under the trained conditions” would be more precise unless accompanied by a proof or an exhaustive search baseline.
- Evaluation depth: While the analyses are insightful, it would be helpful to quantify symbol semantics beyond purity (e.g., mutual information with class labels, systematicity/compositionality metrics, or generalization to out-of-distribution distractors).
- Dependence on pretrained features: Since perception is offloaded to fixed image encoders, it remains unclear how much the observed semantics arise from the communication dynamics versus inherited structure in the visual features. A brief discussion or ablation (e.g., training with weaker or random features) would strengthen the claims.

Minor comments and corrections:
- Clarify how t is incorporated into the sender network (see above).
- Table formatting and percentages:
  - Tables 1 and 2 appear to use percentages inconsistently: one in [0, 100] and the other in [0, 1]. Please unify the convention (preferably [0, 100] with “%”).
  - In Table 1, the “obs-chance purity” column shows extremely small values that look like a scaling or formatting error. Please verify.
- Typos:
  - “assest” → “assess”
  - “usufal” → “usual”
- Reporting:
  - Include random seeds, number of runs, and mean ± standard deviation for key metrics.
  - Specify training details (optimizer, learning rates, batch sizes, number of steps/epochs).
  - If possible, release code or pseudocode for the training loop.

Overall assessment:
This paper offers a clean, well-motivated framework for studying emergent communication, with convincing qualitative and quantitative evidence that meaningful symbol usage can arise even in simple referential games. The progression from basic to class-grounded and multi-task settings is natural and illuminating; in particular, anchoring the sender in a supervised task strengthens the semantic alignment of the learned codes, which is promising for bridging agent communication and human-interpretable language. While some methodological and reporting details should be clarified and a few claims tempered, these issues are addressable.

Recommendation: Accept. The work presents a novel and relevant perspective on emergent communication, provides substantive analyses, and lays groundwork for future research connecting learned communication protocols with human language.
