The paper introduces a two-agent referential game. Both agents view a pair of images; the sender is informed which image is the target (via a binary variable t) and must emit a single symbol from a fixed vocabulary so that the receiver can infer the target. Both agents receive a reward if the receiver correctly identifies the target. The authors parameterize both agents with neural networks, use pretrained image features as inputs, and train with REINFORCE. They show that the agents converge to high-performing policies and that the emergent communication protocol (the sender’s symbolic code) organizes around semantically meaningful concepts. They further evaluate a variant where sender and receiver see different images from the same classes, which appears to steer the learned code toward more abstract, interpretable semantics. Finally, they explore a multi-task setting where the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task; as expected, grounding with supervised labels yields symbols that align even more closely with human-interpretable concepts.

Pursuing shared representations for communication in multi-agent systems is a promising direction. This setting is considerably more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which motivates beginning with a controlled, simple game. To my knowledge, the pipeline of first inducing agent–agent communication and then grounding it in natural language is novel. As the authors note, this offers a complementary paradigm to sequence-to-sequence approaches that primarily model distributional properties of language rather than its functional use. I find the task and framework, together with the analyses and visualizations of symbol meaning, to be a valuable step toward interactive language use. I recommend acceptance.
