- Paper studies training NL systems via multi-agent interactive referential communication games, proposing alternating game-based interaction with supervised learning to stabilize and ground emergent communication.
- Motivation is clear: framing language learning as coordination in interactive environments is intuitive and well-justified.
- Hybrid training strategy (switching between interactive games and supervised learning) appears effective and extensible.
- Framework is versatile: Section 2’s general formulation seems applicable to varied tasks and agent configurations.
- Empirical results are reasonable and consistent with claims, suggesting scalability to larger settings.
- Figure 1 is confusing and omits important network connections; add a detailed caption or revise into clearer subfigures with legend.
- Section 3 is hard to follow; provide an end-to-end episode narrative (inputs → messages → actions → losses → updates).
- Clarify which parameters are shared, message dimensionality and encoding/decoding, and precise training objectives for each component.
- Include pseudocode for the training loop showing alternation between supervised and interactive phases.
- State optimization details and stability techniques (e.g., entropy regularization, curriculum, variance-reduction baselines).
- Positioning needs strengthening: more clearly differentiate from prior multi-agent emergent-communication work in task complexity, signals, scalability, or stability.
- Quantify hybrid training benefits versus purely supervised and purely interactive baselines.
- Perform ablations on message dimensionality, vocabulary size, and agent parameter sharing to assess sensitivity and communication efficiency.
- Add qualitative analysis showing examples of learned messages/protocols and their relation to task semantics.
- Test generalization/transfer to unseen objects/attributes or game variants to evaluate robustness.
- Framework has strong impact potential; integrating supervision with emergent protocols could enable more grounded and compositional behaviors in richer tasks.
- Overall assessment: well-motivated and promising contribution, but main shortcoming is clarity—especially Figure 1 and Section 3—which should be revised to improve readability and impact.
- Reviewer is confident in understanding methods/results but less certain about novelty relative to closest emergent communication literature.
