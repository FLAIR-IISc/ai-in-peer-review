In this work, the authors introduce a referential game between two agents. Both agents see two images. The first agent (the sender) is given a binary target variable (t) and must transmit a symbol (a message) to the second agent (the receiver) so that the receiver can infer the target. Both agents receive a reward if the receiver correctly predicts the target. The agents are instantiated as neural networks—using pretrained image representations as feature vectors—and trained with REINFORCE. In this setting, the authors show that the agents converge to optimal policies and that the emergent communication (i.e., the symbol sent by the sender) encodes meaningful concepts. The paper further evaluates a variant of the game grounded in distinct image classes, where the induced concepts appear even more interpretable. Finally, a multi-game setup is explored in which the sender alternates between playing the referential game and a supervised image classification task. As expected, when anchored by the supervised task, the emergent symbols convey even more semantically meaningful content.

Pursuing shared representations for communication in multi-agent systems is a compelling direction. This is substantially more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which supports beginning with a relatively simple environment. To my knowledge, the idea of first learning communication between two agents and then grounding it in human language is novel. As the authors note, this could offer an alternative paradigm to conventional sequence-to-sequence approaches that emphasize statistical regularities over functional use. I find the proposed task and framework, along with the analysis and visualization of token semantics, to be a valuable stepping stone for future work. Accordingly, I recommend acceptance.
