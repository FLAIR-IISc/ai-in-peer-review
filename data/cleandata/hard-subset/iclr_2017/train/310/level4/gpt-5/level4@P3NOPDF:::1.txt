Summary
This paper studies the emergence of communication between two learning agents in a referential game. The setup is intentionally simple to enable post-hoc analysis of the learned protocol. A key observation is that “informed” agents (with access to richer prior knowledge or features) tend to use a larger set of symbols, whereas “agnostic” agents converge to using very few symbols. The work aims to shed light on whether machine-developed communication resembles human-like problem solving.

Strengths
- Timely and important question: understanding how multi-agent communication emerges in learning systems is central to progress in interactive and embodied AI.
- The task design is simple and interpretable, which facilitates analysis of learned symbols and protocols.
- The contrast between “informed” and “agnostic” agents is intriguing, with suggestive evidence that prior knowledge shapes the richness of emergent codes.

Weaknesses
- The current task appears to reduce to image classification in many cases, particularly when the two images are drawn from different categories. In such settings, the optimal “message” can collapse to the class label of the target image, and the receiver can learn a straightforward mapping from symbols to classes.
- Because of this reduction, the problem can be framed as transfer learning or supervised classification rather than reinforcement learning. Without careful controls, the paper risks conflating emergent communication with simple label transmission.
- The paper lacks strong baselines that would calibrate the benefits and costs of the RL-based approach. For example, a supervised classifier that predicts image categories coupled with a trivial mapping to messages could plausibly achieve similar performance more efficiently.
- The analysis of the emergent code seems preliminary. It would be helpful to quantify symbol usage, stability across random seeds, mutual information between symbols and semantic attributes, and generalization to out-of-distribution images or novel object combinations.
- The distinction between “informed” and “agnostic” agents is not sufficiently precise. It is unclear exactly what prior information, features, or architectural biases differ between the two, and how these differences drive symbol diversity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Make the task robust to the “class label” shortcut:
  - Use distractors from the same fine-grained category (e.g., two dogs) where simple class naming is insufficient.
  - Require discrimination based on attributes, relations, or context (e.g., “the larger dog,” “the red car,” “the object left of the chair”).
  - Evaluate few-shot and zero-shot generalization to novel attribute combinations to probe compositionality.
- Add competitive baselines and ablations:
  - Supervised/transfer-learning baselines that map images to class labels, then to messages, with and without frozen visual backbones.
  - Non-communicative and one-shot lookup baselines to establish lower bounds.
  - Ablations on vocabulary size, message length, channel noise, and perceptual encoder pretraining (to isolate what drives symbol diversity).
- Deepen protocol analysis:
  - Report symbol entropy, type-token ratios, mutual information with class labels and attributes, and cluster purity of symbol-to-concept mappings.
  - Test cross-run consistency: do symbols align across seeds or are they idiosyncratic?
  - Probe human alignment: can human annotators guess image targets from symbols after a small number of examples?
- Clarify methodology and setup details:
  - Precisely define “informed” vs. “agnostic” agents (e.g., pretraining, access to labels or features, architectural priors).
  - Provide dataset composition, the distribution over same-category vs. different-category pairs, vocabulary size, and message constraints.
  - Report training stability, sample efficiency, and wall-clock time compared to supervised/transfer baselines.
- Consider richer games:
  - Multi-step dialogues to test whether agents develop compositional or hierarchical codes over sequences.
  - Referential games with multiple candidate targets and distractors that share high-level categories but differ in attributes or relations.

Minor comments
- Edit for grammar and clarity (e.g., “whereas” instead of “where as”; consistent use of “agents,” “symbols,” and “messages”).
- Briefly motivate why reinforcement learning is preferable in this setting, and under what conditions it yields benefits over supervised alternatives.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important question using a commendably transparent experimental setup, and the observation about symbol diversity across agent priors is interesting. However, as currently framed, the task often collapses to image classification, making it difficult to attribute observed behaviors to genuinely emergent communication rather than label transmission. The absence of strong supervised/transfer baselines and more rigorous analysis of the learned code further limits the strength of the conclusions.

With tighter task design that removes trivial shortcuts, comprehensive baselines, and deeper quantitative analyses of the protocols and their generalization, the paper could make a solid contribution. In its current form, I lean toward a weak reject, primarily due to the risk of task reduction and the lack of comparative baselines, but I believe the work can be significantly strengthened by addressing the points above.
