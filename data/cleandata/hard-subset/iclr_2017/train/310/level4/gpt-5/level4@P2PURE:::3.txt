This paper proposes a referential game between two agents. Both agents observe two images. The first agent (the sender) receives a binary target variable t and must send a symbol (message) to the second agent (the receiver) so that the receiver can recover the target. Both agents receive a reward if the receiver correctly predicts the target. The agents are parameterized as neural networks—using pretrained image representations as feature vectors—and trained with REINFORCE. In this setting, the agents are shown to converge to optimal policies, and their learned communication (i.e., the symbol transmitted from sender to receiver) reflects meaningful concepts. The paper also presents experiments on a variant of the game grounded in different image classes; in this variant, the agents appear to learn even more meaningful concepts. Finally, a multi-game setup is proposed in which the sender alternates between playing the referential game and a supervised learning task (image classification). As expected, when anchored by the supervised task, the learned symbolic communication aligns even more strongly with meaningful concepts.

Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setup is an interesting direction. It is substantially harder than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which justifies starting with a relatively simple task. To the best of my knowledge, the approach of first learning communication between two agents and then grounding this communication in human language is novel. As the authors note, this may offer an alternative paradigm to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to emphasize the statistical properties of language rather than its functional aspects. I believe the contributions of the proposed task and framework, along with the analysis and visualization of what the communicated tokens represent, are a useful stepping stone for future work. For this reason, I recommend acceptance.
