Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a multi-agent framework for learning to communicate through referential games. A sender and a receiver view pairs of images; the sender sends a discrete symbol and the receiver must select the target image. Starting tabula rasa, the agents learn a communication protocol that enables coordination.
- The authors compare an “agnostic” sender to an “informed” sender with an inductive bias to compare images dimension-by-dimension. Both agents learn to communicate reliably; the informed sender converges faster and uses a richer symbol inventory.
- They analyze the emergent code: symbol usage correlates with higher-level semantic properties (e.g., broad categories), measured by cluster purity relative to human semantic categories and via SVD analysis of symbol–pair usage.
- They modify the game to remove low-level common knowledge (showing different instances of the same class to sender and receiver), which nudges the semantics toward object-level categories and improves purity.
- They take a first step toward grounding: mixing self-play with supervised object naming, leading to more interpretable symbols and partial human interpretability on ReferItGame image pairs.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The paper addresses an important question—learning communication protocols in interactive settings—through a clean, well-motivated setup and demonstrates consistent empirical support for the claims. 2) The analyses and environment manipulations provide insight into how to bias emergent codes toward more human-interpretable semantics and how to ground them with minimal supervision.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and motivation: The paper is well placed at the intersection of multi-agent RL, language emergence, and grounding. It clearly articulates why passive supervised learning is insufficient for interactive language use and motivates referential games as a tractable testbed.
- Methodological clarity: The setup, architectures, and training details are clearly described. The informed vs agnostic sender comparison is thoughtful and reveals the effect of inductive bias on symbol usage and convergence speed.
- Empirical correctness and rigor: Agents reliably achieve near-perfect coordination across architectures and settings. The use of purity against McRae categories and SVD of symbol–pair matrices is a sensible way to probe the structure and redundancy of the emergent code. The “object-level reference” variant is a convincing manipulation to reduce reliance on low-level cues and push toward class-level semantics.
- Grounding and human evaluation: Combining self-play with supervised object naming increases symbol interpretability and yields nontrivial success with human partners on out-of-domain images. This is an important step toward human–agent communication.

Main limitation (not decisive for my recommendation, but important to address)
- When target and distractor are from different categories, the task risks collapsing into a form of image classification/discrimination, where symbols effectively denote classes and the receiver learns to map symbols to those classes. From this perspective, parts of the approach resemble transfer learning and might be trainable more efficiently than with REINFORCE. The paper partially addresses this via (i) same-class target/distractor setups and (ii) grounding plus human evaluation, but a stronger empirical control would compare directly against non-communicative baselines (e.g., a discriminative classifier or matching network) and assess sample efficiency.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all part of my decision)
- Baselines and efficiency: Include supervised/discriminative baselines that do not use learned communication (e.g., a classifier over the pair or a learned comparator) and report both accuracy and sample complexity relative to RL training. This would sharpen the claim that communication, rather than classification, is doing the work.
- Task composition: Explicitly report the proportion of games where target and distractor belong to the same class vs different classes, and analyze performance/symbol usage separately for these regimes.
- Robustness: Provide variance over multiple random seeds for convergence speed, symbol inventory size, and purity. Clarify the cross-validated rank in the SVD analysis.
- Purity statistics: Report confidence intervals and significance tests for purity improvements, and include additional semantic diagnostics (e.g., NMI/ARI), or controlled category-level confusion analyses.
- Code semantics: Beyond majority-symbol clustering, consider probing synonymy and polysemy through symbol co-usage patterns and context sensitivity (e.g., how symbol choice shifts with distractor identity).
- Human study details: Expand on participant instructions, inter-rater variability, and error analyses. Include examples of systematic failures to better understand where grounding falls short.
- Communication channel: Discuss extensions to multi-symbol messages or multi-turn interaction, and how these might alleviate the classification-like collapse by enabling compositional references.

Questions for the authors
1) How often, during training and testing, are target and distractor sampled from the same semantic category? Can you break down accuracy and symbol purity by same-category vs different-category trials?
2) How sensitive are convergence speed, symbol inventory size, and purity to the temperature parameter τ and vocabulary size? Did you observe phase transitions or degeneracies (e.g., collapse to 1–2 symbols) across seeds?
3) In the SVD analysis, what is the exact procedure for cross-validated rank selection, and how stable is the inferred dimensionality across runs?
4) Can you report comparisons to simple non-communicative baselines (e.g., a pairwise classifier or a fixed codebook learned with supervised signals) in terms of accuracy and sample efficiency?
5) In the object-level reference setting, do symbols align more tightly with ImageNet classes, or do they still reflect broader visual similarity clusters? Any confusion matrices illustrating this?
6) In the human evaluation, to what extent did success rely on metonymic or contextual associations vs direct denotations? Do certain symbol types (e.g., concrete nouns vs more general categories) generalize better to ReferItGame images?

Overall, this is a timely, well-executed paper that makes a solid empirical and conceptual contribution to emergent communication and grounding. Despite the concern that parts of the task can reduce to image discrimination, the paper takes meaningful steps to mitigate this and to analyze the resulting codes. I recommend acceptance.
