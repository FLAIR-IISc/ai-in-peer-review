- Proposes a framework where two neural agents learn communication from scratch via REINFORCE to solve referential games with real images.
- Shows two sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed) and finds the informed sender converges faster and uses richer symbols.
- Finds emergent symbols reflect higher-level object semantics (e.g., category structure, living vs. non-living) beyond low-level features.
- Introduces a “common knowledge” manipulation (different instances shown to sender/receiver) that promotes class-level, abstract meanings.
- Grounds emergent code in natural language by interleaving supervised image-labeling, increasing symbol purity and human interpretability.
- Reports a crowd study where humans identify referents 68% of the time from sender symbols, suggesting links to human communication.
- Recommendation: Accept — paper is timely, empirically strong, and advances interactive, grounded language learning.
- Tackles how agents can learn communication for coordination and how to shape environments so symbols align with human semantics.
- Well-motivated relative to multi-agent communication and supervised vision/NLP, emphasizing interpretability and human grounding.
- Empirical claims are supported: near-perfect coordination, above-chance category purity, qualitative t-SNE clustering, and intriguing binary splits.
- Removing low-level common knowledge increases symbol purity, matching the authors’ hypothesis.
- Interleaved supervised labeling boosts purity to ~70%, increases symbol usage, and yields partial human interpretability (47% exact match).
- Training setup and analyses (REINFORCE, VGG features, SVD, purity, mixed-effects) are appropriate and generally sound.
- Clarify sender architectures and Figure 1: add precise captions, diagram corrections, and pseudocode for reproducibility.
- Explicitly distinguish learned communication channel from pretrained visual perception (VGG) and discuss “from scratch” claims.
- Report additional evaluation metrics (NMI, ARI), confidence intervals/seed variability, and more SVD/matrix details.
- Add baselines and ablations (no-communication, vocab size, temperature, sampling vs. argmax) and show learning curves across seeds.
- Test cross-play between independently trained agents and generalization to unseen classes and OOD images to assess conventionality.
- Provide more crowd-study details: participant filters, controls, per-word variance, and example failures to contextualize the 68% rate.
- Expand related-work positioning against prior emergent communication, input realism, grounding, and human-evaluation studies.
- Questions on architecture: formalize the informed sender’s convolution, justify 2×1 kernel choice vs. alternatives, and report sensitivity to filters/embeddings/τ.
- Questions on training/eval: clarify sampling vs. argmax at test time, stability of purity/usage across seeds, and cross-play performance.
- Clarify object-level reference setup: whether sender/receiver see different instances for targets/distractors and how class matching is defined.
- Questions on grounding: effect of label overlap/noise and label quantity, inclusion of control trials in ReferIt study, and quantification of metonymic uses.
- Future directions: explore multi-symbol or interactive protocols, compositionality, and whether agents reuse symbols across attribute combinations.
