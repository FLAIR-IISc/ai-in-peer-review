1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a framework for learning language through multi-agent communication, instantiated as referential games between a sender and a receiver. Agents start tabula rasa and must coordinate to identify a target image using a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary. The authors show:
- Two simple neural agents learn to communicate effectively via REINFORCE with a discrete communication bottleneck.
- An “informed” sender architecture (with a bias to compare images dimension-wise) learns faster and uses a richer set of symbols than an “agnostic” sender.
- The emergent symbols correlate with intuitive semantic properties of images: cluster purity with respect to human categories is significantly above chance, and even a very small symbol set captures broad distinctions (e.g., living vs. non-living).
- Modifying the game to remove low-level common knowledge (showing different images of the same class to sender and receiver) nudges agents toward more abstract, class-level semantics.
- Mixing self-play with supervised object naming grounds the agents’ code in natural language: symbol usage expands, purity rises (~70%), and agents often emit the conventional label for targets. In a human study on a different dataset (ReferItGame), humans picked the intended target 68% of the time given the agent’s emitted word, suggesting partial interpretability beyond directly supervised classes.

Overall, the work provides an effective, extensible setup for emergent communication, examines how environment design shapes the semantics of the induced code, and takes initial steps toward human-interpretable grounding.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A clear, well-motivated framework demonstrating that simple agents can learn to communicate about real images, with evidence that symbols align with human-relevant semantics.
- A promising and practical grounding strategy that combines self-play with supervised labeling, producing partial human interpretability on out-of-domain images.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: How to design interactive environments in which agents develop a communication protocol that generalizes and aligns with human semantic intuitions, rather than learning passively from text. The referential game is an appropriate, minimal testbed for this goal.
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well situated relative to supervised language learning, Wizard-of-Oz setups, and prior neural emergent-communication work (e.g., Sukhbaatar et al., Foerster et al.), and connects to the signaling game/cheap talk literature. This combined perspective is compelling. Caveat: I am not deeply familiar with the closest recent emergent-communication literature, so my assessment of novelty within that niche is limited.
- Do results support claims?
  - Communication success: Agents reliably reach near-perfect coordination quickly; the informed sender converges faster, consistent with its inductive bias.
  - Semantics: Above-chance purity by mapping symbol usage to human categories; t-SNE visualizations show symbol coherence in CNN feature space; a small symbol set captures broad semantic splits (living vs. non-living).
  - Shaping via environment: Removing shared low-level details increases symbol purity and yields clearer alignment with class-level structure.
  - Grounding: Joint supervised/self-play training leads to greater symbol diversity and significantly higher purity. The human evaluation (68% accuracy) indicates the induced code is partly interpretable even when emitted words are used metonymically, a natural linguistic phenomenon. Taken together, the empirical evidence is consistent and scientifically reasonable.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of my acceptance decision)
- Figure 1 clarity: As currently drawn, some connections appear omitted, and the architecture description in Section 3 is hard to follow without a detailed, explicit diagram. Please provide a comprehensive caption and ensure the figure faithfully reflects the forward paths (inputs, embedding layers, convolution/filter operations for the informed sender, symbol sampling, and the receiver’s embedding/similarity computation).
- Architecture description: Streamline Section 3 to clearly distinguish the agnostic vs. informed sender computations, including kernel sizes, channel interpretations, and how concatenation vs. per-dimension operations differ. Explicit pseudocode or a small schematic for each module would help reproducibility.
- Reporting details: Consider adding:
  - Variability across random seeds (success rates, symbol usage diversity, purity).
  - Sensitivity to vocabulary size and temperature τ.
  - Brief ablations (e.g., without the dimensionality-by-dimension bias) to pinpoint what drives faster convergence and richer symbol usage.
- Human study: A bit more detail on pair selection, instructions, and controls would strengthen interpretability claims (e.g., how pairs were sampled per symbol; any bias from particularly salient words; whether alternatives were balanced).
- Literature: While the paper cites key strands, a more explicit comparison to the most recent neural emergent-communication works would clarify the specific advances here (e.g., scale of real-image inputs, discrete bottleneck, environment shaping, and human grounding).

Questions for the authors
- Figure 1/Section 3: Could you provide a precise specification of all connections and operations in both sender architectures (including where sigmoids are applied, how filters combine channels, and how scores map to vocab logits)?
- Stability: How consistent are convergence speed, symbol count, and purity across multiple random initializations?
- Synonymy vs. effective dimensionality: Your SVD analysis suggests multiple effective dimensions. Can you quantify synonymy (e.g., clustering symbols by usage similarity) to disentangle redundancy from genuinely distinct meanings?
- Temperature and vocabulary: How do τ and vocabulary size jointly affect symbol diversity, purity, and convergence?
- Grounding transfer: In the human study, did you analyze which supervised labels transferred best to new domains (e.g., concrete vs. scene-like terms) and which metonymic patterns were most effective?
- Supervised mix: What proportion of supervised vs. game steps is needed to achieve the observed purity/interpretability? Does more supervision help or hurt coordination/generalization?

Confidence: I am confident in my understanding of the paper, but I am not fully up to date on the most closely related emergent-communication literature, which limits my assessment of novelty within that subfield.
