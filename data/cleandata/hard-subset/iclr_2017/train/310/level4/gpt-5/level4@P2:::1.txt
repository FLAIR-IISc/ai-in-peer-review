Summary of contributions
The paper studies how communication can emerge between agents through a simple referential game. A sender sees a target and a distractor image, emits a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary, and a receiver must identify the target based on the symbol. The agents are trained end-to-end with RL (REINFORCE), starting tabula rasa. The authors compare two sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed with an inductive bias to compare image embeddings dimension-wise), analyze symbol usage, and evaluate whether the induced “meanings” align with intuitive semantics. They further modify the environment to reduce shared low-level information (different exemplars per class) to encourage higher-level semantics, and introduce a mixed training regime that alternates self-play with supervised object naming to ground symbols in natural language. They report that informed senders coordinate faster and use a larger portion of the vocabulary; symbol clusters correlate with semantic categories above chance; removing low-level common knowledge slightly increases semantic alignment; and supervised grounding boosts purity and human interpretability, with a crowdsourcing study showing nontrivial human success in guessing targets from agent-emitted labels. The paper is clear and the problem setup is simple, which makes post-hoc analysis more interpretable.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The task often collapses to image (or class) identification, so “communication” largely reduces to transmitting a class label; this makes the problem very close to transfer/supervised learning and weakens the core claim about emergent language. (2) The paper lacks strong baselines and ablations to demonstrate that RL-based, emergent communication is necessary or more sample-efficient than simpler alternatives, and it does not convincingly probe settings where nontrivial semantics beyond class identity are required.

Supporting arguments
- Trivial solution pathway: When target and distractor belong to different categories, the sender can succeed by conveying coarse class identity. The receiver can match the label to one of the two images without needing richer or compositional messages. The reported symbol-category purity and t-SNE visualizations suggest that messages mostly track category structure, not finer attributes.
- Missing hard distractors: The main training/evaluation sampling appears to draw two concepts at random. Without systematically controlled same-class or fine-grained distractors, it is hard to show that agents learn features beyond coarse category ID. The “object-level reference” setting (different exemplars per class) helps, but the gains are modest and still compatible with class-based signaling.
- Baseline gap and efficiency: There is no comparison to a simple supervised/transfer baseline (e.g., a sender that predicts the ImageNet class and transmits that discrete label through a learned channel). Such baselines could likely be trained faster and might achieve similar or better accuracy, challenging the need for RL in this setup.
- Architectural bias: The “informed” sender’s advantage may stem from its inductive bias rather than a qualitatively different emergent language. More careful ablations (e.g., removing the convolutional comparison, varying embedding sizes, temperature annealing) are needed to isolate what drives improved symbol usage.
- Grounding and human evaluation: The mixed supervised/self-play regime makes symbols more interpretable, but much of this may reflect direct label use. The 68% human success rate is promising, yet the study mixes cases where the label is present and not, with limited analysis of when and why communication succeeds (e.g., metonymy vs. literal category naming).
- Scientific rigor and generalization: The paper would benefit from reporting variance across random seeds, stability of symbol usage, cross-play (new partner) results, and tests of zero-shot generalization to unseen categories or partners to support claims about portability.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen baselines: Include (i) a purely supervised sender that predicts class labels and sends them; (ii) a transfer baseline with fixed visual features plus a small classifier; (iii) an information-theoretic objective (e.g., contrastive InfoNCE) as a non-RL alternative. Compare sample efficiency and final performance.
- Make the game harder: Systematically evaluate with distractors from the same class or closely related classes; design attribute- or relation-based referential games that cannot be solved by class ID alone; test multi-step or bidirectional communication and longer messages to probe compositionality.
- Measure compositionality and structure: Report topographic similarity between message distances and semantic/attribute distances; quantify synonymy/homonymy, entropy of symbol usage, and mutual information between messages and controlled factors (category, attributes, background).
- Analysis and robustness: Provide confidence intervals across multiple runs, sensitivity to vocabulary size and temperature, and training details (baselines for REINFORCE variance reduction, annealing schedules, early stopping).
- Human study enhancements: Separate cases where the supervised label is literally present vs. absent; analyze error types; compare to random labels or human-produced referring expressions as baselines; report per-word statistics and confidence intervals.
- Clarity: Specify the exact proportion of same-category pairs in train/test, and release code and evaluation scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- What fraction of training and test pairs are from the same high-level category, and how does performance change when distractors are same-class or visually near neighbors?
- How does a supervised sender that predicts class labels and transmits them compare in accuracy and sample efficiency? Could it match or exceed RL-based performance with far fewer iterations?
- Do agents generalize to new partners (cross-play) without fine-tuning? How stable is symbol usage across different random seeds?
- How sensitive are results to vocabulary size, temperature, and the informed sender’s convolutional comparison? Does increased vocabulary automatically inflate purity without improving generalization?
- In the grounding setup, when the supervised label is absent from the scene, what governs label choice? Is there a quantitative link between CNN similarity/semantic relatedness and the emitted word?
- Have you tried sequences or multi-round interaction? Does any compositional structure emerge when messages are longer than one symbol?
