- Proposes a framework for learning language via multi-agent referential games between a sender and a receiver starting tabula rasa.  
- Two simple neural architectures reliably learn to communicate.  
- Environmental design (e.g., different images for sender and receiver) nudges emergent codes toward higher-level, human-like semantics.  
- Mixing interactive self-play with supervised image labeling grounds symbols in natural language and improves human interpretability.  
- Agents reach near-perfect coordination and symbol usage partially aligns with semantic categories.  
- Supervised grounding markedly increases symbol purity and enables humans to pick the correct image above chance (≈68% on ReferIt-derived pairs).  
- Decision: Accept — convincing empirical evidence and a timely, general framework with strong extension potential.  
- Motivation: Tackles inducing functional communication through interaction, emphasizing portability to new partners and human interpretability.  
- Positioning: Sensible relation to multi-agent communication and signaling-game literature, with novel emphasis on steering codes toward human semantics.  
- Empirical claims supported: rapid convergence, informed sender yields richer inventory and higher cluster purity, object-level setup removes low-level common knowledge.  
- Analyses (SVD, t-SNE) indicate non-trivial structure in the learned code.  
- Figure/architecture clarity: Diagram omits connections and is hard to parse; needs detailed caption, input/embedding/convolution shapes, and separate schematics for both sender variants and receiver.  
- Training/stability: Request variance across random seeds, learning curves with confidence intervals, and details on variance-reduction for REINFORCE; justify τ=10 and report sensitivity to τ and vocabulary size.  
- Baselines/ablations: Add continuous-communication, supervised-only sender, pre-trained semantic embeddings, and ablations on the informed sender to isolate contributors to performance and purity.  
- Symbol usage quantification: Report effective vocabulary size over time, synonymy/polysemy measures, mutual information with categories, and dimensionality/redundancy estimates.  
- Generalization: Systematically report performance and interpretability on unseen concepts/classes and out-of-domain images; clarify overlap between supervised labels and game concepts.  
- Human study details: Provide instructions, quality control, inter-annotator variability, and consider alternative prompts (e.g., free-text paraphrases) to probe meaning.  
- Literature: Expand discussion of related neural communication work and classic signaling-game equilibria, relating observed behaviors to theory.  
- REINFORCE/variance-reduction question: What baselines or entropy regularization were used?  
- Sensitivity question: How sensitive are results to temperature τ and vocabulary size K; do larger vocabularies consistently improve purity/interpretability?  
- Receiver sampling question: Is sampling used during training and argmax at test, and does that gap affect performance?  
- Informed vs. agnostic sender question: Can the benefit of per-dimension convolutions be quantified beyond symbol count, and is the advantage robust across visual features and seeds?  
- Object-level reference question: In the different-exemplars setup, how is leakage via low-level cues prevented and confirmed?  
- Supervised grounding question: What is the overlap between supervised labels and game categories; does grounding generalize when labels are disjoint; how often are labels used metonymically vs. denotationally?  
- Human evaluation questions: Which words generalize best, any correlation with concreteness/frequency, and can human receivers learn rapidly from few interactions?  
- Reproducibility question: Will code, trained models, and exact image splits/pairs be released?
