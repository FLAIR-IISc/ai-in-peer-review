- Paper studies emergence of communication in a referential game where a sender emits a discrete symbol and a receiver picks the target; agents trained end-to-end with REINFORCE.  
- Compares two sender architectures (agnostic vs. informed), analyzes symbol usage, and evaluates semantic alignment of induced meanings.  
- Modifies environment to use different exemplars per class to reduce low-level shared information and encourages higher-level semantics.  
- Introduces mixed training alternating self-play with supervised object naming to ground symbols in natural language.  
- Finds informed senders coordinate faster and use a larger portion of the vocabulary.  
- Reports symbol clusters correlate with semantic categories above chance and that removing low-level common features slightly increases semantic alignment.  
- Supervised grounding boosts symbol purity and human interpretability; a crowdsourced study shows nontrivial human success guessing targets from labels.  
- Review decision: Reject.  
- Main criticism 1: Task often reduces to class or image identification, so “communication” mainly transmits class labels, weakening claims about emergent language.  
- Main criticism 2: Lacks strong baselines and ablations to show RL-based emergent communication is necessary or more sample-efficient than simpler alternatives.  
- Supporting: Messages appear to track category structure (t-SNE/purity) rather than finer attributes, suggesting trivial class-based signaling.  
- Supporting: Training/evaluation sampling lacks hard distractors (same-class or fine-grained), so agents may not learn beyond coarse category ID.  
- Supporting: No comparison to simple supervised/transfer baselines that could likely match or outperform RL in sample efficiency.  
- Supporting: Informed sender’s gains may result from inductive bias; need ablations to isolate architecture effects.  
- Supporting: Mixed supervised/self-play grounding may simply reflect direct label use; human study lacks detailed analysis of when communication is literal vs. metonymic.  
- Scientific rigor: Missing variance across seeds, stability of symbol usage, cross-play, and zero-shot generalization tests.  
- Suggested improvements: Add supervised/transfer and non-RL baselines, stronger ablations, and harder game settings (same-class distractors, attribute/relations, multi-step).  
- Suggested analyses: Measure compositionality, synonymy/homonymy, mutual information with factors, and sensitivity to vocabulary/temperature.  
- Suggested human study fixes: Separate cases where labels are present vs. absent, analyze error types, compare to baselines, and report confidence intervals.  
- Questions for authors: Fraction of same-category pairs; performance with same-class/near-neighbor distractors; supervised-label sender comparison; cross-play generalization and seed stability; sensitivity to vocabulary/temperature and informed sender design; behavior when supervised label absent; multi-round message experiments.
