This paper introduces a referential game between two agents. Both observe two images. The sender receives a binary target variable t and transmits a single symbol to the receiver, who must identify the target. Both agents receive a reward if the receiver predicts correctly. The agents are implemented as neural networks using pretrained image features and are trained with REINFORCE. The paper shows that agents learn effective policies and that the emergent communication (the symbol sent by the sender) reflects meaningful concepts. The authors further evaluate a variant grounded in different image classes, where the learned symbols appear even more semantically coherent. Finally, they consider a multi-task setting in which the sender alternates between the referential game and a supervised image classification task; as expected, grounding in supervised labels yields even more interpretable symbols.

Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setting is a compelling direction and considerably more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent RL, which justifies starting with a simple task. To the best of my knowledge, the proposed approach—first inducing communication between agents and then grounding it in human language—appears novel. As the authors note, this offers a complementary paradigm to sequence-to-sequence models that prioritize statistical properties of language over functional use. The task and framework, together with the analyses and visualizations of the learned symbols, provide a useful foundation for future work. I recommend acceptance.
