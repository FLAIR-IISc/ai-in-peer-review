This paper proposes a referential game between two agents. Both agents observe two images. The first agent (the sender) receives a binary target variable t and must send a symbol (message) to the second agent (the receiver) so that the receiver can recover the target. Both agents receive a reward if the receiver predicts the target correctly. The agents are parameterized as neural networks operating on pretrained image feature vectors and are trained with REINFORCE. In this setting, the authors show that the agents converge to optimal policies and that their learned communication (i.e., the symbol transmitted from the sender to the receiver) encodes meaningful concepts. The paper also presents experiments on a variant of the game grounded in different image classes, where the agents appear to learn even more meaningful concepts. Finally, a multi-game setup is proposed in which the sender alternates between playing the referential game and a supervised image classification task. Not surprisingly, when anchored to the supervised learning task, the symbolic communication reflects even more meaningful concepts.

Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setup is an interesting and important research direction. It is substantially harder than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning, which justifies starting from a relatively simple task. To the best of my knowledge, the strategy of first inducing communication between two agents and then grounding that communication in human language is novel. As the authors remark, this may offer an alternative paradigm to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to focus on the statistical properties of language rather than its functional aspects. I believe the proposed task and framework, along with the analysis and visualizations of what the communicated tokens represent, provide a useful stepping stone for future work. For these reasons, I recommend acceptance.
