Summary and contributions
The paper studies how discrete communication protocols can emerge between neural agents through interactive learning. The authors instantiate a simple referential game in which a sender observes two images and a target indicator, emits a single symbol from a fixed vocabulary, and a receiver uses this symbol to pick the target among the two images. Using real images (ImageNet-derived) and simple feed-forward agents trained with REINFORCE, they show: (i) agents reliably learn to coordinate without any pre-specified code; (ii) an “informed” sender architecture with an inductive bias for comparing image embeddings dimension-wise converges faster and uses a richer subset of symbols than an “agnostic” sender; (iii) the induced symbols exhibit non-trivial semantic structure, aligning with intuitive categories above chance, and cluster in the CNN visual space; (iv) modifying common knowledge by showing different exemplars of the same class to sender and receiver nudges the emergent code toward more category-level semantics; and (v) mixing self-play with supervised object naming grounds symbols in natural language and improves human interpretability, as shown by a crowdsourced study in which humans can often use the sender’s words to pick the intended referent (68% accuracy).

Overall, the paper is a clear and timely step toward interactive, grounded language learning. The empirical analysis is thoughtful, and the human evaluation is a valuable addition.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated empirical demonstration that discrete communication emerges between neural agents using real images, with insightful analyses of semantic alignment and a human-in-the-loop evaluation.
- The hybrid self-play + supervised grounding experiment is simple yet compelling, improving interpretability and demonstrating transfer of conventional linguistic labels to a new interactive setting.

Supporting arguments
- Problem formulation and motivation: The paper tackles the core question of how to induce communication protocols that are useful for coordination and potentially transferable to humans. It is well positioned within work on emergent communication, multi-agent RL, and signaling games, and highlights the limitations of passive supervised learning for interactive language use.
- Methodological soundness: The training setup is clean, using discrete symbol sampling and REINFORCE without resorting to special tricks. The agents see natural images, and the environment manipulations are principled (e.g., controlling common knowledge to encourage abstraction).
- Empirical support: Results consistently show near-perfect coordination. Analyses go beyond accuracy to examine symbol usage diversity, SVD spectra of symbol–pair matrices, category purity relative to McRae categories, and t-SNE visualizations. The “object-level reference” variant addresses the concern that agents might rely on low-level idiosyncrasies by forcing them to coordinate over class-level properties. The grounding experiment materially increases purity and allows direct label usage; importantly, human subjects can interpret sender outputs even on images outside the supervised label set.
- Clarity and impact: The work is accessible, the task is simple enough to analyze, and the findings are likely to stimulate further research on compositionality, grounding, and human–AI communication.

That said, two caveats temper my enthusiasm:
- Task simplicity and potential reduction to classification: When target and distractor belong to different classes, a sender that maps images to class labels and a receiver that matches labels would suffice; RL may be an inefficient training mechanism relative to supervised transfer. The authors partially address this with the “object-level reference” setup and with human evaluation, which I appreciate.
- Limited probing of generalization and compositionality: The paper does not yet test zero-shot generalization (unseen classes or attributes), multi-step dialogs, or the emergence of compositional codes.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify symbol usage statistics: Report how many symbols are used (and with what entropy) across seeds and vocab sizes, and whether the “informed” sender truly uses a larger effective lexicon versus synonyms. The SVD analysis is a good start; adding mutual information between symbols and categories, and measuring top-k type–token distributions, would strengthen the case.
- Baselines: Include a non-RL baseline where the sender is a supervised classifier producing one of K labels and the receiver is trained to map labels to images. This would quantify the added value of interactive training. Also consider a continuous-communication baseline (no discretization) to show the effect of the communication bottleneck.
- Generalization: Evaluate with harder distractors (same superordinate category, fine-grained siblings) and out-of-distribution pairs. Report performance when both images come from the same class but differ in attributes, to test whether agents learn attribute-like semantics.
- Human study details: Provide confidence intervals, effect sizes, and clearer description of sampling. It would be useful to analyze which words succeed/fail and whether success correlates with visual or semantic similarity in embedding space.
- Training dynamics and sensitivity: Report variance over multiple seeds, learning curves, and sensitivity to the temperature τ, embedding dimensionality, and vocabulary size. Mention training stability and time.
- Architecture: The “informed” sender’s inductive bias appears beneficial. A brief ablation (e.g., swapping the convolutional comparator for other comparators) would isolate what matters.
- Reproducibility: Release code and data splits. Clean up and standardize references and citations.

Questions for the authors
- How does performance, symbol purity, and human interpretability vary with vocabulary size (e.g., 5, 10, 50, 100, 500)? Do agents saturate at a small lexicon?
- What is the agents’ generalization on image pairs where both images are from the same class but differ in salient attributes (color, pose, part)? Do symbol–attribute correspondences emerge?
- How robust are the results across random seeds and alternative CNN features (e.g., different layers, different backbones)?
- Can you compare against a supervised sender + learned receiver baseline to quantify the specific benefit of interactive RL?
- In the hybrid supervised/self-play setup, do learned label meanings leak beyond their conventional denotations in systematic ways (e.g., metonymy)? Can you characterize these extensions quantitatively?
- Have you tried multi-symbol or multi-turn communication? Does a compositional code emerge under a stronger bottleneck (e.g., short sequences with small vocabularies)?
- How do you prevent degenerate equilibria (e.g., indexical codes tied to dataset idiosyncrasies)? Does the “different-exemplar” trick suffice?

In sum, this is a thoughtful and well-executed paper on emergent communication with a valuable human-grounding component. With stronger baselines and generalization tests, it would be even more compelling. I recommend acceptance.
