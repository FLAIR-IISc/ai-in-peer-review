Summary
The paper explores training natural language systems through multi-agent, interactive referential communication games. This is a timely and compelling direction: leveraging interaction to induce communication protocols has clear potential, and the work thoughtfully connects these games with supervised learning. In particular, the approach of alternating between game-based interaction and supervised signals (as described in Section 5 and suggested in Section 6) seems especially promising for stabilizing learning and grounding emergent communication.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: Framing language learning as coordination in interactive environments is intuitive and well-justified.
- Practical promise: The hybrid training strategy—switching between interactive games and supervised learning—appears both effective and extensible.
- Versatile framework: The general formulation in Section 2 seems applicable to a range of tasks and agent configurations.
- Reasonable results: Empirical outcomes are consistent with the paper’s claims and suggest the approach can scale to more ambitious settings.

Clarity and Presentation
- Figure 1: The figure is difficult to interpret in its current form. It appears that some network connections are omitted for brevity, but given the customized architecture and the already dense description in Section 3, the shorthand diagram adds confusion rather than clarity. At minimum, please add a detailed caption that explicitly maps each diagram element to architectural components, clarifies what is omitted, and explains data/control flow across modules and time steps. Ideally, consider revising the figure to either (a) include the missing connections, or (b) split the architecture into simpler subfigures (e.g., sender, receiver, and interaction loop), accompanied by a legend.
- Section 3 readability: The architectural description is hard to follow. It would help to:
  - Provide a concise, end-to-end narrative of a single episode: inputs, message generation, actions, losses, and parameter updates.
  - Specify which parameters are shared across agents (if any), the dimensionality of messages, how messages are encoded/decoded, and the precise training objectives for each component.
  - Include pseudocode for the training loop (covering the alternation between supervised and interactive phases).
  - Clearly state optimization details and any techniques used for stability (e.g., entropy regularization, curriculum, baselines for variance reduction).

Positioning and Related Work
The authors acknowledge prior efforts in multi-agent games for inducing communication, though that literature appears limited. A more thorough positioning would strengthen the paper: clarify how the proposed framework differs from and advances beyond earlier interactive communication setups (e.g., task complexity, learning signals, scalability, or stability). Given the increasing interest in emergent communication, making the novelty relative to the most closely related work explicit would help readers assess the contribution.

Empirical Evaluation
The reported results are reasonable and support the hypothesis that interaction can facilitate language-like behavior. A few additions could make the evaluation more compelling:
- Hybrid training benefits: Since the alternation between supervised and interactive training is a key idea, quantify its contribution relative to purely supervised and purely interactive baselines.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Explore the effect of message dimensionality, vocabulary size (if discrete), and agent parameter sharing on performance and communication efficiency.
- Qualitative analysis: Provide examples of learned messages/protocols and how they correlate with task semantics.
- Generalization: If feasible, test transfer to unseen objects/attributes or variants of the game to assess robustness.

Impact and Future Directions
The framework is promising and likely to catalyze future work. The idea of integrating external supervision with emergent protocols could unlock more grounded and compositional behaviors, and the generality of the setup suggests applicability to richer tasks (e.g., multi-turn negotiation, instruction following, or grounded reasoning).

Overall Assessment
This is a well-motivated and promising paper. The core idea—training agents through interactive referential games, augmented by supervised learning—is compelling, and the framework appears useful. The main shortcoming is clarity: the architecture and training procedure are difficult to reconstruct from the current text and figure. Addressing these presentation issues (especially revising Figure 1 and clarifying Section 3) would substantially improve readability and impact. Subject to these revisions, I view the contribution positively.

Reviewer Confidence
I am confident in my understanding of the paper’s methods and results. However, I am less certain about its placement within the most closely related emergent communication literature, and thus more tentative in assessing novelty relative to prior work.
