1. Summary of what the paper claims to do/contribute (positive and generous)
- The paper proposes training language-capable agents through interactive multi-agent communication, specifically via referential games where a sender and receiver must coordinate to identify a target image using a discrete symbol from a fixed vocabulary. 
- It demonstrates that simple neural agents (with both “agnostic” and “informed” sender architectures) learn to communicate effectively from scratch, with the informed sender converging faster and employing a richer set of symbols.
- The study examines the semantics of the emergent code, showing that symbol usage aligns above chance with intuitive conceptual categories (e.g., broad object classes, even living vs. non-living), and that modifying common knowledge in the game (object-level vs. image-level reference) nudges agents toward more abstract, category-level semantics.
- It further introduces a straightforward grounding strategy by interleaving self-play with supervised image labeling, leading to increased symbol purity and direct interpretability of many symbols as conventional labels.
- Finally, the authors provide initial human evaluation showing that, even beyond directly supervised categories, human participants can often recover the sender’s intended referent given the produced word, suggesting a promising route toward human-agent communication.

2. Decision: Accept
- Key reasons: (i) A clear, compelling framework that shows interactive emergent communication with real images, and (ii) a practical, effective grounding mechanism (alternating self-play and supervised labeling) that measurably improves interpretability and human compatibility.

3. Supporting arguments
- Empirical strength: Agents consistently achieve high coordination accuracy across architectures. The informed sender converges faster and leverages a broader portion of the vocabulary. Symbol-category purity is reliably above chance, and the living vs. non-living tendency that emerges with very few symbols is intriguing and aligns with well-known cognitive distinctions.
- Environmental design: The object-level (class-consistent, image-mismatched) variant reduces reliance on low-level idiosyncrasies and increases semantic abstraction, as reflected in both clustering purity and t-SNE visualizations.
- Grounding and human relevance: Interleaving supervised object naming substantially increases symbol purity and the number of symbols used, and enables direct interpretability in many cases. The crowdsourced evaluation (68% success; highly significant logistic regression) indicates that the learned code carries semantics that humans can often exploit, even outside the supervised label set.
- Scope and promise: While prior work on neural emergent communication exists, this paper’s combination of realistic visual inputs, controlled environmental manipulations for semantics, and a simple yet effective grounding approach makes the contribution timely and practically relevant.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not necessarily decision-critical)
- Figure 1 clarity: The sender architectures are somewhat intricate, and Section 3’s description is hard to follow on first read. The diagram appears to omit connections and uses shorthand that may confuse readers. Please refine the figure and provide a detailed caption that explains the flow (input ordering, embedding steps, filter dimensions, and how scores over symbols are produced). This would meaningfully improve approachability and reproducibility.
- Architectural description: Expand the comparison between the informed and agnostic senders, including an explicit statement that the agnostic architecture nests the informed one, and a schematic mapping of the informed sender’s convolutional operations to the agnostic sender’s fully connected layers.
- Training/reporting details: Clarify temperature usage at train vs. test time, whether sampling or argmax is used at test, and whether any entropy regularization or baselines are applied in REINFORCE. Reporting variance across random seeds would strengthen claims of robustness.
- Symbol usage analysis: The SVD analysis is a nice touch; consider adding more details (e.g., cross-validated rank selection procedure, scree plots in the appendix) and quantitative measures of “effective vocabulary size” (e.g., perplexity or type-token ratios).
- Purity/statistics: Provide the exact random baselines, statistical tests, confidence intervals, and effect sizes for purity improvements across conditions (sender type, vocabulary size, image vs. object-level setups).
- Related work placement: A brief, clearer positioning relative to closely related neural emergent communication works (e.g., differentiable communication channels, backprop-based multi-agent setups) would help readers situate the contribution.
- Generalization and ablations: Small ablations (e.g., vocabulary size, τ sensitivity, fc vs. sm features) and cross-dataset generalization summaries (beyond the ReferIt experiments) would help characterize when and why the approach works best.

Questions for the authors (to clarify understanding and strengthen confidence)
- Architecture/figure: Could you provide a fully annotated version of Figure 1 showing all connections, the precise flow from inputs to symbol scores, kernel sizes, and the role of the dimension-by-dimension filters in the informed sender?
- Temperature and decoding: What temperatures are used during training and testing? Do you sample or take argmax at test time for sender symbols and receiver choices? How sensitive are results to τ?
- REINFORCE details: Did you employ a baseline or variance-reduction techniques? Any entropy regularization on the sender’s symbol distribution?
- Symbol usage: How stable is the “effective number of symbols” across seeds? Can you quantify synonymy or degeneracy (e.g., via mutual information between symbols and object categories)?
- Purity evaluation: How exactly is the random baseline computed for purity, and what statistical tests/intervals support “significantly above chance” claims across settings?
- Grounding mechanism: When interleaving supervised labeling and self-play, how are the game symbols aligned to the supervised label space (beyond shared embeddings)? Is there explicit regularization to encourage alignment, and did you observe any negative transfer?
- Human study details: How were image pairs/words sampled for the crowdsourced test, and were annotators exposed to any calibration examples? Could the success rate be influenced by familiarity or bias toward certain labels?
- Generalization: How well does the approach transfer to unseen object categories or to settings with longer symbol sequences (multi-turn communication)? Do the authors foresee role rotation (sender/receiver) improving alignment or stability?
