- Proposes a simple multi-agent referential game where a sender emits a single symbol to communicate a target image to a receiver; both trained end-to-end with REINFORCE using pretrained CNN features.  
- Shows agents reliably coordinate; an “informed” sender with dimensionwise comparison learns faster and uses a richer vocabulary.  
- Emergent codes align with intuitive semantics: higher category purity, structured t-SNE clusters, and coarse semantic splits (e.g., living vs. non-living).  
- Removing low-level common knowledge (different images of same class for sender/receiver) promotes more category-level semantics.  
- Mixing the game with supervised image-labeling grounds symbols in natural language, increasing interpretability and enabling limited human-agent communication.  
- Decision: Accept — novel, well-motivated, timely, and scientifically sound with solid analysis and human evaluation.  
- Motivation clarity: paper convincingly argues passive supervised learning is insufficient and positions referential games as a scalable testbed for interactive, interpretable agents.  
- Methodology: VGG features appropriately isolate communication from perception; architectures are simple and transparent; informed sender’s inductive bias is justified.  
- Empirical support: coordination is strong and consistent, informed sender converges faster, and emergent semantics are non-trivial and measurable.  
- Environment design matters: altering common knowledge changes the level of semantic abstraction in the protocol.  
- Grounding benefits: supervised grounding increases symbol use, purity, and allows humans to often infer referents even outside supervised labels, indicating flexible/metonymic usage.  
- Literature positioning: well situated relative to emergent communication and signaling-game work, with emphasis on realistic images and human interpretability.  
- Clarify how target t is encoded to sender (order vs. explicit flag) and evaluate potential order leakage or randomization alternatives.  
- Reporting/formatting fixes: consistent percentage formats, check implausible “obs–chance purity” values, fix typos, and add standard deviations/confidence intervals across seeds.  
- Deeper analysis requested: quantify synonymy/homonymy (entropy, type–token ratio, mutual information), report sensitivity to temperature τ, vocabulary size, embedding dimensionality, and convergence brittleness.  
- Investigate why agnostic sender (which should nest informed) converges to 2-symbol solution — optimization vs. implicit regularization.  
- Test generalization to new partners: can receivers coordinate with held-out senders without finetuning?  
- Additional controls for low-level cues: add augmentations or adversarial distractors beyond the different-image same-class control.  
- Human evaluation improvements: report instructions, interface, prompts, demographics, quality control, response times/confidence, and analyze failure cases/semantic drift.  
- Reproducibility: include random seeds, number of runs, and preferably release code and trained models.  
- Specific questions: how is t encoded; effects of sender input randomization; sensitivity to τ and sampling vs argmax; effects of role rotation or multiple partners; potential for compositionality with multi-symbol messages.  
- More grounding questions: what proportion of supervised labels are used in play, metonymic vs denotational usage, generalization to unseen categories, and cross-play between independently trained agents.  
- Overall verdict: clear, carefully executed work advancing emergent communication toward human interpretability; recommend acceptance.
