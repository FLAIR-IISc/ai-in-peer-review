This paper presents a novel approach to understanding communication between two agents through a referential game framework. The authors tackle a crucial problem in the field of reinforcement learning, exploring whether machines can learn to communicate in a way similar to humans.

The paper's strengths lie in its simplicity and intuitive post-learning analysis. The task of referential games is straightforward, allowing for easy comprehension of the agents' behavior and the symbols they use to communicate. The comparison between informed and agnostic agents is particularly interesting, as it highlights the differences in their communication strategies. Informed agents utilize multiple symbols to convey messages, whereas agnostic agents rely on a limited set of symbols.

However, there are some limitations to the approach. The task can be effectively reduced to image classification, where the symbols used by the agents correspond to image classes. This simplification makes the problem similar to a transfer learning task, which could potentially be solved more efficiently using alternative methods. This raises questions about the scalability and applicability of the proposed approach to more complex communication tasks.

To further improve the paper, the authors could provide more insights into the generalizability of their approach. How do the agents' communication strategies change when faced with more complex or dynamic environments? Can the proposed framework be extended to multi-agent scenarios or more nuanced communication tasks? Additionally, the authors could explore the relationship between the agents' communication and human language, investigating whether the learned symbols can be mapped to meaningful words or concepts.

Overall, the paper presents an intriguing approach to understanding communication between agents. While there are some limitations to the task and the proposed method, the work has the potential to contribute to the development of more sophisticated communication systems in the field of reinforcement learning.
