The authors propose a novel approach to training natural language systems by leveraging multi-agent communication in referential games. This method has been explored in previous work, but the current study contributes significantly to the field by introducing a framework that combines interactive learning with supervised learning. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in teaching agents to communicate and coordinate with each other.

I recommend accepting this paper, with two key reasons for this choice: (1) the proposed framework is well-motivated and grounded in relevant literature, and (2) the experimental results demonstrate the potential of this approach in achieving robust communication among agents.

The authors provide a clear and well-structured explanation of their framework, which includes a general setup for multi-agent communication and a specific implementation for referential games. The use of simple neural network architectures and reinforcement learning techniques makes the approach accessible and easy to follow. The experimental results show that the agents can learn to coordinate and communicate effectively, even when the game environment is modified to encourage more abstract thinking.

One potential area for improvement is the visualization of the network architecture in Figure 1. While the diagram is helpful in illustrating the overall structure, some connections seem to be omitted, which may cause confusion. A more detailed caption or a revised diagram would be beneficial in clarifying the architecture.

Additionally, the authors could provide more context and comparison with related work in the field of multi-agent communication. While they mention some previous studies, a more comprehensive discussion of the current state of research in this area would help to situate their contribution more clearly.

To further improve the paper, the authors may consider addressing the following questions:

* How do the agents' communication protocols evolve over time, and what are the key factors that influence this evolution?
* Can the proposed framework be extended to more complex game environments or tasks that require more sophisticated communication?
* How does the approach compare to other methods for training natural language systems, such as supervised learning or reinforcement learning from human feedback?

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to training natural language systems through multi-agent communication, and with some revisions to address the above points, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
