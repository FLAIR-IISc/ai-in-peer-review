The paper proposes a framework for language learning that relies on multi-agent communication, specifically in the context of referential games. This approach is well-motivated, given the limitations of traditional supervised learning methods in developing interactive machines such as conversational agents. The authors' use of simple neural-network agents to learn coordination in a referential game is a promising direction, and their results show that these agents can develop a language that captures general conceptual properties of objects.

I accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the approach is innovative and well-motivated, and the results demonstrate the potential of multi-agent communication in developing language. Secondly, the framework proposed in Section 2 is clear and effective, and the experiments demonstrate its usefulness in various ways.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance theoretical and empirical contributions. The authors provide a clear framework for understanding the emergence of language in multi-agent systems, and their experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach. The use of simple neural-network agents and the focus on referential games make the results more interpretable and easier to understand.
