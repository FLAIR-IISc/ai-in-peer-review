This paper proposes a referential game between two agents, where both agents observe two images. The first agent, referred to as the sender, receives a binary target variable (t) and is required to send a symbol (message) to the second agent, known as the receiver, such that the receiver can recover the target. Both agents receive a reward if the receiver successfully predicts the target. The authors suggest parameterizing the agents as neural networks, utilizing pre-trained representations of the images as feature vectors, and training them using REINFORCE. In this setting, the agents converge to optimal policies, and their learned communications (i.e., the symbolic code transmitted from the sender to the receiver) exhibit meaningful concepts. Furthermore, the paper presents experiments on a variant of the game grounded on different image classes, where the agents appear to learn even more meaningful concepts. A multi-game setup is also proposed, where the sender agent alternates between playing the original game and a supervised learning task (classifying images). As expected, when anchored to the supervised learning task, the symbolic communications become even more meaningful.

Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setup is an interesting research direction to explore. This task is significantly more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning tasks, justifying the authors' decision to start with a relatively simple task. To the best of my knowledge, the approach of first learning communication between two agents and then grounding this communication in human language is novel. As the authors note, this may offer an alternative paradigm to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to focus on statistical properties of language rather than their functional aspects. I believe the contributions of the proposed task and framework, as well as the analysis and visualization of the communicated tokens, are a useful stepping stone for future work. Therefore, I think the paper should be accepted.
