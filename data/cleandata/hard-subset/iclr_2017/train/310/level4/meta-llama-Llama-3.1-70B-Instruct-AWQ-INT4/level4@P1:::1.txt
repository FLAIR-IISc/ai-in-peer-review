This paper tackles a very crucial problem of understanding communication between two agents, which is essential as more applications of reinforcement learning are being explored. By addressing this fundamental question, the authors bring us back to the basic question of whether the problem-solving approach of machines is similar to that of humans. The task is simple enough to make the post-learning analysis intuitive, allowing for a clear understanding of the agents' communication dynamics.

The paper's strengths include its ability to provide insight into the differences between informed and agnostic agents. It was interesting to see how informed agents made use of multiple symbols to transmit the message, whereas agnostic agents relied only on two symbols. This distinction highlights the potential benefits of incorporating domain knowledge into the agents' decision-making processes.

However, one potential drawback of the approach is that the task effectively boils down to image classification, particularly when the two images sent are from different categories. The symbols used are effectively the image class, which the second agent learns to assign to either of the images. This reduction to image classification raises the question of whether this approach is fundamentally different from a transfer learning problem, which could potentially be trained much faster than a reinforcement learning algorithm.

To further improve the paper, it would be beneficial to discuss the implications of these findings in the context of more complex tasks and environments. How do the authors envision their approach scaling to more realistic scenarios, and what challenges do they anticipate in doing so? Additionally, it would be interesting to explore the potential applications of this work in areas such as human-agent collaboration or natural language processing.

Some specific questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more insight into why the informed agents were able to make use of multiple symbols, while the agnostic agents were not?
* How do you plan to address the issue of the task reducing to image classification, and what steps can be taken to make the approach more robust?
* Have you considered exploring the use of more complex communication protocols, such as sequences of symbols or multi-step communication, and how might these impact the agents' ability to communicate effectively?

Overall, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the understanding of communication between agents and highlights the potential benefits of incorporating domain knowledge into decision-making processes. With some further development and exploration of the approach's limitations, this work has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of reinforcement learning.
