This paper proposes a referential game between two agents to develop a framework for language learning that relies on multi-agent communication. The game involves a sender and a receiver, both observing two images, with the sender receiving a binary target variable and sending a symbol to the receiver to recover the target. The agents are parametrized as neural networks with pretrained representations of the images as feature vectors and trained using REINFORCE. The paper demonstrates that the agents converge to optimal policies and that their learned communications have meaningful concepts.

The authors also present experiments on a variant of the game grounded on different image classes, where the agents learn even more meaningful concepts. Furthermore, a multi-game setup is proposed, where the sender agent alternates between playing the game and a supervised learning task, resulting in symbolic communications with even more meaningful concepts.

I believe the paper should be accepted. Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setup is an interesting research direction to explore, and the approach of first learning communication between two agents and then grounding this communication in human language is novel. The contributions of the proposed task and framework, and the analysis and visualization of what the communicated tokens represent, are a useful stepping stone for future work.

However, I have a few comments and questions to clarify the paper. Firstly, how is the target (t) incorporated into the sender networks? This is not clearly explained in the paper. Additionally, there seems to be a discrepancy in the way percentage values are used in Table 1 and Table 2. In Table 1, percentages appear to be written in the interval [0, 100], while in Table 2, they are in the interval [0, 1]. Furthermore, the "obs-chance purity" column in Table 1 has extremely small values, which seems to be a mistake.

Minor corrections include "assest" -> "assess" and "usufal" -> "usual".

To further improve the paper, I would like the authors to answer the following questions:

* Can you provide more details on how the target (t) is incorporated into the sender networks?
* How do you plan to extend this framework to more complex scenarios, such as multi-agent communication with more than two agents?
* Have you considered using other types of neural networks, such as recurrent neural networks, to model the agents?
* How do you plan to evaluate the performance of the agents in more complex tasks, such as conversational dialogue systems?

Overall, I believe the paper has the potential to contribute to the development of more advanced conversational AI systems, and I look forward to seeing the authors' responses to these questions.
