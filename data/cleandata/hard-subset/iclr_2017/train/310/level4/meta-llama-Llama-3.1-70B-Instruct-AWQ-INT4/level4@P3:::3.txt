**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel framework for language learning through multi-agent communication in referential games. The game involves two agents, a sender and a receiver, who observe two images and must communicate to identify the target image. The agents are parametrized as neural networks and trained using REINFORCE. The paper demonstrates that the agents can learn to coordinate and develop meaningful concepts through their communication. The authors also explore variants of the game, including one where the agents learn to communicate about different image classes, and another where the sender agent is grounded in human language through a supervised learning task.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper. The proposed task and framework are novel and contribute to the field of multi-agent learning and language acquisition. The analysis and visualization of the communicated tokens provide valuable insights into the learning process. While there are some minor issues with the presentation, the overall quality of the paper is high, and the contributions justify its acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper tackles a specific and well-motivated problem in the field of multi-agent learning and language acquisition. The approach of learning communication between two agents and then grounding this communication in human language is novel and has the potential to provide a new paradigm for language learning. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework, and the analysis and visualization of the communicated tokens provide valuable insights into the learning process.

**Minor Issues and Suggestions**

* The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of how the target (t) is incorporated into the sender networks.
* There is an inconsistency in the presentation of percentage values in Table 1 and Table 2. The values in Table 1 seem to be in the interval [0, 100], while those in Table 2 are in the interval [0, 1]. Please correct this.
* The column "obs-chance purity" in Table 1 seems to have extremely small values, which may be a mistake.
* There are a few minor typos in the paper, including "assest" instead of "assess" and "usufal" instead of "usual".

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, the authors may consider providing more details on the neural network architectures used for the sender and receiver agents. Additionally, it would be interesting to see more experiments on the robustness of the learned communication protocols to changes in the game environment or the introduction of noise.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you provide more details on how the target (t) is incorporated into the sender networks?
* How do you plan to extend the proposed framework to more complex language learning tasks?
* Have you considered using other multi-agent learning algorithms, such as policy gradient methods or actor-critic methods, instead of REINFORCE?
