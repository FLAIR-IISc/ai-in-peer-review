This paper tackles a crucial problem of understanding communication between two agents, a fundamental aspect of reinforcement learning applications. The authors propose a framework for language learning that relies on multi-agent communication, specifically in the context of referential games. The task is simple enough to make post-learning analysis intuitive, allowing for a clear understanding of the agents' behavior.

The paper's primary contribution lies in its exploration of how agents can develop a language to coordinate and earn payoffs in a game environment. The authors investigate whether tabula rasa agents can succeed in communication and what features of the environment lead to the development of codes resembling human language. They also examine how to make changes to the game environment to cause the emergent language to better reflect intuitive semantic properties of the images.

The experimental setup is well-designed, using a set of images from ImageNet and modifying the game to show agents different pairs of images while maintaining the ImageNet class of both the target and distractor. The results show that the agents are still able to coordinate, and there is a small increase in symbol usage purity.

The paper also explores grounding the agents' communication in human language by combining the game with a supervised task. The results demonstrate that supervised learning on a separate dataset provides some grounding for communication with humans, which generalizes beyond the conventional word denotations learned in the supervised phase.

One potential limitation of the approach is that the task effectively boils down to image classification, where the symbols used are effectively the image class that the second agent learns to assign to either of the images. This approach can be seen as a transfer learning problem, which could potentially be trained much faster than a reinforcement learning algorithm. However, the authors' focus on the development of a language and the exploration of its semantic properties makes this a valuable contribution to the field.

To further improve the paper, it would be beneficial to explore more complex game environments and tasks that require more sophisticated communication between agents. Additionally, the authors could investigate how to scale their approach to more realistic scenarios, such as human-agent communication.

Decision: Accept
