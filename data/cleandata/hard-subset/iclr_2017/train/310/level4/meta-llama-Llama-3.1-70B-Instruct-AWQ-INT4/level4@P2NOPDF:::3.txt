This paper proposes a referential game between two agents, where both agents observe two images. The first agent, referred to as the sender, receives a binary target variable (t) and must transmit a symbol (message) to the second agent, called the receiver. The goal is for the receiver to recover the target variable based on the received message. Both agents receive a reward if the receiver successfully predicts the target. The authors parameterize the agents as neural networks, utilizing pre-trained representations of the images as feature vectors, and train them using the REINFORCE algorithm.

The results show that the agents converge to optimal policies, and their learned communication (i.e., the symbolic code transmitted from the sender to the receiver) conveys meaningful concepts. The paper also presents experiments on a variant of the game grounded on different image classes, where the agents appear to learn even more meaningful concepts. Furthermore, a multi-game setup is proposed, where the sender agent alternates between playing the original game and a supervised learning task (classifying images). Notably, when anchored to the supervised learning task, the symbolic communications exhibit even more meaningful concepts.

Learning shared representations for communication in a multi-agent setup is an interesting research direction to explore. This task is significantly more challenging than standard supervised learning or single-agent reinforcement learning tasks, justifying the authors' decision to start with a relatively simple task. To the best of my knowledge, the approach of first learning communication between two agents and then grounding this communication in human language is novel. As the authors remark, this may provide an alternative paradigm to standard sequence-to-sequence models, which tend to focus on statistical properties of language rather than their functional aspects.

I believe the contributions of the proposed task and framework, as well as the analysis and visualization of what the communicated tokens represent, are valuable stepping stones for future work. Therefore, I think the paper should be accepted.
