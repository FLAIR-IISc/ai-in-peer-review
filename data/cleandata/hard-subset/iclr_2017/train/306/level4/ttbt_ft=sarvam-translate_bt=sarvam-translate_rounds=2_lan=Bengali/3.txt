Considering the authors' feedback and the updates to the manuscript – especially clarifying the meta-learning task – I am raising my score to 8.

This manuscript proposes a solution to a few-shot learning problem using neural networks with meta-learning. Meta-learning is a classic concept that has been revived in the last 12 months. The authors have framed few-shot learning as a continuous meta-learning problem: each "example" consists of a sequence of "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. Each "step" includes input "bases" (e.g., training loss and gradient), as well as the current state (parameters) of the base learner. This paper applies an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, directly modeling the updated parameters of the base learner using second-level internal memory cells. In doing so, they find similarities between the LSTM memory cells and the gradient family's own update rules. The LSTM meta-learner's updates are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final "test" batch. The authors have made several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-level cells (similar to using the same learning rate for all parameters). The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset proposed by Vinyals et al. 2016 and shows that the meta-learner is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (machine network, Vinyals 2016) in 1- and 5-shot learning.

Strengths:
- It is interesting to consider the few-shot learning problem as a continuous (meta-)learning problem – and looking back, it seems natural. Although the authors did not initially create the concept of learning across continuous learning problems, I think it is fair to say that they have made progress in this area, although I cannot confidently say anything about its novelty as I am not deeply familiar with recent work on meta-learning.
- The proposed method is competitive and performs better than Vinyals 2016 in the 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet tests.
- In this setting, the base learner (simple convex classifier) is quite different from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding method used in Vinyals 2016. When state-of-the-art results can be reported using a very different method, it is always exciting than incremental follow-up work.
- As far as I know, the concept of the relationship between memory cell and gradient family updates is new here. However, it is interesting.
- The paper provides several practical insights into designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, which will make it easier for others to replicate this work and apply these concepts to new problems. This includes the importance of proper initialization, sharing weights across coordinates, and normalizing/rescaling loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. Some insights have been described previously (the importance of simulating the test conditions during meta-training; assuming independence between the meta-learner and base learner parameters when taking the relative gradient of the meta-learner parameters), but the discussion here is useful nonetheless.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is sometimes quite unclear. Although it describes a very interesting work, I would not say that the paper is enjoyable. I had to try multiple times to understand the general learning problem (as well as see related works). The task description in section 2 (page 2) is very abstract and uses notations and language that are not common outside this sub-field. The paper could benefit from a brief practical example (based on MNISt, okay), possibly combined with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks. This would certainly make it more accessible to a wider audience.
- Following that note, the exact nature of the N-class few-shot learning problem here is unclear to me. In particular, the Mini-ImageNet dataset has 100 labels, of which 64/16/20 are used during meta-training/validation/testing. Does this mean that only 64/100 classes are observed through meta-training? Or does it mean that only 64/100 classes are observed in each batch, but all 100 classes are observed on average during meta-training? If it is the former, how many outputs does the convex base learner have during the soft-map layer of meta-training? 64 (only observed in training) or 100 (36 of which were never observed)? Many such details are unclear (see questions).
- The plots in figure 2 are quite informative in themselves, and there is very little insight into them in the discussion section.

This is an interesting paper with quite convincing results. It seems to have মোটামুটি clear গ্রহণযোগ্যতা, but the concept and presentation of the work could be improved further. I would definitely raise my score if the quality of the writing were improved.
