This manuscript proposes to tackle few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a classic idea that has recently seen a renaissance. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each meta-example consists of a sequence of training batches followed by a final test batch. At each step, the inputs to the meta-learner include outputs from a "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradients) as well as the base learner's current parameters. The paper applies an LSTM to this problem, using the memory cells in a second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. In doing so, the authors note an interesting similarity between the update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is trained to minimize the base learner's prediction loss on the final test batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells, which is analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters. The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset from Vinyals et al. (2016) and shows that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks, Vinyals et al., 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning tasks.

**Strengths:**
*   The formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is intriguing and, in hindsight, feels natural. While the general idea of persisting learning across a series of tasks is not new, this work appears to advance the state of the art (though I cannot confidently assert its novelty, as I am not deeply familiar with recent meta-learning literature).
*   The proposed approach is competitive with, and in some cases outperforms, the results from Vinyals et al. (2016) on the 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
*   The choice of a simple ConvNet classifier as the base learner is a notable departure from the nearest-neighbor approach used in Vinyals et al. (2016). It is always exciting when state-of-the-art results are achieved using a significantly different methodology rather than incremental improvements.
*   The insight into the relationship between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent updates appears to be novel and is interesting in its own right.
*   The paper offers several practical insights into designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, which should help others replicate this work and apply these ideas to new problems. These insights include proper initialization, coordinate-wise weight sharing, and the importance of normalizing inputs such as the loss and gradients. While some of these points have been described before (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training), their discussion in this context is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The writing is at times opaque, which makes the paper difficult to read despite its interesting contributions. I had to read the paper multiple times and consult related work to fully grasp the proposed learning problem. The task description in Section 2, for instance, is highly abstract, using notation and terminology that may be unfamiliar to readers outside this specific sub-field. The paper's accessibility would be greatly improved by including a simple, concrete example (e.g., using MNIST), perhaps accompanied by a diagram illustrating the sequence of operations in a few-shot task.
*   Related to the issue of clarity, the precise setup of the N-way, few-shot learning experiments is not fully explained. For the Mini-ImageNet dataset, the paper states that 64/16/20 classes are used for meta-training/validation/testing, respectively. It is unclear if this means that the meta-training phase *only* ever observes 64 distinct classes from the total of 100. If so, how is the base learner's final softmax layer structured during meta-training? Does it have 64 outputs, or 100 (with 36 corresponding to unseen classes)? Clarification on such experimental details is needed.
*   The plots in Figure 2 are difficult to interpret on their own, and the accompanying discussion offers little insight into their meaning or implications.

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. It is a strong candidate for acceptance, but its impact could be significantly greater if the presentation were improved. I will raise my score if the authors can address the clarity issues in a revision.
