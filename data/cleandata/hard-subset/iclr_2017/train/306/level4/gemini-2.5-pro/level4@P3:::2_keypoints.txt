- Proposes a meta-learning method where an LSTM-based meta-learner learns an optimizer for a separate learner network to tackle few-shot classification.
- Treats learner parameters as the LSTM cell state, enabling learning of a shared initialization (c0) and a history-dependent update rule via learned gates.
- Draws an analogy between gradient-descent updates and recurrent cell updates to justify the LSTM-based optimizer.
- Evaluated on Mini-ImageNet, achieving competitive or superior results to state-of-the-art metric-learning methods (notably better in 5-shot).
- Experimental comparisons include strong baselines (fine-tuning, nearest-neighbor) and Matching Networks, supporting empirical claims.
- Authors handle Batch Normalization carefully to avoid information leakage between episodes, bolstering experimental rigor.
- Decision: Accept — primarily due to the novelty/elegance of the approach and strong empirical performance.
- Strength: formulation naturally addresses poor random initialization and inefficient optimization in low-data regimes.
- Suggest clarifying the model analogy since the update rule resembles a GRU more than a standard LSTM.
- Recommend a diagram to clarify class splits into meta-train/validation/test and episode sampling (Section 5).
- Request ablation studies to isolate the contributions of learned input (it) and forget (ft) gates versus simpler alternatives.
- Suggest replacing or repurposing Figure 2 (generic computational graph) for a data-split diagram or detailed per-step meta-learner illustration.
- Minor typo: "it's" should be "its" in Section 3.2.
- Positive note: authors plan to release code, which will aid reproducibility and community uptake.
- Questions for authors: relative importance of learned c0 vs learned optimizer (e.g., using c0 + ADAM or random init + LSTM optimizer).
- Questions for authors: clarification on gate visualizations—does ft act like slight weight decay and does variability in it imply task-specific strategies?
- Questions for authors: have they tried non-batch-dependent normalization (Layer Norm, Weight Norm) in the learner, and how would that affect training/testing complexity and performance?
