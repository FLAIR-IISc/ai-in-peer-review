This paper presents a meta-learning framework that uses an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm for another neural network.

The paper is well-written, with a clear presentation of its core material. The central idea—drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM's update mechanism to satisfy the two primary desiderata of few-shot learning (rapid acquisition of new knowledge and slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge)—is particularly intriguing. The authors effectively motivate their design choices, which include both techniques adapted from prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, and novel elements like their specific implementation of batch normalization.

The experimental results are convincing, making this a strong paper. My only concerns and questions are the following:

1.  Is it potentially redundant to use the loss, gradient, and parameters as simultaneous inputs to the meta-learner? Have the authors performed any ablation studies to determine if a simpler combination of inputs would be sufficient?

2.  It would be compelling if other architectural components of the network (e.g., number of neurons, unit types) could be learned in a similar fashion. I would be interested in the authors' perspective on this possibility.

3.  The related work section, which focuses primarily on meta-learning, is somewhat shallow. Meta-learning is a well-established field, and similar approaches have been explored in the past, even without the use of LSTMs. The review would be strengthened by including:
    *   Samy Bengio's 1989 PhD thesis, which is highly relevant to this topic.
    *   Work on using genetic programming to search for new learning rules for neural networks (e.g., S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
    *   Relevant contributions from Jürgen Schmidhuber, which likely exist and should be included after a thorough literature search.

Overall, I view the paper favorably. The material discussed is relevant and should be of interest to a wide audience at ICLR.
