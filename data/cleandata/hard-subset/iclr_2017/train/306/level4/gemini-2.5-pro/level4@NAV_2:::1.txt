This paper introduces a novel meta-learning approach for few-shot classification. The core contribution is an LSTM-based "meta-learner" designed to learn an effective optimization algorithm for a separate "learner" neural network classifier. The authors draw a clever parallel between the gradient descent update rule and the cell state update within an LSTM. This formulation allows the meta-learner to accomplish two key goals simultaneously: it learns a general parameter initialization for the learner that facilitates rapid convergence, and it learns a specific update rule that is optimized for adapting to new tasks within a small, fixed number of steps. The paper carefully details the model architecture, including practical considerations like parameter sharing and a well-reasoned approach to using Batch Normalization in the meta-learning context. The empirical results on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark demonstrate that this method outperforms strong baselines and is competitive with state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are the novelty and elegance of the proposed approach, and the strong empirical evidence supporting its effectiveness.

The main strength of this work lies in its conceptualization of few-shot learning as a problem of learning an optimization algorithm. The insight to model the parameter update as an LSTM cell state update is highly innovative and effectively addresses the two main challenges in the few-shot regime: finding a good starting point (learning the initial cell state `c_0`) and making efficient use of limited data (learning the input and forget gates `i_t` and `f_t`). This approach provides a principled way to learn both a task-agnostic prior and a task-specific adaptation mechanism within a single, end-to-end differentiable model. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare their model not only against sensible baselines like fine-tuning and nearest-neighbors but also against a strong contemporary method, Matching Networks. The competitive results, particularly in the 5-shot setting, convincingly demonstrate that the proposed meta-learning optimizer is a viable and powerful alternative to metric-learning approaches. The clarity of the paper's writing and the thoughtful discussion of implementation details further contribute to its quality.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not critical to my assessment.

*   The related work section provides a solid overview of recent methods. However, the concept of learning update rules has a long history in the field. Including references to foundational work, such as the early research by Schmidhuber on networks modifying their own weights (e.g., Schmidhuber, 1992; 1993) and by Bengio et al. on learning biologically plausible update rules (e.g., Bengio et al., 1990; 1995), would provide valuable historical context and better situate the current contribution within the broader intellectual lineage of "learning to learn."
*   The visualization of the gate activations in Figure 3 offers a glimpse into the learned strategy. Expanding this analysis could be very insightful. For example, does the meta-learner systematically decrease the learning rate (input gate) over time, or does it exhibit more dynamic, data-dependent behavior? Correlating gate activations with properties of the learner's gradient (e.g., norm, sparsity) could reveal more about the learned optimization policy.

I have a few questions for the authors that would clarify my understanding:

1.  The meta-learner's input includes the learner's loss, gradient, previous parameters, and the previous gate activations. Did you perform any ablation studies on these inputs? For example, is it possible to achieve similar performance using only the gradient and loss, or is the inclusion of the previous parameter values `θ_{t-1}` critical?
2.  The paper focuses on learning the update rule for the learner's parameters. Do the authors have any thoughts on whether this meta-learning framework could be extended to learn other aspects of the learner, such as its architecture or regularization strategies, for the few-shot setting?
