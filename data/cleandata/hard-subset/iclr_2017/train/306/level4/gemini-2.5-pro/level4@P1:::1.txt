This paper presents a novel LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn an optimization algorithm for a separate learner network, specifically targeting the few-shot classification problem. The paper is well-written, and the core concepts are presented with clarity. The central idea—drawing an analogy between the gradient descent update and the LSTM cell state update to address the key challenges of few-shot learning (i.e., requiring both rapid knowledge acquisition and the extraction of general, transferable knowledge)—is intriguing and well-motivated. The authors effectively leverage techniques from prior work, such as parameter sharing and gradient preprocessing, while also introducing novel and well-justified design choices, such as their specific implementation of Batch Normalization for the meta-learning setting. The experiments are convincing and demonstrate the strength of the proposed approach.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The novelty and elegance of the proposed approach, which frames optimization as a learnable process to directly tackle the core challenges of few-shot learning.
2.  The strong empirical results that validate the method's effectiveness, showing performance that is competitive with state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its compelling formulation of the few-shot learning problem. The authors clearly identify why standard gradient-based optimizers fail in this regime—namely, their need for many iterative updates and the handicap of starting from a random parameter initialization for each new task. The proposed meta-learner directly addresses both issues by learning a task-agnostic initial state for the learner's parameters (c0) and a specialized update rule that is optimized for rapid convergence in just a few steps.

The technical execution is solid. The model's design is well-reasoned, and the practical implementation details are thoroughly explained. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the model against appropriate baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) and a strong state-of-the-art competitor (Matching Networks). The results convincingly show that the meta-learner outperforms the baselines and is highly competitive with Matching Networks, even outperforming it on the 5-shot Mini-ImageNet task. This demonstrates that learning an explicit optimization process is a viable and powerful alternative to metric-learning for few-shot classification.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision. I believe the discussed material is relevant to a wide audience at ICLR.

1.  **Ablation Study on Meta-Learner Inputs:** The meta-learner takes the loss, gradient, and current parameters as input to compute the next update. Could this be redundant? An ablation study analyzing the impact of removing some of these inputs (e.g., using only the gradient and loss) would be valuable. Such a study could provide deeper insight into what information is most critical for the learned optimizer to function effectively.

2.  **Scope of Meta-Learned Components:** This work successfully learns the parameter initialization and update rule. Do you have an opinion on whether this framework could be extended to learn other architectural components of the learner network, such as the number of neurons or the type of activation functions? A brief discussion on the future potential and limitations of this approach would be an interesting addition.

3.  **Related Work Context:** The related work section is serviceable but could be strengthened by providing more historical context. Meta-learning is a long-standing field, and while the paper cites key early works from Schmidhuber and Bengio et al., the discussion could be deepened. For instance, elaborating on the connections and differences with early work on learning synaptic update rules using different optimization methods (e.g., the work described in Samy Bengio's PhD thesis or by Bengio et al. in the early 1990s) would better situate this paper's contributions within the field's history. Highlighting how modern tools like LSTMs and end-to-end differentiability advance these foundational ideas would make the paper's impact even clearer.
