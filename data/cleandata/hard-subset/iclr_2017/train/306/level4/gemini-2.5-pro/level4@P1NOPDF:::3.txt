In light of the authors' responsiveness and the updates to the manuscript—in particular the clarification of the meta-learning task—I am updating my score to 8.

This manuscript proposes to tackle few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a classic idea that has seen a recent resurgence. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each meta-example consists of a sequence of "training" batches followed by a final "test" batch. The inputs at each step of the sequence include outputs from a "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradients) as well as the base learner's current state (its parameters).

The paper applies an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, using the memory cells of its second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. A key insight is the noted similarity between the update rules for LSTM memory cells and for gradient descent. Updates to the LSTM meta-learner are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss on the final "test" batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells, which is analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters. The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset proposed in Vinyals et al. (2016) and shows that the LSTM meta-learner is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks) on 1-shot and 5-shot learning tasks.

**Strengths:**

*   Casting the few-shot problem as a sequential meta-learning problem is an intriguing and, in hindsight, natural formulation.
*   While the general idea of persisting learning across a series of tasks is not new, the authors' formulation appears to advance the state of the art. Although I am not deeply familiar with recent work on meta-learning and cannot confidently assert its novelty, the contribution seems significant.
*   The proposed approach is competitive with, and in some cases outperforms, the Matching Networks of Vinyals et al. (2016) on the 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
*   The base learner—a simple convolutional classifier—is notably different from the nearest-neighbor classifier applied to a learned embedding, as used in Vinyals et al. It is encouraging to see state-of-the-art results achieved with such a distinct approach, rather than an incremental modification of existing work.
*   The insight into the relationship between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent appears to be novel and is interesting in its own right.
*   The paper provides several practical insights for designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, which will be valuable for reproducing this work and applying these ideas to new problems. These include techniques for parameter initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and the normalization of inputs (loss, gradients, and parameters). While some of these points have been discussed in prior work (e.g., the importance of simulating test conditions during meta-training), their clear presentation here is a useful contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The primary weakness of the initial submission was its clarity. The manuscript describes compelling work, but the writing was often opaque, making it a challenging read. It required multiple readings and consultation of related work to fully grasp the proposed learning framework.
*   The task description in Section 2 was highly abstract, using notation and terminology that may be unfamiliar to readers outside this specific subfield. The paper would be more accessible to a broader audience if it included a concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) and a diagram illustrating the sequence of few-shot learning tasks.
*   Relatedly, several details of the N-class, few-shot learning setup were initially unclear. For instance, regarding the Mini-ImageNet dataset (split into 64/16/20 classes for meta-training/validation/testing), it was not specified whether the 64 meta-training classes were the *only* classes seen during this phase, or if all 100 classes were sampled from, with 64 appearing in any given batch. This ambiguity raised further questions about the architecture of the base learner's softmax layer during meta-training. Many similar details were also unclear.
*   The plots in Figure 2 are difficult to interpret on their own, and the accompanying discussion section offers little insight into their meaning.

**Conclusion:**

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. The core idea of using an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm for a base model is compelling, and the connection drawn to gradient descent is a novel insight. The experimental results on Mini-ImageNet are strong, demonstrating performance competitive with the state of the art via a significantly different methodology. While my initial review raised serious concerns about the clarity of the presentation, the authors have addressed the most critical points in their response and the revised manuscript. The paper is a solid contribution to the fields of meta-learning and few-shot learning, and I recommend acceptance.
