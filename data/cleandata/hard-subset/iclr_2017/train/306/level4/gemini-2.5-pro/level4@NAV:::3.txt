This manuscript presents a method for addressing few-shot learning with neural networks by employing a meta-learning framework, an established concept that has experienced a resurgence in interest over the past year. The authors frame the few-shot learning challenge as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each meta-example comprises a sequence of "training" batches followed by a final "test" batch. At each step in the sequence, the inputs to the meta-learner include the outputs from a "base learner" (such as its training loss and gradients) along with the base learner's current parameters.

The paper applies an LSTM to this meta-learning task, innovatively using the second-layer inner memory cells to directly model the parameter updates of the base learner. In developing this, the authors observe a similarity between the update rules of LSTM memory cells and the mechanics of gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is trained based on the base learner's prediction loss on the final "test" batch. To make the model tractable, the authors employ several simplifying strategies, like sharing weights across all second-layer cells, which is analogous to using a single learning rate for all parameters. The authors reproduce the Mini-ImageNet dataset from Vinyals et al. (2016) and show that their meta-learner LSTM achieves competitive results against the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks) on 1-shot and 5-shot learning benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
*   The conceptualization of few-shot learning as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is both compelling and, in retrospect, intuitive. Although the general principle of maintaining learning across a sequence of tasks is not new, the authors appear to have advanced the state of the art. However, not being an expert in recent meta-learning literature, I cannot confidently attest to its absolute novelty.
*   The proposed methodology is competitive with and, in some cases, surpasses the performance of Vinyals (2016) in the 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
*   The base learner employed here—a straightforward ConvNet classifier—is fundamentally different from the nearest-neighbor approach applied to learned embeddings used by Vinyals (2016). It is encouraging to see state-of-the-art performance achieved through a distinct methodology rather than an incremental modification.
*   To my knowledge, the observation regarding the connection between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent updates is a novel contribution of this paper and is interesting in its own right.
*   The paper provides several practical insights into designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, which should facilitate replication and the application of these concepts to new problems. These insights include proper initialization, coordinate-wise weight sharing, and the importance of normalizing inputs such as the loss, gradients, and parameters. While some of these points have been discussed in prior work (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training), their inclusion here is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The writing is occasionally opaque, which detracts from the reading experience despite the interesting content. I found it necessary to make multiple passes through the paper and consult related work to fully grasp the learning problem. The task description in Section 2 is highly abstract, using notation and terminology that may be unfamiliar to a broader audience. The paper's accessibility would be greatly improved by including a brief, concrete example (perhaps using MNIST) accompanied by a diagram to illustrate the flow of a few-shot learning task sequence.
*   Following the previous point, the exact nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is not entirely clear. Specifically, for the Mini-ImageNet dataset (split into 64/16/20 classes for meta-training/validation/testing), it is ambiguous whether only 64 of the 100 classes are ever seen during meta-training. If so, what is the output dimension of the ConvNet base learner's softmax layer during this phase—64 or 100? Numerous other details of this nature remain unclear.
*   The plots presented in Figure 2 are not very informative on their own, and the discussion section offers little insight into their meaning or implications.

This is a compelling paper with convincing results that appears to be a clear candidate for acceptance. However, the presentation of the ideas and the overall work could be significantly enhanced. I will certainly consider raising my score if the clarity of the manuscript is improved.
