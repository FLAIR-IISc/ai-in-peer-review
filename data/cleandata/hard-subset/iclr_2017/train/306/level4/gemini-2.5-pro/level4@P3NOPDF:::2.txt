### Summary

This paper introduces a novel and compelling approach to meta-learning by re-framing the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) update rule as a trainable, gated recurrent model. The core idea of learning an optimization algorithm tailored to a specific class of problems is original and holds significant potential for research in transfer learning and few-shot learning. The paper is well-structured, presenting a clear motivation and methodology. The empirical results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods. While the overall contribution is strong, the paper could be improved with greater clarity in its experimental description and a more thorough analysis of the learned optimizer's components.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central idea of modeling the optimizer as a recurrent neural network with trainable parameters is highly original and elegant. This approach allows the learning algorithm itself to be learned directly from data, providing a principled way to incorporate knowledge from related tasks into the optimization process. This is an important contribution to the meta-learning field.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method achieves competitive results when compared against relevant and established baselines on the presented tasks. The empirical validation demonstrates that the learned optimizer is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical and effective tool.
3.  **Practical Recommendations:** The paper provides useful insights and recommendations for implementing such a system in practice (e.g., coordinate-wise preprocessing), which adds to the value of the work for other researchers and practitioners looking to build upon it.

### Areas for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of Experimental Setup:** The description of the data separation into meta-sets, training sets, and test sets is difficult to follow. A clear understanding of this protocol is crucial for interpreting the results and reproducing the experiments. The addition of a diagram or figure visualizing this data partitioning scheme would significantly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the work.
2.  **Insufficient Experimental Analysis:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating competitive performance, feels incomplete. A deeper analysis of the learned optimizer's behavior would substantially strengthen the paper. Specifically, an ablation study on the impact of the learned parameters for the input gate (`i_t`) and the forget gate (`f_t`) would be of great interest. Such an analysis would provide valuable insight into *how* the meta-learner works, what optimization strategies it discovers, and which components are most critical to its success.
3.  **Analogy to Recurrent Architectures:** The paper draws an analogy between the proposed update rule and an LSTM. However, given the use of components that function as input and forget gates without an explicit memory cell or output gate, the architecture appears more closely related to a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU). Clarifying this analogy or providing a stronger justification for the comparison to LSTMs would improve the technical precision of the description.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Figure 2:** In its current form, Figure 2 provides limited insight into the learned optimizer's behavior. A more illustrative figure could, for example, compare the optimization trajectory of the learned optimizer against a standard optimizer (like Adam) on a more complex, non-trivial loss surface to better highlight its advantages.
*   **Code Release:** The authors state, "We plan on releasing the code used in our evaluation experiments." This is highly encouraged and would be a major asset to the community, ensuring reproducibility and facilitating future research in this promising direction.
*   **Minor Typo:** In Section 3.2, there is a small typo: "This means each coordinate has it" should be corrected to "This means each coordinate has its".

### Conclusion

Overall, this paper presents a creative and well-executed contribution to the field of meta-learning. The central idea is strong, and the results are promising. The work would be significantly improved by addressing the points of clarity regarding the experimental setup and by providing a more in-depth analysis of the learned optimizer's internal mechanics. With these revisions, this paper would represent a solid and impactful contribution to the conference.
