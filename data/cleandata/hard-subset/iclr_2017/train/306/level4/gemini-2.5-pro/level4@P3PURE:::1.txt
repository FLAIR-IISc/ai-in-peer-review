This work presents a meta-learning framework based on an LSTM, which learns an optimization algorithm for another neural network (the "optimizee").

The paper is well-written, and the core ideas are presented clearly. The central insight of the paper is particularly intriguing: it draws a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the update mechanism of an LSTM. This parallel is then leveraged to satisfy two key desiderata for few-shot learning: the rapid acquisition of new knowledge and the slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge. The authors effectively adapt techniques from prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, and introduce well-motivated novel design choices, including a specific implementation of batch normalization.

The experimental results are convincing, making this a strong paper overall. My only concerns and questions are the following:

1.  The choice of inputs to the meta-learner (loss, gradient, and parameters) seems potentially redundant. Have the authors conducted ablation studies to determine if a simpler combination of these inputs would suffice?

2.  The framework focuses on learning the optimization algorithm. Could a similar meta-learning approach be extended to learn other architectural components of the optimizee network, such as the number of neurons or the type of activation units? A discussion on the potential for such extensions would be valuable.

3.  The related work section, which focuses primarily on recent meta-learning literature, could be strengthened. Meta-learning is a long-established field, and similar concepts have been explored previously, even without the use of LSTMs. The authors should consider including a discussion of seminal work such as:
    *   Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989), which is highly relevant.
    *   The use of genetic programming to search for new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
    *   Furthermore, given Schmidhuber's extensive work in this area, a thorough literature search to include any pertinent precursors would strengthen the paper's historical context.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper. I believe the material presented is relevant and will be of significant interest to the ICLR community.
