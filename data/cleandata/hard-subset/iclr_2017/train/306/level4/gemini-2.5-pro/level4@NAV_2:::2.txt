This paper presents a novel meta-learning approach for few-shot classification. The core contribution is to frame the optimization of a learner network as a learnable process, executed by a separate LSTM-based meta-learner model. This meta-learner is trained to output both an optimal parameter initialization and a sequence of parameter updates for the learner, tailored to achieve rapid convergence on new tasks with few examples. The authors demonstrate that by directly optimizing for generalization performance after a fixed number of updates, their method achieves results that are competitive with state-of-the-art deep metric-learning techniques on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark.

I recommend to **Accept** this paper. My decision is based on two key strengths:
1.  The proposed method is highly original and conceptually elegant. It provides a principled way to learn an optimization algorithm, including a good initialization, specifically for the few-shot learning setting.
2.  The empirical evaluation is strong, showing that the model is competitive with or superior to powerful baselines, including a state-of-the-art method, Matching Networks, on a challenging benchmark.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The main strength of this work lies in its creative formulation of the few-shot learning problem. By observing the similarity between the gradient descent update rule and the cell-state update in an LSTM, the authors design a meta-learner that explicitly learns how to train another network. This approach directly addresses the two primary weaknesses of standard gradient-based optimization in the low-data regime identified by the paper: the lack of a suitable parameter initialization and the use of generic update rules not designed for a small, fixed number of steps. Learning the initial LSTM cell state corresponds to finding an optimal weight initialization for the learner, while the learned input and forget gates provide a sophisticated, data-dependent update mechanism.

This strong conceptual contribution is well-supported by convincing experimental results. The authors conduct a rigorous comparison on the Mini-ImageNet dataset against relevant baselines. The fact that the proposed meta-learner significantly outperforms a standard fine-tuning approach highlights the benefits of learning the initialization and update rule end-to-end. Furthermore, its ability to outperform Matching Networks in the 5-shot setting and perform competitively in the 1-shot setting validates the model as a viable and powerful alternative to the dominant metric-learning paradigm for few-shot learning. The authors' plan to release the code is a welcome contribution that will benefit the community.

**Additional Feedback and Questions:**
The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Clarity on Data Splits:** The description of how datasets are partitioned into meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets in Section 2 is difficult to follow. A simple diagram visualizing this process—showing how the overall set of classes is split, and how individual k-shot, N-way "episodes" are constructed from these splits—would greatly enhance the clarity of the experimental setup.
*   **Model Analogy:** The paper draws an analogy to the LSTM update. However, the update rule `θt = ft * θt−1 + it * ∇Lt` more closely resembles a GRU, which also directly updates its hidden state without a separate output gate. Acknowledging this connection could provide additional context for readers familiar with recurrent architectures.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The experimental section is solid, but would be strengthened by a more in-depth analysis of the meta-learner's components. The visualizations in Figure 3 are a good start, but an ablation study would provide clearer insights. For example, how does performance change if the forget gate `ft` is fixed to 1 (i.e., simple weight decay is removed) or if the learning rate `it` is a simple learned scalar rather than a function of the loss, gradient, and parameters? This would help quantify the contribution of the more complex learned dynamics.
*   **Value of Figures:** Figure 2, which shows the computational graph, is highly abstract and adds little explanatory value beyond what is already conveyed in Algorithm 1. The space might be better used for the data-split diagram suggested above.
*   **Minor Typo:** In Section 3.2, "This means each coordinate has it own hidden and cell state" should be "...has its own...".

I have a few questions for the authors to help clarify my understanding:
1.  The meta-learner is described as a 2-layer LSTM. Could you elaborate on the role of the first, standard LSTM layer? How does it preprocess the inputs for the second, modified LSTM layer that implements the update rule?
2.  Regarding Batch Normalization (Section 3.4), you note that during meta-training, batch statistics are used for both the training and test sets of an episode, but during meta-testing, running averages are used for the test set. Does this difference not introduce a mismatch between meta-training and meta-testing conditions, a factor the paper otherwise emphasizes as crucial for good performance?
