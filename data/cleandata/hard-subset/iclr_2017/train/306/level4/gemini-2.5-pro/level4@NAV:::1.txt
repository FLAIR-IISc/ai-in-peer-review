This paper introduces a meta-learning framework that employs a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network to learn an optimization algorithm for a separate, target neural network.

The manuscript is well-articulated, and the core concepts are presented with clarity. A particularly compelling aspect of this work is the parallel drawn between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the update mechanism of an LSTM. The authors leverage this connection to address two primary goals in few-shot learning: the rapid acquisition of task-specific information and the slower consolidation of general, transferable knowledge. This insight is intriguing. The paper also provides strong motivation for its design choices, including both the adaptation of existing techniques from prior work (e.g., parameter sharing and normalization from Andrychowicz et al., 2016) and novel contributions like a specific batch normalization implementation.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides convincing support for the proposed framework, positioning this as a strong contribution to the field. My primary questions and suggestions are as follows:

1.  Regarding the inputs to the meta-learner (loss, gradient, and parameters), is there a potential for redundancy? I would be interested to know if the authors performed any ablation studies to assess whether a simpler subset of these inputs might yield comparable performance.
2.  The proposed approach focuses on learning the optimization algorithm. It would be interesting to hear the authors' perspective on the feasibility of extending this framework to learn other architectural aspects of the target network, such as the number of hidden units or the type of activation functions.
3.  The literature review, while covering recent meta-learning, could be strengthened by acknowledging the deeper history of this topic. The concept of learning a learning rule has been explored in earlier work, and including these seminal contributions would provide valuable context. For instance, relevant prior art includes:
    *   The foundational work in Samy Bengio's 1989 PhD thesis.
    *   Early approaches using genetic programming to discover learning rules for neural networks (e.g., S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
    *   It is also highly probable that relevant work by Schmidhuber exists on this topic, which would be a valuable addition.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that I support for acceptance. The research presented is highly relevant and should be of considerable interest to the ICLR community.
