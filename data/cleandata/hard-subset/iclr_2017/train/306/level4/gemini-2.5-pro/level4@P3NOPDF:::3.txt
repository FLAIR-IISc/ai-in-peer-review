### Summary

This manuscript addresses the problem of few-shot learning by framing it as a meta-learning task. The authors propose a sequential model where each meta-example consists of a series of training data batches followed by a final test batch. The core of their model is an LSTM meta-learner that processes a sequence of inputs derived from a "base learner" network, including its training loss and gradients.

A key insight of this work is to use the LSTM's internal cell state to directly represent and update the parameters of the base learner. The authors draw an insightful analogy between the update rule of an LSTM cell and a gradient descent step, providing a natural motivation for their architecture. To make the problem tractable, they introduce several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing the meta-learner's weights across all parameters of the base learner, which is analogous to using a single, learned learning rate for all parameters. The meta-learner itself is trained by minimizing the base learner's prediction loss on the final "test" batch of each sequence.

To evaluate their method, the authors recreate the Mini-ImageNet dataset proposed by Vinyals et al. (2016). Their experiments demonstrate that the LSTM meta-learner achieves results that are competitive with, and in some cases surpass, the state-of-the-art performance of Matching Networks on 1-shot and 5-shot learning tasks.

### Strengths

*   **Elegant Formulation:** The framing of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is elegant and effective. While meta-learning for few-shot tasks is not entirely new, this sequential formulation represents a significant conceptual advance.
*   **State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed approach achieves excellent empirical results, outperforming the Matching Networks baseline on both 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet classification tasks.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** It is particularly notable that these strong results are achieved with a base learner (a standard ConvNet) that is conceptually distinct from the non-parametric, nearest-neighbor-style classifiers used in concurrent work. This demonstrates the viability of multiple distinct approaches to this challenging problem.
*   **Theoretical Insight:** The insight connecting the LSTM cell update mechanism to gradient descent is novel and provides a strong theoretical motivation for the model architecture.
*   **Practical Guidance:** The paper provides valuable practical guidance for implementing and training such meta-learners. Details on parameter initialization, weight sharing, and input normalization will be highly beneficial for researchers aiming to replicate or extend this work. The discussion of these practical considerations, some of which have been noted in prior work, is a useful contribution in its own right.

### Weaknesses

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The primary weakness of the paper is its clarity. The description of the learning problem in Section 2 is highly abstract and uses specialized notation that may be difficult for readers not already deeply familiar with this subfield to follow. The paper would be significantly more accessible to a broader audience if it included a simple, concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) accompanied by a diagram illustrating the flow of a single meta-learning task sequence.
*   **Ambiguity in Experimental Setup:** The description of the N-way, k-shot experimental setup on Mini-ImageNet lacks critical details needed for reproducibility. Specifically, the use of the 64/16/20 class split for meta-training/validation/testing is unclear. Are the meta-test classes entirely disjoint from the meta-training classes? If so, does the base learner's final classification layer have 64 outputs during meta-training, or are all 100 classes included? Clarifying these details is essential.
*   **Uninformative Figures:** The plots in Figure 2 are difficult to interpret on their own, and the accompanying discussion in the text provides limited insight into their significance.

### Conclusion

This is a strong paper with compelling results and a novel, well-motivated approach to few-shot learning. Its main limitation is the presentation, which could be significantly improved for clarity and accessibility. Addressing the ambiguities in the experimental setup is also crucial. Despite these issues with presentation, the technical contribution is significant, and I recommend acceptance.
