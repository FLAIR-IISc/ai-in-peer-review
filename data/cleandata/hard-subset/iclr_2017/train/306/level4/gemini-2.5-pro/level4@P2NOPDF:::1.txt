This paper proposes a meta-learning framework based on a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network to learn an optimization algorithm for a separate neural network, referred to as the "learner."

The paper is well-written, and the core concepts are presented with clarity. The central and most compelling idea is the analogy drawn between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the update dynamics of an LSTM. The authors insightfully exploit this parallel to address two key desiderata of few-shot learning: the rapid acquisition of task-specific knowledge and the slower, more stable extraction of general, transferable knowledge. This connection provides a strong theoretical motivation for the proposed approach.

Furthermore, the authors make several well-motivated design choices. These include adopting established techniques from prior work, such as parameter sharing and input normalization (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), as well as introducing novel contributions, such as a specific batch normalization implementation tailored for this framework. The empirical results are convincing and effectively demonstrate the viability of the method.

Overall, this is a strong paper. My main questions and suggestions for improvement are as follows:

1.  **Input Feature Redundancy:** The meta-learner's input consists of the loss, the gradient, and the previous parameters of the learner network. This raises a question of potential redundancy. Have the authors conducted any ablation studies to investigate whether a simpler combination of inputs (e.g., only gradients and previous parameters) could achieve comparable performance? Justifying this specific input configuration would further strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scope of Meta-Learning:** The current work focuses on learning the optimization update rule. A natural and exciting extension would be to learn other aspects of the network architecture, such as the number of neurons, the type of activation functions, or even the connectivity patterns. Could the authors comment on the feasibility or challenges of extending their framework in these directions?

3.  **Related Work:** The section on related work, while covering recent advances, could be significantly strengthened by acknowledging the deeper history of meta-learning. The concept of "learning to learn" is a foundational topic in machine learning, and several seminal works appear to have been overlooked. For instance:
    *   The problem of learning an update rule was a central theme in Samy Bengio's 1989 Ph.D. thesis.
    *   Bengio, S., Bengio, Y., & Cloutier, J. (1994) explored the use of genetic programming to search for novel learning rules for neural networks.
    *   Given his extensive work on this topic, a search of Jürgen Schmidhuber's publications would likely reveal relevant prior art.

    A more thorough review of this foundational literature would better contextualize the paper's contributions within the broader historical landscape of the field and provide a richer background for the reader.

Despite these points, this is a high-quality and well-executed paper. The proposed method is elegant, the experiments are supportive, and the core ideas are thought-provoking. The work addresses a timely and important problem, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the ICLR community.
