This paper addresses the problem of few-shot learning in neural networks by employing a meta-learning approach, a classic idea that has seen a recent resurgence of interest. The authors frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning task, where each meta-example consists of a sequence of training batches followed by a final test batch. At each step in the sequence, the meta-learner receives inputs such as the training loss, gradients, and the current parameters of a "base learner" model.

The proposed meta-learner is an LSTM, which uses the memory cells of its second layer to directly represent and update the parameters of the base learner. The authors draw an insightful connection between the update rule for LSTM memory cells and the standard gradient descent update. The LSTM meta-learner is trained to minimize the base learner's prediction loss on the final test batch. To make the model practical, several simplifying assumptions are introduced, such as sharing weights across all cells in the second LSTM layer, which is analogous to using a single learning rate for all parameters in a traditional optimizer.

Experiments are conducted on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, where the proposed LSTM meta-learner achieves results that are competitive with, and in some cases surpass, the state-of-the-art Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot learning tasks.

**Strengths:**
*   The formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is both intriguing and natural. While the general concept of "learning to learn" across tasks is not new, this work appears to significantly advance the state of the art in this direction. (Note: I am not an expert in the meta-learning literature, so my assessment of novelty is not definitive.)
*   The empirical results on Mini-ImageNet are strong, showing performance that is competitive with and sometimes exceeds the previous state-of-the-art on 1-shot and 5-shot tasks.
*   The proposed method achieves these results using a simple ConvNet classifier as the base learner, which is a fundamentally different approach from the embedding-and-nearest-neighbor method of Matching Networks. It is compelling to see a distinct and non-incremental approach achieve state-of-the-art performance.
*   The connection drawn between the LSTM memory cell update and the gradient descent update rule appears to be a novel and valuable insight.
*   The paper provides several practical design choices and training heuristics for the LSTM meta-learner, which should aid in reproducibility and future work. These include specific initialization strategies, coordinate-wise weight sharing, and the normalization of inputs like loss and gradients. While some of these practices have been noted in prior work (e.g., matching meta-training and meta-test conditions), their collation and discussion here are useful for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The manuscript's presentation could be significantly improved. The writing is often opaque, which makes it difficult to grasp the core ideas without multiple readings and consultation of related work.
*   The problem description in Section 2 is highly abstract, employing notation that may be unfamiliar to readers outside this specific subfield. The paper's accessibility would be greatly enhanced by including a simple, concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) and a diagram to illustrate the flow of a single meta-learning episode.
*   The description of the experimental setup for the Mini-ImageNet task lacks clarity. The dataset contains 100 classes, which are split into 64 for meta-training, 16 for meta-validation, and 20 for meta-testing. It is unclear if "meta-training" involves only ever seeing the 64 training classes, or if all 100 classes are seen over the course of meta-training, just drawn from a 64-class pool for any given episode. This ambiguity raises further questions: for instance, what is the size of the base learner's final softmax layer during meta-training? Is it 64, or 100 (with 36 outputs never being used)? Clarification on these and other experimental details is needed.
*   Figure 2 is difficult to interpret, and the corresponding discussion in the text provides limited insight into what the plots are meant to convey.

**Conclusion:**
This is an interesting paper with convincing empirical results. While the core ideas are strong and the work appears to be a clear candidate for acceptance, the quality of the presentation is a significant weakness. I will raise my score if the authors can improve the clarity of the manuscript.
