### **Summary**

This paper proposes a meta-learning framework where a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network is trained to act as an optimizer for a separate, target neural network. The central contribution is the insightful parallel drawn between the update rule of an LSTM and the Robbins-Monro stochastic approximation algorithm. The authors leverage this connection to address two key desiderata for few-shot learning: the rapid acquisition of task-specific knowledge and the slower, more stable extraction of general, transferable knowledge across tasks.

### **Strengths**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, and the core ideas are presented with exceptional clarity. The motivation behind the proposed approach is easy to follow.

*   **Novel Conceptual Framing:** The core conceptual contribution—framing the LSTM update as a form of the Robbins-Monro update rule—is both novel and insightful. This framing provides a strong theoretical motivation for why this architecture is well-suited for meta-learning, particularly for balancing fast adaptation with slow, general knowledge acquisition.

*   **Well-Motivated Design:** The paper's design choices, including the use of parameter sharing and input normalization adapted from prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), as well as a novel implementation of batch normalization, are well-justified and contribute to the model's effectiveness.

*   **Convincing Empirical Results:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the proposed method's efficacy on challenging few-shot learning benchmarks.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

Despite the paper's strengths, there are a few areas that could be clarified or improved:

1.  **Input Redundancy and Ablation:** The meta-learner's input consists of the loss, the gradient of the loss with respect to the parameters, and the parameters themselves. This raises a question of potential redundancy. For instance, the gradient is a direct function of the parameters and the data used to compute the loss. Have the authors conducted any ablation studies to determine if a simpler input combination (e.g., only gradients and parameters) would suffice? Understanding the contribution of each input component would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights for future work in this area.

2.  **Broader Architectural Learning:** The work focuses on learning the optimization procedure. A natural extension would be to learn other aspects of the network's architecture, such as the number of hidden units, activation functions, or connectivity patterns. While this is likely beyond the scope of the current work, it would be valuable if the authors could comment on the potential adaptability of their framework to these more challenging structural search problems.

3.  **Depth of Related Work:** The section on related work, while covering recent advances, could be strengthened by acknowledging the deeper history of meta-learning and "learning to learn." The core idea of training one model to tune the learning rule for another is a long-standing concept in machine learning. I would strongly encourage the authors to review and incorporate references to seminal works in this area, which explored similar goals even without modern recurrent architectures. For example:
    *   The early work by Samy Bengio on learning learning rules (e.g., his 1989 PhD thesis).
    *   Research on using genetic programming to evolve learning rules for neural networks (e.g., Bengio, Bengio, and Cloutier, 1994, "Use of genetic programming for the search of a new learning rule for neural networks").
    *   The extensive body of work by Jürgen Schmidhuber on meta-learning and self-referential learning, which is highly relevant to this topic. A thorough review would better position the current contribution within the broader historical context of the field.

### **Conclusion**

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and well-motivated approach to meta-learning for optimization. The connection between LSTMs and classic stochastic approximation methods is a valuable contribution, and the experimental results are compelling. While the paper could be improved by addressing the questions regarding input feature ablation and by significantly expanding the discussion of historical related work, these points do not detract from the core merits of the research. The work is timely, interesting, and should be of great interest to the ICLR community.
