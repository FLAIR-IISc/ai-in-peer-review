This paper presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework for learning the optimization algorithm for another learner, in this case, a neural network.

Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly presents its main contributions. The core idea of the paper—drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe and LSTM update rules and leveraging it to satisfy the two main desiderata of few-shot learning (the rapid acquisition of new knowledge and the slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge)—is particularly intriguing. Several design choices, including techniques adapted from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) such as parameter sharing and normalization, as well as novel contributions like a specific implementation of batch normalization, are well-motivated.

The experimental results are convincing, making this a strong paper. My main questions and suggestions are as follows:

1.  Is it not redundant to use the loss, gradient, and parameters simultaneously as input to the meta-learner? Have the authors performed any ablation studies to determine if a simpler combination of these inputs would be sufficient?

2.  It would be interesting to see if this meta-learning approach could be extended to learn other architectural components, such as the number of neurons or the type of activation units. I would be curious to hear the authors' perspective on this possibility.

3.  The related work section, which focuses primarily on meta-learning, feels somewhat incomplete. Meta-learning is a well-established field, and similar approaches to learning optimization rules have been explored in the past, even without LSTMs. For instance:
    *   Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989) explored many of these core ideas.
    *   Bengio, Bengio, and Cloutier (1994) used genetic programming to search for new learning rules for neural networks.
    *   Additionally, I am convinced that Jürgen Schmidhuber has published relevant work in this area, which should also be surveyed and included in the related work section.

In summary, this is an excellent paper. I believe its contributions will be of significant interest to a wide audience at ICLR.
