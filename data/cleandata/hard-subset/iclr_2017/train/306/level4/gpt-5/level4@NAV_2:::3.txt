This paper addresses few-shot learning with neural networks via meta-learning, a long-standing idea that has recently regained attention. The authors cast few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each “example” consists of a sequence of batches from a small “training” set, followed by a final “test” batch. At each step, inputs include the base learner’s outputs (e.g., training loss and gradients) and the base learner’s current parameters. An LSTM is applied to this meta-learning task, with the memory cells of the second LSTM layer directly representing the evolving parameters of the base learner. The authors highlight the correspondence between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent. The meta-learner itself is trained using the base learner’s loss on the final “test” batch. Several simplifying choices are made, including sharing parameters across all coordinates in the second layer (roughly akin to using a common update rule across parameters). The paper reconstructs the Mini-ImageNet benchmark (Vinyals et al., 2016) and shows that the LSTM meta-learner is competitive with the state of the art (Matching Networks, Vinyals 2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot tasks.

Strengths:
- Framing few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is both appealing and, in hindsight, quite natural. While the notion of carrying knowledge across tasks is not new, this work appears to advance the practical state of the art (I cannot fully attest to novelty across all recent meta-learning efforts).
- The method is competitive with—and for 5-shot surpasses—Vinyals 2016 on Mini-ImageNet.
- The approach (a simple ConvNet classifier as the base learner) contrasts sharply with the learned-embedding plus nearest-neighbor paradigm in Vinyals 2016. It is encouraging to see strong results from a substantively different methodology rather than an incremental variant.
- The observation linking LSTM cell-state updates and gradient descent is, to my knowledge, new here and interesting in its own right.
- The paper provides concrete, useful guidance for building and training an LSTM meta-learner (e.g., initialization strategies, coordinate-wise parameter sharing, and careful normalization/rescaling of loss, gradients, and parameters). Some points reprise prior observations (e.g., matching test-time conditions during meta-training; ignoring second-order dependencies between meta-learner and base-learner parameters), but the consolidated discussion is helpful.

Weaknesses:
- The exposition is often dense. Despite the compelling ideas, the paper is not an easy read. Understanding the core learning setup required multiple passes and cross-referencing prior work. Section 2’s task description is quite abstract and uses niche notation. A short, concrete walk-through (even on MNIST) and a figure depicting a sequence of few-shot episodes would broaden accessibility.
- The exact definition of the N-way, few-shot protocol remains ambiguous. Mini-ImageNet has 100 labels, split 64/16/20 for meta-train/validation/test. Are only 64/100 classes ever seen during meta-training, or are all 100 observed across episodes, with 64 in any given batch? If only 64 are used for meta-training, how many outputs does the base learner’s softmax have during meta-training—64 (limited to seen classes) or 100 (with 36 never instantiated in training)? Several related details are unclear (see above questions).
- The plots in Figure 2 are hard to interpret on their own; the accompanying discussion offers limited insight.

This is a strong paper with persuasive empirical results. It reads as a clear accept; however, the presentation could be substantially improved. I am raising my score and would consider going higher if the clarity issues are addressed.

Questions for the authors:
- In Mini-ImageNet, are the 64/16/20 splits strict (i.e., only 64 classes are ever used during meta-training), or do episodes eventually cover all 100 classes? If strict, what is the dimensionality of the base learner’s softmax during meta-training: 64 or 100? How is this handled when moving to validation/testing splits with disjoint classes?
- During meta-testing, are the base learner’s output heads reinitialized to match the episode’s N classes, and if so, how are parameters shared or initialized across episodes?
- How sensitive is performance to the coordinate-wise parameter sharing assumption? Did you explore grouping parameters (e.g., per-layer sharing) or limited heterogeneity in the update rules?
- What is the effect of relaxing the gradient independence assumption (i.e., incorporating second-order terms) on stability and performance for few-shot tasks?
- Could you provide ablations on the preprocessing of gradients/losses and on the BN-statistics management strategy, to quantify their contribution?

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not central to the decision):
- Add a concrete example of an episode with a small, familiar dataset and a schematic showing the data flow (train sequence → meta-learner updates → final test loss).
- Clarify the class-split protocol, softmax dimensionality during meta-train/val/test, and how output layers are handled across disjoint class sets.
- Enrich the analysis around Figure 2 with quantitative summaries (e.g., distributions of gate values across layers/episodes, correlations with loss reduction) to make the learned strategy more interpretable.
- Include ablations for number of meta-steps, gate bias initializations, and the impact of explicitly training for exactly T steps versus a range of step counts.
