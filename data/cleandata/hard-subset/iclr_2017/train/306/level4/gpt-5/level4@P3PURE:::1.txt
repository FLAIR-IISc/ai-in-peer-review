This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns the optimization algorithm of another learner (here, a neural network). The paper is generally well written, and the core material is clearly presented. The central idea—drawing a parallel between the Robbins–Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule and exploiting this connection to meet two key desiderata of few-shot learning (1) rapid acquisition of new task-specific knowledge, and (2) slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge—is compelling. Several techniques reused from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, along with novel design choices (notably the specific implementation of batch normalization), are well motivated. The experimental results are convincing. Overall, this is a strong paper.

My main concerns/questions are:
1) Inputs to the meta-learner: Is it redundant to feed the loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs? Did you conduct ablation studies to verify that simpler input combinations are not sufficient?
2) Extensibility to architecture search: It would be valuable if other architectural components (e.g., number of neurons, type of units) could be learned in a similar fashion. Do you have thoughts or preliminary results on this?
3) Related work: The related work section (primarily focused on meta-learning) is somewhat shallow. Meta-learning has a long history, and similar ideas have been explored even without LSTMs. Please consider expanding this section to include:
   - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989), which is closely related to this line of work.
   - Use of genetic programming to search for new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
   - Work by J. Schmidhuber along these lines; I am convinced there is relevant prior work—please locate and incorporate it.

Overall, I like the paper and believe the material will be of broad interest to the ICLR community.
