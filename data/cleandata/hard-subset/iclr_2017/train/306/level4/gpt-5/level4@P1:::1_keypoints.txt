- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that stores classifier parameters in the cell state, uses gates for per-coordinate updates, and feeds a preprocessed gradient into the candidate state.  
- Learns a task-common initialization and an update rule tailored to a fixed, small number of optimization steps; shares LSTM params across coordinates and uses gradient/loss preprocessing.  
- Introduces a batch-normalization protocol that avoids information leakage across episodes while keeping train/test conditions aligned.  
- Trains end-to-end to minimize test loss after T updates per episode, using a practical gradient-independence assumption to avoid second derivatives.  
- Empirical results on Mini-ImageNet (custom splits) are competitive with state-of-the-art, outperforming strong baselines especially in 5-shot; gate visualizations reveal nontrivial, dataset-dependent update behaviors.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reasoning: Well-motivated design tightly linked to few-shot constraints and meta-learning goals; convincing empirical performance and useful practical ablations (e.g., batch norm handling).  
- Framing: Reframes few-shot learning as a learned per-task optimization procedure, well positioned relative to learned-optimizer and meta-learning literature.  
- Evidence: Training objective matches evaluation, experimental setup and hyperparameters are described, and visualizations support learned dynamics; main weakness is limited ablations on meta-learner inputs/gates and a thin historical related-work discussion.  
- Requested ablations: Test input redundancy (loss, gradient, params, previous gates), effect of fixing vs learning ft, gate initialization sensitivity, preprocessing hyperparameter p, and use of both LSTM layers.  
- Robustness question: Evaluate generalization when test-time update horizon T differs from training (T±k).  
- Batch-norm ablation request: Compare running averages from meta-training only vs per-episode stats vs the proposed hybrid; clarify whether γ/β are per-episode or shared.  
- Evaluation details requested: Report wall-clock time and memory overhead versus fine-tuning and Matching Networks; justify or equalize conv filter counts across methods; release splits and code.  
- Broader-scope suggestion: Test handling of varying N-way and k-shot within a single meta-learner or report performance degradation when N/k shift at test time.  
- Related-work expansion: Cite earlier learning-to-learn and learned-rule works (Bengio thesis, genetic-programming rule discovery, Schmidhuber’s fast-weights/self-modifying networks) to better situate contributions.  
- Specific questions for authors: Which input signals are essential; does learning ft help vs fixing it; effects of gate tying (GRU-style); sensitivity to preprocessing p; computation/training overhead; batch-norm strategy quantification; potential to learn architectures alongside updates.  
- Overall verdict: Strong, relevant paper with clear idea, solid execution, and competitive results—recommended for acceptance.
