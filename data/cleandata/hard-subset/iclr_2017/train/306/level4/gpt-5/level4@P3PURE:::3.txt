Summary:
This paper addresses few-shot learning with neural networks via meta-learning, a classic idea that has seen a resurgence in the last year. The authors cast few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem in which each “example” consists of a sequence of batches of “training” pairs, followed by a final “test” batch. At each step, the inputs include the outputs of a base learner (e.g., training loss and gradients) as well as the base learner’s current state (parameters). The method applies an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, using the second layer’s memory cells to directly represent the base learner’s updated parameters. The authors highlight parallels between the LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is trained using the base learner’s prediction loss on the final “test” batch. Several simplifying assumptions are made, including sharing weights across all second-layer cells (analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters). On Mini-ImageNet (recreated following Vinyals et al., 2016), the LSTM meta-learner is competitive with the current state of the art (Matching Networks, Vinyals et al., 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

Strengths:
- Casting few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is intriguing and, in hindsight, quite natural. While the general idea of carrying over learning across tasks is not new, the paper advances the state of the art. I cannot confidently claim novelty relative to all recent meta-learning work, but the contribution appears meaningful.
- The approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals et al. (2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner here (a simple ConvNet classifier) is notably different from the nearest-neighbor-on-a-learned-embedding strategy used by Vinyals et al. (2016). It is encouraging to see state-of-the-art results from a substantially different methodology rather than an incremental variant.
- The connection drawn between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent appears to be new and is interesting on its own.
- The paper offers practical guidance for designing and training an LSTM meta-learner that should aid reproducibility and extension: proper initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. Some insights are known (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training; assuming independence between meta-learner and base-learner parameters when taking gradients with respect to the meta-learner’s parameters), but the discussion remains useful.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is often opaque. Despite the interesting content, the paper is not an easy read; it took multiple passes—and consulting related work—to understand the overall setup. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is quite abstract and uses notation/language not common outside this subarea. A brief concrete example (MNIST would suffice), ideally with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot tasks, would make the paper more accessible.
- The precise setup of the N-class, few-shot problem is unclear. Mini-ImageNet has 100 classes, split 64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing. Does meta-training observe only 64 of the 100 classes, or does each batch include a subset of 64 while, on average, all 100 are seen over meta-training? If only 64 classes are observed during meta-training, how many outputs does the ConvNet base learner’s softmax have at meta-training time—64 (only those observed) or 100 (with 36 never observed)? Several related details remain ambiguous.
- The plots in Figure 2 are not informative on their own, and the accompanying discussion adds little interpretive value.

Overall assessment:
This is an interesting paper with convincing results and a compelling framing of few-shot learning as sequential meta-learning. It reads as a clear accept, but the presentation could be substantially improved. Given the authors’ revisions to clarify the task, I have raised my score to 8, and I would consider raising it further if the exposition—especially the task setup and experimental details—is clarified.
