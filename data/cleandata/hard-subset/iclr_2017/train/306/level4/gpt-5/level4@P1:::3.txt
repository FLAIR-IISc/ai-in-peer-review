Summary of contributions
- The paper addresses few-shot learning by casting it as a sequential meta-learning problem. Each “episode” comprises a sequence of training batches followed by a final test batch; the meta-learner observes the base learner’s losses, gradients, and current parameters at each step.
- The meta-learner is an LSTM whose cell state in the second layer directly encodes the base learner’s parameters. The authors draw a clear analogy between the LSTM cell update and gradient descent, and use this to design gates that modulate learning rates and forgetting.
- The LSTM is trained end-to-end to minimize the base learner’s loss on the episode’s test set, effectively learning both an initialization for the base learner and an update rule tailored to a fixed, small number of steps.
- Practical design choices are described (parameter sharing across coordinates, input normalization, careful gate bias initialization, and batch normalization handling) to make training stable and reproducible.
- On Mini-ImageNet (recreated following Vinyals et al., 2016), the approach is competitive with Matching Networks on 1-shot and outperforms it on 5-shot classification.

Decision: Accept (score: 8)
- Key reasons: (1) A compelling and well-motivated formulation of few-shot learning as sequential meta-learning with an LSTM that directly maintains base-learner parameters; (2) Strong empirical performance on Mini-ImageNet with a base learner that is architecturally distinct from metric-learning approaches, demonstrating that the gains are not tied to nearest-neighbor embeddings.

Supporting arguments
- Framing few-shot learning as learning to learn over sequences is both natural and powerful. While meta-learning is not new, the specific use of an LSTM whose cell state encodes the classifier parameters, and the explicit connection to gradient descent, are insightful and practically useful.
- The method advances the empirical state of the art on Mini-ImageNet 5-shot and is competitive on 1-shot. It is especially notable that these results are achieved with a simple ConvNet classifier rather than a metric-learning architecture, strengthening the claim that the meta-learning procedure itself is effective.
- The paper contributes pragmatic guidance (e.g., gate bias initialization to start near small-step gradient descent; normalization of loss/gradients/parameters; parameter sharing across coordinates to control model size) that will ease replication and extension.
- The training protocol aligns meta-training and meta-testing conditions (including careful handling of batch normalization statistics), which is crucial for few-shot generalization and is well argued here.
- The simplifying assumption of ignoring second derivatives (treating learner gradients as independent of meta-learner parameters) matches prior work and appears sufficient in practice.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to my decision)
- Clarity and exposition: The current presentation is often opaque. A brief, concrete end-to-end example (e.g., a 3-way, 1-shot episode on MNIST) with a diagram showing the sequence of train/test batches and how losses/gradients/parameters flow through the meta-learner would greatly improve accessibility.
- Task specification: Section 2 is overly abstract. Please provide a precise, self-contained description of the episodic N-way, K-shot setting you use, including the exact composition of Dtrain and Dtest and how they are sampled.
- Results reporting: Summarize the main numbers for 1-shot and 5-shot with clear confidence intervals and explicitly state where your method is competitive versus where it surpasses baselines.
- Visualizations (Figure 2): The gate-value plots are currently uninformative without context. Consider aggregating statistics across layers/coordinates (means and variances), highlighting consistent patterns (e.g., weight decay behavior in forget gates), and connecting them to learning dynamics or episode difficulty. Ablations showing performance when fixing gates (e.g., ft=1) or removing components would make these analyses more actionable.

Questions for the authors
- Mini-ImageNet splits and classifier heads: During meta-training, do episodes draw exclusively from the 64 training classes, with the base learner’s softmax sized to the N classes within each episode? Or is there a single 64-way (or 100-way) head reused across episodes? Please clarify how the final linear layer is configured per episode and how it changes across meta-training, validation, and testing.
- Episode composition: For 5-way tasks, do you always sample exactly 5 classes per episode with K in {1,5} support examples per class and 15 query examples per class? How many episodes are used for meta-training, and how many for evaluation to form the reported confidence intervals?
- Parameter initialization: Is c0 (the learned initialization of the base learner’s parameters) global across all episodes and fixed after meta-training? Are base learner parameters reinitialized to c0 at the start of every episode at meta-test time?
- Parameter sharing: You share LSTM parameters across coordinates, giving each coordinate its own hidden/cell state. This implies a shared update rule but coordinate-specific behavior via state. Can you comment on the trade-off versus using per-tensor or per-layer meta-learner parameters?
- Gradient-independence assumption: Did you try backpropagating second-order terms for smaller models to quantify the performance gap (if any)?
- Sensitivity and ablations: How sensitive is performance to (a) the number of unrolled updates T; (b) gate bias initializations; (c) the preprocessing hyperparameter p; and (d) batch norm statistics handling? Any negative results would be informative.
- Generalization: Does the learned optimizer transfer to base learners with different architectures (e.g., deeper/wider ConvNets) or to different N/K regimes without retraining?
- Compute: Please report training time, meta-learner parameter count, and memory requirements for unrolling across T steps.

Note on score update
In light of the authors’ responsiveness and the clarifications added to the manuscript—especially around the meta-learning task specification—I am updating my score to an 8. The work is solid and impactful; further improvements to clarity would broaden its reach.
