Summary of contributions
The paper addresses few-shot learning by framing it as a sequential meta-learning problem. Each episode consists of a small training set followed by a test set. An LSTM acts as a meta-learner that learns to update a base classifier’s parameters over a fixed number of steps. The key idea is to use the LSTM’s cell state as the base learner’s parameters and to parameterize the input and forget gates as functions of the current loss, gradient, parameters, and prior gate values, exploiting the analogy between gradient descent and LSTM updates. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end to minimize the base learner’s loss on the episode’s test set after T updates, encouraging both a good initialization and rapid adaptation. To make the approach scalable, the authors share meta-learner parameters across coordinates and normalize gradients/losses. They also discuss practical choices (initialization, batch normalization handling, gradient-independence assumption) that enable stable training. On Mini-ImageNet (5-way, 1-shot and 5-shot), the method is competitive with, and in 5-shot surpasses, Matching Networks, using a simple ConvNet classifier rather than metric learning.

Decision
Accept (score 8). The approach yields strong empirical results with a conceptually clear meta-learning formulation that differs meaningfully from prior metric-learning methods, and it provides practical guidance likely to help others reproduce and extend the work.

Supporting arguments
- Problem framing: Casting few-shot learning as sequential meta-learning is natural and powerful. Modeling parameter updates inside an LSTM, with the cell state representing the base learner’s weights, provides a direct mechanism to learn both initialization and an update rule tailored to a fixed budget of steps.
- Technical novelty and insight: The explicit connection between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent is insightful and used constructively to design the meta-optimizer. While related to prior work on learned optimizers, the focus on few-shot episodes, the objective of minimizing test loss after T steps, and the learned initialization distinguish this work.
- Empirical strength: On Mini-ImageNet, the method is competitive with Matching Networks in 1-shot and achieves clearly superior performance in 5-shot. Baselines (nearest neighbor on learned embeddings, fine-tuning a pre-trained classifier) are appropriate; the fine-tuning baseline underperforms, underscoring the value of learning an initialization and update rule end-to-end for few-shot adaptation.
- Practical contributions: The paper offers concrete, useful training details (coordinate-wise parameter sharing, gradient/loss preprocessing, LSTM gate bias initialization, careful handling of batch normalization within episodes) that improve reproducibility and applicability.

Areas for improvement (to help the paper; not central to my decision)
- Clarity and accessibility: The task description is dense and relies on niche notation. A concise, concrete walkthrough of a single 5-way, 1-shot episode (with a diagram showing the flow of gradients, losses, and parameter updates over T steps) would broaden accessibility.
- Dataset/label setup: The treatment of class splits and classifier outputs is under-specified. Precisely describe how classes are partitioned across meta-train/val/test, and how the base learner’s final layer is configured per episode (e.g., 5-way softmax re-instantiated each time).
- Figure interpretability: The gate-value plots are difficult to interpret as presented. Consider summarizing distributions over coordinates/layers, highlighting consistent patterns (e.g., weight decay via forget gates), and linking them to performance.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Include ablations on key choices (no learned initialization, no gradient/loss preprocessing, no gate for ft, no parameter sharing) and sensitivity to the number of update steps and to mismatches between train-time and test-time T.

Questions for the authors
- Episodic softmax and labels: In 5-way episodes, is the base classifier’s final linear layer re-parameterized to have 5 outputs per episode, with labels remapped locally? Are these weights part of the learned initialization? Please clarify exactly how the final layer is handled across episodes and across meta-train/val/test class splits (64/16/20).
- Class exposure: During meta-training, do episodes draw only from the 64 training classes, with validation/test classes disjoint? Please confirm, and state whether any pretraining on all 100 classes is used.
- Number of updates: You train separate meta-learners for 1-shot and 5-shot with different T. How sensitive is performance to T, and what happens if the test-time number of updates differs from training?
- Parameter sharing granularity: Are gates it and ft learned per parameter coordinate identically (shared weights) but conditioned on each coordinate’s history, or is there any per-layer/per-tensor context? Have you tried sharing within layers only?
- Batch normalization: Please detail the exact statistics used within an episode during meta-train and meta-test, and how running averages are reset to avoid leakage across episodes. How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Training stability and cost: What are the memory/time costs of backpropagating through T updates, and do you use any truncation? How many steps/episodes were needed to converge?
- Variance and confidence intervals: How many test episodes were used to compute the reported means and intervals? Please include exact counts and seeds for reproducibility.
- Comparisons and extensions: Have you compared against other learned-optimizer baselines (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.) adapted to the few-shot objective? Could the method be applied to other base learners (e.g., deeper CNNs) or to semi-supervised episodes?

After the authors’ clarifications and revisions on the task setup, I am raising my score to 8. This is a compelling paper with solid results and practical impact; improved exposition will further strengthen it.
