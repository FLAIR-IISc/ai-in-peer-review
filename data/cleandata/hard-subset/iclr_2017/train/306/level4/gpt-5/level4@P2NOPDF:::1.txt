This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns an optimization algorithm for another learner (here, a neural network). The writing is generally clear, and the core ideas are well presented. The central insight—drawing a parallel between the Robbins–Monro update rule and the LSTM update, and leveraging it to satisfy two key desiderata of few-shot learning: (1) rapid acquisition of task-specific knowledge and (2) slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge—is compelling.

Several techniques borrowed from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, along with new design choices (e.g., a specific batch-normalization implementation), are well motivated. The experiments are convincing. Overall, this is a strong paper. My main questions/concerns are:

1. Potential input redundancy: You feed the loss, gradient, and parameters to the meta-learner. Could this be redundant? Did you run ablation studies to determine whether simpler input sets (e.g., gradients only, or gradients plus parameters) are sufficient without degrading performance?

2. Extending learnable components: It would be exciting if other architectural choices could be learned in the same framework (e.g., number of neurons, types of units). Do you have thoughts on the feasibility or a roadmap for learning such structural aspects?

3. Related work depth: The related work section, focused mainly on recent meta-learning, feels shallow given the long history of the topic. Earlier work pursued similar goals, even without LSTMs. Please consider expanding this section to include:
   - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989), which is closely related.
   - Use of genetic programming to discover learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
   - Relevant contributions by Jürgen Schmidhuber and collaborators; these should be identified and cited appropriately.

Overall, I like the paper and believe it will be of broad interest to the ICLR audience.
