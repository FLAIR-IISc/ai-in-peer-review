This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns the optimization algorithm for another learning system (here, a neural network). The paper is overall well written, and the presentation of the main material is clear. The core idea—drawing a parallel between the Robbins–Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule and exploiting it to meet the two main desiderata of few-shot learning (1) rapid acquisition of new knowledge and (2) slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge—is intriguing. Several techniques reused from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, along with novel design choices (a specific implementation of batch normalization), are well-motivated. The experiments are convincing. This is a strong paper. My only concerns/questions are the following:

1. Might it be redundant to feed the loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner? Did you conduct ablation studies to verify that simpler input combinations are not sufficient?

2. It would be great if other architectural components of the network could be learned in a similar fashion (e.g., number of neurons, unit types, etc.). Do you have an opinion on this?

3. The related work section (primarily focused on meta-learning) is somewhat shallow. Meta-learning is a rather old topic, and similar approaches have been attempted to solve the same problem even without LSTMs:
   - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) focuses extensively on this topic.
   - Use of genetic programming to search for new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
   - I am convinced Schmidhuber has relevant work; please locate it and update the related work section.

Overall, I like the paper. I believe the material is relevant to a wide audience at ICLR.
