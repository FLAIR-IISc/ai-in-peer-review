Summary:
This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns an optimization algorithm for a base learner (a neural network). By drawing a clear parallel between the Robbins–Monro stochastic approximation updates and the LSTM update equations, the method separates two desiderata of few-shot learning: fast, within-task adaptation via recurrent updates, and slower, across-task transfer via meta-parameters. The approach reuses well-motivated components from prior work (e.g., parameter sharing and normalization from Andrychowicz et al., 2016) and introduces thoughtful design choices, including a tailored implementation of batch normalization. The experiments are convincing and suggest that the approach is both practical and effective.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity in framing learned optimization as an LSTM that implements a Robbins–Monro-style update, leading to a compelling interpretation of fast vs. slow learning.
- Solid engineering choices, with good justification for parameter sharing, normalization, and the specific batch-normalization scheme.
- Empirical results are strong and well presented, indicating that the learned optimizer generalizes across tasks and achieves few-shot adaptation.
- Writing and organization are generally clear, making the ideas accessible to a broad ICLR audience.

Questions and concerns:
1) Input redundancy and ablations:
   The meta-learner is fed loss values, gradients, and parameters. Could this be redundant? Please provide ablation studies testing subsets of these inputs (e.g., gradients only; loss+gradients; parameters+gradients; loss only; gradient sign or normalized gradients). Such ablations would clarify which signals are essential, whether the full triplet is necessary, and how each component contributes to performance and stability. Reporting both task performance and training stability/variance in these ablations would be particularly informative.

2) Learning architectural components:
   The current framework learns an optimization rule for fixed architectures. Do you foresee extending the meta-learner to select or adapt architectural aspects (e.g., number of neurons, choice of activation units, layer-wise connectivity) during meta-training? A brief discussion of feasibility and challenges—such as discrete choices vs. continuous relaxations, differentiability, and computational cost—would strengthen the paper. Even a negative result or early experiments (e.g., soft gating of units or layers, hypernetworks that propose per-layer widths) would be interesting.

3) Related work depth:
   The related work on meta-learning is somewhat narrow. Meta-learning has a substantial history predating LSTM-based approaches. It would help to situate your contributions more comprehensively by discussing:
   - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) on learning algorithms and meta-learning ideas.
   - Genetic programming for discovering learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
   - Early works by J. Schmidhuber on learning to learn, self-referential/fast-weight mechanisms, and meta-optimization.
   Expanding this section will clarify how your LSTM-based formulation relates to, departs from, or generalizes these earlier lines of research.

Additional suggestions:
- Clarify the training and testing protocols for few-shot evaluation, including how tasks are sampled, the number of adaptation steps allowed at test time, and whether the meta-learner sees distributional shifts relative to training tasks.
- Report computational overhead: wall-clock training time, memory footprint, and inference-time cost compared with standard optimizers (e.g., SGD/Adam), to contextualize practical deployment.
- If possible, include a simple sanity check showing the meta-learner’s robustness to noisy or scaled gradients (e.g., gradient scaling invariance), given the reliance on normalization and the analogy to Robbins–Monro conditions.

Minor comments:
- A few phrasing issues could be smoothed in the final version (e.g., “another learning algorithm,” “convincing,” “I am convinced”). The current draft is otherwise clearly written.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong and timely paper that advances learned optimization for few-shot learning with a clear conceptual grounding and convincing experimental results. Addressing the ablation on inputs, expanding the related work to cover classic meta-learning efforts, and discussing the feasibility of learning architectural choices would further solidify the contribution. I believe the paper will be of broad interest to the ICLR community and recommend acceptance.
