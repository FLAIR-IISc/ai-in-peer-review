Summary
The paper tackles few-shot classification by learning both an initialization and an update rule for a neural classifier through meta-learning. The key idea is to view parameter updates as an LSTM state transition, with the learner’s parameters as the LSTM cell state and gates producing per-parameter updates. The meta-learner receives the current loss and gradients (with careful preprocessing and parameter sharing across coordinates) and outputs a sequence of updates over a small, fixed number of steps, explicitly optimizing the classifier’s validation loss after those steps. The method also learns an initialization of the classifier that transfers across tasks. Practical considerations—such as handling batch normalization without cross-episode leakage and stabilizing training via gate-bias initialization—are addressed. On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot tasks, the approach outperforms reasonable baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks, with stronger results in the 5-shot setting. The paper further provides qualitative insights by visualizing learned gate behaviors.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and practical relevance: learning an optimizer and a transferable initialization that explicitly targets few-shot generalization after a fixed update budget.
- Empirical competitiveness on a challenging benchmark, supported by thoughtful engineering choices that increase practical utility.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper clearly articulates shortcomings of conventional optimization for few-shot regimes and motivates meta-learning as a solution. The objective (optimize test loss after T inner-loop updates) is well aligned with deployment conditions.
- Placement in literature: The work is positioned with respect to prior meta-learning for optimizers and metric-based few-shot methods. The relationship to GRU/LSTM-style updates is acknowledged, and connections to learned initialization and hyperparameter optimization are made.
- Methodological soundness: Parameter sharing across coordinates and gradient/loss preprocessing keep the meta-optimizer compact and numerically stable. The batch normalization protocol avoids information leakage while keeping train/test conditions aligned. The “gradient independence” simplification (ignoring second derivatives through the learner) follows prior art and is a reasonable trade-off, though it warrants discussion (see questions).
- Results: The method improves over nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks on 1-shot, outperforming it on 5-shot on the authors’ Mini-ImageNet split. The observation that naive fine-tuning overfits in 1-shot supports the need for learned initialization and update rules tailored to the few-update regime.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all points affect my recommendation)
- Clarify the GRU/LSTM analogy: As you note, the formulation is closer to a GRU-style update (separate gates, not constrained to sum to one). Making this explicit earlier will preempt confusion. Also reconcile the sign convention: the mapping suggests c̃t = −∇Lt, whereas Section 3.1 later sets c̃t = ∇Lt.
- Data splits and episode generation: The description of Dmeta-train/val/test and per-episode sampling could benefit from a concise schematic or table. Please specify the exact class lists for each split to aid reproducibility.
- Fairness and architecture details: The learner used by the meta-optimizer has 32 filters per conv layer, while baselines are reported with 64 filters to match prior work. This mismatch could bias comparisons. Either equalize capacities or include results with matched architectures. Provide meta-learner LSTM sizes, total parameter counts (learner+meta-learner), and compute/memory costs.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Initialization vs update rule: Evaluate performance with learned initialization but fixed SGD, and with random initialization but learned gates, to quantify each component’s contribution.
  - Gates: Report results with ft fixed to 1 (no forget/decay), with it fixed or learned via a simpler schedule, and with tied gates (GRU-like), to validate the design.
  - Number of inner updates: How robust is performance when the meta-learner is evaluated with a different number of updates than seen in training?
  - Gradient independence assumption: Include a small-scale experiment with full higher-order differentiation (or truncated variants) to estimate the effect of the assumption on accuracy and stability.
- Broader evaluation: Consider adding Omniglot or CIFAR-FS/Tiered-ImageNet (if feasible) and reporting variance across seeds. Even a brief summary in the appendix would strengthen generality claims.
- Visualizations: Figure 2 adds limited insight; consider replacing it with gate trajectories for representative layers/episodes and aggregate statistics (e.g., distributions of it and ft over time).
- Minor edits:
  - Section 3.2: “each coordinate has its” (not “it”).
  - Standardize notation and boldface for vectors; ensure equation references are consistent.

Questions for the authors
1) Architecture capacity and fairness: Did you run baselines with 32-filter backbones to match the learner used by the meta-optimizer? If not, please comment on how capacity differences might affect the comparisons.
2) Higher-order gradients: Did you attempt training with second-order terms (even on smaller models) to assess the impact of the gradient independence assumption on final accuracy or convergence?
3) Robustness to update count: How does accuracy change if the meta-learner is evaluated with more/fewer inner updates than seen in training? Can it extrapolate beyond the trained horizon?
4) Gate roles: Your visualizations suggest ft behaves like weight decay. Can you quantify this by measuring effective L2 decay coefficients or by correlating ft with loss/gradient magnitudes across layers?
5) Batch normalization: At meta-test time, are running BN statistics computed only from Dtrain and then frozen for Dtest within each episode? Please clarify to ensure no leakage from test examples into normalization.
6) Generalization across tasks: If trained on 1-shot episodes, how well does the meta-learner adapt to 5-shot (and vice versa) without retraining? Any evidence of meta-overfitting to specific N-way/K-shot settings?
7) Reproducibility: The paper references released code. Could you confirm availability and include commit hashes, dataset split files, and scripts to reproduce the reported numbers?

Overall, this is a clear and impactful contribution to few-shot learning via learned optimization. With stronger ablations and clarity on architectural parity and BN handling, the paper would be even more compelling.
