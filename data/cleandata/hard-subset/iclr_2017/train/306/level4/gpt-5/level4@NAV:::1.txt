This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns the optimization algorithm of another learner (here, a neural network).

Overall, the paper is well written and the exposition of the core ideas is clear. The central insight—drawing a parallel between the Robbins–Monro update rule and the LSTM update and leveraging this to meet two key desiderata of few-shot learning (1) rapid acquisition of new knowledge, and (2) slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge—is compelling.

The reuse of techniques from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, together with novel design choices (notably a specific implementation of batch normalization), is well justified.

The experiments are persuasive. This is a strong submission. My only concerns/questions are:

1. Might it be redundant to provide the loss, gradients, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner? Did you perform ablation studies to confirm that simpler input combinations are insufficient?
2. It would be interesting if other architectural components could be learned in a similar manner (e.g., number of neurons, types of units, etc.). Do you have thoughts on this?
3. The related work section (largely centered on meta-learning) is somewhat shallow. Meta-learning has a long history, and similar approaches have been explored for the same problem even without LSTMs:
   - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) is largely about this topic.
   - Use of genetic programming to search for new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
   - I am quite sure Schmidhuber has relevant work; please locate it and update the related work accordingly.

Overall, I like the paper and believe it will be of interest to a broad ICLR audience.
