Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot learning by casting it as a meta-learning problem in which an LSTM “meta-learner” learns to optimize a base neural network classifier (“learner”) from very few labeled examples. The core idea is to exploit the correspondence between gradient-descent updates and the LSTM cell update to (a) learn an update rule tailored to a fixed, small number of training steps, and (b) learn a transferable initialization of the learner’s parameters. The meta-learner operates coordinate-wise with parameter sharing and gradient/loss preprocessing (following Andrychowicz et al., 2016), and it is trained end-to-end to minimize the loss on the held-out test set within each episode, aligning the meta-objective with test-time conditions. The paper further addresses batch-normalization leakage across episodes with a careful protocol, and provides qualitative analyses of the learned input/forget gate dynamics. Empirically, on Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot classification, the method is competitive with Matching Networks and clearly outperforms strong baselines (nearest neighbor on learned embeddings and fine-tuning from a pre-trained network). The paper is clearly written and the approach is well motivated.

Decision
Accept. The method offers a clear and well-executed meta-learning formulation with a principled objective, strong empirical results on a standard benchmark, and thoughtful engineering choices (parameter sharing, preprocessing, and batch normalization handling) that make it practical and of broad interest.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: The paper aims to enable rapid adaptation of high-capacity neural classifiers from very few labeled examples by learning both an optimizer and an initialization that are specialized to the episodic few-shot regime. This is a central and timely question in meta-learning.
- Motivation and placement: The LSTM-as-optimizer idea is well grounded in the similarity between gradient updates and LSTM cell updates and is positioned appropriately relative to prior work on learning to optimize (Andrychowicz et al., 2016) and memory-based few-shot learning (Matching Networks, Santoro et al., 2016). The paper’s distinct focus is few-shot adaptation with a fixed, small number of updates and learning an initialization jointly with the update rule.
- Empirical support: The experiments on Mini-ImageNet use standard 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot setups with clear baselines. The proposed method is competitive with Matching Networks in 1-shot and surpasses it in 5-shot in the authors’ reimplementation/split. The batch norm protocol is carefully designed to avoid information leakage while matching train/test conditions. Qualitative gate analyses suggest the meta-learner is not merely learning a fixed schedule. The experimental methodology (episodic training, reporting confidence intervals) is sound.
- Rigor and clarity: The training objective directly optimizes for test performance within each episode, which is the right metric for few-shot learning. Assumptions (e.g., ignoring second derivatives to avoid expensive meta-gradients) are stated clearly, with references, and the engineering details are transparent enough to support reproduction.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all are decision-critical)
- Ablations and analysis:
  - Input features: Please include ablations that remove some of the meta-learner inputs (e.g., provide only gradients; gradients+loss; gradients+parameters) to determine which signals are essential and whether the loss and parameter vectors add beyond gradients. This would directly address potential redundancy in the inputs.
  - Initialization vs. optimizer: Separate the contribution of a learned initialization from the learned update rule by (i) using the learned initialization with a simple optimizer (e.g., SGD with tuned step size), and (ii) using the learned optimizer with a random initialization. This will clarify which component drives the gains.
  - Preprocessing and gating: Report the effect of the gradient/loss preprocessing and analyze the benefit of the learned forget gate versus a fixed ft=1 update.
- Generalization across regimes: Evaluate cross-regime robustness, e.g., train the meta-learner for 5-shot and test on 1-shot (and vice versa), or train for a fixed number of classes/steps and test on different numbers. This would demonstrate how specialized the learned optimizer is and how well it extrapolates.
- Baselines: The fine-tuning baseline appears under-regularized. Strengthen it with weight decay, dropout, and/or early stopping within the fixed number of updates to ensure a fair comparison. Consider a baseline that meta-learns only the initialization (with a fixed optimizer), which is close in spirit to your approach and isolates the value of the learned update rule.
- Scope and datasets: Results are currently limited to Mini-ImageNet with a small CNN. Discuss computational cost and memory overhead relative to Matching Networks and standard fine-tuning, and comment on scaling to deeper learners and other datasets (e.g., Omniglot, tiered-ImageNet).
- Related work: While you cite relevant classic references (Bengio’s thesis and early work on learned learning rules; Schmidhuber’s self-referential and fast-weights work), the discussion could be deepened. Clarify conceptual and practical differences from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) beyond the few-shot focus, and better contrast with memory-based meta-learners (e.g., what your approach gains/loses relative to nearest-neighbor style adaptation).
- Reproducibility: You note code availability; please also release the exact Mini-ImageNet splits used to facilitate comparison.

Questions for the authors
1. Input ablations: How do performance and training stability change if you remove some inputs to the meta-learner (e.g., omit the loss or the previous gate values)? Which inputs matter most?
2. Initialization vs. optimizer: What is the relative contribution of the learned initialization compared to the learned update rule? Do you observe strong gains from either in isolation?
3. Cross-regime generalization: If trained for T steps and N-way, how does performance degrade when tested with a different number of steps or classes? Can the meta-learner adapt at test time to a different T?
4. Regularization: Did you try regularizing the learner during adaptation (e.g., L2, dropout) in the fine-tuning baseline? Does adding such regularization reduce the gap to your method?
5. Computational footprint: What are the wall-clock and memory costs of meta-training and per-episode adaptation compared to Matching Networks and fine-tuning? Any tricks needed for longer unrolls?
6. Preprocessing and gates: How sensitive is performance to the gradient/loss preprocessing parameter p? What happens if ft is fixed to 1 or if it and it are tied as in a GRU?
7. Batch normalization: Could you quantify how much the proposed BN protocol improves over naive alternatives (e.g., using global running averages only), and whether BN statistics learned on meta-train transfer across splits?
8. Transfer across datasets: If you meta-train on one dataset and meta-test on another (e.g., train on a subset of classes or a related dataset), how well does the learned optimizer/initialization transfer?
9. Architectural meta-learning: Do you see a principled path to extending the approach to learn other architectural choices (e.g., layer widths, activation types) within this meta-learning framework?

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that will interest the ICLR community.
