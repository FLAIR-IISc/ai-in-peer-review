- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that frames learned optimization as Robbins–Monro-style updates, separating fast within-task adaptation from slow across-task meta-parameters.  
- Reuses prior components (parameter sharing, normalization) and adds a tailored batch-normalization scheme; experiments are convincing and show practical few-shot effectiveness.  
- Strength: clear conceptual framing of learned optimization as LSTM implementing fast vs. slow learning.  
- Strength: well-justified engineering choices (parameter sharing, normalization, BN design).  
- Strength: strong empirical results demonstrating generalization and few-shot adaptation.  
- Strength: clear writing and organization appropriate for ICLR.  
- Concern: potential input redundancy—ablation studies needed to test subsets of inputs (gradients, loss, parameters; normalized or sign gradients) and report performance plus training stability/variance.  
- Concern: currently fixes architectures; discuss feasibility and challenges of extending meta-learner to adapt architectural choices (discrete vs. continuous, differentiability, compute) and consider early/negative experiments (soft gating, hypernetworks).  
- Concern: related work is narrow—should situate against earlier meta-learning work (Samy Bengio PhD 1989, Bengio/Bengio/Cloutier 1994 on genetic programming for learning rules, Schmidhuber’s learning-to-learn and fast-weight/meta-optimization papers).  
- Suggestion: clarify few-shot training/testing protocols (task sampling, allowed adaptation steps, distributional shift at test time).  
- Suggestion: report computational overhead (wall-clock training time, memory footprint, inference cost vs. SGD/Adam).  
- Suggestion: include sanity checks for robustness to noisy or scaled gradients (gradient scaling invariance) given reliance on normalization.  
- Minor: smooth a few phrasings in the final draft.  
- Overall: strong, timely paper with clear grounding and convincing experiments; recommend acceptance pending ablations, expanded related work, and discussion/experiments on learning architectures.
