1. Summary of contributions (positive and generous)
- The paper tackles few-shot learning by training a meta-learner to learn both an optimizer and an initialization for a base classifier. The key insight is to reinterpret gradient-based updates as gated recurrent updates, mapping parameter updates to the cell state dynamics of a gated RNN.
- Concretely, an LSTM-like meta-learner produces coordinate-wise updates to the learner’s parameters using inputs that include the current gradient and loss, and it learns a shared, task-common initialization of the learner’s weights. Parameter sharing across coordinates and gradient/loss preprocessing keep the meta-learner compact and stable.
- The training objective directly optimizes for few-step generalization by minimizing test-set loss after a fixed number of inner-loop updates, aligning training and evaluation conditions. Practical guidance is provided for initialization, batch normalization in episodic settings, and stability (e.g., gradient clipping, small initial learning rates).
- On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the method is competitive with deep metric-learning approaches (e.g., Matching Networks), and surpasses reasonable baselines, demonstrating that learning an optimizer plus initialization is a viable path for few-shot learning.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and feasible meta-learning formulation with a clear, well-motivated mapping from gradient descent updates to gated recurrent dynamics, yielding a practical, end-to-end trainable few-shot learner.
- Competitive empirical results against strong baselines, with thoughtful training/evaluation protocol design that directly targets the few-update regime.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Learning to rapidly adapt a high-capacity classifier from very few labeled examples by meta-learning both the update rule and a beneficial initialization across tasks.
- Motivation and placement: The approach is well grounded in meta-learning literature and optimizer-learning work. Framing few-shot adaptation as a fixed-budget optimization process is compelling, and the gating interpretation provides an intuitive mechanism to control learning-rate-like and forgetting behavior per-parameter.
- Evidence for claims: Results on Mini-ImageNet are competitive with state-of-the-art metric-learning methods and outperform natural baselines; the training procedure matches test-time constraints (few inner updates, episodic splits). That said, the empirical analysis would be stronger with additional ablations isolating the roles of the input/forget gates and other design choices; some presentation aspects (e.g., figure utility, split descriptions) could be clearer.

4. Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Clarify the gated-update analogy: The proposed rule resembles a GRU-style update in spirit (separate gates, not constrained to sum to one). A short discussion justifying the LSTM-style choice (and non-tied gates) versus GRU, and any empirical comparison, would preempt confusion.
- Make the meta-set construction easier to follow: The description of train/validation/test meta-splits and episode sampling is dense; a compact diagram or table would greatly improve clarity.
- Strengthen empirical analysis:
  - Gate/function ablations: e.g., ft fixed to 1 (no forgetting), no loss input, or scalar global it versus learned per-parameter gates; show how these affect convergence and generalization.
  - Sensitivity to the number of inner updates T and to the gradient/loss preprocessing (parameter p).
  - Briefly report the cost/overhead of the coordinate-wise meta-learner (memory/time) relative to the learner.
  - If feasible, include confidence intervals consistently and ensure identical backbone capacities across comparisons (or explain differences).
- Figures: If Figure 2 adds little beyond text, consider moving it to the appendix or enriching it (e.g., highlighting how gradients/losses flow into gates).
- Minor: Typo in 3.2 (“each coordinate has it” -> “its”).
- Code: Public release would be a major plus for reproducibility and adoption.

5. Questions for the authors
- Gated-update choice: Why choose an LSTM-style formulation (separate input/forget gates not tied to sum to one) rather than a GRU-style update? Did you compare them empirically, and what trade-offs did you observe?
- Gate importance: How much do ft and it respectively contribute? What happens if ft is fixed at 1 or if it is a learned scalar (shared) rather than coordinate-wise? Any evidence from ablations or gate-value statistics beyond the provided visualizations?
- Sensitivity to inner-loop length: How sensitive is performance to the number of inner updates T, especially when T at test time differs from training?
- Preprocessing and stability: How sensitive are results to the gradient/loss preprocessing parameter p and to the “gradient independence” assumption (no second derivatives)? Did you try a limited second-order variant?
- Batch normalization: Could you detail the exact procedure for collecting and resetting BN statistics per episode, including the decay schedule used to bias later steps?
- Dataset splits and fairness: Please clarify the exact class splits and sampling protocol to rule out leakage across meta-train/val/test. Also, confirm that model capacities and data augmentations are matched across baselines (and, if not, justify differences).
- Generalization across tasks: How well does the learned optimizer+initialization transfer when N and/or k differ from training (e.g., trained on 5-way 1-shot, tested on 5-way 5-shot or 10-way 1-shot)?
