This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns an optimizer to train another learner (a neural network classifier) in the few-shot regime. The manuscript is generally well written and the key ideas are clearly conveyed. The central insight—linking the Robbins–Monro/gradient-descent style update to the LSTM cell update—and leveraging it to address the two main few-shot desiderata (1) rapid within-task adaptation and (2) slower accumulation of cross-task transferable knowledge—is compelling. Reusing elements from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) such as coordinate-wise parameter sharing and input normalization, together with new design choices (e.g., the careful handling of batch normalization across episodes), is well argued. The empirical results are convincing. Overall, this is a strong submission.

My main concerns/questions are:
1. Inputs to the meta-learner: Is it redundant to feed the loss, gradient, and parameters jointly? Did you conduct ablation studies to determine whether simpler input combinations would suffice without sacrificing performance?
2. Extending what is learned: Beyond learning the optimizer and initialization, could other architectural choices (e.g., number of units, activation types, or layer configurations) be learned in a similar meta-learning framework? What is your perspective on this direction?
3. Related work depth: The meta-learning background could be broadened. There is earlier, closely related work that predates LSTMs:
   - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis on learning learning rules (early 1990s) is highly relevant.
   - The use of genetic programming to discover learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, J. Cloutier, mid-1990s).
   - There is related work by Schmidhuber on fast weights/self-modifying networks and learning to learn; please expand this coverage and clarify similarities/differences.

Overall, I am positive about the paper and believe it will be of broad interest to the ICLR community.
