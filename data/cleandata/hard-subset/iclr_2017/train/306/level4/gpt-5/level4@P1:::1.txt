1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The key idea is to cast the learner’s parameter update as the LSTM cell-state update: the cell state holds the classifier parameters, the input and forget gates modulate per-coordinate updates, and the candidate state uses the (preprocessed) gradient signal. This design allows the meta-learner to (a) learn a task-common initialization of the classifier and (b) learn an update rule tailored to a fixed, small number of optimization steps. The model shares LSTM parameters across parameter coordinates to remain compact and uses gradient/loss preprocessing, following Andrychowicz et al. (2016). The paper also proposes a careful batch-normalization protocol to avoid information leakage across episodes while keeping train/test conditions aligned. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end to minimize test loss after T updates on each episode, with a practical gradient-independence assumption to avoid second derivatives. On Mini-ImageNet (custom splits), the approach is competitive with Matching Networks and outperforms strong baselines, particularly in the 5-shot setting. Visualizations of gate values provide insight into the learned optimization dynamics.

2. Decision: Accept
Reasons:
- Well-motivated approach that tightly links model design to the few-shot constraint (fixed small number of updates) and to meta-learning principles (fast within-task learning, slower cross-task transfer).
- Empirical results are convincing and competitive with the state of the art, with clear experimental protocols and ablations that matter for practice (e.g., batch norm handling).

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Few-shot classification where standard gradient-based training underperforms due to limited updates and poor random initializations. The paper reframes optimization as a learned, per-task procedure guided by a meta-learner.
- Motivation and placement in the literature: The work is well positioned relative to meta-learning and learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al., Santoro et al., and classical meta-learning ideas). The LSTM-cell update analogy provides a crisp architectural motivation, and the choices around parameter sharing and preprocessing are sensible.
- Are claims supported? The training objective matches evaluation (optimize test loss after T updates per episode), the batch-normalization discussion is careful, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail (learner architecture, meta-learner structure, optimization settings). On Mini-ImageNet (custom splits), the method beats strong baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks, with a clear advantage in 5-shot. Visualizations suggest the meta-learner learns nontrivial, dataset-dependent update behaviors beyond static heuristics. Limitations include a lack of ablations on the meta-learner inputs and gate roles, and a relatively shallow related-work discussion given the long history of meta-learning, but these do not outweigh the overall contribution.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect my decision)
- Ablations on meta-learner inputs and gates:
  - Is it redundant to feed loss, gradient, and parameters to the meta-learner? Please provide ablations (e.g., gradient only; gradient+loss; gradient+parameters; removal of it−1/ft−1) to assess which signals matter.
  - Evaluate the effect of fixing ft=1 versus learning ft; and the impact of the proposed gate initializations (large forget bias, small input bias).
  - Report sensitivity to the preprocessing hyperparameter p and to the choice of using both the first-layer LSTM and the modified LSTM layer.
- Generalization beyond the trained update horizon:
  - You note improved performance when training with the exact number of test-time updates. How robust is the meta-learner when T at test time differs from training? Please include results for T±k.
- Batch normalization:
  - The per-episode statistics strategy is well motivated. Please add a small ablation comparing: (i) using running averages from meta-training only; (ii) using per-episode stats for both train/test; (iii) the proposed hybrid. Clarify whether γ/β are learned per episode or shared across episodes.
- Evaluation details:
  - Include wall-clock time and memory overhead relative to standard fine-tuning and Matching Networks.
  - Discuss the impact of using different conv filter counts across methods (learner uses 32 filters; baselines/matching nets use 64) to ensure fairness or justify the choice.
  - Since the Mini-ImageNet splits differ from prior work, releasing splits and code (you link code) will help reproducibility and fair comparison.
- Broader scope:
  - The approach is tailored to few-shot and fixed N-way tasks. It would be valuable to show preliminary results on varying N and k within a single meta-learner, or to report how performance degrades/improves when k and N shift at test time.
- Related work:
  - The meta-learning section could be deepened. Please include and discuss earlier lines of work that learned update rules or search procedures, even if not LSTM-based:
    - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis on learning learning rules (1993).
    - Genetic programming for learning-rule discovery (Bengio, Bengio, Cloutier, 1994/1995).
    - Schmidhuber’s early work on self-modifying networks and fast-weights (1992, 1993, 1997).
  - This will better situate your contribution within the broader historical context.

Questions for the authors
- Input redundancy: Which of {Lt, ∇θt−1Lt, θt−1, it−1/ft−1} are essential? Do simpler input sets perform comparably?
- Gate behavior: How much does learning ft help versus fixing it to 1? Did you try a GRU-style constraint tying gates?
- Horizon mismatch: How well does the method generalize when the number of updates at test time differs from meta-training?
- Preprocessing: How sensitive are results to the preprocessing transform and the choice of p=10?
- Computation: What is the training/inference overhead of the meta-learner compared to standard fine-tuning?
- Batch norm: Can you quantify performance differences among the batch-norm strategies you considered and confirm no leakage across episodes?
- Architecture learning: Do you foresee extending the framework to learn other architectural aspects (e.g., layer widths, unit types) alongside initialization and updates?

Overall, this is a strong, relevant paper with a clear idea, solid execution, and competitive results. I recommend acceptance.
