1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The key idea is to align the LSTM cell update with the gradient-descent update, enabling the meta-learner to (a) learn an initialization of the classifier’s parameters that facilitates rapid adaptation, and (b) learn an update rule (via input and forget gates) tailored to a fixed, small number of optimization steps. The approach incorporates parameter sharing across parameter coordinates and normalization of losses/gradients (as in Andrychowicz et al., 2016), and introduces a careful batch-normalization protocol to avoid leakage across episodes while matching train/test conditions. Experiments on Mini-ImageNet demonstrate performance competitive with metric-learning methods (e.g., Matching Networks) and better results in the 5-shot setting, with clear, well-presented analyses and visualizations of the learned gates. Overall, the paper is clearly written and addresses a timely and important problem.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated formulation that directly optimizes episodic generalization for few-shot learning by learning both initialization and update dynamics.
- Convincing empirical results on Mini-ImageNet and a clear exposition of design choices (parameter sharing, input preprocessing, and batch-norm handling).

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the core difficulty of gradient-based training in low-data, few-iteration settings. Framing the optimizer as an LSTM that is explicitly trained for a fixed number of updates is compelling and addresses both fast per-task adaptation and cross-task knowledge sharing.
- Placement in the literature: The work builds appropriately on meta-optimization (Andrychowicz et al., 2016) and episodic evaluation protocols for few-shot learning (Vinyals et al., 2016), while differing in its focus on few-shot adaptation rather than large-scale training. The discussion of batch normalization in the episodic setting is a practical and valuable contribution.
- Empirical support: Results are competitive or superior to strong baselines (nearest neighbor embeddings, fine-tuning) and comparable to or better than Matching Networks, especially in 5-shot. The analysis of gate behaviors suggests the meta-learner is adapting across tasks rather than applying a fixed schedule. While additional ablations would strengthen the case, the current experiments support the main claims.

4. Suggestions and questions to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Ablations on meta-learner inputs:
  - The meta-learner ingests loss, gradient, parameters, and previous gate states. Are all components necessary? Please provide ablations (e.g., gradient-only; gradient+loss; with/without θt−1; with/without previous gate values) and report the impact on accuracy and stability.
- Capacity and fairness controls:
  - The learner CNN for the meta-learner uses 32 filters per layer, while some baselines (e.g., Matching Networks) use 64. Please clarify model capacity and training-time parity across methods, and, if possible, include a setting with matched capacities.
- Number of unrolled steps:
  - The meta-learner is trained for the same number of steps used at test time. How sensitive is performance to a mismatch between training and testing unroll lengths? This would clarify generalization beyond the exact T used.
- Gradient-independence assumption:
  - You ignore second-derivative terms by assuming loss/gradient inputs are independent of meta-learner parameters. Could you quantify the empirical trade-off (e.g., small-scale experiments with/without second derivatives) or provide evidence that including them does not materially change performance?
- Batch normalization protocol:
  - The episodic BN scheme is crucial. Please add a brief sensitivity study (e.g., test-phase running averages vs per-batch stats; momentum choices for running averages) to show robustness, especially given very few updates.
- Learning other architectural aspects:
  - You focus on learning initialization and update rules. Do you foresee extending the framework to learn other architectural or training hyperparameters (e.g., layer widths, activation types, number of updates T) via the meta-learner, or via a joint outer loop?
- Related work:
  - Please expand the historical context on meta-learning and learned update rules, including:
    - Early work on learning learning rules and biologically plausible updates (e.g., Bengio et al., 1990; Bengio, 1993; Bengio et al., 1995).
    - Search-based methods for learning rules (e.g., genetic programming: Bengio, Bengio, Cloutier, 1994/1995 era).
    - Schmidhuber’s early work on networks that learn to modify their own weights (e.g., 1992, 1993), beyond the items already cited.
  - These additions would situate the contribution more fully in the meta-learning lineage.
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Provide more implementation details for the LSTM input/forget gate networks (hidden sizes, nonlinearities), preprocessing hyperparameters, and gradient clipping settings for the inner learner (if any).
  - Include wall-clock considerations and memory usage for unrolled training, to help practitioners gauge feasibility.

Questions for the authors
- Which meta-learner inputs are most critical for performance and stability? Please share ablation results and any insights.
- How well does the learned optimizer generalize across datasets or to different backbone architectures without retraining?
- What happens when you vary the number of classes (N) at test time relative to training? Does the meta-learner transfer across N?
- Can the method benefit from or coexist with distance-based inference at test time (e.g., using the learned embedding plus nearest neighbors), and if so, how does that compare?
- Do you observe overfitting of the meta-learner to the specific unroll length or training curriculum, and how do you mitigate it?

Overall assessment
A strong and clearly presented paper with a well-justified approach and solid empirical evidence. I recommend acceptance.
