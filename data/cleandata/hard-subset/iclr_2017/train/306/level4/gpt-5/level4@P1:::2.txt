Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a meta-learning approach for few-shot classification that learns an optimizer and an initialization for a learner network. The core idea is to interpret the parameter update of gradient descent as a gated recurrent update and to train an LSTM-based meta-learner to output per-parameter updates for a CNN classifier. The LSTM’s cell state holds the learner’s parameters; its input and forget gates (it and ft) act as learned, data-dependent learning-rate and forgetting terms computed from gradients, losses, and parameter history. The method shares meta-learner parameters across coordinates, preprocesses gradients and losses to stabilize scale, and is trained end-to-end by minimizing test loss over episodes, aligning training with evaluation conditions. The paper offers practical guidance on initialization, gradient-independence to avoid second derivatives, and careful handling of batch normalization in episodic settings. On Mini-ImageNet 1-shot and 5-shot tasks, the method outperforms strong baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks, with clear gains in 5-shot. Visualizations of gate values give some insight into the learned optimization strategy.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Original and well-motivated framing of optimizer-as-LSTM specifically tailored to few-shot learning, with a learned initialization and update rule that match the episodic evaluation objective.
- Competitive empirical results against a strong metric-learning baseline, supported by thoughtful practical recommendations (e.g., BN handling) and clear placement in the literature.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper directly addresses rapid generalization from few examples by learning both how to initialize and how to update a learner, rather than relying on generic optimizers or off-the-shelf transfer learning. This targets a recognized bottleneck in gradient-based training under few updates.
- Positioning: Related work is covered well, distinguishing this setting from prior learned-optimizer work aimed at large-scale training and situating the approach alongside metric-learning and memory-augmented methods.
- Method: The mapping between gradient updates and gated recurrent dynamics is sound. While the paper uses LSTMs, the update more closely resembles a GRU in spirit (gating the carry-over and the update); acknowledging this strengthens the conceptual clarity without undermining the approach.
- Training protocol: Minimizing test loss over episodes matches the evaluation objective, which is an important design choice. The discussion of parameter sharing, preprocessing, and stability (gate bias initialization, gradient clipping) is practical and helpful.
- Batch normalization: The handling of BN statistics to avoid information leakage across episodes while keeping training/test conditions aligned is careful and insightful.
- Empirical evidence: On Mini-ImageNet-like splits, the approach outperforms a nearest-neighbor baseline and a fine-tuning baseline, and is competitive with Matching Networks, especially in 5-shot. This supports the central claim that learning both initialization and update dynamics can accelerate adaptation.

Areas for improvement (not blocking)
- Analogy and nomenclature: The learned update is arguably closer to GRUs than LSTMs, given the form of gated carry and update. Clarifying this would avoid potential confusion.
- Clarity of meta-dataset splits: The description of Dmeta−train/validation/test and per-episode train/test is dense. A compact figure illustrating the episode construction and statistics flow (especially with BN) would help.
- Evaluation depth: Additional ablations would strengthen the claims, especially regarding the roles of it and ft. For example, fix ft=1 (no forgetting), or use a fixed learning rate, to quantify the contribution of each gate and of learning c0.
- Figure 2: As presented, it adds limited value. Either enhance it to clarify the full computation graph (including preprocessing, parameter sharing, and the flow of gradients) or move it to the appendix.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide an ablation on the learned initialization c0 versus random or pre-trained initializations, isolating how much of the gain comes from initialization versus learned updates.
- Report sensitivity to the number of updates: how robust is performance if meta-test uses fewer/more steps than trained for?
- Include runtime/memory overhead comparisons with SGD/Adam fine-tuning and Matching Networks at meta-test time.
- Analyze sensitivity to preprocessing (e.g., p=10) and whether scale invariance is critical.
- Expand gate visualizations: aggregate gate statistics per layer and step, and correlate with loss/gradient norms to make the learned strategy easier to interpret.
- Minor: fix the typo in Section 3.2 (“each coordinate has its”).
- Code release (“We plan on releasing the code used in our evaluation experiments.”) would be a major plus for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- GRU vs LSTM: Did you try a GRU-based parameterization (with tied gates or sum-to-one constraints)? If so, how does it compare?
- Gate ablations: What is the effect of setting ft=1 (no forgetting) or using a fixed it? How much do gains depend on the learned gating versus the learned initialization?
- Initialization: How much improvement comes from learning c0 alone (with a standard optimizer) versus learning both c0 and the update rule?
- Steps mismatch: If the meta-learner is trained for T steps but evaluated with T±k steps, how does accuracy degrade?
- BN statistics: Could you quantify performance differences between your chosen BN strategy, using only running averages, and using per-episode statistics throughout?
- Gradient independence: How large is the empirical gap (if any) between using the gradient-independence assumption versus including second-order terms on small models?
- Parameter sharing granularity: Did you explore per-layer or per-tensor meta-learners versus per-coordinate sharing? Any trade-offs in performance versus model size?
- Computational cost: What is the meta-test-time latency compared to fine-tuning with SGD/Adam for the same number of steps?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and timely contribution with solid empirical support and practical insights. With the suggested clarifications and ablations, it would be even stronger.
