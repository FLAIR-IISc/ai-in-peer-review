Given the authors’ responsiveness and the manuscript updates—especially the clarification of the meta-learning task—I am raising my score to an 8.

This paper addresses few-shot learning in neural networks via meta-learning, a classical idea that has resurged over the past year. The authors cast few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each “example” consists of a sequence of batches of “training” pairs, followed by a final “test” batch. At each step, the inputs include the outputs of a base learner (e.g., training loss and gradients) along with the base learner’s current state (parameters). They apply an LSTM to this meta-learning setup, using the second-layer memory cells to directly represent the base learner’s updated parameters, and highlight parallels between LSTM memory updates and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is trained using the base learner’s prediction loss on the final test batch. The approach includes simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells (analogous to using a single learning rate across parameters). The authors recreate the Mini-ImageNet dataset from Vinyals et al. (2016) and show that the LSTM meta-learner is competitive with the state of the art (Matching Networks, Vinyals et al., 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

Strengths:
- It is compelling—and, in hindsight, natural—to frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem. While the authors are not the first to explore learning across a series of tasks, they appear to advance the state of the art, though I cannot confidently assess novelty given my limited familiarity with recent meta-learning work.
- The proposed method is competitive with, and in some cases outperforms, Vinyals et al. (2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner here (a simple ConvNet classifier) differs markedly from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-an-embedding approach used in Vinyals et al. (2016). It is encouraging to see state-of-the-art results from a substantially different method, rather than incremental extensions.
- To my knowledge, the observation connecting LSTM memory cell updates to gradient descent is novel here and interesting in its own right.
- The paper offers practical guidance for designing and training an LSTM meta-learner—e.g., proper initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling loss, gradient, and parameter inputs—which should aid reproducibility and extension. Some insights are known (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training; treating meta-learner and base-learner parameters as independent when differentiating w.r.t. meta-learner parameters), but the discussion remains useful.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is frequently opaque. Despite the interesting content, the paper is not an easy read; I needed multiple passes and external references to grasp the overall setup. The task description in Section 2 (page 2) is highly abstract and uses notation and terminology uncommon outside this sub-area. A brief concrete example (MNIST would suffice), ideally with a diagram showing a sequence of few-shot tasks, would broaden accessibility.
- The precise definition of the N-class, few-shot problem remains unclear. Mini-ImageNet has 100 classes, split 64/16/20 for meta-train/validation/test. Does meta-training observe only 64 of the 100 classes overall, or 64 per batch with all 100 seen on average over meta-training? If it is only 64 overall, how many outputs does the ConvNet’s softmax have during meta-training—64 (only those seen) or 100 (with 36 never observed)? Several related details remain ambiguous (see questions).
- The plots in Figure 2 are not very informative on their own, and the accompanying discussion adds little insight.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with convincing results and seems like a clear accept, but the presentation could be significantly improved. I would raise my score further if the writing were clarified.
