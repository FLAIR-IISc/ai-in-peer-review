This paper addresses few-shot learning with neural networks by employing meta-learning, a long-standing idea that has resurged over the past year. The authors cast few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning task: each “example” consists of a sequence of batches of “training” pairs, followed by a final “test” batch. At each “step,” the inputs include outputs from a “base learner” (e.g., training loss and gradients) and the base learner’s current state (parameters). The method applies an LSTM to this meta-learning setting, using the second-layer memory cells to directly represent the base learner’s updated parameters. They highlight parallels between the LSTM memory cell update and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is trained using the base learner’s prediction loss on the final “test” batch. The approach adopts simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells (analogous to using a uniform learning rate across parameters). The authors reconstruct the Mini-ImageNet dataset from Vinyals et al., 2016, and show that the LSTM meta-learner is competitive with the current state of the art (Matching Networks, Vinyals et al., 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

Strengths:
- Framing few-shot learning as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is compelling—and, in hindsight, natural. While the authors did not originate the general idea of carrying knowledge across a sequence of learning tasks, it seems fair to say they have advanced the state of the art, though I cannot firmly assess novelty as I am not fully up to date on recent meta-learning work.
- The proposed method is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals 2016 on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner here (a simple ConvNet classifier) differs substantially from the nearest-neighbor-on-embedding approach in Vinyals 2016. It is encouraging to see state-of-the-art results achieved via a qualitatively different strategy, rather than incremental follow-ups.
- To my knowledge, the connection drawn between the memory cell update and gradient descent is novel here. It is interesting in its own right.
- The paper provides practical guidance for designing and training an LSTM meta-learner that should aid reproducibility and broader application: appropriate initialization, coordinate-wise weight sharing, and normalizing/rescaling loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. Some of these points are known (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training; treating meta-learner and base-learner parameters as independent when differentiating w.r.t. the meta-learner), but the discussion is still valuable.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is sometimes quite opaque. Although the work is interesting, the paper is not an easy read; I needed multiple passes and related work to grasp the overall learning setup. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is highly abstract and relies on subfield-specific notation. A brief concrete example (MNIST would suffice), perhaps alongside a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot tasks, would broaden accessibility.
- The exact N-way, K-shot formulation is unclear. Mini-ImageNet has 100 labels split 64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing. Does this imply only 64/100 classes are ever seen during meta-training, or that each batch samples 64/100 while, over time, all 100 are observed? If it’s the former, how many outputs does the ConvNet base learner’s softmax have during meta-training—64 (only those seen) or 100 (with 36 never observed)? Many similar details are ambiguous (see questions).
- The plots in Figure 2 are largely uninformative in isolation, and the accompanying discussion offers little additional insight.

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. It appears to be a clear accept, but the presentation of the ideas could be strengthened. I will gladly raise my score if the writing is improved.
