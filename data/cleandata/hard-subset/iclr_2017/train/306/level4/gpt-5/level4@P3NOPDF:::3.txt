In light of the authors’ responsiveness and the revised manuscript—especially the clearer description of the meta-learning task—I am updating my score to an 8.

Summary
This paper addresses few-shot learning via meta-learning. The authors cast N-way K-shot classification as a sequential meta-learning problem: each task consists of a sequence of “training” batches followed by a “test” batch. At each step, the meta-learner receives signals from a base learner (e.g., loss, gradients, and current parameters), and updates its internal state. The second-layer LSTM cell states are used to represent and update the base learner’s parameters, leveraging the analogy between LSTM updates and gradient descent. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end to minimize prediction loss on the final test batch of each task. The approach includes practical design choices (e.g., weight sharing across parameter coordinates and careful normalization of inputs). On Mini-ImageNet, the method is competitive with and often outperforms Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot classification with a simple ConvNet base learner.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling framing of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem. While not wholly unprecedented, the formulation is natural and well-motivated here.
- Strong empirical results: competitive or better than Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet 1-shot and 5-shot settings.
- Architectural diversity: achieving state-of-the-art performance with a ConvNet classifier base learner rather than nearest-neighbor on learned embeddings demonstrates the robustness of the idea.
- Insightful connection between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent. Even if partly intuitive in retrospect, the explicit use of LSTM memory cells as parameter surrogates is novel and interesting.
- Practical guidance that should aid reproducibility: parameter initialization, coordinate-wise weight sharing, normalization/rescaling of losses, gradients, and parameters, and simulating test-time conditions during meta-training.
- The revised version improves clarity on the meta-learning setup and training objective.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Exposition remains dense. The task description in Section 2 is abstract and uses specialized notation that may hinder accessibility. A brief, concrete walk-through of one N-way K-shot episode (even with MNIST-style placeholders), paired with a small diagram, would substantially help readers unfamiliar with meta-learning.
- Experimental setup details could be clearer. The Mini-ImageNet split (64/16/20 train/val/test classes) is standard, but the manuscript should make explicit:
  - During meta-training, are tasks sampled only from the 64 training classes, with no leakage to validation/test classes?
  - What is the output dimensionality of the base learner’s softmax during meta-training—N for the current task or the number of meta-training classes? If task-specific, how is the classifier head instantiated/reset across tasks?
  - How are support and query (train/test) batches constructed within each episode? Please specify numbers of images per class, number of steps, and whether classes are balanced at each step.
  - Are the base learner’s parameters reinitialized between tasks, or are they carried over? If reinitialized, describe the initialization; if not, clarify how catastrophic forgetting is handled.
- Figures (notably Figure 2) are not very informative on their own. Consider:
  - Adding labels and captions that directly state the takeaway.
  - Including ablations isolating the impact of key design choices: coordinate-wise weight sharing, input normalization schemes, and the form of the auxiliary signals (loss vs. gradients vs. parameters).
  - Providing learning curves across episodes and within an episode to illustrate how the meta-learner adapts over time.
- Discussion of computational cost would be useful. Please comment on:
  - Training stability and variance across random seeds.
  - Wall-clock time and memory usage relative to baselines.
  - Sensitivity to episode length and N/K.
- While the LSTM-as-optimizer connection is appealing, the paper would benefit from a short analytical or empirical comparison with vanilla gradient descent or learned optimizers that do not embed parameters directly in cell states (e.g., learned step sizes) to clarify what is uniquely gained.

Questions for clarification
- Do the LSTM second-layer cells map one-to-one to base learner parameters? If so, how is this handled when parameter counts differ across layers or architectures?
- Are the meta-learner parameters shared across all tasks and all coordinates permanently, or do you allow per-layer/per-module specializations?
- How are gradients with respect to the meta-learner computed across long sequences (e.g., truncated BPTT)? If truncated, how does truncation length affect performance?

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and timely paper that advances meta-learning for few-shot classification with strong empirical results and useful implementation insights. Despite remaining clarity issues, the revised manuscript addresses key concerns around the task setup, and the method’s performance is convincing. I recommend acceptance. With further improvements to exposition (especially a concrete episode example, clearer dataset/protocol details, and more informative figures/ablations), this will be a strong and impactful contribution.
