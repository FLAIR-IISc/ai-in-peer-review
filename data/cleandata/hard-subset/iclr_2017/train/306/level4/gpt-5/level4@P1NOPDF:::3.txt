This manuscript tackles few-shot learning with neural networks via meta-learning, a classic idea that has seen a resurgence over the past year. The authors cast few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each “example” consists of a sequence of training batches, followed by a final test batch. At each step, the inputs include the outputs of a base learner (e.g., training loss and gradients) as well as the base learner’s current state (its parameters). An LSTM is applied to this meta-learning problem, with the second-layer memory cells used to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. The authors note parallels between LSTM memory updates and gradient descent. The meta-learner is trained based on the base learner’s prediction loss on the final test batch. Several simplifying assumptions are made, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells (analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters). The Mini-ImageNet dataset from Vinyals et al. (2016) is recreated, and the LSTM meta-learner is shown to be competitive with the state of the art (Matching Networks, Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

Strengths:
- Casting few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is intriguing—and, in hindsight, natural. While the general idea of carrying knowledge across tasks is not new, this work appears to advance the state of the art, though I cannot confidently assess its novelty as I am not deeply familiar with all recent meta-learning literature.
- The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals (2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner here (a simple ConvNet classifier) is quite different from the nearest-neighbor-on-a-learned-embedding approach in Vinyals (2016). It is encouraging to see state-of-the-art results achieved with a substantially different methodology rather than incremental follow-up.
- The observation about the relationship between LSTM memory updates and gradient descent appears novel and is interesting in its own right.
- The paper offers several practical insights into designing and training an LSTM meta-learner that should aid reproducibility and broader application: proper initialization, coordinate-wise weight sharing, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradients, and parameters provided as inputs. Some of these points have been noted before (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training; assuming independence between meta-learner and base-learner parameters when differentiating with respect to the meta-learner), but the discussion here is still useful.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is often opaque. Although the work is interesting, the paper is not an easy read. It took me multiple passes (and consulting related work) to understand the learning problem. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is very abstract and uses notation and terminology that are not widely used outside this subarea. A brief concrete example (MNIST would suffice), ideally with a diagram showing a sequence of few-shot tasks, would make the paper accessible to a broader audience.
- The precise setup of the N-way, K-shot problem is unclear. Mini-ImageNet has 100 labels, split 64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing. Does meta-training expose only the 64 training classes overall, or 64 per batch while eventually covering all 100 across meta-training? If only 64 are seen during meta-training, how many outputs does the ConvNet base learner’s softmax have during meta-training—64 (only the observed classes) or 100 (with 36 never observed)? Many related implementation details remain unclear.
- The plots in Figure 2 are not informative on their own, and the accompanying discussion provides little interpretation.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with convincing results. It feels like a clear accept, but the presentation could be substantially improved. I will raise my score further if the writing is clarified.
