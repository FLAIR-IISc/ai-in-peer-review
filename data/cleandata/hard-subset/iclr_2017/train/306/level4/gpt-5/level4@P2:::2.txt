Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. By casting parameter updates as gated state updates, the meta-learner outputs per-parameter update rules (input and forget gates) and a learned initialization for the classifier.
- The training objective matches test-time conditions: for each episode, the meta-learner observes gradients and losses computed on the small training set and is optimized via the resulting loss on the episode’s test set.
- The model shares optimizer parameters across coordinates to keep the meta-learner compact, employs gradient/loss preprocessing, and carefully handles batch normalization to avoid cross-episode leakage while keeping train/test conditions aligned.
- On Mini-ImageNet 1-shot and 5-shot, the approach outperforms strong baselines and is competitive with, or better than, Matching Networks, while providing practical advice on initialization and BN.

Decision
Accept. The approach is original and well-motivated, with competitive empirical results and practical insights for few-shot learning. The paper could improve clarity and deepen ablations, but the core idea and evidence warrant acceptance.

Supporting arguments
- Significance and novelty: Learning an optimizer tailored to a fixed small number of updates, jointly with a task-common initialization, is compelling for few-shot learning. The mapping from gradient descent to gated updates is elegant and enables end-to-end training of an optimizer that directly targets generalization after few steps.
- Positioning and motivation: The paper situates itself against metric-learning methods and prior work on learned optimizers, highlighting the few-shot setting and the value of matching train/test conditions at the episode level.
- Methodological soundness: The parameter-sharing scheme across coordinates, the preprocessing for numerical stability, and the careful BN protocol are sensible and practically relevant. The gradient-independence approximation follows prior work and appears effective in practice.
- Empirical support: On Mini-ImageNet, the method beats a nearest-neighbor baseline and a fine-tuning baseline, and is competitive with Matching Networks (notably stronger on 5-shot). The observation that naive fine-tuning overfits in 1-shot further motivates meta-learned initialization and updates.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the GRU/LSTM analogy: The update rule is arguably closer to a GRU-style gated update than a full LSTM cell. The paper briefly notes this; making the connection explicit earlier and explaining why the LSTM framing is preferred would reduce confusion.
- Meta-set/data split clarity: The description of meta-training/validation/test splits and episode construction would benefit from a concise diagram and a short, concrete example to improve readability.
- Ablations on gates and components: Please include analyses isolating the contributions of the input and forget gates (e.g., fixing ft=1, removing it’s dependence on Lt, or sharing gates across layers) and the learned initialization (optimizer+random init vs. learned init+standard optimizer).
- Sensitivity studies: Report sensitivity to the number of update steps T (train-time vs. test-time mismatch), preprocessing hyperparameter p, and the BN strategy (e.g., using only running stats vs. per-episode stats).
- Figure 2: The computational graph figure adds limited insight. Consider replacing it with a schematic showing the per-parameter gated update and information flow over an episode.
- Reproducibility: Public code release (as stated) would be a major plus. Include key training details (batch sizes, episode counts, wall-clock time, memory) and parameter counts for the meta-learner.

Questions for the authors
- GRU vs. LSTM: Given the closer resemblance to GRU-style updates, did you experiment with a GRU formulation? If so, how did performance and stability compare?
- Role of gates: What is the empirical effect of disabling or simplifying ft or it, tying gates across layers, or removing Lt from the gate inputs?
- Learned initialization: How much of the gain comes from the learned initialization alone? For example, how does “learned init + SGD/Adam” compare to “random init + learned optimizer” and to the full model?
- Number of steps: How sensitive is performance to the number of updates at meta-test time if it differs from training? Does the meta-learner generalize to more/fewer steps?
- Preprocessing and BN: How critical are the log-scaling preprocessing and your BN protocol? Please provide results without preprocessing and with alternative BN choices.
- Gradient-independence assumption: Did you try variants that partially account for second-order terms (e.g., truncated backprop or limited higher-order gradients)? Any observed trade-offs?
- Compute/memory: What are the training and inference overheads of the meta-learner relative to standard fine-tuning? Please include parameter counts and runtime/memory measurements.
- Generality: Have you tested deeper learners or different backbones, and do the learned updates transfer across architectures or datasets?

Minor issues
- Typo in Section 3.2: “each coordinate has it” → “its.”
- Consider adding confidence intervals directly in the main results table and clarifying the exact Mini-ImageNet split used.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and practically useful contribution to few-shot learning, with competitive results and a clear path to further strengthening via ablations and improved clarity.
