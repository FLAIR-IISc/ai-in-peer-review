In light of the authors’ responsiveness and the revisions—especially the clearer description of the meta-learning task—I am updating my score to an 8.

This paper addresses few-shot learning with neural networks via meta-learning. The authors cast few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each episode consists of a sequence of “training” batches followed by a final “test” batch. At each step, the meta-learner receives the base learner’s current parameters along with signals such as training loss and gradients. An LSTM is used as the meta-learner, with the second-layer memory cells directly representing the base learner’s parameters, leveraging the analogy between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent. The meta-learner is trained based on the base learner’s loss on the final test batch. Several simplifying assumptions are made, including sharing weights across second-layer cells (analogous to a shared learning rate across parameters). On Mini-ImageNet (reproduced from Vinyals et al., 2016), the method is competitive with, and in some cases outperforms, Matching Networks on 1-shot and 5-shot tasks.

Strengths:
- Casting few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is intriguing and, in hindsight, natural. While the general idea of carrying knowledge across tasks is not new, this work appears to advance the state of the art (though I cannot fully assess novelty relative to the most recent meta-learning literature).
- The approach is competitive with or superior to Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet.
- The base learner is a simple ConvNet classifier, contrasting with the nearest-neighbor-on-embeddings strategy in Vinyals et al. It is encouraging to see strong results from such a different approach rather than an incremental variant.
- The connection drawn between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent appears novel here and is interesting in its own right.
- The paper provides practical guidance for building and training an LSTM meta-learner—e.g., careful initialization, coordinate-wise weight sharing, and normalizing/rescaling losses, gradients, and parameters as inputs. Some points echo prior advice (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training; treating base- and meta-learner parameters as independent when differentiating), but the consolidated discussion is useful and should aid reproducibility and extension.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is often opaque. Despite the interesting ideas, the paper is not an easy read. I needed multiple passes and external references to fully grasp the setup. Section 2 is especially abstract and uses notation uncommon outside this niche. A brief concrete example (MNIST would suffice), ideally with a diagram of an episode, would substantially improve accessibility.
- The N-way, few-shot setting on Mini-ImageNet is not precisely specified. Mini-ImageNet has 100 labels, split 64/16/20 for meta-train/val/test. Are only 64 classes ever seen during meta-training, or do episodes eventually cover all 100? If only 64 are used for meta-training, how many outputs does the base learner’s softmax have during meta-training—64 (only the observed classes) or 100 (with 36 never observed)? Several similar details remain unclear.
- The plots in Figure 2 are not very informative, and the accompanying discussion provides little interpretation.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with convincing results and a thoughtful formulation of few-shot learning as sequential meta-learning. It looks like a clear accept, but the presentation could be significantly improved. I am prepared to raise my score further if the exposition is clarified.
