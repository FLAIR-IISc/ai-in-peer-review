This paper presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns the optimization algorithm for another learning algorithm (here, a neural network). The paper is generally well written, and the presentation of the core ideas is clear. The crux of the work—drawing a parallel between the Robbins–Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule and exploiting this to meet two key desiderata of few-shot learning: (1) rapid acquisition of new knowledge, and (2) slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge—is intriguing.

Several techniques reused from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, along with novel design choices (e.g., a specific implementation of batch normalization), are well motivated. The experiments are convincing. Overall, this is a strong paper. My only concerns/questions are as follows:

1. Could it be redundant to use the loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner? Did you conduct ablation studies to ensure that simpler input combinations are not sufficient?

2. It would be great if additional architectural components could be learned in a similar fashion (e.g., number of neurons, unit types, etc.). Do you have thoughts on the feasibility or challenges of this?

3. The related work section (mainly focused on meta-learning) is somewhat shallow. Meta-learning has a long history, and related approaches have addressed similar problems without using LSTMs:
   - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) focuses on this topic.
   - Use of genetic programming to search for new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
   - I am convinced Schmidhuber has relevant work; please locate and cite it, and update the related work section accordingly.

Overall, I like the paper and believe the material will be of interest to a broad ICLR audience.
