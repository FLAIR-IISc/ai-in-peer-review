This manuscript addresses few-shot learning using a meta-learning approach with neural networks, building on a recently revitalized area of research. The authors frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each “example” consists of a series of batches used for “training,” followed by a final “test” batch. The inputs to the meta-learner at each step include the base learner’s outputs (loss and gradients) and its current parameters. An LSTM is employed as the meta-learner, with the second layer’s memory cells designed to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. The authors highlight a connection between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is trained based on the base learner’s prediction loss on the final “test” batch. Several simplifying assumptions are made, such as weight sharing across all second layer cells, mirroring the use of a consistent learning rate for all parameters. The method is evaluated on a recreation of the Mini-ImageNet dataset (Vinyals et al., 2016), demonstrating competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods like Matching Networks (Vinyals, 2016) in both 1-shot and 5-shot learning scenarios.

**Strengths:**

- The formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is insightful and, in retrospect, intuitive. While the authors didn’t originate the core idea of persistent learning, their work represents a significant advancement in the field, though I lack the deep expertise to definitively assess its novelty.
- The proposed approach achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to Vinyals (2016) on the 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The use of a simple ConvNet as the base learner contrasts with the embedding-based approach of Vinyals (2016), which is encouraging – demonstrating strong results with distinct methodologies is a valuable contribution.
- The observation regarding the relationship between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent appears novel and is an interesting finding.
- The paper provides practical guidance for designing and training LSTM meta-learners, facilitating replication and application to new problems. This includes recommendations for initialization, weight sharing, and loss/gradient/parameter normalization. While some of these insights have been previously reported (simulating test conditions during meta-training, assuming independence between meta-learner and base learner parameters), the discussion is nonetheless helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

- The writing style is occasionally dense and difficult to follow. Despite presenting compelling work, the paper is not particularly accessible. Understanding the core learning problem required multiple readings and consultation of related literature. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is abstract and employs specialized notation, hindering broader understanding. A concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) accompanied by a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks would significantly improve clarity.
- The precise definition of the N-class, few-shot learning problem remains unclear. Specifically, regarding the Mini-ImageNet dataset (100 labels, split 64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing), it’s unclear whether only 64 classes are observed during meta-training, or if all 100 are observed on average across batches. If the former, the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner during meta-training should have 64 outputs, but it’s unclear if this is the case. Several other details require clarification.
- The plots in Figure 2 lack sufficient information and the accompanying discussion provides limited insight. 

Overall, this is a promising paper with convincing results, and appears to be a strong candidate for acceptance. However, improvements to the presentation of the ideas and work would be beneficial. I would be inclined to increase my score if the writing is revised for greater clarity.
