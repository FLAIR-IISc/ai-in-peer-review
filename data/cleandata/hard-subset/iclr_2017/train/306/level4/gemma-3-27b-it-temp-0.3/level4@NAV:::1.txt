This paper introduces a meta-learning framework based on LSTMs, designed to learn the optimization algorithm for another learning algorithm – specifically, a neural network. 

The paper is generally well-written and presents the core ideas clearly. The central contribution – establishing a connection between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update mechanism, and leveraging this to address the key requirements of few-shot learning (rapid acquisition of new information and slower extraction of generalizable knowledge) – is particularly compelling. 

The authors effectively justify both the re-implementation of techniques from prior work (e.g., parameter sharing and normalization, as in Andrychowicz et al. 2016) and their novel design decisions, including the specific implementation of batch normalization.

The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the strength of the proposed approach. However, I have a few questions and suggestions:

1.  Is the inclusion of loss, gradient, *and* parameters as input to the meta-learner necessary? Have ablation studies been conducted to determine if simpler input combinations would suffice?
2.  Could other aspects of the network architecture – such as the number of neurons or the type of activation units – also be learned through a similar meta-learning process? What are your thoughts on extending the framework in this direction?
3.  The related work section, while focused on meta-learning, could benefit from greater depth. Meta-learning has a substantial history, and related approaches to learning learning rules have been explored previously, even outside the context of LSTMs. For example:
    *   Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) directly addresses this problem.
    *   The application of genetic programming to discover new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994) is also relevant.
    *   I recommend investigating the work of Jürgen Schmidhuber, as he has likely made contributions in this area; please ensure his work is included in the related work section.

Overall, this is a strong paper with material highly relevant to the ICLR audience. I recommend its acceptance.
