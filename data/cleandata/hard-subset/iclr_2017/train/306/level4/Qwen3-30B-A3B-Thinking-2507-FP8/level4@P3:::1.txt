

This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm for training neural network classifiers in the few-shot learning regime. The core insight—drawing a parallel between gradient update rules and LSTM cell state dynamics—enables the meta-learner to simultaneously learn a task-specific parameter initialization and an adaptive update mechanism optimized for a fixed number of training steps. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of standard optimization algorithms in few-shot settings and effectively addresses two key challenges: the need for rapid convergence from a strong initialization and the requirement for a tailored update strategy. The paper demonstrates competitive results against strong baselines and Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet, with convincing empirical validation.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper makes a compelling contribution to meta-learning with two key strengths: (1) a novel, theoretically grounded connection between LSTM dynamics and optimization updates that directly targets the few-shot learning constraints, and (2) rigorous experimental validation showing clear performance gains over baselines and competitive results with state-of-the-art metric-learning approaches.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper’s core innovation lies in leveraging the LSTM’s gating mechanism to learn *both* the initial weights of the learner network and the update rule for a fixed number of steps. This addresses two critical weaknesses of gradient-based optimization in few-shot learning: (i) the lack of task-specific initialization (mitigated by learning the LSTM’s cell state initialization), and (ii) the suboptimality of generic optimizers like Adam for constrained update counts (addressed by the adaptive input/forget gates). The design choices—parameter sharing, gradient preprocessing, and the batch normalization strategy—are well-justified and build cohesively on prior work (e.g., Andrychowicz et al., 2016), while the batch normalization adaptation for meta-learning is particularly noteworthy for its technical precision. The empirical results are robust: the method significantly outperforms baseline fine-tuning and nearest-neighbor approaches, and achieves results comparable to Matching Networks (with stronger performance in 5-shot settings). The ablation study showing that meta-learned initialization prevents overfitting in few-shot scenarios further validates the approach’s core thesis.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
To strengthen the paper, consider the following:  
1. **Ablation on input features**: The meta-learner uses loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs. While motivated, an ablation study comparing simpler input combinations (e.g., gradient-only or gradient+loss) would clarify whether all components are necessary. This would strengthen the claim that the input design is optimal.  
2. **Architectural flexibility**: The paper focuses on learning optimization parameters. Exploring whether other architectural choices (e.g., layer depth, activation functions) could be meta-learned similarly would broaden the method’s scope and align with the "learning to learn" paradigm. A brief discussion of this as future work would be valuable.  
3. **Related work depth**: The related work section omits key historical context. Bengio’s 1989 PhD thesis ("Learning a synaptic learning rule") and Schmidhuber’s early work on learning optimizers (e.g., Schmidhuber, 1992) directly address the same problem using different techniques. Adding these would better position the work within meta-learning’s evolution.  

**Clarifying Questions**  
To ensure confidence in the assessment:  
1. The meta-learner’s input includes the current loss \(L_t\), gradient \(\nabla_\theta L_t\), and parameters \(\theta_{t-1}\). Did you test whether the loss \(L_t\) is redundant (e.g., via ablation removing it)? If so, how did performance change?  
2. In the batch normalization adaptation, you reset running statistics between meta-testing episodes. How sensitive is the performance to the exact method of resetting (e.g., full reset vs. partial reset)?  
3. The paper states the meta-learner learns a "general initialization" for the learner. To what extent is this initialization task-specific versus truly transferable across datasets? Could you share examples of initialization weights across different tasks?  

This work makes a significant and well-executed contribution to meta-learning, with clear theoretical grounding and strong empirical results. The minor gaps noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper’s overall strength. The method is relevant to a broad ICLR audience and advances the state of the art in few-shot learning.
