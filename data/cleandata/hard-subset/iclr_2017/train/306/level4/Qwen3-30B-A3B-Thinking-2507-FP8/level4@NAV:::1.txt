

This work introduces an LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm (specifically, a neural network). The paper is well-written and presents the main contributions clearly. The core contribution—establishing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule to address two key desiderata of few-shot learning (rapid acquisition of new knowledge and gradual extraction of transferable knowledge)—is compelling. The reuse of techniques from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, alongside novel design choices (e.g., a specific batch normalization implementation), is well-justified. The experimental results are convincing, making this a strong paper. My primary concerns are:  

1. Is the inclusion of loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner redundant? Ablation studies confirming that simpler input combinations suffice would strengthen the design.  
2. Could other architectural components (e.g., number of neurons, unit types) be learned similarly? The authors’ perspective on this extension would be valuable.  
3. The related work section (focused mainly on meta-learning) is somewhat superficial. Given the maturity of meta-learning, earlier approaches addressing similar problems should be acknowledged, including:  
   - Samy Bengio’s 1989 PhD thesis  
   - Genetic programming for learning neural network optimization rules (Bengio, Bengio, & Cloutier, 1994)  
   - Relevant work by Schmidhuber on this topic.  

Overall, I find the paper relevant and impactful for a broad ICLR audience.
