

This work introduces an LSTM-based meta-learning framework for learning the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm (specifically, a neural network). The paper is well-written with clear presentation of the main contributions. The core insight—drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule to satisfy two key desiderata of few-shot learning (1) rapid acquisition of new knowledge and (2) slower extraction of generalizable knowledge—is intriguing. Several techniques, including parameter sharing and normalization (reused from Andrychowicz et al., 2016), and novel design choices (such as a specific implementation of batch normalization), are well-motivated. The experimental results are compelling, making this a strong paper. My main concerns are as follows:  
1. Is it redundant to use loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner? Ablative studies should be conducted to verify that simpler input combinations are insufficient.  
2. It would be valuable to explore whether other architectural components (e.g., number of neurons, unit types) could be learned similarly. What are your thoughts on this?  
3. The related work section (focused primarily on meta-learning) is somewhat superficial. Meta-learning is a well-established field, and prior work addressing similar problems—though not LSTM-based—should be acknowledged:  
   - Samy Bengio’s 1989 PhD thesis (focused on this problem)  
   - Genetic programming approaches for learning neural network update rules (Bengio, Bengio, & Cloutier, 1994)  
   - Ensure Schmidhuber’s relevant prior work is included and cited in the related work section.  
Overall, I find the paper compelling and relevant to a broad ICLR audience.
