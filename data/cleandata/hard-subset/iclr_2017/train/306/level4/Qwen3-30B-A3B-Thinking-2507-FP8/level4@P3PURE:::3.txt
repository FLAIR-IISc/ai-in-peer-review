

In light of the authors' responsiveness and the manuscript's updates—including clarifications to the meta-learning task—I am updating my score to an 8.  

This manuscript addresses few-shot learning via meta-learning, a concept that has recently gained renewed interest. The authors frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each "example" consists of a sequence of training batches followed by a test batch. At each step, the input includes outputs from a "base learner" (e.g., training loss, gradients) and its current parameter state. The approach employs an LSTM to model this meta-learning process, using the second-layer memory cells to directly represent the base learner’s updated parameters. Notably, the authors observe parallels between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent. Meta-learner updates are computed based on the base learner’s prediction loss over the final test batch. Key simplifications include weight sharing across second-layer cells (analogous to a uniform learning rate) and normalization of loss/gradient/parameter inputs. The method achieves competitive results on Mini-ImageNet (recreated from Vinyals et al., 2016), outperforming Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) in 1- and 5-shot settings.  

**Strengths:**  
- The sequential meta-learning formulation is natural and compelling, even if not entirely novel. The authors advance the state of the art in this space, though novelty requires deeper familiarity with recent meta-learning literature.  
- The approach is competitive with and exceeds Vinyals et al. (2016) on Mini-ImageNet, using a fundamentally different base learner (a simple ConvNet classifier versus their embedding-based nearest-neighbor method). This diversity of successful approaches is valuable.  
- The insight linking LSTM memory cells to gradient descent updates is novel and conceptually interesting.  
- The paper provides actionable practical guidance for implementing LSTM meta-learners (e.g., proper initialization, weight sharing, input normalization), easing replication and adaptation to new tasks. While some insights (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training) are previously noted, the discussion here is exceptionally clear and useful.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The writing is often opaque, requiring multiple readings to grasp the core problem. Section 2’s abstract description (Page 2) relies on specialized notation and language, hindering accessibility. A concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot tasks would significantly improve clarity.  
- The precise setup of the N-class few-shot problem remains ambiguous. Mini-ImageNet uses 100 classes (64 for meta-training, 16 for validation, 20 for testing). Does meta-training observe only 64 classes total, or 64 classes per batch (with all 100 classes covered across batches)? If the former, does the base learner’s softmax output 64 or 100 classes during meta-training? Similar ambiguities persist (e.g., in the loss function and data splits).  
- Figure 2’s plots are uninformative without context, and the discussion offers minimal interpretation.  

This is an interesting paper with strong empirical results. The technical contributions are sound, and the insights are valuable. However, the presentation requires significant improvement to reach its full potential. I would recommend acceptance contingent on clarifying the methodological details and enhancing the exposition.
