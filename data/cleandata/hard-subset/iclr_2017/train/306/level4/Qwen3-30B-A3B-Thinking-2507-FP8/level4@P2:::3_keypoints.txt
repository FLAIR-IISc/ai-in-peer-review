- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm for a ConvNet classifier to solve few-shot learning.  
- Frames few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem where the LSTM processes gradients and losses to update learner parameters.  
- Leverages similarity between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent to design a parameter-efficient meta-optimizer.  
- Learns both task-specific initialization and an adaptive update rule tailored to a fixed number of training steps.  
- Empirical results on Mini-ImageNet outperform baselines and are competitive with Matching Networks on 1-shot and 5-shot tasks.  
- Decision: Accept — novel, theoretically grounded meta-optimization framework with strong empirical results.  
- Core innovation: reinterpret gradient descent as LSTM updates, using cell state as parameters and gates as adaptive learning rates.  
- Benefits: avoids task-specific hyperparameter tuning and addresses optimizer inefficiency under strict update constraints and lack of task-agnostic initialization.  
- Practical contributions: details for reproducibility (parameter sharing across coordinates, gradient preprocessing, LSTM initialization).  
- Paper acknowledges limitations (gradient independence assumption) and validates via ablation studies.  
- Evaluation: rigorous benchmarking on Mini-ImageNet with clear comparisons to nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines.  
- Suggested improvements: add a simple illustrative example/diagram (e.g., MNIST) to clarify the meta-learning workflow.  
- Suggested clarification: explicitly state how the ConvNet softmax handles classes during meta-training (e.g., number of output units).  
- Suggested expansion: better interpret gate-value variability (Figure 3) to explain why dataset-dependent gate behavior matters.  
- Questions for authors: clarify softmax output size during meta-training and how generalization to unseen classes is achieved.  
- Questions for authors: indicate whether the 1-shot performance difference versus Matching Networks is statistically significant.  
- Questions for authors: provide a brief qualitative example of learned task-specific update strategies (e.g., gate behaviors for 1-shot vs 5-shot).
