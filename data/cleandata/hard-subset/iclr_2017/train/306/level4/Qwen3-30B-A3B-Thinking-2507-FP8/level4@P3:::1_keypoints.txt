- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimizer for few-shot neural network classification.  
- Core insight: parallel between gradient update rules and LSTM cell state dynamics to learn initialization and adaptive updates for a fixed number of steps.  
- Well-motivated by limitations of standard optimizers in few-shot settings.  
- Addresses two main challenges: rapid convergence from a strong initialization and a tailored update strategy.  
- Shows competitive empirical results vs. strong baselines and Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Two primary strengths: novel, theoretically grounded LSTM–optimizer connection; rigorous experimental validation with clear performance gains.  
- Technical innovation: LSTM gates jointly learn initial weights and update rules, mitigating lack of task-specific initialization and generic optimizer suboptimality.  
- Design choices (parameter sharing, gradient preprocessing, batch-norm adaptation) are coherent and well-justified; batch-norm adaptation is notable.  
- Empirical robustness: outperforms fine-tuning and nearest-neighbor baselines, stronger in 5-shot; ablation shows meta-initialization prevents overfitting.  
- Suggested ablation: test necessity of input features (loss, gradient, parameters) to validate input design.  
- Suggested extension: consider meta-learning other architectural choices (depth, activations) or discuss as future work.  
- Related work gap: include Bengio 1989 and Schmidhuber’s early work (e.g., 1992) for historical context.  
- Clarifying questions: was loss input redundant in ablation? how sensitive is performance to batch-norm reset method? how transferable is the learned initialization across datasets?  
- Overall assessment: significant, well-executed contribution to meta-learning relevant to the ICLR audience.
