

In light of the authors' responsiveness and manuscript revisions—particularly the clarification of the meta-learning task—I am updating my score to 8.  

This manuscript addresses few-shot learning using neural networks via meta-learning, a concept recently revitalized over the past year. The authors frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each "example" comprises a sequence of training batches followed by a test batch. Inputs at each step incorporate the base learner's outputs (e.g., training loss and gradients) and its current state (parameters). The approach employs an LSTM, utilizing the second-layer memory cells to directly model the base learner's updated parameters. The authors draw parallels between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent rules. Meta-learner updates are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final test batch. Key simplifying assumptions include weight sharing across all second-layer cells (analogous to a uniform learning rate for all parameters). The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset from Vinyals et al. (2016) and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Matching Networks, Vinyals et al. 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.  

**Strengths:**  
- The formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is intriguing and, in hindsight, natural. While the general idea of persistent learning across tasks is not novel, the authors advance the state of the art; I cannot confidently assess novelty due to limited familiarity with recent meta-learning work.  
- The approach is competitive and outperforms Vinyals et al. (2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet benchmarks.  
- The base learner (a simple ConvNet classifier) differs significantly from Vinyals et al.'s nearest-neighbor-on-learned-embedding approach, making the state-of-the-art results particularly compelling through a novel methodology rather than incremental refinement.  
- The insight linking LSTM memory cells to gradient descent updates appears novel and is of independent interest.  
- The paper provides valuable practical guidance for designing and training LSTM meta-learners, including proper initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and normalization of loss/gradient/parameter inputs. While some insights (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training) are previously documented, the discussion here is useful and accessible for replication.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The writing is often opaque. Despite presenting interesting work, the paper is difficult to follow without multiple readings and reference to related work. Section 2 (Page 2) describes the task abstractly using non-standard notation and terminology, hindering accessibility. A concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) paired with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot tasks would significantly broaden appeal.  
- The precise setup of the N-class few-shot learning problem remains unclear. Mini-ImageNet uses 100 classes (64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing). It is ambiguous whether only 64 classes are observed during meta-training (implying a 64-output softmax) or all 100 classes are observed across batches (implying a 100-output softmax with 36 never seen). Critical details like this are missing.  
- Figure 2's plots are largely uninformative, and the discussion offers minimal insight into their significance.  

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. While the core contributions warrant acceptance, the presentation requires substantial improvement to enhance clarity and accessibility. I will maintain this score upon satisfactory revisions to the writing.
