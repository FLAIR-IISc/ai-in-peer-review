- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm and a transferable initialization for few-shot classifier training.  
- Core contribution: formal analogy between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent, enabling learned per-step optimization and forgetting.  
- Motivated by limitations of standard gradient methods in few-shot settings (fixed small update counts, poor random initialization).  
- Addresses these limitations via meta-learning that optimizes for constrained update counts and beneficial initialization.  
- Demonstrates competitive empirical performance on Mini-ImageNet versus strong baselines and metric-learning methods.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons for acceptance: principled theoretical connection and rigorous experimental validation showing advantages over baselines and parity with state-of-the-art.  
- Theoretical support: maps LSTM update (Eq.2) to gradient descent (Eq.1) and parameterizes gates to learn learning rates and forgetting mechanisms.  
- Explicitly optimizes for constrained update counts (e.g., 5–12 steps), matching the few-shot regime.  
- Implementation choices (parameter sharing across coordinates, gradient/loss preprocessing, batch-norm strategy) effectively address computational/stability issues.  
- Experiments are thorough: include ablations, careful dataset construction, and confidence intervals; e.g., 5-shot accuracy 57.3% vs. 49.6% fine-tune baseline and 57.1% Matching Networks.  
- Constructive feedback 1: perform input-feature ablation to test necessity of gradients, loss, parameters, and prior gate values.  
- Constructive feedback 2: discuss or explore extending meta-learner to learn architectural components (layers, activations).  
- Constructive feedback 3: expand related work to include foundational meta-learning work (Bengio 1989, 1990, 1995; Schmidhuber 1992, 1993).  
- Clarifying question 1: Is including both loss and gradient as inputs necessary, given gradients already encode loss information?  
- Clarifying question 2: Is resetting batch-norm statistics at meta-test required because training uses dataset-specific stats or merely to prevent leakage?  
- Overall relevance: Highly relevant to ICLR, technically strong, and opens promising directions for meta-learning.
