

This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn the optimization algorithm for another learning algorithm (specifically, a neural network). The paper is well-written, with clear presentation of the core material. The central contribution—drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule to satisfy two key desiderata of few-shot learning (rapid knowledge acquisition and slower extraction of transferable knowledge)—is highly intriguing. Several techniques reused from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, along with novel design choices (specifically, the implementation of batch normalization), are well motivated. The experimental results are convincing, making this a strong submission. My minor concerns are as follows:

1.  **Input Redundancy:** It is unclear whether using loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner is redundant. Ablative studies demonstrating that simpler input combinations suffice would strengthen the design choice.
2.  **Architectural Flexibility:** It would be valuable to explore whether other architectural components (e.g., number of neurons, unit types) could be learned via a similar meta-learning mechanism. The authors' perspective on this is requested.
3.  **Related Work:** The related work section (focused primarily on meta-learning) is somewhat shallow. Meta-learning is a well-established field, and prior work addressing similar problems (even without LSTMs) should be acknowledged. Specifically:
    *   Samy Bengio's 1989 PhD thesis (focused on learning to learn) is highly relevant.
    *   The use of genetic programming to evolve learning rules (Bengio, Bengio, & Cloutier, 1994) represents an earlier approach to the same problem.
    *   Schmidhuber's earlier work on learning to learn should be verified and properly cited.

Overall, I find this paper compelling and highly relevant to ICLR's audience. The technical contributions are significant and well-executed.
