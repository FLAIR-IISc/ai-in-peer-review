

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents an innovative approach to few-shot learning by framing it as a meta-learning problem where an LSTM-based meta-learner learns to optimize a classifier's parameters through a sequence of updates. The authors make a compelling observation that the LSTM cell update rule bears a strong resemblance to gradient descent updates, enabling them to train a meta-learner that simultaneously learns both the initialization of the classifier and the optimization algorithm specifically tailored for the few-shot regime. The method demonstrates competitive performance with state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques on Mini-ImageNet, achieving strong results in both 1-shot and 5-shot classification tasks. The authors provide valuable practical insights regarding the implementation of such meta-learners, including weight sharing across parameters, proper initialization strategies, and preprocessing techniques for gradients and losses.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the meta-learning literature with a novel, well-motivated approach that demonstrates strong empirical results. The core idea of leveraging LSTM cell updates to model gradient descent for few-shot learning is both elegant and effective.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a critical limitation of standard deep learning approaches: their poor performance in few-shot learning scenarios due to the need for many training examples to converge. The authors' key insight—that the LSTM cell update rule resembles gradient descent—provides a natural and principled way to design a meta-learner that optimizes for the few-shot constraint. Their approach is well-justified by the literature and builds upon recent advances in meta-learning.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-conducted. The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet (addressing the lack of official splits in Vinyals et al., 2016) and demonstrate that their method outperforms two strong baselines while achieving competitive results with Matching Networks. The ablation study showing the meta-learner's ability to learn different optimization strategies for different tasks (1-shot vs. 5-shot) provides compelling evidence of the model's flexibility and effectiveness.

The paper's technical contributions are sound and well-supported. The authors make appropriate simplifying assumptions (such as gradient independence) to avoid computational complexity, and they carefully validate that these assumptions do not significantly harm performance. Their implementation details regarding parameter sharing, preprocessing, and batch normalization are particularly valuable for reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper's core contribution is strong, several improvements would enhance its clarity and accessibility:

- The task description in Section 2 is overly abstract. A concrete example (e.g., using MNIST with a few examples per class) would help readers grasp the few-shot learning framework more intuitively. A simple diagram illustrating the meta-training process would also be valuable.

- The description of the Mini-ImageNet setup needs clarification. Specifically, it's unclear whether the base learner's softmax layer has 64, 100, or another number of outputs during meta-training. The paper should explicitly state how the number of classes in the base learner relates to the meta-training setup.

- The discussion of Figure 2 (the computational graph) is insufficient. A more detailed explanation of what the diagram shows and why it matters would help readers understand the model's architecture.

- The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with related work. While the authors mention Andrychowicz et al. (2016), a more detailed discussion of how their approach differs from this prior work would strengthen the contribution statement.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

To ensure confidence in my assessment, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. The paper states "we create our own version of the Mini-ImageNet dataset by selecting a random 100 classes from ImageNet." It would be helpful to know whether the 64/16/20 split refers to 64 classes for meta-training (with 16 for validation and 20 for testing), or whether these numbers refer to the number of examples per class (e.g., 64 examples per class for meta-training). This is critical for understanding how the base learner's classification layer is configured.

2. In Section 3.3.1, the authors state they make the "gradient independence assumption" to avoid second derivatives. Could they provide more intuition about why this assumption is reasonable, particularly given the meta-learner's direct optimization of the base learner's performance? How might the model behave if this assumption were violated?

3. The paper mentions "we noticed that better performance for each task was attained if the meta-learner is explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing." Could the authors elaborate on why this specific training strategy was necessary, and whether this suggests a fundamental limitation of the approach?

This is a strong submission with a clear contribution to the meta-learning literature. The authors have made a significant advance in few-shot learning by developing a principled meta-learning approach that learns both the initialization and optimization algorithm for the few-shot regime. The empirical results are convincing, and the practical insights provided will be valuable to the community. With minor improvements to clarity and exposition, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
