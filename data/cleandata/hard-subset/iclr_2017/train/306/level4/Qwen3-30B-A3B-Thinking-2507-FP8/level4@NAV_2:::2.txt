

1. This paper presents a novel LSTM-based meta-learner that directly optimizes the training process of a classifier for few-shot learning tasks. The authors propose framing gradient-based optimization as a gated recurrent process where the meta-learner learns both a task-specific update rule and an optimal initialization for the learner network. The approach demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art metric-learning methods on Mini-ImageNet, with strong empirical validation across 1-shot and 5-shot settings.

2. Accept. The paper addresses a well-motivated problem in few-shot learning through a technically sound meta-learning framework that directly tackles the core limitations of gradient-based optimization under data scarcity. The method's competitive results and clear theoretical grounding provide compelling evidence for its validity.

3. The contribution is significant as it systematically addresses two key challenges in few-shot learning: the need for task-specific optimization algorithms constrained to a fixed number of updates and the requirement for optimal initialization. The LSTM meta-learner effectively captures both short-term task-specific dynamics and long-term cross-task knowledge, as evidenced by its superior performance over baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) and competitive results with Matching Networks. The implementation details—including parameter sharing, gradient preprocessing, and batch normalization handling—are technically rigorous and well-justified. The training procedure (Algorithm 1) correctly mirrors test-time conditions, and the gradient independence assumption is appropriately acknowledged as a simplification with demonstrated effectiveness.

4. To further strengthen the paper:  
- Clarify the LSTM/GRU analogy in Section 3.1 (the model more closely resembles GRUs due to the lack of gate sum constraints).  
- Include a visualization of the meta-learner's gate dynamics (it/ft) across different datasets to better illustrate its adaptive behavior.  
- Add a sensitivity analysis for the gates' parameters (e.g., how varying the input gate bias affects performance).  
- Consider replacing Figure 2 with a clearer computational graph that emphasizes the meta-learner's role in parameter updates.  
- Address the minor typo in Section 3.2 ("each coordinate has it" → "its").  

Questions for authors:  
- How does the meta-learner's performance vary when trained on a fixed number of updates versus a flexible update count?  
- Could the learned initialization strategy generalize to other network architectures beyond the 4-layer CNN used in experiments?  
- What is the computational overhead of the meta-learner compared to standard fine-tuning?  
- How would the method perform on non-image few-shot tasks (e.g., text or tabular data)?
