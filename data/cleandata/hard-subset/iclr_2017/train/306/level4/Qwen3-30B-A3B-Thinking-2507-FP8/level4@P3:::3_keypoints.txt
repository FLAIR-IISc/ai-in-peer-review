- Proposes framing few-shot learning as meta-learning where an LSTM-based meta-learner learns to optimize a classifier via sequence of updates.  
- Observes LSTM cell update rule closely resembles gradient descent, enabling learned initialization and optimization tailored to few-shot tasks.  
- Demonstrates competitive performance on Mini-ImageNet, achieving strong 1-shot and 5-shot classification results comparable to Matching Networks.  
- Provides practical implementation insights: parameter sharing, initialization strategies, and preprocessing of gradients/losses.  
- Decision: accept — novel, well-motivated approach with strong empirical results.  
- Addresses limitation of standard deep learning in few-shot settings by optimizing for few-shot constraints via meta-learning.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough, includes a custom Mini-ImageNet split, outperforms two strong baselines, and includes ablation studies.  
- Ablation study shows meta-learner can learn different optimization strategies for different shot settings (1-shot vs 5-shot).  
- Technical approach is sound; simplifying assumptions (e.g., gradient independence) are justified and validated as not harming performance.  
- Implementation details (parameter sharing, preprocessing, batch norm) aid reproducibility.  
- Suggests clearer, more concrete task description and a diagram to improve accessibility (e.g., MNIST example).  
- Requests clarification of Mini-ImageNet setup—whether 64/16/20 refers to class splits or examples per class.  
- Recommends expanding Figure 2 discussion to better explain the computational graph and its importance.  
- Advises a more thorough comparison with related work, especially detailed differentiation from Andrychowicz et al. (2016).  
- Asks authors to justify the gradient independence assumption and to explain why meta-training must match the number of meta-testing updates.
