

This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm specifically designed for few-shot learning tasks. The authors frame the problem within a meta-learning framework, where the meta-learner learns both a task-specific initialization for the classifier and an effective parameter update strategy for training on limited examples. The key insight is that the LSTM cell state can represent the learner's parameters, with gates controlling the learning rate and parameter forgetting. The approach demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques on few-shot classification tasks.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to meta-learning by addressing the specific challenges of few-shot learning through a well-motivated approach that directly optimizes for the few-update constraint. The method is theoretically sound, with clear connections to gradient-based optimization principles.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. A novel perspective on optimization as a gated recurrent process that directly addresses the few-shot learning constraint
2. Competitive results on Mini-ImageNet that outperform natural baselines and match state-of-the-art metric-learning approaches
3. Clear experimental design with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines
4. Practical insights into how meta-learning can improve few-shot performance

The key weaknesses that would need addressing for acceptance include:
1. The authors claim their model is LSTM-based, but the update mechanism more closely resembles a GRU (with gates not constrained to sum to one), which should be clarified
2. The description of meta-set partitioning (Section 2) is unclear and would benefit from a visual explanation
3. The experimental evaluation lacks depth in analyzing the behavior of the input and forget gates (it and ft), which is crucial to understanding the meta-learner's strategy
4. Figure 2 provides limited value and could be improved or removed

Additional constructive feedback:
- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the meta-learner's initialization differs from standard transfer learning approaches
- The claim that "the meta-learner model is competitive with deep metric-learning techniques" needs more precise quantification, particularly for the 1-shot case where confidence intervals overlap
- The preprocessing method (Section 3.2) is described but not thoroughly justified; the authors should explain why this specific transformation works better than alternatives
- The small typo in Section 3.2 ("each coordinate has it" should be "its") should be corrected

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Revising the LSTM vs. GRU discussion to clarify the relationship between the proposed architecture and standard recurrent units
2. Adding a visual diagram illustrating the meta-set partitioning process
3. Including additional analysis of how the input and forget gate values vary across different tasks and training steps
4. Improving Figure 2 or replacing it with a more informative visualization of the meta-learner's behavior

I would like to ask the authors to clarify the following points:
1. The paper claims the meta-learner "learns appropriate parameter updates specifically for the scenario where a set amount of updates will be made." How does the model's performance change if the number of updates during meta-training differs from the number used during meta-testing?
2. In Section 3.3.1, the authors make a gradient independence assumption to avoid second derivatives. How sensitive is the model's performance to this approximation, and what evidence do they have that this assumption doesn't significantly degrade the meta-learner's effectiveness?
3. The paper states that the meta-learner learns a task-common initialization. How does this initialization compare to standard pre-training approaches, and what specific aspects of the initialization make it beneficial for few-shot learning?

The paper presents an important contribution to meta-learning with a novel approach to few-shot learning that is well-motivated and empirically validated. With the suggested revisions to address the clarity issues and deepen the experimental analysis, this work would make a strong contribution to the field. The authors' plan to release code would be a major plus for the community.
