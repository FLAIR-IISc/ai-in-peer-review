

This paper presents an innovative LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn an optimization algorithm for few-shot classification tasks. The core insight—leveraging the structural similarity between LSTM updates and gradient-based optimization—enables the model to simultaneously learn both a task-specific optimization trajectory and a task-agnostic initialization for the learner network. The authors compellingly argue that this dual learning capability directly addresses two critical challenges in few-shot learning: the need for rapid adaptation with minimal examples and the requirement for a strong initialization that generalizes across tasks. The approach is well-motivated, drawing clear connections to prior work on meta-learning while introducing novel technical elements like task-specific gate dynamics and a carefully designed batch normalization strategy for meta-testing.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that a learnable optimization algorithm can outperform standard baselines and achieve competitive results with state-of-the-art metric-learning methods in few-shot settings. Crucially, the authors provide rigorous empirical validation across multiple experimental configurations (1-shot and 5-shot learning on Mini-ImageNet), and their ablation studies effectively highlight the necessity of key design choices like explicit update count matching.

The technical arguments are robust and well-supported. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their meta-learner learns adaptive update rules—evidenced by the gate values in Figure 3—rather than a fixed optimization strategy. This adaptability is critical for handling the varying demands of different few-shot tasks. The experimental design aligns perfectly with few-shot learning conventions (e.g., matching meta-training and meta-testing conditions), and the results consistently show that the meta-learner’s end-to-end training of both initialization and update rules significantly outperforms coarse baselines (e.g., fine-tuning a pre-trained network). The observation that the fine-tuned baseline overfits more severely than the meta-learner in 1-shot settings further underscores the value of the proposed approach.

The paper’s strengths lie in its elegant problem framing and practical implementation details. The reuse of parameter sharing from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) is well-justified, and the preprocessing method for gradient normalization is clearly explained and validated. The batch normalization adaptation for meta-learning is particularly thoughtful—addressing a subtle but critical issue that could otherwise introduce dataset leakage during evaluation. The training procedure (Algorithm 1) is transparent and mirrors the test-time conditions, which is essential for meta-learning success.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following minor revisions:

1. **Clarify input design**: The draft review rightly questions whether combining loss, gradient, and parameters as meta-learner inputs is redundant. The authors should explicitly report ablation studies comparing simpler input combinations (e.g., gradients-only vs. full inputs) to demonstrate the necessity of their design. This would strengthen the technical argument.

2. **Expand architectural flexibility discussion**: The authors note that the meta-learner focuses on parameter updates. A brief discussion of how their framework could extend to learning other architectural choices (e.g., layer depth, activation functions) would provide valuable context for future work. Even a short paragraph on this would be helpful.

3. **Deepen related work**: While the related work section covers key meta-learning advances, it overlooks foundational contributions. Specifically, the authors should cite Bengio’s 1993 PhD thesis (which pioneered learning update rules via gradient descent) and Schmidhuber’s early work on learning optimization algorithms (e.g., *Learning to Learn Using Gradient Descent*, 1992). These are direct predecessors to the current approach and would better position the work within the field’s history.

These suggestions are intended to enhance the paper’s scholarly rigor and historical context—they do not affect the core contribution or experimental validation. The paper is already strong, and these additions would make it even more comprehensive.

To ensure clarity on the meta-learner’s design, I have two questions:
1. The meta-learner uses the current loss $L_t$ as input to the gates. Is this loss value (computed on the entire training set) used to guide the update direction, or is it primarily for adaptive learning rate control? A brief explanation would clarify the mechanism.
2. The paper states that the meta-learner learns a task-common initialization ($c_0$) for the classifier. How does this initialization interact with the task-specific update trajectory? Does the meta-learner learn to reset the classifier parameters to $c_0$ before each task, or does $c_0$ serve as a starting point for fine-tuning?

The answers to these questions would further solidify the understanding of the model’s inner workings without requiring major revisions. Overall, this is an excellent contribution to meta-learning that effectively bridges optimization theory and few-shot learning practice. The work is clearly written, technically sound, and empirically compelling—making it a strong candidate for ICLR 2017.
