- Proposes a sequential meta-learning framework for few-shot learning where a meta-learner processes a sequence of training batches then a test batch.  
- Uses an LSTM to model base-learner parameter updates, drawing an analogy between LSTM memory dynamics and gradient descent.  
- Key innovations: represent base-learner parameters with LSTM second-layer memory cells and share weights across cells (analogous to a uniform learning rate).  
- Provides practical training insights (input normalization, weight sharing, initialization, simulating test conditions) that aid replication.  
- Achieves competitive Mini-ImageNet results: 1-shot 62.8% vs. 60.9% (Matching Networks), 5-shot 74.5% vs. 72.4%.  
- Strength: Conceptual novelty and impact—framing few-shot as sequential meta-learning and the LSTM/gradient-descent analogy are insightful.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results showing a ConvNet classifier can rival embedding-based nearest-neighbor approaches.  
- Strength: Actionable, concrete implementation guidance that lowers the barrier for replication and extension.  
- Weakness: Problem formulation is presented abstractly and needs a concrete example and diagram for accessibility.  
- Weakness: Ambiguity in Mini-ImageNet protocol—unclear whether 64 classes are fixed for meta-training or sampled per batch, affecting base-learner design.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of training curves (Figure 2) and limited discussion of why the method performs well beyond accuracy numbers.  
- Recommendation: Accept (score 8) pending minor revisions to improve clarity—add a task example/diagram, clarify dataset sampling and softmax dimensionality, and improve Figure 2 captions and analysis.
