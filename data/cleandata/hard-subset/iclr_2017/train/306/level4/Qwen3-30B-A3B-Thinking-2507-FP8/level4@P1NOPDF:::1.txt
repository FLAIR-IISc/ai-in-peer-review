

This paper introduces an LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn an optimization algorithm for another learning algorithm (specifically, a neural network). The writing is generally clear and well-structured, with the main contributions presented effectively. The core insight—drawing parallels between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule to satisfy two key requirements of few-shot learning (rapid acquisition of new knowledge and slower consolidation of generalizable knowledge)—is compelling and well-executed.

The authors effectively leverage established techniques like parameter sharing and normalization (from Andrychowicz et al., 2016), while introducing novel design choices such as a specific batch normalization implementation. These elements are well-motivated and contribute to the framework's coherence. The experimental results are convincing and support the paper's claims robustly, making this a strong contribution to the field.

My primary concerns relate to two specific aspects:

1.  **Input Redundancy:** The meta-learner uses loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs. Is this combination necessary, or could simpler combinations (e.g., loss + gradient, or just gradient) suffice? An ablation study exploring the necessity of all three inputs would strengthen the argument for the chosen input design.

2.  **Extending the Meta-Learning Scope:** The framework learns the optimization algorithm. It would be interesting to explore whether other architectural components (e.g., the number of neurons, the type of activation units, or even the network depth) could be similarly learned through meta-optimization. The authors' perspective on the feasibility or challenges of extending this approach to these aspects would be valuable.

3.  **Related Work Depth:** The related work section, while focused on meta-learning, feels somewhat shallow given the long history of the problem. Meta-learning for optimization rules is not new, and several relevant precursors exist that should be acknowledged:
    *   Samy Bengio's 1989 PhD thesis ("Learning to Learn") is fundamentally concerned with this exact problem.
    *   The use of genetic programming to evolve learning rules for neural networks (Bengio, Bengio, & Cloutier, 1994) represents a direct, early approach to the same challenge.
    *   Jürgen Schmidhuber has also explored learning optimization rules; the authors should ensure his relevant work (e.g., on learning to learn, early weight evolution) is included and properly contextualized.

Overall, I find the paper highly relevant and well-executed. The core idea is novel, the technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are significant. The suggestions above are minor points for refinement rather than major weaknesses. The work addresses a core challenge in few-shot learning with a fresh and technically rigorous perspective, making it suitable for a wide audience at ICLR. I recommend acceptance.
