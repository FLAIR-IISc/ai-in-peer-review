

The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner to address few-shot learning challenges by directly learning optimization algorithms for classifier networks. It frames few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning task where the meta-learner processes gradient and loss information from the base learner to produce parameter updates. The key contribution lies in modeling the learner's parameter updates through LSTM cell states, enabling the meta-learner to learn both task-specific update rules and a beneficial initialization for the classifier. The approach demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art metric learning methods on Mini-ImageNet for 1-shot and 5-shot classification.

Accept. The paper presents a novel perspective on few-shot learning through meta-optimized gradient updates, and the experimental results show clear advantages over baselines while matching the performance of Matching Networks. The insight linking LSTM cell updates to gradient descent is technically sound and well-validated.

The authors successfully establish two critical aspects: (1) the LSTM meta-learner's formulation explicitly captures the relationship between gradient-based optimization and LSTM cell updates, with the parametric gates (it and ft) learning adaptive learning rates and forgetting behavior; (2) the method achieves competitive results without requiring metric learning or embedding spaces, demonstrating that meta-learned optimization can effectively handle few-shot classification. The ablation studies confirm that the meta-learner's explicit training for the target number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot) is crucial for performance, and the visualization in Figure 3 provides evidence that the learned update rules vary meaningfully across tasks.

To strengthen the paper, consider the following: (1) Include a concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) to illustrate the sequential meta-learning task in Section 2, which would improve accessibility; (2) Clarify the Mini-ImageNet setup: specify whether the 64/16/20 classes represent disjoint subsets (e.g., 64 classes exclusively for meta-training) or whether all 100 classes are sampled during meta-training (with 64/16/20 used for each meta-set), and state the softmax dimensionality in the base learner (64 vs. 100 classes); (3) Enhance Figure 2 with annotations explaining the computational graph's relevance to the meta-optimization process.

Please clarify: (1) In Section 5.1, the baseline "Baseline-finetune" uses "a fixed number of updates for fine-tuning" – what specific number was used for 1-shot and 5-shot tasks, and how does this compare to the meta-learner's update counts? (2) The paper states that the meta-learner is "explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training" – does this imply the meta-learner's internal update count is fixed per task (e.g., 12 updates for all 1-shot tasks), and if so, how does this affect generalization to unseen tasks with different update requirements?
