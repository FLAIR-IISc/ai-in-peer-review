- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns both task-specific initialization and an optimization/update strategy for few-shot learning, using LSTM cell state as learner parameters and gates to control learning rate and forgetting.
- Demonstrates competitive performance on few-shot classification (Mini-ImageNet), matching state-of-the-art metric-learning approaches.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
- Contribution: novel framing of optimization as a gated recurrent process that directly targets the few-update constraint.
- Theoretical grounding: clear connections to gradient-based optimization principles.
- Strength: competitive results that outperform natural baselines and match metric-learning methods.
- Strength: clear experimental design with appropriate baselines.
- Strength: provides practical insights into how meta-learning can improve few-shot performance.
- Weakness: the claimed LSTM architecture more closely resembles a GRU in practice and needs clarification.
- Weakness: description of meta-set partitioning (Section 2) is unclear and would benefit from a visual explanation.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of input and forget gate behavior (it and ft), limiting understanding of the meta-learner’s strategy.
- Weakness: Figure 2 adds little value and should be improved or removed.
- Suggestion: clarify how the meta-learner’s initialization differs from standard transfer learning approaches.
- Suggestion: provide more precise quantification of competitiveness (especially 1-shot where confidence intervals overlap).
- Suggestion: justify the preprocessing method (Section 3.2) and explain why it outperforms alternatives; correct the minor typo (“each coordinate has it” → “its”).
- Recommended revisions: clarify LSTM vs GRU relationship; add a visual diagram for meta-set partitioning; include analyses of input/forget gate dynamics across tasks and steps; improve or replace Figure 2.
- Clarification requested: how does performance change if the number of updates during meta-training differs from meta-testing?
- Clarification requested: how sensitive is performance to the gradient-independence approximation that avoids second derivatives, and is there evidence this doesn’t degrade effectiveness?
- Clarification requested: how does the learned task-common initialization compare to standard pre-training, and what aspects make it beneficial for few-shot learning?
- Practical note: releasing code would be a major plus for reproducibility and community use.
