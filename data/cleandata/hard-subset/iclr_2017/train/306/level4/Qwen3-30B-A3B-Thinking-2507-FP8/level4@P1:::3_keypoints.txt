- Proposal: Introduces an LSTM-based meta-learner that frames few-shot learning as sequential optimization, learning a parameterized update rule for a classifier.
- Key insight: LSTM cell updates resemble gradient descent, enabling learning of both a task-common initialization and task-specific rapid update rules.
- Empirical result: Shows competitive performance on Mini-ImageNet, particularly strong in the 5-shot setting.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reason for acceptance: Method is compelling, well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective.
- Contribution: Properly frames few-shot learning as meta-learning where the meta-learner optimizes a classifier within a fixed number of updates.
- Problem addressed: Tackles lack of task-specific optimization algorithms and suboptimal random initializations in standard gradient-based fine-tuning.
- Technical execution: Sound implementation with careful handling of parameter sharing, gradient preprocessing, and batch normalization; simplifying assumptions validated.
- Training procedure: Well-defined and follows best practices, matching meta-training and meta-testing conditions.
- Ablations and analysis: Ablation studies validate training for the exact number of updates; gate visualizations provide insight into adaptation strategies.
- Clarity issues: Task description is abstract and would benefit from a concrete example and a diagram illustrating training/testing sequence.
- Experimental clarity needed: Unclear whether ConvNet base learner uses 64 or 100 output classes during meta-training—must be explicitly stated.
- Figure 2 explanation: Computational graph description is minimal and needs a more detailed explanation relating design choices to the meta-learning approach.
- Complexity context: Paper should include a brief comparison of meta-learner parameter counts versus other approaches to contextualize model complexity.
- Recommendations to authors: (1) Add a simple concrete example (e.g., MNIST) and diagram, (2) state number of output classes during meta-training, (3) provide detailed analysis of Figure 2/computational graph, (4) compare parameter counts with other methods.
- Clarification questions for authors: (1) Did you use the same 100 Mini-ImageNet classes as Vinyals et al. or a different selection, and how do you ensure comparability? (2) How is the task-common initialization applied at meta-test time—global start for all tasks or task-specific? (3) Why do your results differ from Vinyals et al.—dataset differences, implementation details, or other factors?
- Overall conclusion: Strong, significant contribution to few-shot learning with minor presentation clarifications needed for publication.
