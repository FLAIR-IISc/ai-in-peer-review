

This paper introduces an LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn optimization algorithms for neural networks, presenting a compelling connection between the Robbins-Monro update rule and LSTM dynamics to address key challenges in few-shot learning: rapid adaptation to new tasks and gradual extraction of transferable knowledge. The core insight is well-motivated and elegantly executed, with the authors successfully leveraging this parallel to satisfy both desiderata. The presentation is clear, the writing is strong, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the approach's effectiveness. The reuse of established strategies (e.g., parameter sharing and normalization from Andrychowicz et al., 2016) is well-justified, and the novel implementation of batch normalization adds meaningful value.

The work is broadly relevant to the ICLR audience and represents a significant contribution to meta-learning. However, I have three specific concerns that warrant clarification before final acceptance:

1.  **Input Redundancy:** The meta-learner utilizes loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs. While the rationale for this combination is plausible, it is unclear whether this specific input set is strictly necessary. Ablation studies comparing this full input to simpler combinations (e.g., loss + gradient, or gradient alone) would strengthen the argument and help determine if the input design is optimal or potentially redundant. The authors should address whether a more minimal input representation suffices.

2.  **Extending to Architectural Learning:** The paper focuses on learning the *optimization rule*. It would be highly valuable to explore whether the meta-learning framework could be extended to learn *architectural components* of the base network (e.g., number of neurons, layer types, activation functions) in a similar fashion. The authors should discuss the feasibility and potential challenges of such an extension, as this would significantly broaden the scope and impact of their approach.

3.  **Related Work Depth:** The related work section, while covering recent meta-learning advances, is insufficiently comprehensive for a topic with deep historical roots. Key foundational work addressing the *same core problem* (learning learning rules) predates modern meta-learning and should be acknowledged:
    *   Yoshua Bengio’s 1989 PhD thesis ("Learning to Learn with Backpropagation") explicitly explored learning optimization rules for neural networks using recurrent structures.
    *   The 1994 work by S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier ("A genetic algorithm for optimizing the learning rate of a neural network") used evolutionary methods to learn learning rules, a direct precursor.
    *   Jürgen Schmidhuber’s early work on "Learning to Learn" (e.g., *Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent*, 1992) is highly relevant and must be cited to properly contextualize the paper’s contribution within the broader history of the field. The authors should substantially revise the related work to include these seminal references.

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed paper with a novel and insightful core contribution. The experimental validation is robust, and the potential impact on meta-learning is clear. Addressing the three points above—particularly providing ablation results for the input design, discussing the extension to architectural learning, and significantly expanding the related work to include foundational literature—would solidify the paper’s position and ensure its contribution is fully contextualized. I find the material highly relevant to ICLR and recommend acceptance, provided these minor revisions are incorporated.
