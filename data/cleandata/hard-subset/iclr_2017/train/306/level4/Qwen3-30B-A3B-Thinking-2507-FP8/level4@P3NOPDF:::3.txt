

This paper proposes a novel sequential meta-learning framework for few-shot learning, casting the problem as a task where the meta-learner processes a sequence of training batches followed by a test batch. The authors leverage an LSTM to model the base learner's parameter updates, drawing a compelling analogy between LSTM memory cell dynamics and gradient descent. Key innovations include using the LSTM's second-layer memory cells to directly represent the base learner's parameters, sharing weights across cells (analogous to a uniform learning rate), and deriving practical training insights (e.g., input normalization, weight sharing). The approach achieves competitive results on Mini-ImageNet (1-shot: 62.8% vs. 60.9% for Matching Networks; 5-shot: 74.5% vs. 72.4%), demonstrating that a simple ConvNet base learner can rival the embedding-based nearest-neighbor approach of Vinyals et al. (2016).  

**Strengths:**  
1. **Conceptual Novelty & Impact:** Framing few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is insightful and well-motivated. The LSTM-memory/gradient descent analogy is a fresh perspective, and the work advances the state of the art beyond incremental improvements.  
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The method outperforms Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet in both 1-shot and 5-shot settings, validating the approach’s effectiveness. Crucially, it uses a distinct architecture (ConvNet classifier) rather than building directly on prior embedding-based methods.  
3. **Actionable Practical Guidance:** The paper provides valuable, concrete advice for implementing LSTM meta-learners—e.g., proper initialization, weight sharing, input normalization, and simulating test conditions during meta-training. These insights significantly lower the barrier for replication and extension.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Clarity of Problem Formulation:** The core meta-learning task (Section 2) is described abstractly using notation unfamiliar to general ML audiences. A brief, concrete example (e.g., "For a 5-way 1-shot task on MNIST, the sequence comprises 5 batches of 1 training example each, followed by a test batch") paired with a simple diagram would greatly improve accessibility. Without this, the paper risks being misinterpreted or inaccessible to non-specialists.  
2. **Ambiguous Dataset Setup:** The Mini-ImageNet protocol requires clarification. The paper states 64/16/20 classes are used for meta-training/validation/testing. However, it is unclear whether:  
   - Only 64 classes are observed *during meta-training* (with the base learner’s softmax outputting 64 classes), or  
   - 64 classes are sampled *per meta-training batch* (with the softmax outputting 100 classes, but 36 never appearing in training).  
   This ambiguity affects the interpretation of the base learner’s architecture and generalization.  
3. **Insufficient Analysis of Results:** Figure 2 (training curves) lacks explanatory captions and fails to highlight key trends (e.g., convergence speed, stability). The discussion offers minimal insight into *why* the method performs well or how it compares to alternatives beyond accuracy numbers.  

**Recommendation:**  
This is a technically sound and impactful contribution with compelling results. The core idea is novel, the implementation is robust, and the practical insights are valuable for the community. However, the current presentation significantly hinders comprehension and reproducibility. The weaknesses are primarily presentational—not conceptual—and can be addressed with targeted revisions:  
- Add a concrete task example and diagram (e.g., in Section 2).  
- Clarify the Mini-ImageNet class sampling protocol and base learner output dimensionality.  
- Revise Figure 2 with clear captions and add a brief analysis of the curves.  

Given the authors’ responsiveness to prior feedback and the paper’s strong technical merit, I maintain my updated score of **8** (Accept). With the proposed clarifications, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017. I strongly recommend acceptance pending these minor revisions.
