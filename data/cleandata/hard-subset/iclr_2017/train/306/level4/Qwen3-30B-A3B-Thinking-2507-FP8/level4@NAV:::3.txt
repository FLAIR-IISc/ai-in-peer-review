

In light of the authors' responsiveness and the manuscript updates—particularly the clarification of the meta-learning task—I am updating my score to an 8.  

This manuscript addresses few-shot learning via neural networks using meta-learning, a well-established concept experiencing renewed interest. The authors frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each "example" comprises a sequence of training batches followed by a test batch. Inputs at each step incorporate the base learner's outputs (e.g., training loss and gradients) and its current state (parameters). An LSTM is applied to this framework, with second-layer memory cells directly modeling the base learner's updated parameters. The authors highlight parallels between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent. Meta-learner updates are derived from the base learner's prediction loss on the test batch. Key simplifications include weight sharing across second-layer cells (analogous to a uniform learning rate). The approach recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset (Vinyals et al., 2016) and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art methods (Matching Networks, Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.  

Strengths:  
- Framing few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is insightful and, in retrospect, natural. While the core concept of sequential learning is not novel, the authors advance the state of the art, though I cannot fully assess novelty given my familiarity with recent meta-learning literature.  
- The method is competitive and outperforms Vinyals et al. (2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet.  
- The base learner (a simple ConvNet classifier) differs significantly from Vinyals’ nearest-neighbor approach, demonstrating state-of-the-art results via a distinct methodology rather than incremental improvements.  
- The insight linking LSTM memory cells to gradient descent updates appears novel and is compelling.  
- The paper provides valuable practical guidance for implementing LSTM meta-learners (e.g., initialization, weight sharing, input normalization), enhancing reproducibility and applicability. While some insights overlap with prior work (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training), the discussion is useful.  

Weaknesses:  
- The writing lacks clarity in places, making the paper difficult to follow. The abstract task description in Section 2 (Page 2) uses non-standard notation and language, requiring multiple readings and reference to related work for comprehension. A concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) with a diagram illustrating the sequential learning process would greatly improve accessibility.  
- The precise definition of the few-shot learning problem is ambiguous. For Mini-ImageNet (100 classes, 64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing), it is unclear whether only 64 classes are observed during meta-training (implying a 64-output softmax) or if all 100 classes are covered over time (implying a 100-output softmax). Other details remain unclear (see specific questions).  
- Figure 2’s plots lack informative content, and the accompanying discussion offers minimal insight.  

This is an interesting paper with strong empirical results. While the technical contributions warrant acceptance, the presentation requires significant improvement. I will raise my score further if the writing and clarity issues are addressed.
