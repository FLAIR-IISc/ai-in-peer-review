

The paper presents a novel LSTM-based meta-learner framework that learns the optimization algorithm for a classifier neural network in few-shot learning scenarios. The core insight—drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update mechanism—effectively addresses two critical requirements for few-shot learning: rapid adaptation to new tasks and the extraction of transferable knowledge across tasks. The authors successfully leverage this insight to develop a meta-learner that learns both optimal parameter updates for a specific number of training steps and a beneficial initialization for the classifier network. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings and builds upon prior work with thoughtful modifications, particularly in the implementation of batch normalization for meta-learning.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach that effectively tackles the few-shot learning problem, and (2) The experimental results demonstrate strong performance that is competitive with state-of-the-art metric learning methods.

The supporting arguments for acceptance include the paper's clear theoretical foundation, the well-executed implementation of the meta-learner, and the convincing experimental validation. The authors effectively demonstrate that their method outperforms natural baselines and achieves competitive results with Matching Networks, particularly for the 5-shot setting. The paper's structure is logical, the writing is clear, and the technical content is presented with appropriate rigor.

For further improvement, I suggest addressing the following points: First, while the paper uses loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner, it would be valuable to see ablative studies confirming that these specific inputs are necessary, or that simpler combinations would not suffice. Second, it would be interesting to explore whether other architectural components (such as the number of neurons or type of units) could be learned in a similar fashion through meta-learning. Finally, the related work section would benefit from more comprehensive coverage of earlier meta-learning approaches; specifically, the paper should acknowledge Samy Bengio's 1989 PhD thesis (which focuses on learning learning rules), the 1994 work on genetic programming for learning rule search by Bengio et al., and relevant contributions by Schmidhuber that predate the current LSTM-based meta-learning approaches. These additions would provide better historical context for the current work.
