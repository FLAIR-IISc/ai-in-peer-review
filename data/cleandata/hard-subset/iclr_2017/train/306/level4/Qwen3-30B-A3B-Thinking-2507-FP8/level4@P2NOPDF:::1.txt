

This paper introduces an LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn optimization algorithms for neural networks. The writing is clear and well-structured, with the core contribution—establishing a conceptual parallel between the Robbins-Monro stochastic approximation framework and the LSTM update mechanism—being both novel and compelling. This insight effectively addresses two key requirements for few-shot learning: rapid adaptation to new tasks and gradual extraction of transferable knowledge. The authors thoughtfully incorporate established techniques (e.g., parameter sharing and normalization from Andrychowicz et al., 2016) while introducing well-motivated innovations, particularly in the implementation of batch normalization. The experimental results are robust and convincingly demonstrate the approach’s effectiveness, making this a strong contribution to the field.

My primary concerns are limited to three specific points:

1.  **Input Redundancy:** The meta-learner utilizes loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs. Given that gradients inherently encode loss and parameter changes, it is worth investigating whether simpler combinations (e.g., gradients alone, or gradients plus parameters) suffice. Ablation studies comparing input configurations would strengthen the justification for the chosen input design.

2.  **Extending Learnable Architectural Components:** The framework learns the optimization *algorithm*. It would be valuable to explore whether other architectural elements could similarly be learned (e.g., layer sizes, activation functions, or network depth). The authors’ perspective on the feasibility and potential benefits of such extensions would be insightful.

3.  **Related Work Depth:** While the related work section focuses appropriately on meta-learning, it overlooks significant historical context. Meta-learning for optimization is not a recent phenomenon. Key prior work deserving citation includes:
    *   Samy Bengio's 1989 PhD thesis, which extensively explored learning learning rules.
    *   The 1994 work by Bengio, Bengio, and Cloutier on using genetic programming to evolve learning rules.
    *   Early meta-learning contributions by Schmidhuber (e.g., on learning to learn via recurrent networks), which should be explicitly acknowledged and compared. Ensuring these foundational works are properly integrated into the related work section is essential for a comprehensive literature review.

Overall, this is a well-executed, high-quality paper with a clear, novel technical insight. The approach is relevant to a broad ICLR audience, and the experimental validation is sound. The concerns raised are minor and do not detract from the paper's overall strength. I recommend acceptance.
