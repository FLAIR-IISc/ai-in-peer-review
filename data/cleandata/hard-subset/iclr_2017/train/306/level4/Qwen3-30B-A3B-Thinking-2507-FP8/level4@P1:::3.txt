

This paper proposes a novel meta-learning approach for few-shot learning tasks by framing the problem as sequential optimization. The authors introduce an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a classifier network through a parameterized update rule inspired by gradient descent. Their key insight is that the LSTM cell state update mechanism resembles gradient-based optimization, allowing the meta-learner to simultaneously learn both an optimal initialization for the classifier and a task-specific update rule that converges rapidly after only a few training examples. The authors demonstrate competitive performance against state-of-the-art metric learning methods on Mini-ImageNet, achieving particularly strong results in the 5-shot setting.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons: The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning that is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective. The method's ability to learn both a good initialization and an optimal update rule for the classifier represents a significant advance over standard fine-tuning approaches, as evidenced by the strong experimental results.

The paper makes a strong contribution by properly framing few-shot learning as a meta-learning problem where the meta-learner learns to optimize a classifier within a fixed number of updates. The authors effectively address two key limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot scenarios: the lack of task-specific optimization algorithms and the suboptimal random initialization of classifiers. Their LSTM meta-learner successfully learns both a task-common initialization and an update rule that adapts to the specific constraints of few-shot learning, as shown in their experiments.

The technical execution is sound, with careful attention to important implementation details such as parameter sharing, gradient preprocessing, and batch normalization handling. The authors make appropriate simplifying assumptions (e.g., gradient independence) that are standard in this literature, and they validate that these do not significantly impact performance. The training procedure (Algorithm 1) is well-defined and follows best practices for meta-learning, particularly the critical step of matching meta-training and meta-testing conditions.

The experimental results are convincing, with the proposed method outperforming both naive baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) and achieving competitive results with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet. The authors provide appropriate ablation studies showing that explicitly training the meta-learner for the exact number of updates used during testing leads to better performance. The visualization of learned gate values (Figure 3) offers interesting insights into how the meta-learner adapts its optimization strategy across different tasks.

The paper would benefit from several improvements to enhance clarity and accessibility. First, the task description (Section 2) is quite abstract and would be significantly improved with a concrete example, perhaps using MNIST as a simple illustration, along with a diagram showing the sequence of training and test examples. This would help readers unfamiliar with the meta-learning literature better understand the problem setup.

Second, the paper's description of how Mini-ImageNet is used needs clarification. Specifically, it's unclear whether the ConvNet base learner has 64 or 100 output classes during meta-training. The authors should explicitly state whether the classifier is trained to recognize only the 64 classes seen during meta-training or all 100 classes (with some never observed during training). This distinction is critical for understanding the experimental setup.

Third, the discussion of Figure 2 could be strengthened. The current description of the computational graph is minimal and doesn't provide insights into why the architecture was designed this way or how it relates to the meta-learning approach. A more detailed explanation would help readers understand the model's structure.

To help the authors further strengthen their paper, I recommend they:
1. Add a concrete example of the few-shot learning task with a simple dataset to clarify the problem formulation
2. Explicitly state the number of output classes for the base learner during meta-training
3. Provide more detailed analysis of the computational graph in Figure 2
4. Include a brief comparison of the number of parameters in their meta-learner versus other approaches to contextualize the model's complexity

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to better understand the paper's contributions:
1. When the authors create their Mini-ImageNet dataset, do they use the same 100 classes as Vinyals et al. (2016), or do they select a new set of 100 classes? If new, how do they ensure this doesn't affect the comparison with previous work?
2. The paper mentions that the meta-learner learns a "task-common initialization" - how is this initialization used during meta-testing? Does it serve as the starting point for all tasks, or is it specific to each task?
3. The authors state that their approach is competitive with Matching Networks but does not match the exact numbers from Vinyals et al. (2016). Could they clarify whether this discrepancy is due to dataset differences, implementation details, or other factors?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the few-shot learning literature. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. With minor clarifications to the presentation, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
