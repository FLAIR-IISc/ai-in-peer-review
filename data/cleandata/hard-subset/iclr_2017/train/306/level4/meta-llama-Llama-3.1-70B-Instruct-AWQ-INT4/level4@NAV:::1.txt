This work proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework for learning the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm, specifically a neural network. The paper is well-structured and effectively presents its core ideas. The key contribution of the paper - establishing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule to satisfy the primary objectives of few-shot learning, namely rapid acquisition of new knowledge and slower extraction of generalizable knowledge - is particularly compelling.

The incorporation of various techniques from existing literature, such as parameter sharing and normalization (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), along with novel design choices, including the specific implementation of batch normalization, are well-justified. The experimental results are convincing, making this a strong paper.

However, I have a few concerns and questions that merit consideration:

1. Is it necessary to utilize loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner? Were ablation studies conducted to determine whether simpler combinations could achieve comparable results?
2. It would be interesting to explore the possibility of learning other architectural components of the network in a similar manner, such as the number of neurons or types of units. Do the authors have any thoughts on this?
3. The related work section, primarily focused on meta-learning, could be more comprehensive. Meta-learning has a rich history, and similar approaches have been employed to address the same problem, even if they did not involve LSTMs. Notably, the authors may want to consider citing:
     - Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989), which extensively explores this topic.
     - The use of genetic programming for searching new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
     - The authors may also want to investigate and include relevant work by Schmidhuber.

Overall, I find the paper engaging, and the discussed material is relevant to a broad audience at ICLR.
