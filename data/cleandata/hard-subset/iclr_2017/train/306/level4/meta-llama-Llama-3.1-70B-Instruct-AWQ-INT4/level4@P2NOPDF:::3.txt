This manuscript proposes a novel approach to few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a classic idea that has gained significant attention in the last 12 months. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each "example" consists of a sequence of batches of "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. The inputs at each "step" include the outputs of a "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradients) and the base learner's current state (parameters). By applying an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, the authors directly model the updated parameters of the base learner using the inner memory cells in the second layer. They note similarities between the respective update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is updated based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final "test" batch.

The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second layer cells (analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters). The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet data set proposed in Vinyals et al 2016 and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks, Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning tasks.

Strengths:

* The authors' approach to casting the few-shot learning problem as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is intriguing and natural. While they did not originate the general idea of persisting learning across a series of learning problems, they have advanced the state of the art.
* The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals 2016 in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
* The base learner in this setting (simple ConvNet classifier) is distinct from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach used in Vinyals 2016, making the state-of-the-art results more exciting.
* The insight about the relationship between the memory cell and gradient descent updates is novel and interesting.
* The paper offers practical insights about designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, including proper initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. These insights will facilitate replication and application of these ideas to new problems.

Weaknesses:

* The writing is often opaque, making the paper difficult to understand. A brief concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) paired with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks would improve accessibility.
* The precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is unclear. Specifically, it is unclear whether the Mini-ImageNet data set's 100 labels are divided into 64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing, and whether the ConvNet base learner's softmax layer has 64 or 100 outputs during meta-training.
* The plots in Figure 2 are uninformative, and the discussion section offers little insight around them.

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. While the presentation of the ideas and work could be improved, it seems like a clear accept. I will consider raising my score if the writing is improved.
