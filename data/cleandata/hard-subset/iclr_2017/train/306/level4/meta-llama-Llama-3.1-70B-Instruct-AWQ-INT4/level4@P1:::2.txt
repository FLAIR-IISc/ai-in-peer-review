This paper describes a new approach to meta-learning by interpreting the SGD update rule as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. The idea is original and important for research related to transfer learning. The paper has a clear structure, but clarity could be improved at some points.

The authors tackle the specific problem of few-shot learning, where a classifier must quickly generalize after seeing very few examples from each class. They propose an LSTM-based meta-learner model to learn the exact optimization algorithm used to train another learner neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the limitations of traditional gradient-based optimization methods in the few-shot learning setting.

I decide to accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to meta-learning, which is well-motivated and clearly explained. Secondly, the authors provide competitive results and a proper comparison to state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

The paper's main strength lies in its ability to learn a beneficial common initialization for the learner network, which allows for quick convergence of training. The authors also provide a good analysis of the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, which helps to understand how the model works.

However, there are some points that could be improved. The analogy between the SGD update rule and the LSTM model would be closer to GRUs than LSTMs. The description of the data separation in meta-sets is hard to follow and could be visualized to improve clarity. Additionally, the experimental evaluation is only partly satisfying, and it would be interesting to see a more detailed analysis of the effect of the parameters of it and ft.

Some minor remarks include a small typo in Section 3.2, where "This means each coordinate has it" should be "its". Figure 2 does not add much value to the paper and could be removed. However, the authors' plan to release the code used in their evaluation experiments is a major plus.

To improve the paper, the authors could consider providing more details on the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, such as the effect of the parameters of it and ft. They could also provide more visualizations to help illustrate the data separation in meta-sets and the optimization process. Additionally, a more detailed comparison to other meta-learning methods would be helpful to understand the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, such as the effect of the parameters of it and ft?
* How do you plan to extend this approach to more challenging scenarios, such as few-shot learning with many classes?
* Can you provide more visualizations to help illustrate the data separation in meta-sets and the optimization process?
* How does the proposed approach compare to other meta-learning methods, such as those based on memory-augmented neural networks or gradient-based hyperparameter optimization?
