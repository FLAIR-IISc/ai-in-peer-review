- Paper frames few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem where each example is a sequence of training batches followed by a test batch.  
- Uses an LSTM meta-learner that ingests base-learner outputs (loss, gradients) and base-learner parameters, with inner cells modeling parameter updates.  
- Notes a novel insight: similarity between LSTM memory-cell updates and gradient-descent updates.  
- Meta-learner updates are computed from the base learner’s loss on the final test batch.  
- Simplifying assumptions include weight sharing across second-layer cells (analogous to a shared learning rate).  
- Recreates Mini-ImageNet and shows the LSTM meta-learner is competitive with or outperforms Matching Networks on 1- and 5-shot tasks.  
- Strength: Casting few-shot as sequential/meta-learning is an intriguing and timely perspective that advances the state of the art.  
- Strength: Competitive empirical results using a different base learner (ConvNet) than prior embedding-based methods.  
- Strength: The memory cell vs. gradient-descent relationship appears novel and interesting.  
- Strength: Paper provides practical design/training insights (initialization, weight sharing, normalization) useful for replication.  
- Weakness: Writing is sometimes opaque and the abstract task description is hard to follow; a concrete example/diagram would help accessibility.  
- Weakness: The exact setup of the N-class few-shot problem (which classes are seen during meta-training and softmax output dimensionality) is unclear.  
- Weakness: Figure 2 plots are uninformative and the discussion around them lacks insight.  
- Decision: Accept — innovative approach with competitive results; strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Additional feedback: add a concrete illustrative example, clarify N-class/meta-training details and softmax outputs, improve Figure 2, and expand discussion of memory-cell/gradient-descent implications.  
- Questions for authors: clarify number of classes observed during meta-training and softmax size; how to scale to many-class few-shot problems; elaborate on the memory-cell vs. gradient-descent relationship and its implications.
