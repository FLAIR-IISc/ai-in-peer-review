In light of the authors' responsiveness and the updates to the manuscript, particularly in clarifying the meta-learning task, I am revising my score to an 8.

This manuscript tackles few-shot learning with neural networks by utilizing meta-learning, a concept that has seen significant resurgence in the past year. The authors reformulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each example comprises a sequence of batches of training pairs followed by a final test batch. The inputs at each step include the outputs of a base learner (e.g., training loss and gradients) and the base learner's current state (parameters). The authors apply a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network to this meta-learning problem, leveraging the inner memory cells in the second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. They observe similarities between the update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner updates are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final test batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells (analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters). The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet data set proposed in Vinyals et al. (2016) and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks, Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

Strengths:
- The authors' approach of casting the few-shot learning problem as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is intriguing and natural. Although the authors did not originate the general idea of persisting learning across a series of learning problems, they have advanced the state of the art. However, I am not deeply familiar with recent work on meta-learning, so I cannot confidently assert its novelty.
- The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals (2016) in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner in this setting (simple ConvNet classifier) differs significantly from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach used in Vinyals (2016). It is exciting to see state-of-the-art results achieved using distinct approaches rather than incremental follow-up work.
- The insight about the relationship between the memory cell and gradient descent updates appears to be novel and is interesting in its own right.
- The paper provides practical insights on designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, which will facilitate replication and application of these ideas to new problems. These insights include proper initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. While some insights have been previously described (simulating test conditions during meta-training and assuming independence between meta-learner and base learner parameters when taking gradients with respect to the meta-learner parameters), the discussion here is still valuable.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is often opaque, making the paper challenging to follow. Although it describes interesting work, it is not an enjoyable read. Multiple passes (and consulting related work) were necessary to understand the general learning problem. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is abstract and uses notation and language not common outside this sub-area. A brief concrete example (based on MNIST is fine), paired with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks, would make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
- The precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is unclear. Specifically, the Mini-ImageNet data set has 100 labels, of which 64/16/20 are used during meta-training/validation/testing. It is unclear whether only 64/100 classes are observed through meta-training or whether only 64/100 are observed in each batch, but all 100 are observed on average during meta-training. If it is the former, how many outputs does the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner have during meta-training? 64 (only those observed in training) or 100 (of which 36 are never observed)? Many other details like these are unclear.
- The plots in Figure 2 are not informative on their own, and the discussion section offers little insight around them.

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. While the presentation of the ideas and work could be improved, it seems like a clear accept. I will consider raising my score if the writing is improved.
