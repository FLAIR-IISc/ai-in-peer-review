This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework for learning the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm, specifically a neural network. The paper is well-written, and the main ideas are clearly presented. The core contribution of the paper, drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM update rule, and exploiting this connection to satisfy the two primary desiderata of few-shot learning – rapid acquisition of new knowledge and slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge – is intriguing.

The authors effectively reuse several techniques from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, and introduce novel design choices, including a specific implementation of batch normalization, which are well-motivated. The experiments are convincing, and overall, this is a strong paper. However, I have a few concerns and questions:

1. Is it necessary to use the loss, gradient, and parameters as inputs to the meta-learner? Did you conduct ablation studies to determine whether simpler combinations are sufficient?

2. It would be beneficial to explore whether other architectural components of the network, such as the number of neurons or type of units, can be learned in a similar fashion. Do you have any thoughts on this?

3. The related work section, which primarily focuses on meta-learning, could be more comprehensive. Meta-learning is a well-established topic, and similar approaches have been proposed to address the same problem, albeit without using LSTMs. For example:
    - Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989) is particularly relevant to this work.
    - The use of genetic programming for searching new learning rules for neural networks, as proposed by S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier (1994), is also worth mentioning.
    - I am convinced that Schmidhuber has made relevant contributions to this area, and I recommend updating the related work section to include his work.

Overall, I like the paper and believe the discussed material is relevant to a wide audience at ICLR.
