**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel approach to tackle few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each "example" includes a sequence of batches of "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. They apply an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, using the inner memory cells in the second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. The paper shows that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks, Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend accepting this paper, with a score of 8. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper proposes a novel and intriguing approach to few-shot learning, casting it as a sequential meta-learning problem.
2. The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms the current state-of-the-art on 1- and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths include:

* The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms the current state-of-the-art on 1- and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
* The base learner in this setting (simple ConvNet classifier) is quite different from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach used in Vinyals 2016.
* The paper offers several practical insights about how to design and train an LSTM meta-learner, which should make it easier for others to replicate this work and apply these ideas to new problems.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper's weaknesses include:

* The writing is at times quite opaque, making it difficult to understand the general learning problem.
* The precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is unclear, and several details are not well-explained.
* The plots in Figure 2 are pretty uninformative in and of themselves, and the discussion section offers very little insight around them.

To improve the paper, I suggest:

* Adding a brief concrete example (based on MNIST is fine), perhaps paired with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks, to make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
* Clarifying the precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem, including details such as the number of outputs in the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner during meta-training.
* Improving the plots in Figure 2 and adding more insight around them in the discussion section.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you clarify the precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem, including details such as the number of outputs in the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner during meta-training?
* How do you plan to extend this work to more challenging scenarios, such as few-shot learning with many classes or few-shot learning with limited labeled data?
* Can you provide more insight into the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, and how it adapts to different few-shot learning tasks?
