The authors propose a novel approach to tackle few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a classic idea that has seen a renaissance in the last 12 months. They formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each "example" includes a sequence of batches of "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. The inputs at each "step" include the outputs of a "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradients), as well as the base learner's current state (parameters). The paper applies an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, using the inner memory cells in the second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner.

Strengths:
- The approach is intriguing and in hindsight, natural, to cast the few-shot learning problem as a sequential (meta-)learning problem.
- The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals 2016 in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner in this setting (simple ConvNet classifier) is quite different from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach used in Vinyals 2016.
- The insight about the relationship between the memory cell and gradient descent updates is interesting and novel.
- The paper offers practical insights about how to design and train an LSTM meta-learner, which should make it easier for others to replicate this work and apply these ideas to new problems.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is at times quite opaque and could benefit from a brief concrete example to illustrate the learning problem.
- The precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is unclear and requires further clarification.
- The plots in Figure 2 are pretty uninformative in and of themselves, and the discussion section offers very little insight around them.

Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

Supporting arguments for the decision:
The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals 2016 in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments, demonstrating its effectiveness in few-shot learning tasks. The paper offers practical insights about how to design and train an LSTM meta-learner, which should make it easier for others to replicate this work and apply these ideas to new problems. However, the writing is at times quite opaque, and the precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem requires further clarification.

Additional feedback:
To improve the paper, the authors could consider adding a brief concrete example to illustrate the learning problem, clarifying the precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem, and providing more informative plots and discussion around them.

Questions to the authors:
- Could you provide a brief concrete example to illustrate the learning problem and make it more accessible to a wider audience?
- Could you clarify the precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem, specifically how many outputs the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner has during meta-training?
- Could you provide more informative plots and discussion around them to help understand the results better?
