This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm, specifically a neural network. The paper is well-written, and the presentation of the main material is clear.

The core idea of drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM update rule, and exploiting this connection to satisfy the two primary desiderata of few-shot learning - rapid acquisition of new knowledge and slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge - is intriguing.

Several techniques reused from (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, as well as novel design choices, including the specific implementation of batch normalization, are well-motivated. The experiments are convincing, making this a strong paper.

However, I have a few concerns and questions:

1. Is it redundant to use the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner? Did you conduct ablative studies to ensure that simpler combinations are not sufficient?
2. It would be beneficial to explore whether other architectural components of the network can be learned in a similar fashion, such as the number of neurons or type of units. Do you have any opinions on this?
3. The related work section, primarily focused on meta-learning, is somewhat shallow. Meta-learning is a relatively old topic, and similar approaches have been attempted to solve the same problem, albeit not using LSTMs. Notably, Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989) and the work by S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier (1994) on using genetic programming for the search of new learning rules for neural networks are relevant. I am also convinced that Schmidhuber has made contributions to this area, and I suggest updating the related work section accordingly.

Overall, I like the paper and believe the discussed material is relevant to a wide audience at ICLR.
