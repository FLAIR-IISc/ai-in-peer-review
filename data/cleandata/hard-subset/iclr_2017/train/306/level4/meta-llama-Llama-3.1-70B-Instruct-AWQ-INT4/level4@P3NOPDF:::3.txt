I am updating my score to an 8 in light of the authors' responsiveness and the updates to the manuscript, particularly in clarifying the meta-learning task.

This manuscript proposes to tackle few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a classic idea that has seen a resurgence in the last 12 months. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each "example" consists of a sequence of batches of "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. The inputs at each "step" include the outputs of a "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradients), as well as the base learner's current state (parameters). The paper applies a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network to this meta-learning problem, utilizing the inner memory cells in the second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. This approach is motivated by the similarities between the respective update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. Updates to the LSTM meta-learner are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final "test" batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells (analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters). The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset proposed in Vinyals et al. (2016) and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks, Vinyals, 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning tasks.

Strengths:

* The authors' formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is intriguing and, in hindsight, natural. While they did not originate the general idea of persisting learning across a series of learning problems, they have advanced the state of the art. Although I am not deeply familiar with recent work on meta-learning, I believe their contribution is significant.
* The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals (2016) in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
* The base learner in this setting (a simple ConvNet classifier) is distinct from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach used in Vinyals (2016). It is encouraging to see state-of-the-art results achieved using different approaches, rather than incremental follow-up work.
* The insight about the relationship between the memory cell and gradient descent updates appears to be novel and is interesting regardless.
* The paper offers practical insights on designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, which should facilitate replication and application of these ideas to new problems. These insights include proper initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. While some of these insights have been previously described, the discussion here is useful nonetheless.

Weaknesses:

* The writing is sometimes opaque, making the paper a challenging read. It took me multiple passes (as well as consulting related work) to understand the general learning problem. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is abstract and uses notation and language that is not common outside of this sub-area. A brief concrete example (e.g., based on MNIST) paired with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks would make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
* The precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is unclear. Specifically, the Mini-ImageNet dataset has 100 labels, of which 64/16/20 are used during meta-training/validation/testing. It is unclear whether only 64/100 classes are observed through meta-training or whether all 100 classes are observed on average during meta-training. If it is the former, the number of outputs in the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner during meta-training is also unclear (64 or 100). Many other details, such as these, are unclear.
* The plots in Figure 2 are not informative on their own, and the discussion section offers limited insight around them.

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. While the presentation of the ideas and work could be improved, it seems like a clear accept. I would raise my score if the writing is improved.
