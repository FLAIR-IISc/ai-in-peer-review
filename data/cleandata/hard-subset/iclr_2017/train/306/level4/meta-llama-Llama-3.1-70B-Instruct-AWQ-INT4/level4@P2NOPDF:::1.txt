This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm, specifically a neural network. The paper is well-written, and the presentation of the main material is clear.

The core idea of drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM update rule to satisfy the two primary desiderata of few-shot learning - quick acquisition of new knowledge and slower extraction of general transferable knowledge - is intriguing. The authors effectively reuse several tricks from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, and introduce novel design choices, including a specific implementation of batch normalization, which are well-motivated.

The experiments are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed framework. Overall, this is a strong paper. However, I have a few concerns and questions that I believe the authors should address:

1. Using the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner may be redundant. Did the authors conduct ablative studies to ensure that simpler combinations of these inputs are not sufficient? It would be beneficial to provide a more in-depth analysis of the input components to the meta-learner.

2. It would be interesting to explore whether other architectural components of the network can be learned in a similar fashion, such as the number of neurons or the type of units. The authors may want to provide their opinion on this aspect and discuss potential future research directions.

3. The related work section, primarily focused on meta-learning, could be more comprehensive. Meta-learning is a well-established topic, and similar approaches have been proposed to address the same problem, albeit not using LSTMs. Notably, Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989) and the work by S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier (1994) on using genetic programming for the search of new learning rules for neural networks are relevant examples. Additionally, the authors should ensure that they acknowledge any relevant contributions by Schmidhuber.

Overall, I like the paper and believe that the discussed material is relevant to a wide audience at ICLR.
