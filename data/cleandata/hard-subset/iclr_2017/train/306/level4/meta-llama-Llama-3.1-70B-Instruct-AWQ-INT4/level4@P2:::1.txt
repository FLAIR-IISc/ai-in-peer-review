This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm (here a neural network). The paper is globally well-written, and the presentation of the main material is clear. The crux of the paper - drawing the parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule and exploiting it to satisfy the two main desiderata of few-shot learning (1- quick acquisition of new knowledge, 2- slower extraction of general transferable knowledge) - is intriguing.

Several tricks reused from (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, and novel design choices (specific implementation of batch normalization) are well-motivated. The experiments are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons for this choice:

1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning using an LSTM-based meta-learning framework.
2. The experiments are convincing, showing the effectiveness of the proposed approach in comparison to other methods.

Supporting arguments for the reasons for the decision:

1. The paper provides a clear and well-written presentation of the main material, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and its contributions.
2. The use of an LSTM-based meta-learning framework is well-motivated, and the parallel drawn between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule is intriguing.
3. The experiments are well-designed and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in comparison to other methods.

Additional feedback:

1. It would be interesting to explore whether simpler combinations of inputs to the meta-learner (e.g., using only the gradient and parameters) are sufficient, and whether the use of all three inputs (loss, gradient, and parameters) is truly necessary.
2. Considering the related work section, it would be beneficial to provide a more comprehensive overview of the history of meta-learning, including earlier work by Samy Bengio, Yoshua Bengio, and Jürgen Schmidhuber.
3. Future work could explore the possibility of learning other architectural components of the network, such as the number of neurons or type of units, in a similar fashion.

Questions for the authors:

1. Have you considered exploring simpler combinations of inputs to the meta-learner, and if so, what were the results?
2. Can you provide more details on the specific implementation of batch normalization used in the paper, and how it differs from other approaches?
3. Are there any plans to explore the application of this approach to other domains or tasks beyond few-shot learning?
