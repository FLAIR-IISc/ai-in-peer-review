I am updating my score to an 8 in light of the authors' responsiveness and the updates to the manuscript, particularly in clarifying the meta-learning task.

This manuscript proposes to tackle few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a classic idea that has seen a renaissance in the last 12 months. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each "example" comprises a sequence of batches of "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. The inputs at each "step" include the outputs of a "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradients) and the base learner's current state (parameters). The paper applies a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network to this meta-learning problem, utilizing the inner memory cells in the second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. In doing so, they note similarities between the respective update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. Updates to the LSTM meta-learner are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final "test" batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells (analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters). The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset proposed in Vinyals et al. (2016) and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks, Vinyals et al., 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning tasks.

Strengths:

* The authors' formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is intriguing and, in hindsight, natural. Although they did not originate the general idea of persisting learning across a series of learning problems, it is fair to say that they have advanced the state of the art. While I am not deeply familiar with recent work on meta-learning, the proposed approach appears to be a significant contribution.
* The proposed approach is competitive with and, in some cases, outperforms the state-of-the-art results reported in Vinyals et al. (2016) on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
* The base learner used in this setting (a simple ConvNet classifier) is distinct from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach employed in Vinyals et al. (2016). It is exciting to see state-of-the-art results achieved using different approaches, rather than incremental follow-up work.
* The insight about the relationship between the memory cell and gradient descent updates appears to be novel and is interesting regardless.
* The paper provides several practical insights on designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, which should facilitate replication of this work and application of these ideas to new problems. These insights include proper initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. Although some of these insights have been previously described (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training and assuming independence between meta-learner and base learner parameters when taking gradients with respect to the meta-learner parameters), the discussion is still useful.

Weaknesses:

* The writing is sometimes opaque, making the paper a challenging read. It took multiple passes, as well as consulting related work, to understand the general learning problem. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is very abstract and uses notation and language that may not be common outside of this sub-area. A brief, concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) paired with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks would make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
* The precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is unclear. Specifically, the Mini-ImageNet dataset has 100 labels, of which 64/16/20 are used during meta-training/validation/testing. It is unclear whether this means that only 64/100 classes are observed during meta-training or if only 64/100 classes are observed in each batch, but all 100 are observed on average during meta-training. If it is the former, the number of outputs in the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner during meta-training is unclear – is it 64 (only those observed in training) or 100 (of which 36 are never observed)? Several other details, such as these, are unclear and require further clarification.
* The plots in Figure 2 are not very informative on their own, and the discussion section offers limited insight into these plots.

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. Although the presentation of the ideas and work could be improved, it seems like a fairly clear accept. I will definitely raise my score if the writing is improved.
