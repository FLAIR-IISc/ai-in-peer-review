- Reviewer raised score to 8 after authors clarified the meta-learning task and were responsive to feedback.
- Paper reframes few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem where each example is a sequence of training batches followed by a test batch.
- Meta-learner inputs include base learner outputs (loss, gradients) and the base learner’s current parameters.
- Authors use an LSTM meta-learner, leveraging second-layer memory cells to model base-learner parameter updates.
- Observed similarity between LSTM memory-cell updates and gradient-descent update rules.
- LSTM meta-learner updates are driven by the base learner’s prediction loss on the final test batch.
- Authors make simplifying assumptions, e.g., sharing weights across second-layer cells (analogous to a single learning rate).
- They recreated the Mini-ImageNet dataset and show the LSTM meta-learner is competitive with or outperforms Matching Networks on 1- and 5-shot tasks.
- Casting few-shot learning as sequential meta-learning is an intriguing, natural formulation that advances the state of the art, though novelty is uncertain.
- The method outperforms Vinyals (2016) on Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- Using a simple ConvNet base learner (vs learned-embedding + nearest neighbor) is a distinct and encouraging approach.
- The insight linking memory cells to gradient descent appears novel and interesting.
- Paper provides practical, replicable insights on designing and training LSTM meta-learners (initialization, weight sharing, normalization/rescaling).
- Writing is often opaque and hard to follow; a concrete example and diagram would improve accessibility.
- The precise N-class few-shot setup (which classes are seen during meta-training and softmax output size) is unclear.
- Figure 2’s plots are not informative on their own, and the discussion around them is weak.
- Overall: convincing results and clear accept recommendation, contingent on improving presentation/writing.
