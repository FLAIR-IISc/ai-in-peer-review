This manuscript tackles few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a classic idea that has gained significant attention in the last 12 months. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each "example" comprises a sequence of batches of "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. The inputs at each "step" include the outputs of a "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradients) and the base learner's current state (parameters). By applying an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, the authors directly model the updated parameters of the base learner using the inner memory cells in the second layer. They note similarities between the respective update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. The updates to the LSTM meta-learner are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final "test" batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second layer cells (analogous to using the same learning rate for all parameters). The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet data set proposed in Vinyals et al 2016 and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks, Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

Strengths:
- The authors' approach of casting the few-shot learning problem as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is intriguing and natural. Although they did not originate the general idea of persisting learning across a series of learning problems, they have advanced the state of the art. While I am not deeply familiar with recent work on meta-learning and therefore cannot confidently assert its novelty, the idea is well-executed.
- The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals 2016 in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner in this setting (simple ConvNet classifier) differs significantly from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach used in Vinyals 2016. It is exciting to see state-of-the-art results achieved using distinct approaches, rather than incremental follow-up work.
- The insight about the relationship between the memory cell and gradient descent updates appears to be novel and is interesting regardless.
- The paper provides practical insights into designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, making it easier for others to replicate this work and apply these ideas to new problems. These insights include proper initialization, weight sharing across coordinates, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. Although some of these insights have been previously described (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training and assuming independence between meta-learner and base learner parameters when taking gradients with respect to the meta-learner parameters), the discussion here is useful nonetheless.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is sometimes opaque, making the paper challenging to follow. While it describes interesting work, the text could be improved for better clarity. I required multiple passes and consultations with related work to understand the general learning problem. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is abstract and uses notation and language that may be unfamiliar to readers outside this sub-area. A brief, concrete example (e.g., using MNIST) paired with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks would make the paper more accessible to a wider audience.
- The precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is unclear. Specifically, the Mini-ImageNet data set has 100 labels, of which 64/16/20 are used during meta-training/validation/testing. It is unclear whether only 64/100 classes are observed through meta-training or if only 64/100 are observed in each batch, but on average, all 100 are observed during meta-training. If it is the former, the number of outputs in the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner during meta-training is unclear – is it 64 (only those observed in training) or 100 (of which 36 are never observed)? Several other details, such as these, are unclear and require further clarification.
- The plots in Figure 2 are not very informative on their own, and the discussion section offers limited insight into them.

This is an interesting paper with convincing results. Although the presentation of the ideas and work could be improved, it seems like a clear accept. I will consider raising my score if the writing is improved.
