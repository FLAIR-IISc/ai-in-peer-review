This paper proposes an innovative approach to meta-learning by interpreting the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) update rule as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. The idea is original and significant for research related to transfer learning. The paper has a clear structure, but clarity could be improved at some points.

The authors claim to contribute a novel method for few-shot learning tasks, where a classifier must quickly generalize after seeing very few examples from each class. They propose an LSTM-based meta-learner model to learn the exact optimization algorithm used to train another learner neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The parametrization of their model allows it to learn appropriate parameter updates specifically for the scenario where a set amount of updates will be made, while also learning a general initialization of the learner (classifier) network that allows for quick convergence of training.

The paper's main strength lies in its competitive results and proper comparison to state-of-the-art methods, such as deep metric-learning techniques for few-shot learning. The authors demonstrate that their meta-learning model is competitive with these techniques, which is a significant achievement.

However, there are some areas that require improvement. Firstly, the analogy between the SGD update rule and the gated recurrent model would be closer to Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) than Long Short-Term Memory (LSTMs). The authors should clarify this point and provide a more accurate comparison.

Secondly, the description of the data separation in meta-sets is hard to follow and could be visualized for better understanding. The authors should consider adding a diagram or figure to illustrate this process.

Thirdly, the experimental evaluation is only partly satisfying. The authors should provide more insights into the effect of the parameters of the input gate (it) and the forget gate (ft) on the performance of the meta-learner. This would help to better understand the behavior of the model and its limitations.

Lastly, Figure 2 does not add much value to the paper and could be removed or replaced with a more informative figure.

In conclusion, I accept this paper, but I encourage the authors to address the above-mentioned points to improve the clarity and quality of their work.

Additional feedback:

* The authors should consider releasing the code used in their evaluation experiments, as mentioned in the paper. This would be a significant plus for the research community.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the meta-learner's behavior and its limitations. The authors should consider providing more insights into the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner.
* The authors should also consider exploring the application of their meta-learning model to other domains and tasks, such as natural language processing or reinforcement learning.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more insights into the effect of the parameters of the input gate (it) and the forget gate (ft) on the performance of the meta-learner?
* How do you plan to extend your meta-learning model to other domains and tasks?
* Can you provide more details on the computational resources required to train the meta-learner model?
