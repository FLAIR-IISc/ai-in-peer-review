This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm (here a neural network). The paper is globally well written, and the presentation of the main material is clear.

The authors tackle the specific question of few-shot learning, where a classifier must quickly generalize after seeing very few examples from each class. They propose an LSTM-based meta-learner model to learn the exact optimization algorithm used to train another learner neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The approach is well motivated, including being well-placed in the literature, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their method addresses the weaknesses of gradient-based optimization in the few-shot learning setting.

The paper supports its claims through convincing experiments on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, demonstrating that the proposed approach is competitive with deep metric-learning techniques for few-shot learning. The authors also provide a clear and detailed description of their model, including the use of parameter sharing and preprocessing, as well as a novel implementation of batch normalization.

My decision to accept this paper is based on its strong contributions to the field of meta-learning and few-shot learning. The authors present a well-motivated and well-executed approach that addresses a significant problem in machine learning.

Supporting arguments for this decision include:

* The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach, including the use of LSTM-based meta-learning and parameter sharing.
* The experiments are convincing, demonstrating that the proposed approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods for few-shot learning.
* The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, including a detailed comparison with other meta-learning approaches.

Additional feedback to improve the paper includes:

* Consider including ablative studies to evaluate the importance of each component of the meta-learner input (loss, gradient, and parameters).
* Discuss the potential for learning other architectural components of the network, such as the number of neurons or type of units.
* Provide a more detailed discussion of the related work, including a broader survey of meta-learning approaches and a more in-depth comparison with other methods.

Questions to the authors include:

* Can you provide more details on the initialization of the meta-learner LSTM and how it affects the training process?
* How do you plan to extend this approach to more challenging scenarios, such as few-shot learning with many classes or few examples per class?
* Have you considered applying this approach to other machine learning problems, such as reinforcement learning or natural language processing?
