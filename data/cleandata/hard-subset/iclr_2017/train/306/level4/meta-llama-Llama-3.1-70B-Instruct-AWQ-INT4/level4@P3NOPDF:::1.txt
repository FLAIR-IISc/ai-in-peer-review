This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm, specifically a neural network (NN). The paper is well-written, and the presentation of the main material is clear.

The core contribution of the paper, drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule, is intriguing. By exploiting this connection, the authors aim to satisfy the two primary desiderata of few-shot learning: rapid acquisition of new knowledge and slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge. Several techniques, such as parameter sharing and normalization, borrowed from (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), are well-motivated, as are the novel design choices, including the specific implementation of batch normalization.

The experiments presented in the paper are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed meta-learning framework. Overall, this is a strong paper. However, I have a few concerns and questions that I would like to see addressed:

1. Is it necessary to use the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner? Did the authors conduct ablative studies to ensure that simpler combinations of these inputs are not sufficient? It would be beneficial to understand the importance of each input component and whether they can be reduced or modified without affecting performance.

2. It would be interesting to explore whether other architectural components of the network can be learned in a similar fashion, such as the number of neurons or the type of units. Do the authors have any opinions or insights on this topic? Investigating the possibility of learning these components could lead to even more efficient and effective meta-learning frameworks.

3. The related work section, primarily focused on meta-learning, could be more comprehensive. Meta-learning is a relatively old topic, and similar approaches have been proposed to address the same problem, even if they did not utilize LSTMs. For example, Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989) and the work by S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier (1994) on using genetic programming for the search of new learning rules for neural networks are relevant references that should be included. Additionally, I am convinced that Jürgen Schmidhuber has made contributions to this area, and it would be beneficial to include his work in the related work section to provide a more complete overview of the field.

In conclusion, I like the paper and believe that the discussed material is relevant to a wide audience at ICLR. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of meta-learning.
