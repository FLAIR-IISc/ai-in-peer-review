In light of the authors' feedback and the paper's updates – especially clarifying the meta-learning task – I am updating my score to 8.

This paper proposes leveraging meta-learning to solve few-shot learning with neural networks, a classic idea that has seen a resurgence in the past 12 months. The authors frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each "example" consists of a sequence of input "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. Each "step" includes the input, the output of the "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradient), as well as the current state of the base learner (parameters). This paper implements an LSTM for this meta-learning problem, which uses internal memory cells in the second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. In doing so, they note similarities between the LSTM memory cells and gradient descent update rules. The LSTM meta-learning updates are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final "test" batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second-layer cells (similar to using the same learning rate for all parameters). This paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset proposed by Vinyas et al. in 2016 and shows that meta-learning LSTMs are competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Machine Network, Vinyas 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

Strengths:
- Introducing the few-shot learning problem as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is interesting – and natural to look back at. While the authors did not express the general idea of learning in a series of continuous learning problems, I think it is fair to say that they have advanced the state of the art, although I cannot confidently emphasize its novelty because I am not deeply familiar with recent work on meta-learning.
- The proposed approach is competitive with and performs better than Vinyas 2016 in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- In this setting, the base learner (the simple ConvNet classifier) is quite different from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach used in Vinyas 2016. It is always exciting when cutting-edge results can be reported using very different approaches, rather than a sequential follow-up task.
- As far as I know, insight into the relationship between memory cells and gradient descent updates is new here. Whatever it is, it is interesting.
- This paper provides several practical insights into designing and training LSTMs, making it easier for others to replicate this work and apply these ideas to new problems. These include proper initialization, sharing weights in coordination, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. Some insights have been described before (the importance of simulating test conditions during meta-training; assuming independence between meta-learning and base learner parameters when the gradient is taken in relation to the meta-learning parameter), but the discussion here is still useful.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is sometimes quite unclear. While it describes very interesting work, I would not call this paper an enjoyable read. I had to make several attempts (as well as consult related work) to understand the general learning problem. The task description in section 2 (page 2) is very abstract and uses notations and language that are not common outside this sub-field. The paper could benefit from a brief concrete example (based on NISHT, okay), perhaps combined with a diagram illustrating the sequence of few-shot learning tasks. This would certainly make it accessible to a wider audience.
- Moving on to that note, the exact nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem is unclear to me here. Specifically, the Mini-ImageNet dataset has 100 labels, of which 64/16/20 are used during meta-training/validation/testing. Does this mean that only 64/100 classes are seen through meta-training? Or does it mean that only 64/100 are seen in each batch, but on average all 100 are seen during meta-training? If so, how many outputs are in the Softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner during meta-training? 64 (only seen in training) or 100 (36 of which were never seen)? Many other such details are unclear (see questions).
- The plots in Figure 2 are quite informative in themselves, and the discussion section provides very little insight around them.

This is an interesting paper with reliable results. It seems to be quite clear acceptance, but the presentation of ideas and the work done in it could be improved. If the writing were improved, I would certainly increase my score.
