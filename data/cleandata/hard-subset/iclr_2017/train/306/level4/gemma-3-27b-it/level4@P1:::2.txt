This paper describes a novel approach to meta-learning by framing the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) update rule as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. The idea is original and represents a valuable contribution to research related to transfer learning. The paper is well-structured, though clarity could be improved in certain areas.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles an important problem – few-shot learning – with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed LSTM-based meta-learner.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of learning an optimizer with an LSTM is compelling. The authors convincingly argue that gradient-based optimization struggles in the few-shot setting due to both the limited data and the lack of a good parameter initialization. By framing the problem as meta-learning, and specifically by modeling the update rule within an LSTM, they address both of these issues. The meta-learner learns a task-common initialization and adapts the optimization process specifically for scenarios with a limited number of updates. 

The experimental results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms strong baselines and achieves competitive performance compared to Matching Networks, a current state-of-the-art technique. The ablation study (though limited) and the analysis of the learned gate values provide some insight into the meta-learner’s behavior. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their experimental setup and provide a clear roadmap for future work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be addressed to further improve clarity and impact:

*   **GRU Analogy:** The authors correctly note the similarity between their formulation and LSTMs, but also mention the analogy to GRUs. Expanding on this connection, and explicitly discussing the differences and motivations for choosing an LSTM over a GRU, would strengthen the technical argument.
*   **Meta-Set Visualization:** The description of how the meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets are constructed could be clearer. A simple diagram illustrating this process (similar to Figure 1, but focusing on the dataset splitting) would be very helpful for understanding the experimental setup.
*   **Gate Parameter Analysis:** While the visualization of the gate values is interesting, a more detailed analysis of the learned parameters of the `it` and `ft` gates would be valuable. For example, are there consistent patterns in the learned weights? How do these patterns relate to the specific tasks being learned?
*   **Figure 2:** Figure 2, illustrating the computational graph, is not particularly informative and could be removed. The text already provides a clear explanation of the computation.
*   **Typo:** In Section 3.2, “This means each coordinate has it” should be “its.”

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions making a simplifying assumption regarding the gradients of the meta-learner. Could you elaborate on the potential impact of this assumption on the performance of the model? Were any experiments conducted to assess the validity of this approximation?
*   You trained separate meta-learners for the 1-shot and 5-shot tasks. Did you observe any significant differences in the learned optimization strategies between these two models? If so, could you describe these differences?
*   The results show that the fine-tuned baseline performs worse than the nearest neighbor baseline. Is this a consistent finding, and can you offer any explanation for this phenomenon? Does it suggest a particular sensitivity to overfitting in the few-shot setting?
*   You mention the potential for fine-tuning the meta-learner using the training set at test time. Have you explored this option? If so, what were the results? If not, what are the challenges associated with this approach?
