- Proposal: Frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem using an LSTM whose memory cells model base-learner parameter updates.
- Inputs: Each step uses base-learner outputs (loss, gradients) and current parameters; LSTM updates are analogous to gradient descent.
- Implementation detail: Authors share weights across LSTM cells and make other simplifying assumptions.
- Empirical result: Recreated Mini-ImageNet and show meta-learner LSTM is competitive with Matching Networks on 1- and 5-shot tasks.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Strength: Novel, well-executed sequential meta-learning formulation that achieves strong performance.
- Strength: Useful practical insights on LSTM initialization, weight sharing, and input normalization.
- Contribution: Method differs meaningfully from Matching Networks, offering a complementary approach to few-shot learning.
- Insight: The analogy between LSTM updates and gradient descent provides a solid rationale for the model design.
- Writing: Paper could be clearer and more accessible; task description (Section 2) is abstract and needs a concrete example and diagram.
- Visualization: Discussion of Figure 3 and gate-value interpretations is shallow and could be expanded.
- Ablation: Recommend a small ablation study on design choices (e.g., weight sharing, normalization) and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Question: Clarify Mini-ImageNet split—are only 64 classes seen during meta-training and what is the base ConvNet softmax size during training?
- Question: Provide details on computational cost and memory requirements compared to Matching Networks.
- Question: How sensitive are results to meta-learner hyperparameters (learning rate, gradient clipping); include ablation if possible.
- Question: Is computing loss/gradient on the entire small Dtrain at each step efficient, or would mini-batching within Dtrain help?
- Question: Explain the “gradient independence assumption,” its potential impact if removed, and why it’s reasonable here.
