This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn the optimization algorithm *for* another learning algorithm (in this case, a neural network). 

The paper is generally well-written and the presentation of the core material is clear. The central idea – drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule, and leveraging this connection to satisfy the two main desiderata of few-shot learning (1 – rapid acquisition of new knowledge, 2 – slower extraction of general, transferable knowledge) – is intriguing. 

Several techniques reused from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, alongside novel design choices (specifically, the implementation of batch normalization), are well-motivated. 

The experiments are convincing and demonstrate a strong contribution. My only concerns and questions are as follows:

1. Is it necessary to provide the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner? Were ablation studies conducted to determine whether simpler combinations of these inputs are sufficient?

2. It would be valuable to explore whether other architectural components of the network could be learned in a similar fashion (e.g., the number of neurons or the type of units used). Do you have any thoughts on this possibility?

3. The related work section, particularly concerning meta-learning, feels somewhat limited in scope. Meta-learning is a relatively established field, and similar approaches have been attempted to address the same problem, even predating the use of LSTMs. For example:
    - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) is directly relevant to this work.
    - The application of genetic programming for discovering new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994) should be considered.
    - I am confident that Jürgen Schmidhuber has also explored related concepts; please ensure a thorough search and update the related work section accordingly.

Overall, I find this to be a strong paper. I believe the presented material is highly relevant to a broad audience at ICLR.
