This manuscript proposes to tackle few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem where each “example” consists of a sequence of batches of “training” pairs, followed by a final “test” batch. The inputs at each “step” include the outputs of a “base learner” (e.g., training loss and gradients), and the base learner's current state (parameters). An LSTM is applied to this meta-learning problem, using the inner memory cells to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner, noting similarities between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent. Updates to the LSTM meta-learner are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final “test” batch. The authors make simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all LSTM cells. They recreate the Mini-ImageNet dataset and demonstrate that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the state-of-the-art (Matching Networks) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to few-shot learning that achieves competitive results with existing state-of-the-art methods. The framing of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is insightful and the LSTM-based meta-learner demonstrates promising performance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Insight:** The core idea of leveraging an LSTM to *learn* an optimization algorithm for a base learner in the few-shot setting is novel and insightful. The connection drawn between LSTM updates and gradient descent is a key contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with Matching Networks, a strong baseline. The ability to achieve comparable performance with a fundamentally different approach (a trainable optimizer versus metric learning) is significant.
*   **Practical Contributions:** The paper provides valuable practical insights into training an LSTM meta-learner, including initialization strategies, weight sharing techniques, and the importance of input normalization. These details will be helpful for researchers attempting to reproduce and extend this work.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the few-shot learning problem within a meta-learning framework, establishing a solid foundation for the proposed approach.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Clarity of Writing:** The paper could benefit from improved clarity and conciseness. The writing is occasionally dense and requires multiple readings to fully grasp the concepts. Simplifying the language and providing more intuitive explanations would broaden the paper’s accessibility.
*   **Concrete Example:** A concrete example illustrating the meta-learning process, perhaps using a simplified dataset like MNIST, would significantly improve understanding. A diagram visualizing the sequence of tasks and the information flow between the learner and meta-learner would also be highly beneficial.
*   **Mini-ImageNet Details:** The description of how the Mini-ImageNet dataset is used needs clarification. Specifically, how many classes are visible to the base learner *during training* in each meta-training episode? Is the softmax layer size fixed at 64 during training, even though only a subset of those classes are present in a given episode? Providing these details is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the experimental setup.
*   **Visualization Discussion:** The visualizations in Figure 3 are currently not very informative. Providing a more detailed analysis of what the gate values reveal about the meta-learner’s optimization strategy would enhance the paper's impact. For instance, are there consistent patterns in how the forget and input gates respond to different types of gradients or loss values?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the Mini-ImageNet experiment: During meta-training, if a meta-training episode only includes *N* classes from the available 64, what is the size of the softmax layer in the base learner network? Is it fixed at 64 (with 36 classes effectively “unused” in that episode), or is it dynamically adjusted to *N*?
2.  The paper mentions the "gradient independence assumption." Could you elaborate on the potential impact of *not* accounting for the dependency of learner losses and gradients on the meta-learner’s parameters? What empirical evidence do you have to suggest that this simplification does not significantly degrade performance?
3.  In Section 3.3.2, you mention initializing the input gate bias to a small value. What specific value was used, and how sensitive is the performance to this initialization parameter?
4.  Could you provide some insight into the computational cost of training the meta-learner LSTM compared to training the baseline methods (e.g., Matching Networks)? Are there any significant bottlenecks or opportunities for optimization?
5.  You mention that the meta-learner can also be fine-tuned using the test set data. Were any experiments performed with this additional fine-tuning step? If not, can you comment on the potential benefits and drawbacks of doing so?
