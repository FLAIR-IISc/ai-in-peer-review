## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper presents a novel approach to meta-learning by framing the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) update rule as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. This interpretation is original and represents a potentially significant contribution to research in transfer learning and optimization. The paper is generally well-structured, though clarity could be improved in certain areas.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel and Feasible Approach:** The proposed method offers an interesting and plausible lens through which to view meta-learning. Representing the learning process as a recurrent network opens up opportunities for learning update rules tailored to specific task distributions.
* **Competitive Results:** The authors demonstrate competitive performance against existing state-of-the-art meta-learning algorithms. The results suggest the proposed method is a viable alternative and worthy of further investigation.
* **Practical Recommendations:** The paper offers useful recommendations for implementing the system in practice, which enhances its immediate applicability and potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Architectural Analogy:** While the authors draw a parallel between their approach and LSTMs, the architecture more closely resembles a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU). The gating mechanism employed appears simpler than that of a standard LSTM, lacking the cell state. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the technical justification.
* **Meta-Set Data Separation:** The description of how data is separated into meta-training and meta-testing sets is difficult to follow. A visual illustration depicting this data separation process would significantly improve understanding. The current textual explanation lacks sufficient detail to fully grasp the experimental setup.
* **Limited Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating overall effectiveness, is somewhat incomplete. A more thorough analysis of the impact of key hyperparameters, particularly those controlling the learned update rule (specifically, parameters 'it' and 'ft' mentioned in the draft), would provide valuable insight into the method’s behavior and sensitivity. Understanding how these parameters influence performance is crucial for practical application.
* **Fig 2 – Limited Informative Value:** Figure 2, as presented, does not provide substantial insight. Its contribution to understanding the method or results is minimal, and its inclusion may be reconsidered.

**Remarks:**

* A minor typographical error exists in Section 3.2: "This means each coordinate has it" should be corrected to “its”.
* The authors’ commitment to releasing the code used in the evaluation experiments is highly encouraging and would be a significant asset to the research community, facilitating reproducibility and further development.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new direction in meta-learning. The core idea is both innovative and well-motivated. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly improving the clarity of the data separation, providing a more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity, and reconsidering Figure 2 – would substantially strengthen the paper. Despite these minor concerns, I believe this work has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field and recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the points raised.
