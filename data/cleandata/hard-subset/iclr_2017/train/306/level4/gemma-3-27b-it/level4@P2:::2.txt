This paper presents a novel approach to meta-learning by framing the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) update rule as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. This is an original and promising idea with potential significance for transfer learning research. The paper is well-structured, though clarity could be enhanced in certain areas.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles an important problem in a novel way and demonstrates competitive performance against established methods. The core idea is well-motivated and the experimental results, while not exhaustive, provide sufficient evidence to support the claims.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for this work is clear: gradient-based optimization struggles in low-data regimes. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of standard optimization algorithms when applied to few-shot learning and proposes a meta-learning framework to address these issues. The approach of learning an optimizer, rather than relying on hand-designed algorithms, is a compelling direction. 

The proposed LSTM-based meta-learner is a clever interpretation of the SGD update rule. The authors effectively leverage the LSTM structure to learn both a good initialization for the learner network and a task-specific update strategy. The experimental results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrate that the proposed method achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques like Matching Networks. Specifically, the method outperforms baselines and achieves comparable results to Matching Networks, especially in the 5-shot setting. The ablation study and visualization (while needing some improvements, see below) help to provide some insight into how the meta-learner operates.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is strong, several areas could benefit from further refinement:

*   **GRU vs. LSTM:** The authors acknowledge that the analogy to gradient descent is perhaps closer to a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) than a full LSTM. Expanding on this and perhaps discussing the implications of this difference would strengthen the argument.
*   **Meta-set Visualization:** The description of how datasets are separated into meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets could be made clearer. A visual illustration (similar to Figure 1 but focusing on dataset splitting) would significantly improve understanding.
*   **Gate Parameter Analysis:** A more detailed analysis of the learned parameters of the input (it) and forget (ft) gates would be valuable. Exploring how these parameters vary across different datasets and tasks could reveal more about the learned optimization strategy. Presenting the results of ablation studies focusing on these gates would be helpful.
*   **Figure 2:** The current Figure 2 does not provide substantial insight and could be removed or replaced with a more informative diagram. Perhaps a schematic showing the flow of gradients and losses through the meta-learner and learner networks.
*   **Minor Typo:** In Section 3.2, "This means each coordinate has it" should be "its".



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose to use an LSTM over a GRU, given the closer analogy to gradient descent? Were there any empirical reasons for this decision?
2.  The paper mentions the “gradient independence assumption.” Could you discuss the potential impact of relaxing this assumption and the computational challenges associated with doing so?
3.  Regarding the learned input and forget gate parameters, can you provide more examples or visualizations of their behavior across different datasets? Are there any common patterns or characteristics you've observed?
4.  You mention that the meta-learner is trained to make a specific number of updates. How sensitive is the performance to this number of updates, and how would you approach choosing the optimal number of updates for a new task?
