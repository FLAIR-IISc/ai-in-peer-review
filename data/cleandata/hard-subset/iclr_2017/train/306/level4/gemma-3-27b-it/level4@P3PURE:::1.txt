This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn the optimization algorithm *of* another learning algorithm (in this case, a neural network). 
The paper is globally well-written and the presentation of the core material is clear. The central idea – drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM update rule, and leveraging this connection to satisfy the two main desiderata of few-shot learning (1 - rapid acquisition of new knowledge, 2 - slower extraction of general transferable knowledge) – is intriguing. 
Several techniques reused from (Andrychowicz et al. 2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, alongside novel design choices (specifically, the implementation of batch normalization), are well motivated.
The experiments are convincing, and this is a strong paper. My main concerns and questions are as follows:

1. Is it necessary to provide the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner? Were ablation studies performed to determine if simpler input combinations suffice?
2. It would be valuable to explore whether other architectural components of the network (e.g., the number of neurons, type of units) could be learned in a similar fashion. Do the authors have any thoughts on this possibility?
3. The related work section, focused primarily on meta-learning, is somewhat shallow. Meta-learning is a long-standing research area, and analogous approaches have been explored to solve this problem even without utilizing LSTMs:
     - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) directly addresses this concept.
     - The use of genetic programming for searching for new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994) is also relevant.
     - I am confident that Jürgen Schmidhuber has also made contributions in this area; please ensure a thorough search and update the related work section accordingly.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. I believe the material discussed is relevant to a wide audience at ICLR.
