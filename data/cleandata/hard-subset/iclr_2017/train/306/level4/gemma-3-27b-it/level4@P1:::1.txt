This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn the optimization algorithm for another learning algorithm (here, a neural network).

The paper is generally well-written and the presentation of the core material is clear. The central idea – drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM update rule, and exploiting it to address the two main desiderata of few-shot learning (1) quick acquisition of new knowledge, and (2) slower extraction of general transferable knowledge – is intriguing. 

Several techniques reused from (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, and novel design choices (specifically the implementation of batch normalization) are well-motivated. The experiments are convincing. This is a strong paper. My main concerns and questions are the following:

1. Could it be redundant to use the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner? Were ablative studies performed to determine if simpler combinations are sufficient?

2. It would be beneficial if other architectural components of the learner network could be learned in a similar fashion (e.g., the number of neurons, the type of units). Do you have any thoughts on this?

3. The related work section, primarily focused on meta-learning, feels somewhat shallow. Meta-learning is a relatively established field, and similar approaches have been explored to solve the same problem, even without using LSTMs:
    * Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) is highly relevant.
    * The use of genetic programming for searching for new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994) should be acknowledged.
    * I am confident Jürgen Schmidhuber has also contributed relevant work; please ensure you identify and discuss it in the related work section.

Overall, I like this paper. I believe the presented material is relevant to a wide audience at ICLR.

**Decision:** Accept. The approach is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results are promising. The core idea of framing few-shot learning as a meta-optimization problem, and leveraging LSTMs to learn the update rules, is a significant contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully addresses a challenging problem in machine learning – few-shot learning – with a novel and well-reasoned approach. The connection between LSTM updates and optimization algorithms is insightful, and the experiments demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive performance compared to existing methods. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the existing literature, and the inclusion of techniques from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) indicates an awareness of related work in meta-learning. The discussion of parameter sharing and batch normalization is thorough and justified.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive review of the related work, particularly in the area of early meta-learning approaches.
*   Ablative studies to analyze the contribution of each input feature to the meta-learner (loss, gradient, parameters) would strengthen the analysis and potentially simplify the model.
*   It would be interesting to explore the possibility of learning other architectural parameters of the learner network within the meta-learning framework.
