This manuscript proposes a novel approach to few-shot learning using meta-learning with LSTMs. The authors frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each “example” consists of a sequence of training batches followed by a test batch. An LSTM is employed as the meta-learner, leveraging its internal memory cells to directly model updates to the parameters of a base learner (a convolutional neural network). The authors highlight a compelling connection between the LSTM memory cell update rule and gradient descent, offering a potentially insightful perspective on the meta-learning process. Results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrate that this approach is competitive with, and in some cases outperforms, existing state-of-the-art methods like Matching Networks. 

**Strengths:**

*   The formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is both intuitive and promising. While the concept of meta-learning has gained traction recently, this work presents a significant advancement by demonstrating a practical and effective implementation using LSTMs.
*   The proposed method achieves competitive performance on the challenging 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet benchmark, surpassing the results reported in Vinyals et al. (2016).
*   The use of a convolutional neural network as the base learner provides a valuable contrast to embedding-based approaches (e.g., Matching Networks). Achieving strong results with distinct architectural choices is encouraging for the field.
*   The observation regarding the analogy between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent is a novel and intriguing insight that could inform future research.
*   The paper offers practical guidance for designing and training LSTM meta-learners, including recommendations for initialization, weight sharing, and input normalization. These insights will be valuable for researchers seeking to reproduce and extend this work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its presentation. The abstract and technical language, particularly in Section 2, make it difficult to grasp the core concepts without significant effort and prior knowledge. A concrete example, perhaps using MNIST, along with a clear diagram illustrating the sequence of few-shot learning tasks, would greatly improve accessibility.
*   The description of the N-class, few-shot learning problem and the usage of the Mini-ImageNet dataset is ambiguous. Specifically, the handling of unseen classes during meta-training needs clarification. Does the base learner’s softmax layer output probabilities for all 100 classes, even those not observed during a particular meta-training batch? Or is the output layer size dynamically adjusted to match the classes present in the training batch? Addressing this, and other similar details, is crucial for understanding the methodology.
*   The visualizations in Figure 2 are not particularly informative and lack sufficient context for interpretation. A more detailed discussion of the insights gleaned from these plots would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of meta-learning and few-shot learning. The approach is well-motivated, the results are promising, and the insights offered are potentially significant. However, the presentation of the material could be substantially improved to enhance clarity and accessibility. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the clarity of the task description and the interpretation of the experimental results, would strengthen the paper considerably.
