This paper introduces a meta-learning framework based on LSTMs designed to learn the optimization algorithm *for* another learning algorithm – specifically, a neural network. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its core ideas. The central contribution – establishing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update equation, and leveraging this connection to address the two key challenges of few-shot learning (rapid acquisition of new knowledge *and* slower extraction of generalizable, transferable knowledge) – is particularly insightful. 

The authors effectively motivate both the reuse of techniques from previous work, such as parameter sharing and normalization (Andrychowicz et al. 2016), and their novel design choices, including a specific implementation of batch normalization. The experimental results are compelling and support the efficacy of the proposed approach. Overall, this is a strong submission.

I have a few questions and suggestions for improvement:

1. The meta-learner utilizes the loss, gradient, and network parameters as input. While this provides a comprehensive view of the learning process, it raises the question of redundancy. Have the authors conducted ablation studies to determine if a simpler input combination would achieve comparable performance? Understanding the contribution of each input component would strengthen the justification for the chosen architecture.

2. The paper focuses on learning the *update rule* itself. It would be interesting to consider whether other architectural aspects of the network – such as the number of neurons per layer or the type of activation units used – could also be learned within a similar meta-learning framework. Do the authors have any thoughts on the feasibility or potential benefits of extending the approach in this direction?

3. The related work section, while covering meta-learning, feels somewhat limited in scope. Meta-learning is a relatively established field, and the problem of learning learning algorithms has been addressed through various methods predating the current resurgence of interest. Specifically:

    * Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) is fundamentally concerned with this problem.
    *  Bengio, Bengio, and Cloutier (1994) explored the use of genetic programming to discover new learning rules for neural networks. 
    * Given Jürgen Schmidhuber's extensive work in the area of meta-learning and adaptive computation, I encourage the authors to investigate his publications and ensure they are appropriately represented in the related work section. 

Despite these points, I find this to be a valuable and well-executed paper. The presented material is highly relevant to the ICLR audience and contributes meaningfully to the growing body of research in meta-learning. I believe it has the potential to stimulate further investigation into LSTM-based approaches to learning optimization algorithms.
