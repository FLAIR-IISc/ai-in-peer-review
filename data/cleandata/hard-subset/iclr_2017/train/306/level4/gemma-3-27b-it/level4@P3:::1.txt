This work presents an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn the optimization algorithm for another learning algorithm (here, a neural network).

The paper is well-written and the presentation of the core material is clear. The central idea – drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM update rule, and exploiting this to address the two main requirements of few-shot learning (1) rapid acquisition of new knowledge, and (2) slower extraction of generalizable knowledge – is intriguing. Several techniques reused from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, alongside novel design choices (specifically, the implementation of batch normalization) are well-motivated. The experiments are convincing, and this is a strong paper overall.

**Decision: Accept**

My primary reasons for accepting this paper are the novelty of framing the optimization process *as* a learning problem tackled by an LSTM, and the demonstrated competitive performance on a challenging few-shot learning benchmark. The authors successfully leverage meta-learning to address a critical limitation of deep learning – its data inefficiency.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper convincingly argues that standard gradient-based optimization struggles in few-shot regimes due to both poor initialization and suboptimal update rules for limited data. The proposed LSTM-based meta-learner directly addresses these issues by learning a task-common initialization and an adaptive optimization strategy. The experimental results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrate that the method achieves competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art techniques like Matching Networks, particularly in the 5-shot setting. The visualization of the learned optimization strategy, while preliminary, offers valuable insight into the meta-learner’s behavior. The careful consideration of batch normalization in the meta-learning context, and the parameter sharing strategy, demonstrate a thoughtful approach to the practical challenges of implementation.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several points could further enhance it. 

1. **Input Feature Redundancy:** The authors use the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner. While intuitively reasonable, it would be valuable to investigate whether all these inputs are necessary. Ablation studies exploring simpler combinations of inputs could clarify their relative importance and potentially simplify the model.

2. **Architectural Meta-Learning:** The current work focuses on learning the *optimization* algorithm. Exploring whether other architectural components of the learner network (e.g., number of neurons, type of units) could also be learned in a similar fashion would be a natural extension. Even a discussion of the challenges and potential approaches to this broader scope of meta-learning would be beneficial.

3. **Related Work Expansion:** The related work section, while relevant, could be more comprehensive, particularly concerning the history of meta-learning. The field has roots extending back several decades. Specifically, the authors should consider including:
    * Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989), which explores related concepts.
    * Work using genetic programming for learning neural network update rules (e.g., S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
    * Relevant contributions from Jürgen Schmidhuber's group, which have explored learning to learn and adaptive learning rules.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper mentions the gradient independence assumption following Andrychowicz et al. (2016). Could you elaborate on the potential impact of this assumption on the performance and stability of the meta-learner? Are there scenarios where this assumption might be particularly problematic?

2. In Section 5.2, you visualize the gate values of the LSTM meta-learner. Could you provide more quantitative analysis of these gate values? For example, what is the average magnitude of the forget gate across different datasets, and how does it vary with the number of updates?

3. The preprocessing step described in Section 3.2 appears crucial. Could you explain the rationale behind the specific value of *p* = 10, and how sensitive the performance is to variations in this parameter? Is there a theoretical justification for this choice?
