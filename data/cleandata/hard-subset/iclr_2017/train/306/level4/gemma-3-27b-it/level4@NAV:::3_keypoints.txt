- Frames few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem where each instance is a series of training batches ending in a test batch.  
- Meta-learner is an LSTM that uses its second-layer memory cells to directly model base-learner parameter updates, drawing a parallel to gradient descent.  
- Base-learner inputs to the meta-learner include loss, gradients, and current parameters; the LSTM is trained using the base-learner’s loss on the final test batch.  
- Makes simplifying assumptions such as weight sharing across all second-layer cells, effectively imposing a uniform learning rate.  
- Validated on a recreated Mini-ImageNet dataset, showing competitive or superior performance to Matching Networks in 1- and 5-shot settings.  
- Strength: The sequential/meta-learning framing is insightful and advances the state-of-the-art in few-shot learning.  
- Strength: Empirical results are competitive with or better than Vinyals et al. (2016) on Mini-ImageNet.  
- Strength: Uses a conventional ConvNet base learner rather than an embedding-only approach, demonstrating architectural flexibility.  
- Strength: The observed connection between LSTM memory updates and gradient descent is novel and interesting.  
- Strength: Paper provides practical guidance for designing and training LSTM meta-learners (initialization, weight sharing, normalization/rescaling).  
- Weakness: Writing is dense and sometimes unclear; the abstract task description and notation hinder accessibility and require concrete examples/diagrams.  
- Weakness: Ambiguity in the precise N-class few-shot formulation and Mini-ImageNet class split—unclear whether only 64 classes are seen during meta-training and what softmax size is used.  
- Weakness: Figure 2 plots lack sufficient explanatory context and accompanying discussion.  
- Overall: Promising and convincing work recommended for acceptance pending improvements in clarity and presentation.
