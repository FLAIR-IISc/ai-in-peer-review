This manuscript proposes a novel approach to few-shot learning using neural networks and meta-learning, a recently revitalized area of research. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each “episode” consists of a sequence of batches representing “training” data, followed by a final “test” batch. The inputs at each step include the outputs of a “base learner” (e.g., training loss and gradients), as well as the base learner’s current state (parameters). An LSTM is applied to this meta-learning problem, with the second layer’s memory cells directly modeling the updated parameters of the base learner. The authors observe a compelling similarity between the update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. Updates to the LSTM meta-learner are computed based on the base learner’s prediction loss on the final “test” batch. Several simplifying assumptions are made, such as weight sharing across all second layer cells (analogous to a uniform learning rate for all base learner parameters). The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset proposed in Vinyals et al (2016) and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM achieves competitive performance, even outperforming the original Matching Networks (Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning tasks.

**Strengths:**

- It is an intriguing – and, in retrospect, natural – approach to frame few-shot learning as a sequential (meta-)learning problem. While the authors do not originate the general concept of persisting learning across a series of learning problems, their work significantly advances the state-of-the-art, though I am not deeply enough versed in recent meta-learning literature to definitively assess its novelty.
- The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals (2016) in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner employed in this work (a simple ConvNet classifier) differs substantially from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach used in Vinyals (2016). Reporting state-of-the-art results with such distinct architectures is commendable, and distinguishes this work from incremental follow-up studies.
- The insight regarding the relationship between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent appears novel and is intellectually interesting.
- The paper provides several practical insights into designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, facilitating replication and application of these ideas to new problems. These insights include proper initialization strategies, the benefits of weight sharing across coordinate dimensions, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. While some of these insights have been previously reported (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training, assuming independence between meta-learner and base learner parameters when computing gradients), the discussion presented here is nonetheless valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

- The writing is occasionally opaque. While the work itself is interesting, the paper is not particularly accessible. It required multiple readings (and consultation with related work) to fully grasp the learning problem being addressed. Section 2 (Page 2) is particularly abstract in its task description, relying on notation and terminology uncommon outside of this specific research area. The paper would benefit from a concrete example (using MNIST, for instance) coupled with a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks to broaden its appeal.
- The precise nature of the N-class, few-shot learning problem remains unclear. Specifically, the Mini-ImageNet dataset comprises 100 labels, with 64/16/20 used for meta-training/validation/testing. Does this imply that only 64/100 classes are ever observed during meta-training? Or are all 100 classes observed *on average* across meta-training, even if not within each individual batch? If the former is true, how many outputs does the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner have during meta-training – 64 (corresponding to the observed classes) or 100 (with 36 classes never seen)? Several such details require clarification.
- The plots in Figure 2 are, on their own, uninformative, and the accompanying discussion provides limited insight.

This is an interesting paper with compelling results, and appears to be a clear accept. However, the presentation of the ideas and the work itself could be substantially improved. I would gladly raise my score further if the writing is refined to enhance clarity and accessibility.
