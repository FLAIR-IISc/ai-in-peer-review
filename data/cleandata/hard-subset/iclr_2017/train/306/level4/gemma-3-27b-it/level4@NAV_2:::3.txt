This manuscript proposes to tackle few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a classic idea that has seen a renaissance in recent years. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each "example" includes a sequence of batches of "training" pairs, followed by a final "test" batch. The inputs at each "step" include the outputs of a "base learner" (e.g., training loss and gradients), as well as the base learner's current state (parameters). The paper applies an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, using the inner memory cells to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. In doing this, they note similarities between the respective update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. Updates to the LSTM meta-learner are computed based on the base learner's prediction loss for the final "test" batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all LSTM cells. The paper recreates the Mini-ImageNet dataset and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM is competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks) on 1- and 5-shot learning.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to few-shot learning, achieving competitive results and offering valuable insights into meta-learning with LSTMs.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of framing few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is compelling and well-executed. The performance on the Mini-ImageNet dataset is strong, demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed method. The authors' insights regarding LSTM initialization, weight sharing, and input normalization are particularly valuable for practitioners in this field. The approach differs significantly from existing methods like Matching Networks, which is a positive sign, suggesting a potentially different and complementary path towards solving the few-shot learning problem. The analogy drawn between LSTM updates and gradient descent is insightful and provides a strong rationale for the model design.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper presents strong technical results, the writing could be improved for clarity and accessibility. The task description in Section 2 is somewhat abstract and could benefit from a concrete example, perhaps using a simplified dataset like MNIST alongside a clarifying diagram. The discussion of the visualization results (Figure 3) lacks depth and doesn’t fully leverage the potential insights from the gate values. Expanding on these points would enhance the paper’s impact. Consider adding a small ablation study examining the sensitivity of the results to the various design choices (e.g., weight sharing, normalization).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Regarding the Mini-ImageNet dataset split (64/16/20 classes for training/validation/testing): Does this mean that only 64 classes are ever observed during meta-training, or are all 100 classes seen on average across all training batches? If only 64 classes are used during training, what is the size of the softmax layer in the base learner ConvNet during meta-training (64 or 100)?
2. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the meta-learner compared to training other few-shot learning models like Matching Networks? Are there significant differences in training time or memory requirements?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate for the meta-learner and the gradient clipping value? A brief ablation study exploring these parameters would be helpful.
4. In Algorithm 1, the learner's loss and gradient are computed on the *entire* training set Dtrain at each time step. Given the small size of these training sets (k=1 or k=5), is this efficient, or would mini-batching within Dtrain be beneficial?
5. In Section 3.3.1, you mention making the “gradient independence assumption.” Could you provide more intuition on the potential impact of *not* making this assumption, and why you believe it is a reasonable simplification in this context?
