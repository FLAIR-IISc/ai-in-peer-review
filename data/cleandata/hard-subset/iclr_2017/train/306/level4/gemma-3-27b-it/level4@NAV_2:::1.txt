This work presents an LSTM based meta-learning framework to learn the optimization algorithm of another learning algorithm (here a NN).

The paper is globally well written and the presentation of the main material is clear. The core idea – drawing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM update rule, and leveraging this to address the two key challenges of few-shot learning (1 - rapid acquisition of new knowledge, 2 - slower extraction of generalizable knowledge) – is intriguing. 

Several techniques reused from (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), such as parameter sharing and preprocessing, along with novel design choices (specifically the implementation of batch normalization), are well motivated. 

The experiments are convincing. This is a strong paper. My primary questions and suggestions for improvement are as follows:

1. Is it necessary to provide the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner? Were ablation studies conducted to determine if simpler combinations of these inputs are sufficient?

2. It would be beneficial to explore whether other architectural components of the learner network could also be learned through a similar meta-learning approach (e.g., number of neurons, type of units). Do you have any thoughts on the feasibility and potential benefits of this?

3. The related work section, particularly concerning meta-learning, could be more comprehensive. Meta-learning is a relatively mature field, and similar approaches have been explored to address this problem, even predating the use of LSTMs. Consider including:
    - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) which is highly relevant.
    - Work on using genetic programming to search for new learning rules for neural networks (e.g., S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
    - Research by Jürgen Schmidhuber, who has previously explored similar concepts; a thorough literature search is recommended to identify relevant publications.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. I believe the presented material is relevant and of significant interest to a broad audience at ICLR.
