This paper describes a novel approach to meta-learning by framing the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) update rule as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. The idea is original and represents a valuable contribution to research related to transfer learning. The paper is well-structured and presents a clear line of reasoning, though clarity could be enhanced in certain areas.

**Decision: Accept** with minor revisions. The work presents a promising new direction for meta-learning, demonstrates competitive performance against established techniques, and is generally well-executed.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The proposed LSTM-based meta-learner offers an insightful perspective on optimization algorithms within the few-shot learning paradigm. The core concept of learning an optimization algorithm, rather than relying on hand-designed methods, is compelling. The experimental results demonstrate that the approach achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques, specifically Matching Networks, particularly in the 5-shot setting. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, situating their contribution within the broader context of meta-learning and few-shot learning. The inclusion of an implementation detail regarding batch normalization and its adaptation to the meta-learning setting is commendable.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, several points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact. The analogy to LSTMs, while functional, feels somewhat forced. The connection to Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) is arguably more direct, given the absence of a separate cell state and input gate manipulation. Explicitly acknowledging this would strengthen the discussion.

The description of the data separation into meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets (Section 2) could benefit from a visual illustration. A diagram depicting the hierarchical structure of datasets within meta-sets would significantly improve understanding.

The experimental evaluation, while solid, could be enriched by investigating the learned parameters of the input (it) and forget (ft) gates. Analyzing how these gates adapt during training and across different datasets would provide valuable insights into the meta-learner’s optimization strategy. A more detailed analysis of the learned initial weights and their impact on convergence speed would also be beneficial.

Figure 2 currently provides limited insight and could be omitted or replaced with a more informative visualization, perhaps illustrating the flow of gradients and parameters during training.

A minor typographical error exists in Section 3.2: "This means each coordinate has it" should be "its."

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for choosing an LSTM architecture over a GRU, considering the structural similarities between the proposed model and a GRU?
2.  Would you be willing to share a visualization illustrating the hierarchical structure of meta-sets and datasets, as described in Section 2?
3.  Have you investigated the learned parameters of the input and forget gates (it and ft) to gain insights into the meta-learner’s optimization strategy? If so, could you share any observations?
4.  What is the computational overhead associated with this approach compared to standard fine-tuning or metric-learning methods? Are there scalability concerns when applying this to larger models or datasets?
