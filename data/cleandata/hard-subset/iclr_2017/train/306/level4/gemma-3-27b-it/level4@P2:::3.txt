This manuscript proposes a novel approach to few-shot learning by framing it as a sequential meta-learning problem. The authors leverage an LSTM-based meta-learner to learn the optimization algorithm for a base learner neural network classifier. This meta-learner receives the base learner’s training loss and gradients as input and updates the classifier’s parameters, effectively learning an initialization and update strategy tailored for rapid adaptation to new tasks with limited data. The method demonstrates competitive performance on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art techniques like Matching Networks.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling approach to few-shot learning with strong empirical results. While the writing could be improved for clarity and accessibility, the core ideas are innovative and the results are promising.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Insight:** The formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, and the realization of parallels between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent, offer a fresh perspective on the challenge. This connection is insightful and provides a strong motivation for the proposed approach.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results on Mini-ImageNet demonstrate that the proposed method achieves performance comparable to existing state-of-the-art techniques, indicating its effectiveness in addressing the few-shot learning problem. The fact that it performs well with a different architecture than Matching Networks (a CNN instead of embedding-based) is noteworthy.
*   **Practical Insights:** The paper provides valuable practical guidance on training LSTM meta-learners, including initialization strategies, weight sharing techniques, and normalization methods. These details are crucial for reproducibility and facilitate future research in this area.
*   **Strong Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of traditional deep learning in low-data regimes and highlights the potential benefits of few-shot learning, establishing a solid motivation for the proposed work.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting the acceptance decision):**

*   **Clarity of Writing:** The paper's writing style can be dense and difficult to follow, particularly for readers unfamiliar with meta-learning. Consider simplifying the language, adding more intuitive explanations, and breaking down complex concepts into smaller, more digestible parts.
*   **Task Description:** Section 2, describing the meta-learning formulation, is abstract and could benefit from a concrete example, perhaps using a simplified dataset like MNIST, accompanied by a diagram illustrating the sequence of tasks. This would greatly enhance accessibility.
*   **Mini-ImageNet Details:** The details of how the Mini-ImageNet dataset is utilized in the meta-learning setting are unclear. Specifically, regarding the 64/16/20 class split: are these classes fixed during training, or are different subsets of classes sampled for each meta-training batch? If fixed, how many outputs does the base learner’s softmax layer have during training (64 or 100)? Clarifying this will strengthen the experimental setup description.
*   **Figure 3 Interpretation:** The visualization of the meta-learner's behavior in Figure 3 is not particularly insightful. Provide a more detailed analysis of the observed gate values and explain their implications for the learned optimization strategy.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a concrete example of a single “episode” (dataset D) used in the meta-training process, illustrating the sequence of batches and the inputs/outputs at each step? A visual diagram would be helpful.
2.  Regarding the Mini-ImageNet dataset, is the set of 64 classes fixed during meta-training, or are different subsets of classes sampled for each batch? If fixed, what is the number of outputs for the softmax layer of the base learner during training?
3.  The paper mentions a "gradient independence assumption" (Section 3.3.1). Could you elaborate on the potential impact of this assumption on the accuracy and stability of the meta-learner? Are there any plans to explore alternative gradient calculation methods?
4.  In the experiments, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the LSTM hidden size, learning rate, and gradient clipping value? Were any extensive hyperparameter searches performed, and if so, could you provide some details?
5.  Could you comment on the computational cost associated with training the meta-learner LSTM compared to training the base learner directly? What are the main bottlenecks in terms of computational resources?
