This manuscript presents a novel approach to few-shot learning using meta-learning with LSTMs. The core idea is to formulate few-shot learning as a sequential learning problem, where each “episode” consists of a sequence of training batches followed by a test batch. An LSTM is used as the meta-learner, taking as input the base learner’s training loss, gradients, and parameters, and outputting updates to the base learner. Notably, the authors draw a compelling parallel between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent, offering a potentially insightful perspective on the meta-learning process. The approach achieves competitive results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, matching or surpassing the performance of Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016).

**Strengths:**

*   The framing of few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is both intuitive and promising. While the authors don’t originate the broad idea of meta-learning, they demonstrably advance the field with this specific implementation.
*   The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on 1- and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet learning, and importantly, utilizes a fundamentally different base learner (a convolutional neural network) than previous approaches like Matching Networks, which rely on learned embeddings and nearest neighbors.
*   The observation regarding the relationship between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent appears to be a novel and potentially valuable insight.
*   The paper provides practical guidance for implementing and training LSTM meta-learners, including effective initialization strategies, weight sharing techniques, and the importance of input normalization. While some of these insights build on existing work (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training), the consolidated discussion is highly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its presentation. The writing is dense and requires significant effort to understand. Section 2, in particular, presents an abstract description of the learning problem using specialized notation that may be inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with the sub-area. A concrete example, potentially using MNIST, alongside a clarifying diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks, would greatly improve accessibility.
*   The precise details of the N-class few-shot learning setup are unclear. Specifically, regarding the use of the Mini-ImageNet dataset (100 classes, divided into 64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing), it’s unclear whether the 64 training classes are *exclusively* observed during training, or whether all 100 classes are seen on average across batches. If the former is true, it raises questions about the softmax layer of the base learner: does it have 64 or 100 outputs during meta-training? Clarification on these details is needed.
*   The plots in Figure 2 offer limited insights and lack sufficient discussion to contextualize their findings.

**Overall:**

This is a compelling paper with promising results. The core idea is well-motivated and the experimental results are convincing. While the presentation could be substantially improved to enhance clarity and accessibility, the potential impact of this work warrants a strong recommendation for acceptance. Addressing the clarity issues, particularly the description of the experimental setup and the interpretation of Figure 2, would further strengthen the manuscript.
