In light of the authors' responsiveness and the updates to the manuscript – particularly to clarify the meta-learning task – I am updating my score to an 8.
-----
This manuscript tackles few-shot learning with neural networks by leveraging meta-learning, a concept that has recently experienced a resurgence. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each “episode” consists of a sequence of batches representing “training” data, followed by a final “test” batch. The inputs at each step include the outputs of a “base learner” (e.g., training loss and gradients) as well as the base learner’s current state (parameters). The paper applies an LSTM to this meta-learning problem, utilizing the inner memory cells of the second layer to directly model the updated parameters of the base learner. They observe similarities between the parameter update rules of LSTM memory cells and gradient descent. Updates to the LSTM meta-learner are computed based on the base learner’s prediction loss on the final “test” batch. The authors make several simplifying assumptions, such as sharing weights across all second layer cells (analogous to using a consistent learning rate for all parameters). The paper replicates the Mini-ImageNet dataset proposed in Vinyals et al. 2016 and demonstrates that the meta-learner LSTM achieves performance competitive with the current state-of-the-art (Matching Networks, Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning tasks.

Strengths:

- It is both intriguing and, in retrospect, natural to frame the few-shot learning problem as a sequential (meta-)learning problem. While the authors do not originate the broader idea of transferring learning across a series of tasks, their work represents a significant advancement in the field, though I cannot definitively assess its novelty due to my limited familiarity with the latest meta-learning research.
- The proposed approach is competitive with, and outperforms, Vinyals 2016 in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner used in this work (a simple ConvNet classifier) differs substantially from the nearest-neighbor-on-top-of-learned-embedding approach employed in Vinyals 2016. It is encouraging to see state-of-the-art results achieved using distinct approaches, rather than merely incremental improvements.
- To the best of my knowledge, the insight regarding the relationship between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent is novel and interesting.
- The paper provides several practical insights into designing and training an LSTM meta-learner, potentially facilitating replication and application of these ideas to new problems. These include proper initialization, weight sharing across dimensions, and the importance of normalizing/rescaling the loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. Some of these insights have been previously described (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training; assuming independence between meta-learner and base learner parameters when computing gradients with respect to the meta-learner), but the discussion remains valuable.

Weaknesses:

- The writing is occasionally opaque. Although the paper describes very interesting work, it is not a particularly accessible read. I required multiple readings (and consultation with related work) to fully grasp the overall learning problem. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is quite abstract and employs notation and terminology uncommon outside this specific subfield. The paper would benefit from a brief, concrete example (using MNIST, for instance), perhaps accompanied by a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning episodes. This would broaden its accessibility.
- Expanding on that point, the precise formulation of the N-class, few-shot learning problem remains unclear. Specifically, the Mini-ImageNet dataset comprises 100 labels, with 64/16/20 used for meta-training/validation/testing. Does this mean that only 64/100 classes are ever observed during meta-training? Or does each batch contain only 64/100 classes, but all 100 are observed on average during meta-training? If it is the former, how many outputs does the softmax layer of the ConvNet base learner have during meta-training? 64 (corresponding to the observed training classes) or 100 (with 36 classes never observed)? Several other details are similarly ambiguous (see my questions).
- The plots in Figure 2 are, on their own, not particularly informative, and the discussion provides limited insight into their meaning.

This is an interesting paper with convincing results, and appears to be a clear acceptance. However, the presentation of the ideas and the work could be improved. I would be happy to raise my score further if the writing is refined.
