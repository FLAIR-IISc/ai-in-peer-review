This work introduces a compelling LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn the optimization algorithm *for* another learning algorithm – in this case, a neural network. The paper is generally well-written and presents the core material with commendable clarity. The central idea – establishing a parallel between the Robbins-Monroe update rule and the LSTM update rule, and then leveraging this connection to address the key challenges of few-shot learning (rapid acquisition of new knowledge *and* slower extraction of generalizable, transferable knowledge) – is both insightful and intriguing.

The authors effectively integrate established techniques, such as parameter sharing and normalization (building on Andrychowicz et al., 2016), with novel design choices, notably a specific implementation of batch normalization. These choices are well-motivated and contribute to the overall effectiveness of the approach. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the promise of the proposed method. Overall, this is a strong submission. My primary concerns and questions are outlined below:

1. **Input Redundancy:** The meta-learner receives the loss, gradient, and parameters as input. While this provides a comprehensive view of the learning process, it raises the question of potential redundancy. Have the authors conducted ablation studies to determine if simpler input combinations – for example, excluding the parameters – are sufficient to achieve comparable performance? Understanding the contribution of each input component would strengthen the analysis.

2. **Architectural Meta-Learning:** This work focuses on learning the *optimization algorithm*. It would be interesting to consider extending this approach to learn other architectural components of the network, such as the number of neurons per layer or the type of activation units used. While this is a significant undertaking, I am curious about the authors' perspectives on the feasibility and potential benefits of such an extension.

3. **Related Work:** The related work section, while addressing meta-learning, feels somewhat limited in scope. Meta-learning is a relatively mature field, and prior work predates the recent resurgence driven by deep learning. To provide a more complete context, the authors should consider including earlier contributions. Specifically:
    * Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) is foundational work in this area.
    * The use of genetic programming to discover new learning rules for neural networks (Bengio, Y. Bengio, & Cloutier, 1994) offers a relevant historical perspective.
    * I strongly recommend investigating and citing relevant work by Jürgen Schmidhuber, who has made significant contributions to learning-to-learn paradigms. A more thorough review of the literature would enhance the paper’s impact and demonstrate a deeper understanding of the field’s history.

Despite these minor concerns, I find this to be a valuable and well-executed piece of research. The presented material is highly relevant to a broad audience at ICLR, and I believe it has the potential to stimulate further investigation into learning-to-optimize techniques. I recommend acceptance.
