This manuscript addresses few-shot learning using a meta-learning approach with neural networks, building upon a recently revitalized classic idea. The authors formulate few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each “example” consists of a series of batches used for “training” followed by a final “test” batch. The inputs at each step incorporate outputs from a “base learner” (e.g., training loss and gradients) as well as its current parameters. An LSTM is applied to this meta-learning problem, with the LSTM’s second-layer memory cells modeling the base learner's parameter updates directly, leveraging a noted similarity between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is trained based on the base learner’s prediction loss on the final “test” batch. Several simplifying assumptions are made, including weight sharing across all second-layer cells, analogous to a uniform learning rate across parameters. The paper reproduces the Mini-ImageNet dataset (Vinyals et al 2016) and demonstrates that the LSTM meta-learner achieves competitive performance with current state-of-the-art methods like Matching Networks (Vinyals 2016) on 1- and 5-shot learning tasks.

Strengths:
- Framing few-shot learning as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is insightful and, in retrospect, intuitive. While the authors did not originate this general idea, their work represents a significant advancement in the field, though I am unable to definitively assess its novelty due to my limited familiarity with the most recent meta-learning literature.
- The proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Vinyals 2016 in 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The base learner, a simple ConvNet classifier, differs significantly from the learned-embedding, nearest-neighbor approach used in Vinyals 2016. Achieving state-of-the-art results with fundamentally different approaches is encouraging.
- The observation regarding the relationship between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent appears novel and is a valuable contribution.
- The paper provides practical guidance for designing and training LSTM meta-learners, potentially facilitating replication and application of these ideas to new problems. This includes recommendations for initialization, weight sharing, and normalization/rescaling of loss, gradient, and parameter inputs. While some of these insights have been previously described (e.g., simulating test conditions during meta-training, assuming independence between meta-learner and base learner parameters when calculating gradients), the discussion is nonetheless useful.

Weaknesses:
- The writing is occasionally opaque and hinders readability. Despite presenting interesting work, the paper is not particularly accessible. Understanding the core learning problem required multiple readings and consultation with related work. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is overly abstract and utilizes specialized notation and terminology. A brief, concrete example (perhaps based on MNIST) accompanied by a diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks would greatly improve clarity and broaden the paper's audience.
- The precise definition of the N-class, few-shot learning problem remains unclear. Specifically, given that Mini-ImageNet contains 100 labels, with 64/16/20 used for meta-training/validation/testing, it is unclear whether only 64 classes are observed during meta-training, or if all 100 are observed on average across batches. If the former, what is the size of the softmax layer in the ConvNet base learner during meta-training – 64 (reflecting only observed classes) or 100 (with 36 classes never encountered)? Several other details require clarification (see question).
- The plots in Figure 2 provide limited information and lack substantial discussion. 

This is a compelling paper with convincing results, suggesting a clear acceptance. However, improving the presentation of the ideas and work would be beneficial. I will increase my score if the writing is improved.
