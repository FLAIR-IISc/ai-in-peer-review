This paper introduces a meta-learning framework based on LSTMs, designed to learn the optimization algorithm employed by another learning algorithm – in this case, a neural network. 

The paper is well-written overall, with a clear and concise presentation of the core ideas. The central contribution – establishing a connection between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update mechanism, and leveraging this to address the key requirements of few-shot learning (rapid acquisition of new information, and gradual extraction of generalizable knowledge) – is a compelling one. 

The authors provide strong justification for both the re-implementation of established techniques from prior work (such as parameter sharing and normalization, following Andrychowicz et al. 2016) and their novel design decisions, including the specific implementation of batch normalization.

The experimental results are persuasive and support the claims made in the paper. This is a strong submission. However, I have a few questions and suggestions for improvement:

1.  Is providing the meta-learner with the loss, gradient, *and* parameters as input strictly necessary? Have ablative studies been performed to determine whether simpler input combinations might achieve comparable performance?
2.  Could other architectural aspects of the network, such as the number of neurons or the type of activation units, also be learned through a similar meta-learning approach? What are the authors’ thoughts on extending the framework in this direction?
3.  The related work section, particularly concerning meta-learning, could be expanded. Meta-learning has a substantial history, and several earlier approaches have tackled similar problems, even without utilizing LSTMs. For example:
    *   Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) is directly relevant to this topic.
    *   The application of genetic programming for discovering new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994) should be discussed.
    *   I suggest a thorough search for relevant work by Jürgen Schmidhuber, who has likely contributed to this area.  Updating the related work section to reflect this broader context would be beneficial.

In conclusion, I find this to be a valuable and interesting paper. The material presented is highly relevant to the ICLR audience, and I believe it would be a worthwhile addition to the conference program.
