This work introduces an LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn the optimization algorithm for another learning algorithm (in this case, a neural network). 
The paper is well-written overall, and the presentation of the core ideas is clear. The central contribution – establishing a parallel between the Robbins-Monro update rule and the LSTM update rule, and leveraging this connection to address the key requirements of few-shot learning (1 – rapid acquisition of new knowledge, 2 – gradual extraction of generalizable knowledge) – is particularly intriguing. 
The authors effectively motivate several techniques adapted from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, alongside novel design choices, including a specific implementation of batch normalization.
The experimental results are compelling, and this is a strong paper. My main concerns and questions are as follows:

1. Is providing the loss, gradient, and parameters as input to the meta-learner necessary? Have ablation studies been conducted to determine whether simpler input combinations are sufficient?
2. Would it be possible to learn other architectural aspects of the network – such as the number of neurons or the type of units – using a similar approach? What are your thoughts on this potential extension?
3. The related work section, particularly concerning meta-learning, feels somewhat limited in scope. Meta-learning is a relatively established field, and related approaches have previously been investigated to solve the same problem, even without utilizing LSTMs. For example:
     - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) directly addresses this topic.
     - The application of genetic programming to discover new learning rules for neural networks (S. Bengio, Y. Bengio, and J. Cloutier, 1994).
     - I suspect Jürgen Schmidhuber has also made relevant contributions; please investigate and update the related work section accordingly.

Overall, I recommend this paper. I believe the material presented is highly relevant and will be of interest to a broad audience at ICLR.
