This manuscript addresses few-shot learning using a meta-learning approach with neural networks, building on a recently revitalized area of research. The authors frame few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each instance comprises a series of batches representing “training” data, culminating in a final “test” batch. The input at each step incorporates the outputs of a “base learner” (including loss and gradients) alongside its current parameters. An LSTM network is employed as the meta-learner, leveraging the inner memory cells of its second layer to directly model the base learner’s parameter updates, drawing a noted parallel between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent. The LSTM meta-learner is trained based on the base learner’s prediction loss on the final test batch. Several simplifying assumptions are made, notably weight sharing across all second layer cells, mirroring a uniform learning rate for all parameters. The authors validate their approach on a recreated Mini-ImageNet dataset, demonstrating competitive performance with current state-of-the-art methods like Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) in both 1- and 5-shot learning scenarios.

**Strengths:**

- The conceptualization of few-shot learning as a sequential (meta-)learning problem is insightful and, in retrospect, quite natural. While the general idea of persistent learning isn’t novel, the authors contribute to the advancement of the state-of-the-art, though I lack comprehensive knowledge of recent meta-learning developments to definitively assess the novelty.
- The proposed method achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, performance to Vinyals et al. (2016) on the 1- and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet experiments.
- The use of a conventional ConvNet classifier as the base learner distinguishes this work from the embedding-based approach of Vinyals et al. (2016). The demonstration of strong results with differing architectural choices is encouraging.
- The observed connection between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent appears novel and is of inherent interest.
- The paper provides valuable practical guidance for designing and training LSTM meta-learners, facilitating replication and application of these ideas to new problems. This includes recommendations for initialization, weight sharing, and loss/gradient/parameter normalization/rescaling. While some of these insights have been previously reported (simulating test conditions during meta-training, assuming independence of meta-learner and base learner parameters), the discussion remains beneficial.

**Weaknesses:**

- The writing style is occasionally dense and lacks clarity. Despite presenting compelling work, the paper is not easily accessible. Understanding the core learning problem required multiple readings and external reference materials. The task description in Section 2 (Page 2) is highly abstract and utilizes specialized notation. A concise, concrete example (e.g., based on MNIST) coupled with a clarifying diagram illustrating a sequence of few-shot learning tasks would substantially improve comprehension for a broader audience.
- The precise formulation of the N-class, few-shot learning problem remains ambiguous. Specifically, regarding the Mini-ImageNet dataset (100 labels, split 64/16/20 for meta-training/validation/testing), it is unclear whether only 64 classes are observed during meta-training, or if each batch contains a subset of classes, but all 100 classes are encountered on average. If the former, what is the size of the softmax layer in the base learner during meta-training – 64 (representing only trained classes) or 100 (with 36 classes never observed)? Numerous details of this nature require clarification.
- The plots in Figure 2 lack sufficient explanatory context and the accompanying discussion provides limited insight. 

Overall, this is a promising paper with convincing results. While the presentation could be improved, it appears to be a clear accept, and I am increasing my score accordingly, contingent on improvements to the clarity and accessibility of the writing.
