This paper proposes a meta-learning approach utilizing an LSTM to learn an update rule for training a neural network classifier, aiming to address the challenges of few-shot learning. The core idea is to frame the optimization process as an LSTM cell update, allowing the meta-learner to capture both short-term and long-term knowledge across tasks, and to learn a beneficial initial parameterization for the learner.

**Reject.** While the idea of using an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to demonstrate its effectiveness and novelty compared to existing approaches. The results are competitive with, but not convincingly superior to, established methods like Matching Networks, and the gains are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the proposed approach. The experimental evaluation feels somewhat cursory, and the reliance on a re-implementation of baselines introduces potential for bias.

My primary concern lies with the limited validation and the lack of a compelling argument for why this LSTM-based meta-optimizer is fundamentally better than existing methods. The paper acknowledges the Gradient Independence Assumption, which is a significant simplification, and the potential impact of this assumption on the learned optimization strategy is not thoroughly discussed. The visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior, while present, doesn't offer deep insight into *what* the LSTM is learning beyond a general observation about forget gate behavior.

Further, the paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from Andrychowicz et al. (2016). While the authors highlight the difference in application (few-shot learning vs. large-scale classification), the core concept of learning an optimizer with an LSTM is shared. The paper needs to more explicitly articulate what improvements or novel contributions are made beyond adapting this existing idea to a new domain. The claim of learning a task-common initialization is also not particularly strong – transfer learning already achieves this, and the benefit isn’t clearly demonstrated to be superior. The comparison with a fine-tuning baseline is also perplexing – a properly regularized fine-tuning approach should arguably outperform the proposed method.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be helpful to include ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., the forget gate, input gate, initial parameter learning).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the meta-learner to different hyperparameters.
*   **Scalability:** The computational cost of training the meta-learner is not discussed. How does this approach scale to larger datasets or more complex learner networks?
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While not strictly required, some theoretical justification for why an LSTM is a suitable model for learning an optimizer would strengthen the paper.
*   **More robust baseline comparison:** Please provide a direct comparison with the original implementation of Matching Networks and a more thorough hyperparameter search for the fine-tuning baseline. Also, consider comparing against more recent state-of-the-art few-shot learning methods.
*   **Further elaboration on the gradient independence assumption:** What is the potential impact of this assumption on the quality of the learned optimization strategy? Would incorporating gradient information improve performance, and if not, why?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The authors mention a simplifying assumption regarding gradient independence. Could you elaborate on the potential errors introduced by this assumption, and did you experiment with methods to mitigate its impact?
2.  How does the computational cost of meta-training scale with the complexity of the learner network?
3.  What specific aspects of the learned optimization strategy (as captured by the LSTM gates) distinguish it from standard gradient descent or other optimization algorithms? Can you provide a more detailed interpretation of the visualized gate values?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the meta-learner to the choice of LSTM architecture (e.g., number of layers, hidden size)?
5.  Considering the similarities to Andrychowicz et al. (2016), what specific innovations or improvements does this work offer beyond adapting their LSTM optimizer to the few-shot learning setting?
