This paper proposes a meta-learning approach to few-shot learning, framing the optimization process itself as a learning problem. The authors present an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier. The core idea—using an LSTM to generate updates for the learner—is novel and well-motivated, addressing the challenges of gradient-based optimization in low-data regimes. The paper is clearly written and provides a good overview of related work.

**Reject**

While the approach is interesting and potentially promising, the experimental results presented do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods, particularly Matching Networks. The performance is competitive but not clearly superior, and the ablation studies or sensitivity analysis are lacking to provide deeper insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the reliance on the gradient independence assumption, while acknowledged, raises concerns about the validity of the learned optimization strategy.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The results in Table 1 show that the proposed method achieves comparable, but not demonstrably better, performance than Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset. The confidence intervals overlap, suggesting that the observed differences may not be statistically significant. The claim of “much better” performance for 5-shot classification is not strongly supported by the data. A clearer demonstration of superiority is needed, perhaps through more extensive experimentation on different datasets or with larger model sizes.

2. **Gradient Independence Assumption:** The simplification of ignoring the dependency of gradients on the meta-learner's parameters during training is a significant concern. While the authors acknowledge this, it's not clear how much this simplification might affect the quality of the learned optimization strategy.  Ignoring this dependency could lead to a suboptimal meta-learner, hindering its ability to effectively train the learner network. The justification for this assumption—that these contributions are "not important"—is weak and requires further investigation. 

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the model. For example, how much does the parameter sharing mechanism contribute to performance? What is the impact of the LSTM architecture (e.g., comparing it to a GRU)? What is the effect of different preprocessing techniques?  Without such analyses, it’s difficult to determine which aspects of the proposed approach are most critical.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** Consider providing some theoretical justification for why an LSTM is a suitable choice for learning an optimization algorithm. While the analogy to the LSTM cell update is intuitive, a more formal argument would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, LSTM size, and number of updates. This would provide insights into the robustness of the approach.
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of training the meta-learner compared to other few-shot learning methods. Meta-learning approaches can be computationally expensive, and it’s important to understand the trade-offs between performance and efficiency.
* **Visualization of Learned Updates:** The visualization of the meta-learner's gate values (Figure 3) is a good start, but could be expanded. Visualizing the actual updates (i.e., the changes to the learner's parameters) suggested by the meta-learner could provide a more intuitive understanding of its optimization strategy.
* **Comparison to other Meta-Learning methods:** Include a comparison to other recent meta-learning algorithms, beyond Matching Networks. This would position the proposed approach within the broader context of the field.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the gradient independence assumption? Have you explored any methods to mitigate its potential negative effects, such as using approximations of the second derivatives?
2.  What is the computational overhead of training the LSTM meta-learner compared to a standard fine-tuning approach?
3.  The paper mentions that the meta-learner is explicitly trained to do a fixed number of updates.  What happens if the number of updates changes during meta-testing? Does the model generalize to different update schedules?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the fine-tuning baseline performs worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline? Is this a consistent observation across different datasets?
5.  Are there any insights into *what* the meta-learner is learning? For example, is it learning to adjust the learning rate dynamically based on the loss landscape, or is it learning more complex optimization strategies?
