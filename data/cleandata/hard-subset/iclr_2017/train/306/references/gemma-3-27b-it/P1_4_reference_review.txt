## Review for ICLR 2024

**Paper Title:** Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenge of few-shot learning, arguing that standard gradient-based optimization struggles in this regime due to a lack of task-specific initialization and slow convergence. The authors propose a meta-learning approach where an LSTM-based meta-learner *learns* an optimization algorithm to train a learner neural network classifier.  Crucially, the meta-learner not only adapts the learner’s parameters but also learns a beneficial common initialization, intended to accelerate learning across a set of related tasks. The core idea is elegantly framed as mimicking the update rule within an LSTM cell, allowing the meta-learner to capture both short-term and long-term dependencies in the learning process. The paper demonstrates competitive performance on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, showing improvements over baselines and comparable results to state-of-the-art metric learning methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a significant problem in machine learning – learning with limited data – and presents a novel and well-motivated approach. The results are promising and demonstrate the potential of meta-learning to learn effective optimization strategies.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The introduction convincingly argues why standard optimization techniques fail in the few-shot setting and clearly articulates the need for a meta-learning approach. The connection to human learning is also well made.
* **Novelty and Insightful Approach:** The framing of the optimization process as an LSTM update rule is a clever and insightful idea. It provides a natural way to incorporate both short-term gradient information and long-term meta-knowledge.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on Mini-ImageNet demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with, and in some cases outperforms, existing approaches.  The ablation with the fine-tuning baseline highlights the benefit of the meta-learned initialization.  The visualization of the meta-learner’s gates provides some initial insight into the learned optimization strategy, although further analysis would be beneficial (see feedback).
* **Well-written and Structured:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and results. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is helpful.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Deeper Analysis of the Meta-Learner:** While the visualization of the gates is a good starting point, a more thorough analysis of *what* the meta-learner is actually learning would be valuable. For instance, are there specific patterns in the gate values that correlate with successful learning? Is the learned learning rate schedule significantly different from standard schedules (e.g., step decay)?  Ablation studies focusing on specific components of the LSTM (e.g., removing the forget gate) could also provide more insights.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of training the meta-learner. It would be helpful to provide some quantitative comparison with other few-shot learning methods, particularly regarding training time and memory requirements.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While ADAM is used with a specified learning rate, a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity for the meta-learner itself would be useful.  How robust is the performance to changes in the LSTM size, the number of updates (T), or other key parameters?
* **Generalization to Other Datasets:** The experiments are limited to Mini-ImageNet. Evaluating the approach on other few-shot learning datasets (e.g., tieredImageNet, CIFAR-FS) would strengthen the conclusions and demonstrate the generalizability of the method.
* **Clarification on Gradient Independence Assumption:** While the authors acknowledge the gradient independence assumption, a brief discussion of the potential impact of this simplification on the accuracy of the meta-learned gradients would be beneficial.  Are there any theoretical or empirical indications of how this assumption might affect performance?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the motivation behind the specific architectural choices for the LSTM meta-learner (e.g., the number of layers, the hidden state size)?
* What is the typical training time for the meta-learner on Mini-ImageNet, compared to training a standard classifier from scratch or fine-tuning a pre-trained model?
* Do you observe any differences in the learned optimization strategy for different numbers of shots (k = 1 vs. k = 5)?
* How sensitive is the performance of the meta-learner to the specific preprocessing method used for the gradients and losses? Have you experimented with alternative normalization techniques?
* In the visualization of the meta-learner's gates (Figure 3), are the plots averaged over many datasets? If so, what is the variance? Could you show examples of gate values for individual datasets to highlight the variability?
