This paper proposes a meta-learning approach using an LSTM to learn an update rule for a learner neural network classifier in the few-shot learning setting. The core idea is to frame the optimization process as an LSTM cell update, allowing the meta-learner to capture both short-term and long-term knowledge, and learn a task-common initialization for the learner.

**Decision:** Reject. While the idea is interesting and the motivation is clear, the experimental results are not sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance. The performance is competitive with, but not significantly better than, existing methods like Matching Networks, and the ablation studies or analysis justifying the design choices (e.g., the LSTM architecture, preprocessing steps) are limited.  The reliance on results from a re-implemented dataset and baseline methods further weakens the claim of improved performance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The paper demonstrates competitive but not superior performance compared to Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset. The advantage is only observed in the 5-shot setting, and even then, the confidence intervals overlap with Matching Networks in the 1-shot setting. This suggests that the proposed method doesn't offer a substantial improvement over existing approaches.
2. **Implementation Details & Reproducibility:** The authors acknowledge reimplementing Matching Networks and creating their own version of the Mini-ImageNet dataset. This raises concerns about the direct comparability of the results. While acknowledging this, the impact of these choices on the conclusions isn't thoroughly discussed.  Reproducibility is also a concern, as details about the exact splits of the Mini-ImageNet dataset are not fully provided.
3. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks comprehensive ablation studies to justify the specific design choices of the meta-learner. For example, why an LSTM was chosen over other recurrent architectures (e.g., GRU), or the impact of different preprocessing techniques.  The claim that initializing the forget gate bias to a high value is important is stated but not thoroughly demonstrated through ablation.  A more detailed exploration of the different components of the model would significantly strengthen the paper.
4. **Visualization Interpretation:** While the visualization of the meta-learner's gate values offers some insight, the interpretation is somewhat subjective and lacks quantitative analysis.  The authors state there's variability between datasets but don't provide metrics to support this observation or connect it to the performance.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:**  Consider comparing against more recent and stronger few-shot learning baselines beyond Matching Networks.  Meta-OptNet or ProtoNets, for example, may provide a more challenging comparison.
* **Detailed Dataset Information:**  Provide a clear description of how the Mini-ImageNet dataset was constructed, including the exact class splits used for meta-training, validation, and testing. Ideally, release the splits for reproducibility.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct a series of ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the model. This could involve varying the LSTM architecture, removing the preprocessing steps, and experimenting with different initialization strategies.
* **Quantitative Analysis of Gate Values:** Develop quantitative metrics to analyze the behavior of the meta-learner's gate values. For instance, calculate the variance of the forget and input gate values across different datasets.
* **Error Analysis:** Include an error analysis to identify the types of examples where the proposed method struggles. This could provide insights into potential areas for improvement.
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of training and evaluating the meta-learner compared to other methods.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, LSTM hidden size, and number of updates.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Given the reliance on a re-implemented dataset and baseline, how confident are you that the observed improvements are not due to differences in implementation details?
2.  Could you elaborate on the reasons for choosing an LSTM over other recurrent architectures like GRUs?  Have you experimented with GRUs, and if so, what were the results?
3.  How does the performance of the meta-learner change when the gradient independence assumption is relaxed, and second-order gradients are considered (even if approximately)?
4.   Can you provide more details on the datasets used for meta-training, validation, and testing? Specifically, what criteria were used to select the classes for each set?
5.  Beyond the qualitative visualization, are there any other ways to interpret or understand the learned optimization strategy of the meta-learner?
