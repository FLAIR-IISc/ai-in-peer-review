This paper proposes a meta-learning approach to few-shot learning, framing the optimization process itself as a learning problem. Specifically, the authors present an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to update the parameters of a learner network, aiming to find a beneficial common initialization and an effective optimization strategy for a set of few-shot tasks. The motivation, stemming from the limitations of gradient-based optimization in low-data regimes, is well articulated, and the connection to human learning is noted.

**Reject.** While the idea of learning to optimize is interesting, this paper presents a relatively complex method without sufficiently compelling evidence of its effectiveness. The performance gains over strong baselines, particularly Matching Networks, are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity, and the ablation studies (though present) do not fully dissect the contributions of the various components. The reliance on a simplifying “gradient independence assumption” also raises concerns about the validity of the results.

The core argument rests on the LSTM meta-learner's ability to learn a task-common initialization and an effective update rule. However, the provided experimental results (Mini-ImageNet) show only marginal improvements compared to existing approaches. The claim of competitive performance needs stronger substantiation. While the authors acknowledge differences in results compared to the original Matching Networks paper due to re-implementation, the fact that their results are *not consistently better* given the proposed method's sophistication is a significant weakness. The observation that fine-tuning *worsens* baseline performance, while intriguing, does not necessarily validate the meta-learning approach; it simply highlights a potential issue with standard fine-tuning in this setting. 

Furthermore, the visualization of the meta-learner's behavior (gate values) is descriptive but doesn't offer deep insights into *why* the method works (or doesn’t work better). Stating the forget gate adopts a “simple weight decay strategy” feels like a post-hoc rationalization rather than a clear understanding of the learned dynamics. The preprocessing step described seems rather ad-hoc and its impact isn't thoroughly explored. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters of the meta-learner itself (e.g. LSTM size, learning rate).

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **More Extensive Baselines:** The comparison should include more recent and relevant meta-learning methods beyond Matching Networks. Consider Prototypical Networks or Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML).
*   **Ablation Studies:** More targeted ablation studies are needed. For example, what is the performance impact of parameter sharing in the LSTM? What happens if the forget gate is fixed to 1? How sensitive is performance to the choice of preprocessing method?
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While a full theoretical analysis might be beyond the scope of the paper, exploring the convergence properties of the LSTM-based optimizer, or providing insights into its inductive bias, could strengthen the work.
*   **Scalability:** The paper does not address the computational cost of meta-training and meta-testing. How does the complexity of the LSTM meta-learner scale with the size of the learner network and the number of tasks?
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide a detailed error analysis to understand where the proposed method struggles. What types of images or classes are most often misclassified?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The "gradient independence assumption" seems significant. Have you explored the impact of including this dependency in the meta-learner's gradients, even with the increased computational cost? Could this explain the limited performance gains?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter search performed for both the learner and meta-learner? Were any automated hyperparameter optimization techniques employed?
3.  You mention that the meta-learner learns a task-common initialization. Is this initialization consistently better than random initialization across different datasets? Can you quantify this improvement?
4.  The preprocessing step seems crucial. Why was the value *p=10* chosen? Have you experimented with different values, and how does the performance change?
5.  Are there any insights into *why* the fine-tuning baseline performs worse than the nearest neighbor baseline? Is this a common observation in the few-shot learning setting?
