## Review of "LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes a meta-learning approach to address the challenges of few-shot learning. The authors frame the problem as learning an optimizer – specifically, an LSTM – that can efficiently train a learner network given limited data. The LSTM meta-learner learns to update the learner’s parameters, effectively capturing both a good initialization and a task-specific optimization strategy. The approach leverages the similarity between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent updates, and incorporates parameter sharing and preprocessing techniques to manage the complexity of optimizing a high-dimensional parameter space.  The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, outperforming strong baselines and achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art metric learning methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning. The results are promising, demonstrating competitive performance. The writing is clear, and the paper is well-structured. However, some aspects of the experimental setup and analysis could be strengthened.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of learning an optimizer using an LSTM is a compelling one. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of standard optimization techniques in the few-shot setting and provide a strong rationale for their meta-learning approach. The connection drawn between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent is insightful.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model is well-defined and the implementation details (parameter sharing, preprocessing, training procedure) appear reasonable and well-justified. The use of the gradient independence assumption, while a simplification, is acknowledged and commonly employed in related work. The choice of LSTM as the meta-learner is logical, given its ability to model sequential dependencies in the optimization process.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on Mini-ImageNet are encouraging.  The comparison against strong baselines, including Matching Networks, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The analysis of the learned gate values provides some qualitative insight into the meta-learner's optimization strategy.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its implementation. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and visualizations is helpful.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:**  While the results are good, more detailed ablation studies would strengthen the paper. Specifically, it would be valuable to see the impact of:
    * Parameter sharing: How does performance change without parameter sharing? What is the effect of different parameter sharing strategies?
    * Preprocessing: What is the sensitivity of the results to the preprocessing parameters (p=10)?
    * LSTM architecture: How does the performance change with different LSTM sizes (number of layers, hidden units)?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions hyperparameter selection using the meta-validation set.  A more thorough discussion of the hyperparameters used, and their impact on performance, would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to Other Meta-Learning Approaches:**  The paper primarily compares to metric learning methods.  It would be helpful to include a comparison to other recent meta-learning approaches, such as Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) or Reptile, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.  Even a brief discussion of how the approach differs and potentially complements these techniques would be valuable.
* **Generalization to Other Datasets:** The experiments are currently limited to the Mini-ImageNet dataset. Evaluating the method on other few-shot learning benchmarks (e.g., tieredImageNet, Omniglot) would demonstrate its generalizability.
* **Visualization Enhancement:** While the visualization of the gate values is helpful, further insights could be gained by visualizing the learned learning rate (output of the input gate) over time for different datasets.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the Gradient Independence Assumption:**  How sensitive are the results to this assumption? Have you explored techniques to mitigate its potential impact, such as using a higher-order approximation?
* **Choice of LSTM Size:** What motivated the choice of a 2-layer LSTM with the specific hidden unit size? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed to determine the optimal LSTM architecture?
* **Meta-Testing Stability:**  During meta-testing, you collect batch statistics per dataset and then discard them. Have you observed any instability or performance fluctuations due to this process? Could using a running average across datasets during testing improve stability without significantly compromising performance?
* **Scalability:** How does the computational cost of the meta-learner scale with the size of the learner network? Do you foresee any scalability challenges when applying this approach to larger, more complex models?
* **Transferability of the Meta-Learner:**  Could a meta-learner trained on one set of tasks (e.g., Mini-ImageNet) be effectively transferred to a different set of tasks with different classes or modalities?  Have you investigated the transferability of the learned optimization strategy?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of few-shot learning. Addressing the points outlined in the "Additional Feedback" section will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
