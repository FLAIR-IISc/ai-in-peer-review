This paper proposes a meta-learning approach to few-shot learning, framing the problem as learning an optimizer via an LSTM. The LSTM meta-learner learns to update the parameters of a learner neural network, aiming to discover a good initialization and an effective update rule for rapid adaptation to new tasks with limited data. The core idea of representing parameter updates as LSTM cell updates is novel and intuitively appealing.

**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the experimental results are not compelling enough to warrant acceptance. While the authors show competitive performance with Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, the reported results do not demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over existing methods, and the implementation details (re-implementations of baselines) introduce uncertainty about a fair comparison. Furthermore, the visualization of the learned optimizer, while interesting, does not provide strong evidence of a qualitatively superior optimization strategy.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear presentation of the meta-learning formulation and the innovative approach of utilizing an LSTM to model the optimization process. The motivation for addressing the challenges of few-shot learning with a meta-learned optimizer is well articulated. The connection between gradient descent updates and LSTM cell updates is a clever observation. The inclusion of parameter sharing and preprocessing techniques, borrowed from prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), shows awareness of relevant implementation challenges.

However, the lack of statistically significant improvements over existing methods is a major concern. The authors acknowledge that their results do not exactly match those reported by Vinyals et al. (2016) due to their reimplementation, which raises questions about the fairness of the comparison. The impact of the gradient independence assumption (Section 3.3.1) on the performance is not thoroughly investigated. While the authors mention the initialization strategies for the LSTM meta-learner, a more detailed ablation study exploring the sensitivity of the model to different initialization schemes would be beneficial. Additionally, the claim that the fine-tuned baseline performs worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline is unexpected and warrants further investigation. The authors attribute this to overfitting, but a more detailed analysis of the model’s behavior during fine-tuning would strengthen this conclusion. The visualization of the learned optimizer (Figure 3) is descriptive but lacks quantitative analysis to support claims about the learned optimization strategy.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the impact of key components, such as parameter sharing, preprocessing, and the gradient independence assumption.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report confidence intervals or p-values to demonstrate the statistical significance of the performance improvements.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, the number of LSTM layers, and the hidden state size.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Compare the proposed approach to more recent and stronger baselines in the few-shot learning literature.
*   **Analysis of Learned Optimizer:** Develop quantitative metrics to evaluate the quality of the learned optimization strategy. For example, analyze the convergence speed and stability of the learner network under the guidance of the meta-learned optimizer.
*   **Explain the choice of 12 and 5 updates:** Why were these specific numbers of updates chosen for the 1-shot and 5-shot settings respectively? What experiments were done to evaluate this choice?
*   **Impact of Batch Normalization:** A more detailed discussion of the rationale behind the modified batch normalization strategy and its impact on performance is warranted.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the gradient independence assumption, and discuss potential ways to mitigate its impact?
2.  What experiments did you conduct to ensure that the reimplemented baselines (Matching Networks, fine-tuning baseline) are comparable to their original implementations?
3.  Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the fine-tuning baseline and explain why it performs worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline? What regularization techniques were explored?
4.  What is the computational cost of training the meta-learner compared to other meta-learning approaches?
5.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the LSTM architecture (number of layers, hidden state size) and the rationale behind it? Were other architectures (e.g., GRUs) considered?
