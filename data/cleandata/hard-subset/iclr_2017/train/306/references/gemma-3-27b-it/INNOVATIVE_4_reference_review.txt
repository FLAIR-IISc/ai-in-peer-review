This paper tackles the challenging problem of few-shot learning, specifically addressing the difficulties gradient-based optimization faces with limited data. The authors propose a novel meta-learning approach utilizing an LSTM to learn an update rule for training a learner classifier, effectively learning a task-common initialization and optimization strategy. This is a bold and interesting idea, framing optimization itself as a learnable process within a meta-learning framework.

**Reject.** While the core idea is highly innovative and promising, the paper feels incomplete in its validation and lacks a compelling demonstration of superiority over existing, well-established methods like Matching Networks, particularly in the 1-shot setting. The results, while competitive, aren’t decisively better, and the claims of learning a truly *optimal* initialization feel overstated given the limited experimentation.

The strength of this paper lies in its novelty. The formulation of the problem as learning an optimization algorithm with an LSTM is a significant conceptual leap. The idea of capturing both short-term (within-task) and long-term (across-task) knowledge in the meta-learner is appealing. The connection to LSTM cell updates provides a neat theoretical justification for the model architecture. The analysis of the learned gate values, although preliminary, offers some insight into the meta-learner’s behavior. However, the empirical evaluation doesn't fully convince. The dataset creation process (re-implementing Mini-Imagenet) introduces potential inconsistencies with prior work, making direct comparisons difficult. While the authors acknowledge this, it weakens the impact of the results. The ablation studies are minimal; for example, the impact of the parameter sharing strategy isn’t thoroughly explored. The claim that fine-tuning baselines *worse* than nearest neighbors due to overfitting seems surprising and deserves further investigation. A more comprehensive comparison, including more recent and stronger few-shot learning methods (e.g., Prototypical Networks, Relation Networks) is needed. The Gradient Independence Assumption is also a significant simplification, and the authors should elaborate on the potential impact of this assumption on the learned optimization strategy. The results, while reasonable, don't *justify* the complexity of the proposed method relative to simpler approaches.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity of the Objective:** The objective function being optimized isn't explicitly stated. Is it simply the test loss after T updates? Clarifying this would improve understanding.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the performance to hyperparameters of the meta-learner (e.g., LSTM size, learning rate)? A sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of training the meta-learner. Is this approach computationally feasible for larger datasets or more complex learner networks?
*   **Visualization of Learned Initialization:** Visualize the learned initial weights of the learner classifier. Does the meta-learner learn meaningful initialization patterns?
*   **Ablation Study on LSTM components:** A more targeted ablation study on the different components of the LSTM meta-learner (forget gate, input gate, cell state) would help identify which aspects are most crucial for performance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the Gradient Independence Assumption and discuss potential ways to mitigate its impact?
2.  What is the computational overhead of the meta-learning process compared to standard fine-tuning?
3.  How does the learned initialization compare to other initialization strategies (e.g., Xavier, Kaiming)? Have you visualized the learned weights to understand if there is any discernable pattern?
4.  You mention the fine-tuning baseline overfitting. What regularization techniques were explored to mitigate this issue?
5.  Why did you choose to reimplement Mini-Imagenet, and how do you ensure consistency with previous work? Can you provide statistics on your split to assure comparability?
