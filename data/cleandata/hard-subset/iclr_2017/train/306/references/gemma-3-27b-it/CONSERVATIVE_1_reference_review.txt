This paper proposes a meta-learning approach using an LSTM to learn an update rule for training a learner neural network classifier in the few-shot learning regime. The core idea is to frame the optimization process as an LSTM cell update, allowing the meta-learner to capture both short-term and long-term knowledge for rapid adaptation to new tasks. The authors aim to learn a task-common initialization for the learner, improving generalization from limited data.

**Reject.** While the idea of using an LSTM to meta-learn an optimizer is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims and falls short of demonstrating a clear advantage over established methods. The experimental results, while competitive with Matching Networks, are not decisively superior, and the reliance on a self-created Mini-Imagenet split introduces concerns about reproducibility and generalizability. Furthermore, the ablation studies and analysis presented are insufficient to justify the complexity of the proposed approach.

The motivation for the work is well-articulated, recognizing the challenges of gradient-based optimization in low-data scenarios and the potential of meta-learning. The literature review is comprehensive, covering relevant work in transfer learning, meta-learning, and few-shot learning. However, the paper's contribution feels incremental given the established landscape. Simply framing gradient descent as an LSTM cell is a conceptual observation rather than a breakthrough. 

The primary weakness lies in the empirical evaluation. The chosen datasets and experimental setup feel somewhat arbitrary. Creating a custom Mini-Imagenet split introduces bias and makes direct comparison with previous work difficult. The ablation studies are limited; for example, a clear comparison of the learned initialization versus random initialization is missing. The claim of competitive performance with Matching Networks is not compelling enough given the self-reported implementation details. The visualization of the meta-learner's behavior, while interesting, doesn't offer strong insights into *why* it works or where its limitations lie. The gradient independence assumption, while acknowledged, needs stronger justification. Ignoring second-order gradients is a significant simplification; the authors need to demonstrate the robustness of their method to this approximation. 

The parameter sharing strategy is a sensible way to manage the dimensionality of the meta-learner's parameter space, but its impact on performance isn’t thoroughly investigated. The choice of preprocessing method is borrowed from existing work without extensive justification for its effectiveness in this specific context. Batch normalization details require further clarification, especially the handling of statistics during meta-testing. The observation regarding overfitting in the fine-tuning baseline is insightful, but it doesn't necessarily validate the meta-learning approach; a more regularized fine-tuning procedure might yield better results.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Reproducibility:** Release the code and dataset split used for experimentation. This is crucial for the community to verify and build upon the results.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more rigorous ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., the forget gate, the input gate, the learned initialization).
*   **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** Compare against more recent and robust few-shot learning methods, including those based on metric learning and Prototypical Networks.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Provide some theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the LSTM-based optimizer, perhaps exploring its convergence properties or its ability to escape local optima.
*   **Scalability:** Discuss the scalability of the approach to larger datasets and more complex tasks.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, the LSTM hidden size, and the number of updates.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why was a custom Mini-Imagenet split created instead of using the publicly available one from Vinyals et al. (2016)? How might the results differ on the original split?
2.  What is the computational cost of training the meta-learner compared to training a standard classifier?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the gradient independence assumption and its potential impact on the results?
4.  How does the performance of the meta-learner degrade as the number of classes increases?
5.  Could you provide examples of the learned initializations and how they differ across different tasks? What properties of these initializations contribute to faster convergence?
