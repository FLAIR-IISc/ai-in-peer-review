This paper proposes a meta-learning approach, specifically an LSTM-based meta-learner, to optimize the learning process of a classifier in the few-shot learning setting. The core idea is to learn an update rule for the learner network's parameters, effectively learning a good initialization and optimization strategy tailored for rapid adaptation to new tasks with limited data. The authors frame this as an LSTM cell state update, allowing the meta-learner to capture both short-term and long-term dependencies in the learning process.

**Reject.** While the idea of employing an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm for few-shot learning is intriguing, the paper suffers from a multitude of small, yet significant, deficiencies in presentation, methodological rigor, and experimental validation that collectively undermine its contributions. The level of detail is often insufficient, making it difficult to fully assess the validity of the proposed approach.

Here's a breakdown of the supporting arguments:

1. **Lack of Clarity and Precision:** The introduction, while attempting to motivate the problem, lacks a concise statement of the paper's core contribution. The phrasing is often verbose and imprecise. For example, stating the motivation "models excelling at this task would have many useful applications" is vague and doesn't pinpoint the specific novelty. Similarly, the transition between problem statement and proposed solution feels abrupt.

2. **Insufficient Detail in Model Description:** While the analogy to LSTM cell updates is presented, critical implementation details are glossed over. Specifically, the authors mention parameter sharing "as in Andrychowicz et al. (2016)," but fail to adequately explain *how* this sharing is implemented within their specific LSTM architecture. The pre-processing step is similarly referenced without complete clarity regarding its application to both gradients *and* losses. What exactly constitutes “the dimensions of the gradients and the losses”? This lack of specificity hinders reproducibility.

3. **Questionable Simplification - Gradient Independence Assumption:** The justification for ignoring the dependency of learner losses and gradients on the meta-learner parameters is weak. Simply stating it's a simplification to avoid second derivatives doesn't justify its potential impact on the learning process. The authors state it “was still able to train effectively,” but this isn't substantiated with any ablation studies demonstrating the effect of including/excluding this dependency.

4. **Experimental Details and Baselines:** The experimental setup raises concerns. The authors acknowledge they recreated the Mini-ImageNet dataset and their implementation of Matching Networks, immediately casting doubt on comparability with established results. The explanation of why their results don't match Vinyals et al. (2016) is insufficient – simply stating they recreated the dataset isn't a valid excuse for significant performance discrepancies. Moreover, the finetuning baseline seems unfairly implemented and doesn’t represent a strong baseline. Why does overfitting occur so readily? The hyperparameters for the baseline are not adequately detailed.

5. **Visualization Interpretation:** The interpretation of the LSTM gate values is subjective and lacks quantitative support. Stating there is “a lot of variability” isn’t a strong claim without statistical analysis. The assertion that the meta-learner adopts different strategies for different tasks needs to be demonstrated more rigorously.

6. **Formatting and Referencing:** There are minor, but consistent, formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, incomplete referencing). While not major, they contribute to a perception of a lack of meticulousness.  The reference list is not formatted consistently.



**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Clarity of Contribution:** Restate the paper’s core contribution in a concise and impactful manner in the introduction.
*   **Detailed Implementation:** Provide a complete and detailed description of the LSTM architecture, including the exact parameter sharing mechanism.  Include a diagram.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the impact of the gradient independence assumption.
*   **Rigorous Comparison:**  Re-implement the baselines using publicly available code, or provide a detailed justification for the differences in implementation. Explain any significant deviations.
*   **Statistical Analysis:**  Support the interpretation of the LSTM gate values with quantitative statistical analysis.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameters.
*   **Code Release:** Make the code publicly available to ensure reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the parameter sharing mechanism within the LSTM, perhaps with a diagram illustrating the flow of information?
2.  How did you validate that the gradient independence assumption does not significantly impact the performance of the meta-learner?
3.  What specific steps were taken to ensure the Mini-ImageNet dataset recreated for this work is statistically comparable to the original dataset used in Vinyals et al. (2016)?
4.  Why is the fine-tuning baseline performing so poorly, and how does this compare to the results reported in other studies?
5.  Can you provide more detail regarding the hyperparameters used for both the meta-learner and the learner networks?
