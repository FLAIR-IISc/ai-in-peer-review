This paper proposes a meta-learning approach to few-shot learning, framing the problem as learning an optimizer via an LSTM network. The LSTM meta-learner aims to provide a beneficial initialization and update rule for a learner classifier, addressing the challenges of gradient-based optimization in low-data regimes. The approach is motivated by the limitations of standard optimization algorithms and transfer learning in few-shot scenarios. The authors present experiments on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods.

**Reject.** While the idea of learning an optimizer within a meta-learning framework is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and lacks sufficient rigor, ultimately undermining confidence in the presented results. The sheer number of these issues, despite the potential of the core idea, necessitates rejection.

The primary concerns stem from a lack of detail in experimental setup, inconsistencies in implementation details versus cited baselines, and an overreliance on claims without sufficient supporting evidence. Specifically:

1.  **Dataset Creation:** The paper mentions creating their own version of Mini-ImageNet, deviating from the established splits used by Vinyals et al. (2016). This is a critical issue. Without releasing the specific splits used, reproducing and comparing against the existing state-of-the-art becomes impossible. The authors state the splits weren't released but do not detail *how* they created their own, introducing potential bias.

2.  **Baseline Implementation:** The authors state they implemented their own versions of Matching Networks. While permissible, this requires extensive justification, particularly when claiming competitive results. The description of the implementation details (e.g., number of convolutional layers, dropout) is provided but lacks a direct comparison to the original implementation choices, raising concerns about fair comparison.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Ablation:** The paper states different meta-learner models were trained for 1-shot and 5-shot tasks with varying update steps. However, there’s a lack of exploration into the sensitivity of the meta-learner to the number of updates *during* meta-testing. Was this number optimized, or was it simply chosen? An ablation study examining the impact of different LSTM architectures (number of layers, hidden size) is also missing.

4.  **Preprocessing Detail:** While the preprocessing method from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) is mentioned, the rationale for choosing *p=10* is not provided. A sensitivity analysis exploring different values of 'p' would strengthen the claim of its effectiveness.

5.  **Gradient Independence Assumption:** The authors acknowledge the simplifying assumption of ignoring second derivatives in the meta-learner’s gradients. However, the potential impact of this simplification on the learning process is not discussed. Is there any evidence to suggest that this assumption doesn't significantly affect the meta-learner's ability to learn?

6.  **Visualization Interpretation:** The interpretation of the LSTM gate values feels speculative and lacks quantitative support. Saying there is "a lot of variability" is not insightful without statistical analysis.

7. **Formatting and minor errors**: Throughout the paper, there are minor formatting issues like inconsistent spacing and incorrect citations. For example, references aren't consistently formatted.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Release Dataset Splits:** Absolutely crucial. Releasing the exact dataset splits used will allow for reproducibility and fair comparison.
*   **Detailed Baseline Comparison:** Provide a comprehensive comparison of your implementation of Matching Networks with the original implementation, highlighting any differences.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to analyze the impact of different hyperparameters, LSTM architectures, and preprocessing parameters.
*   **Quantitative Visualization Analysis:** Instead of simply stating "a lot of variability", provide quantitative metrics to support the observations from the gate value visualizations.
*   **Error Analysis:** Analyze the types of errors the meta-learner makes. Are there specific classes or scenarios where it struggles?
*   **Clarify Gradient Independence:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the gradient independence assumption and its potential implications.
*   Thoroughly proofread for formatting inconsistencies and errors.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What was the rationale behind choosing the specific dataset splits for Mini-ImageNet, and how do these splits compare to those used in other state-of-the-art papers (e.g., in terms of class difficulty)?
2.  Can you provide a direct comparison of your implementation of Matching Networks with the original implementation, including code snippets or a detailed explanation of any deviations?
3.  Have you performed any sensitivity analysis on the number of updates used during meta-testing, and how does performance vary with different update numbers?
4.  What steps were taken to ensure the reliability and validity of the results, given the gradient independence assumption?
5.  Can you provide more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of errors the meta-learner makes and potential areas for improvement?
