## Review for ICLR 2024

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper proposes a meta-learning approach to few-shot learning using an LSTM-based meta-learner to learn an update rule for a learner classifier. The core idea is to frame the optimization process as an LSTM cell update, allowing the meta-learner to capture both short-term task-specific knowledge and long-term common knowledge across tasks.  The authors claim their method learns a beneficial common initialization for the learner and provides an optimization strategy that converges quickly on new tasks with limited data. The paper presents empirical results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, demonstrating competitive performance compared to baselines and existing metric learning methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the idea is interesting and the paper is well-written, the empirical results are not compelling enough to warrant acceptance. The performance improvements over strong baselines (like Matching Networks) are not consistently significant, and the ablation studies (particularly regarding batch normalization) are not fully convincing.  The lack of code availability also hinders a thorough evaluation of the method.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The core claim of outperforming existing methods isn't definitively shown. The results in Table 1 show performance within the confidence intervals of Matching Networks for the 1-shot setting. While better in 5-shot, the margin isn't substantial enough given the complexity of the proposed method.  The authors acknowledge the numbers might not directly compare to Vinyals et al. (2016) due to dataset creation, but this introduces uncertainty regarding the validity of the comparison.
* **Ablation Study Concerns:** The discussion around batch normalization feels incomplete. The authors describe the difficulties of maintaining consistency between meta-training and meta-testing and the chosen solution, but the impact of this decision on the overall performance isn't clearly quantified.  A more thorough ablation comparing performance *with* and *without* the specific batch norm strategy would be beneficial.
* **Gradient Independence Assumption:** The paper acknowledges a simplifying assumption regarding gradient independence during meta-learner training, potentially sacrificing some accuracy. While acknowledging it, the impact of this simplification on performance isn’t thoroughly investigated or justified. A discussion on the potential drawbacks or sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper.
* **Lack of Code:** The absence of publicly available code significantly hinders reproducibility and independent verification of the results.  This is especially important for a complex method like this where implementation details can heavily influence performance.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Visualization of Meta-Learner:** The visualization of the gate values is a good attempt to provide insight into the meta-learner’s behavior. Expanding this analysis to show how the learned learning rate (from the input gate) changes across different tasks and datasets would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more in-depth analysis of the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters (learning rates, LSTM layer size, etc.) would improve the robustness of the findings.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational overhead of using an LSTM-based meta-learner compared to simpler optimization algorithms or metric learning approaches. Adding a discussion of computational costs would provide a more complete picture of the method’s practicality.
* **Connection to Second-Order Methods:** The LSTM update rule bears some resemblance to second-order optimization methods (e.g., K-FAC). Discussing the potential connections and differences could enrich the paper.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required for training and evaluating your meta-learner compared to the baseline methods?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the choice of the LSTM architecture (number of layers, hidden state size)?
3.  Have you explored the impact of different preprocessing techniques for the gradients and losses, beyond the method proposed by Andrychowicz et al. (2016)?
4.  Would you be willing to release the code for the experiments presented in the paper to facilitate reproducibility and further research?
5.  Considering the gradient independence assumption, what is the expected impact on the performance of the method? Have you performed any experiments to quantify this effect?
