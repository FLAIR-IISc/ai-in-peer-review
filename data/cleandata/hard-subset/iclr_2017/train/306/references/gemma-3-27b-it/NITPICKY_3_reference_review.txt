This paper proposes a meta-learning approach to few-shot learning, framing the optimization process as an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to update the parameters of a learner classifier. The core idea is to train an LSTM to mimic a good optimization algorithm tailored for rapid adaptation to new tasks with limited data, effectively learning a beneficial initialization and update rule.

**Reject.** While the idea of using an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm is interesting and builds upon prior work, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and lacks sufficient rigor in its presentation and experimental validation. The sheer number of these issues, despite the potential of the core idea, leads me to recommend rejection.

The primary concern is the lack of precision and justification for numerous implementation details. The choice of LSTM architecture (2-layer), specific hyperparameters (learning rate of 0.001, gradient clipping of 0.25), and the preprocessing method (p=10) are presented without sufficient motivation or ablation studies. Why these specific values? What sensitivity analyses were performed? The claim that these settings “worked well” is insufficient. Similarly, the initialization scheme for the LSTM (large forget gate bias, small input gate bias) is described as a workaround without detailed explanation. 

Furthermore, the experimental setup introduces ambiguity. The creation of a “version” of the Mini-ImageNet dataset, differing from the original used in Vinyals et al. (2016), without a clear justification and potential impact analysis is problematic. The statement that the results don’t match Vinyals et al. (2016) "simply because" a new dataset was created is dismissive and doesn’t address potential differences in the data distribution or task difficulty. The comparison to Matching Networks feels incomplete – the authors implemented their *own* version, and this is acknowledged without a strong justification for doing so. Were the hyperparameters for Matching Networks also tuned, and if so, how? The claim that the fine-tuned baseline performs worse due to overfitting is plausible, but needs further investigation, perhaps with regularization techniques.

The visualization of the meta-learner's behavior (Figure 3) is descriptive but doesn’t provide quantitative insights. Stating that there is “variability” and “differences” is not insightful; the authors should provide statistical analysis or metrics to support these observations.  

Finally, the writing quality and attention to detail are concerning. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased or unclear. Minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent citation styles) detract from the professionalism of the paper. The inclusion of “∗Work done as an intern...” feels unnecessary and somewhat informal for a conference submission. Algorithm 1 is missing descriptions for the variables.

**Additional Feedback (For Potential Improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the meta-learner (e.g., forget gate, input gate, parameter sharing).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests for all experimental results. Report confidence intervals.
*   **Dataset Details:** Clearly explain the differences between the created Mini-ImageNet dataset and the original. Justify the creation of a new dataset.
*   **Implementation Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the implementation, including specific library versions and hardware specifications.
*   **Code Release:** The claim of code availability (https://github.com/twitter/meta-learning-lstm) is good but needs to be verified during the review process.
*   **Clarify Gradient Independence Assumption:** Provide a more thorough justification for the gradient independence assumption and discuss its potential limitations.
*   **Improve Writing Quality:** Carefully review the paper for clarity, conciseness, and grammatical errors.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific justifications were used to choose the LSTM architecture (number of layers, hidden state size)?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the initialization scheme for the LSTM and its impact on training stability?
3.  What steps were taken to ensure that the "version" of Mini-ImageNet used in your experiments is comparable to the original dataset?
4.  How were the hyperparameters for the Matching Networks baseline tuned?
5.  Can you quantify the observations made in Figure 3 regarding the behavior of the forget and input gates?
