## Review of "Learning to learn by gradient descent by gradient descent"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of few-shot learning, specifically addressing the difficulty of optimizing neural networks with limited data. The authors propose a meta-learning approach where an LSTM-based meta-learner learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier. This meta-learner aims to discover a beneficial common initialization and an effective update rule, enabling rapid convergence even with very few examples per class. The approach frames the optimization process *as* an LSTM update, leveraging the similarities between gradient descent steps and LSTM cell state updates.  The paper presents a clear formulation, detailed model description, and empirical results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrating competitive performance compared to existing methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning, demonstrates promising empirical results, and is clearly written. The connection between gradient descent and LSTM updates is a clever insight.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The problem of few-shot learning is well-defined and important. The motivation for using meta-learning to learn a good initialization and optimization strategy is sound. Framing the optimization process *as* an LSTM is a novel idea that provides a compelling connection between two seemingly disparate areas.
* **Technical Soundness:** The model is clearly described, and the technical details (parameter sharing, preprocessing, training procedure, and gradient independence assumption) are well-explained and justified. The use of LSTMs for learning update rules builds on prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Hochreiter et al., 2001) but extends it to the specific context of few-shot learning with a focus on initialization and adaptation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Mini-ImageNet demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The results are competitive with state-of-the-art approaches like Matching Networks and, crucially, show improvement over simpler baselines (nearest neighbor, fine-tuning). The visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior (forget and input gate values) provides some qualitative insights into the learned optimization strategy.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem and motivation, while the subsequent sections provide a detailed and understandable description of the approach and experiments.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:**  While the paper demonstrates strong performance, it would be beneficial to include ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components of the model. For instance, how much does parameter sharing contribute? What impact does the specific preprocessing scheme have?  What is the effect of learning the initial cell state?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices would strengthen the paper.  Which hyperparameters are most critical, and how does performance change with different settings?
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost of training the meta-learner would be helpful. How does the training time compare to other few-shot learning methods?
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the LSTM framing is insightful, a more formal theoretical justification for why this approach might be effective would be a valuable addition.  
* **Error Analysis:** Examining examples where the model fails could provide insights into the limitations of the approach and suggest avenues for future research.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the gradient independence assumption:** You mention this simplifying assumption.  Were any attempts made to incorporate gradient dependencies, and if so, why were they abandoned?  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of this assumption on the learned optimization strategy?
* **Regarding the choice of LSTM:**  Why was an LSTM specifically chosen over other recurrent architectures (e.g., GRU)? Were other architectures explored?
* **Regarding the visualization of the meta-learner:** The visualization of gate values is interesting. Can you provide any examples of specific datasets where the meta-learner’s behavior differs significantly? What are the characteristics of these datasets that might explain the observed differences?
* **Regarding scalability:** Do you anticipate challenges in scaling this approach to larger datasets and more complex models? What modifications might be necessary to address these challenges?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a compelling approach to few-shot learning. The minor revisions suggested above would further enhance the quality and impact of the work.
