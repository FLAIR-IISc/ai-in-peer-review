This paper tackles the challenging problem of few-shot learning, specifically addressing the limitations of gradient-based optimization in data-scarce regimes. The authors propose a novel meta-learning approach, framing the optimization process itself as a learning problem solved by an LSTM-based meta-learner. This meta-learner learns to update the parameters of a learner network, effectively discovering a good initialization and an efficient optimization strategy tailored for rapid adaptation to new, few-shot tasks. The core idea – using an LSTM to *learn* an optimizer – is compelling and represents a genuinely new direction within meta-learning.

**Reject.** While the idea is innovative and promising, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to convince me of its practical effectiveness. The results presented, while showing improvement over simple baselines, are only competitive with existing metric-learning methods like Matching Networks, and not a clear, demonstrable advance. The improvements are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the proposed approach, and the analysis feels somewhat incomplete. I prioritize novelty, but novelty *must* be accompanied by convincing evidence of benefit.

The key strength is the novelty of the approach. Learning an optimizer, rather than relying on hand-designed algorithms, is a conceptually strong idea. Framing the optimization process as a sequence-to-sequence learning problem via an LSTM is insightful. The connection to existing LSTM optimization work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016) is well-established, and the authors appropriately acknowledge related work. The attempt to address the initialization problem within the meta-learning framework is also a valuable contribution.

However, the empirical evaluation is the main weakness. The experiments are conducted on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, which, as the authors acknowledge, is a recreation of the original dataset. Results are presented only for 1-shot and 5-shot learning, and the performance gap compared to Matching Networks is not consistently significant. The visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior, while interesting, doesn’t offer a definitive understanding of *why* the approach works (or doesn’t work better). Furthermore, the ablation studies are limited. The impact of parameter sharing and the preprocessing method aren’t rigorously evaluated. The gradient independence assumption is mentioned but without sufficient justification or analysis of its potential impact. The ablation on the batch norm strategy is promising, but again, lacks depth. The reported results are presented as averages, and the inclusion of error bars or confidence intervals would greatly improve the assessment of statistical significance.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Test the model on more diverse datasets beyond Mini-ImageNet, including datasets with different image resolutions, complexities, and domain shifts.
*   **More rigorous comparison:** Provide a more thorough comparison against stronger baselines and state-of-the-art meta-learning methods. Include error bars for all performance metrics.
*   **Ablation studies:** Conduct more extensive ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the model (parameter sharing, preprocessing, forget gate design, etc.).
*   **Justify the Gradient Independence Assumption:** Either provide theoretical justification or empirical evidence demonstrating the validity of this simplification. What is the impact on performance of including the gradient dependency?
*   **Analyze the learned optimizer:** Provide more detailed analysis of the learned optimization strategy. What types of updates does the meta-learner favor? How does it adapt to different tasks? What distinguishes it from standard optimization algorithms like SGD or Adam?
*   **Computational cost:** Discuss the computational cost of training and using the meta-learner compared to other approaches.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Given the similarity in performance to Matching Networks, what are the specific scenarios where your meta-learning approach demonstrably outperforms these methods?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the LSTM architecture (number of layers, hidden units)?
*   How does the choice of pre-processing impact performance? Are there alternative pre-processing methods that could be more effective?
*   Could you elaborate on the benefits of using an LSTM specifically? Why not a simpler recurrent network or a feedforward network for learning the update rule?
*   Could you show some examples of the learned updates (i.e., the values of *it* and *ft*) for different tasks?
