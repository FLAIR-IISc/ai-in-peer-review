This paper proposes a meta-learning approach to few-shot learning, framing the optimization process itself as a learning problem. Specifically, the authors introduce an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier, aiming to find a beneficial common initialization and effective update rules for rapid adaptation to new, few-shot tasks. The core idea – using an LSTM to model the optimization dynamics – is intriguing and builds upon prior work in meta-learning and learning-to-optimize.

**Reject.** While the idea is interesting, the paper presents insufficient evidence to support its claims. The improvements over strong baselines (like Matching Networks) are marginal and not consistently demonstrated, and the methodological choices, particularly the gradient independence assumption, are concerning without further justification. The reliance on a specific implementation and dataset split further limits the generalizability of the findings.

The primary weakness lies in the limited empirical validation. The results in Table 1 show improvements over baselines, but these improvements are modest and, in some cases, the confidence intervals overlap. The claim of being “competitive with state-of-the-art” is therefore overstated. The authors acknowledge they recreated the Mini-ImageNet dataset and implemented Matching Networks themselves, raising concerns about potential inconsistencies in experimental setup and implementation details that could influence the comparison. A more thorough comparison against established, publicly available implementations of Matching Networks and other strong few-shot learning methods is crucial. 

The gradient independence assumption is a significant concern. While the authors cite Andrychowicz et al. (2016) as justification, ignoring the dependency of learner losses and gradients on the meta-learner parameters is a strong simplification that could invalidate the learned optimization strategy. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of why this assumption is reasonable in their context and potentially investigate the impact of including these dependencies, even if it requires approximations. 

The visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior (Figure 3) offers some insight, but is largely descriptive and lacks quantitative analysis. While observing varying gate values across datasets is interesting, it doesn’t reveal *what* the meta-learner is actually learning or *why* it's effective (or not). Further analysis, such as correlating gate values with performance improvements or identifying learned patterns in the update rules, would be valuable.

Finally, the preprocessing step for gradients and losses, while borrowed from existing work, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a clear theoretical justification within the context of the LSTM meta-learner.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters, learner network architectures, and dataset characteristics.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the meta-learner, such as the forget gate and input gate mechanisms.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Explore theoretical properties of the learned optimization strategy, such as convergence rates or generalization bounds.
*   **Broader Evaluation:** Evaluate the method on more diverse datasets beyond Mini-ImageNet.
*   **Clarify Implementation Details:** Provide a clear and concise description of the implementation, including code availability, to facilitate reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you justify the gradient independence assumption with more detail? Have you explored approximations to include this dependency, and if so, what were the results?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the meta-learner to the choice of LSTM architecture (e.g., number of layers, hidden state size)?
3.  The preprocessing step seems crucial. Could you explain the reasoning behind the specific parameters chosen (e.g., p=10) and their impact on performance?
4.  You mention the importance of training the meta-learner for a specific number of updates. Have you explored meta-learning a variable number of updates, potentially based on the task at hand?
5.  Can you provide a more quantitative analysis of the learned optimization strategy, beyond the descriptive visualization in Figure 3? What specific patterns or behaviors did you observe?
