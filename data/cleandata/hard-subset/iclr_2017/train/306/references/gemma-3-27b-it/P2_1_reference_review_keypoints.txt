- Paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns update rules for few-shot classifiers, aiming to capture short- and long-term task knowledge.  
- Authors claim the method finds a useful common initialization and quickly adapts to new tasks, with experiments on Mini-ImageNet.  
- Decision: Reject — idea interesting and writing good, but empirical results not compelling enough for acceptance.  
- Limited empirical advantage: improvements over strong baselines (e.g., Matching Networks) are not consistently significant, especially for 1-shot.  
- Dataset/benchmark differences introduce uncertainty in comparisons to prior work (Vinyals et al.).  
- Ablation study on batch normalization is incomplete; impact of the chosen BN strategy on performance isn’t clearly quantified.  
- Gradient independence assumption during meta-training is a simplification that may hurt accuracy and isn’t thoroughly analyzed.  
- Lack of publicly available code hampers reproducibility and independent verification.  
- Visualization of gate values is promising; authors should expand to show how learned learning rates vary across tasks/datasets.  
- Need for hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (learning rates, LSTM size, etc.) to assess robustness.  
- Paper omits discussion of computational cost of the LSTM-based meta-learner versus simpler methods.  
- Suggest discussing connections and differences between the LSTM update rule and second-order optimization methods (e.g., K-FAC).  
- Questions: provide detailed computational resource breakdown compared to baselines.  
- Questions: report sensitivity to LSTM architecture choices (layers, hidden size).  
- Questions: explore other preprocessing techniques for gradients/losses beyond Andrychowicz et al. (2016).  
- Questions: consider releasing code to facilitate reproducibility.  
- Questions: quantify the impact of the gradient independence assumption with experiments or analysis.
