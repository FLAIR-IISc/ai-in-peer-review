## Review for ICLR 2024

**Paper Title:** Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenge of few-shot learning by framing it as a meta-learning problem. The authors propose a novel LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer that learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier.  The core idea is to train the LSTM to generate updates for the learner’s parameters, effectively learning an optimization algorithm tailored for few-shot scenarios.  Crucially, the meta-learner also learns a task-common initialization for the learner, providing a beneficial starting point for fine-tuning. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the Mini-ImageNet dataset compared to existing methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning. The LSTM-based meta-learner is a novel idea and shows promising results, competitive with state-of-the-art metric learning techniques. The experimental validation on Mini-ImageNet is solid, and the ablation study, particularly the comparison against finetuning, is insightful.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of using an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm for few-shot learning is a fresh take on meta-learning.  The connection between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent is insightful and forms a solid basis for the proposed model.
* **Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in the few-shot setting and provides a convincing argument for why a meta-learned optimizer is beneficial.  The problem statement and motivation are well-defined and relevant to the broader research community.
* **Experimental Validation:** The results on Mini-ImageNet demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive performance. The comparison with baselines, particularly the observation that finetuning often *underperforms* a nearest-neighbor approach due to overfitting, highlights the advantages of learning a good initialization and optimization strategy.
* **Ablation & Analysis:** The visualization of the meta-learner’s gate values provides some initial understanding of its learned optimization strategy, adding to the credibility of the approach. 
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the model and training procedure. Algorithm 1 and the corresponding computational graph (Figure 2) are particularly helpful.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions using ADAM for training the meta-learner with a learning rate of 0.001 and gradient clipping of 0.25. A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper. Were other values explored? How robust are the results to changes in these parameters?
* **Computational Cost:**  The computational cost of meta-training can be significant.  A discussion of the computational resources required to train the model would be helpful, especially considering the complexity of the LSTM meta-learner. 
* **Scalability:** While the results on Mini-ImageNet are encouraging, it would be beneficial to discuss the potential scalability of the approach to larger datasets and more complex tasks.
* **Comparison to other Meta-Learning Approaches:**  A more comprehensive comparison to other prominent meta-learning approaches (e.g., MAML, Reptile) would be valuable.  A table summarizing performance across different datasets would be helpful.
* **Detailed Initialization Strategy:** The paper mentions initializing the forget gate bias to a large value and the input gate bias to a small value.  Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind these specific values and their impact on training stability?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* You mention that the simplifying assumption of ignoring the dependency of gradients on meta-learner parameters did not significantly impact performance. Could you quantify this impact – perhaps by comparing results with and without including these dependencies? Even a brief qualitative assessment would be helpful.
* The visualization of gate values (Figure 3) is intriguing. Could you provide more examples of these visualizations across different tasks and datasets? Do you observe any consistent patterns or behaviors in the meta-learner’s optimization strategy?
* In the ablation study, the finetuned baseline performs worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline. Does this observation hold across different learning rates and number of updates for finetuning?
* Could you discuss the potential benefits of exploring different LSTM architectures or other recurrent neural network variants for the meta-learner?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel approach to few-shot learning. Addressing the points above would further enhance the quality of the work and increase its impact on the research community.
