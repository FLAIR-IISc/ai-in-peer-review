

# Review of "LSTM-based Meta-Learner Optimizer for Few-Shot Learning"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer designed specifically for few-shot learning tasks. The authors identify two key limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings: (1) standard optimizers weren't designed for few updates, and (2) random initialization hinders convergence with few examples. They propose framing the problem within a meta-learning framework where an LSTM meta-learner learns to optimize a classifier network by mimicking LSTM cell state updates. The meta-learner simultaneously learns a task-common initialization for the classifier and an effective update strategy, aiming to converge quickly on new tasks with limited examples. The authors report competitive results on Mini-ImageNet for 5-shot learning and reasonable performance for 1-shot learning compared to baselines and Matching Networks.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Reject**. The paper's main claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous experimental validation and comparison.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The primary issue is the lack of rigorous experimental validation that would support the paper's claims of "competitive performance with state-of-the-art" methods. The authors created their own version of the Mini-ImageNet dataset, which makes their results incomparable to prior work. As they acknowledge: "the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset and implemented our own versions of their model." This undermines the fundamental claim of the paper, as it's impossible to determine if their method is truly competitive when using a different dataset. The standard practice in few-shot learning is to use the exact dataset splits from prior work to ensure fair comparison, which this paper fails to do.

Furthermore, the paper lacks sufficient statistical validation of the results. While the authors mention "confidence intervals," they don't report them or demonstrate whether the performance differences are statistically significant. This is particularly concerning given that they claim their method "attains results that are much better than the baselines" and "competitive with Matching Networks," but don't provide evidence that these differences are meaningful.

The paper also fails to clearly distinguish its approach from prior work, particularly Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which also proposed using an LSTM to learn an optimizer. The authors don't adequately explain how their method differs from or improves upon this prior work for the specific few-shot learning problem.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. **Use the exact dataset splits from Vinyals et al. (2016)** to enable a fair comparison with prior work. If they cannot obtain these splits, they should acknowledge this limitation and avoid claiming "competitive" results without proper benchmarking.

2. **Provide statistical significance testing** for all performance comparisons, including confidence intervals and p-values. Without this, it's impossible to determine if the reported performance differences are meaningful.

3. **Clarify the distinction from prior work**. The authors should provide a detailed comparison of their approach with Andrychowicz et al. (2016), explaining specifically how their method is different and why it's better suited for few-shot learning.

4. **Include more details about dataset creation**. The paper should specify exactly how they selected the 100 classes from ImageNet and how they split them into training, validation, and test sets.

5. **Justify the gradient independence assumption**. The paper makes a significant simplifying assumption (Section 3.3.1) that the losses and gradients are independent of the meta-learner parameters. The authors should provide analysis of how this assumption affects performance, or explain why it's reasonable in their setting.

## Questions for Authors

1. How exactly did you create your Mini-ImageNet dataset? Specifically, how did you select the 100 classes from ImageNet, and how did you split them into training, validation, and test sets? Without this information, it's impossible to reproduce your results or make a fair comparison with prior work.

2. Given that your dataset differs from the one used in Vinyals et al. (2016), how can you claim your results are "competitive with state-of-the-art" without a proper comparison to the standard benchmark?

3. Can you provide statistical significance testing for the differences in performance between your method and the baselines? Are the differences you report statistically significant?

4. How does your approach specifically differ from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) in both methodology and results, and what specific improvements does it offer for the few-shot learning problem?

The paper presents an interesting idea that connects LSTM cell state updates with gradient descent, but the lack of rigorous experimental validation and fair comparison to prior work makes it difficult to assess the true contribution and significance of the approach. Without addressing these fundamental issues, the paper's claims cannot be adequately evaluated.
