

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning tasks. The authors frame the optimization process as an LSTM, where the LSTM's cell state represents the learner's parameters, and the LSTM gates control the learning rate and forgetting behavior. The key claim is that this approach can learn a task-common initialization and an effective update rule for few-shot classification, outperforming baselines and achieving competitive results with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet. The paper presents empirical results on 1-shot and 5-shot classification tasks, claiming that their method significantly outperforms simple baselines and is competitive with state-of-the-art metric learning approaches.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims, particularly regarding the novelty and superiority of the proposed approach. The empirical validation is insufficiently rigorous for a top-tier conference like ICLR, and the paper does not adequately address critical limitations of the proposed method.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
My skepticism stems from three main concerns:

First, the empirical validation is inadequate. The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet rather than using the standard benchmark, which makes comparison with prior work impossible. Their results don't match those reported by Vinyals et al. (2016) for Matching Networks, and they don't provide enough details about their dataset construction to ensure reproducibility. Without using the standard benchmark, it's impossible to verify whether their claimed improvements are meaningful or merely artifacts of dataset differences.

Second, the paper lacks proper ablation studies to identify which components of the LSTM meta-learner are actually contributing to performance gains. The authors claim their method outperforms baselines, but they don't show how much of the improvement comes from the LSTM structure versus the task-specific training procedure. Without these ablations, it's unclear whether the LSTM-based approach is truly necessary or whether simpler methods could achieve similar results.

Third, the theoretical justification for using an LSTM is weak. The paper draws a superficial analogy between the LSTM cell update equation and gradient descent updates, but doesn't provide a compelling argument for why this specific architecture would work better for few-shot learning than existing meta-learning methods. The authors cite Andrychowicz et al. (2016) as a similar approach but for a different problem (large-scale classification vs. few-shot learning), yet they don't explain why their adaptation to few-shot learning is justified or how it overcomes the limitations of the prior work.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve this paper, the authors should:

1. Use the standard Mini-ImageNet benchmark to enable fair comparison with prior work. Without this, the empirical claims are difficult to evaluate.

2. Conduct thorough ablation studies to isolate the contributions of different components in the LSTM meta-learner. For instance, they should compare:
   - The LSTM meta-learner versus a simple learning rate scheduler
   - The full LSTM approach versus a modified version without certain gates
   - The end-to-end meta-training versus a two-stage approach (pre-train a good initialization, then fine-tune)

3. Provide more detailed analysis of the optimization process learned by the meta-learner. The visualization in Figure 3 is interesting but doesn't explain why the observed gate behaviors lead to better performance.

4. Address the computational cost of their approach. For few-shot learning, efficiency is crucial, but the paper doesn't discuss training time or inference speed compared to baselines.

5. Include more rigorous statistical analysis of their results. The paper mentions confidence intervals but doesn't provide sufficient details about the number of test episodes or the variance in results.

## Questions for Authors
1. Why did you choose to create your own Mini-ImageNet dataset rather than using the standard benchmark? How do you justify the differences between your dataset and the standard one?

2. Can you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component of your LSTM meta-learner (e.g., the input gate, forget gate, and cell state update rule) to the final performance?

3. How does your method compare to the recent MAML (Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning) approach by Finn et al. (2017), which has become a standard in meta-learning? The paper doesn't discuss this important baseline.

4. The paper mentions a "gradient independence assumption" (Section 3.3.1) that avoids second derivatives. How did you verify that this assumption doesn't significantly impact the quality of the learned optimizer?

5. The paper claims to outperform Matching Networks for 5-shot learning but not for 1-shot. Can you explain the qualitative difference between these two settings that leads to this performance gap?
