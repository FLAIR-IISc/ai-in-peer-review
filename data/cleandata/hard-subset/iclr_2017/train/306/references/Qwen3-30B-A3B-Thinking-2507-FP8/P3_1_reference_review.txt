

# Peer Review: "Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent" (ICLR Submission)

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm specifically designed for few-shot learning tasks. The authors frame the problem as a meta-learning task where the meta-learner must optimize a classifier network with only a few updates (1-shot or 5-shot). The key insight is that the LSTM update rule resembles the parameter update in gradient descent, allowing them to train an LSTM to learn an adaptive optimization strategy. The meta-learner learns both a good initialization for the classifier parameters and an effective update rule that can achieve good generalization performance with very few training examples. The authors claim their method outperforms baseline approaches (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) and is competitive with state-of-the-art metric learning methods like Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark.

The approach is well-motivated, as the authors correctly identify the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization for few-shot learning (not designed for few updates) and the limitations of transfer learning (benefits diminish as task divergence increases). The LSTM-based meta-learner is a natural extension of prior work by Andrychowicz et al. (2016), but with a clear focus on the few-shot setting.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

**Key reasons for rejection:** The paper's experimental evaluation lacks scientific rigor and proper comparison to the state-of-the-art, making it impossible to validate the claimed performance improvements. The dataset creation methodology, baseline implementations, and statistical analysis are insufficiently detailed to support the paper's main claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is in the conceptual space (LSTM-based meta-learner for few-shot learning), but the empirical validation falls short in several critical ways:

1. **Inadequate Dataset Comparison:** The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet by selecting 100 random classes from ImageNet and using 64/16/20 classes for training/validation/testing. However, they don't provide a clear comparison to the original Mini-ImageNet results from Vinyals et al. (2016), which is the standard benchmark. They acknowledge their results don't match Vinyals et al. but don't provide a justification for their dataset split or demonstrate that their version is comparable to the original. Without this, it's impossible to determine if the reported performance is actually competitive with the state-of-the-art.

2. **Poor Baseline Implementation:** The Baseline-finetune is described as "a coarser version of our meta-learner model" but lacks critical details. Specifically:
   - The paper doesn't specify how many fine-tuning updates were used (only mentions "a fixed number of updates")
   - It doesn't clarify if hyperparameter search was properly conducted on the meta-validation set
   - The fine-tune baseline is claimed to be worse than nearest-neighbor, which is surprising given that fine-tuning is a standard approach, but no explanation is provided for why the fine-tune baseline overfits (especially in 1-shot setting)

3. **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** The paper provides mean accuracy values without confidence intervals or statistical significance tests. In few-shot learning, performance can vary significantly across different class splits, so this is a critical omission. The authors claim "the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks" but don't provide these intervals or the actual statistical tests.

4. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper only compares against Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) but fails to compare against more recent and established methods like Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017), Relation Networks (Sung et al., 2018), or Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) (Finn et al., 2017), which have become standard baselines in the few-shot learning community.

5. **Implementation Ambiguities:** The paper doesn't clarify which version of Matching Networks was used (basic or fully-conditional embedding), and the details of their implementation are insufficient for reproducibility. They mention adding dropout to prevent overfitting but don't specify the dropout rate.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

The core idea of learning an optimization algorithm for few-shot learning is interesting and potentially valuable. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. **Reproduce the original Mini-ImageNet benchmark:** Use the exact dataset splits from Vinyals et al. (2016) to ensure a fair comparison. If the original splits aren't available, provide a clear justification for their alternative dataset and demonstrate that it's comparable in difficulty to the original benchmark.

2. **Provide complete implementation details:** For all baselines, specify the number of updates used, hyperparameters searched over, and how the hyperparameter search was conducted. For the fine-tune baseline, explicitly state how many updates were used (e.g., 10, 20) and whether this was optimized on the validation set.

3. **Include statistical significance testing:** Perform t-tests or other appropriate statistical tests to demonstrate that the performance differences between methods are meaningful, not just due to random variation in the class splits.

4. **Compare against more recent methods:** Add comparisons to at least two more recent state-of-the-art few-shot learning methods (e.g., Prototypical Networks and MAML) to properly position their work within the current literature.

5. **Clarify the Matching Networks implementation:** Specify which version of Matching Networks was used (basic or FCE) and provide results for both if they were implemented.

6. **Include ablation studies:** Perform ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of different components of the meta-learner (e.g., input gate, forget gate, initial parameter learning).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. Can you provide the exact dataset splits used in your Mini-ImageNet version and compare your results to the original Vinyals et al. (2016) results on the same splits? If you cannot reproduce the original splits, please justify your dataset creation methodology.

2. How many fine-tuning steps did you use for the Baseline-finetune? Was this number chosen through hyperparameter search on the meta-validation set, and if so, what range of values was searched?

3. Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values from t-tests) for the reported performance differences between your method and the baselines/Matching Networks?

4. Why did you choose to use a 2-layer LSTM for the meta-learner instead of a single-layer LSTM? What ablation studies were performed to justify this architectural choice?

5. How does your method compare to more recent meta-learning approaches like MAML or Reptile, which have become standard baselines in few-shot learning? Have you considered adding these as baselines?

6. The paper claims that "for 5-shot, we are able to do much better than Matching Networks, whereas for 1-shot, the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks." Can you provide the exact confidence intervals for these results?

The paper presents an interesting conceptual contribution, but the lack of rigorous experimental validation and comparison to the current state-of-the-art prevents me from recommending acceptance. With the suggested improvements, particularly regarding the experimental setup and comparison to the current literature, this work could make a valuable contribution to the few-shot learning literature.
