

# Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning, framing the optimization process itself as a learning problem. The authors make the insightful observation that the cell state update in an LSTM resembles gradient descent updates, and they propose training an LSTM to learn the optimization algorithm rather than using fixed optimization methods. The meta-learner learns both a good initialization for the classifier parameters and an effective update rule tailored to the few-shot learning setting. The approach is grounded in the recognition that standard gradient-based optimization fails in few-shot scenarios due to limited updates and poor random initialization. The authors demonstrate competitive results on Mini-ImageNet, outperforming baselines and approaching state-of-the-art performance.

## Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a bold and novel conceptual approach to meta-learning that reframes optimization as a learnable process. While the experimental results are competitive rather than groundbreaking, the core idea of learning the optimization algorithm itself represents a significant conceptual shift in how we think about training neural networks.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution is genuinely novel: it proposes learning the optimization algorithm through a meta-learner LSTM, rather than using fixed optimization methods. This is a significant departure from previous meta-learning approaches (like Matching Networks or memory-augmented networks) that focus on learning representations or meta-features rather than the optimization process itself. The connection between LSTM cell state updates and gradient descent updates is clever and well-justified.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's conceptual contribution sufficiently bold to outweigh the methodological limitations. The gradient independence assumption (3.3.1) is acknowledged and reasonable for the problem at hand, and the paper's approach is more conceptually innovative than technically perfect.

The paper's novelty is further strengthened by its clear connection to the literature, particularly building on Andrychowicz et al. (2016) while extending their work to the few-shot learning regime. This is not just another meta-learning method but a fundamentally different perspective on how to approach optimization in low-data regimes.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the theoretical limitations of the gradient independence assumption. While the authors acknowledge it, a more detailed analysis of when this assumption might fail would strengthen the paper.

2. The visualization of gate values (Figure 3) is interesting but could be more informative. The authors might consider showing how the meta-learner's behavior differs across different classes or tasks to better demonstrate its adaptability.

3. The paper mentions future work on extending to "a full spectrum of settings" (few or lots of training examples, few or lots of classes). This is a crucial direction for the approach, and the authors might consider including some preliminary experiments or analysis on how their method might scale to these more challenging scenarios.

## Questions for Authors
1. How would you expect the meta-learner to behave when presented with tasks that have different numbers of examples (e.g., 1-shot vs. 10-shot) than it was trained on? Could it adapt to these different regimes without retraining?

2. The paper mentions that the meta-learner learns a "task-common initialization" for the learner classifier. Could you provide more concrete evidence or examples of how this initialization differs from random initialization and why it's beneficial for few-shot learning?

3. The paper states that the meta-learner is trained to make a specific number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot). How sensitive is the performance to the number of updates the meta-learner is trained for? Would training for a different number of updates (e.g., 10 instead of 12) yield significantly different results?
