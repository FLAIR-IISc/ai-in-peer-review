

# Peer Review: "Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a novel meta-learning approach for few-shot learning by framing optimization as a sequence of LSTM updates. The authors propose an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier through a parameter update rule inspired by gradient descent. The key contributions are:

1. An elegant LSTM-based meta-learner that represents the learner's parameter updates as the LSTM cell state, with input and forget gates learning adaptive learning rates and parameter forgetting mechanisms.

2. A method that simultaneously learns a task-common initialization for the learner classifier and an effective optimization strategy for few-shot learning.

3. Empirical results demonstrating that the proposed approach outperforms simple baselines and achieves competitive performance with the state-of-the-art Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet for 1-shot and 5-shot classification.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the meta-learning literature, particularly for the challenging few-shot learning setting. The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of meta-learning and few-shot learning, citing relevant prior work and clearly articulating how their approach differs from existing methods.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning (few-shot learning) with a well-motivated and novel approach that builds upon the meta-learning literature in a meaningful way.
2. The experimental results are compelling, showing clear improvements over baselines and competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods on a standard benchmark.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field for several reasons:

**Methodological Innovation:** The key insight that the LSTM cell state update can be used to represent the learner's parameter updates is elegant and well-executed. The authors successfully leverage the LSTM architecture to learn both the initialization of the learner network and the optimization strategy simultaneously. This is a significant improvement over previous work on learning optimization algorithms (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), which focused on general optimization rather than few-shot learning.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experiments are well-designed and appropriately conducted. The authors use a standard benchmark (Mini-ImageNet) and compare against relevant baselines, including a nearest-neighbor baseline and a fine-tuned baseline, as well as the state-of-the-art Matching Networks. The results are statistically sound, with the paper noting confidence intervals where appropriate.

**Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology, careful discussion of related work, and a thorough presentation of the experimental results. The computational graph in Figure 2 is particularly helpful for understanding the meta-learning process.

**Theoretical Foundation:** While the paper doesn't provide strong theoretical guarantees, the LSTM analogy to gradient updates provides a reasonable and intuitive foundation for the approach. The authors carefully discuss the gradient independence assumption and justify its use.

The paper's contribution is significant because it directly addresses a core limitation of standard deep learning in the few-shot setting: the inability of gradient-based optimization to converge quickly from random initialization. By learning a task-common initialization and an effective optimization strategy, the authors provide a principled way to overcome this limitation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements to enhance its quality:

1. **Dataset Construction Details:** The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet by selecting 100 classes from ImageNet. They should provide more details about their dataset construction process (e.g., how they selected the 100 classes, whether they used the same splits as Vinyals et al., 2016, or how their dataset differs). This would improve reproducibility and help readers better understand the experimental setup.

2. **Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component. For example:
   - What happens if you remove the forget gate or input gate?
   - How does the performance change if you use a simpler initialization (e.g., random weights instead of learned initial weights)?
   - How does the performance change if you fix the number of updates to be the same for 1-shot and 5-shot tasks?

3. **Comparison with Andrychowicz et al.:** While the paper cites Andrychowicz et al. (2016) as related work, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach. A more detailed comparison of the key differences between their method and Andrychowicz et al.'s (e.g., focus on few-shot learning vs. general optimization) would strengthen the paper's positioning.

4. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the meta-learner compared to standard training approaches. A brief discussion of training time and memory requirements would be valuable for practitioners.

5. **Limitations Section:** The paper should include a more thorough discussion of limitations. For example, how does the approach scale to more complex architectures (e.g., ResNets) or larger datasets? How does it handle cases where the number of updates during meta-testing differs from meta-training?

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. **Dataset Construction:** You mention creating your own version of Mini-ImageNet by selecting 100 classes from ImageNet. Could you provide more details about your class selection process, and how your dataset differs from the original Mini-ImageNet used in Vinyals et al. (2016)? Would you expect your results to generalize to the original Mini-ImageNet dataset?

2. **Fine-tuning Baseline Performance:** You note that the fine-tuned baseline performed worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline, especially in the 1-shot case. Could you elaborate on why this occurred? Is it due to overfitting during the few updates, or are there other factors at play?

3. **Update Number Sensitivity:** You train different models for 1-shot (12 updates) and 5-shot (5 updates) tasks. How sensitive is the performance to the number of updates used during meta-training? What happens if you train a 1-shot model with 5 updates instead of 12?

4. **Scalability:** How do you expect your approach to scale to more complex architectures (e.g., ResNets) and larger datasets? Have you experimented with more complex learners, and if so, what were the results?

5. **Theoretical Justification:** While the LSTM analogy is compelling, could you provide more theoretical justification for why this approach should work better than standard optimization methods in the few-shot setting? Are there any guarantees about the convergence properties of the learned optimization strategy?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the meta-learning literature. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. With the minor revisions suggested above, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
