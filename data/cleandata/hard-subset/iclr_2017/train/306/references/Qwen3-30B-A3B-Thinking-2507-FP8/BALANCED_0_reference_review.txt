

# Review for "LSTM-based Meta-Learner Optimizer for Few-Shot Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm for few-shot classification tasks. The key insight is to frame the optimization process as an LSTM cell state update, where the meta-learner learns both a beneficial initialization for the classifier and an effective update rule for the parameters. The authors claim their approach outperforms baseline methods and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art metric learning approaches like Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's core contribution is interesting but the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims due to critical methodological issues and an unjustified simplifying assumption.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper addresses an important problem in few-shot learning and builds on relevant prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016). However, two key issues prevent acceptance:

First, the paper makes a significant simplifying assumption (Section 3.3.1) that the gradients of the meta-learner with respect to the learner's parameters can be computed without considering the dependency between the learner's gradients and the meta-learner's parameters. The authors acknowledge this complicates gradient computation but provide no justification for why this assumption is valid. Without this assumption, the approach would require expensive second derivatives, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that this simplification doesn't substantially degrade the learned optimization strategy. This is a critical gap in the methodological foundation.

Second, the experimental evaluation contains several methodological flaws. The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet (Section 5.1) but don't specify how it differs from the standard version used in prior work (Vinyals et al., 2016). The paper claims they select "a random 100 classes from ImageNet" but doesn't explain how they handled class balance or other potential differences. Additionally, they use a different network architecture (4 layers with 32 filters) for Matching Networks compared to the original paper (4 layers with 64 filters), making the comparison potentially misleading. The paper also lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the meta-learner's components (e.g., input gate vs. forget gate).

The claim that the meta-learner "learns a task-common initialization" is not adequately supported by the results. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the learned initialization is superior to other initialization methods (e.g., standard random initialization or pre-training), nor does it show how the meta-learned initialization compares to other initialization strategies.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Theoretical justification**: The paper needs a stronger theoretical foundation for the gradient independence assumption. The authors should either provide empirical evidence that this assumption is reasonable or propose a more rigorous alternative that doesn't require this simplification.
- **Experimental rigor**: The authors should reproduce experiments using the standard Mini-ImageNet split to enable fair comparison with prior work. They should also include ablation studies showing the contribution of each component of their meta-learner (e.g., comparing models with and without the forget gate).
- **Architecture details**: Provide complete details of the network architectures used for all methods, including the exact number of filters per layer for Matching Networks, to ensure fair comparison.
- **Visualization analysis**: The gate visualization (Figure 3) is interesting but lacks interpretation. The authors should explain how the observed gate behavior corresponds to the optimization strategy and why it leads to better performance.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How exactly did you create your Mini-ImageNet dataset? What specific classes were selected from ImageNet, and how did you handle class distribution differences compared to Vinyals et al. (2016)?
2. The paper states that Matching Networks used "4 layers each with 64 filters" but your implementation uses "4 layers with 32 filters" for the meta-learner. How did you ensure this architectural difference doesn't significantly impact the comparison?
3. The paper states "we were still able to train the meta-learner effectively in spite of this simplifying assumption" (Section 3.3.1). What evidence do you have that this assumption doesn't substantially degrade the quality of the learned optimization strategy?
4. How did you select the hyperparameters for the meta-learner (e.g., LSTM size, learning rate)? Were these selected on the validation set, and if so, how did you prevent overfitting to the validation set?
