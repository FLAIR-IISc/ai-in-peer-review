

# Review for ICLR Submission

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims/Contribution

This paper proposes an innovative LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning tasks. The authors make a compelling argument that standard gradient-based optimization algorithms fail in few-shot settings due to two main issues: (1) these algorithms weren't designed for a limited number of updates, and (2) random initialization hurts convergence with few examples. Their key insight is that the update rule of gradient descent resembles an LSTM cell state update, allowing them to frame the optimization process as an LSTM. The meta-learner learns both a good initialization for the classifier (via the initial cell state) and an effective update strategy (via the input and forget gates). The paper demonstrates that their approach outperforms simple baselines and achieves competitive results with Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides useful visualizations of the learned optimization strategy.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

**Key reasons for this decision:**
1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning by framing optimization as an LSTM update process, which is conceptually elegant and makes a clear contribution to the meta-learning literature.
2. The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms baselines and achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### Strengths:
- **Novel Conceptual Framework**: The connection between LSTM cell updates and optimization steps is clever and well-explained. This provides a fresh perspective on meta-learning that is both theoretically grounded and practically implementable.
- **Clear Motivation**: The paper effectively explains why standard optimization fails in few-shot settings and how their approach addresses these limitations.
- **Strong Baseline Comparisons**: The paper includes two meaningful baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) that provide a good reference point for evaluating their method.
- **Useful Visualizations**: The visualization of the meta-learner's behavior (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how the meta-learner adapts its strategy across different tasks.
- **Rigorous Implementation Details**: The paper provides sufficient technical details about the architecture, training procedure, and preprocessing steps.

### Minor Weaknesses:
- **Limited Comparison to Recent State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares against Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) but doesn't include more recent methods like Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017) or Relation Networks (Sung et al., 2018), which have since achieved better results. This makes it difficult to assess how competitive the method truly is with the current state-of-the-art.
- **Dataset Construction Limitation**: The paper creates its own version of Mini-ImageNet, which makes direct comparison with Vinyals et al. (2016) challenging. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't thoroughly discuss how their dataset construction might affect the results.
- **Lack of Ablation Study**: The paper doesn't provide an ablation study to show the importance of different components (e.g., the input gate vs. forget gate, the initial cell state learning, etc.).

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen the paper for publication, I recommend addressing the following points:

1. **Expand the Experimental Comparison**: Include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art methods in few-shot learning (e.g., Prototypical Networks, Relation Networks, MAML) to better establish the relative performance of your method. This would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution claim.

2. **Clarify Dataset Construction**: Provide more details about how your Mini-ImageNet version was constructed and discuss potential limitations or differences compared to the standard Mini-ImageNet. Consider running experiments on the standard Mini-ImageNet if possible to ensure fair comparison.

3. **Add an Ablation Study**: Include an ablation study to show the contribution of different components of your meta-learner (e.g., the input gate, forget gate, and initial cell state learning). This would help readers understand which aspects of your approach are most important.

4. **Discuss Computational Efficiency**: While not the focus of the paper, briefly discuss the computational cost of training and using your meta-learner compared to other methods. This would help readers understand the practical trade-offs of your approach.

5. **Clarify the "Optimal Starting Point" Claim**: The paper claims the meta-learner learns "an optimal starting point for fine-tuning" but doesn't provide strong evidence for this beyond the performance gains. Consider adding a small experiment that explicitly shows the meta-learned initialization outperforms random initialization or other initialization methods.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me better understand and assess the paper, I'd appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. The paper states "For 5-shot, we are able to do much better than Matching Networks, whereas for 1-shot, the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks." Could you provide more details about the confidence intervals (e.g., standard deviations or standard errors) to help readers better assess the statistical significance of your results?

2. The paper mentions that the meta-learner is explicitly trained to make the set number of updates used during meta-testing. How sensitive is the performance to this choice? Would the model still work well if trained for a different number of updates than it's used with during meta-testing?

3. In Section 3.2, the paper describes parameter sharing across gradient coordinates. Could you clarify how this sharing works in practice? For example, does the same LSTM update rule apply to all gradient coordinates, or are there subtle differences based on the specific coordinate?

4. The paper states "we found that the suggested value of p = 10 in the above formula worked well in our experiments." What happens if you use different values of p? Is the performance robust to changes in this hyperparameter?

5. The paper claims to "learn a task-common initialization for the learner classifier." How does this initialization compare to other common initialization methods (e.g., Xavier, He initialization) when used with the same number of updates? This would help clarify the value of the meta-learned initialization.

Thank you for your thoughtful contribution to the meta-learning literature. The paper presents a novel and promising approach that, with the suggested revisions, would make a strong contribution to the ICLR community.
