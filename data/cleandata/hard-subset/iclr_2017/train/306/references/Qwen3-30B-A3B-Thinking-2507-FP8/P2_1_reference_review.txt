

# Review of "LSTM-based Meta-Learner Optimizer for Few-Shot Learning"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper addresses the challenging few-shot learning problem where models must generalize from very few labeled examples per class. The authors propose a novel LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier. Their key insight is that the update rule of gradient descent resembles the cell state update in an LSTM, allowing them to train a meta-learner to learn both a beneficial initialization for the learner network and an effective update rule. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms natural baselines and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art metric learning methods on the Mini-ImageNet dataset for both 1-shot and 5-shot classification tasks. The paper provides a clear visualization of the meta-learner's learned strategies through gate values, showing that it adapts different optimization approaches for different tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper tackles a meaningful problem in machine learning (few-shot learning) with a well-motivated approach that builds on relevant literature.
2. The methodology is clearly articulated, and the experimental results support the claims made in the paper, showing promising performance compared to baselines and state-of-the-art methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the few-shot learning literature by proposing a meta-learner that simultaneously learns both a good initialization and an effective update rule for the learner network. This is a significant insight because it addresses two key limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in the few-shot setting: the lack of task-specific initialization and the need for an optimization strategy tailored to limited updates.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough related work section that properly situates their approach relative to previous meta-learning and few-shot learning work. The connection to Andrychowicz et al. (2016) is particularly relevant, and the authors clearly differentiate their focus on few-shot learning from the broader optimization problem addressed in that work.

The experimental results are convincing, with the authors showing that their method outperforms baselines and achieves competitive results with Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016). The visualization in Figure 3 provides additional evidence that the meta-learner is learning meaningful strategies that vary between tasks, which supports the paper's claims about the meta-learner's ability to adapt to different learning conditions.

While the paper has some minor issues (detailed below), these don't undermine the core contribution. The problem is important, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the results are scientifically rigorous.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend addressing the following points:

1. **Clarify the training of the initial cell state (c0)**: The paper mentions that the meta-learner learns the initial value of the cell state, but the training algorithm (Algorithm 1) doesn't show how this is updated. Please provide more details about how c0 is learned and updated during meta-training. Is it a parameter that's optimized alongside the LSTM parameters, or is it learned during the meta-training process?

2. **Justify the gradient independence assumption**: The paper makes the simplifying assumption (Section 3.3.1) that the losses and gradients of the learner are independent of the meta-learner's parameters. Please provide more justification for this assumption and discuss potential limitations. How might violating this assumption affect the quality of the meta-learner?

3. **Clarify the meta-learner architecture**: The paper mentions a "2-layer LSTM" where "the first layer is a normal LSTM and the second layer is our modified LSTM meta-learner." This is confusing. Please clarify what the second layer does and why it's necessary. Is the second layer a standard LSTM layer or something else?

4. **Provide more evidence for the "task-common initialization" claim**: The paper states that the meta-learner learns a "task-common initialization," but it's not clear how this is evaluated. Please provide specific experiments or metrics that demonstrate why this initialization is better than standard initialization methods (e.g., Xavier or He initialization).

5. **Detail the learner network architecture**: The paper describes the learner as a "simple CNN containing 4 convolutional layers," but doesn't specify the number of filters in each layer or the exact architecture. Please provide a more detailed description of the learner network architecture.

## 5. Questions for Clarification

1. How is the initial cell state c0 trained? Is it updated during the meta-training process, or is it a fixed parameter learned during meta-training? The paper states it's "treated as a parameter of the meta-learner," but it's not clear how this parameter is optimized.

2. The paper mentions that for 1-shot learning, "the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks." How confident are the authors that their method is actually better than Matching Networks for 1-shot learning, given this overlap? What statistical tests were performed to establish significance?

3. The paper states that "the meta-learner model is trained to converge a learner classifier to a good solution quickly on each task." What metrics were used to define "quickly" and "good solution" during training? How does the meta-learner's performance on the meta-training set correlate with its performance on the meta-test set?

4. The paper mentions that they "use a fixed number of updates for fine tuning" in their baselines. What was this fixed number, and how was it chosen? How does this compare to the number of updates used by the meta-learner?

5. The paper states that "the meta-learner is trained to converge a learner classifier to a good solution quickly on each task." Can the authors provide specific examples of how the meta-learner's update rule differs from standard gradient descent, particularly in the 1-shot vs. 5-shot settings?
