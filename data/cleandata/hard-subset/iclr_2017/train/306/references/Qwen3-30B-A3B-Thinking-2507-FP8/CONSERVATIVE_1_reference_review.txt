

# Review

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims

The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer that learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier for few-shot learning tasks. The authors claim that their approach addresses the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings by learning both a beneficial initialization for the classifier and an effective update rule. The meta-learner is framed as an LSTM whose cell state represents the learner's parameters, with gates learning to adjust the learning rate and "forget" parts of the parameter state. The method is evaluated on Mini-ImageNet for 1-shot and 5-shot classification, claiming to outperform baselines and achieve competitive results with Matching Networks.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant improvement over established methods, and the approach doesn't provide compelling evidence that it substantially advances the state of the art in few-shot learning.

## 3. Supporting arguments

The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous empirical validation against appropriate baselines. While the authors compare against two baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning), they fail to compare against the most relevant recent meta-learning approaches that have become standard in the field, such as MAML (Finn et al., 2017) or Reptile (Nichol et al., 2018). These are now considered essential baselines for few-shot learning work, and their absence severely undermines the paper's claim of being "competitive with state-of-the-art."

The paper's dataset creation method (selecting 100 classes from ImageNet and using 64/16/20 for train/val/test) differs from the standard Mini-ImageNet splits used in prior work (Vinyals et al., 2016), making direct comparison impossible. The authors acknowledge this by noting "the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset," but this undermines their ability to claim competitive performance.

The paper lacks statistical significance testing for their performance claims. The paper states that "for 1-shot, the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks," but doesn't provide the actual confidence intervals or statistical tests, making it impossible to assess whether the differences are meaningful.

The paper's theoretical justification for framing optimization as an LSTM update is tenuous. The authors claim the update resembles an LSTM cell state update, but this is a superficial analogy that doesn't provide any theoretical justification for why this specific architecture would outperform existing methods. The paper doesn't demonstrate that their LSTM-based optimizer has any fundamental advantages over standard optimization algorithms.

The paper's claim that their method "learns a task-common initialization for the learner classifier" is not adequately supported. The paper doesn't compare against standard pre-training approaches (e.g., ImageNet pre-training) that are commonly used in few-shot learning, making it impossible to determine if the meta-learned initialization provides any meaningful advantage.

## 4. Additional feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Compare against current standard baselines in few-shot learning (MAML, Reptile, etc.) rather than only using simple baselines.

2. Use the standard Mini-ImageNet splits to allow direct comparison with prior work.

3. Provide statistical significance testing for all performance claims.

4. Clarify the relationship between their approach and existing meta-learning work, particularly Andrychowicz et al. (2016) which also uses LSTMs for meta-learning.

5. Address the "gradient independence assumption" more thoroughly - this is a significant simplification that could impact performance, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that ignoring these terms doesn't substantially affect the results.

6. Provide more insight into why their approach works better than standard fine-tuning. The paper notes that "the fine-tuned baseline is worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline" due to overfitting, but doesn't explain why their meta-learner avoids this problem, which is crucial for their claim of being a better approach.

7. The paper claims to learn "a good initialization for the learner's parameters" but doesn't demonstrate that this initialization is indeed better than standard pre-training approaches. A comparison against ImageNet pre-training would be essential.

## Questions for authors

1. Why didn't you compare against MAML or Reptile, which are now standard baselines in few-shot learning? How would your method compare to these approaches?

2. The paper claims "the fine-tuned baseline is worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline" due to overfitting, but doesn't provide evidence that your meta-learner avoids this problem. How does your method specifically address the overfitting issue that plagues standard fine-tuning?

3. You mention "we found that the suggested value of p = 10 in the above formula worked well in our experiments." How sensitive is your method to this parameter choice? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis?

4. Your paper claims to "learn a task-common initialization," but doesn't compare against standard pre-training approaches (e.g., ImageNet pre-training). How does your meta-learned initialization compare to standard pre-training approaches in terms of performance?

5. How does your approach differ from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which also uses an LSTM to learn optimization algorithms? The paper cites this work but doesn't clearly distinguish their approach from this prior work.
