- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that frames classifier optimization as a sequence of LSTM updates for few-shot classification.
- Learns both an optimal initialization and a task-specific update rule, leveraging similarity between LSTM updates and gradient descent.
- Trains the optimizer to maximize performance after a fixed number of updates (e.g., 5 or 12), matching few-shot constraints.
- Demonstrates clear improvements over baselines and competitive performance with state-of-the-art on Mini-ImageNet 1-shot and 5-shot tasks.
- Visualization (Figure 3) provides insight into how the meta-learner adapts across tasks.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Strengths: well-motivated problem, thoughtful extension of learned optimizers, appropriate experimental design, and strong empirical results.
- Limitation: comparisons only include Matching Networks and not more recent few-shot methods (e.g., Prototypical Networks, Relation Networks, MAML).
- Limitation: insufficient details about how the Mini-ImageNet dataset was created and split, hurting reproducibility.
- Limitation: paper lacks discussion of computational/training costs of the meta-learner versus standard approaches.
- Limitation: unclear how the method differs concretely from Andrychowicz et al. (2016); needs clearer positioning.
- Suggestion: provide full dataset selection and train/val/test split details for Mini-ImageNet.
- Suggestion: expand experimental comparisons to include recent state-of-the-art few-shot methods.
- Suggestion: report training time and resource requirements to clarify practical trade-offs.
- Question: why does fine-tuning underperform nearest-neighbor in 1-shot (overfitting), and can regularization or meta-learner techniques mitigate this?
- Question: are performance differences statistically significant for 1-shot results (request p-values or more detailed analysis)?
- Question: how does the method generalize when the number of shots varies at meta-test time if trained for a fixed number of updates?
- Question: how does batch normalization affect performance in few-shot settings versus alternatives like layer normalization or no normalization?
