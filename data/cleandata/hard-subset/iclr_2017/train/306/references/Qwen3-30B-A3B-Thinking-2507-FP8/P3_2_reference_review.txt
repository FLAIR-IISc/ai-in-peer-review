

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes an innovative LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer that learns to optimize a classifier for few-shot learning tasks. The key contribution is recognizing that the LSTM cell state update rule resembles gradient descent optimization, allowing the meta-learner to learn a task-specific optimization algorithm. The authors frame this as a meta-learning problem where the meta-learner learns both a good initialization for the classifier's parameters and an effective update rule for the classifier. The method is trained to converge the learner classifier to a good solution quickly (with few updates) on each task, which is particularly valuable for few-shot learning where the number of training examples per class is limited (k=1 or k=5).

The paper demonstrates that their approach outperforms simple baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) and achieves competitive results with the state-of-the-art Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet. The authors also provide visualizations of the learned optimization strategy, showing that the meta-learner adapts its input and forget gate values based on the specific task.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and makes a novel connection between LSTM cell state updates and gradient descent optimization. The approach is grounded in solid theoretical foundations and addresses a meaningful problem in machine learning.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for this decision:
1. The paper makes a novel and well-motivated contribution to meta-learning, specifically addressing the few-shot learning problem with a theoretically grounded approach.
2. The experimental results are generally convincing and show that the method outperforms baselines and is competitive with state-of-the-art approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strengths are significant. The connection between LSTM cell state updates and gradient descent optimization is insightful and provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach. The method directly addresses a key limitation of standard optimization in few-shot settings (need for many updates and poor initialization), and the experimental results support the claim that the approach is effective.

However, there are some concerns that need to be addressed before final acceptance:

1. **Experimental comparison concerns**: The authors created their own version of Mini-ImageNet (selecting 100 classes from ImageNet), which makes direct comparison to Vinyals et al. (2016) problematic. The paper should provide more details about their dataset creation process and explicitly acknowledge that their results aren't directly comparable to the original Vinyals et al. results. They should also consider testing on additional few-shot learning benchmarks (like Omniglot or CIFAR-FS) to demonstrate broader applicability.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components (e.g., the input gate vs. forget gate, the initialization learning). This makes it difficult to determine what aspects of their approach are most important.

3. **Comparison to Matching Networks**: The authors compare to Matching Networks but note that "the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset." This undermines the significance of their comparison. They should either use the exact dataset splits from Vinyals et al. or provide a more detailed explanation of their dataset creation process to enable fair comparison.

4. **Theoretical justification**: While the connection between LSTM cell state and gradient descent is insightful, the paper lacks a more rigorous theoretical analysis of why their approach should work better than standard optimization methods in the few-shot setting.

5. **Comparison to other meta-learning methods**: The paper mentions MAML (Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning) was published around the same time as some of the cited work, but doesn't compare their approach to MAML or other contemporary meta-learning methods. This is a significant omission in the related work section.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Provide more details about dataset creation**: Include a table showing exactly which classes were selected from ImageNet for the Mini-ImageNet dataset, and provide the exact procedure for selecting 600 examples per class. This will make the results more reproducible and the comparison to prior work more meaningful.

2. **Add ablation studies**: Perform ablation studies to show the contribution of different components of the meta-learner (e.g., the input gate, forget gate, and initialization learning). This would help readers understand what aspects of the approach are most important.

3. **Test on additional benchmarks**: Extend the experiments to include other few-shot learning benchmarks like Omniglot or CIFAR-FS to demonstrate the broader applicability of the method.

4. **Improve comparison to Matching Networks**: Either use the exact dataset splits from Vinyals et al. (2016), or provide a more detailed explanation of the differences in dataset creation that affect the results. The paper should also clarify whether their implementation of Matching Networks matches the exact method described in Vinyals et al.

5. **Expand related work**: Include a more thorough comparison to contemporary meta-learning methods, particularly MAML (Finn et al., 2017), which was published in 2017 and has become a standard baseline for few-shot learning.

6. **Clarify the "beneficial common initialization" claim**: The paper claims the meta-learner learns a "task-common initialization" but doesn't provide strong evidence for this. Include an analysis showing that the initial weights learned by the meta-learner are indeed better than random initialization or transfer learning from ImageNet.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and provide additional evidence for my assessment, I'd appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. You mention that "the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks" for 1-shot learning. Could you provide the exact confidence intervals (e.g., mean ± standard error) for both methods?

2. The paper states that "we found that better performance for each task was attained if the meta-learner is explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing." Could you explain why this is the case? Is this a general property of the meta-learner or specific to your implementation?

3. You mention that the fine-tuned baseline was worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline. Given that this is a common approach in few-shot learning, could you provide more details about why this happened (e.g., overfitting due to lack of regularization)?

4. Did you consider using the meta-learner to fine-tune the network using the training set during meta-testing (as mentioned in Vinyals et al., 2016)? If so, how did this affect the results, and why did you choose not to include it in your main experiments?

5. How does your method scale to more complex tasks or larger datasets? Have you tested it on tasks with more than 5 classes, or on datasets with more complex visual patterns?

These questions would help me better understand the strengths and limitations of your approach and provide a more confident assessment of your paper.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning. With the suggested revisions, it would make a valuable contribution to the meta-learning literature.
