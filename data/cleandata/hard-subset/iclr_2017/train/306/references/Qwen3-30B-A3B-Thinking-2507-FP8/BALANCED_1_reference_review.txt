

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning tasks. The authors frame the problem within a meta-learning setting, where the meta-learner is trained to optimize a learner neural network classifier by learning both a beneficial initialization and an effective update rule for the classifier's parameters. The key insight is that the LSTM cell state update resembles gradient descent updates, allowing the meta-learner to learn adaptive optimization strategies. The approach is evaluated on Mini-ImageNet for 1-shot and 5-shot classification, showing competitive results compared to baselines and Matching Networks.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's contribution is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work, and the experimental evaluation does not provide convincing evidence to support the claims of novelty and effectiveness.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary reason for rejection is that the paper fails to adequately demonstrate the novelty of its approach. While the authors cite Andrychowicz et al. (2016) as related work, their method builds directly on that work (using an LSTM to learn optimization updates) without clearly articulating how their specific formulation differs or improves upon it. The paper does not provide a thorough comparison of their method to Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which would be necessary to establish their contribution's significance. 

Second, the experimental evaluation has critical limitations. The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet (using 100 randomly selected classes from ImageNet) rather than using the exact splits from Vinyals et al. (2016), making direct comparison of results impossible. The results in Table 1 show different accuracy values than reported in Vinyals et al. (2016), but the authors do not explain why these differences exist or whether their dataset creation procedure affects performance. Without comparable experimental conditions, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed competitive results are truly meaningful.

Third, the paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to understand why their method works. For example, the authors claim their approach learns "a task-common initialization" but provide no evidence that this initialization is actually beneficial compared to standard initialization methods. The visualization in Figure 3 shows some gate values but doesn't clearly demonstrate how these values lead to better performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper for future submission, the authors should:

1. Clearly differentiate their contribution from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which is the closest prior work. Specifically, explain how their approach is tailored for few-shot learning (e.g., different number of updates, different task structure) and why these differences matter.

2. Conduct experiments using the exact Mini-ImageNet splits from Vinyals et al. (2016) to enable fair comparison. If they cannot use those exact splits, they should explicitly discuss the potential impact of their dataset creation procedure on results.

3. Include ablation studies that isolate the contribution of the learned initialization versus the learned update rule. For example, they could compare their meta-learner with a fixed initialization (e.g., random or pre-trained) to determine the relative benefits of each component.

4. Provide more detailed analysis of the learned update rules (Figure 3) to explain how they differ from standard optimizers and why they're beneficial for few-shot learning.

5. Clarify the computational complexity and scalability of their approach compared to standard optimizers, which is particularly important for practical applications.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does the specific architecture of your LSTM meta-learner differ from Andrychowicz et al.'s approach, and what specific modifications were made to adapt it to the few-shot learning setting?

2. Given that your dataset creation procedure differs from Vinyals et al. (2016), what evidence do you have that the differences in dataset composition (100 randomly selected classes vs. specific splits) do not significantly impact the performance comparison?

3. In Figure 3, you show variations in gate values between datasets. Can you provide more detailed analysis of how these variations correlate with task difficulty or performance?

4. The paper claims your method learns "a beneficial common initialization." What specific evidence do you have that this learned initialization is better than standard initialization methods (e.g., random, Xavier, or pre-trained weights) when applied to the few-shot learning task?
