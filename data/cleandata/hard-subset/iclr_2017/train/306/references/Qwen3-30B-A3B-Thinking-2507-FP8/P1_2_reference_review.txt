

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes an innovative LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning, addressing a fundamental challenge in machine learning: training effective classifiers from very few labeled examples. The authors make a compelling connection between the cell state update in LSTMs and gradient descent optimization, framing the meta-learning problem as training an LSTM to learn both an optimal initialization for a learner network and an effective update rule for few-shot tasks. The key contributions include:

- A novel meta-learner architecture that uses an LSTM to learn the update rule for a classifier network, where the LSTM's cell state represents the learner's parameters.
- A method that simultaneously learns a task-common initialization for the learner network (addressing the random initialization problem) and an effective optimization strategy.
- Empirical results demonstrating that the approach outperforms natural baselines and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art metric learning methods on Mini-ImageNet.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and makes a significant contribution to the meta-learning literature by providing a systematic way to learn beneficial initialization and optimization strategies for few-shot learning.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for this decision:
1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning that addresses fundamental limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in the small data regime.
2. The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in the 5-shot setting.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong case for the proposed approach. The problem of few-shot learning is well-motivated, with compelling examples from both human learning and practical applications. The authors correctly identify two key limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in this setting: the lack of convergence guarantees with limited updates and the poor performance of random initialization.

The connection between LSTM cell state updates and gradient descent is insightful and well-executed. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation showing how the LSTM update rule can be adapted to learn an optimization strategy. The implementation details, including parameter sharing and preprocessing methods, are well-considered and address potential challenges in applying this approach.

The empirical results are generally convincing. The method outperforms the two baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) and achieves competitive results with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet. The ablation study showing the benefits of meta-training the initialization is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates the value of end-to-end training of the classifier initialization.

However, there are some concerns that need addressing before acceptance:
- The claimed superiority over Matching Networks in 5-shot learning is somewhat overstated, as the confidence intervals for the 1-shot results overlap, and the exact numbers differ from Vinyals et al. due to dataset differences.
- The comparison with Matching Networks does not include the fully-conditional embedding (FCE) version, which was the state-of-the-art in Vinyals et al.
- The paper lacks sufficient analysis of why the meta-learner learns the specific update rules it does, beyond the visualizations in Figure 3.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend addressing the following points:

1. **Clarify experimental setup**: Provide a more detailed explanation of how your Mini-ImageNet differs from the original version used by Vinyals et al. (2016), including class selection, number of examples per class, and any preprocessing differences. This will help readers better interpret the results and understand the comparison.

2. **Expand comparison with Matching Networks**: Include results for the fully-conditional embedding version of Matching Networks, as this was the state-of-the-art in the original paper. The current comparison is limited to the basic version, which makes the claim of "competitive with state-of-the-art" less compelling.

3. **Deepen analysis of learned meta-learner behavior**: While the visualization in Figure 3 is interesting, provide more analysis of the learned strategies. For example, discuss how the meta-learner's behavior differs between 1-shot and 5-shot settings, and why it might learn specific gate values. Consider including a comparison with standard optimization methods to highlight the meta-learner's advantages.

4. **Address computational efficiency**: Discuss the computational cost of your approach compared to standard fine-tuning or other meta-learning methods. This is particularly important for practical applications of few-shot learning.

5. **Consider broader applicability**: While the paper focuses on few-shot learning, briefly discuss how the approach might be extended to other settings beyond the few-shot regime, as suggested in the conclusion.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that your results for 5-shot are "much better than Matching Networks" while for 1-shot the confidence intervals intersect. Could you provide more detail on the statistical significance of these results, and whether the difference in 5-shot is statistically significant?

2. You mention that you created your own version of Mini-ImageNet because the exact splits from Vinyals et al. (2016) weren't released. Could you provide more details about how you constructed your dataset (e.g., how you selected the 100 classes, whether you used the same train/val/test splits as the original paper, etc.) to help readers better understand the comparison?

3. In your experimental setup, you note that you train different models for 1-shot and 5-shot tasks with different numbers of updates. Could you explain why the optimal number of updates differs between these settings, and whether this is a general property of the meta-learner or specific to this dataset?

4. The paper mentions that the fine-tuned baseline is worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, particularly in the 1-shot setting where the fine-tuned model is more prone to overfitting? This insight would help readers understand the value of your meta-learner approach.

5. Have you considered applying your meta-learner to other few-shot learning benchmarks beyond Mini-ImageNet, such as Omniglot or tiered-ImageNet? If not, would you expect similar performance gains on these datasets?

These questions would help clarify the strengths and limitations of your approach, and I believe addressing them would substantially strengthen the paper.
