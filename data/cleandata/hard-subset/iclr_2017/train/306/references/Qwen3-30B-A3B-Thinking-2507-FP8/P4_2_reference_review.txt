

# Review

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer specifically designed for few-shot learning problems. The authors address the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in the few-shot regime (where each task has only k labeled examples per class) by framing the problem within a meta-learning framework. Their key contribution is a meta-learner that learns both an optimal initialization for the learner network and a task-specific update rule, allowing the learner to quickly converge to good solutions with minimal updates. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and positioned within the relevant literature of meta-learning and few-shot learning.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the few-shot learning literature with promising empirical results that outperform baselines and are competitive with state-of-the-art methods at the time of writing.

## Supporting Arguments
1. **Problem and Motivation**: The paper clearly identifies the specific problem of few-shot learning and provides a strong motivation for why standard optimization approaches fail in this regime. The authors effectively position their work within the literature, distinguishing their approach from previous meta-learning methods (particularly noting that Andrychowicz et al. (2016) trained a general optimizer, while this work is specifically tailored to few-shot learning).

2. **Technical Soundness**: The LSTM-based meta-learner formulation is well-justified, with the update rule directly borrowing from LSTM cell state updates. The authors provide a clear explanation of the parameter sharing strategy and preprocessing methods, which are necessary for scalability. The training procedure properly matches test-time conditions, which is crucial for meta-learning. The gradient independence assumption is acknowledged as a simplification but the authors demonstrate they can still achieve good results with it. The results on Mini-ImageNet are scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines, statistical significance testing (confidence intervals), and careful comparison to Matching Networks.

3. **Novelty**: While the idea of using LSTMs for meta-learning has been explored (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), this paper makes a novel contribution by specifically targeting the few-shot learning problem with a focus on learning both initialization and update rules. The approach is distinct from metric-based methods like Matching Networks, which represent a different paradigm for few-shot learning.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper could strengthen its comparison by including a brief discussion of how the approach relates to more recent meta-learning methods (like MAML) that emerged after the cited Vinyals et al. (2016) paper, even if a direct comparison wasn't included in the original submission.

2. The dataset creation process for Mini-ImageNet could be described more precisely to aid reproducibility (e.g., exact procedure for selecting classes from ImageNet, ensuring no overlap between training/validation/test sets).

3. The visualization of gate values (Figure 3) provides interesting insights, but the paper could offer more interpretation about what specific patterns in the gate values correspond to in terms of optimization behavior.

## Clarification Questions
1. The paper states "we create our own version of the Mini-Imagenet dataset by selecting a random 100 classes from ImageNet and picking 600 examples of each class." Could you provide more details about how the 600 examples were selected (e.g., random sampling, ensuring diversity within classes) and whether this differs from the standard Mini-ImageNet split used in other works?

2. The paper compares against Matching Networks but doesn't include a comparison to MAML, which was published in 2017 (after the Vinyals et al. paper cited). Did the authors have access to MAML when writing this paper? If so, why wasn't it included in the comparison? If not, would you be able to provide a brief comparison to MAML in a revised version?

3. The paper mentions that "the meta-learner is explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing." How did the authors determine the optimal number of updates for the 1-shot and 5-shot tasks, and how sensitive is the performance to this choice?
