

# Review for "LSTM-based Meta-Learner Optimizer"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer designed specifically for few-shot learning tasks. The core idea is to frame the optimization process as an LSTM, where the cell state represents the learner's parameters, and the input and forget gates learn how to update these parameters. The authors claim their method learns both a beneficial initialization for the classifier and an effective update rule that enables rapid convergence with few examples. The paper demonstrates competitive results on Mini-ImageNet compared to baselines and Matching Networks for 1-shot and 5-shot learning.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. The main reasons are: (1) the experimental comparison with prior work lacks rigor and fairness, making it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method, and (2) the paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from prior work on learning optimization algorithms, particularly Andrychowicz et al. (2016).

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core concept—using an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm—is not novel, as it directly builds on Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which developed a similar approach for general optimization. While the authors position their work in the few-shot learning context, they fail to convincingly demonstrate why this specific approach is particularly advantageous for few-shot learning rather than being a general optimization method. The paper doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why their LSTM meta-learner would be especially effective in few-shot scenarios compared to standard optimization approaches.

The experimental evaluation suffers from several critical issues that undermine the claimed performance benefits. The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet without providing details on how it differs from the original, making direct comparison with Vinyals et al. (2016) difficult. The comparison with Matching Networks is further complicated by using different network architectures (32 vs. 64 filters), adding dropout to Matching Networks, and not clarifying whether hyperparameters were tuned for Matching Networks. The paper claims to outperform Matching Networks in 5-shot learning but only matches in 1-shot learning, yet provides no confidence intervals or statistical tests to assess whether these differences are meaningful.

Additionally, the paper lacks ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of specific components of the meta-learner (input gate, forget gate, learned initialization). Without these, it's impossible to determine which aspects of the approach are actually driving the performance gains.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this work, the authors should:

1. Conduct a more rigorous comparison with prior work by using the exact dataset splits and network architectures as in the original papers they're comparing to, or clearly document the differences and justify their choices.

2. Include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (input gate, forget gate, learned initialization) to the overall performance.

3. Provide statistical significance tests or confidence intervals for all results to demonstrate the robustness of their claims.

4. Better distinguish their contribution from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) by explicitly addressing why their specific focus on few-shot learning makes their approach novel and advantageous.

5. Provide more theoretical justification for why the LSTM meta-learner is particularly well-suited for few-shot learning, rather than being a general optimization method.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How exactly does your version of Mini-ImageNet differ from the one used in Vinyals et al. (2016)? What specific classes were selected, and how were they split?

2. Why did you choose 32 filters for your CNN instead of 64 as used in Matching Networks? Did you tune this hyperparameter, and if so, how did it affect performance?

3. Can you provide confidence intervals or statistical significance tests for your results, particularly for the 1-shot case where your performance intersects with Matching Networks?

4. Did you tune the hyperparameters for Matching Networks (e.g., learning rate, number of updates) to ensure a fair comparison, or did you use the same hyperparameters as the original paper?

5. What is the computational cost of your method compared to the baselines and Matching Networks, and how does it scale with the number of updates?
