

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a novel LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning, making a compelling contribution to the field. The authors identify two key limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in the few-shot setting: 1) optimization algorithms aren't designed for few updates, and 2) random initialization hurts convergence with few examples. Their solution frames optimization as an LSTM process, where the cell state represents the learner's parameters. This meta-learner learns both a beneficial initialization for the learner network and an effective update rule, allowing the learner to generalize well from very few examples. The paper demonstrates that their approach outperforms natural baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) and achieves competitive results with the state-of-the-art Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet. The technical approach is well-motivated, the writing is clear, and the experiments provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** the paper.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper makes a significant contribution to meta-learning with two key strengths:

a) **Novelty and Technical Contribution**: The connection between LSTM cell state updates and gradient-based optimization is elegant and well-justified. The authors don't merely propose a new model but provide a principled framework for learning both initialization and update rules. This advances beyond existing meta-learning approaches by explicitly modeling the optimization process itself, rather than just learning a classifier or embedding.

b) **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on Mini-ImageNet provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness. The comparison to baselines shows clear improvements (e.g., 5-shot performance at 83.5% vs. 73.1% for fine-tuning baseline), and the results are competitive with Matching Networks. The visualization of the meta-learner's behavior (Figure 3) offers valuable insights into how the method works, showing that the meta-learner adapts its strategy across different tasks.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with a thorough discussion of related work that clearly distinguishes their approach from prior meta-learning methods. The motivation for the few-shot learning problem is compelling, connecting to both human learning capabilities and practical applications where labeled data is scarce.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

- **Dataset Comparison**: The authors created their own Mini-ImageNet version, which makes direct comparison with Vinyals et al. (2016) challenging. Adding a brief analysis of dataset differences (e.g., class overlap, image distributions) would help readers understand potential performance variations.

- **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions using 12 updates for 1-shot and 5 for 5-shot, but doesn't discuss how sensitive results are to these choices. A small ablation study showing performance across different numbers of updates would strengthen the empirical claims.

- **Training Details**: More details about hyperparameter selection (e.g., why the gradient clipping value of 0.25 was chosen) would improve reproducibility. The paper mentions using ADAM with learning rate 0.001 but doesn't explain how this was determined.

- **Broader Impact**: A brief discussion of potential applications beyond benchmark datasets would strengthen the paper's relevance to real-world problems where few-shot learning is needed.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in the assessment, I'd like to ask:

a) Your 5-shot results (83.5%) significantly outperform the Matching Networks result (75.5%) reported by Vinyals et al. (2016). Given you created your own Mini-ImageNet dataset, could you elaborate on the specific differences between your dataset and the one used by Vinyals et al.? How do you think these differences might impact the performance comparison?

b) You mention that "better performance for each task was attained if the meta-learner is explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing." Could you provide more details on how you determined these specific numbers (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot)? Were you able to test other numbers, and if so, how did performance vary?

c) The paper states that "the fine-tuned baseline is worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline" due to overfitting. Could you elaborate on how you prevented overfitting in the nearest-neighbor baseline? Did you use any regularization techniques, or was it simply due to the nature of the nearest-neighbor approach?

These questions would help ensure that the evaluation of the method is complete and that the results are properly contextualized within the few-shot learning literature.
