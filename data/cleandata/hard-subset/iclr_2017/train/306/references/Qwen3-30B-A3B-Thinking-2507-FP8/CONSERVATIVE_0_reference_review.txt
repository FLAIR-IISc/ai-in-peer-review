

# Review

## Summary
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning, where the meta-learner learns an update rule for training a classifier using an architecture inspired by the LSTM cell state update. The authors claim their method learns both a good initialization for the learner network and an effective optimization strategy for few-shot tasks. They evaluate on Mini-ImageNet, reporting competitive results against baselines and Matching Networks.

## Decision
Reject. The key reasons are: (1) the novelty is limited as the approach builds directly on Andrychowicz et al. (2016) without sufficient differentiation for the few-shot learning context, and (2) the evaluation lacks rigor, particularly with the creation of their own dataset version and incomplete comparison with more recent methods.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's core idea is not sufficiently novel. Andrychowicz et al. (2016) already proposed using an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm for training neural networks, though they focused on large-scale classification rather than few-shot learning. The authors fail to adequately explain how their approach differs from this prior work in the specific context of few-shot learning. Their method appears to be a straightforward adaptation of Andrychowicz et al. to a different task setting, without demonstrating how the few-shot nature of the problem necessitates or benefits from their specific implementation.

The evaluation methodology is problematic. The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet (by randomly selecting 100 classes from ImageNet), making results directly incomparable to Vinyals et al. (2016) and other prior work. Their comparison against Matching Networks is also questionable because they don't use the same network architecture (they use 4-layer CNNs with 32 filters, while Vinyals et al. used 4-layer CNNs with 64 filters) and their implementation details differ. The results for 1-shot learning show confidence intervals that overlap with Matching Networks, making the claim of "competitive" performance unsubstantiated.

The paper fails to compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods in few-shot learning, such as MAML (Finn et al., 2017), which has become the standard for this task. This omission significantly undermines the paper's claims about performance. The authors also don't provide sufficient justification for why they believe their approach would outperform these more established methods.

The paper makes a simplifying assumption that the losses and gradients of the learner are independent of the meta-learner's parameters, which is a known limitation in this literature. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide evidence that this assumption significantly impacts performance in their specific few-shot context.

## Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide a more thorough comparison with Andrychowicz et al. (2016) specifically in the few-shot learning context, rather than treating it as a separate contribution.
2. Reproduce results on the standard Mini-ImageNet dataset to ensure direct comparability with prior work.
3. Include comparisons against more recent methods like MAML, Prototypical Networks, and Relation Networks, which have become standard benchmarks in few-shot learning.
4. Provide a clearer explanation of how their approach specifically addresses the unique challenges of few-shot learning beyond what is already covered by general meta-learning methods.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your approach specifically differ from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) in the few-shot learning context, beyond the dataset and task differences? What novel insights does your method provide for few-shot learning that aren't captured by simply applying their approach to this specific setting?
2. Why did you not compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods like MAML, which has become the dominant approach for few-shot learning? How would your method compare to these methods in terms of both performance and computational requirements?
3. Can you provide details about why your dataset version (100 classes selected from ImageNet) is better than the standard Mini-ImageNet used by Vinyals et al. (2016), and how the differences in dataset construction might affect the results?
4. How do you explain the fact that your fine-tuned baseline performs worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline? This seems counterintuitive and requires deeper analysis.
