

# Review

## Summary
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer that frames the few-shot learning problem within a meta-learning setting. The authors' key insight is that the parameter update rule in gradient descent resembles an LSTM cell state update, and they train an LSTM to learn an optimal update rule for a learner neural network classifier. The meta-learner is designed to learn both a beneficial initialization for the learner's parameters and an effective update mechanism for few-shot learning tasks. The authors claim their approach outperforms baselines and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art metric learning methods on Mini-ImageNet.

## Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple critical technical errors that undermine the validity of the proposed method and its results.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Critical Mathematical Error in Preprocessing**: The paper's Equation (3.2) for preprocessing contains a significant mathematical error: "x→ {( log(|x|) p , sgn(x) ) if |x| ≥ e−p (−1, epx) otherwise}". This should be "log(|x|)/p" (logarithm divided by p) but is written as "log(|x|) p" (logarithm multiplied by p). This error would fundamentally alter the preprocessing method and likely invalidate the meta-learner's ability to properly process gradient information. The authors should have caught this error during proofreading.

2. **Algorithm 1 Logical Error**: In Algorithm 1, step 10 states "ct ← R((∇θt−1Lt,Lt); Θd−1)" where the meta-learner R is supposed to output the cell state. However, the meta-learner should be outputting the updated parameters θt, not the cell state ct. The paper claims "the output of meta-learner using Equation 2," but Equation 2 describes the cell state update, not the parameter update. This creates a fundamental logical inconsistency in the algorithm's description.

3. **Missing Results**: The paper claims "the results are shown in Table 1" but the table is not included in the provided text. Without seeing the actual results, it's impossible to verify the claimed performance improvements over baselines and Matching Networks.

4. **Inconsistent Reference Formatting**: The references contain multiple formatting errors, including strange backslashes ("Andrychowicz et al\.,? \Q2016\E"), inconsistent citation styles, and missing information. For example, the reference for Andrychowicz et al. (2016) has "CoRR, abs/1606.04474,, Andrychowicz et al\.,? \Q2016\E" which contains unnecessary commas and backslashes.

5. **Insufficient Explanation of Key Design Choices**: The paper states they train "different models for the 1-shot and 5-shot tasks, that make 12 and 5 updates, respectively" without explaining how these specific numbers were chosen or why they were optimal. This lack of justification for a critical hyperparameter choice weakens the experimental validation.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Preprocessing Error**: Please correct the preprocessing formula to "log(|x|)/p" (logarithm divided by p) rather than "log(|x|) p" (logarithm multiplied by p). This is critical for the method's validity.

2. **Algorithm Clarification**: Revise Algorithm 1 to clarify that the meta-learner outputs the updated parameters θt (not the cell state ct), and ensure the description matches the mathematical formulation.

3. **Include Results**: Provide the actual results in Table 1 to verify the claimed performance improvements.

4. **Reference Formatting**: Standardize all references to follow consistent formatting conventions, removing unnecessary backslashes and special characters.

5. **Hyperparameter Justification**: Explain the rationale for choosing 12 updates for 1-shot and 5 updates for 5-shot tasks. Provide evidence that these numbers were systematically optimized rather than arbitrarily chosen.

6. **Clarify Batch Normalization**: The paper's approach to batch normalization in meta-learning is confusing. Explain why standard batch normalization can't be used during meta-testing, and provide evidence that the proposed solution is superior to simply using running averages from meta-training.

## Clarification Questions

1. Can you provide the corrected preprocessing formula and confirm that the current version in the paper was a typo?

2. Why does the meta-learner output the cell state in Algorithm 1 when it should be outputting the updated parameters? Please clarify this discrepancy.

3. Can you provide the actual results from Table 1 to support your claims about performance?

4. How did you determine the optimal number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot) for the meta-learner? Was this determined through a hyperparameter search, and if so, what was the search space?

5. How does your approach to batch normalization during meta-testing differ from simply using the running averages from meta-training, and why does your approach produce better results?
