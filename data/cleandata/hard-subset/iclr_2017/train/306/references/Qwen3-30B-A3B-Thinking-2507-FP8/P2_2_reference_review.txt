

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper presents an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning, where the goal is to train a model that can generalize from very few labeled examples (1-5 examples per class). The authors correctly identify two main challenges with standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot learning: (1) optimization algorithms aren't designed for a limited number of updates, and (2) random initialization hurts convergence. Their key contribution is framing the optimization process within a meta-learning setting, where an LSTM meta-learner learns both a good initialization for the learner network and an effective update rule for few-shot tasks. The paper demonstrates competitive performance against baselines and Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet, with promising results for 5-shot learning and comparable results for 1-shot learning. The approach is well-motivated, clearly articulated, and makes a meaningful contribution to the meta-learning literature.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is the omission of comparison to MAML (Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning), which was published in the same year (2017) and has become the standard baseline for few-shot learning. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the paper's contribution is truly novel or significant in the current landscape of meta-learning research.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper has several strong aspects: the motivation for few-shot learning is solid, the technical approach is well-explained, and the empirical results are promising. However, the omission of MAML comparison is a critical flaw that significantly weakens the paper's contribution claim.

The paper claims to be "competitive to the state-of-the-art in metric learning for few-shot learning," but fails to compare against MAML, which was published in the same year (2017) and has since become the de facto standard for few-shot learning benchmarks. MAML represents a major advance in meta-learning that would be expected to be compared against, especially since it's a direct competitor to the authors' approach. The paper's comparison to Matching Networks is insufficient without this key benchmark.

Additionally, the paper creates its own version of the Mini-ImageNet dataset (selecting 100 classes from ImageNet), which differs from Vinyals et al. (2016). The authors note "the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset," but this makes it impossible to assess the true performance of their method relative to the established benchmark. Without using the same dataset splits as the original Vinyals et al. work, the results cannot be directly compared.

The paper also lacks an ablation study to show the contribution of specific components of their model (e.g., the forget gate vs. input gate), which would help readers understand what aspects of their approach are most valuable.

## 4. Additional Feedback

### To Improve the Paper:

1. **Compare with MAML**: The most critical improvement needed is to compare against MAML, which was published in the same year and has become the standard baseline in meta-learning. Without this comparison, the paper's contribution claim is unsubstantiated.

2. **Use Standard Dataset Splits**: To enable direct comparison with prior work, the authors should use the exact dataset splits from Vinyals et al. (2016) rather than creating their own version.

3. **Include Ablation Study**: Add an ablation study showing the contribution of specific components of the meta-learner (e.g., the forget gate vs. input gate, the effect of the preprocessing method).

4. **Expand Discussion on Scalability**: The paper mentions future work on "moving towards this more challenging scenario" (few or lots of training examples and classes), but this should be discussed more thoroughly in the current paper.

5. **Clarify Methodology**: The paper mentions "batch normalization" but doesn't fully explain how it's implemented in the meta-learning context. A clearer explanation would help readers understand the methodology.

### Questions for the Authors:

1. Why was MAML not included in the comparison? Given that MAML was published in the same year (2017), it would be expected to be compared against in a paper claiming to be competitive with the state-of-the-art.

2. How would you explain the discrepancy in results with Vinyals et al. (2016) if you had used the same dataset splits? The authors note "the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset," but this makes it impossible to assess the true performance of their method.

3. Can you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the forget gate versus the input gate in the LSTM meta-learner? This would help readers understand which components are most valuable.

4. How would your approach scale to more complex tasks with larger datasets? The paper focuses on simple CNNs and Mini-ImageNet, but the authors mention future work on more challenging scenarios—how does the current approach lay the foundation for this?

This paper has a solid core idea and promising results, but the omission of MAML comparison and the use of a non-standard dataset split significantly weaken its contribution. With these critical improvements, the paper would be much stronger and more suitable for acceptance at ICLR.
