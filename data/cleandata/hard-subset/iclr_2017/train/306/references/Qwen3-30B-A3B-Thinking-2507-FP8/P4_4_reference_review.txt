

1. Summary:
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer designed specifically for few-shot learning tasks. The authors observe that gradient descent updates resemble LSTM cell state updates, and leverage this insight to train an LSTM that learns both an optimal initialization for a learner network and an effective update rule. The meta-learner processes gradients and losses from the learner to produce parameter updates, with the goal of achieving good generalization after only a few training examples. The approach is evaluated on Mini-ImageNet, where it achieves competitive results compared to baselines and Matching Networks, particularly for 5-shot learning.

2. Decision:
Reject. The main reasons are: (1) the paper's core idea is not sufficiently novel compared to prior work, and (2) the experimental evidence does not convincingly demonstrate the claimed advantages for few-shot learning.

3. Supporting arguments:
The key contribution appears to be applying an LSTM-based optimizer (previously proposed by Andrychowicz et al., 2016) to the few-shot learning problem. While Andrychowicz et al. trained their optimizer for standard classification tasks, the current paper adapts it for few-shot learning. However, the paper fails to clearly articulate why this adaptation is necessary or why the prior approach wouldn't work for few-shot learning. The authors acknowledge Andrychowicz et al. but don't sufficiently differentiate their contribution.

The experimental results are somewhat limited and lack sufficient statistical rigor. The authors created their own Mini-ImageNet dataset (using 100 random classes from ImageNet), which makes direct comparison with Vinyals et al. (2016) difficult. The paper states that their results don't match Vinyals et al. due to dataset differences, but doesn't provide evidence that their dataset is a fair comparison. For the 1-shot case, the confidence intervals for their method and Matching Networks overlap, making the claimed superiority unclear.

The paper also lacks theoretical justification for why their approach should work better in the few-shot regime. While they demonstrate empirical success, they don't analyze how the learned meta-optimizer differs from standard optimizers in ways that specifically address the few-shot challenge. The connection to the few-shot setting is primarily empirical rather than theoretical.

4. Additional feedback:
- The paper would benefit from a clearer, more detailed comparison with Andrychowicz et al. (2016), including an analysis of why their approach is better suited for few-shot learning. The authors should explicitly state what limitations of the prior work their method addresses.
- Provide more details about the Mini-ImageNet dataset construction process to enable better reproducibility and comparison with prior work.
- Include statistical significance tests for the performance differences, especially for the 1-shot results where confidence intervals overlap.
- Add a more detailed analysis of the meta-optimizer's learned behavior specifically in the few-shot regime, rather than general observations about gate values.

5. Questions for the authors:
- How does your meta-learner specifically address the challenges of few-shot learning that Andrychowicz et al.'s (2016) approach does not? Can you provide evidence that Andrychowicz et al.'s method would perform poorly on few-shot tasks without your adaptation?
- The paper states that you created your own version of Mini-ImageNet. How do you ensure that your dataset is comparable to the one used by Vinyals et al. (2016) for fair comparison? What specific differences exist between the datasets, and how might these affect the results?
- For the 1-shot results, the confidence intervals for your method and Matching Networks overlap. Could you provide statistical tests to determine if the performance differences are significant?
- The paper mentions that the meta-learner learns a task-common initialization. How does this initialization differ from standard pre-training approaches, and why is it particularly beneficial for few-shot learning?
