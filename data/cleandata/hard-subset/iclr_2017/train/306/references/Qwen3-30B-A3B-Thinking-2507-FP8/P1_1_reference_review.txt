

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning tasks, addressing the challenge of training neural networks when only a small number of labeled examples are available per class. The authors frame the problem within a meta-learning setting, where the meta-learner is trained to learn an update rule for training a neural network classifier. The key contributions include:

1. An LSTM-based meta-learner that captures both short-term knowledge within individual tasks and long-term knowledge common across all tasks.
2. A formulation that allows the meta-learner to learn a task-common initialization for the learner classifier, effectively replacing random initialization.
3. An objective that directly optimizes for good generalization performance given a limited number of updates.

The authors claim that their approach outperforms natural baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuned) and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art metric learning methods like Matching Networks on few-shot classification tasks.

The paper is well-structured, with a clear motivation for the problem, a solid theoretical foundation, and a detailed experimental evaluation. The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of meta-learning and few-shot learning literature, building on previous work by Andrychowicz et al. (2016) while addressing the specific challenges of the few-shot learning regime.

## 2. Decision

**Weak Accept (with major revisions)**

The paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning that builds upon existing meta-learning techniques. However, the experimental results need stronger statistical support and more direct comparison to current state-of-the-art methods to fully validate the claims. The paper would benefit from additional ablation studies and more transparent reporting of the experimental setup.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper has several strengths that make it suitable for acceptance with revisions:

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly articulates the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in the few-shot learning setting and provides a compelling rationale for the meta-learning approach. The problem statement is well-justified and aligned with current challenges in machine learning.

2. **Novelty and Technical Contribution**: While building on Andrychowicz et al. (2016), the authors successfully adapt the LSTM-based optimizer to the few-shot learning problem, which is a significant contribution. The focus on learning both a good initialization and an effective update mechanism is well-motivated.

3. **Clear Experimental Setup**: The experimental design follows standard practices in few-shot learning literature, using the Mini-ImageNet dataset and comparing against reasonable baselines.

However, several concerns prevent a strong acceptance:

1. **Limited Comparison to Current State-of-the-Art**: The paper only compares against Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) and two baselines. It does not compare against more recent meta-learning methods like MAML (Finn et al., 2017) or Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017), which have become standard benchmarks in few-shot learning. This makes it difficult to assess how competitive the approach truly is.

2. **Lack of Statistical Significance Analysis**: The paper claims to "attain results that are much better than the baselines" and "competitive with Matching Networks" but does not provide statistical significance tests for these claims. The confidence intervals for the 1-shot results overlap with Matching Networks, making the claim of "much better" for 5-shot questionable.

3. **Dataset Creation Details**: The paper creates its own version of Mini-ImageNet by selecting 100 classes from ImageNet, but does not provide details on how these classes were selected (e.g., whether they were randomly chosen or based on some criteria). This lack of transparency makes it difficult to reproduce the experiments or compare directly with previous work.

4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost**: The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the meta-learner training compared to other methods, which is an important consideration for practical applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen this paper for publication, I recommend the following revisions:

1. **Expand Experimental Comparisons**: Add comparisons against more recent meta-learning methods (MAML, Prototypical Networks, Relation Networks) to better position the work within the current literature.

2. **Include Statistical Significance Tests**: Provide p-values or more detailed confidence intervals to support the claims about performance differences.

3. **Clarify Dataset Creation Process**: Provide specific details about how the 100 classes for Mini-ImageNet were selected from ImageNet, including the random seed if applicable, to ensure reproducibility.

4. **Add Computational Cost Analysis**: Include a discussion of the training and inference time for the meta-learner compared to other methods.

5. **Conduct Ablation Studies**: Perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of learning the initialization versus learning the update rule. This would help clarify which aspect of the approach is most important.

6. **Improve Figure 3 Interpretation**: Provide more detailed analysis of the visualization in Figure 3 to explain what the varying gate values mean in terms of optimization strategy.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. How did you select the 100 classes for your Mini-ImageNet dataset? Was this selection random, and if so, can you provide the specific random seed or selection process used?

2. Why did you choose to compare only against Matching Networks rather than other recent meta-learning methods like MAML or Prototypical Networks, which have become standard benchmarks in few-shot learning?

3. The paper claims to outperform Matching Networks for 5-shot but notes that the confidence interval for 1-shot performance intersects with Matching Networks. Can you provide more detailed statistical analysis (e.g., p-values) to support the claim of "much better" performance for 5-shot?

4. How does the computational cost of training and using your meta-learner compare to the baselines and Matching Networks? Specifically, what are the training time and inference time differences?

5. In Figure 3, the input gate values show significant variation between different datasets. Can you provide more insight into what this variation means for the optimization strategy and how it contributes to the meta-learner's effectiveness?

6. The paper mentions that the meta-learner is explicitly trained to make the set number of updates used during meta-testing. Was this number of updates a hyperparameter that was tuned, and if so, how did you determine the optimal number of updates?

These questions are intended to help clarify the experimental setup and results, which would strengthen the paper's claims and make the contribution more clearly understood.
