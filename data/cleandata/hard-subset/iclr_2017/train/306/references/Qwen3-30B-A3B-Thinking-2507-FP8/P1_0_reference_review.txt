

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer designed for few-shot learning tasks. The authors identify the key challenges in few-shot learning: standard gradient-based optimization algorithms aren't designed for few updates, and random initialization hurts convergence. They propose framing the optimization process as an LSTM where the cell state represents the learner's parameters, with the LSTM gates learning how to update these parameters effectively for new tasks with very few examples.

The key contributions include:
- A novel LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm specifically tailored for few-shot learning
- A formulation that allows the meta-learner to learn both a good initialization for the learner's parameters and an effective update mechanism
- Empirical results showing their method outperforms simple baselines and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art metric learning approaches

The paper is well-motivated, clearly positioning itself within the meta-learning literature and explaining the limitations of existing approaches to few-shot learning. The connection between gradient descent updates and LSTM cell states is insightful and forms a solid foundation for their approach.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

Key reasons for rejection:
1. The experimental results are not presented with sufficient statistical rigor to support the paper's claims of "much better" performance than baselines
2. The dataset creation methodology is not properly justified, making it difficult to compare results with previous work

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core idea is interesting and theoretically sound, but the evaluation section has significant issues that undermine the contribution:

**Experimental Rigor Concerns:**
- The paper claims "we attain results that are much better than the baselines" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing or confidence intervals to support this claim. The statement that "the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks" is vague and doesn't provide clear statistical evidence.
- The paper states "we create our own version of the Mini-Imagenet dataset" without explaining why the standard version from Vinyals et al. (2016) wasn't used. This is a major red flag because it prevents direct comparison with previous work, which is essential for few-shot learning papers.
- The paper doesn't discuss computational costs of training the meta-learner, which is critical for a method that involves training a meta-learner on top of a learner network.

**Methodological Concerns:**
- The paper claims the meta-learner "learns a task-common initialization" but provides no clear evidence for this beyond the visualization in Figure 3, which doesn't clearly demonstrate commonality across tasks.
- The learner network architecture (4 convolutional layers with 32 filters each) is very simple and not comparable to the more complex architectures used in state-of-the-art few-shot learning methods.
- The paper doesn't address the memory requirements of the meta-learner, which could be substantial given the LSTM-based approach.

**Comparison with Related Work:**
- The paper cites Andrychowicz et al. (2016) as similar work but doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from this prior work. The paper claims "we are interested in the few-shot learning problem" while Andrychowicz et al. focus on "learning a general optimization algorithm for large-scale classification," but the paper doesn't explain how their approach differs in a meaningful way from this prior work.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper significantly, the authors should:

1. **Use the standard Mini-ImageNet dataset** (or at least provide a clear comparison between their dataset and the standard one) to allow proper benchmarking against previous work.

2. **Provide statistical significance testing** for all results, including confidence intervals, p-values, or other appropriate statistical measures to support claims about performance differences.

3. **Conduct a thorough ablation study** to demonstrate the contribution of different components of their model (e.g., the forget gate vs. input gate, the meta-learner initialization, etc.).

4. **Include computational cost analysis** comparing training time, inference time, and memory requirements with other methods.

5. **Provide more evidence for the "task-common initialization" claim** through additional visualizations or quantitative analysis.

6. **Clarify the relationship with Andrychowicz et al. (2016)** and explain how their approach is specifically tailored for few-shot learning rather than general optimization.

7. **Consider using more complex learner architectures** to better compare with state-of-the-art few-shot learning methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. You state "we create our own version of the Mini-Imagenet dataset" but don't explain why you didn't use the standard version from Vinyals et al. (2016). How does your dataset differ from the standard one, and how might these differences impact your results?

2. The paper claims your method "attains results that are much better than the baselines" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing. Can you provide p-values or confidence intervals to support this claim?

3. The paper mentions that "the meta-learner is trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing" - what happens if the number of updates during meta-testing differs from meta-training? Have you tested this?

4. How does your method scale to more complex datasets or larger numbers of classes? The paper focuses on 5-class settings but few-shot learning often involves more classes.

5. Can you provide more details on the computational requirements (training time, memory usage) of your meta-learner compared to standard few-shot learning methods?
