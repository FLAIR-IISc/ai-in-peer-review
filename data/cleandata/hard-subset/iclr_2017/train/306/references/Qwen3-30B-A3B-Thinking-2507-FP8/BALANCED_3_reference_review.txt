

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning, where the meta-learner learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier. The key insight is that the LSTM update rule resembles gradient descent, allowing the meta-learner to learn both a task-common initialization for the learner and an effective update strategy for few-shot learning. The authors claim their method outperforms baselines and achieves competitive results with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes an incremental contribution to meta-learning with insufficient experimental rigor to support its claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is incremental rather than transformative. While the authors correctly cite Andrychowicz et al. (2016) as prior work on learning optimization algorithms with LSTMs, they fail to clearly articulate how their specific adaptation for few-shot learning advances the field. The core idea of using an LSTM to learn optimization is not novel.

The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient rigor for a top-tier conference. The authors create their own version of Mini-ImageNet without explaining how it differs from Vinyals et al.'s benchmark, making direct comparison impossible. They acknowledge this limitation but don't provide sufficient justification for their dataset creation process. The comparison with Matching Networks is also problematic—the authors implemented their own versions but don't provide implementation details or hyperparameters, making it difficult to assess whether they're fairly comparing to the state-of-the-art.

The results are presented without statistical significance testing. The paper claims their method outperforms Matching Networks for 5-shot learning, but the confidence intervals for 1-shot learning overlap, making the claim less compelling. The visualization of gate values (Figure 3) is interesting but doesn't provide clear insights into why their method works better.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Clarify novelty**: Explicitly state how your approach differs from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) in the context of few-shot learning. What specific challenges of few-shot learning require this adaptation, and why wouldn't their general optimization approach work directly?
- **Improve experimental rigor**: Use the exact Mini-ImageNet splits from Vinyals et al. (2016) to enable direct comparison. Provide complete implementation details for all baselines, including hyperparameters and training procedures.
- **Add statistical validation**: Include statistical significance tests for your results to demonstrate that performance improvements are meaningful.
- **Expand analysis**: Go beyond visualizations of gate values to analyze *why* your method works better. What specific aspects of the optimization strategy does the meta-learner learn that lead to better performance?
- **Discuss limitations**: Address potential limitations such as computational cost, scalability to more complex tasks, and sensitivity to hyperparameters.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How does your method specifically adapt to the few-shot learning setting compared to Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which also used an LSTM to learn optimization?
2. Can you provide details on how you created your Mini-ImageNet dataset and why it's a valid benchmark for comparison with prior work?
3. Why did you choose to use the same number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot) as the number of examples per class (k)? How would performance change if you used a different number of updates?
4. What is the computational cost of your meta-learner compared to standard training and the baselines? Is the additional complexity justified by the performance gains?
5. How does your method handle cases where the number of examples per class is larger than the number of updates (e.g., k=10 with T=5 updates)?

The paper has potential but requires significantly more rigorous experimentation and clearer articulation of its contribution to the field. With these improvements, it could be a valuable contribution to meta-learning literature.
