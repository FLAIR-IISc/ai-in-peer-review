

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper proposes an innovative LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning. The authors argue that standard gradient-based optimization fails in few-shot settings due to two main reasons: (1) existing optimizers aren't designed for limited updates, and (2) random initialization hinders convergence with few examples. Their key contribution is framing the problem within a meta-learning framework where an LSTM meta-learner learns to optimize a classifier network by treating the learner's parameter updates as an LSTM cell state. The meta-learner simultaneously learns both a task-common initialization for the classifier and an effective update rule for the few-shot setting. The approach is evaluated on Mini-ImageNet for 1-shot and 5-shot classification, showing competitive results against baselines and Matching Networks. The paper makes a strong conceptual connection between gradient-based optimization and LSTM cell state updates, offering a fresh perspective on meta-learning.

## 2. Decision: Weak Accept

I recommend a **weak accept** with the following key reasons:
1. The paper presents a novel and conceptually sound approach that effectively addresses a well-identified problem in few-shot learning.
2. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines and competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's core idea is compelling and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard optimization in few-shot settings and provide a principled solution by learning an update rule that incorporates both short-term task knowledge and long-term task-common knowledge. The conceptual connection between LSTM cell state updates and gradient-based optimization is elegant and well-explained, making the approach theoretically grounded.

The experimental results are generally convincing. The method outperforms the baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) by a significant margin, particularly in the 5-shot setting. The comparison with Matching Networks is fair, and the results are competitive (especially for 5-shot). The visualization of gate values provides interesting insights into the meta-learner's behavior, suggesting it adapts its strategy to different tasks and shot settings.

However, there are some concerns that prevent a stronger recommendation. The dataset version differs from Vinyals et al. (2016), making direct comparison challenging. The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about the exact implementation (e.g., specific hyperparameters, training details) to ensure reproducibility. While the authors note their dataset differs, they could have provided more context about how this affects the comparison. The confidence intervals for the results aren't clearly reported, which would strengthen the statistical claims. The paper also doesn't thoroughly analyze why the method works better than baselines beyond the overfitting explanation for the fine-tuning baseline.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend:

1. **Provide more experimental details**: Include specific hyperparameters (learning rates, batch sizes, LSTM dimensions), training time, and computational resources. This would help other researchers reproduce your results.

2. **Clarify dataset differences**: More explicitly discuss how your Mini-ImageNet version differs from Vinyals et al.'s version and how this might affect the comparison. Consider running experiments with the exact Vinyals dataset if possible.

3. **Expand the analysis**: Provide more in-depth analysis of why the meta-learner's approach outperforms baselines beyond the overfitting observation. For instance, how does the learned initialization differ from standard pre-training? Could you analyze the quality of the embeddings produced by your method?

4. **Include ablation studies**: Ablate the different components of your meta-learner (e.g., removing the forget gate, changing the preprocessing) to demonstrate the necessity of each design choice.

5. **Discuss limitations**: Acknowledge limitations more explicitly, such as scalability to larger datasets or whether the method generalizes to other modalities beyond images.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that "the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset." Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your dataset construction process versus Vinyals' original version? How might these differences affect the comparison?

2. In Section 3.3.1, you mention making the gradient independence assumption. How did you verify that ignoring these second-order terms doesn't significantly degrade performance? Could you provide evidence that the approximation is valid for your task?

3. In the visualization (Figure 3), you note "there seems to be a lot of variability between different datasets." Could you quantify this variability and discuss how it relates to the diversity of the tasks in your meta-training set?

4. The paper states that "for 5-shot, we are able to do much better than Matching Networks, whereas for 1-shot, the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks." Could you provide the exact confidence intervals for these results?

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to few-shot learning that makes a valuable contribution to the meta-learning literature. With some additional experimental details and analysis, it would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
