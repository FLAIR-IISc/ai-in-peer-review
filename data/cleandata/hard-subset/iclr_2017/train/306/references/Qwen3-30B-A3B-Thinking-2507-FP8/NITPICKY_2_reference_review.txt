

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer for few-shot learning, framed within a meta-learning setting. The authors claim their method learns both a beneficial common initialization and an effective update rule for a learner neural network classifier, allowing it to generalize from very few labeled examples. The approach is inspired by gradient descent optimization and leverages the LSTM cell state update to represent the learner's parameter updates. The authors report competitive results compared to baselines and Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper references Algorithm 1, Table 1, and Figure 3 but fails to include them in the submission, making it impossible to evaluate the methodology, results, and visualizations. Additionally, there are multiple formatting inconsistencies that undermine the paper's credibility.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The primary reason for rejection is the absence of key components referenced in the paper. Algorithm 1 is described as "the training algorithm" but not included (Section 3.3). Table 1 is cited as showing "results" (Section 5.1) but is not present. Figure 3 is referenced as providing "visualization of the meta-learner" (Section 5.2) but is missing. Without these critical elements, it is impossible to assess the technical contribution or empirical results, which are essential for a machine learning paper.

Beyond the missing components, the paper contains multiple minor but significant formatting inconsistencies that suggest a lack of meticulous attention to detail, which is crucial for a top-tier conference like ICLR. For example:
- Inconsistent use of hyphens in "t-1" (Section 3.1) vs "t−1" (Section 3.1)
- Mathematical notation errors in the preprocessing formula: "e−p" should be "e^{-p}" (Section 3.2)
- Citation format inconsistencies: "Yosinski et al., 2014" vs "Yosinski et al.,? \Q2014\E" (References)
- Inconsistent terminology: "5-shot, 5-class" should be "5-shot, 5-way" to align with standard few-shot learning conventions (Section 5.1)

The paper also makes a questionable assumption in Section 3.3.1 (gradient independence) without proper justification or discussion of its implications. While the authors acknowledge they avoid second derivatives, they fail to provide evidence that this simplification doesn't significantly impact performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- **Include all referenced components**: Please ensure Algorithm 1, Table 1, and Figure 3 are included in the final submission. These are essential for evaluating the paper's claims.
- **Fix mathematical notation**: Standardize the use of "t-1" vs "t−1" throughout the paper, and correct the preprocessing formula to use proper mathematical notation (e.g., "e^{-p}" instead of "e−p").
- **Clarify terminology**: Use standard few-shot learning terminology (e.g., "5-shot, 5-way" instead of "5-shot, 5-class").
- **Improve citation format**: Ensure all citations follow a consistent format (e.g., "Andrychowicz et al., 2016" instead of "Andrychowicz et al.,? \Q2016\E").
- **Justify the gradient independence assumption**: Provide a theoretical or empirical justification for why ignoring the dependency of the learner's gradients on the meta-learner's parameters is reasonable.

## Questions for Authors
1. Why did you choose to make the gradient independence assumption (Section 3.3.1), and what evidence do you have that this doesn't significantly impact the meta-learner's performance?
2. What is the theoretical basis for choosing p=10 in the preprocessing formula (Section 3.2), and how sensitive is the model to this parameter?
3. How does your 2-layer LSTM architecture (Section 3.3) compare to using a single-layer LSTM, and what was the specific motivation for the second layer being a "modified LSTM meta-learner"?
4. Could you clarify the relationship between your method and Andrychowicz et al. (2016)? The authors note that their work differs from Andrychowicz et al. in being focused on few-shot learning rather than general optimization, but the technical approach appears very similar. Please elaborate on the key differences and why your approach is better suited for few-shot learning.
