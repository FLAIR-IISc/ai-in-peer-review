

# Review

## Summary of Contribution
This paper presents a novel meta-learning approach for few-shot classification by framing the optimization process itself as an LSTM-based meta-learner. The key insight is recognizing the structural similarity between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent parameter updates, allowing the meta-learner to learn both an optimal initialization and an adaptive update rule for the learner network. The authors propose a bold conceptual shift: rather than using fixed optimization algorithms, they train an LSTM to learn the optimal update strategy for a given few-shot learning task. This approach directly addresses the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings by learning task-specific adaptation mechanisms. The paper is well-motivated, clearly positioned within the meta-learning literature, and presents a theoretically sound yet practically implementable framework.

## Decision
Accept. This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to the meta-learning field by reimagining the optimization process as a learnable component within the meta-learning framework. The novelty of framing optimization as an LSTM-based process represents a bold and creative direction that goes beyond incremental improvements in few-shot learning.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's primary contribution is its novel conceptual framework that bridges LSTM architecture design with optimization algorithm learning. This is not merely another meta-learning method but a fundamental rethinking of how optimization algorithms themselves can be learned. The authors make a compelling case for why existing gradient-based optimizers fail in few-shot settings and how their approach directly addresses these limitations through a task-specific initialization and update rule.

While the empirical results are competitive rather than revolutionary (outperforming baselines and matching state-of-the-art metric learning methods), the conceptual novelty outweighs the incremental nature of the performance gains. The paper demonstrates a strong theoretical foundation with clear connections to both LSTM architectures and optimization theory. The implementation details, including parameter sharing and preprocessing techniques, show thoughtful consideration of practical challenges in applying this approach.

The paper successfully addresses the key problem of few-shot learning by learning both a good initialization and adaptive update strategy, which is a more comprehensive solution than previous approaches that focused solely on initialization (like transfer learning) or metric learning.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, consider the following suggestions:

1. Include more detailed statistical analysis of results (confidence intervals, p-values) to better demonstrate the significance of the performance differences against baselines and Matching Networks.

2. Provide a more thorough comparison of the computational complexity and training time requirements of your method versus previous approaches. This would help readers understand the practical trade-offs of your method.

3. Discuss potential limitations of the gradient independence assumption more thoroughly, including how it might affect performance on more complex tasks or with larger networks.

4. The paper mentions future work on "moving towards more challenging scenarios" - it would be helpful to provide a concrete roadmap for how the authors plan to extend their approach to handle varying numbers of examples and classes.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "the meta-learner is trained to converge a learner classifier to a good solution quickly on each task." Could you elaborate on how the training objective (Ltest) ensures that the meta-learner learns to converge quickly rather than just achieving good final performance?

2. In Table 1, your 5-shot results outperform Matching Networks, but your 1-shot results have overlapping confidence intervals. Given the importance of the 1-shot setting in few-shot learning, could you provide more detailed analysis of why the performance gap appears in 5-shot but not 1-shot?

3. The paper mentions that "the meta-learner could also be fine-tuned using this data" regarding Matching Networks. How would your method's performance change if you applied similar fine-tuning strategies to your meta-learner during meta-testing?

4. The paper states that "the meta-learner starts close to normal gradient descent with a small learning rate." Could you elaborate on how this initialization strategy compares to other initialization techniques for meta-learners, and why it was particularly effective for your approach?
