

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer designed specifically for few-shot learning tasks. The authors make the insightful observation that LSTM update rules closely resemble gradient descent update rules, allowing them to frame the problem of learning an optimal optimization algorithm as training an LSTM to generate parameter updates. The meta-learner simultaneously learns a task-common initialization for the learner's parameters and an optimization strategy that can quickly converge the learner to a good solution with only a few updates. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms natural baselines and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art methods on Mini-ImageNet for both 1-shot and 5-shot classification tasks, while providing valuable insights into the learned optimization strategy.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:
1) The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning that makes a clear contribution to the meta-learning literature.
2) The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with careful attention to the meta-learning setup and thoughtful comparison to appropriate baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings (insufficient data for convergence, poor random initialization) and position their work within the meta-learning literature. Their distinction from previous work (e.g., Andrychowicz et al., 2016) is particularly strong, as they focus specifically on few-shot learning rather than general optimization.

The methodology is sound and the connection between LSTM updates and gradient descent is elegantly leveraged. The authors' attention to critical details like batch normalization in the meta-learning context (Section 3.4) demonstrates deep understanding of the problem space. Their solution to the batch normalization issue—collecting statistics per dataset during meta-testing while erasing running statistics between datasets—is particularly thoughtful and addresses a common pitfall in meta-learning evaluations.

The experimental results are compelling. The authors provide a thorough comparison to two carefully chosen baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuned), correctly identifying that the fine-tuned baseline overfits in the 1-shot setting due to insufficient regularization. Their visualization of the meta-learner's gate values (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into how the meta-learner adapts its optimization strategy across different tasks, which is a significant contribution beyond just performance metrics.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, these improvements would strengthen it further:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper makes the gradient independence assumption (Section 3.3.1) without sufficient theoretical justification. A brief discussion of why this approximation is reasonable in the few-shot context would strengthen the methodology.

2. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper compares to Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016) but doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods like Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017) or Relation Networks (Sung et al., 2018). Including these comparisons would better position the work in the current literature.

3. **Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the meta-learner approach. For a field where efficiency is often critical, providing analysis of training time, inference time, and parameter counts would be valuable.

4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper uses a fixed number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot) but doesn't explore sensitivity to this choice. A brief analysis would help readers understand how robust the approach is to this hyperparameter.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does your meta-learner approach compare to more recent few-shot learning methods like Prototypical Networks? Would your meta-learner be compatible with these methods, or would you need to modify your approach?

2. The paper notes that the meta-learner's update rule "bears similarity to the GRU hidden state update." Could you elaborate on the specific differences and why you chose LSTM over GRU for this application?

3. You mention that the preprocessing parameter p=10 "worked well in our experiments." Could you provide more details on how you determined this value, and whether you observed sensitivity to this choice across different datasets or tasks?

4. In Figure 3, the input gate values show significant variation between different datasets. Could you elaborate on what patterns you observe in these variations and whether they correlate with specific dataset characteristics (e.g., class similarity, image complexity)?

This paper makes a significant contribution to meta-learning by providing a novel approach to few-shot learning that effectively leverages the LSTM's structure to learn a task-specific optimization strategy. The methodology is sound, the experiments are well-executed, and the insights into the learned optimization process add substantial value to the literature. With the suggested improvements, this would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
