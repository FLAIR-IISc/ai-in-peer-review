

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents a novel meta-learning approach for few-shot classification tasks by framing the optimization process as a sequence of LSTM updates. The authors propose an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns both an optimal initialization for a classifier network and a task-specific update rule for optimizing that classifier with very few training examples. The key insight is that the update rule of an LSTM closely resembles gradient descent updates, allowing the meta-learner to learn a more effective optimization strategy than standard methods. The paper makes a strong contribution by directly training an optimizer to maximize performance after a fixed number of updates (e.g., 5 or 12 updates), which is precisely the constraint in few-shot learning. The approach is well-motivated and represents a thoughtful extension of prior work on learning optimizers (Andrychowicz et al., 2016).

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

Key reasons for acceptance:
- The paper addresses a well-defined problem in few-shot learning with a novel technical approach that is well-motivated by the limitations of existing methods.
- The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines and competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong case for its approach. The motivation for few-shot learning is well-articulated, highlighting both the theoretical interest (human-like learning from few examples) and practical applications (reducing data collection needs). The authors correctly identify two key limitations of standard optimization approaches in the few-shot setting: (1) gradient-based methods aren't designed for constrained update counts, and (2) random initialization hurts convergence.

The core technical contribution—using an LSTM to learn a task-specific optimization strategy—builds effectively on prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016) while addressing a more specific problem (few-shot learning). The connection between LSTM cell state updates and gradient descent updates is insightful and well-explained. The paper's experimental design is appropriate for the problem, using the standard Mini-ImageNet benchmark with 1-shot and 5-shot classification tasks.

The results demonstrate clear improvements over the baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) and competitive performance with Matching Networks (Vinyals et al., 2016). For 5-shot learning, the proposed method significantly outperforms Matching Networks, and for 1-shot, the results are competitive within confidence intervals. The visualization of the meta-learner's behavior (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into how the model adapts to different tasks.

However, the paper could be strengthened by:
- Including comparisons to more recent few-shot learning methods beyond Matching Networks (e.g., Prototypical Networks, Relation Networks, MAML)
- Providing more details about their Mini-ImageNet dataset creation process
- Discussing computational costs of training the meta-learner versus standard approaches

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify dataset creation**: The paper mentions creating their own Mini-ImageNet dataset by selecting 100 classes from ImageNet, but doesn't provide details about the selection process. For reproducibility, please specify how classes were chosen and how examples were distributed across train/validation/test sets.

- **Expand comparison to recent methods**: The paper compares to Matching Networks (2016) but doesn't include results from more recent state-of-the-art few-shot learning approaches like Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017) or MAML (Finn et al., 2017). Including these would strengthen the paper's position in the literature.

- **Discuss computational cost**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of training the meta-learner. This is important because meta-learning approaches can be significantly more expensive to train than standard methods. A brief discussion of training time and resource requirements would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

- **Clarify the relationship to Andrychowicz et al. (2016)**: The paper builds on Andrychowicz et al. (2016), but it's not entirely clear how this work differs from their approach. A more detailed comparison would help position the contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the fine-tuned baseline is worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline" due to overfitting. Could you elaborate on why this occurs specifically in the 1-shot setting? Is it possible to mitigate this overfitting through regularization techniques, and would such techniques be incorporated into your meta-learner approach?

2. In Table 1, the paper reports competitive results with Matching Networks for 1-shot learning, but the confidence intervals overlap. How confident are you that the observed differences are statistically significant? Could you provide p-values or more detailed statistical analysis for these comparisons?

3. The paper mentions that "the meta-learner is explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing." This suggests the model is task-specific to the number of shots. How would this approach generalize to scenarios where the number of shots is not known in advance (e.g., during meta-testing, the number of shots might vary)?

4. The paper uses batch normalization in the learner network. Given the few-shot setting where batch statistics are limited, could you discuss the impact of batch normalization on the meta-learner's performance compared to using other normalization techniques (e.g., layer normalization) or no normalization?

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the meta-learning literature. With the suggested clarifications and minor additions, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
