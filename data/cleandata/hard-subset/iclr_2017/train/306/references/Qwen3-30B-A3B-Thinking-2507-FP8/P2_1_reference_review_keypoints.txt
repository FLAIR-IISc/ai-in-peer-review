- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns both a task-common initialization and an update rule to optimize a learner network for few-shot classification, showing competitive Mini-ImageNet results and gate-value visualizations.

- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

- Key reasons for acceptance: tackles an important few-shot problem with a well-motivated approach and clear methodology.

- Key reasons for acceptance: experimental results support claims, outperform baselines and are competitive with state-of-the-art metric learning methods.

- Main contribution: jointly learning initialization and update rules addresses limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings.

- Strong literature placement and clear differentiation from prior optimizer-learning work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016).

- Empirical evidence: results and Figure 3 indicate the meta-learner adapts optimization strategies across tasks.

- Minor issues identified do not undermine the core contribution.

- Revision request 1: Clarify how the initial LSTM cell state c0 is trained/updated during meta-training and whether it is an optimized parameter.

- Revision request 2: Justify the assumption that learner losses/gradients are independent of meta-learner parameters and discuss limitations if violated.

- Revision request 3: Clarify the “2-layer LSTM” architecture, specifically the role and nature of the second (modified) LSTM layer.

- Revision request 4: Provide evidence or experiments showing the claimed “task-common initialization” is superior to standard initializations (e.g., Xavier/He).

- Revision request 5: Give detailed specifications of the learner CNN (filter counts, layer details) beyond “4 convolutional layers.”

- Clarification question 1: Precisely how is c0 trained—updated during meta-training or treated as a fixed learned parameter—and how is it optimized?

- Clarification question 2: Given overlapping confidence intervals with Matching Networks in 1-shot, how confident/significant is the claimed improvement; what statistical tests were used?

- Clarification question 3: Define metrics for “quickly” and “good solution” during training and report how meta-training performance correlates with meta-test performance.

- Clarification question 4: What fixed number of fine-tuning updates were used for baselines, how was it chosen, and how does it compare to the meta-learner’s number of updates?

- Clarification question 5: Provide concrete examples showing how the meta-learner’s update rule differs from standard gradient descent, especially between 1-shot and 5-shot settings.
