

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer designed specifically for few-shot learning tasks. The authors frame the problem within a meta-learning setting, where the meta-learner is trained to optimize a learner neural network classifier through a process that learns both a beneficial common initialization for the learner and an effective update rule for a limited number of training steps. The key insight is that the standard gradient descent update rule resembles an LSTM cell state update, allowing the authors to use an LSTM to learn a task-specific optimization algorithm. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of standard optimization methods in few-shot settings and builds on prior work in meta-learning while addressing a specific gap in the literature related to few-shot learning.

## 2. Decision

Reject

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper presents an interesting approach to few-shot learning by framing optimization as a meta-learning problem. However, the decision to reject is based on two key issues:

First, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods in meta-learning. The authors compare against two baselines and Matching Networks, but fail to include a comparison with more recent meta-learning approaches like Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) or Reptile, which have become standard baselines for few-shot learning. The absence of these comparisons makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method relative to current research in the field. The paper's claim of being "competitive with the state-of-the-art" is not sufficiently supported by the experimental results.

Second, the experimental setup on Mini-ImageNet is problematic. The authors state they created their own version of the Mini-ImageNet dataset (by selecting 100 classes from ImageNet), but do not provide sufficient details about their dataset creation process or how it differs from the standard Mini-ImageNet used in prior work. This makes direct comparison with previous results difficult. The paper acknowledges this issue but does not sufficiently address it or provide a justification for why their version is appropriate for comparison. The lack of standardization in the experimental setup undermines the scientific rigor of the evaluation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Add comparisons with more recent meta-learning methods (MAML, Reptile, etc.) that have become standard baselines for few-shot learning. This would provide a more complete picture of the method's performance relative to current approaches.

2. Provide detailed documentation of their Mini-ImageNet dataset creation process, including how the 100 classes were selected and whether the class distribution matches the standard Mini-ImageNet. If their dataset differs significantly from the standard, they should justify why their version is appropriate for comparison.

3. Include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of the meta-learner (e.g., the initialization learning vs. the update rule learning).

4. Provide a more thorough analysis of the meta-learner's behavior beyond the visualization of gate values. For example, they could analyze how the meta-learner's performance varies with different numbers of updates or how it handles different types of classification tasks.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed description of how your Mini-ImageNet dataset was created, including the exact selection criteria for the 100 classes, and how it compares to the standard Mini-ImageNet used in previous work?

2. Why did you choose to compare against Matching Networks but not against MAML or Reptile, which are now standard baselines for few-shot learning? How would your method perform against these more recent approaches?

3. The paper states that "for 5-shot, we are able to do much better than Matching Networks, whereas for 1-shot, the confidence interval for our performance intersects the interval for Matching Networks." Can you provide more details about the statistical significance of these results, including exact p-values and confidence intervals?

4. The paper mentions that the meta-learner is trained to make a specific number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot). How sensitive is the performance to this choice of number of updates, and what is the optimal number for different few-shot settings?
