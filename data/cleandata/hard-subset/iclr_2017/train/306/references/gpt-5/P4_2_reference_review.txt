1. Summary of contributions
The paper addresses few-shot, N-way classification by casting learning as meta-learning: an LSTM-based meta-learner learns to update a base learner’s parameters from a handful of labeled examples. The key idea is to interpret the LSTM cell state as the learner’s parameters and to learn input/forget gates that act as a coordinate-wise optimizer, conditioned on per-parameter gradients, losses, current parameters, and previous gates. The method simultaneously learns: (i) a task-common initialization (the LSTM’s initial cell state), and (ii) a per-step update rule that rapidly adapts the learner within a fixed, small number of updates. Practical details include parameter sharing across coordinates, log-scaled preprocessing of gradients/losses, a training objective that directly optimizes test-set loss after T adaptation steps, and careful episode-wise handling of batch normalization statistics to avoid leakage. On Mini-ImageNet 5-way, 1-shot and 5-shot tasks (custom split), the method is reported to outperform strong baselines and be competitive with, or better than, Matching Networks, with qualitative analyses of learned gate behaviors.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient and under-specified empirical evidence to support the claims (missing quantitative results in the provided text, limited ablations, and potential fairness issues in comparisons).
- Limited novelty relative to prior learned-optimizer work (e.g., coordinate-wise LSTM meta-optimizers), with the main difference being application to few-shot episodes and inclusion of a learned initialization.

3. Supporting arguments
- What is tackled: The paper targets rapid adaptation in few-shot classification by learning an optimizer and an initialization using meta-learning on episodes. The problem framing is clear and relevant.
- Motivation and placement: The approach builds directly on learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.) and episodic meta-learning; applying a learned optimizer to the few-shot regime and explicitly optimizing test performance after T steps is a reasonable and well-motivated instantiation. However, the connection to prior coordinate-wise LSTM optimizers is very close; differences are mainly in the episodic objective and learned initialization.
- Claims and evidence:
  - Quantitative support: The text references Table 1 with results and confidence intervals, but no numbers are shown in the provided content. Without explicit accuracies, episode counts, and statistical uncertainty, it is not possible to verify the strength of the claims.
  - Comparisons: The Matching Networks implementation details differ (e.g., matching nets and baseline conv nets reportedly use 64 filters per layer, while the learner in the proposed method uses 32), which complicates fairness of comparisons. Results on a custom Mini-ImageNet split are reasonable, but comparability to existing literature is limited; no experiments on standard splits (or multiple datasets) are shown here.
  - Scientific rigor: Important ablations are missing. The paper does not disentangle the impact of (a) the learned initialization versus (b) the learned update rule versus (c) batch-norm handling. There is no comparison to a simpler meta-learned initialization with standard SGD updates. The gradient-independence assumption (ignoring second derivatives) is noted but its effect is not quantified.
  - Technical clarity: The LSTM update formulation is generally clear, but there is an inconsistency in the sign of the candidate cell update relative to gradient descent (earlier mapping suggests c̃t = −∇Lt; later the text sets c̃t = ∇Lt). Gate ranges (sigmoid) imply bounded per-coordinate learning rates and decay; implications for convergence are not discussed. Implementation details such as hidden sizes, per-parameter state memory overhead, and computational cost are not specified.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Provide complete, standardized quantitative results: mean ± 95% CI over a large number of episodes, explicit number of evaluation episodes, and per-task adaptation steps. Include the missing Table 1. Ensure identical backbones and data preprocessing across methods to enable fair comparisons.
- Add ablations:
  - Fix ft = 1 (no forgetting) to isolate the benefit of the forget gate.
  - Remove learned c0 (use random or pre-trained initialization) to isolate the impact of the learned initialization.
  - Replace the learned optimizer with SGD/Adam while keeping the learned initialization, and vice versa.
  - Evaluate the effect of the gradient-independence assumption by comparing to a second-order variant on smaller models.
  - Study sensitivity to T (number of adaptation steps) and to k and N at meta-test time (out-of-distribution episode configurations).
- Expand benchmarks: Report on standard Mini-ImageNet splits if possible, and consider additional few-shot datasets to assess generality. Report cross-domain generalization if feasible.
- Clarify technical details:
  - Resolve the sign ambiguity in c̃t and the mapping to gradient descent.
  - Report meta-learner architecture sizes, per-parameter state size, memory/runtime overhead, and training stability measures (e.g., gradient clipping rationale).
  - Justify the choice of sigmoid-bounded gates for effective step sizes and discuss whether this constrains learning unduly.
  - Further detail the batch normalization protocol and show sensitivity analyses.
- Improve related work positioning by more explicitly differentiating from prior learned-optimizer approaches and by discussing the specific novelty of learning an initialization jointly with the optimizer in the episodic objective.

Questions for the authors
- Results: Please provide the full numerical results (means, 95% CIs, number of episodes) referenced in Table 1, with matched backbones across methods. How many episodes were used for meta-training and evaluation?
- Ablations: What is the performance when (i) ft is fixed to 1, (ii) c0 is not learned, and (iii) the learned optimizer is replaced with SGD or Adam while keeping c0 learned?
- Gradient-independence: Did you try including second-order terms on small models? How much does performance change?
- Update rule details: Is c̃t the negative gradient or the gradient? If the latter, how is the sign handled to ensure descent?
- Capacity and overhead: What are the hidden sizes and per-parameter state sizes for the LSTM? What is the memory and runtime overhead relative to standard fine-tuning?
- Generalization: How does the meta-learner perform when N or k at meta-test time differs from meta-training? Does the learned optimizer transfer across different learner architectures?
- Batch normalization: Can you quantify the impact of your episode-wise BN strategy versus alternatives on accuracy and stability?

Overall, the paper presents a plausible and well-motivated approach, but the current presentation lacks sufficient, clearly comparable empirical evidence and ablations to substantiate the claims and to distinguish the contribution from closely related learned-optimizer work.
