- Tackles few-shot N-way classification via bilevel meta-learning with an LSTM-based meta-optimizer that stores learner parameters in cell state and learns per-parameter update rules; jointly learns initialization c0 and update rule.  
- Shares optimizer parameters across coordinates, preprocesses gradients/losses, and carefully handles BatchNorm to avoid cross-episode leakage while matching train/test.  
- Empirically outperforms reasonable baselines and is competitive with or better than Matching Networks on 5-way Mini-ImageNet 1-/5-shot, using few inner-loop updates; gate visualizations appear meaningful.  
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).  
- Reason: Novel, well-motivated unification of learn-to-initialize and learn-to-update with competitive empirical performance.  
- Reason: Clear methodology, practical handling of pitfalls (e.g., BatchNorm), and released code improve reproducibility and impact.  
- Strength: Episodic training/testing protocol is appropriate and comparisons to embedding-based and fine-tuning baselines are provided; Matching Networks reimplemented carefully; inner steps tuned per task.  
- Concern: Evaluation is limited to Mini-ImageNet with custom splits and author reimplementations, reducing external validity; additional benchmarks (Omniglot, other N) needed.  
- Concern: Architectural mismatch — base learner uses 32 filters vs. 64-filter backbone for Matching Networks/baselines — potentially confounds capacity comparisons; parameter counts not reported.  
- Concern: Missing ablations to disentangle benefits of learned initialization vs learned optimizer (e.g., learned init + SGD, fixed init + learned optimizer, removal of inputs/gate deps/preprocessing).  
- Concern: Uses “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring second-order terms) without quantifying its impact; small-scale full second-order comparison recommended.  
- Concern: Clarity/sign inconsistency in Section 3.1 mapping of gradients to LSTM input (c̃t sign ambiguous) — needs unambiguous clarification.  
- Feedback: Clarify two-layer LSTM design — what signals the first layer passes to the coordinate-wise second layer and whether a global context is broadcast.  
- Feedback: Provide exact quantitative results with confidence intervals, number of evaluation episodes, per-episode variance, and calibration plots.  
- Feedback: Report training/inference cost (wall-clock, memory), inner-loop compute vs. Matching Networks, and discuss scalability to deeper learners.  
- Feedback: Explore robustness to mismatched numbers of inner updates at test time, sensitivity to k and N, and cross-domain transfer performance.  
- Feedback: Ablate and report the effect of the proposed episodic BatchNorm handling and sensitivity to running-average momentum with tiny inner-loop batches.  
- Feedback: Strengthen fine-tuning baseline with regularization (weight decay, early stopping on support split, dropout) to ensure a strong comparison.  
- Feedback: Expand visualizations with per-layer/per-parameter gate statistics (distributions of it, ft across steps) and correlation with loss curvature.  
- Question: Resolve sign ambiguity in Eq.(2)/Section 3.1 — is c̃t the negative gradient or is sign handled via learned gates?  
- Question: What is the output dimensionality/role of the first-layer LSTM and how is it combined with per-coordinate inputs in the second layer?  
- Question: How many parameters does the meta-optimizer add relative to the learner; report total parameter counts per method for fair comparison.  
- Question: How sensitive are results to the gradient-independence assumption; how do results change if second-order terms are backpropagated for a small model?  
- Question: Can you provide results with matched base architectures across all methods (same number of filters/parameters)?  
- Question: How does performance vary with the number of inner updates at test time, including beyond the trained number?  
- Question: Have you evaluated additional N (e.g., 20-way) and datasets like Omniglot to contextualize results within prior few-shot literature?
