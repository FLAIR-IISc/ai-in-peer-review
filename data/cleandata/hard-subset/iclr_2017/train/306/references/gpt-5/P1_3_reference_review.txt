Summary of contributions
- Problem: Few-shot learning where a classifier must generalize from k labeled examples per class across many tasks (episodes).
- Idea: Cast learning as meta-learning with an LSTM-based optimizer (meta-learner) that trains a base classifier (learner) in a small, fixed number of updates.
- Method: Identify a correspondence between gradient descent updates and LSTM cell updates. The learner’s parameters are the LSTM cell state; the input gate serves as a learned, stateful learning rate; the forget gate acts like learned weight decay. The meta-learner also learns the initial parameters c0 of the classifier, capturing task-common knowledge.
- Practicalities: Share meta-learner parameters across coordinates to keep the meta-optimizer compact; preprocess gradients/losses (log-scale and sign) for stability; handle batch normalization carefully to avoid leakage across episodes; train using the test loss after T learner updates to match evaluation conditions, with a simplifying “gradient independence” assumption to avoid second derivatives.
- Experiments: On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the approach outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks (better on 5-shot; overlapping CIs with Matching Nets on 1-shot). Gate visualizations suggest learned optimization strategies that differ by task.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Well-motivated and novel formulation that directly learns an optimizer and initialization tailored for few-shot learning, grounded in a clean LSTM–SGD correspondence.
2) Empirical results are strong on a challenging benchmark and the paper is clear about design choices (parameter sharing, preprocessing, batch norm treatment) with code provided.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper targets the core few-shot challenge: learning effective classifiers via very few updates from tiny per-task datasets. It addresses two known failure modes of standard training: generic optimizers not tuned for a strict update budget, and suboptimal random initializations for each new task.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed? Yes. The work sits naturally at the intersection of meta-learning and learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al., Santoro et al., Matching Networks). The central LSTM-as-optimizer view is elegant and leverages known successes of LSTMs while tailoring the objective to few-shot generalization. The discussion of batch norm in meta-learning is practical and valuable.
- Do the results support the claims? Within the provided setup, yes. The method beats natural baselines and is competitive with a strong metric-learning baseline. The training-equals-testing philosophy (optimize test loss after T steps) matches the problem constraints, and visualizations provide qualitative insight. However, a few experimental choices slightly weaken the rigor: (i) use of a custom Mini-ImageNet split prevents direct comparison to prior numbers, (ii) Matching Networks are reimplemented with certain deviations (dropout, no fine-tuning), and (iii) capacity mismatches (32 filters for the meta-learner’s CNN vs 64 for Matching Nets/baselines) warrant clearer justification, even if they arguably disadvantage the proposed method.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Ablations:
  - Learned initialization: Show performance when c0 is random vs meta-learned to quantify its contribution.
  - Gate roles: Fix ft=1 (no forgetting) or it constant to isolate the effect of each gate; compare to hand-tuned SGD/Adam with per-parameter learning rates.
  - Update budget: Sensitivity to the number of inner updates T; generalization when trained for T but tested with T’≠T.
  - Gradient independence assumption: Report results with/without second-order terms on a smaller model to validate the approximation.
- Fairness and reproducibility:
  - Clarify and justify architecture capacities: the learner is described as 32 filters while baselines use 64; reconcile and, if possible, equalize capacities.
  - Provide full Table 1 with means and 95% CIs, and release your Mini-ImageNet split or evaluate on a commonly used split to facilitate comparison.
  - For Matching Networks, report numbers both with and without the optional fine-tuning step (as in Vinyals et al.) to bound the baseline.
- Scope and generalization:
  - Evaluate across more N and k (e.g., 20-way, k∈{1,5,10}); show whether a single meta-learner can handle multiple settings.
  - Cross-dataset generalization (e.g., train on Mini-ImageNet, test on a distinct but related domain) to probe the learned initialization’s robustness.
  - Scalability: Discuss/measure memory and time overhead of per-parameter LSTM states for larger backbones; any parameter tying across layers/blocks?
- Implementation details:
  - Batch normalization: Provide quantitative evidence that the per-episode running-statistics strategy materially improves meta-test performance over alternatives.
  - Training stability: Expand on initialization choices (gate biases) and gradient clipping’s role; give counts of meta-iterations, wall-clock, and compute budget.

Questions for the authors
1) How much of the performance gain comes from the learned initialization versus the learned per-parameter update rule? Can the learned c0 alone (with standard SGD/Adam) match much of the improvement?
2) Can you reconcile the described filter counts: 32 filters for the learner vs 64 for baselines/Matching Nets? Did you run matched-capacity comparisons?
3) How sensitive is the meta-learner to the number of inner-loop steps at test time? Does it degrade gracefully if you run fewer/more steps than trained for?
4) Did you try including second-order terms (i.e., not using the gradient independence assumption) on a smaller setup? If so, does it help?
5) How well does the learned initialization transfer across architectures (e.g., wider CNN) or to different but related datasets?
6) For batch normalization, have you compared per-episode stats vs freezing BN entirely or group norm as a BN-free alternative?

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated contribution with a neat formulation and encouraging results on a challenging benchmark. With the suggested clarifications and ablations, the paper would be even stronger.
