Summary of contributions
- The paper addresses few-shot classification by framing learning as meta-learning: a meta-learner is trained to quickly optimize a base classifier on new tasks with very few labeled examples.
- The proposed meta-learner is an LSTM that treats the classifier’s parameters as its cell state and learns per-parameter update rules via input and forget gates that depend on the current loss, gradient, parameters, and previous gate values. It also learns a task-common initialization for the classifier.
- To scale to many parameters, the meta-learner shares parameters across coordinates (coordinate-wise LSTM), using gradient/loss preprocessing. Training directly optimizes test loss on each episode after a fixed number of inner updates. Practical considerations include a “gradient independence” simplification (ignoring second derivatives) and a careful handling of batch normalization across episodes.
- Empirically, on a custom split of Mini-ImageNet, the method outperforms a nearest-neighbor baseline and a fine-tuning baseline, and is competitive with (better than in 5-shot) Matching Networks.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
1) Insufficient and potentially unfair empirical validation: evaluation is limited to a single custom Mini-ImageNet split and a narrow set of baselines (mostly in-house implementations). The strongest established baselines for few-shot (simple and metric-based) are missing, ablations are minimal, and architectural choices raise fairness questions.
2) Methodological soundness and novelty concerns: the approach closely follows prior work on learned optimizers (coordinate-wise LSTM) and relies on a strong “gradient independence” assumption without evidence it does not materially affect results. Several key design choices (gates, inputs, BN handling) lack ablations.

Supporting arguments
- Baselines and datasets: Results are reported only on a custom Mini-ImageNet split; direct comparison to the literature is thus unreliable. Matching Networks are reimplemented, with no verification against known numbers, and the optional fine-tuning step is omitted. Established and simple, strong baselines are missing (e.g., Prototypical Networks, cosine classifier on frozen features, logistic regression on top of pretrained embeddings, ridge classifiers), as well as more recent meta-learning baselines (e.g., MAML/FOMAML/Reptile, Relation Networks). Without these, it is difficult to assess the claimed advantages.
- Fairness/architecture parity: The learner CNN for the proposed method uses 4×32 filters, whereas baselines use 4×64 (per the authors). Although this may disadvantage the proposed method, it also makes the comparison asymmetric and hinders interpretability. Results should be reported at matched capacity and training budgets to draw clean conclusions.
- Sensitivity and ablations: The paper introduces several nontrivial modeling choices: input and forget gate parameterizations (including dependence on loss), learning the initialization, preprocessing of gradients/losses, the number of inner updates, BN statistic handling, and the “gradient independence” assumption. There are essentially no ablations quantifying the contribution of each component (e.g., fixed ft=1; exclude loss input; learned init only vs learned optimizer; with/without preprocessing; different T; with/without gradient-independence). The visualization of gate values is qualitative and does not substitute for controlled experiments.
- Methodological concerns: 
  - Ignoring second-order terms may substantially change the meta-gradient. This requires empirical justification beyond anecdotal convergence (e.g., compare to a truncated second-order or FOMAML-like variant).
  - BatchNorm handling across episodes is delicate. The proposed scheme may still create train/test discrepancies; the paper should quantify the impact and ensure no inadvertent leakage.
  - Computational cost and memory: Per-parameter LSTMs can be heavy even with shared parameters. No wall-clock, memory, or complexity analysis is provided. Practical viability in larger backbones remains unclear.
- Scope/generalization: The method is evaluated only on N=5, k∈{1,5}; no results for different N, k, or cross-domain tasks, and no test of robustness outside the training regime. The claim of learning a generally useful initialization and optimizer is therefore weakly supported.
- Positioning/novelty: The core idea—coordinate-wise LSTM optimizer trained by backpropagating through optimization—follows Andrychowicz et al. The adaptation to few-shot (optimize test loss after few inner steps, learn init) is natural and overlaps with subsequent widely adopted frameworks (e.g., MAML). The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is truly new and why it is preferable to simpler established approaches.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Expand and standardize evaluation: include standard Mini-ImageNet splits, Omniglot, tieredImageNet, and/or Meta-Dataset. Report mean ± 95% CI with many episodes. Provide code and exact splits.
- Strengthen baselines: add Prototypical Networks, cosine classifier on frozen features, ridge/logistic regression on top of fixed embeddings, MAML/FOMAML/Reptile, Relation Networks. Ensure matched architectures and training budgets across all methods.
- Provide ablations: 
  - Learned init only vs learned optimizer; fixed ft=1; remove loss from inputs; remove preprocessing; different T; effect of gradient independence (compare to first/second-order variants); BN handling variants.
  - Report sensitivity to hyperparameters and inner-loop step count at meta-test time.
- Analyze efficiency: wall-clock, memory usage, and scalability to larger backbones.
- Clarify BatchNorm procedure and verify no leakage; quantify its contribution.
- Improve clarity: explicitly distinguish your contribution from Andrychowicz et al.; discuss why the proposed gating choices are necessary and what they learn beyond simple per-parameter learning rates or per-layer step sizes.

Questions for the authors
1) How does performance change when you:
   - Fix ft=1 (no forgetting), or remove Lt from the gate inputs?
   - Remove gradient/loss preprocessing?
   - Learn only the initialization (no learned optimizer), keeping vanilla SGD/Adam in the inner loop?
2) Can you report results on standard Mini-ImageNet splits and Omniglot, with matched architecture sizes across all methods, and include ProtoNets, cosine classifier, and MAML/FOMAML as baselines?
3) What is the computational and memory overhead versus simple inner-loop optimizers? Please include wall-clock per episode and peak memory for your method and baselines.
4) How sensitive is performance to the number of inner updates at test time? Does the meta-learner generalize when T at test differs from training?
5) Can you quantify the impact of the gradient-independence assumption by comparing to a first-order and a second-order variant?
6) Please detail the BatchNorm statistic handling to rule out any information leakage and report performance with and without the proposed BN scheme.
