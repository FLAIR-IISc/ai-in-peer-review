Summary
The paper tackles few-shot classification by framing it as meta-learning an optimizer and an initialization for a learner network. The key idea is to reinterpret gradient descent updates as the cell-state update of an LSTM; the meta-learner is an LSTM whose cell state stores the learner’s parameters, with input and forget gates learning per-parameter learning rates and shrinkage, respectively. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end to minimize the test loss after a fixed small number of training updates on each episode, and it also learns an initialization for the learner. The approach includes practical design choices: parameter sharing across coordinates, preprocessing of gradients/losses, an episode-safe batch normalization protocol, and a “gradient independence” approximation to avoid second derivatives. On mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot/5-shot, the method outperforms sensible baselines and is competitive with (and in 5-shot better than) Matching Networks. Visualizations suggest the gates learn meaningful behaviors.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper introduces a bold and conceptually novel framing of few-shot learning as learning an optimizer and initialization specialized for the episodic setting, with a concrete and well-engineered LSTM realization that delivers competitive performance. While the empirical section is not exhaustive, the idea is impactful and opens a promising direction.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: The mapping of gradient descent updates to an LSTM cell update and using this to learn per-parameter learning rates/forgetting plus a shared initialization is elegant and well motivated for few-shot regimes where rapid adaptation is critical. The training objective directly optimizes for generalization after a fixed small number of steps, aligning train/test conditions.
- Placement in literature: The paper is well situated w.r.t. meta-learning and learn-to-optimize works (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.) and few-shot metric-learning (Matching Nets), and clarifies differences: here the optimizer and init are specialized to episodes and trained for rapid adaptation rather than general large-scale optimization.
- Design/engineering: Coordinate-wise parameter sharing, robust preprocessing, careful BN handling across episodes, and practical initialization of LSTM gates show thoughtfulness and likely contribute to stability—important given the difficulty of backpropagating through unrolled optimization.
- Empirical evidence: On mini-ImageNet 5-way (1- and 5-shot), the approach beats nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks; it is notably better in 5-shot. While not definitive, these results substantiate the central claim that learning the optimizer and initialization yields fast adaptation from few examples.
- Analysis: Gate visualizations provide qualitative insight that the method is not simply learning a fixed schedule but adapts across tasks.

Areas that weaken the case (but do not outweigh the novelty)
- Limited evaluation breadth and comparability: Only one dataset (custom mini-ImageNet split), and results cannot be compared to prior work directly. No experiments on Omniglot or additional splits.
- Ablations missing: No systematic separation of learned initialization vs learned update rule; no variant with fixed ft=1; no study of the BN strategy’s effect; no analysis of the “gradient independence” approximation.
- Fairness/details: The learner for the meta-optimizer uses 32 filters per conv layer, while baselines/Matching Nets use 64, making comparisons potentially confounded. Sensitivity to T (number of updates), preprocessing parameter p, and hyperparameters is not reported.
- Generality: Separate meta-learners are trained for 1-shot vs 5-shot, suggesting specialization. It is unclear how well a single meta-learner generalizes across N-way/N-shot settings, architectures, and tasks.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Use standard splits (or release yours) and report confidence intervals and the number of evaluation episodes. Add Omniglot or tiered-ImageNet, and more N-way settings.
- Ensure architectural parity across methods (same conv widths) or provide controls showing the effect of width on all methods.
- Ablations:
  - Freeze the initialization to random to measure the benefit of the learned update rule alone; conversely, use the learned init with vanilla SGD/Adam to isolate contributions.
  - Fix ft=1 to test the necessity of the forget gate; analyze learned learning-rate dynamics (it distribution).
  - Turn BN off or vary the proposed episode BN protocol to quantify its impact.
  - Examine the effect of the gradient-independence assumption by including a small-scale experiment with exact second derivatives.
- Report compute: wall-clock, memory use, unroll length T, and training stability across seeds.
- Generalization studies: train a single meta-learner across mixed N-shot/N-way episodes; test transfer to different backbones; evaluate performance when the number of adaptation steps at test differs from training.

Questions for the authors
- Architectural parity: Why is the meta-learner’s CNN (32 filters) smaller than baselines/Matching Nets (64)? Did you run all methods with matched widths, and does the ranking persist?
- Training details: How many episodes were used for meta-training, and how many for evaluation to obtain the reported CIs? How sensitive is performance to T and to the preprocessing parameter p?
- Gradient independence: Did you empirically compare to a version that backpropagates exact meta-gradients (with second derivatives) on a smaller model to validate the approximation?
- Generalization: How well does a meta-learner trained for 1-shot perform on 5-shot (and vice versa)? Can a single model trained on mixed shots/ways perform well across them?
- Stability and variance: What is the variance across random seeds for meta-training? Are there failure modes or divergence issues, and how do gate bias initializations affect this?
- BN protocol: Can you quantify the performance drop from alternative BN choices (e.g., using only running averages, or no BN) to justify the proposed episode-wise reset strategy?
- Scaling: What is the computational overhead of per-parameter LSTM updates for larger backbones, and do you foresee approaches (e.g., block-wise updates) to scale?

Overall, despite some empirical limitations, the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to influence subsequent work on gradient-based meta-learning for few-shot adaptation. I recommend acceptance.
