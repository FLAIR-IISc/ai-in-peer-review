Summary
This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-optimizer for few-shot classification. The core idea is to cast the learner’s parameter updates as an LSTM cell-state evolution, with per-parameter input and forget gates that learn to control step sizes and decay, and to learn an initialization that is optimal across tasks. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end to minimize the test loss after a small, fixed number of updates on each episode, with parameter sharing across coordinates and a simple gradient/loss pre-processing scheme. Care is taken to handle batch normalization in a way that avoids leakage across episodes. On 5-way Mini-ImageNet (1-shot and 5-shot), the approach is competitive with Matching Networks and substantially outperforms reasonable transfer/fine-tuning baselines. The paper also provides introspection into the learned gating dynamics. Overall, the paper introduces a bold and elegant optimization-as-meta-learning formulation for few-shot learning.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novelty and conceptual significance: casting per-parameter updates as an LSTM that simultaneously learns an initialization and a task-adaptive optimizer tailored for few-shot regimes is an appealing and distinct direction from existing metric learning methods.
- Promising empirical evidence: competitive or better performance than a strong metric-learning baseline (especially in 5-shot) and clearly stronger than transfer/fine-tuning baselines; code release improves reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Few-shot classification where standard gradient-based training on tiny datasets fails due to limited updates and poor initialization.
- Is the approach well motivated and positioned? Yes. The mapping between SGD-style updates and LSTM cell updates is insightful, connects to Andrychowicz et al. (learning to learn optimizers), and adapts it to the few-shot episodic setting with a training objective that directly optimizes post-adaptation test loss. The paper situates itself among meta-learning optimizers, memory-augmented methods, and metric learning.
- Do results support claims? The Mini-ImageNet experiments show consistent gains over two transfer baselines and competitive results with Matching Networks, with a clear advantage in 5-shot. The BN episode-handling detail is important and practical. Visualizations suggest the meta-learner learns meaningful (and task-dependent) update schedules. While the evaluation scope is somewhat narrow, the evidence supports the core claim that the learned optimizer/initialization can rapidly adapt in few steps.

Caveats (not blocking, given novelty)
- Evaluation breadth and fairness:
  - Only Mini-ImageNet is considered; generalization to other benchmarks (Omniglot, cross-domain) is not shown.
  - Backbone capacity differs across methods (32 filters for the learner vs 64 for Matching Nets/baselines), which complicates fairness; please clarify and, if possible, equalize backbones.
  - Limited ablations: it is unclear how much each component contributes (learned initialization vs gates vs preprocessing vs BN strategy).
- Methodological details:
  - The “gradient independence” assumption avoids second derivatives. While practical, a small ablation quantifying its effect would strengthen the claims.
  - There is a sign inconsistency: Eq. (2) mapping suggests c̃t = −∇Lt, but Section 3.1 sets c̃t = ∇Lt while using a positive input gate; please clarify how the descent direction is enforced.
  - Scalability and compute: per-parameter LSTM with per-coordinate states can be costly for larger backbones; runtime and memory costs vs baselines are not reported.
- Robustness and generality:
  - The meta-learner is trained for a fixed number of updates (e.g., 12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot). How robust is performance to T mismatch at test time, and to different numbers of shots/classes?
  - Sensitivity to the preprocessing hyperparameter p and to BN choices is not quantified.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide ablations:
  - Fix ft = 1 (no forget) to isolate the benefit of the forget gate.
  - Random vs learned initialization, with the same optimizer.
  - With/without gradient/loss preprocessing; with/without the first LSTM layer.
  - With/without the BN episode-handling trick.
- Report confidence intervals and number of test episodes for all methods; include wall-clock and memory comparisons for adaptation and meta-training.
- Equalize the backbone across methods (same filter counts) or provide results under both 32/64-filter settings for fairness.
- Explore T mismatch (train with T, test with different T) and cross-shot/class generalization (train on 1-shot, test on 5-shot and vice versa).
- Clarify the meta-parameter count relative to learner parameters, and discuss scalability to deeper/wider architectures.
- Briefly discuss applicability beyond vision and to heterogeneous tasks.

Questions for the authors
1) Sign and update rule: In Section 3.1, c̃t is set to ∇Lt, but the SGD mapping implies −∇Lt. Is the negative sign absorbed elsewhere (e.g., gate parametrization or preprocessing)? Please clarify to avoid confusion.
2) Fairness of comparisons: The learner uses 32 filters while Matching Nets/baselines use 64. How do results change when all methods use the same capacity and data augmentations/dropout?
3) Gradient independence assumption: How much does ignoring second-order terms affect performance or stability? Did you try a small-T experiment with exact second-order gradients?
4) Robustness to T and task mismatch: If the meta-learner is trained for T updates and 1-shot, how does it perform with different T at test time and with 5-shot (and vice versa)?
5) Computational overhead: What are the adaptation and meta-training runtimes and peak memory vs Matching Nets/fine-tuning? How does the per-parameter LSTM scale with larger networks?
6) Component importance: Which contributes most—learned initialization or the learned optimizer gates? Any ablations or qualitative evidence?
7) BN handling: Can you quantify the gain from the episode-specific BN running statistics vs the naïve alternatives?
8) Preprocessing sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to the choice of p in the log-scaling preprocessing? Did you try simpler normalizations?
9) Generalization across datasets: Have you tested on Omniglot or cross-domain splits to assess robustness?
10) Visualization: Do gate dynamics correlate with intuitive metrics (e.g., gradient norms, loss plateaus) across layers, and can you connect this to learned learning-rate schedules?

Overall, despite some experimental limitations, the paper introduces a compelling and novel direction—learning an optimizer and initialization tailored for few-shot adaptation—and demonstrates tangible benefits. I recommend acceptance.
