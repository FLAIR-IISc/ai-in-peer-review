Summary
This paper tackles few-shot learning by casting optimization itself as a meta-learned process. The core idea is to view a learner’s parameter update as an LSTM cell update, with the LSTM’s cell state representing the learner parameters and input/forget gates adapting per-coordinate update magnitudes. The meta-learner is trained episodically to minimize the test loss after a small, fixed number of updates, thereby learning both: (i) an initialization for the learner (the LSTM’s initial cell state) and (ii) an update rule that rapidly reaches good generalization in the few-shot regime. The paper also addresses practical issues such as per-episode batch normalization statistics to avoid leakage, and reports competitive results on Mini-ImageNet, outperforming baselines and matching or exceeding Matching Networks in 5-shot.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and bold formulation: an LSTM-based optimizer tailored explicitly to few-shot learning that jointly learns an initialization and an update rule from episodic supervision.
- Empirically compelling: competitive or superior few-shot results vs. strong baselines on Mini-ImageNet, alongside practical insights (BN handling) and qualitative analyses of the learned gates.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Few-shot generalization is central to data-efficient learning. The paper clearly formulates the meta-learning setting and aligns training and test conditions via episodic training with a fixed number of updates.
- Motivation and positioning: The approach is well-motivated and placed relative to meta-learning and learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.). The observation that a gradient step resembles an LSTM cell update is elegant and operationalized thoughtfully (per-coordinate parameter sharing, gradient/loss preprocessing).
- Claims vs. evidence: Results on Mini-ImageNet show clear gains over nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines; 5-shot improves over Matching Networks, and 1-shot is competitive within overlapping confidence intervals. The BN protocol is a useful contribution for episodic setups. Gate visualizations suggest the meta-learner learns nontrivial, dataset-dependent behaviors.
- Risk-taking: The paper makes a strong conceptual step—learning the optimizer to maximize generalization after few steps—despite forgoing second-order terms via the gradient independence assumption. Given the scope and the evidence, this is an acceptable simplification for a first demonstration.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Empirical scope and fairness:
  - Consider adding a second benchmark (e.g., Omniglot or a cross-domain split) and varying ways and shots at meta-test to assess robustness and generalization across N and k.
  - Clarify fairness of Matching Networks comparisons (architectural differences, dropout, and whether FCE and test-time fine-tuning are enabled). Where feasible, standardize backbones and protocols.
- Ablations and analysis:
  - Ablate key design choices: learned initialization vs. random, forget gate vs. fixed ft=1, loss/gradient preprocessing, per-coordinate sharing, number of LSTM layers, and the impact of the gradient independence assumption (e.g., limited experiments with higher-order terms).
  - Sensitivity to the number of unrolled steps T and whether a model trained for T generalizes to T’ at test time.
  - Provide memory/time complexity and wall-clock comparisons to baselines; discuss scalability to larger learners.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Be precise about the sign convention in the update (c̃t defined as gradient vs. negative gradient) and how the input gate compensates for descent direction.
  - Clean up the references formatting and ensure equations and algorithm steps are consistent and easy to follow.
  - Report the number of meta-test episodes and confidence interval computation; include full hyperparameters (LSTM hidden sizes, initialization scales, gate biases).

Questions for the authors
- Generalization across conditions: If trained for 5-way, does the meta-learner generalize to 10-way at test time without retraining? Similarly for k (1-shot to 5-shot)? What happens if the number of updates at test time differs from training?
- Ablations: How much of the performance comes from the learned initialization vs. the learned update rule? Can you show performance with fixed ft=1 or with a simple learned scalar step size (i.e., without the LSTM gates)?
- Gradient independence: Did you try including higher-order terms for small models or short horizons to gauge the benefit vs. cost?
- Scalability: What is the computational overhead (memory, runtime) relative to standard fine-tuning? How does the approach scale to deeper learners (e.g., 64+ filters, ResNets)?
- BN protocol: Can you quantify the performance gap between different BN strategies (always using running stats vs. per-episode stats reset) and confirm no leakage in your implementation?
- Reproducibility: Please provide the exact Mini-ImageNet splits used, hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM sizes, gate bias initializations), number of episodes for evaluation, and training curves. If possible, release the code and splits to standardize future comparisons.

Overall, this work proposes a fresh and promising direction—learning a task-specialized optimizer for few-shot classification with an LSTM—which is conceptually impactful and empirically supported. Despite some limitations in breadth and ablations, the novelty and demonstrated potential justify acceptance.
