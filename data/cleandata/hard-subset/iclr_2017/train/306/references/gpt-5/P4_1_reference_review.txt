1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles few-shot supervised classification via meta-learning. It proposes an LSTM-based meta-optimizer that updates the parameters of a learner network (a small CNN) over a fixed, small number of steps, using the LSTM cell state as the learner’s parameters. The input and forget gates are learned functions of the current gradient, loss, parameter value, and previous gate value; the initial LSTM cell state is also learned, yielding a task-common initialization. Gradients and losses are preprocessed to stabilize magnitudes, and parameters are shared across coordinates to keep the meta-learner compact. Training matches test-time episodic conditions: the meta-objective is the learner’s test loss after T inner updates on each episode. The approach uses a standard gradient-independence approximation to avoid second derivatives and a careful BatchNorm protocol to avoid cross-episode leakage. On 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet, the method outperforms transfer/fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with, and better on 5-shot than, Matching Networks implemented by the authors. The paper provides implementation details and qualitative gate visualizations suggesting the meta-learner learns nontrivial, dataset-dependent update behavior.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Technical novelty and soundness: a coherent formulation of a coordinate-wise LSTM optimizer specialized for few-shot generalization, jointly learning a rapid-update rule and an initialization, trained on a meta-objective aligned with test-time conditions.
- Empirical support: clear improvements over reasonable baselines and competitive/better results than a strong metric-learning method on a standard benchmark, with attention to practical issues (BatchNorm, preprocessing, parameter sharing).

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled?
The paper addresses learning classifiers from few labeled examples per class by meta-learning an optimizer that can adapt a base network to a new task in few gradient-like steps, while capturing cross-task regularities via a learned initialization and gating dynamics.

- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
Yes. The motivation—gradient-based training struggles with few updates and random initializations—is clearly articulated. The method builds on and differs from prior LSTM optimizer work (Andrychowicz et al.) by targeting episodic few-shot generalization and learning an initialization as part of the optimizer’s state. The paper situates itself relative to metric learning (Matching Networks), memory-augmented meta-learners, and transfer learning, and discusses practical BatchNorm concerns specific to episodic training/testing.

- Do the results support the claims with rigor?
Largely yes. The training protocol matches test-time usage; the architecture and training details are specified. Results on 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet demonstrate consistent gains over nearest-neighbor and fine-tune baselines and competitiveness with Matching Networks; 5-shot results are notably stronger. Confidence intervals are reported, and the authors acknowledge their own dataset split and reimplementation. However, empirical rigor could be strengthened with more ablations (e.g., effect of input/forget gates, learned initialization, gradient-independence approximation) and broader comparisons (e.g., additional meta-learning baselines). Computational cost and sensitivity analyses are limited.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the accept decision)
- Clarify the sign convention: Eq. (2) mapping suggests c̃t = −∇Lt, whereas Section 3.1 sets c̃t = ∇Lt. Explicitly state where the negative sign is absorbed (e.g., via the gate outputs or preprocessing) to avoid confusion.
- Baseline parity: The learner uses 32 filters, while baselines use 64. Although this makes the meta-learner’s gains more convincing, consider reporting results with matched capacities and identical augmentation regularization for direct fairness.
- Ablations: Report the contribution of (a) learned initialization c0 vs. random/pretrained, (b) forget gate vs. fixed ft=1, (c) gradient preprocessing, (d) number of inner steps T and generalization when test-time steps differ from training.
- Gradient-independence assumption: Provide a small-scale experiment comparing with full second-order gradients or truncated variants to quantify its impact.
- Efficiency: Include wall-clock or FLOP comparisons and memory footprint versus Matching Networks and fine-tuning, especially since unrolling T steps and per-parameter LSTM states may be costly.
- Generality: Explore variability in N-way and k-shot without retraining, and cross-domain generalization, to support claims of broader applicability.
- Reproducibility: Release the exact Mini-ImageNet split and random seeds for reported confidence intervals.

Questions for the authors
- How many meta-test episodes were used to compute the reported means and confidence intervals, and what is the exact CI computation?
- What is the runtime and memory overhead per episode relative to (i) fine-tuning SGD/Adam and (ii) Matching Networks? How does this scale with parameter count?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of inner updates at test time? Does the meta-learner degrade gracefully if given fewer/more steps than trained for?
- Can you quantify the benefit of the learned initialization c0 independently from the learned update rule (e.g., training with ft=1, fixed small it)?
- Did you compare a GRU-style tied gate variant or removing the forget gate entirely? Any insights from gate saturation statistics across tasks?
- What is the effect of the gradient-independence approximation in practice? Any evidence that including some second-order terms improves or harms stability?
- Does the BatchNorm protocol ever leak information across the train/test split within an episode? Please clarify the exact statistics used at each phase and their reset schedule.
- How robust are results to different random Mini-ImageNet splits? Will you release the split used to facilitate comparisons?
