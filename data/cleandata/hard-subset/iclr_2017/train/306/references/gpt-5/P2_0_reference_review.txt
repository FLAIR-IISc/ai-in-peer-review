1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot classification by framing learning as meta-learning: an LSTM meta-learner learns to update the parameters of a base classifier (learner) using gradients and losses computed on an episode’s small training set, with the objective of achieving good generalization on that episode’s test set after a fixed, small number of updates. Key elements:
- The LSTM cell state stores the learner’s parameters; input and forget gates implement adaptive step sizes and forgetting, respectively.
- The meta-learner jointly learns an optimizer and a task-common initialization for the learner.
- Parameter sharing across coordinates and preprocessing stabilize training; a “gradient independence” approximation avoids second derivatives.
- Careful handling of batch normalization to avoid leakage across episodes.
- Empirical results on mini-ImageNet (custom split) show the method is competitive with Matching Networks and better than strong baselines, especially in 5-shot. Some qualitative analysis of learned gate dynamics is provided. Code is released.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, well motivated by the small-data regime, and positions meta-learning as a way to learn both an initialization and a rapid adaptation procedure.

2) Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons:
- The approach is well motivated, conceptually simple, and leverages a clear episodic training objective aligned with test-time conditions.
- Results are competitive with a strong metric-learning baseline and surpass fine-tuning baselines; the work provides practical insights (BN treatment, parameter sharing) and released code.

3) Supporting arguments
Novelty and positioning:
- The work is closely related to Andrychowicz et al. (learning to learn by gradient descent), but adapts the idea specifically to few-shot classification with an episodic meta-objective that directly optimizes post-adaptation test loss, and explicitly learns the initialization. This is a meaningful and well-justified specialization that addresses the unique constraints of few-shot (very few update steps; small training sets; fast convergence).
- It complements metric-learning approaches (e.g., Matching Networks) by showing gradient-based adaptation can be learned end-to-end and be competitive.

Technical soundness and clarity:
- The mapping of LSTM updates to parameter updates is clear, and the design of the gates (conditioning on current loss, gradient, parameters, and previous gate value) is reasonable.
- The parameter-sharing scheme and input preprocessing follow prior art and are appropriate to control complexity.
- The batch-norm protocol to prevent cross-episode leakage while keeping train/test conditions aligned is thoughtful and practically important.
- The “gradient independence” assumption is a standard approximation in learned optimizers; while it sacrifices exactness, it is a pragmatic choice and empirically effective.

Empirical evaluation:
- The mini-ImageNet evaluation is appropriate and aligned with prior few-shot works; the method is competitive with Matching Networks and clearly outperforms nearest-neighbor on frozen embeddings and simple fine-tuning, especially in 5-shot.
- Training separate meta-learners for 1-shot and 5-shot and matching the number of update steps between meta-train and meta-test are sensible and supported by the results.
- The gate visualizations suggest the meta-learner learns nontrivial, dataset-dependent behaviors.

Limitations:
- Only one dataset (custom mini-ImageNet split) is reported; no cross-dataset evaluation or additional benchmarks (e.g., Omniglot, tieredImageNet) are shown.
- Reproducing Matching Networks in-house may introduce discrepancies; while architectures are stated, a more extensive parity check would strengthen fairness claims.
- No ablations disentangle the contributions of learned initialization versus learned optimizer; nor do we see ablations on the forget gate, the gradient independence approximation, or BN handling.
- Resource/memory considerations for per-parameter LSTM states are not quantified.

4) Suggestions for improvement (to help the authors; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide ablations:
  - Learned initialization only (c0 learned, standard SGD updates) vs learned optimizer only vs full model.
  - Fix ft=1 (no forgetting) or remove conditioning inputs to gates to assess what matters.
  - With/without gradient independence assumption (second-order terms) on smaller models to quantify its effect.
  - BN handling variants to show the importance of the proposed protocol.
- Expand evaluation:
  - Report results on at least one additional benchmark (e.g., Omniglot, tieredImageNet or CIFAR-FS), and/or multiple random mini-ImageNet splits to assess robustness.
  - Test generalization to variable N and k within a single meta-learner.
  - Report sensitivity to the number of adaptation steps T and meta-train/meta-test mismatch.
- Fairness and reproducibility:
  - Ensure exact architectural parity and hyperparameters with Matching Networks; provide a small table with parameter counts and training details.
  - Report wall-clock training time, memory usage, and meta-learner parameter count; discuss scalability to larger learners.
- Clarity:
  - Clean up bibliography formatting; fix minor typos.
  - Clarify the computational graph and what’s differentiated through (especially with the independence assumption).

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain comes from the learned initialization versus the learned optimizer? Can you report performance with (a) learned c0 + fixed small-step SGD; (b) random init + learned optimizer?
- Is the forget gate essential? What happens with ft fixed to 1 or with simpler weight decay?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of adaptation steps T and to a train/test mismatch in T?
- Can a single meta-learner handle both 1-shot and 5-shot (and different N) without retraining? If not, what breaks?
- What is the memory and time overhead of maintaining per-parameter LSTM states for the learner? How does this scale with larger backbones?
- How critical is the gradient independence approximation? Any small-scale experiment quantifying its impact?
- Can you quantify the effect of your BN protocol, e.g., performance deltas vs naïve alternatives?
- Given that you use your own Matching Networks implementation and custom split, can you provide confidence intervals and ensure hyperparameter parity across methods to alleviate fairness concerns?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and practically relevant contribution to few-shot learning, with a clear method and promising results. The main weaknesses are limited breadth of evaluation and missing ablations, but these are addressable. Hence, I recommend acceptance.
