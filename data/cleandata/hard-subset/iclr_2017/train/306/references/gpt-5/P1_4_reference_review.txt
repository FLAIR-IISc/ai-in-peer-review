Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot N-way classification by framing learning as meta-learning: it trains a recurrent “meta-learner” optimizer that updates the parameters of a base classifier (“learner”) using an LSTM whose cell state corresponds to the learner’s parameters. The method:
- Derives an LSTM-style update rule that learns per-parameter input/forget gates (learning rate and weight decay-like behavior) conditioned on current loss, gradient, parameter value, and previous gate values.
- Learns a task-common initialization (the LSTM initial cell state c0), enabling rapid adaptation with very few updates.
- Shares meta-learner parameters across coordinates to keep the optimizer compact and uses log-preprocessing of loss/gradients.
- Trains the meta-learner to optimize the learner such that performance on each episode’s test set after a fixed number of updates is directly maximized, aligning training and test conditions.
- Provides practical guidance for batch normalization in episodic meta-learning to avoid leakage while maintaining matched train/test conditions.
Empirically, on Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the approach outperforms simple baselines and is competitive with, and better on 5-shot than, Matching Networks. Visualizations suggest the meta-learner learns meaningful gating strategies across updates.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) The approach is well motivated and offers a novel, elegant instantiation of a learned optimizer specialized for few-shot generalization, including learning the initialization. (2) Results on Mini-ImageNet are competitive with strong metric-learning baselines and surpass them in 5-shot, with a clear experimental protocol and useful implementation insights.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Clear articulation of the failures of standard gradient-based training in few-shot settings and the benefit of meta-learning to learn both an update rule and a good initialization for quick adaptation.
- Positioning: The work is well situated relative to learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.) and few-shot methods (e.g., Matching Networks), and explains how it differs: specialized training objective over episodes, alignment to few-shot settings, and explicit learned initialization.
- Method soundness: The LSTM cell-state-as-parameters formulation is compelling and consistent with learned optimization literature. Parameter sharing across coordinates and gradient/loss preprocessing are appropriate and necessary for scalability and stability.
- Training details: Sensible choices (gate bias initialization, gradient clipping), practical BN handling to prevent cross-episode leakage while matching train/test statistics, and use of the test-set loss as meta-objective are strengths.
- Empirical support: The method beats two natural baselines (nearest neighbor on pre-trained embeddings; fine-tuning a pre-trained network) and is competitive with Matching Networks (outperforming in 5-shot), consistent with the claim that learning an initialization and update rule mitigates overfitting and accelerates adaptation.

Limitations and concerns (do not outweigh contributions)
- Evaluation scope: Results are limited to Mini-ImageNet with a small CNN; generalization to other datasets (Omniglot, tieredImageNet), deeper backbones, or non-vision tasks is not shown.
- Baseline comparability: Matching Networks are reimplemented with 64-filter conv layers, while the learner uses 32-filter layers. Although this should, if anything, favor the baselines, architectural mismatches complicate fairness. Also, the paper does not include the fine-tuned Matching Nets variant mentioned in the original work (though noted).
- Ablations: Missing ablations on key design choices: learned initialization vs. fixed; effect of forget gate (ft) vs. it-only updates; effect of ignoring second derivatives; impact of preprocessing; number of inner updates T; removing the first LSTM layer; role of BN handling.
- Scalability: A per-parameter LSTM state can be memory-intensive for large models; the paper does not quantify compute/memory costs or training time, and it trains separate meta-learners for 1-shot and 5-shot, which limits flexibility.
- Reproducibility: The Mini-ImageNet split differs from prior work; reproducibility would benefit from releasing the exact splits and more detailed hyperparameters.

Suggestions for improvement (to help the paper, not all required for acceptance)
- Add ablations to quantify: (a) benefit of learned initialization; (b) removing the forget gate; (c) effect of gradient-independence approximation; (d) preprocessing and BN strategies; (e) sensitivity to number of inner steps and meta-objective horizon.
- Expand evaluation to at least one more benchmark (e.g., Omniglot or tieredImageNet) and report cross-architecture transfer (e.g., train meta-learner on one backbone, evaluate on another) to probe generality.
- Harmonize model capacity across methods or provide results with matching capacities; include the Matching Nets fine-tuning variant for completeness.
- Report compute and memory overhead (per-parameter state size, wall-clock) and discuss scaling strategies (block-wise sharing, layer-wise optimizers).
- Release the Mini-ImageNet splits used and code to ensure comparability.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain comes from the learned initialization c0 versus the learned update gates? Can you provide an ablation where c0 is random or pre-trained and only gates are learned?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of inner-loop steps T, and does the meta-learner generalize to different T than trained on?
- What is the computational and memory footprint of the per-parameter LSTM (e.g., per-weight state size, total memory for your CNN)? Any issues when scaling to larger networks?
- Did you evaluate the gradient-independence assumption (ignoring second-order terms)? How does full higher-order training compare on small models?
- Can you provide results with matched model capacities across baselines and your learner, and with the fine-tuned Matching Networks variant?
- Do the learned gate policies and initialization transfer across N-way settings or to unseen architectures?
- Will you release the exact Mini-ImageNet class splits and episode generation code to facilitate fair comparison?

Overall, this paper presents a clear and interesting meta-learning approach with promising results and practical insights. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and ablations would further solidify the claims.
