1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot classification by framing learning as meta-learning: training a meta-learner that learns to rapidly optimize a base learner given only a few labeled examples per task. The key idea is to parameterize the base learner’s parameter updates using an LSTM whose cell state holds the learner’s parameters. The LSTM’s input and forget gates act as learned, per-coordinate learning-rate and “weight-decay/forget” controls, respectively; the meta-learner thus simultaneously learns (i) an initialization for the base learner’s parameters and (ii) a task-adapted update rule that produces good generalization after a small, fixed number of steps. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end to minimize each episode’s test loss after T updates, with shared LSTM parameters across coordinates (to keep it compact) and with gradient/loss preprocessing for stability. The paper also discusses practicalities such as batch normalization usage in episodic settings and an approximation that ignores second derivatives (gradient independence assumption). Experiments on a Mini-ImageNet split (5-way, 1-shot/5-shot) show results competitive with Matching Networks and better in the 5-shot case, and significantly stronger than nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines. Visualizations of gate dynamics provide qualitative insight. Code is released.

2) Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: (1) The approach is well-motivated, conceptually clean, and placed appropriately in the meta-learning literature; (2) Empirical results on a challenging benchmark are competitive with a strong contemporary method (Matching Networks), with clear gains in 5-shot; (3) The paper presents a concrete, end-to-end differentiable algorithm with practical considerations (BN, training stability) and open-source code.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work addresses the limitations of gradient-based training in low-data regimes and the mismatch between transfer learning and target tasks. Framing optimization itself as a learned, task-dependent process is compelling and aligns with the meta-learning literature.
- Novelty and positioning: While learning to learn with LSTMs as optimizers has been explored (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.), the contribution here is to specialize the learned optimizer to few-shot classification and to use an episodic objective that directly optimizes test performance after a small number of updates. The mapping of the LSTM cell update to parameter updates (and leveraging the forget/input gates) is thoughtful. The paper connects to related memory-based and metric-based few-shot methods and older meta-learning literature.
- Methodological clarity: The model is described clearly, including gate parametrizations, preprocessing, parameter sharing, initialization choices, the training protocol, and handling of BN in episodic settings. The gradient-independence approximation is stated explicitly.
- Empirical support: Results on Mini-ImageNet 5-way indicate the method is competitive with Matching Networks in 1-shot (overlapping CIs) and stronger in 5-shot. Baselines include nearest-neighbor and a fine-tuning baseline; the latter underperforms due to overfitting, which underscores the value of learning both initialization and update rules end-to-end. Visualizations of gate values suggest the optimizer adapts across episodes and adopts intuitive strategies (e.g., consistent forget/decay). Code availability enhances reproducibility.

4) Feedback to improve the paper (to help, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Fairness and comparability of baselines:
  - Matching Networks: You implement your own variant and omit the optional fine-tuning proposed in the original work; please discuss whether including that fine-tuning changes the ranking. Ensure hyperparameters and architectures match as closely as possible.
  - Network capacity: In Section 5, the learner is described with 32 filters per conv layer, but baseline/Matching Nets are reported with 64 filters. Please reconcile this and ensure apples-to-apples comparisons (or at least report both configurations).
  - Release the exact Mini-ImageNet split used to enable consistent comparisons with future work (you note the original splits weren’t released).
- Ablations and diagnostics:
  - Initialization vs. optimizer: What fraction of the gains comes from learning the initialization c0 versus learning the update rule? A control that learns c0 but uses fixed SGD/Adam with tuned LR would be very informative.
  - Gates and inputs: Ablate the gate inputs (e.g., remove loss Lt, or previous gate state) and report impact. Compare to a learned per-parameter LR schedule without a forget gate.
  - Gradient independence assumption: Quantify the effect of ignoring second derivatives (even on a smaller model), or at least report evidence that including them does not change conclusions.
  - Sensitivity to number of steps T: You train separate models for 1-shot/5-shot with different T. How sensitive is performance to mismatch between train-time and test-time T? Can a single meta-learner generalize across T?
- Generalization:
  - N-way and k-shot variability: Evaluate cross-way generalization (e.g., train 5-way, test 10-way) and cross-shot generalization (train 1-shot, test 5-shot) to probe robustness.
  - Other datasets: Even a small-scale result on a different domain (e.g., Omniglot or a different subset of ImageNet) would strengthen evidence of generality.
- Practical considerations:
  - Complexity and scalability: Provide wall-clock training/inference time, memory footprint, and parameter counts for the meta-learner, and discuss scalability to larger learners.
  - BN details: The episodic BN strategy is reasonable; please formalize it and clarify the decay used for running averages, and whether test-time batch size variations affect performance.

Questions for the authors
- Architecture parity: Did the meta-learner’s base CNN use 32 or 64 filters in the reported main results? If different from baselines, how do results change when capacities are matched?
- Matching Networks reproduction: How close are your reproduced numbers to those in the original paper on comparable settings? Have you tried enabling the optional fine-tuning at test time for fairness?
- Contribution of c0: What is the performance if you only learn initialization c0 and use a fixed optimizer (e.g., SGD/Adam) during adaptation? Conversely, if you randomize c0 but keep the learned optimizer, how does performance change?
- Number of update steps: How was T selected (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot)? What happens if you allow the meta-learner more or fewer steps than trained for?
- Second derivatives: Did you attempt a smaller-scale experiment with exact meta-gradients (no gradient independence assumption)? If yes, how do results compare?
- Stability: How sensitive is training to the preprocessing parameter p and to gate bias initializations? Any failure modes (e.g., divergence) you can document?
- Final layer adaptation: Since episodes are 5-way, the final layer has episode-specific dimensionality. Can your approach handle variable N without retraining (e.g., via a shared classifier head or dynamic parameterization)?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and carefully executed paper that pushes meta-learning for few-shot classification with a learned optimizer and demonstrates competitive performance with open-source code. I recommend acceptance.
