Summary
The paper tackles few-shot classification by framing learning as meta-optimization. It proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner whose cell state represents the learner’s parameters; the gates produce per-parameter updates conditioned on the current gradient, loss, parameter value, and previous gate values. The method learns both an initialization for the learner and an update rule that aims to achieve good test performance after a fixed, small number of inner-loop updates. The approach includes coordinate-wise parameter sharing, gradient/loss preprocessing, a “gradient independence” simplification to avoid second-order terms, and a careful protocol for BatchNorm statistics to avoid cross-episode leakage. Experiments on Mini-ImageNet (custom split) show results better than simple baselines (nearest neighbor and fine-tuning) and competitive with Matching Networks (outperforming it in 5-shot). Code is promised.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient and potentially unfair empirical validation relative to established baselines, with missing essential details (numbers, CIs, capacity matching, tuning) and reliance on custom splits and self-implemented baselines. (2) Methodological concerns: heavy reliance on unproven approximations (ignoring second-order terms), task-specific training of the inner-loop horizon, and unclear scalability, without ablations or robustness analyses.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning/novelty: The core idea—learning an optimizer via an LSTM with coordinate-wise sharing that updates parameters using gradients—is very close to Andrychowicz et al. (2016). The main novelty here is applying that paradigm to the few-shot episodic setting with a post-adaptation test loss objective and learning the initialization. While appropriate to the problem, this is an incremental adaptation rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution.

- Experimental rigor and fairness:
  - Dataset split: Results are reported on a custom Mini-ImageNet split; exact numerical results and confidence intervals (“Table 1”) are not present in the text provided. Without standardized splits or comprehensive cross-split validation, comparisons are difficult to trust.
  - Baseline implementations: Matching Networks are reimplemented by the authors; no evidence is given that performance matches published numbers on a standardized setting. Furthermore, the authors do not enable the fine-tuning option mentioned by Vinyals et al., potentially disadvantaging that baseline. Hyperparameter search protocols for baselines are not exhaustively documented.
  - Capacity mismatch: The learner in the proposed method uses 32 filters per block, whereas the Matching Networks and baseline convnets use 64 filters. This asymmetry could affect both directions (learned optimizer may benefit from smaller capacity that is less prone to overfitting; conversely, larger baselines might overfit without matching regularization). Fair comparisons require tightly matched capacities and regularization across methods.
  - Limited scope: Only Mini-ImageNet is reported. Omniglot or other few-shot benchmarks (and cross-domain evaluation) are not included. The method’s generality across N-way, k-shot settings is not demonstrated beyond separate models trained specifically for 1-shot (12 steps) and 5-shot (5 steps).

- Methodological soundness and robustness:
  - Gradient independence assumption: The approach drops second-order terms entirely (as per Andrychowicz et al.), but presents no empirical analysis of the impact. For a meta-optimizer whose purpose is to learn effective updates, ignoring these dependencies could be significant; at minimum, an ablation or comparison to a truncated second-order variant would be valuable.
  - Sensitivity and ablations: No ablations on the gating design (inputs to i_t and f_t), the preprocessing p, the role of the learned initialization c0, the number of LSTM layers, or the BN protocol are reported. The paper claims the forget gate behaves like weight decay, but does not tie that to performance differences.
  - Scalability and compute: The method maintains per-parameter LSTM states, which is memory and compute intensive. There is no discussion of memory footprint, wall-clock time, or scalability to larger backbones, deeper networks, or higher-resolution inputs.
  - Generalization across adaptation horizons: The meta-learner is trained for a fixed number of inner-loop steps equal to test-time steps. There is no evaluation of how brittle performance is to deviations in the number of updates at test time, or to different N/k than seen during meta-training.

- Batch normalization protocol: While the paper proposes a reasonable episode-wise BN scheme to avoid leakage, there is no quantitative comparison against alternative BN strategies (e.g., freezing stats from meta-train or using group/instance norm), nor evidence that the approach does not inadvertently leak across episodes via running averages.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide full numerical results with 95% CIs, number of episodes used, and hyperparameter tuning details for all baselines. Match model capacity, data augmentation, and regularization across methods.
- Evaluate on standard splits (or provide your splits and re-run baselines on them), and consider including Omniglot and cross-domain tests to demonstrate robustness.
- Add ablations: with/without learned initialization; remove f_t or tie gates; vary preprocessing p; one vs two LSTM layers; effect of ignoring second-order terms (e.g., compare to a first-order vs second-order variant on smaller models); effect of BN strategy.
- Report compute: memory overhead, training/inference time per episode, and how these scale with network size.
- Test generalization to different inner-loop step counts and to N-/k-values unseen at meta-train. Demonstrate whether a single meta-learner can handle multiple settings without retraining.
- Ensure the Matching Networks baseline includes the recommended evaluation-time options and that its performance is competitive with published reports on comparable splits.

Questions for the authors
1. What are the exact 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot accuracies (mean ± 95% CI) and the number of test episodes? Please include the exact “Table 1.”
2. How do your Matching Networks numbers compare to a faithful reimplementation validated on a standard split? Did you tune its hyperparameters and consider the evaluation-time fine-tuning option?
3. Why is the learner CNN set to 32 filters while baselines use 64? Can you provide results with matched capacities across all methods?
4. How sensitive is your method to the number of inner-loop updates at test time? Can a single meta-learner generalize to both 1-shot and 5-shot without retraining?
5. What is the memory/time overhead of maintaining per-parameter LSTM states? Can you scale to larger backbones (e.g., 64/128 filters, ResNet-12) and report the impact?
6. Do you have ablations quantifying the effect of the gradient independence assumption? Have you tried truncated second-order meta-gradients on smaller models?
7. Can you report results on Omniglot and/or cross-domain generalization to assess robustness?
8. Could you provide ablations on the BN protocol and show that no performance is gained by inadvertent leakage?
