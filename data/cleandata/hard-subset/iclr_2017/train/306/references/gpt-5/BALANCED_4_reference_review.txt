1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles few-shot N-way classification via meta-learning. It proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that treats the learner’s parameters as the LSTM cell state and learns both (i) a good initialization for the learner and (ii) a learned update rule through input/forget gates that depend on current loss/gradient, their history, and the parameters themselves. The meta-learner is trained episodically to minimize test loss after a fixed small number of updates on each episode’s train set, matching evaluation conditions. The model shares optimizer parameters across coordinates to keep the meta-learner compact and uses gradient/loss preprocessing for stability. The paper also carefully handles batch normalization to prevent information leakage between episodes. On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the method outperforms simple fine-tuning and nearest neighbor baselines and is competitive with or better than Matching Networks; it also provides qualitative insight via visualizing learned gate values. Code is promised.

2. Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The approach is simple, well-motivated, and places optimization within a clear meta-learning framework that directly optimizes for rapid generalization after few updates. It cleanly ties the LSTM cell update to gradient-based learning and learns an initialization, which is impactful for few-shot tasks.
- Empirical results on Mini-ImageNet are competitive with a strong metric-learning baseline and clearly surpass natural transfer/fine-tuning baselines, with reasonable experimental care (episodic training, BN handling), and the method brings useful insights (gate visualizations).

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Learning to quickly adapt a classifier to new tasks from k=1 or 5 labeled examples per class, using many related tasks (episodes) for meta-training.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The formulation is tightly connected to prior work on learning to optimize (Andrychowicz et al.) and meta-learning for few-shot (e.g., Matching Networks, memory-augmented nets). The observation that the GD update structurally matches the LSTM cell update is elegant and motivates the design. The paper argues for learning both initialization and optimizer in an episodic setting; this is a reasonable and timely perspective.

Do the results support the claims?
- Positive: The method beats sensible non-episodic baselines and is competitive with a strong episodic metric-learning method on a challenging dataset. Training/evaluation protocols are matched, and the BN discussion shows awareness of leakage pitfalls. Visualizations suggest the optimizer learns nontrivial behavior, and the dependence on the number of updates is discussed.
- Caveats: The comparison suite is limited. Matching Networks are not allowed their optional fine-tuning. The learner backbone capacity differs across methods (the meta-learner uses 32 filters while baselines use 64), potentially confounding fairness. Ablations to disentangle the contributions of learned initialization vs learned optimizer vs preprocessing are missing. The “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring second derivatives through the learner) is adopted without empirical justification of its effect. Scaling concerns (per-parameter LSTM state) are not evaluated.

Overall, the paper offers a solid, well-argued contribution with promising results, but the empirical section could be more thorough and fair.

4. Additional feedback and questions for the authors (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
Suggestions for improvement:
- Fair comparisons: Use the same learner backbone across all methods (same number of filters, dropout, etc.). Report parameter counts. Allow Matching Networks their best-known protocol (including fine-tuning if applicable), or justify strongly why not.
- Ablations:
  - Learned init only + vanilla SGD/Adam (per-parameter or per-layer LR) to isolate the value of c0.
  - Fixed init + learned optimizer to assess the optimizer independently.
  - Remove gradient/loss preprocessing to quantify its impact.
  - Tie gates (ft+it=1) or set ft=1 to test design choices.
- Robustness/generalization: Train a single meta-learner that generalizes across shots (1 and 5) and ways; report cross-shot generalization. Evaluate on an additional benchmark (e.g., Omniglot or tieredImageNet) for breadth.
- Report compute: meta-training wall-clock, memory overhead due to per-parameter states, and inference-time cost compared to baselines.
- Sensitivity studies: number of inner-loop steps T, effect of clipping and the p parameter in preprocessing, and the stability benefits of your BN strategy vs alternatives (e.g., layer norm or group norm).
- Reproducibility: Release your Mini-ImageNet splits and code (link suggests this), and include precise hyperparameters (hidden sizes of LSTMs, gate initializations, schedule for running means in BN).
- Clarify the benefit of ignoring second derivatives with an experiment (e.g., a small model where full higher-order gradients are feasible) to quantify the approximation gap.

Questions for the authors:
- Architecture details: What are the hidden sizes of the first-layer LSTM and the meta-learner LSTM? How many parameters/states does the meta-learner maintain per coordinate? What is the total parameter count of learner vs meta-learner?
- Capacity parity: Why does the learner for your method use 32 filters while baselines use 64? Did you run ablations matching capacities, and if so, how do the results change?
- Matching Networks protocol: Did you attempt the variant with fine-tuning on Dtrain at meta-test time as in Vinyals et al.? If not, can you report those results for completeness?
- Higher-order gradients: Have you tried enabling second derivatives for a smaller setting? Does performance improve or training become unstable?
- Generalization across shots/ways: If you train on mixed shots (e.g., random k∈{1,5}), how does the learned optimizer adapt? Does performance degrade relative to specialized models?
- BN handling: Could you provide quantitative evidence that your “per-episode running stats” strategy improves over “frozen running stats” at meta-test, and confirm no leakage between episodes?
- Stability and initialization: How sensitive is training to the initial gate biases? Does the learned optimizer ever diverge on episodes, and how is this handled?
- Runtime: What is the meta-training time and memory footprint relative to standard fine-tuning or Matching Networks? How does the cost grow with learner size?

With the above clarifications and more balanced comparisons/ablations, this work would make a strong and influential contribution to meta-learning for few-shot classification.
