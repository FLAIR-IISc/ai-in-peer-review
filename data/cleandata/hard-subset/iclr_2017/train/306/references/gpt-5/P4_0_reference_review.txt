1) Summary of contributions
- The paper addresses few-shot, N-way classification via meta-learning. It proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to update a base classifier’s parameters over a small, fixed number of gradient steps. The key design is to interpret the classifier’s parameters as the LSTM cell state, with input/forget gates learning per-parameter update magnitudes and decay, respectively.
- The method shares meta-learner parameters across parameter coordinates to keep the meta-learner compact and uses preprocessing of gradients and losses to stabilize training. It trains the meta-learner to minimize each episode’s test loss after T inner-loop updates, thereby aligning the training objective with few-shot evaluation.
- Practical aspects are addressed: initialization of LSTM gate biases for stability, a strategy for batch normalization that avoids leakage across episodes, and an approximation that ignores second derivatives through the learner (gradient independence).
- Empirically, on Mini-ImageNet (with their own class split), the method outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with, or better than, Matching Networks, particularly on 5-shot. Some qualitative analysis of learned gate behaviors is provided.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) a well-motivated and technically sound meta-learning formulation that directly optimizes for rapid generalization within a few updates, and (ii) competitive to state-of-the-art empirical performance with careful engineering details for stability and a reasonable analysis of learned dynamics.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper clearly targets the challenge that standard gradient-based training does not adapt efficiently in the few-shot regime. Framing this as meta-learning over episodes (k-shot, N-way) is appropriate and well aligned with prior work.
- Placement in literature: The paper is well-positioned relative to Matching Networks and meta-learning with learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.). The novelty here is in tailoring a learned optimizer to few-shot classification, explicitly using episode test loss after a fixed number of inner updates as the meta-objective, and leveraging the LSTM cell state to represent classifier parameters, thereby jointly learning a task-common initialization and an update rule.
- Technical soundness:
  - The LSTM-as-optimizer formulation is coherent: per-parameter input gate acts as a learned step size, and the forget gate as a decay/escape mechanism. Parameter sharing across coordinates and gradient/loss preprocessing are standard and justified for stability and scalability.
  - The training objective (minimize Dtest loss after T inner steps) matches evaluation and is a strength.
  - The “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring second derivatives) is acknowledged; while it removes exactness, prior work shows it can still be effective. The paper includes measures (bias initialization, gradient clipping) to stabilize training.
  - Batch normalization treatment is carefully reasoned to avoid leakage and to keep training/testing conditions aligned.
- Empirical support:
  - The Mini-ImageNet experiments (5-way, 1- and 5-shot) show improvements over fine-tuning and nearest-neighbor baselines and competitive/better performance than Matching Networks on 5-shot. This supports the claim that meta-training an optimizer/initialization improves rapid adaptation over naive fine-tuning, and can match or surpass metric-learning approaches in some settings.
  - The gate visualization provides some insight that the meta-learner learns nontrivial and dataset-dependent behaviors.
- Weaknesses and limitations:
  - Evaluation is limited to a single dataset/split (their own Mini-ImageNet variant), without broader cross-dataset validation. Comparisons do not include contemporaneous or stronger metric-learning baselines beyond Matching Networks (e.g., prototypical variants) or ablations to isolate the contributions of learned initialization vs. learned update rule.
  - The second-order approximation (ignoring meta-gradients through gradients) is not empirically justified via comparison to the exact variant or ablations.
  - Some claims are stronger than what is demonstrated (e.g., “guarantee” of optimal initialization), as no theoretical optimality guarantees are given.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (for authors; not all part of the accept decision)
- Clarify and temper claims: avoid suggesting guarantees of optimality; state that the initialization is optimized for the chosen meta-objective over the training task distribution.
- Provide ablations:
  - Initialization only vs. learned updates (e.g., fix ft=1 and learn it; or vice versa).
  - Effect of the gradient-independence assumption vs. full second-order meta-gradients on accuracy and stability.
  - Impact of the preprocessing and of the first LSTM layer.
  - Vary the number of inner updates T and test whether models trained for one T generalize to different T, k, and N.
- Expand evaluation:
  - Include additional few-shot datasets or standard splits (and release your splits) to facilitate reproducibility.
  - Compare to stronger baselines (e.g., prototypical-style methods, basic cosine classifier with learned embedding).
  - Report confidence intervals, exact table values, and number of test episodes. Discuss computational cost (meta-training time/memory) and inner-loop wall-clock overhead at test time.
- Batch normalization: more explicit description of how running statistics are computed and reset per episode; verify no leakage from test images during meta-training.
- Notational/clarity edits: ensure consistent subscripts for meta-learner parameters (Θ), define all symbols once, and provide a concise pseudo-code or diagram mapping the two-layer LSTM to the parameter update rule.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain comes from the learned initialization vs. the learned update rule? Can you report results for a variant that only learns c0 but uses fixed SGD (and vice versa)?
- What is the sensitivity to the number of inner steps T at meta-test time? Does a model trained for T=5 remain effective for T=1 or T=10?
- Can you quantify the effect of the gradient-independence assumption by comparing to a (smaller) setting where you backpropagate second-order terms?
- How does the method perform across different N-way tasks than those seen in meta-training (e.g., trained on 5-way, tested on 10-way)?
- What is the computational overhead of the meta-learner at test time relative to standard fine-tuning?
- Could you release the Mini-ImageNet split you used and the exact Matching Networks implementation details to ensure comparability?
- Does the approach generalize to larger backbones, and how does performance scale with model capacity?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, well-motivated meta-learning approach with competitive empirical results and thoughtful engineering, meriting acceptance.
