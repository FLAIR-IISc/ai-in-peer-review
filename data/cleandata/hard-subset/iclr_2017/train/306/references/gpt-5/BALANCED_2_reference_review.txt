1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles few-shot, N-way classification by casting learning as meta-learning of an optimizer. It proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner whose cell state encodes the learner’s parameters and whose input/forget gates implement learned per-parameter update rules. The method jointly learns (i) a task-common initialization for the classifier and (ii) an update rule that uses gradients, losses, and parameter history to adapt quickly from k labeled examples. The training objective matches test-time conditions by backpropagating from the test-set loss of each episode, with parameter sharing across coordinates and simple gradient/loss preprocessing. The paper addresses batch normalization carefully in the episodic setting to avoid leakage across tasks. On mini-ImageNet (custom split), the method outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with, and better in 5-shot than, Matching Networks. Visualizations suggest the learned optimizer uses consistent decay but task-dependent input gating, supporting the claim that it learns nontrivial, data-dependent adaptation strategies.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- A clear and well-motivated meta-learning formulation that directly optimizes for rapid generalization with few updates, coupled with a simple yet elegant LSTM-as-optimizer construction that learns both initialization and update dynamics.
- Empirical results on a challenging benchmark showing consistent gains over strong transfer baselines and competitive performance with a top metric-learning method, alongside thoughtful treatment of batch normalization in the episodic setting.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Few-shot learning where conventional training fails due to limited updates and random initialization; the paper aims to learn an initialization and optimizer that produce rapid generalization.

Is the approach well motivated and situated? Yes. The method is closely related to learning-to-learn and learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.), but it is positioned for few-shot regimes and trained with an objective that explicitly matches episodic evaluation on test sets. The connection between gradient descent and the LSTM update is natural, and the choice to share optimizer parameters across coordinates is practical.

Do the claims hold, and is the evidence rigorous?
- The experimental setup follows standard episodic protocols (k-shot, N-way; meta-train/val/test class splits). The classifier backbone is simple and comparable across methods. The baselines include nearest-neighbor with learned embeddings and fine-tuning from pretraining; Matching Networks are reimplemented with reasonable care.
- Results: The proposed method surpasses the transfer baselines by clear margins and is competitive with Matching Networks; it appears stronger in 5-shot. Confidence intervals are reported and overlap discussed.
- Design choices are examined: number of updates is matched between training and testing; BN handling is carefully reasoned to avoid leakage while keeping train/test conditions aligned.
- The gradient-independence approximation (ignoring second derivatives through the learner) mirrors prior practice and appears to work empirically, though the paper does not ablate its effect.

Limitations that temper the strength of acceptance:
- Limited breadth of evaluation: only one dataset (custom mini-ImageNet split) and one backbone; no results on Omniglot or other domains, making generality harder to assess.
- Baseline coverage is narrow: only one family of metric learners (Matching Nets) and transfer baselines; comparisons to alternative meta-learners or learned optimizers are missing, and the fairness of comparisons is affected by custom splits and reimplementations.
- Ablations are light: it is unclear how much gain comes from the learned initialization versus the learned gates; sensitivity to the number of updates, gradient preprocessing, and the gradient-independence assumption is not quantified.
- Computational aspects (memory/time overhead of per-parameter LSTM state) are not reported.

4. Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide comprehensive ablations:
  - Learned init only + SGD/Adam vs learned gates only vs full model.
  - Remove/alter forget gate; test simple per-parameter learned learning rates (e.g., diagonal adaptive rule) as a control.
  - With and without gradient/loss preprocessing.
  - With and without the gradient-independence assumption (where feasible).
- Report compute/memory costs: hidden size of the per-parameter LSTM, total meta-learner parameters, training time per episode, and scalability to larger backbones.
- Extend evaluation:
  - Additional datasets (e.g., Omniglot, CUB) and cross-domain generalization.
  - Mismatched N-way/K-shot between meta-train and meta-test.
  - Robustness to varying number of adaptation steps at test time.
- Strengthen baselines:
  - More metric-learning baselines (e.g., nearest-centroid/prototype-based) and a learned-optimizer baseline (e.g., applying Andrychowicz et al. to the episodic objective).
  - Fine-tuning baselines with regularization (weight decay, dropout, early stopping) and modern optimizers.
- Clarify episodic BN protocol: explicitly state that running statistics are computed only on Dtrain per episode and reset between episodes; quantify the performance drop if BN is mismatched to highlight the importance of this choice.
- Improve clarity/typos: fix minor typos (“neutral networks”), LaTeX artifacts in references, and provide a more detailed schematic of the two-layer LSTM and data flow.

Questions for the authors
- Meta-learner architecture:
  - What is the hidden size and parameter count of the LSTM(s)? How many meta-parameters are introduced relative to the learner?
  - How are scalar losses Lt fed per coordinate—replicated across dimensions or aggregated features?
- Computational cost:
  - What are the memory and time overheads per episode for 12-step (1-shot) and 5-step (5-shot) adaptation? How does it scale to deeper backbones?
- Training details:
  - How many episodes are used for meta-training, and what is the meta-batch size? Any curriculum or schedule for T across training?
  - How sensitive is performance to the number of adaptation steps at test time if it differs from meta-training?
- Ablations:
  - What is the performance when learning only the initialization (with fixed SGD/Adam) versus learning only the gate dynamics from a random init?
  - Did you test removing the forget gate (ft=1) or tying gates (GRU-like)? Any stability issues?
  - What is the impact of the gradient-independence assumption? Did you attempt limited second-order training on small models?
- Generalization:
  - Does a meta-learner trained on 5-way transfer to 10-way without retraining? How does accuracy degrade?
  - Can the learned optimizer transfer across different learner architectures?
- Reproducibility:
  - Please release the exact class splits and seeds for the mini-ImageNet variant and details for Matching Nets reimplementation (dropout rates, embedding sizes), to ensure fair comparison.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and promising approach with solid early results; addressing the evaluation breadth and ablations would strengthen the contribution further.
