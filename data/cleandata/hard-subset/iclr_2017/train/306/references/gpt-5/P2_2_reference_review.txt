1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot N-way classification by learning an optimizer and initialization for a neural classifier via meta-learning. The key idea is to cast parameter updates as an LSTM cell update: the learner’s parameters are the LSTM cell state, and input/forget gates produce per-parameter learning rates and shrinkage. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end, across episodes, to minimize the test loss after a small, fixed number of updates on the episode’s training set, thereby explicitly optimizing for fast generalization from few examples. The method uses coordinate-wise parameter sharing for scalability, gradient/loss preprocessing, and a careful treatment of batch normalization to avoid information leakage across episodes. On miniImageNet (custom split), the method outperforms sensible baselines (nearest neighbor on pretrained embeddings; fine-tuning) and is competitive with or better than Matching Networks, notably outperforming Matching Nets on 5-shot. The paper provides code and useful training details (initializations, clipping, gate biasing).

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A well-motivated and elegant formulation that unifies learned optimization and meta-learning specifically for few-shot generalization, with clear conceptual novelty over prior learned-optimizer work (which targeted general training) and over metric-learning approaches.
- Empirical results show meaningful gains over baselines and competitive performance against a strong contemporary method (Matching Nets), with clear, replicable training protocol and code.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The few-shot setting is clearly defined through episodic meta-training and meta-testing. The limitations of standard gradient-based training under few updates and random initialization are convincingly argued, and meta-learning is an appropriate framework. The paper targets both rapid adaptation (through learned update rules) and good priors (through learned initialization).
- Relation to literature: The work is well placed among learned optimizers (Andrychowicz et al.) and meta-learning/few-shot methods (Matching Nets, MANN). The novelty is to train the optimizer specifically to minimize post-adaptation test loss under a fixed budget of updates and to encapsulate the learner’s parameters in the LSTM cell state, simultaneously learning an initialization and update rule. This is a meaningful contribution at the time.
- Methodological soundness: The LSTM parameterization for input/forget gates using current loss, gradient, parameters, and previous gate is reasonable; the coordinate-wise sharing and preprocessing are standard and justified. The “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring second derivatives through the learner’s loss/gradients) is a known simplification in learned optimizer work; while it weakens theoretical exactness, it is pragmatic and appears effective here.
- Experimental evidence: The evaluation protocol mirrors test conditions (episodic training with few updates), a clear strength. The treatment of batch normalization in meta-learning is thoughtful and important to replicate. Results on miniImageNet demonstrate improvements over strong baselines, and the 5-shot gains over Matching Nets are notable. Visualizations of learned gate dynamics provide qualitative insight suggesting nontrivial adaptation strategies.

Caveats:
- Comparability and breadth: Results are on a custom miniImageNet split and the authors’ re-implementation of Matching Nets, making cross-paper comparison harder. The paper would benefit from additional datasets (e.g., Omniglot) for breadth.
- Ablations: There is no ablation disentangling the benefits of learned initialization vs. learned optimizer (e.g., fixing ft=1 or using a constant learning rate) or quantifying the effect of the “gradient independence” assumption.
- Generality: The approach is evaluated with a small CNN; scalability to deeper backbones (e.g., ResNet) or to variable N and k is not shown.

Overall, despite these limitations, the core idea is compelling and the empirical evidence is strong enough to merit acceptance.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Add ablations:
  - Learned init only vs. learned updates only vs. both; fixed ft=1; constant vs. learned it; with/without loss input to gates.
  - With and without gradient independence assumption (where feasible) to assess its impact.
  - Sensitivity to number of inner-loop steps T and mismatch between train-time T and test-time T.
- Fairness and capacity control:
  - Align the learner’s CNN capacity across methods (filters, dropout) and report parameter counts to ensure fairness. Clarify the 32 vs 64 filter discrepancy.
- Breadth of evaluation:
  - Report on at least one additional dataset (e.g., Omniglot) and, if possible, a stronger backbone on miniImageNet to demonstrate scalability.
  - Evaluate cross-episode generalization: train on one k,N and test on others to measure robustness of the learned optimizer/initialization.
- Reporting:
  - Provide exact accuracies with 95% confidence intervals and the number of evaluation episodes for all methods.
  - Include computational cost (time per episode, memory) and meta-training stability (e.g., variance across random seeds).
- Analysis:
  - Quantify the distribution of gate activations (saturation, sparsity) and correlate with adaptation success.
  - Discuss failure modes (e.g., overfitting in 1-shot) and whether the learned decay/forgetting mitigates them versus standard fine-tuning.
- Writing and references:
  - Fix minor typos and incomplete/broken references; ensure consistent notation.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the performance gain comes from the learned initialization versus the learned per-parameter update rule? Can you provide ablations?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of inner-loop updates T, and does the learned optimizer generalize to different T than seen in training?
- Can the same meta-learner handle variable N and k without retraining? If trained on mixed episodes, how does it perform across k and N?
- What is the effect of removing loss inputs from the gates (i.e., using gradients only)? Do the gates saturate during adaptation?
- How does the approach scale to larger architectures (e.g., ResNets)? What are the compute/memory implications of backpropagating through T steps for such models?
- Please clarify model capacity parity across baselines and the meta-learner (32 vs 64 filters) and report parameter counts and exact CI/episode counts.
