1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot classification by framing learning as meta-learning of an optimizer and an initialization. The core idea is to treat the learner’s parameters as the cell state of an LSTM and to learn per-parameter input/forget gates that implement updates driven by current gradients, losses, and the previous state. The meta-learner is trained episodically to minimize test loss after a fixed number of inner updates, thereby explicitly optimizing for fast generalization. Key components include: parameter sharing across coordinates, gradient and loss preprocessing, learning the initializer c0, an explicit episodic training objective with unrolled optimization, and careful handling of batch normalization statistics to avoid leakage across episodes while maintaining matching train/test conditions. Experiments on miniImageNet (5-way, 1- and 5-shot) show performance better than natural baselines and competitive with, or better than, Matching Networks. The paper also includes gate visualizations to shed light on the learned optimization behavior.

2) Decision: Weak accept
Reasons:
- The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and distinct in that it unifies a learned optimizer with a learned initialization optimized for few-shot generalization.
- Empirical results on miniImageNet are competitive and particularly strong in the 5-shot setting, and the paper provides useful implementation insights (e.g., BN handling) and some analysis.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The paper is well-positioned within meta-learning. Compared to Andrychowicz et al. (learned optimizers), the novelty is the episodic, few-shot meta-objective (optimize test loss after T inner steps), the explicit learning of the initialization for rapid adaptation, and the use of loss signals (not just gradients) in the update rule. It also differs from metric-learning approaches (Matching Nets) by adapting a parametric classifier rather than relying purely on nearest-neighbor inference. This framing is compelling and addresses the small-data failure modes of standard SGD/Adam and naive transfer.
- Methodological soundness: The LSTM-as-optimizer formulation is coherent. The gate parameterizations and preprocessing are reasonable. The “gradient independence” assumption (ignoring higher-order terms) is a standard pragmatic choice that simplifies training; while it may bias gradients, the empirical success suggests it suffices in this setup.
- Empirical support: The miniImageNet setup aligns with prior work (episodic training, 5-way 1/5-shot). Baselines include nearest neighbor on learned embeddings and fine-tuning a pre-trained classifier; Matching Networks are reimplemented with comparable architecture depth/width to prior reports. The learned optimizer’s advantage in 5-shot is noteworthy and supports the claim that learning both initialization and update rule is beneficial. The analysis of BN and gate visualizations adds credibility.
- Limitations affecting strength of evidence:
  - Baseline coverage: Missing comparisons to other initialization-based meta-learners (e.g., MAML-style methods) and to Prototypical Networks; these would strengthen the positioning.
  - Fairness and architecture parity: The learner in the meta-optimizer uses 32 filters while Matching Nets and some baselines use 64. This asymmetry could either handicap or benefit different methods; a controlled ablation with matched capacities would clarify fairness.
  - Scope: Only 5-way tasks on miniImageNet are reported; it would be valuable to see 20-way, cross-way generalization (train/test with different N), and robustness across shots. Cross-dataset generalization (e.g., CUB or CIFAR-FS) would test transfer.
  - Ablations: No ablations on the importance of (i) learned initialization c0, (ii) feeding loss vs only gradient, (iii) forget gate vs ft=1, (iv) preprocessing, and (v) the number of inner steps and train/test mismatch T.
  - Scalability: The per-parameter LSTM state implies significant memory/compute. The paper doesn’t analyze scaling to deeper/wider networks (e.g., ResNets) or strategies like block-wise updates.
  - Training stability: The special initializations of gate biases are necessary; sensitivity analyses would be helpful.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Provide matched-capacity experiments across all methods, and report parameter counts. Include stronger baselines (Prototypical Networks; an initialization-based meta-learner with vanilla GD inner loop).
- Add ablations isolating the contribution of learned initialization vs learned update rule. Show that fixing ft=1 and/or removing loss input degrades performance. Report with/without preprocessing.
- Report sensitivity to number of inner updates and to train/test mismatch (e.g., train with 5 steps, test with 10).
- Analyze compute and memory overhead; discuss strategies for scaling (e.g., per-layer/ per-block LSTMs, low-rank parametrization, or grouping parameters).
- Clarify whether BN handling is identical across all compared methods and whether any tuning advantage accrues to the proposed method.
- Consider adding results on additional benchmarks and settings (20-way, variable-way/shot training, cross-domain test) to substantiate generality.
- Improve clarity in Algorithm 1 around gradient flow: explicitly state where gradients are stopped under the gradient-independence assumption.
- Check for double-blind compliance; the code link and acknowledgments appear to reveal identities.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain comes from the learned initialization vs the learned per-parameter update? Can you report an ablation that learns c0 but uses fixed ft=1 and a global learned step size?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of inner steps T at meta-train vs meta-test? Does the learned optimizer generalize beyond the trained horizon?
- Can you provide results with matched model capacities (same number of filters/parameters) across all methods, and report parameter counts?
- What is the memory/compute overhead (vs SGD/Adam) for your per-parameter LSTM on the 4-layer CNN, and how does this scale to larger architectures?
- Did you try including second-order terms (without the gradient-independence assumption) on smaller models to assess their impact?
- How critical is feeding the scalar loss into the optimizer compared to gradient-only inputs?
- Are BN statistics handled identically for baselines and Matching Networks? Please confirm no unintended information leakage.
- Does the learned optimizer trained on 5-way generalize to 20-way without retraining? Likewise, does a model trained on 1-shot generalize to 5-shot?
