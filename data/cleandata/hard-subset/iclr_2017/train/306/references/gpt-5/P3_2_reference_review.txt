Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles few-shot, N-way classification by framing learning as meta-learning of an optimizer. An LSTM meta-learner updates a base classifier’s parameters; the LSTM cell state is identified with the classifier weights, and gates produce per-coordinate update magnitudes (input gate) and decay (forget gate) as functions of current loss, gradients, parameters, and previous gate values.
- The meta-learner is trained end-to-end across episodes to minimize test loss after a fixed number of inner-loop updates, thereby learning both a task-common initialization and an update rule tailored for rapid adaptation. Practicalities include coordinate-wise parameter sharing, gradient/loss preprocessing, an initialization scheme for stability, a “gradient independence” approximation to avoid second derivatives, and careful handling of batch normalization to avoid leakage across episodes.
- Experiments on Mini-ImageNet (custom split) show significant gains over nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and competitive or better performance compared to Matching Networks, with qualitative analyses of the learned gates.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
1) The idea of casting inner-loop optimization for few-shot learning as an LSTM whose state is the model parameters is elegant, well-motivated, and distinct from prior “learned optimizer” work by explicitly optimizing test performance under few updates and by learning the initialization.
2) Empirical results on a challenging benchmark are promising and the paper offers useful practical insights (BN handling, preprocessing, stability), suggesting real applicability.

Supporting arguments
Addressing the three core questions:
1) Problem: Rapid adaptation from k examples per class without large-scale fine-tuning. The paper seeks a meta-learned optimizer that performs well in a fixed, small number of inner updates, learning both an initialization and an update rule.
2) Motivation and placement: The paper is well situated among meta-learning (Santoro et al., Matching Nets) and learned optimizers (Andrychowicz et al.). The contrast to Andrychowicz et al. is clear: here, the objective is explicitly the episode test loss after few updates; the optimizer is specialized for few-shot adaptation rather than general long-horizon training. The mapping of GD updates to LSTM cell updates is neat and leveraged meaningfully.
3) Support for claims: The design is coherent, implementation details are given, and the reported results indicate improvements over reasonable baselines and competitiveness with a strong metric-learning method. The visualizations suggest the optimizer is not trivial and differs by task.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Identifying the LSTM state with parameters and exposing loss and gradient statistics to the gates is an intuitive, architecture-agnostic way to learn adaptation strategies.
- Training protocol matches test-time constraints (fixed update budget), aligning with best practices in episodic meta-learning.
- Practical contributions: Parameter sharing across coordinates, gradient/loss preprocessing, BN handling per episode, and initialization choices are valuable; many are crucial to reproduce and scale the approach.
- Empirical performance: On Mini-ImageNet, the method improves over a fine-tuning baseline (which overfits in 1-shot) and nearest neighbor, and achieves competitive/better results than Matching Nets in 5-shot.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical scope: Only Mini-ImageNet is evaluated. Omniglot or other benchmarks (and cross-dataset generalization) would strengthen claims. The paper uses a custom Mini-ImageNet split and a reimplementation of Matching Nets; this raises fairness concerns and makes external comparison difficult. Precise numbers and confidence intervals (Table 1) are not visible here.
- Baseline breadth: Missing comparisons to other episodic methods (e.g., prototypical networks) and to meta-learning via backpropagating through inner updates (second-order or first-order approximations). The paper acknowledges avoiding second derivatives; a comparison would clarify the cost/benefit of the “gradient independence” approximation.
- Ablations: No disentanglement of learned initialization vs learned update rule. For example: learn only initialization with vanilla SGD; or fix initialization but learn gates; or remove loss from gate inputs; or drop the forget gate. Such ablations would illuminate where the gains come from.
- Scalability and efficiency: The coordinate-wise LSTM over all parameters imposes non-trivial memory/compute overhead; experiments remain on relatively small Conv-4 backbones. Evidence on deeper architectures (e.g., ResNet-12/18) would strengthen impact.
- Minor clarity issues: There is an inconsistency in the backbone filter counts (32 vs 64) across sections and a mismatch in reported update counts (10 vs 12 for 1-shot). These should be clarified to ensure fairness and reproducibility.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide full numerical results with 95% CIs, number of episodes, and seeds; release the exact Mini-ImageNet splits alongside the code (the link is appreciated).
- Add ablations to quantify: (i) learned init vs learned optimizer; (ii) effect of ft (forget) and it (input) gates; (iii) input features to the gates (gradient only vs gradient+loss vs including θt−1); (iv) preprocessing and BN handling choices.
- Analyze sensitivity to mismatch between the number of meta-trained updates and meta-test updates; show adaptation curves over steps.
- Report compute/memory overhead and wall-clock times per episode compared to SGD or first-order meta-learning.
- Clarify Matching Nets implementation choices (backbone capacity, dropout, data augmentation) to ensure fairness; align backbones across methods when possible.
- Consider additional benchmarks (Omniglot, tieredImageNet) and cross-way/cross-shot generalization (train on 5-way, test on 20-way; train on 1-shot, test on 5-shot).
- Discuss stability with BN in extreme low-shot regimes (batch of 5 examples) and whether alternative normalizations (Layer/GroupNorm) help.

Questions for the authors
1) How much of the performance gain comes from the learned initialization versus the per-step learned optimizer? Can you provide ablations isolating these factors?
2) How sensitive is performance to the number of inner updates at meta-test time, especially when it differs from meta-training? Does the optimizer generalize to longer/shorter horizons?
3) What is the compute/memory overhead of the coordinate-wise LSTM relative to standard SGD? Can the method scale to deeper backbones (e.g., ResNet-12/18) without prohibitive cost?
4) How does the “gradient independence” assumption affect performance? On small models, can you compare against full higher-order meta-gradients?
5) Please clarify backbone capacities across methods (32 vs 64 filters) and ensure Matching Nets comparisons use identical encoders. What are the exact results and CIs?
6) How robust is the BN strategy in 1-shot settings? Did you evaluate alternatives such as LayerNorm/GroupNorm to reduce reliance on per-episode batch statistics?
7) Does the learned optimizer generalize across different N-way and k-shot configurations than those seen during meta-training?
