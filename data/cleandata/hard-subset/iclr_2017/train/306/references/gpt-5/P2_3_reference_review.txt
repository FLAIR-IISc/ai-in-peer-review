Summary of contributions
- Problem: Few-shot N-way classification where standard gradient-based training from random init underperforms. The paper frames few-shot learning as meta-learning: learning an optimizer and initialization that enable fast adaptation with very few updates.
- Approach: An LSTM-based meta-learner whose cell state is the learner’s parameters θ. The LSTM’s input/forget gates implement per-parameter learning-rate and decay, driven by current loss, gradients, parameter values, and previous gate values. Parameters are shared across coordinates to keep the meta-learner compact; gradients and losses are preprocessed to stabilize scale.
- Training: Episodic training that minimizes the post-adaptation test loss of each episode, unrolling T learner steps and backpropagating through the unroll (while applying a “gradient independence” approximation to avoid second derivatives).
- Practicalities: Careful handling of batch normalization statistics to avoid leakage between episodes; initialization of gate biases to stabilize training.
- Empirics: On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the method beats nearest-neighbor and fine-tune baselines and is competitive with, and better than Matching Networks in 5-shot, despite using a smaller backbone than the matching nets. Some qualitative analysis of the learned gates suggests sensible behaviors.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A well-motivated, novel instantiation of meta-learning that unifies learned initialization and learned update rule, with clear architectural insight (LSTM cell-as-optimizer) and practical training recipe; (2) Solid empirical results on a challenging benchmark with fair episodic protocol, outperforming strong baselines and competitive with state of the art at the time.

Supporting arguments
- What is tackled? The paper addresses fast adaptation from few examples by learning both an initialization and an optimizer that are explicitly trained for rapid generalization in an episodic setting. This directly targets the failure modes of standard optimizers and random inits in the small-data regime.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well-situated within meta-learning (Schmidhuber, Thrun) and builds on learning-to-optimize (Andrychowicz et al.) but adapts it specifically to few-shot learning with episodic supervision and an update rule explicitly tied to an LSTM gating interpretation. The connection to GRU/LSTM updates, parameter sharing across coordinates, and gradient/loss preprocessing are clearly motivated.
- Do the results support the claims? Yes, within scope. The method:
  - Outperforms pretrain+nearest-neighbor and pretrain+fine-tune baselines in both 1-shot and 5-shot, with clear rationale for the fine-tune baseline’s overfitting under few updates.
  - Matches or exceeds Matching Networks (notably in 5-shot) even with a smaller learner backbone (32 vs 64 filters), strengthening the empirical case.
  - Provides sensible design choices for BN in episodic meta-learning, an often-overlooked but impactful detail.
  - Shows qualitative gate behaviors consistent with learned weight decay and adaptive learning rates, supporting the optimizer interpretation.
- Rigor and correctness: The training objective (minimizing post-adaptation test loss) is appropriate and matches evaluation. The “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring higher-order terms) is acknowledged, justified by prior work, and empirically sufficient. The episodic split protocol is standard; code availability improves reproducibility.

Points that temper enthusiasm (but do not outweigh the positives)
- Scope and flexibility: Separate meta-learners are trained per k-shot and with a fixed number of unrolled steps, which limits flexibility across k and adaptation horizons. Experiments on cross-k/N generalization would strengthen claims.
- Ablations: There is limited ablation on the architectural choices (e.g., effect of ft=1, no loss input, no preprocessing, per-layer vs per-parameter sharing, impact of the second LSTM layer). Such ablations would clarify which components are essential.
- Baselines: While Matching Networks and reasonable nonparametric/fine-tune baselines are included, additional contemporaneous comparisons (e.g., Prototypical Networks) would provide a fuller picture. That said, the provided comparisons are nontrivial and informative.
- Compute/memory: The per-parameter LSTM update can be expensive for large models; no runtime/memory analysis or scaling discussion is provided.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Add ablations:
  - Remove loss input; fix ft=1; constant vs learned it; no preprocessing; per-layer vs per-parameter sharing; one vs two LSTM layers.
  - Evaluate sensitivity to the number of adaptation steps at test time (using fewer/more than trained T).
- Report:
  - Confidence intervals for all methods; the exact Mini-ImageNet split used; data augmentation; and identical BN/dropout policies across methods to ensure fairness.
  - Wall-clock and memory overhead vs standard SGD/Adam across backbones.
- Generalization:
  - Train on 5-way and test on 20-way; train on 1-shot and test on 5-shot (and vice versa) to probe robustness.
  - Cross-domain tests (e.g., train on one dataset, test on another) to assess learned optimizer/initialization transfer.
- Analysis:
  - Quantify the contribution of learned initialization alone (freeze gates to emulate small fixed-step SGD) vs dynamic gating.
  - Explore per-layer gate statistics and whether the meta-learner learns layer-wise schedules.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the number of unrolled steps T? If you train with T=5, how does it perform with T=1,3,10 at test time?
- Did you try per-layer gating (sharing LSTM parameters within a layer but not across layers) or combining per-parameter and per-tensor statistics (e.g., RMS of gradients) to reduce compute while keeping adaptivity?
- How many meta-training episodes and unroll length per episode were used, and how did you address vanishing/exploding gradients through long unrolls?
- How much does the “gradient independence” approximation affect performance? Did you try second-order training on smaller models to quantify the gap?
- Are the BN handling choices applied identically to baselines? Could mismatched BN policies explain part of the fine-tune baseline underperformance?
- Can a single meta-learner trained jointly on mixed k and N generalize well across k/N at test time?
- What is the training and inference time overhead relative to SGD/Adam fine-tuning on the same backbone? How does it scale with model size?

Overall, this paper proposes a clear, well-motivated meta-learning optimizer for few-shot learning with competitive results and useful practical insights. The work is impactful and timely; a strong accept with suggested additions would make it more comprehensive.
