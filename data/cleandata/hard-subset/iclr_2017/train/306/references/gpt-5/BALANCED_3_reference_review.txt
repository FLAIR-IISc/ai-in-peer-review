1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles few-shot classification by casting learning as meta-learning: an LSTM-based meta-learner is trained to update the parameters of a base classifier (learner) within a small, fixed number of gradient steps. The key idea is to view the learner’s parameter update as the LSTM cell-state update, enabling the meta-learner to (a) learn a task-common initialization for the classifier and (b) learn per-parameter input/forget gating that modulates update magnitudes, informed by current loss, gradients, past gate values, and parameters. The method shares meta-learner parameters across coordinates to maintain scalability and uses gradient/loss preprocessing to stabilize training. Careful handling of batch normalization avoids information leakage across episodes while matching train/test conditions. On Mini-ImageNet (custom split), the approach outperforms simple baselines and is competitive with or better than Matching Networks, especially in 5-shot. Visualizations of gate dynamics suggest nontrivial, dataset-dependent optimization behaviors. The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and accompanied by code.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- A principled, elegant formulation that learns both a good initialization and an update rule tailored to few-shot, with clear connections to LSTM dynamics and meta-learning.
- Competitive empirical results on a challenging benchmark, plus valuable implementation insights (BN treatment, gradient preprocessing) that are likely of practical interest.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Few-shot N-way classification, where a learner must generalize from k labeled examples per class. The paper aims to meta-learn an optimizer that can rapidly adapt a base network with very few updates, and to meta-learn a beneficial initialization.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The motivation—standard optimizers and random initializations are ill-suited for very few updates—is convincing. The method builds on meta-learning ideas and relates to LSTM-based optimizers (Andrychowicz et al.), but is specialized to the episodic few-shot regime, including a training objective that directly optimizes test performance after a fixed small number of updates. Positioning with metric-learning methods (Matching Networks) is appropriate; however, comparisons to other meta-learning baselines (e.g., Prototypical Networks, MAML-style methods) are missing and would strengthen placement.

Do the results support the claims? Broadly, yes. The method beats nearest-neighbor and fine-tune baselines and is competitive or better than Matching Networks on the authors’ Mini-ImageNet split, with clear gains in 5-shot. The training protocol mirrors test-time conditions, and the ablation-like visualization of gates provides qualitative support that the meta-learner is learning nontrivial update rules. That said, several pieces of evidence are missing that would solidify the claims:
- No ablations to disentangle the contributions of meta-learned initialization vs. update rule (e.g., learned init + vanilla SGD, or fixed init + learned gates).
- No sensitivity to the gradient-independence assumption (ignoring second-order terms).
- No analysis of computational/memory overhead of per-parameter LSTM updates vs. standard fine-tuning.
- Use of a custom Mini-ImageNet split complicates comparability.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines and literature: Please compare against Prototypical Networks and, if feasible, optimization-based meta-learning baselines (e.g., MAML/Reptile). Even brief experiments or a discussion would clarify where your learned optimizer sits relative to metric- and gradient-based meta-learners.
- Ablations:
  - Learned initialization vs. learned optimizer: Freeze gates to approximate SGD (f≈1, i as scalar lr) to assess the value of the meta-learned init alone; conversely, random or standard init + learned gates.
  - Remove loss input to the LSTM or remove gradient sign/magnitude preprocessing to quantify their impact.
  - Vary number of unrolled steps T and test generalization when T at test-time differs from training.
- Clarity/details:
  - Sign convention: Equation (2) and the mapping from gradients to the candidate cell state c̃t occasionally suggest a sign mismatch (c̃t = ∇Lt vs. −∇Lt). Please clarify how descent is enforced.
  - Precisely specify hidden sizes for the LSTM(s), number of parameters, and per-update computational cost.
  - Report confidence intervals and number of evaluation episodes for all methods. Ensure baseline fine-tuning is reasonably tuned (e.g., weight decay, early stopping, data augmentation) to avoid a strawman.
- Generalization:
  - Report cross-way and cross-shot generalization (train at 5-way/1-shot and test at different N/k).
  - Discuss generalization across domains or at least across disjoint class splits beyond one random split; consider releasing your splits for reproducibility.
- BN handling: The proposed episode-wise statistic reset is sensible. Please provide a brief empirical comparison vs. alternative BN strategies to quantify its benefit.
- Reproducibility and presentation: The code link is appreciated; releasing the exact dataset split and hyperparameters would help. Clean up reference formatting issues.

Questions for the authors
- How large are the meta-learner LSTM states per parameter, and what is the runtime/memory overhead compared to standard fine-tuning with SGD/Adam? How does this scale with larger backbones?
- Can you clarify the sign in the update rule: is c̃t set to −∇Lt or ∇Lt? If the latter, how is the negative step encoded (via it or learned transformations)?
- How sensitive is performance to the gradient-independence assumption? Did you attempt truncated second-order gradients on small models to gauge impact?
- What happens if the number of adaptation steps at test-time differs from training (e.g., fewer or more updates)?
- To what extent do results depend on the loss input to the meta-learner vs. gradients alone?
- Could you report ablations showing the effect of learning the initialization c0 vs. random init with the learned gates?
- How many episodes are used for meta-validation/test, and what are the exact 95% CIs for each method?
- Did you try training a single meta-learner to handle both 1-shot and 5-shot (and variable N-way), and if so, how does it perform?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, elegant approach with promising results and practical insights. Addressing the above questions/ablations and adding broader baselines would substantially strengthen the paper.
