Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot learning by framing it as meta-learning of an optimizer that can rapidly adapt a base classifier from a handful of examples. The key idea is to cast parameter updates as the cell-state update of an LSTM: the learner’s parameters are the LSTM cell state; the input gate learns a per-parameter learning rate and the forget gate learns shrinkage/weight decay; and the meta-learner also learns a task-common initialization. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end across episodes to minimize the test-set loss after a fixed small number of updates, matching the few-shot evaluation protocol. To make this scalable, the meta-optimizer shares parameters across coordinates and uses gradient/loss preprocessing; it also proposes a careful batch normalization protocol to avoid information leakage across episodes. On mini-ImageNet (custom splits), the approach outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with or better than Matching Networks (notably better on 5-shot). The paper also provides qualitative analyses of the learned gates across layers and tasks.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper introduces a bold and compelling formulation—an LSTM meta-optimizer specialized for few-shot learning that jointly learns initialization and per-parameter update rules and is trained with a test-time-aligned objective. Despite some experimental limitations, the conceptual novelty and the promising empirical results merit acceptance.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Clear and well-motivated. The limitations of standard gradient-based training in the small-update regime and the need for learned initialization and rapid adaptation are convincingly argued. The objective of optimizing test performance after a fixed number of updates directly targets the few-shot setting.

- Novelty and positioning: The work connects learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.) with few-shot learning. The architectural mapping from gradient descent to LSTM gates and the emphasis on learning both initialization and update dynamics for few-shot is a meaningful and fresh direction. The discussion of memory-augmented/meta-learning methods and metric-learning baselines is appropriate.

- Method correctness: The training procedure (unrolling for T steps, optimizing test loss) is sound. The “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring second-order dependencies through gradients) follows prior work and is acceptable here, though it weakens guarantees. The BN handling is thoughtfully designed to avoid leakage while matching train/test conditions—an easy-to-overlook but important detail.

- Empirical support: Results on mini-ImageNet (custom splits) are competitive with Matching Networks in 1-shot and better in 5-shot, and clearly better than reasonable baselines. The observation that naive fine-tuning overfits underscores the value of learning initialization and update rules tailored to few-shot. However, the scope is limited to one dataset and modest ablations; fairness of comparisons could be improved (different convolutional widths for the learner vs. baselines).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Fairness and reproducibility:
  - Align backbone architectures across all methods (same number of filters, same regularization) to ensure fair comparisons.
  - Release the exact mini-ImageNet splits used (you provide code; please include splits) to enable reproducibility.
  - Report standard errors and number of episodes used for evaluation; this is partially included—ensure consistency across all tables.

- Ablations and analysis:
  - Disentangle contributions: (i) learning initialization only (ft=1, simple optimizer), (ii) learning per-parameter learning rates only, (iii) learning both gates, (iv) effect of preprocessing. These would clarify where gains come from.
  - Compare to a meta-learned optimizer trained on train loss rather than test loss to support the claim that test-aligned training is crucial.
  - Investigate robustness to mismatch in number of updates (e.g., train for T but test at T/2 and 2T), and to varying N and k at meta-test.
  - Provide runtime/memory comparison vs. SGD fine-tuning and Matching Networks at meta-test time; discuss scalability to deeper backbones.
  - Quantify the impact of the “gradient independence” assumption by including a small-scale experiment with truncated second-order terms.

- Broader evaluation:
  - Add at least one additional benchmark (e.g., Omniglot or tieredImageNet) to strengthen generality claims.
  - Consider a cross-domain test (meta-train on one set of classes/domain, meta-test on a related but shifted set) to probe robustness.

- Clarity and presentation:
  - Clean up references (several entries have formatting artifacts) and ensure consistent citation of concurrent/related meta-learning work.
  - Expand on the exact LSTM architecture (hidden sizes, gating networks), optimization hyperparameters, and the choice of T for each setting.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the performance gain comes from learning the initialization versus learning the per-parameter update gates? Can you provide an ablation?
- Can the same meta-learner handle variable N and k at meta-test time without retraining, and how does performance degrade/improve as these vary?
- How sensitive is performance to T (number of updates) mismatch between training and testing?
- What is the meta-test-time computational overhead compared to standard fine-tuning and Matching Networks?
- Did you try incorporating second-order information (even partially) to relax the gradient independence assumption? Any observed benefits or instabilities?
- When using identical backbones and regularization for all methods, do the conclusions versus Matching Networks hold?
- Does the learned optimizer generalize to different base architectures (e.g., changing filter widths or switching to ResNet-12), or does it need retraining?

Overall, this paper proposes a clear and original direction—explicitly learning an optimizer and initialization for rapid adaptation in few-shot settings—and supports it with promising results and thoughtful design choices. With stronger ablations and broader evaluation, it could become a foundational reference for optimization-as-meta-learning in few-shot learning.
