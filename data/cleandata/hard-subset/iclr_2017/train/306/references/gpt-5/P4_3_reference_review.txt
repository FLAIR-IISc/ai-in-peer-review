1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper addresses few-shot learning by framing it as meta-learning: a meta-learner is trained to quickly adapt a learner classifier to new tasks with very limited labeled data.
- The core contribution is an LSTM-based meta-optimizer whose cell state encodes the learner’s parameters; the input and forget gates implement data-dependent parameter updates using gradients, current loss, past gate values, and current parameters. The approach also learns an initialization c0 that serves as a task-common prior for the learner.
- To scale to deep networks, parameters are shared across coordinates in the meta-learner, and gradient/loss preprocessing is used. Training is episodic: the meta-learner is optimized to minimize the learner’s test loss after a fixed number of inner updates, matching the few-shot evaluation protocol.
- Practical details include a simplifying “gradient independence” assumption to avoid second derivatives, careful initialization of gate biases, and a batch normalization strategy that avoids leakage across episodes yet keeps training and testing conditions aligned.
- Empirically, on 5-way Mini-ImageNet with 1-shot and 5-shot, the method substantially outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with, or better than, Matching Networks (notably stronger in 5-shot). Visualizations of learned gate dynamics suggest consistent weight decay and task-dependent adaptation. Code is provided.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Technical soundness and clarity: the formulation is precise, motivated by the resemblance between gradient updates and LSTM state updates, and the training protocol matches test conditions. Implementation details and algorithmic choices are well articulated.
- Evidenced improvements: competitive or superior results on a standard few-shot benchmark relative to strong baselines, with a clearly described and reproducible setup.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: learning to rapidly adapt from few examples by learning both a good initialization and an update rule. This is a well-defined, important problem with direct practical value.
- Placement and motivation: The approach builds on meta-learning and learned optimizers (notably Andrychowicz et al.) but explicitly targets the few-shot episodic setting, optimizing for generalization after a fixed number of updates. The linkage of LSTM gating to gradient descent is well motivated, and the learned initialization c0 provides an explicit task-common prior.
- Technical correctness: The LSTM update mapping to parameter updates is consistent. Parameter sharing across coordinates is standard for scalability. The “gradient independence” approximation avoids second-order terms; this is a known heuristic that has been shown to work and is acknowledged. The batch normalization protocol is carefully reasoned to avoid information leakage while maintaining matched train/test conditions.
- Empirical support: The evaluation on Mini-ImageNet follows established 5-way 1-shot/5-shot protocols with episodic training/testing and includes competitive Matching Networks baselines. The method beats natural baselines and is strong versus Matching Networks, particularly in 5-shot, which supports claims about fast adaptation and the value of the learned initialization and update rule.
- Limitations: Evaluation is confined to Mini-ImageNet; there are no ablations to isolate the contribution of initialization vs. learned update, or of the forget gate vs. a fixed ft=1. Sensitivity to the gradient independence assumption is not empirically analyzed. Runtime/memory overhead for per-parameter LSTM states is not reported. These are opportunities to strengthen, but do not undermine the core claims.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide complete quantitative results with confidence intervals in the main text and detail the number of test episodes. Explicitly report parameter counts and FLOPs to contextualize compute overhead.
- Add ablations:
  - Fix ft=1 (no learned forget) to quantify the benefit of learned shrinkage.
  - Remove c0 learning (random or generic pretrained init) to isolate the impact of learned initialization versus the learned optimizer.
  - Replace learned it with a fixed or schedule-based learning rate to show the gain from adaptive gates.
- Analyze sensitivity to the “gradient independence” approximation (e.g., compare on smaller models with/without second-order terms).
- Report robustness across shots/classes beyond 5-way (e.g., 20-way) and variable numbers of inner updates, including whether the meta-learner generalizes when T differs at test time.
- Include additional datasets or cross-domain tests (e.g., Omniglot, tieredImageNet) to support generality.
- Discuss memory/time complexity for coordinating per-parameter states, and any techniques (e.g., grouping, block-wise updates) to scale to larger models.
- Consider direct comparisons to additional contemporary few-shot methods if feasible (e.g., metric-based variants), and clarify whether Matching Networks were fine-tuned at test time.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide the exact numerical accuracies with 95% CIs, number of evaluation episodes, and statistical significance versus Matching Networks for both 1-shot and 5-shot?
- How much of the performance gain comes from the learned initialization c0 versus the learned update rule? Can you report ablations for each?
- What is the effect of setting ft=1 (no forgetting) or of constraining ft+it=1 (GRU-like)? Does this hurt/help?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of inner updates T, and does the meta-learner generalize when T at test time differs from training?
- What is the compute/memory overhead (per episode) relative to standard fine-tuning, given per-parameter LSTM states?
- How robust is the method to larger N-way tasks and to different CNN backbones? Any results on Omniglot or tieredImageNet?
- Did you empirically assess the impact of the gradient independence assumption (e.g., via limited second-order training on smaller nets)?
- For batch normalization, can you confirm that running means/vars are computed only on Dtrain and are frozen for Dtest within an episode? Any performance change if group/layer normalization is used instead?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound within stated approximations, and demonstrates competitive performance on a recognized benchmark with a principled meta-learning formulation.
