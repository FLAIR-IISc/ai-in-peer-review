- Proposes meta-learning an optimizer and initialization for few-shot N-way classification by casting parameter updates as LSTM cell state updates.  
- Learner parameters are LSTM cell state; input/forget gates yield per-parameter learning rates and shrinkage.  
- Meta-learner is trained end-to-end to minimize test loss after a fixed small number of inner-loop updates, explicitly optimizing for fast generalization.  
- Uses coordinate-wise parameter sharing, gradient/loss preprocessing, and careful batch-norm handling to avoid episode leakage.  
- Empirical results on a custom miniImageNet split outperform sensible baselines and are competitive with or better than Matching Networks, especially in 5-shot.  
- Provides code and detailed training recipes (initializations, clipping, gate biasing).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: elegant, well-motivated unification of learned optimization and meta-learning targeted at few-shot generalization.  
- Key reason: strong empirical gains over baselines and competitive performance versus contemporary methods, with replicable protocol and code.  
- Problem/motivation: few-shot setting is well defined; meta-learning is appropriate to overcome limitations of standard gradient-based training with few updates.  
- Literature placement: novel focus on minimizing post-adaptation test loss and encapsulating parameters in LSTM state distinguishes it from prior learned-optimizer and metric-learning work.  
- Methodological soundness: LSTM gate parameterization and coordinate-wise sharing are reasonable; gradient-independence approximation is pragmatic despite weakening theoretical exactness.  
- Experimental protocol: mirrors test conditions (episodic training) and includes thoughtful batch-norm treatment; visualizations provide qualitative insights.  
- Caveat: Results use a custom miniImageNet split and reimplementation of Matching Nets, complicating cross-paper comparisons.  
- Caveat: Lack of ablations separating learned initialization vs. learned optimizer and effects of gradient-independence assumption.  
- Caveat: Scalability/generalization to deeper backbones (e.g., ResNet) and variable N/k is not demonstrated.  
- Suggested ablations: compare learned init only vs learned updates only vs both; fixed vs learned gates; with/without loss input to gates; test gradient-independence effects.  
- Fairness recommendation: align CNN capacity across methods, report parameter counts, and clarify filter discrepancies (32 vs 64).  
- Breadth recommendation: evaluate additional datasets (e.g., Omniglot), stronger backbones, and cross-episode generalization across different k and N.  
- Reporting recommendation: provide exact accuracies with 95% CIs, number of evaluation episodes, compute cost, and meta-training stability across seeds.  
- Analysis recommendation: quantify gate activation distributions and correlate with adaptation success; discuss failure modes like 1-shot overfitting and mitigation by learned decay.  
- Writing/references: fix typos, broken references, and ensure consistent notation.  
- Questions for authors: ablate contribution of learned initialization vs learned update rule.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to inner-loop steps T and generalization to different T at test time.  
- Questions for authors: can a single meta-learner handle variable N and k, and how does mixed-episode training perform?  
- Questions for authors: what is the effect of removing loss inputs to gates and do gates saturate during adaptation?  
- Questions for authors: how does the approach scale to larger architectures (compute/memory implications of backprop through T steps)?  
- Questions for authors: clarify model capacity parity across baselines/meta-learner and provide parameter counts and exact CI/episode counts.
