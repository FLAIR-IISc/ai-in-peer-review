### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a meta-learning approach for few-shot classification. The core contribution is an LSTM-based "meta-learner" designed to function as an optimizer for a "learner" neural network classifier. Drawing an analogy between the LSTM cell state update and the gradient descent update rule, the authors parameterize the update steps (e.g., learning rate, momentum-like terms) as gates of an LSTM. This meta-learner is trained end-to-end over a distribution of few-shot tasks (episodes) to minimize the learner's final loss on the test set of each task. The proposed method simultaneously learns a task-agnostic parameter initialization (the initial cell state `c0`) and an optimization algorithm that can rapidly adapt this initialization to a new task using only a few examples. The authors evaluate their model on a self-constructed version of the Mini-ImageNet dataset, claiming performance competitive with state-of-the-art methods like Matching Networks.

**Decision: Reject**

I recommend rejecting this paper for two primary reasons:

1.  There appear to be critical methodological flaws, most notably in the handling of Batch Normalization, which contradicts the central meta-learning principle of matching training and testing conditions.
2.  The manuscript is exceptionally unpolished, suffering from numerous omissions of critical information (all figures and tables are missing), severe formatting issues, violations of anonymity, and a general lack of clarity that precludes a rigorous evaluation of its claims.

**Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on a substantial number of issues that, in aggregate, undermine the paper's scientific contribution.

**1. Critical Methodological Concerns:**
The treatment of Batch Normalization (Section 3.4) is highly problematic. The authors state that meta-learning requires matching train and test conditions, yet their described procedure violates this. They claim: "during meta-training, we use batch statistics for both the training and testing set whereas during meta-testing, we use batch statistics for the training set... but then use the running averages during testing." Using batch statistics from the test set (`D_test`) during meta-training constitutes information leakage and creates a significant discrepancy with the meta-testing procedure, where running averages are used. This mismatch means the meta-learner is trained in a setting that relies on test batch statistics, a privilege it is not afforded during evaluation. This is a fundamental flaw that could artificially inflate training performance and calls into question the validity of the learned optimizer. Furthermore, the description of how running averages are computed to give "higher preference... to the later values" is too vague to be reproducible.

**2. Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility:**
The paper is plagued by ambiguity and missing details that make it impossible to fully understand or reproduce the work.
*   **Missing Content:** All figures (1, 2, 3) and the main results table (Table 1) are referenced but not included in the manuscript. Without the results table, the central empirical claims of the paper are entirely unsubstantiated.
*   **Architectural Ambiguity:** The learner architecture is described as a CNN with 32 filters per layer. However, Section 5.1 states that the baseline and Matching Networks use 64 filters. It is unclear if the authors' own method was evaluated with a smaller network, which would render the comparison unfair. The 2-layer LSTM meta-learner architecture is also not fully specified; the interaction between the "normal LSTM" first layer and the "modified LSTM" second layer is not explained.
*   **Technical Inaccuracies:** The preprocessing formula in Section 3.2 appears to be an incorrect transcription from the cited work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016). The term `epx` is likely a typo for `e^p * x`. Such an error in a core component of the methodology is concerning.
*   **Counter-intuitive Experimental Design:** The authors state they use 12 updates for the 1-shot task and 5 updates for the 5-shot task. It is highly counter-intuitive that the more data-scarce and difficult 1-shot setting would receive more than double the optimization steps. This choice is not justified.

**3. Poor Presentation and Formatting:**
The manuscript's quality is far below the standard expected for a top-tier conference.
*   **Anonymity Violation:** The paper is not anonymized. A footnote on the first page explicitly names the author, their university, email address, and affiliation with Twitter. Another footnote in the evaluation section provides a public GitHub link. This is a severe breach of the double-blind review process.
*   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** Minor but distracting errors are present, such as "neutral networks" instead of "neural networks" in the introduction.
*   **Reference Formatting:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. It is riddled with formatting artifacts (e.g., `et al\.,? \Q2016\E`), incomplete citations (e.g., "Bosc"), and inconsistencies, suggesting a complete lack of proofreading.

While the core idea is interesting, the execution and presentation of the research are insufficient for publication. The combination of methodological flaws, missing information, and extreme sloppiness makes it impossible to verify the paper's contributions.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The central idea of framing the optimizer as an LSTM is elegant. If the above issues are addressed, this could form the basis of a strong paper.
*   Ablation studies are needed. For instance, what is the impact of the learned initialization (`c0`) versus a random one? How much does the learned forget gate (`ft`) contribute versus fixing it to 1?
*   The gradient independence assumption (Section 3.3.1) is a major simplification. While computationally necessary, a brief discussion of its potential limitations or impact would be welcome.
*   The entire paper requires a thorough proofreading and editing pass to fix the numerous errors and improve clarity. All figures, tables, and a properly formatted bibliography must be included.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact procedure for Batch Normalization? Is it correct that during meta-training, the evaluation on `D_test` uses statistics computed from that same `D_test` batch? If so, how do you justify this mismatch with the meta-test procedure?
2.  What were the exact learner CNN architectures (number of filters, etc.) used for your proposed method, the baselines, and the Matching Networks implementation? Was the architecture consistent across all compared methods?
3.  What is the rationale for using more optimization steps for 1-shot learning (12 steps) than for 5-shot learning (5 steps)?
4.  Can you confirm the correct preprocessing formula used in your experiments and clarify the input structure for the LSTM gates (e.g., how vectors like `∇θ` and `θ` are concatenated and processed)?
