### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a meta-learning approach for few-shot classification. The core idea is to frame the optimization of a "learner" neural network as the task for a "meta-learner." The meta-learner is an LSTM that learns a custom optimization algorithm, taking the learner's gradients and loss as input at each step and outputting the updated parameters for the learner network. The LSTM's cell state update is analogous to a gradient descent step, where the input and forget gates learn to modulate the learning rate and parameter decay, respectively. The model is trained end-to-end to minimize the learner's final performance on a test set after a fixed number of updates on a small training set. Crucially, the meta-learner also learns a task-agnostic initial set of parameters for the learner, providing a strong starting point for fine-tuning. The authors demonstrate on a custom split of the Mini-ImageNet dataset that their method outperforms standard fine-tuning baselines and achieves performance competitive with Matching Networks, a well-established metric-learning approach.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  **Insufficient and Non-Standard Empirical Validation:** The central claims of the paper regarding performance are not supported by rigorous evaluation on established benchmarks. The experiments are conducted on a newly created split of Mini-ImageNet, and the primary comparison is against the authors' own implementation of the baseline method (Matching Networks). This makes it impossible to directly compare the results to the broader literature and verify the claim of achieving competitive performance.
2.  **Incremental Novelty:** The proposed method is a direct application of the framework from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) to the few-shot learning problem. While the application is clever, the core technical components—using an LSTM to learn an optimizer, the parameter sharing scheme, and the gradient preprocessing—are adopted from prior work. The main novelties, learning the initialization and adapting the objective for few-shot learning, are not sufficiently isolated and analyzed to justify the introduction of such a complex model over simpler, more established alternatives.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary weakness of this work lies in its experimental methodology. The authors state, "the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset and implemented our own versions of their model." While the difficulty in obtaining original splits is understandable, the solution is not to create a new, un-vetted benchmark and claim state-of-the-art competitiveness. Without validation on a standardized split (such as the one later established by Ravi & Larochelle) or other common few-shot datasets (e.g., CUB, Omniglot), the performance claims are unsubstantiated. Furthermore, comparing against a self-implemented version of a competing method without extensive validation of that implementation is not a sound scientific practice. The reported performance of Matching Networks could be suboptimal, which would artificially inflate the perceived performance of the proposed model.

The paper also relies on a significant simplifying assumption—the independence of the learner's gradients from the meta-learner's parameters—which is inherited from Andrychowicz et al. (2016). While the authors note that training is still effective, the impact of this assumption is not discussed. In the context of few-shot learning, where optimization trajectories are extremely short and sensitive, this assumption could have non-trivial effects on the learned optimizer. A more rigorous treatment would involve at least a discussion of its potential implications or an analysis on a smaller-scale problem where exact gradients are computable.

Finally, the method itself introduces considerable complexity compared to alternatives. Metric learning approaches like Matching Networks or Prototypical Networks, or even carefully tuned fine-tuning approaches, are conceptually simpler and more interpretable. The proposed LSTM meta-optimizer is a black box. The visualizations provided in Figure 3 are difficult to interpret beyond general observations, and the authors themselves admit that the input gate values are "harder to interpret." While complexity is not inherently a flaw, it must be justified by a significant and rigorously demonstrated performance gain, which is absent here. The fine-tuning baseline, a critical point of comparison, performs surprisingly poorly, even worse than a nearest-neighbor baseline. This result is counter-intuitive and may point to an insufficiently thorough hyperparameter search for this established method, further weakening the paper's empirical grounding.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Standardized Benchmarking:** The most critical step to strengthen this paper is to re-evaluate the model on standard, widely-used few-shot learning benchmarks. This would allow for a direct and fair comparison with the extensive body of existing work.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The contribution of the learned initialization (`c0`) versus the learned update rule is a key aspect of the model. An ablation study where the initialization is fixed (e.g., using a pre-trained network from the base classes) would help disentangle these two effects and provide a clearer picture of what the meta-learner is contributing.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** The fine-tuning baseline should be strengthened. It is a very powerful and established method. More extensive hyperparameter tuning, potentially including modern regularization techniques, should be explored to provide a more challenging and realistic baseline.
*   **Batch Normalization Clarification:** The section on Batch Normalization (3.4) is confusing. The strategy for meta-testing, where running statistics are seemingly computed on the small support set of each episode, is unconventional. This needs to be explained more clearly, with justification for why this approach is sound and does not deviate problematically from the meta-training conditions.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide results for your method on the standard Mini-ImageNet splits introduced by Ravi & Larochelle, or another well-known benchmark like the CUB-200-2011 dataset?
2.  The fine-tuning baseline's poor performance is a key result supporting your method. Could you provide details on the hyperparameter search space and methodology used? Is it possible that this baseline was not sufficiently optimized?
3.  The paper argues that the meta-learner learns a task-specific optimization strategy, evidenced by the variability in gate activations (Figure 3). Have you considered an ablation where the meta-learner is constrained to learn a single learning rate and momentum term for all parameters (i.e., a simpler optimizer)? This would help clarify whether the coordinate-wise adaptation is essential.
