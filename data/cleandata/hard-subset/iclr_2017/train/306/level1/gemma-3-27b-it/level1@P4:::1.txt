This paper proposes a meta-learning approach using an LSTM-based meta-learner to learn an optimization algorithm for training a learner classifier in the few-shot regime. The key idea is to model the parameter update process of the learner network with an LSTM, allowing the meta-learner to learn a beneficial initialization and an effective update strategy tailored for few-shot learning. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on the Mini-ImageNet dataset compared to deep metric-learning techniques.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents an interesting idea, but lacks sufficient clarity in the experimental setup and justification for certain design choices, hindering a full assessment of its novelty and effectiveness.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of clear comparison with strong baselines, particularly those specifically designed for meta-learning. While the authors compare to Matching Networks, the implementation details and hyperparameters used are not sufficiently detailed for proper evaluation. Furthermore, the baselines presented (nearest neighbor and fine-tuning) are somewhat weak and do not provide a convincing benchmark against which to assess the contribution of the proposed LSTM meta-learner. The performance gain over these baselines is expected and doesn’t necessarily highlight the novelty of the approach.

A second reason for rejection is the lack of detailed analysis and justification for several key design decisions. The choice of LSTM architecture, the specific preprocessing steps, and the initialization strategies are presented without sufficient motivation. For instance, the reason for choosing a 2-layer LSTM and the impact of different LSTM sizes are not discussed. Similarly, the rationale behind the preprocessing method and the initialization biases is not fully explained, making it difficult to assess their contribution to the overall performance. The authors mention that certain choices were made based on empirical observation, but a more systematic investigation would strengthen the paper.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Reproducibility:** Providing detailed implementation details, including the exact hyperparameters used for all experiments, is crucial for reproducibility. This includes the optimizer used for the learner network during meta-training and testing, learning rates, batch sizes, and the number of training iterations.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** A more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art meta-learning algorithms such as MAML (Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning) or Reptile would be beneficial. These algorithms represent strong baselines in the field and would provide a better understanding of the proposed method's strengths and weaknesses.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conducting ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different components of the proposed model (e.g., the initialization strategy, the LSTM architecture, the preprocessing steps) would help to identify the key factors driving the performance.
*   **Visualization:** While the visualization of gate values is interesting, it would be helpful to provide more insights into the learned optimization strategy. For example, visualizing the gradient norms or the parameter updates during training could provide a better understanding of how the meta-learner is adapting the optimization process.
*   **Dataset Details:** Providing more specifics about the data splits used in the Mini-ImageNet dataset would improve transparency. Specifically, detailing how the classes were divided into meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets is essential.
*   **Gradient Independence Assumption:** The authors mention making a simplifying assumption regarding gradient independence. While this is a common practice, a discussion on the potential impact of this assumption on the results would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison against MAML or Reptile, which are commonly used baselines for meta-learning?
2.  What is the sensitivity of the performance to the LSTM architecture (e.g., number of layers, hidden state size)? Did you explore different LSTM configurations?
3.  Can you elaborate on the reasoning behind the choice of the specific preprocessing method and initialization biases?
4.  How does the performance change when the gradient independence assumption is relaxed, and second-order gradients are considered (even if computationally expensive)?
5.  Can you provide the exact hyperparameters used for all experiments, including the learning rate, batch size, and number of training iterations for both the meta-learner and the learner network?
