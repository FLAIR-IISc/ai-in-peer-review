## Review of "LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning"

This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner to learn the optimization algorithm for training a learner neural network in the few-shot learning regime. The core idea is compelling: instead of directly learning a classifier from limited data, the meta-learner learns *how* to learn a classifier efficiently, including a good initialization and update strategy. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of gradient-based optimization with small datasets and the potential benefits of meta-learning. The authors clearly articulate the problem and position their work within the existing literature on both meta-learning and few-shot learning techniques like metric learning.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel and the results demonstrate competitive performance against established methods, particularly metric-learning techniques like Matching Networks. The paper presents a clear and well-structured methodology, and the visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior provides valuable insight into its learned optimization strategy.

The strength of this paper lies in its innovative application of LSTMs to learn an optimization algorithm. The authors successfully frame the few-shot learning problem as a meta-learning task and demonstrate that an LSTM can effectively capture both a beneficial initialization and a task-specific update rule. The experimental results on Mini-ImageNet are encouraging, showing competitive performance and highlighting the potential of this approach. The ablation study and analysis of the learned gate values further strengthen the claims.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification and elaboration. For example, the gradient independence assumption, while acknowledged, could be discussed in more detail regarding its potential impact on the learned optimization strategy. Additionally, the choice of hyperparameters for both the meta-learner and the learner network could be justified more thoroughly. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the gradient independence assumption? Were any experiments conducted to assess its impact?
*   How was the number of LSTM units in the meta-learner determined? Was an ablation study performed to evaluate the effect of different LSTM sizes?
*   The paper mentions that the meta-learner is trained to make a specific number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot). How sensitive is the performance to this fixed number of updates? Would a variable number of updates, potentially learned by the meta-learner, improve performance?
*   Could you provide more details on the computational resources required for training and evaluating the meta-learner? This would help assess the scalability of the approach.
