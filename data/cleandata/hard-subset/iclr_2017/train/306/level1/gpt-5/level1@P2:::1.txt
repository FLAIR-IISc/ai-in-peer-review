Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles few-shot N-way classification by learning an optimizer and an initialization that enable a base learner (CNN) to reach good test performance in a fixed, small number of updates. The core idea is to cast the learner’s parameter update as the cell-state update of an LSTM: the LSTM’s cell state stores the learner’s parameters; input and forget gates are learned functions of gradients, loss, previous gates, and parameters.
- The meta-learner shares parameters across coordinates, making it scalable to large learners, and uses gradient/loss preprocessing for stability. It directly trains on test loss after T updates so training matches testing conditions. It also learns the initial parameters c0 of the learner, providing a meta-learned prior/initialization well-suited for fast adaptation.
- Practical issues (e.g., BN statistics per episode; initialization of gate biases) are handled thoughtfully. Empirically, on a Mini-ImageNet 5-way benchmark, the method outperforms standard baselines (nearest neighbor and fine-tuning) and is competitive with Matching Networks (stronger on 5-shot; comparable on 1-shot). Visualizations suggest the learned optimizer adopts meaningful strategies (e.g., weight decay-like forget behavior).

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) Well-motivated, simple, and novel adaptation of learned optimizers to the few-shot regime that jointly learns an initialization and update rule tailored to a fixed small number of steps; (2) Competitive results on a challenging benchmark with code released, and careful considerations (objective matches test-time, BN per episode) that increase credibility.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Rapid generalization in k-shot N-way classification by optimizing for performance after very few gradient steps on each task. The paper addresses both the update rule (learned via LSTM) and the initial point (learned c0).
- Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature? Yes. It is a clear extension of Andrychowicz et al. (learned optimizers) to meta-learning/few-shot, with a crucial change: optimizing test loss after a fixed number of updates per episode. It contrasts with metric learning (Matching Nets) and memory-augmented approaches, and relates to hyperparameter optimization via backprop through updates. Positioning is appropriate and timely.
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes but with caveats. The method shows strong 5-shot gains and comparable 1-shot performance relative to a strong baseline. The paper carefully describes training/eval protocol and addresses BN leakage. Notably, the baselines use a higher-capacity CNN (64 filters) than the meta-learner’s learner (32 filters), which, if anything, advantages the baselines—this strengthens the empirical signal. However:
  - The Mini-ImageNet split is not the one used in prior work; the authors reimplement Matching Nets and omit an optional fine-tuning variant. This limits direct comparability.
  - There is limited ablation: it is unclear how much gain comes from learned initialization vs learned optimizer gates; the contribution of preprocessing and gate architectures; and the effect of the “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring second-order terms).
  - Reporting is missing details such as confidence intervals in the text and computational overhead (time/memory), which matter for practicality.
Despite these issues, the approach is sound, the optimization objective matches the evaluation, and the gains—especially in 5-shot—are meaningful.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
- Provide ablations:
  - Learned init only (fix gates to SGD/Adam) vs learned gates only (random init) vs full model.
  - Remove/alter forget gate; tie gates (GRU-like) vs untied; remove gradient/loss preprocessing.
  - Vary number of inner-loop updates at test time to show robustness outside the trained T.
- Strengthen baselines and comparability:
  - Report results on the canonical Mini-ImageNet split if possible; include Omniglot for broader comparison.
  - Include the FCE Matching Nets variant with its recommended fine-tuning setting; ensure equivalent data augmentation and early stopping.
  - Consider MANN or Prototypical Networks-style baselines (if available).
- Clarify and quantify:
  - Provide confidence intervals and the number of test episodes. Show episode-level variance.
  - Training/inference cost: time per episode, memory overhead from per-parameter LSTM states, and comparison to metric-learning baselines.
  - Sensitivity to learner architecture (deeper CNNs, different widths) and cross-task generalization (training with mixed N and k).
- Methodological details:
  - Discuss the impact of the gradient independence assumption; if feasible, show a small-scale experiment with exact second-order gradients.
  - Expand on BN handling and its impact via an ablation (using running stats only vs per-episode).
  - Clarify whether c0 is learned per-parameter of the learner (implies as many meta-parameters as learner weights) and how this scales.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the performance comes from the learned initialization vs the learned update rule? Can you report numbers for (a) learned c0 + standard optimizer, (b) random init + learned gates?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of adaptation steps at test time when deviating from the trained T? Does the learned optimizer generalize to T±k steps?
- What is the computational overhead (time and memory) per episode relative to Matching Nets? How many meta-parameters are in the optimizer and in c0?
- How robust are results to different CNN learners (e.g., 64 filters for the meta-learner to match baselines)? If matched capacity, do trends persist?
- Did you re-tune Matching Nets hyperparameters on your split and include the FCE and fine-tuning variants? If not, can you add them?
- Does the approach generalize across N (e.g., training on 5-way but testing on 20-way) and across k? Any evidence for transfer beyond the trained setting?
- Can you provide results on a standard Mini-ImageNet split to facilitate comparison with prior work?
