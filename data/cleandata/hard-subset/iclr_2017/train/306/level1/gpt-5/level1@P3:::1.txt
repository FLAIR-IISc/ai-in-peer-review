Summary and contributions
The paper addresses few-shot N-way classification by framing learning as a two-level meta-learning problem. The core contribution is an LSTM-based meta-learner that (a) learns an optimizer to update the parameters of a base learner (a CNN classifier) over a fixed, small number of steps, and (b) simultaneously learns a task-common initialization for the base learner. A key insight is to align the LSTM cell-state update with a parametric form of gradient descent: the LSTM cell state stores the learner parameters, the input gate modulates a per-parameter learning rate, and the forget gate acts as an adaptive decay/overwrite mechanism. The meta-objective directly optimizes test loss after T inner updates on the training set, matching test-time conditions. The paper introduces practicalities for scalability and stability: coordinate-wise parameter sharing in the meta-optimizer, gradient/loss preprocessing, careful LSTM gate bias initialization, and an episodic batch-normalization strategy to avoid leakage. On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the approach outperforms two strong transfer-learning baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks (and superior on 5-shot in the authors’ setup). Visualizations suggest the meta-optimizer learns nontrivial, task-dependent update behaviors.

Decision: Accept
Reason 1: The method is well-motivated, conceptually clear, and novel in explicitly learning both an optimizer and an initialization tailored to fixed-budget few-shot adaptation, with a meta-objective aligned to test-time performance.
Reason 2: Empirical results on Mini-ImageNet show competitive or superior performance to strong baselines and a leading metric-learning method, while the paper provides useful implementation insights (BN handling, preprocessing) likely of value to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a recognized limitation of standard optimization when data is scarce and the number of allowed updates is small. The two-fold failure mode (generic optimizers not tuned for fixed T; random initialization per task) is well articulated. Framing this within meta-learning is apt and consistent with prior insights on matching train/test episodes.

- Positioning and novelty: The work builds on LSTM-based learned optimizers (Andrychowicz et al., Hochreiter et al.) but pivots the objective to few-shot generalization with fixed update budgets and couples optimizer learning with learning an initialization. It also contrasts with metric learning (Koch, Vinyals) and “one-shot weight generators” (Bertinetto), explaining that here the classifier is iteratively adapted over multiple steps rather than produced feedforward. This is an appropriate and timely contribution.

- Methodological soundness: The mapping of gradient descent to LSTM cell updates is sound, and the choice of inputs to the gates (current loss, gradient, parameter value, previous gate value) is reasonable. Coordinate-wise parameter sharing makes the meta-optimizer tractable for convolutional nets. The gradient independence assumption (ignoring second derivatives through the inner gradients) is a standard pragmatic approximation in learned optimizers and empirically works here, though it weakens theoretical guarantees. The BN protocol for episodic learning is carefully considered to prevent leakage while aligning train/test conditions.

- Empirical evaluation: Mini-ImageNet is a sensible benchmark. The method beats meaningful baselines (nearest-neighbor on a pre-trained embedding, fine-tuning a pre-trained classifier) and is competitive with Matching Networks; it wins clearly in 5-shot. The ablation on training the meta-learner with the exact number of test-time inner steps is a useful observation. The gate-value visualizations, while not definitive, lend qualitative support that the optimizer is not learning a trivial fixed schedule.

- Limitations/risks: Results rely on the authors’ Mini-ImageNet split and their own reimplementation of Matching Networks; this makes absolute SOTA claims hard to interpret. Comparisons are limited to 5-way classification; broader N and cross-shot generalization are not explored. An ablation isolating the benefit of learned initialization vs learned update rule is missing. Scalability concerns remain: per-parameter state for large learners may be memory-intensive.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Fairness and capacity: Clarify the capacity of the base learner across methods. The meta-learner uses 4 conv layers with 32 filters, while baselines and Matching Nets use 64. Although this likely advantages baselines, equalizing architectures (or reporting both settings) would strengthen fairness.

- Comparisons and benchmarks: Consider adding Omniglot (standard 20-way/5-way) and/or cross-dataset tests, and report more N-way settings on Mini-ImageNet. If possible, compare against a reimplementation of Andrychowicz et al. trained with your few-shot meta-objective, to disentangle the benefit of your LSTM design choices and objective.

- Ablations: Provide ablations on (i) learning only initialization with fixed SGD/Adam vs learning both; (ii) fixing ft=1 vs learnable forget gate; (iii) preproc of gradients/losses; (iv) robustness to mismatch in the number of inner updates (train at T, test at T±k). These would clarify what matters most.

- Objective and derivatives: The gradient independence assumption could be discussed more deeply; report empirical evidence (e.g., small-scale experiments) comparing with exact higher-order gradients for several steps to quantify the approximation’s effect.

- BN protocol: The episodic BN strategy is important. Please provide an explicit, reproducible recipe (e.g., momentum, whether running stats are reset at each episode during meta-test, and whether test-time stats depend only on Dtrain). A small ablation demonstrating the sensitivity to this choice would be valuable.

- Efficiency: Include wall-clock and memory footprints per episode and during meta-training, and discuss scaling to larger backbones (e.g., 64+ filters) or residual blocks.

- Clarity: The sign convention for c̃t should be unambiguous (you state c̃t = ∇Lt but earlier note the negative gradient; clarify whether the negative sign is absorbed into the gate or preprocessing). Summarize hyperparameters of the meta-optimizer (hidden sizes, depth) in an appendix.

Questions for the authors
1) How much of the gain comes from the learned initialization vs the learned update rule? What is the performance if you meta-learn only c0 and then use vanilla SGD/Adam with a tuned schedule for T steps?
2) How robust is the method to a mismatch in the number of inner updates at meta-test time? Can a model trained for T=5 generalize well to T=10 without retraining?
3) Does the learned optimizer generalize across k and N (e.g., train on mixed 1-shot/5-shot, 5-way/10-way) or do you need separate meta-learners?
4) Can you provide results on the standard Omniglot 20-way protocols for comparability with prior meta-learning work?
5) What is the memory/time overhead of maintaining per-parameter LSTM states for larger architectures? Any tricks to scale beyond small CNNs?
6) Please clarify the BN pipeline precisely at meta-test: are running means/vars computed only on Dtrain and then frozen for Dtest, and are they reset between episodes?
7) How sensitive are results to the gradient/loss preprocessing hyperparameter p and to gate bias initializations?

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated paper with a neat formulation and promising results. With stronger experimental breadth and ablations, it would be a solid contribution.
