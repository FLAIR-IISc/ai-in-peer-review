1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles few-shot classification by meta-learning an optimizer that can rapidly adapt a base classifier from very few labeled examples. The key idea is to cast the learner’s parameters as the cell state of an LSTM, so that LSTM gates implement parameter updates analogous to gradient descent. The meta-learner takes as input per-parameter gradients and the current loss and outputs update gates (input/forget) that control both learning rate and weight decay adaptively across a fixed, small number of steps. The approach jointly learns (i) a task-common initialization for the classifier (the initial LSTM cell state) and (ii) an update rule tuned to few-shot conditions. To remain scalable, the meta-learner shares parameters across coordinates, uses gradient/loss preprocessing, and is trained episodically with an objective that matches test-time usage: minimize test loss after T inner-loop updates. The paper addresses batch normalization carefully to avoid leakage between episodes. On Mini-ImageNet (custom split), the method substantially outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with, and better in 5-shot than, Matching Networks. Visualizations of the learned gates offer insight into the learned optimization dynamics.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (i) A clear and well-motivated formulation that bridges optimization and meta-learning with a simple, elegant LSTM construction that learns both initialization and update rule specifically for few-shot adaptation; (ii) Empirical results on a challenging benchmark showing strong gains over natural baselines and competitive performance with a state-of-the-art metric-learning method.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper directly addresses the central challenge of gradient-based learning in the few-shot regime—converging in a handful of steps from a good initialization using a rule that is effective under tight update budgets. The mapping from gradient descent to an LSTM update is intuitive and provides a principled parameterization of the optimizer with gates controlling learning rate and decay.
- Placement in literature: The work is well placed relative to Matching Networks and memory-augmented meta-learners, and to learned optimizers (Andrychowicz et al., Hochreiter et al.). The novelty is in specializing a learned optimizer to the episodic few-shot setting, training against test loss after a fixed number of steps, and explicitly learning the initialization jointly with the update rule for rapid adaptation.
- Methodology: The episodic training objective matches test-time conditions, which is known to be crucial in few-shot learning. Parameter sharing across coordinates and gradient/loss preprocessing are sensible and make the approach scalable. The batch normalization discussion is careful and addresses a common pitfall (data leakage across episodes).
- Empirical support: On Mini-ImageNet 5-way, the meta-learner outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks; notably it surpasses Matching Networks in 5-shot. The observation that naive fine-tuning overfits in 1-shot supports the claim that learning initialization and update rule jointly is beneficial. Gate visualizations provide qualitative evidence that the optimizer learns nontrivial, dataset-dependent behaviors rather than a fixed schedule.
- Practicality: The design readily applies to standard CNNs and is trained with standard tools (ADAM, gradient clipping). Code availability adds to reproducibility.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to the decision)
- Fairness and clarity of comparisons: It appears the learner CNN used by the meta-learner has 32 filters per layer, while Matching Networks and the baselines use 64. Please either align capacities or report parameter counts and confirm no hidden disadvantages/advantages. Report exact accuracies with confidence intervals and the number of test episodes to quantify statistical significance.
- Ablations: Show the contribution of key components: learned initialization vs. random; effect of the forget gate (ft) and loss input; impact of gradient/loss preprocessing; and the batch norm strategy. A comparison to standard optimizers (SGD/Adam) with a tuned schedule over the same fixed number of steps would strengthen claims.
- Generalization across conditions: Evaluate how well a meta-learner trained for T updates or 5-way tasks generalizes to other T and N (e.g., 10-way, varying k). This addresses whether the method over-specializes to a fixed protocol.
- Second-order effects: The “gradient independence” assumption simplifies training but may bias learning. A small-scale experiment including second-order terms (or truncated backprop) would be informative.
- Efficiency: Provide runtime and memory overhead relative to standard fine-tuning to help practitioners assess trade-offs.
- Broader evaluation: When feasible, add Omniglot or other few-shot benchmarks and report on more standard Mini-ImageNet splits to facilitate comparison. Consider reporting results with the same backbone and training budget as Matching Networks with and without FCE and fine-tuning.
- Analysis: Expand the gate visualizations with distributions over layers/time and correlate gate behavior with loss/gradient norms to better interpret the learned dynamics.

Questions for the authors
- Architecture parity: Did the meta-learner’s CNN indeed use 32 filters per conv layer while baselines used 64? If so, why the difference, and how do results change when capacities are matched?
- Exact numbers: Please provide the full Table 1 with mean accuracies, 95% CIs, number of evaluation episodes, and random seeds used.
- Contribution of initialization: How much of the performance gain comes from the learned initialization c0 versus the learned update rule? Can you report results with a random c0 but learned gates, and vice versa?
- Robustness to T and N,k: How does performance change if, at meta-test time, you use more/fewer inner updates than trained for? Does a meta-learner trained on 5-way generalize to 10-way? From 1-shot to 5-shot without retraining?
- Second-order terms: Have you tried including the dependence of Lt and ∇Lt on meta-parameters (e.g., via higher-order gradients)? Any gains or training instabilities?
- Batch norm scheme: How sensitive are results to the proposed per-episode statistics handling? What happens if BN is removed or replaced (e.g., Group Norm) to avoid episodic statistic issues?
- Overfitting control: Do you use any regularization on the learner during inner updates (e.g., weight decay, dropout), or is regularization effectively handled by the meta-learner’s forget gate?
- Compute: What is the training/inference cost overhead of the meta-learner per episode relative to standard fine-tuning with SGD/Adam?

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated contribution with a clean idea, solid execution, and promising results for few-shot learning.
