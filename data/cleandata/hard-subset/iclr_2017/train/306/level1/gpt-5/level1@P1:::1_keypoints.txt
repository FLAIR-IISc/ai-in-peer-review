- Tackles few-shot classification by meta-learning an LSTM-based optimizer that treats learner parameters as LSTM cell state.  
- Meta-learner consumes per-parameter gradients and current loss and outputs input/forget gates controlling adaptive learning rate and weight decay across a few steps.  
- Jointly learns a task-common initialization (initial cell state) and an update rule tuned for rapid few-shot adaptation.  
- Scales via parameter sharing across coordinates, gradient/loss preprocessing, and episodic training that minimizes test loss after T inner-loop updates.  
- Carefully handles batch normalization to avoid data leakage between episodes.  
- Empirically on a custom Mini-ImageNet split, outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks, surpassing them in 5-shot.  
- Gate visualizations provide qualitative insight that the optimizer learns nontrivial, dataset-dependent dynamics.  
- Decision: Accept — clear, well-motivated formulation bridging optimization and meta-learning with an elegant LSTM construction.  
- Novelty: Specializes a learned optimizer to episodic few-shot setting and jointly learns initialization and update rule to operate under tight update budgets.  
- Methodology strength: Episodic objective matches test-time use; preprocessing and parameter sharing make approach practical and scalable.  
- Empirical support: Shows naive fine-tuning overfits in 1-shot, supporting benefit of learned initialization and updates.  
- Practicality: Readily applies to standard CNNs, trained with ADAM and gradient clipping; code availability aids reproducibility.  
- Request to align model capacities: clarify whether meta-learner used 32 filters vs baselines’ 64, report parameter counts and effect of matching capacities.  
- Ask for numerical rigor: report exact accuracies with 95% CIs, number of test episodes, and random seeds.  
- Ablation requests: quantify contribution of learned initialization vs random, effect of forget gate and loss input, preprocessing impact, and batch-norm strategy.  
- Compare to standard optimizers: include tuned SGD/Adam baselines over the same fixed number of inner steps.  
- Generalization check: evaluate robustness when changing T, N, k at meta-test time (e.g., 10-way, different shots).  
- Investigate second-order effects: test including higher-order gradients or truncated backprop to assess bias from gradient-independence assumption.  
- Efficiency request: provide runtime and memory overhead relative to standard fine-tuning.  
- Broader evaluation: add Omniglot and standard Mini-ImageNet splits and compare using identical backbones/training budgets as competitors.  
- Analysis enhancement: expand gate visualizations (distributions over layers/time) and correlate gate behavior with loss/gradient norms.  
- Specific author questions: confirm architecture parity (32 vs 64 filters), provide full Table 1 with CIs and seeds, report isolated effects of c0 vs learned gates, robustness to T/N/k changes, experiments with second-order terms, sensitivity to BN scheme or alternatives, inner-update regularization practices, and compute overhead per episode.  
- Overall verdict: timely, well-motivated contribution with a clean idea, solid execution, and promising few-shot performance.
