

This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm specifically designed for few-shot learning scenarios. The authors argue that standard gradient-based optimization struggles in few-shot settings due to the need for many iterative updates and poor random initialization. Their key contribution is a meta-learner that simultaneously learns both an optimal initialization for the classifier network and a task-specific parameter update rule that works well within a fixed number of updates. The approach is well-motivated by the literature on meta-learning and few-shot learning, and the authors provide a clear theoretical connection to LSTM cell state updates.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the problem is well-defined and significant within few-shot learning research, and (2) the empirical results demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines and matching networks, particularly for 5-shot learning.

The paper provides thorough experimental validation on Mini-ImageNet, with careful attention to the meta-learning setup, including proper handling of batch normalization during meta-training and meta-testing. The authors address a critical challenge in meta-learning—matching training and testing conditions—which is often overlooked in related work. The visualization of the meta-learner's learned strategies (input and forget gates) provides valuable insight into how the model adapts to different tasks.

To further improve the paper, consider adding a more detailed discussion of the limitations of your approach, particularly regarding how the meta-learner might generalize to different network architectures beyond the simple CNN used in experiments. Also, explicitly address how your method would scale to larger datasets or more complex tasks.

I would like to ask the authors: How would your meta-learner perform on a dataset with significantly more classes than Mini-ImageNet (e.g., 100+ classes), where the few-shot setting becomes more challenging? Additionally, can you provide more details about the computational complexity of your approach compared to standard meta-learning methods?
