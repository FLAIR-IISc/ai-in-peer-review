Paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an initialization and update rule to enable fast few-shot learning by treating optimization as a learnable function.  
Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
Core insight: learning the optimization algorithm (via LSTM) is theoretically motivated by similarity to gradient descent and practically effective.  
Empirical result: competitive performance on Mini-ImageNet, outperforming baselines and matching/ beating Matching Networks for 5-shot tasks.  
Visualization: learned gate values indicate task-specific optimization strategies rather than a fixed rule.  
Reproducibility note: authors handle practical issues (e.g., batch normalization) and present a clear training algorithm.  
Methodological shortcut: assumes gradient independence to avoid second derivatives, which is validated empirically.  
Revision request 1: clarify dataset construction and differences from Vinyals et al. (2016) to explain performance discrepancies.  
Revision request 2: provide detailed meta-learner hyperparameters (e.g., LSTM hidden size and layer count) for reproducibility.  
Revision request 3: include statistical significance tests for comparisons with Matching Networks, especially for 1-shot results.  
Revision request 4: expand discussion and analysis of task-specific adaptation observed in Figure 3.  
Question 1: Could the meta-learner be trained to adaptively choose the number of updates per task, and would that help?  
Question 2: Why does the meta-learner avoid overfitting that hurts the fine-tuning baseline despite also using few updates?  
Question 3: How does this approach differ from Maclaurin et al. (2015) that tunes gradient descent hyperparameters via backpropagation?  
Question 4: Can the authors provide more detailed insight into how 1-shot and 5-shot learned optimization strategies differ and what task characteristics drive those differences?
