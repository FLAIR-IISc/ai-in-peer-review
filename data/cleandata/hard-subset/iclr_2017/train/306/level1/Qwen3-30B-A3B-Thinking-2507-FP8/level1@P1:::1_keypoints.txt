- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner for few-shot classification that learns both an optimizer and a good weight initialization.
- Key motivation: standard gradient methods aren't designed for a fixed small number of updates and random initialization harms convergence.
- Method trains an LSTM to produce parameter updates (analogous to LSTM cell updates) tailored to the few-shot regime.
- Empirical claim: outperforms natural baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — novel, well-motivated, and empirically supported.
- Strength: Strong theoretical connection between LSTM updates and gradient descent with clear, justified architecture and parameter sharing.
- Strength: Experimental design matches meta-training/testing, includes ablation studies and visualizations of learned gating behavior.
- Strength: Well-positioned in literature with a clear relation to prior meta-learning work, notably Andrychowicz et al. (2016).
- Improvement: Need detailed analysis of computational cost and training time vs baselines and Matching Networks.
- Improvement: Provide hyperparameter search details and ablation on the chosen number of update steps (e.g., 12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot).
- Improvement: Describe differences between their Mini-ImageNet variant and Vinyals et al. (2016) to aid result comparability.
- Improvement: More explicitly clarify novelty relative to Andrychowicz et al. (2016) and how the approach is tailored to few-shot learning.
- Question: How does meta-learner training time compare to baselines and Matching Networks in practice?
- Question: Can you provide statistical significance or confidence intervals for the results reported (Table 1)?
- Question: Please elaborate specific methodological differences from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) regarding few-shot adaptation.
- Question: Why did fine-tuning perform worse than nearest-neighbor, and how does the meta-learner avoid this overfitting issue?
