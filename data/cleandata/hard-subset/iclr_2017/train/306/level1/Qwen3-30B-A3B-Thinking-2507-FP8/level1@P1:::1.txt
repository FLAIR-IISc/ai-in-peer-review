

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learner approach to address the few-shot learning problem, where the goal is to train a classifier that can generalize well after seeing only a few examples per class. The authors make the key observation that standard gradient-based optimization algorithms aren't designed for the constraint of a limited number of updates, and that random initialization of the classifier hurts convergence. Their solution involves training an LSTM meta-learner to learn both an optimal parameter update rule for the classifier (resembling LSTM cell state updates) and a good initialization for the classifier's weights. The model is trained to optimize the learner network specifically for the few-shot regime, where a fixed number of updates will be made. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms natural baselines and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques like Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset.

## 2. Decision

Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a well-motivated, novel approach to few-shot learning that is well-placed within the literature and provides compelling empirical results supporting its claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a significant challenge in machine learning with clear motivation. The few-shot learning problem is well-articulated, and the authors effectively identify the two main limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in this regime: (1) optimization algorithms aren't designed for a fixed number of updates, and (2) random initialization hurts convergence. Their meta-learning approach directly addresses both limitations by learning both the optimization algorithm and the initialization.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with the insightful connection between LSTM cell state updates and gradient descent updates. The model architecture is clearly explained and well-justified, particularly the use of parameter sharing to prevent an explosion of meta-learner parameters. The experimental design is appropriate, with careful attention to matching meta-training and meta-testing conditions, and the authors include useful visualizations of the learned optimization strategy.

The empirical results are compelling: the meta-learner significantly outperforms baselines and achieves results competitive with the state-of-the-art Matching Networks approach. The authors provide appropriate ablation studies (comparing to both nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines) and interesting visualizations of the learned gate values that demonstrate the meta-learner adapts its strategy to different tasks.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with a thorough related work section that properly contextualizes the contribution relative to prior meta-learning and few-shot learning work. The connection to Andrychowicz et al. (2016) is particularly well-handled, with clear differentiation of how their approach is specifically tailored to the few-shot learning problem.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance it further:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the meta-learner compared to baselines and Matching Networks. Given that the meta-learner needs to be trained on many episodes, the training time could be a significant factor for practical applications.

2. The authors could provide more specific details about the hyperparameter search for the meta-learner and how the number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot) was determined. A brief ablation study on the number of updates would be valuable.

3. The paper mentions that "the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset." It would be helpful to provide more details about the differences between the dataset versions to help readers assess the comparability of results.

4. The paper could better clarify the novelty relative to Andrychowicz et al. (2016) in the related work section. While they mention that Andrychowicz et al. trained a neural network to learn an optimization algorithm for large-scale classification, they don't explicitly state how their approach is specifically designed for the few-shot setting.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does the training time of your meta-learner compare to the training time of the baselines and Matching Networks? Given that the meta-learner needs to be trained on many episodes, this could be a significant factor for practical applications.

2. The paper states that the meta-learner is "competitive with Matching Networks" but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the statistical significance of the results. Could you provide more details about the confidence intervals for the results shown in Table 1?

3. In the related work section, you mention that your approach differs from Andrychowicz et al. (2016). Could you elaborate on the specific differences between the two approaches, especially regarding how your method is tailored to the few-shot learning problem?

4. The paper notes that the fine-tuned baseline performed worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and how the meta-learner avoids this overfitting issue?
