

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner model that learns to optimize a neural network classifier in the few-shot learning regime. The authors argue that standard gradient-based optimization fails in few-shot settings due to two main reasons: optimization algorithms aren't designed for a fixed number of updates, and random network initialization hinders quick convergence. Their key contribution is framing the optimization process as a meta-learning problem, where an LSTM meta-learner learns both an optimal initialization for the classifier network and a task-specific update rule. The model is trained to produce updates that lead to good generalization after a fixed number of steps, which they demonstrate is competitive with metric-learning approaches on Mini-ImageNet.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper fails to sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work and lacks rigorous empirical validation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution appears to be applying an LSTM-based optimizer to few-shot learning, but it doesn't clearly distinguish itself from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which also uses an LSTM to learn an optimizer. The authors acknowledge this work but don't adequately explain how their approach differs or why it's better suited for few-shot learning. The paper states: "This work also builds upon Hochreiter et al. (2001) and Bosc, both of which used LSTMs to train multi-layer perceptrons to learn on binary classification and timeseries prediction tasks. Another related method is the work of Bertinetto et al. (2016), who train a meta-learner to map a training example to the weights of a neural network that is then used to classify future examples from this class; however, unlike our method the classifier network is directly produced rather than being fine-tuned after multiple training steps." However, they don't provide a clear comparison to Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which is critical for establishing novelty.

Furthermore, the empirical evaluation is limited and lacks proper ablation studies. The paper only compares against two baselines and Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet, without including other meta-learning approaches that were emerging in 2016-2017 (e.g., MAML). The authors claim their method is "competitive with deep metric-learning techniques," but don't provide enough evidence to support this claim. The results table shows they perform better than baselines but only marginally better than Matching Networks on 1-shot (with overlapping confidence intervals), and slightly better on 5-shot. Without ablation studies to show the contribution of the different components (initialization learning vs. update rule learning), it's unclear what specific aspects of their approach drive the performance gains.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- **Clarify novelty**: The paper must clearly articulate how it differs from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) and why their approach is better for few-shot learning. A table comparing key aspects of related methods would be helpful.
- **Expand empirical validation**: Include comparisons with other meta-learning methods beyond Matching Networks (e.g., MAML, which was published in 2017), and provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of different components of the model.
- **Strengthen theoretical justification**: The gradient independence assumption (section 3.3.1) needs more justification. The authors should discuss the potential impact of this assumption on the model's performance and whether it's valid for few-shot learning.
- **Clarify dataset details**: The paper mentions creating their own Mini-ImageNet version but doesn't provide sufficient detail about the class selection process or how it differs from the original version.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How does your approach specifically differ from Andrychowicz et al. (2016), which also uses an LSTM to learn an optimizer? What makes your method better suited for few-shot learning?
2. Why did you choose a 2-layer LSTM architecture (with the second layer being the modified LSTM meta-learner) instead of a single-layer LSTM? What benefits does this architecture provide?
3. The paper states you train different models for 1-shot and 5-shot tasks (with 12 and 5 updates, respectively). How sensitive is performance to the number of updates? How did you determine these specific numbers?
4. How would your method compare to MAML (Finn et al., 2017), which was published in the same year and has become a standard for few-shot learning? Would your approach be compatible with or improve upon MAML?

The paper addresses an important problem in few-shot learning, but the lack of clear differentiation from prior work and insufficient empirical validation prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions, the paper could become a stronger contribution to meta-learning.
