- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that jointly learns a parameter update rule and task-common initialization for few-shot learning.

- Targets two challenges: optimizing with a fixed small number of updates and finding good initialization for rapid adaptation.

- Models parameter updates as LSTM cell-state updates and trains the meta-learner to optimize learner performance after few updates.

- Demonstrates competitive results on Mini-ImageNet, outperforming baselines and matching or exceeding Matching Networks in some settings (better in 5-shot, competitive in 1-shot).

- Decision: accept — novel contribution with rigorous evaluation.

- Well-motivated problem statement highlighting weaknesses of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot regimes (fixed T and poor random initialization).

- Provides a clear mathematical connection between LSTM updates and gradient descent, and justifies architectural/design choices (parameter sharing, preprocessing, batch-norm handling).

- Experiments are rigorous with proper meta-train/val/test splits, relevant baselines (nearest-neighbor, fine-tuning), and informative visualizations of learned gates.

- Suggests expanding discussion on how the learned update rule maps to traditional optimizer parameters (learning rate, momentum).

- Recommends analysis of computational complexity (parameter count and training time) compared to other methods.

- Notes lack of exploration of training the meta-learner across a range of update counts to assess flexibility/generalization.

- Asks for quantification of how different dataset splits (vs. Vinyals et al.) affect absolute performance comparisons.

- Questions for authors: impact of training on a range of update counts (generalization to different T); what dataset characteristics correlate with input-gate variations; why the meta-learner avoids overfitting that hurts fine-tuning in 1-shot cases.
