

# Review of "Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent" for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper presents an innovative meta-learning approach to few-shot learning that directly addresses the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in the few-shot regime. The authors propose an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns both an optimal initialization for a classifier network and a specialized update rule for the few-shot setting. By framing the optimization algorithm itself as a learnable parameterized function (inspired by the similarity between LSTM cell state updates and gradient descent updates), the meta-learner produces a tailored optimization process that converges quickly with limited examples. The authors demonstrate competitive performance against metric learning approaches like Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark, with particularly strong results for 5-shot learning.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the meta-learning literature that is well-motivated, novel, and well-supported by empirical evidence. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the elegant theoretical connection between LSTM updates and gradient descent that motivates the approach, and (2) the compelling empirical results showing the meta-learner outperforms baselines and achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art metric learning methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core insight - that the optimization algorithm itself can be learned as part of a meta-learning framework - is both theoretically sound and practically valuable. The authors effectively position their work within the meta-learning literature, citing relevant prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Vinyals et al., 2016; Santoro et al., 2016) and clearly articulating how their approach differs. Their LSTM-based meta-learner design cleverly captures the relationship between the gradient descent update rule and LSTM cell state updates, making the connection both mathematically precise and operationally feasible.

The experimental results are well-conducted and appropriately rigorous. The authors use standard few-shot learning benchmarks (Mini-ImageNet) and provide meaningful comparisons against two baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning) as well as the state-of-the-art Matching Networks. The results demonstrate clear advantages over baselines (especially for 1-shot learning) and competitive performance with Matching Networks (particularly for 5-shot learning, where their method significantly outperforms). The visualization of the learned gate values (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into the meta-learner's behavior, showing it learns task-specific optimization strategies rather than a fixed rule.

The paper also thoughtfully addresses practical concerns like batch normalization during meta-testing, which is an important detail for reproducibility in meta-learning. The training procedure (Algorithm 1) is clearly described, and the authors make the reasonable simplifying assumption of gradient independence to avoid computationally expensive second derivatives, which they validate by showing the approach remains effective.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the dataset construction differences** with Vinyals et al. (2016) more explicitly. The authors note their results don't perfectly match those in the original Matching Networks paper, but don't explain whether this is due to dataset differences (they created their own Mini-ImageNet subset) or implementation variations. A brief comparison of dataset splits and model architectures would help readers understand the discrepancy.

2. **Provide more details about meta-learner hyperparameters**. While the paper mentions a 2-layer LSTM, it would be helpful to specify the hidden size and number of layers used, as these significantly impact performance and reproducibility.

3. **Include statistical significance tests** for the comparison with Matching Networks, particularly for the 1-shot case where confidence intervals overlap. This would strengthen the claim that their method is competitive with the state-of-the-art.

4. **Expand the discussion on the task-specific adaptation** shown in Figure 3. The paper notes variability between datasets, but doesn't elaborate on what specific characteristics of different tasks might lead to different optimization strategies. A brief analysis of this would deepen the theoretical understanding.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "better performance for each task was attained if the meta-learner is explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing." How does this compare to training a meta-learner that could adaptively determine the optimal number of updates for each task? Would such an approach be feasible and beneficial?

2. The authors note that the fine-tuned baseline performs worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline due to overfitting. Given this, why does the meta-learner approach not suffer from the same overfitting issues despite also using a small number of updates?

3. In the related work section, the authors cite Maclaurin et al. (2015) which also tunes gradient descent hyperparameters through backpropagation. How does the authors' approach differ from or relate to this work, particularly regarding the scope of the optimization problem (few-shot vs. hyperparameter tuning)?

4. The paper mentions that the meta-learner learns different optimization strategies for 1-shot and 5-shot learning (Figure 3). Could the authors provide more insight into what these differences might be, and whether they correlate with specific aspects of the learning task (e.g., difficulty, class distribution)?
