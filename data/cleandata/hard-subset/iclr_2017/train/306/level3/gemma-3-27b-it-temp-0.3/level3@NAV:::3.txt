## Review of "LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning"

This paper presents an interesting approach to few-shot learning by framing it as a sequential meta-learning problem. The core idea – using an LSTM meta-learner to directly learn an optimization algorithm for a base learner – is novel and well-motivated, particularly given the limitations of traditional gradient-based methods in low-data regimes. The connection drawn between LSTM memory cells and gradient descent updates is insightful and represents a potentially significant contribution. The experimental results are competitive with, and in some cases outperform, Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The practical insights provided regarding LSTM meta-learner training (initialization, weight sharing, normalization) are valuable for future research.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is innovative, the results are promising, and the work addresses a crucial challenge in machine learning. The method offers a distinct and compelling alternative to existing approaches like metric learning, showcasing a different path towards state-of-the-art performance in few-shot learning.

My decision is primarily based on the novelty of the approach and the convincing empirical results. While the paper initially lacked clarity regarding the meta-learning task, the authors’ subsequent clarification significantly improved my understanding and appreciation of the work. The competitive performance against established baselines further strengthens the paper’s contribution.

However, the presentation could be significantly improved. The writing is often opaque, and the task description would benefit greatly from a concrete example and a clarifying diagram. Specifically, it’s unclear how the N-class few-shot setup is implemented during meta-training – which classes are sampled, and what is the size of the softmax output layer? The plots in Figure 2 are uninformative and lack insightful discussion. Further, a more detailed explanation of the gradient independence assumption and its potential impact on the results would be beneficial. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you provide a concrete example illustrating the entire process, from dataset sampling to learner training and evaluation within a single meta-learning episode? A diagram would be extremely helpful.
*   Regarding the N-class few-shot setup, how are classes selected for each meta-training/validation/testing split? Is the softmax output layer size fixed, or does it dynamically adjust based on the N classes in each episode?
*   Can you elaborate on the potential consequences of the gradient independence assumption? Have you explored methods to mitigate its impact, or performed sensitivity analysis to assess its influence on the results?
*   In Figure 3, could you provide more context for the observed patterns in the gate values? What do these patterns suggest about the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner?
