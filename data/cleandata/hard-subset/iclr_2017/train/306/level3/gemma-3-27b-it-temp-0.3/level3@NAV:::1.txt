## Review of "LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning"

This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn an optimizer specifically tailored for few-shot learning scenarios. The core idea, drawing a parallel between Robbins–Monro updates and LSTM updates, is compelling and well-motivated, aiming to address the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in data-scarce regimes. The authors effectively leverage successful techniques from prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016) like parameter sharing and normalization, while also introducing a well-justified batch-norm implementation. The experimental results on Mini-ImageNet demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing methods, suggesting a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend **accepting** this paper for ICLR. The approach is innovative, well-presented, and the empirical results are convincing. The paper tackles a significant problem – few-shot learning – with a creative and theoretically sound solution. The clear writing and organization further enhance the paper’s quality.

The strength of this work lies in its elegant formulation and successful implementation. The authors convincingly demonstrate that an LSTM can effectively learn to optimize another neural network, achieving competitive results. The visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into the learned optimization strategy. The inclusion of parameter sharing and the careful consideration of batch normalization are also commendable design choices. 

However, to further strengthen the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Ablation Studies:** The authors mention the potential redundancy of feeding loss, gradient, and parameters to the meta-learner. Conducting ablation studies to assess the impact of each input component would be highly valuable. Does performance significantly degrade when only the gradient is provided, for example?
*   **Generalizability of the Approach:** Could this meta-learning approach be extended to learn other architectural components beyond the optimizer? For instance, could the number of neurons, activation functions, or even layer types be learned in a similar fashion? The authors’ insights on the limitations and potential of this broader application would be appreciated.
*   **Expanded Related Work:** The related work section feels somewhat limited. While the cited papers are relevant, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of earlier meta-learning efforts, including seminal work like Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989), Bengio et al. (1994) on genetic programming, and Schmidhuber’s pioneering work on meta-learning.

Finally, could the authors elaborate on the sensitivity of the meta-learner’s performance to the choice of LSTM architecture (number of layers, hidden unit size)? Were different LSTM configurations explored, and if so, what were the results? Also, what is the computational cost of meta-training compared to training a standard classifier?
