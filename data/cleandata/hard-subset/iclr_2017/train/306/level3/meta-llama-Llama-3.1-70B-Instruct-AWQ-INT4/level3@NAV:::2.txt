**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel meta-learning approach that interprets SGD updates as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. The authors present an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizer that captures both short-term knowledge within a task and long-term knowledge common among all tasks. The meta-learner is trained to converge a learner classifier to a good solution quickly on each task, and it also learns a task-common initialization for the learner classifier. The authors demonstrate that their approach is competitive with deep metric-learning techniques for few-shot learning.

**Decision**

Based on the evaluation of the paper, I decide to **Accept** the paper. The main reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper proposes an original and important idea for transfer learning research, which is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature.
2. The approach is feasible and has shown competitive results with state-of-the-art methods in few-shot learning.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation of the proposed approach, including a detailed description of the model, training, and experimental results. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the related work and position their approach within the broader context of meta-learning and few-shot learning.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing that it outperforms natural baselines and is competitive with state-of-the-art methods in few-shot learning. The authors also provide a visualization of the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, which helps to understand the behavior of the model.

**Additional Feedback**

While the paper is well-written and clear, there are some minor issues that could be improved:

* The analogy between the proposed approach and LSTMs is closer to GRUs than LSTMs, as mentioned in the paper. It would be helpful to clarify this point in the introduction.
* The description of data separation in meta sets is hard to follow and would benefit from visualization.
* The experimental evaluation is only partially satisfying, and more analysis of the effects of parameters (especially it and ft) would be helpful.
* Figure 2 has limited value and could be removed or replaced with a more informative figure.
* There is a minor typo in Section 3.2 ("it" → "its").

**Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence for my assessment, I would like to ask the authors the following questions:

* Can you provide more details on the initialization of the meta-learner LSTM and how it affects the performance of the model?
* How do you plan to extend the proposed approach to more challenging scenarios, such as few-shot learning with many classes or few-shot learning with limited data?
* Can you provide more analysis of the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, including the behavior of the input and forget gates?
* How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in the meta-learner, especially when the number of updates is small?
