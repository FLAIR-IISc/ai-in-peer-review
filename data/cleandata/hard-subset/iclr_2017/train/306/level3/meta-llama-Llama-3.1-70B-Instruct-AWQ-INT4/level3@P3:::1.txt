This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn an optimizer for another neural network. The authors draw a parallel between Robbins-Monro updates and LSTM updates to satisfy quick adaptation vs. slower transferable learning. The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear.

The core idea is to learn a meta-learner LSTM that captures both short-term knowledge within a task and long-term knowledge common among all tasks. The formulation of the meta-learner model allows it to learn a task-common initialization for the learner classifier, which captures fundamental knowledge shared among all tasks. The authors reuse useful tricks from prior work, such as parameter sharing and normalization, and introduce well-motivated novel design choices, including a specific batch-norm implementation.

The experiments are convincing, and the results demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms natural baselines and is competitive with the state-of-the-art in metric learning for few-shot learning.

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons for this choice. First, the approach is well-motivated, including being well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the related work and position their contribution within the broader context of meta-learning and few-shot learning. Second, the paper supports its claims with convincing experiments and results.

However, I have some concerns and suggestions for improvement. One concern is the potential redundancy of feeding loss, gradient, and parameters to the meta-learner. Ablation studies are needed to test simpler inputs and evaluate their impact on the performance of the meta-learner. Another concern is whether other architectural components, such as neurons or unit types, could be learned similarly. The authors' opinion or insight on this matter would be valuable.

Additionally, the related work section could be improved by citing earlier meta-learning efforts, such as Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989) and Bengio et al. (1994) on genetic programming. Schmidhuber's work on meta-learning could also be mentioned.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Provide more details on the implementation of the meta-learner LSTM, including the architecture and hyperparameter settings.
* Include more visualizations of the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, such as the gate values and the learning rate.
* Discuss the potential applications of the proposed approach beyond few-shot learning, such as in other areas of machine learning or robotics.
* Consider including more baselines or comparison methods to evaluate the performance of the proposed approach.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of meta-learning and few-shot learning. With some minor revisions and additional experiments, it has the potential to be a highly influential work in the field.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the meta-learner LSTM, including the architecture and hyperparameter settings?
* How do you think the proposed approach could be applied to other areas of machine learning or robotics?
* Can you include more visualizations of the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, such as the gate values and the learning rate?
* Have you considered including more baselines or comparison methods to evaluate the performance of the proposed approach?
