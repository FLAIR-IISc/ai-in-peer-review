This paper presents a novel approach to few-shot learning by formulating it as a sequential meta-learning problem. The authors propose an LSTM-based meta-learner model that learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier. The meta-learner captures both short-term knowledge within a task and long-term knowledge common among all tasks, allowing it to learn a task-common initialization for the learner classifier.

The paper is well-motivated, and the approach is well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the meta-learning formulation and the LSTM-based meta-learner model. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the related work in meta-learning and few-shot learning.

The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed approach is competitive with and outperforms Matching Networks on 1- and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet. The results also show that the meta-learner model is able to learn a beneficial common initialization that allows for quick convergence of training.

I raised my score to 8 after the authors clarified the meta-learning task. However, I still have some concerns regarding the presentation and clarity of the paper.

One of the main issues with the paper is that the writing is often opaque, making it difficult to follow. The task description is abstract and would benefit from a concrete example and diagram to improve accessibility. Additionally, the details of the N-class few-shot setup are unclear and need clarification.

Furthermore, Figure 2 plots are uninformative, and the discussion around them lacks insight. The paper could also benefit from more detailed explanations of the preprocessing method and the gradient independence assumption.

Despite these issues, the paper presents interesting and convincing results. The insight about the relationship between LSTM memory cells and gradient descent updates appears novel and interesting. The paper also provides practical training/design insights for LSTM meta-learners, including initialization, weight sharing, and normalization/rescaling.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, but the presentation must be improved for clarity. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above and provide more detailed explanations of the methodology and results.
