This paper proposes a novel approach to few-shot learning by formulating it as a sequential meta-learning problem. The authors introduce an LSTM-based meta-learner model that learns to optimize the parameters of a base learner neural network classifier. The meta-learner is trained to learn both a good initialization for the learner's parameters and a successful mechanism for updating the learner's parameters given a small training set for a new classification task.

The approach is well-motivated and placed in the context of existing work on meta-learning and few-shot learning. The authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of traditional gradient-based optimization methods in the few-shot learning setting and how their approach addresses these limitations.

The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with and outperforms state-of-the-art methods, including Matching Networks, on the Mini-ImageNet dataset. The authors also provide insights into the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, which suggests that it is able to adapt to different training sets and learn a distinct route to state-of-the-art performance.

I recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to improve the presentation and clarity. The writing is often opaque, and the task description could benefit from a concrete example and diagram to make it more accessible. Additionally, some details of the N-class few-shot setup are unclear and need clarification. Finally, the plots in Figure 2 are uninformative, and the discussion around them lacks insight.

Specifically, I would like the authors to:

* Provide a concrete example of the few-shot learning task and a diagram to illustrate the meta-learning setup.
* Clarify the details of the N-class few-shot setup, including which classes are seen during meta-training and the softmax output size.
* Revise the writing to make it more accessible and clear, avoiding overly technical language and providing more intuitive explanations of the key concepts.
* Replace the plots in Figure 2 with more informative visualizations that provide insight into the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on how the meta-learner is initialized and how the weights are shared across the second-layer cells?
* How do you handle the case where the number of classes in the few-shot learning task is not fixed?
* Can you provide more insight into the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner and how it adapts to different training sets?
* How do you plan to extend this approach to more challenging scenarios, such as few-shot learning with many classes or few-shot learning with limited labeled data?
