This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns an optimizer for another neural network, aiming to tackle the challenge of few-shot learning tasks. The authors draw a parallel between Robbins-Monro updates and LSTM updates, leveraging the strengths of both to achieve quick adaptation and slower transferable learning.

The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear. The core idea is innovative and well-motivated, building upon prior work by Andrychowicz et al. (2016) while introducing novel design choices, such as a specific batch normalization implementation. The experiments are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in few-shot learning tasks.

I accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice:

1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to meta-learning, which is relevant to a wide ICLR audience.
2. The experiments are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in few-shot learning tasks.

Supporting arguments for the reasons for the decision:

* The paper's core idea is innovative and well-motivated, building upon prior work while introducing novel design choices. The authors draw a parallel between Robbins-Monro updates and LSTM updates, leveraging the strengths of both to achieve quick adaptation and slower transferable learning.
* The experiments are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in few-shot learning tasks. The authors evaluate their approach on the Mini-ImageNet dataset and compare it to several baselines, including a nearest-neighbor baseline, a fine-tuning baseline, and a state-of-the-art metric-learning approach (Matching Networks). The results show that the proposed approach outperforms the baselines and is competitive with the state-of-the-art approach.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

* Concern 1: Potential redundancy of feeding loss, gradient, and parameters to the meta-learner—ablation studies are needed to test simpler inputs. The authors should consider conducting ablation studies to evaluate the importance of each input component and to determine whether simpler inputs could achieve similar results.
* Concern 2: Could other architectural components (neurons, unit types, etc.) be learned similarly—authors' opinion/insight requested. The authors should provide more insight into whether other architectural components could be learned using a similar approach and what potential benefits or challenges this might entail.
* Concern 3: Related work is shallow; should cite earlier meta-learning efforts (e.g., Samy Bengio PhD thesis 1989, Bengio et al. 1994 on genetic programming, Schmidhuber's work). The authors should expand their related work section to include earlier meta-learning efforts and provide a more comprehensive overview of the field.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more insight into the importance of each input component (loss, gradient, and parameters) to the meta-learner? How did you determine the optimal inputs for your approach?
* Have you considered applying your approach to other architectural components, such as neurons or unit types? What potential benefits or challenges do you see in this regard?
* Can you provide more details on the specific batch normalization implementation used in your approach? How did you determine the optimal implementation for your meta-learning framework?
