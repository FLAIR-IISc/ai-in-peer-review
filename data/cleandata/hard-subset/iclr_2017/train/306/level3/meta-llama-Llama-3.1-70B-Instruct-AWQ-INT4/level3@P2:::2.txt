This paper proposes a novel meta-learning approach that interprets stochastic gradient descent (SGD) updates as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. The idea is original and important for transfer learning research, as it provides a new perspective on how to learn optimization algorithms that can adapt to different tasks.

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its relation to previous work in meta-learning and few-shot learning. The writing is generally clear, although some parts could benefit from more detail and clarity.

The proposed approach has several advantages. It provides an interesting and feasible meta-learning approach that can learn to optimize neural networks for few-shot learning tasks. The experimental evaluation is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and the authors provide useful recommendations for practical systems.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The analogy between the proposed approach and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks is not entirely accurate, as the model is closer to gated recurrent units (GRUs) than LSTMs. Additionally, the description of data separation in meta-sets is hard to follow and would benefit from visualization. The experimental evaluation is only partially satisfying, as the effects of parameters (especially it and ft) need more analysis.

Furthermore, Figure 2 has limited value and could be improved or removed. There is also a minor typo in Section 3.2 ("it" → "its").

Despite these limitations, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The idea is original and important, and the experimental evaluation is competitive with state-of-the-art methods. Releasing the evaluation code would be a major plus.

Decision: Accept

Reasons for decision:

1. The paper proposes a novel and original meta-learning approach that is important for transfer learning research.
2. The experimental evaluation is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and the authors provide useful recommendations for practical systems.

Supporting arguments:

* The paper provides a clear explanation of the proposed approach and its relation to previous work in meta-learning and few-shot learning.
* The experimental evaluation is well-designed and provides a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
* The authors provide useful recommendations for practical systems, which is an important aspect of any machine learning paper.

Additional feedback:

* The authors could benefit from providing more detail and clarity in some parts of the paper, such as the description of data separation in meta-sets.
* The experimental evaluation could be improved by analyzing the effects of parameters (especially it and ft) in more detail.
* Figure 2 could be improved or removed, as it has limited value.
* Releasing the evaluation code would be a major plus, as it would allow other researchers to reproduce the results and build upon the paper.

Questions for the authors:

* Could you provide more detail on how the proposed approach relates to previous work in meta-learning and few-shot learning?
* How did you choose the hyperparameters for the proposed approach, and what is the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters?
* Could you provide more analysis on the effects of parameters (especially it and ft) on the performance of the proposed approach?
* Are there any plans to release the evaluation code, and if so, when can we expect it to be available?
