This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn an optimizer for another neural network. The core idea is to draw a parallel between Robbins-Monro updates and LSTM updates to satisfy quick adaptation vs. slower transferable learning. The authors present a well-written and clear paper, reusing useful tricks from prior work (Andrychowicz et al. 2016) like parameter sharing and normalization, and introducing well-motivated novel design choices (specific batch-norm implementation).

The paper tackles the specific question of few-shot learning, where a classifier has to quickly generalize after seeing very few examples from each class. The authors propose a meta-learner model to learn the exact optimization algorithm used to train another learner neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The approach is well-motivated, including being well-placed in the literature, and the paper provides a clear and thorough explanation of the proposed method.

The paper supports its claims through convincing experiments, demonstrating that the proposed approach outperforms natural baselines and is competitive with the state-of-the-art in metric learning for few-shot learning. The authors also provide a clear and well-organized presentation of the results, making it easy to follow and understand the findings.

Based on these strengths, I recommend accepting this paper. However, I have a few concerns that I would like the authors to address.

Firstly, I am concerned about the potential redundancy of feeding loss, gradient, and parameters to the meta-learner. The authors should consider conducting ablation studies to test simpler inputs and evaluate their impact on the performance of the meta-learner.

Secondly, I would like the authors to provide more insight into whether other architectural components (neurons, unit types, etc.) could be learned similarly. The authors' opinion on this matter would be valuable in understanding the potential applications and limitations of their approach.

Lastly, I believe that the related work section could be improved by citing earlier meta-learning efforts (e.g., Samy Bengio PhD thesis 1989, Bengio et al. 1994 on genetic programming, Schmidhuber's work). This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the context and contributions of the paper.

To address these concerns, I would like the authors to provide more information on the following:

* Ablation studies to evaluate the impact of simpler inputs on the performance of the meta-learner
* The authors' opinion on whether other architectural components could be learned similarly
* A more comprehensive related work section that includes earlier meta-learning efforts

Overall, I believe that this paper is relevant to a wide ICLR audience and has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of meta-learning.
