Summary of contributions
- The paper formulates few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem: each episode is a short sequence of training updates (on a small support set) followed by evaluation on a held-out query set. The meta-learner is trained end-to-end to optimize test performance after a fixed, small number of inner-loop updates.
- A 2-layer LSTM serves as the meta-learner. The second layer’s cell state directly models the base learner parameters, and its update rule is designed to mirror gradient descent: c_t = f_t ⊙ c_{t-1} + i_t ⊙ (−∇_θ L_t). This makes the connection between LSTM gates and learned learning-rate/weight-decay updates explicit.
- Practical modeling choices enable scaling to convnets: parameter sharing across coordinates (shared LSTM weights per-parameter, akin to a shared learning rule/learning rate), gradient/loss preprocessing for stable magnitudes, careful gate initialization (large forget, small input), and a BN protocol to avoid leakage across episodes while matching train/test conditions.
- Empirically, on Mini-ImageNet 5-way, the method is competitive with and outperforms Matching Networks on 5-shot, and is comparable on 1-shot. Unlike nearest-neighbor/embedding methods, the base learner is a ConvNet classifier whose parameters are explicitly updated by the learned optimizer, demonstrating a distinct path to state-of-the-art few-shot performance.
- The insight relating LSTM memory updates to gradient-based updates is novel and interesting, and the paper offers concrete design guidance for training such meta-optimizers.
- After rebuttal clarified the precise meta-learning task and episode construction, my score increased to 8.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong, convincing results on Mini-ImageNet with a method that is conceptually clean and distinct from prevailing metric-learning approaches. (2) A novel and well-motivated connection between LSTM dynamics and gradient-descent-style updates, with practical training insights that will likely be useful to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Problem: The paper targets few-shot N-way classification, aiming to learn an initialization and update rule that yields high test accuracy after a small, fixed number of inner-loop updates. This is a timely and important problem.
- Motivation and placement: The method is well positioned relative to Matching Networks and other metric-learning baselines, and clearly builds on “learning to learn by gradient descent” while adapting it to the few-shot regime with an episode-level objective that optimizes test performance after T steps. The use of per-parameter shared-LSTM update rules follows prior work but the explicit LSTM-as-optimizer mapping to gradient updates, plus the sequential episode training objective, are thoughtfully adapted for few-shot learning.
- Claims vs. evidence: Results on Mini-ImageNet show clear gains over strong baselines (nearest neighbor and fine-tuning) and competitive or superior performance to Matching Networks (notably better at 5-shot; 1-shot overlaps in confidence). The paper also provides qualitative visualizations of gate behaviors and ablations on training design (e.g., number of steps) that support the modeling choices. Although the authors use their own Mini-ImageNet split and reimplementation, they fairly disclose this and still outperform despite using a smaller ConvNet than Matching Nets, which strengthens the empirical claim.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarity and accessibility: The task description is abstract. Add a concrete, end-to-end episode example (e.g., 5-way 1-shot) with a schematic that shows: (a) sequence of T training batches passed through the base learner; (b) gradients/losses fed to the LSTM; (c) LSTM updating θ; and (d) final evaluation on Dtest.
- N-way setup details: Clarify exactly which classes are used in meta-training vs. meta-testing (disjointness is stated—make it prominent). Specify the softmax head: is the final linear layer reparameterized to have N outputs per episode? Are label indices remapped each episode? Is that layer part of the learned initialization and updated by the meta-learner?
- Figures: Figure 2 (computation graph) is hard to parse and adds limited insight. Replace with a cleaner block diagram of the episode-level unrolled computation and a per-parameter update zoom-in. For gate visualizations, include distributions over parameters per layer and how they evolve over steps; add commentary tying patterns to optimization intuitions (e.g., learned weight decay via f_t).
- Simplifying assumptions: The gradient-independence assumption (ignoring second derivatives through the learner) is reasonable; quantify its empirical impact with a small-scale comparison that includes second-order terms for a subset of parameters or steps.
- Scalability and memory: Discuss the per-parameter LSTM state cost and runtime overhead for larger networks. Consider reporting wall-clock and memory vs. baselines, and whether per-layer or per-channel parameterization can retain performance while reducing cost.
- Generality: The meta-learner is trained separately for 1-shot and 5-shot and for a fixed number of steps. Comment on training a single model that handles variable k, N, and step budgets, and on performance when the number of updates at test time deviates from training.
- Baselines and fairness: Note explicitly that Matching Nets used a larger backbone (64 filters vs. 32 in the base learner), which makes your gains on 5-shot more compelling. If feasible, include an apples-to-apples backbone comparison.

Questions for the authors
- Episode specification: For 5-way tasks, is the classifier’s final layer reinitialized per episode to have exactly 5 outputs, and are those weights part of the learned initialization c0 and updated by the LSTM? How are label permutations handled?
- Data splits: Can you release the exact Mini-ImageNet class split and episode sampling code to enable reproduction and direct comparison?
- Batch normalization: During meta-testing, you collect BN stats on the support set and then freeze them for the query set. How sensitive is performance to this choice? What happens if you use per-episode BN per layer vs. group/layer norm?
- Gradient-independence: Did you observe any degradation from ignoring second derivatives? Any small-scale experiment where including them improved stability or final accuracy?
- Ablations: What is the effect of removing parameter sharing (e.g., per-layer instead of per-parameter LSTM), disabling loss preprocessing, or changing gate bias initialization? Can you share quantitative deltas?
- Step budget mismatch: If the meta-learner trained for T steps is run for T±k at test time, how does accuracy change? Do the learned gates exhibit an interpretable schedule?
- Beyond Mini-ImageNet: Have you evaluated on Omniglot or other few-shot benchmarks to test robustness across domains?

Overall assessment
An interesting and well-motivated contribution with strong empirical results and useful insights. I recommend acceptance, contingent on improving clarity and adding the requested clarifications and ablations.
