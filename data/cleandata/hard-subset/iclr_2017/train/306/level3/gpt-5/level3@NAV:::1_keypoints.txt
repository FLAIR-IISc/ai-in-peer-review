- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning optimizer that maps Robbins–Monro stochastic updates to LSTM state updates for few-shot learning.  
- Decomposes learning into fast per-task adaptation via LSTM gates and slower cross-task transfer via learned initialization.  
- Reuses effective prior ingredients (coordinate-wise parameter sharing, input normalization) while introducing a BN scheme that avoids inter-episode leakage.  
- Paper is clearly written with sufficient architecture/training details and available code.  
- Experiments on Mini-ImageNet are competitive with strong metric-learning baselines, especially strong in 5-shot.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason 1: Clear, principled formulation unifying optimization and meta-learning intuitions.  
- Reason 2: Convincing empirical evidence and careful experimental setup; broadly relevant to ICLR.  
- Motivation/placement: Robbins–Monro/LSTM analogy is insightful and justifies gating roles as adaptive step-size/decay.  
- BN treatment is a practical contribution for episodic meta-learning.  
- Correctness/rigor: Training procedure (including gradient-independence simplification) is carefully described and aligns with prior art.  
- Experiments: Thorough on Mini-ImageNet, with competitive 1-shot and superior 5-shot results plus qualitative gate analyses.  
- Clarity/reproducibility: Presentation is lucid and code availability increases credibility.  
- Request ablations on meta-learner inputs: compare gradients only; {gradient, loss}; {gradient, parameters}; and full {loss, gradient, parameters}.  
- Ask to discuss extending the LSTM-update view to learn other components (per-layer neurons/activations, normalization params, optimizer unit types) and stability constraints.  
- Request expanded related work covering early meta-learning/learned-rules literature (Bengio thesis/1994–95, Schmidhuber fast-weights) and clearer distinctions.  
- Ask for robustness analysis: sensitivity to number of update steps (train/test mismatch), computational overhead, and effect of the gradient-independence assumption with a small second-derivative check.  
- Questions for authors: Which of {loss, gradient, parameters} is most critical and effect of removing θ or L?  
- How well does a meta-learner trained for T steps generalize to T±k at test time?  
- Can the approach jointly learn BN affine parameters and activation choices under the same meta-optimizer?  
- Did you try gradient-sign-only or norm-clipped inputs to improve robustness?  
- What is the meta-learner’s compute/memory overhead versus MAML-like baselines?
