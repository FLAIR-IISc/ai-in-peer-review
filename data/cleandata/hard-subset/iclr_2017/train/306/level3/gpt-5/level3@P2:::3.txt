1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles few-shot learning by casting it as a sequential meta-learning problem: each episode consists of a sequence of training updates on a tiny support set followed by evaluation on a held-out query set from the same novel classes. The core idea is elegant and technically interesting: an LSTM meta-learner parameterizes the optimizer that trains a base ConvNet classifier. The LSTM cell state encodes the base learner’s parameters, and the LSTM update (with learned input/forget gates) is designed to mimic and extend gradient-descent updates, effectively learning both (i) an initialization of the base learner suitable for rapid adaptation and (ii) a data-driven update rule tuned for a fixed, small number of steps. To keep the meta-learner compact, parameters are shared across coordinates of the base learner’s parameters; gradient/loss preprocessing stabilizes training. Practical guidance is provided on gate initialization, normalization, and batch-norm handling in episodic training. On Mini-ImageNet (5-way, 1- and 5-shot), the approach is competitive with or better than Matching Networks and clearly outperforms nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines. The connection drawn between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent is novel and insightful, and the empirical results are convincing.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- A well-motivated, novel formulation that unifies learned initialization and learned update rules, with a clear conceptual contribution (LSTM-as-optimizer for few-shot).
- Strong empirical performance on a standard benchmark, competitive with or better than the best metric-learning methods, using a distinct base-learner paradigm.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:

- What problem is tackled? Few-shot N-way classification where a learner must rapidly adapt from k examples per class. The paper frames this as meta-learning across episodes, optimizing test performance after a fixed small number of within-episode updates.

- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The work is positioned against two major lines: (a) learning to learn/learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.) and (b) metric learning for few-shot (e.g., Matching Networks). Unlike prior learned optimizers aimed at large-scale training, this work explicitly optimizes for rapid adaptation in few-shot episodes and learns an initialization. The mapping from gradient descent to LSTM gating is thoughtfully developed.

- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1- and 5-shot, the method is competitive with or better than Matching Networks and substantially better than fine-tuning and nearest-neighbor baselines. The experiments adhere to an episodic protocol, and practical details (e.g., batch-norm statistics handling) are addressed. That said, some aspects could be strengthened:
  - Comparisons are on an internal Mini-ImageNet split; while justified, it complicates direct numerical comparison to prior reports.
  - The “gradient independence” simplification (ignoring second-order terms) is reasonable but not ablated.
  - Parameter sharing across coordinates is sensible for scalability; an explicit analysis of its impact would increase confidence in generality.

Clarity and presentation: The writing is sometimes opaque, and the task/episode setup would benefit from a concrete walkthrough and clearer diagrams. Figure 2 adds limited insight in its current form. After rebuttal clarifications on the meta-learning task, my confidence improved and I raise my score to 8.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify the episodic setup with a concrete example and a figure:
  - Show one episode: how the base learner’s final layer is sized to N, how c0 initializes all parameters, how T unrolled updates consume the support set, and how the query set loss is used for meta-updates.
  - Explicitly state which classes are used in meta-train/val/test splits and that N is fixed per experiment (5-way).
- Precisely specify the softmax head: Is the final linear layer re-initialized per episode to N outputs from learned c0? Are there any class-conditioned parameters, or is c0 entirely class-agnostic?
- Expand the experimental section:
  - Report 95% CIs, number of test episodes, and the exact convnet/backbone widths used in each method to ensure fairness.
  - Provide ablations: effect of number of unrolled steps T (train vs test), removing the forget gate (ft=1), removing gradient/loss preprocessing, with/without parameter sharing, and with/without the “gradient independence” assumption.
  - Analyze computational cost: memory usage for unrolling, wall-clock time vs Matching Nets.
  - Sensitivity to gate bias initialization, the preprocessing parameter p, and batch-norm statistics strategy.
  - Generalization to different N and k: can a single meta-learner handle variable shots/classes or variable T at test time?
- Improve analysis/visualization:
  - Replace or augment Figure 2 with plots that reveal behavior: learning curves across episodes, gate statistics stratified by layers/parameter groups, and histograms of learned learning rates (it).
  - Provide qualitative episodes where the learned optimizer recovers from poor local optima (when ft deviates from 1), supporting the intended role of the forget gate.
- Strengthen baselines:
  - A stronger fine-tuning baseline with proper regularization (weight decay, dropout, early stopping on the episode’s support via held-out within-support splits) and a small validation split when possible.
  - A linear probe on frozen features vs end-to-end fine-tuning to further disentangle the benefit of learned initialization.

Questions for the authors
- Initialization and head: Is the final linear classifier layer part of c0 and fixed to N=5 across all episodes? How do you handle episodes with different N; is a separate meta-learner required?
- Parameter sharing: Are LSTM parameters shared across all coordinates uniformly, including convolutional filters and biases? Did you explore group-wise sharing (e.g., per-layer) and observe any gains?
- Gradient independence: Did you try including second-order terms (e.g., truncated backprop with higher-order grads) on smaller models to quantify the approximation gap?
- Robustness to T mismatch: If the meta-learner is trained for T updates, how does performance change when given fewer or more updates at test time?
- Batch normalization: Could you elaborate on how the running averages are computed within an episode and whether per-episode statistics materially affect performance vs using meta-train averages?
- Computational budget: What are the memory and time costs per episode during meta-training, and how do they scale with T and model size?
- Generalization breadth: Have you tested transfer of a meta-learner trained on 5-way 1-shot to 5-way 5-shot (or to different datasets), without retraining?
- Failure modes: In which episodes does the method underperform Matching Networks (especially in 1-shot), and do gate dynamics provide diagnostic signals?

Overall, this is an interesting and timely contribution with strong results and a clean idea. With improved exposition and a few ablations, it will be a very solid paper.
