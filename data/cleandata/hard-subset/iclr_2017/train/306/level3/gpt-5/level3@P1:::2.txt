Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a meta-learning approach that interprets the learner’s parameter updates as the state dynamics of a gated recurrent model. Concretely, it uses an LSTM-style meta-learner whose cell state stores the learner’s parameters, with input and forget gates producing per-parameter update coefficients conditioned on current gradients, losses, parameter values, and previous gate values. This yields a learned optimizer specialized for few-shot regimes with a fixed, small number of updates.
- The meta-learner is trained end-to-end to minimize the learner’s test loss after T inner-loop updates within each episode, simultaneously discovering (i) a task-common initialization for the learner and (ii) a task-adaptive update rule.
- Practical design choices (per-coordinate parameter sharing, loss/gradient preprocessing, careful BN usage to avoid inter-episode leakage, small-gate initialization, gradient-independence assumption to avoid second derivatives) make the approach feasible.
- On mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the method is competitive with Matching Networks and substantially stronger than nearest-neighbor and simple fine-tuning baselines. The paper also provides qualitative analyses of learned gates and actionable guidance for stable training.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Original and well-motivated formulation that learns both initialization and an update rule explicitly matched to few-shot evaluation. 2) Solid empirical results that are competitive with state-of-the-art metric learning, coupled with clear practical insights for building such systems.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The few-shot regime stresses conventional SGD-based training due to the limited number of steps and the disadvantage of random initialization. The paper positions itself well in meta-learning literature and makes a compelling case for optimizing the test loss after a fixed small number of updates.
- Novelty and significance: Framing parameter updates as gated recurrent state transitions and learning per-parameter gates conditioned on gradients/losses is both elegant and practically important for transfer/few-shot learning. Learning the initialization c0 jointly with the update rule is crucial and well justified.
- Methodological soundness: The objective matches test-time conditions, including episode construction and the fixed number of updates. The BN handling is thoughtful and addresses leakage. The gradient-independence simplification follows prior art and is a reasonable engineering trade-off.
- Empirical support: Despite differences from prior splits/implementations, the results meaningfully compare to strong baselines (including Matching Networks), with a clear advantage in 5-shot and overlapping CIs in 1-shot. The observation that naive fine-tuning overfits underlines the value of meta-learned initialization and updates.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not strictly part of the accept decision)
- Clarify the gating analogy: The proposed update is closer to a GRU-style gating (untied gates that need not sum to one). Consider discussing this explicitly and/or reporting an ablation with GRU-tying or ft fixed to 1 (pure additive update) to isolate the role of forgetting.
- Episode construction clarity: The description of meta-train/val/test splits and episode sampling is hard to follow. A small schematic showing class partitioning, episode sampling, and train/test flows would help substantially. Publishing the exact class splits used would aid reproducibility.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Gate roles: Provide quantitative ablations for ft and it (e.g., fix ft=1, constrain gates, or remove dependence on Lt) to measure their contributions. Report correlations between gate values, gradient norms, and loss.
  - Initialization vs update: Disentangle the gains from learned c0 and learned updates by comparing (a) learned c0 with standard optimizer, (b) random c0 with learned optimizer, and (c) both.
  - Number of steps: Since the meta-learner is trained for a fixed T, evaluate robustness to T’ at test time (fewer/more steps) and report performance curves vs steps.
  - Preprocessing: Show sensitivity to the preprocessing parameter p and to turning preprocessing off.
  - BN strategy: Include an ablation demonstrating that the proposed BN protocol is necessary and does not leak information across episodes.
- Figure 2: It adds limited insight beyond the algorithm. Consider replacing with plots of accuracy vs inner-loop step, distributions of gate values across layers/tasks, or t-SNE of parameter trajectories.
- Fairness of baselines: The fine-tune baseline may be under-regularized. Consider adding L2-SP or layer-wise regularization to narrow the gap, strengthening the claim that the meta-learned initialization/update is the key.
- Minor: Section 3.2 has a typo (“it” → “its”). Public release of code and the mini-ImageNet splits would be a major plus for the community.

Questions for the authors
1) How critical is the forget gate? What is the performance when ft is fixed to 1, or when ft and it are tied (GRU-like)? Do you observe systematic benefits from explicit forgetting?
2) Can you disentangle and quantify the contributions of learned initialization vs learned update rule (see ablations above)?
3) Robustness to step mismatch: If trained for T steps but tested with T±Δ, how does performance degrade? Could the meta-learner be trained to generalize across a range of T?
4) Transfer across learners: Does the meta-learner trained on the 4-layer CNN transfer to a different learner architecture (e.g., deeper CNN or residual net), or must it be retrained?
5) Computational profile: What are the memory/compute overheads vs SGD or Matching Networks at meta-test time (per episode and per inner step)?
6) Effect of per-coordinate sharing: Have you tried augmenting with cross-parameter context (e.g., layer-wise summaries) to capture coupling, and does it help?
7) BN details: Could you report the exact momentum schedule for running statistics and demonstrate that using per-episode statistics at meta-test does not leak information across episodes?
8) Reproducibility: Will you release code and the exact mini-ImageNet splits to ensure comparability with future work?
