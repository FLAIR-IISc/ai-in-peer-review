Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles few-shot learning by framing it as meta-learning of an optimizer that quickly adapts a base classifier from very few labeled examples. The key idea is to parameterize the learner’s weight updates as an LSTM’s cell-state dynamics: the learner’s parameters are the LSTM cell state, with the input gate acting like a learned, stateful learning rate and the forget gate enabling rapid escape from bad local minima. The meta-learner jointly learns (a) a task-common initialization of the classifier and (b) an update rule specialized for a fixed, small number of training steps.
- The approach reuses effective design choices from prior work on learned optimizers (parameter sharing across coordinates; gradient/loss preprocessing) and introduces well-motivated adaptations for the few-shot regime, notably the training objective that optimizes test loss after T steps and a careful, episode-safe batch normalization protocol that aligns meta-train and meta-test conditions.
- On 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet, the method outperforms natural baselines (nearest neighbor on embeddings; fine-tuning a pretrained network) and is competitive with, and in 5-shot superior to, Matching Networks, while using a relatively simple CNN learner. Visualizations of the learned gates provide some qualitative insight (e.g., forget-gate behavior akin to weight decay).

Decision: Accept
- Reason 1: Well-motivated and clearly presented meta-learning formulation that unifies learned initialization and learned optimizer dynamics; the LSTM-as-optimizer view is elegant and practically instantiated for few-shot learning.
- Reason 2: Strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark with thoughtful training protocol choices; competitive with state-of-the-art metric-learning methods and better than strong baselines.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses the failure mode of standard gradient-based training in few-shot settings, where both the optimizer and the random initialization are poorly matched to the constraint of very few updates and scarce labels. The goal is to learn an optimizer and initialization that yield high test performance after a fixed, small number of steps.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The LSTM update–gradient descent analogy is sound, and the method builds directly on and adapts learned-optimizer ideas (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.) to the episodic meta-learning setting with a test-after-T-steps objective. The paper covers relevant meta-learning and metric-learning work, and the design choices (parameter sharing, preprocessing, BN handling) are well justified. The related work could be deepened (see feedback), but the method is appropriately positioned.
- Do the results support the claims? The experiments on 5-way Mini-ImageNet with 1-/5-shot settings demonstrate consistent gains over natural baselines and competitive or better performance than Matching Networks. The training/testing protocol matches the intended use (optimize for T steps; evaluate on a disjoint test split). The visualizations, while qualitative, are informative. Some ablations are missing (e.g., role of loss/gradient/parameter inputs; contribution of learned initialization vs update rule), but the evidence presented is compelling overall.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all affecting the score)
- Ablations and component analysis:
  - Input redundancy: Evaluate the meta-learner with subsets of inputs (e.g., gradient-only; gradient+loss; with/without θt−1; with/without gate recurrence) to assess necessity and robustness.
  - Fixed forget gate: Report performance with ft ≡ 1 to quantify the benefit of learned forgetting versus pure additive updates.
  - Learned initialization vs learned optimizer: Hold c0 fixed/random and learn only the gates, and conversely learn only c0 with a simple optimizer, to decouple contributions.
  - Preprocessing and parameter sharing: Sensitivity to the log-scaling parameter p and to sharing granularity (per-parameter vs per-tensor).
- Generalization and robustness:
  - Mismatch in step count: Train with T and evaluate with different numbers of adaptation steps to assess robustness beyond the trained horizon.
  - Vary N-way and k-shot at meta-test time; report how well a model trained for one setting transfers to nearby settings.
  - Provide results without BN or with alternative normalization to quantify dependence on the BN protocol.
- Fairness and reproducibility:
  - Clarify architectural parity across methods. The learner CNN is described with 32 filters, while Matching Nets/baselines use 64; discuss how this affects fairness (if anything, this may strengthen your results but should be explicit).
  - Release the exact Mini-ImageNet split used to facilitate comparison.
  - Report wall-clock training/inference cost and memory overhead versus standard fine-tuning.
- Theory/clarifications:
  - Discuss the “gradient independence” assumption; provide a small-scale experiment comparing to second-order training (where feasible) to validate the approximation.
  - Expand related work with a more thorough account of early meta-learning and learned-rule work (e.g., Bengio’s thesis and early papers on learned/plausible rules; Schmidhuber’s early self-referential/fast weight ideas; genetic programming for update rules), and tighten reference formatting.

Questions for the authors
- What is the quantitative contribution of each input to the meta-learner (loss, gradient, parameters, previous gate values)? Do simpler input sets perform similarly?
- How much does the learned initialization (c0) contribute versus the learned gating dynamics? Can you provide ablations isolating these factors?
- How robust is the meta-learner to using a different number of adaptation steps at test time than during training?
- Does the model trained for 5-way generalize to 10- or 20-way classification without retraining? Likewise, can a model trained for 5-shot handle 1-shot?
- What is the computational and memory overhead during meta-training and meta-testing relative to standard fine-tuning with SGD/Adam?
- Could the same meta-learning framework be extended to learn other components (e.g., per-layer architectures, normalization parameters, data augmentation policies), and if so, what constraints would be needed?
- Can you report results with ft fixed to 1 and/or with no loss input to the meta-learner to assess whether the forget gate and direct loss access are essential?
- Please confirm that the per-episode BN protocol cannot leak information across episodes and provide a sensitivity analysis to the averaging schedule.
- Will you release the exact Mini-ImageNet splits and seeds to ensure reproducibility?
