Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns an optimizer to train another (learner) neural network for few-shot classification. The learner’s parameters are directly stored in the LSTM cell state, so each meta-update resembles a Robbins–Monro/gradient-descent step. By drawing a clear parallel between gradient descent updates and LSTM state updates, the method neatly separates fast, within-task adaptation (via input/forget gates) from slower, transferable knowledge (via learned initialization).
- The approach reuses effective design choices from Andrychowicz et al. (2016)—notably, parameter sharing across coordinates and input preprocessing/normalization—while introducing well-motivated novelties, especially a batch-normalization protocol designed to avoid cross-episode leakage yet keep train/test conditions aligned.
- Training uses a test-set loss objective per episode, which explicitly optimizes for rapid generalization under a fixed small number of updates. Empirically, on mini-ImageNet 1-shot and 5-shot 5-way classification, the approach outperforms strong baselines and is competitive with or better than Matching Networks.
- The paper is clearly written, the core idea is simple and elegant, and the experiments are compelling.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A well-motivated and principled meta-optimizer design with a crisp connection to Robbins–Monro and LSTM dynamics; (2) convincing empirical results with careful engineering (normalization, parameter sharing, BN handling) supporting the central claims about rapid adaptation.

Supporting arguments
1. Problem clarity. The paper targets few-shot learning where standard gradient-based methods underperform due to limited updates and poor initializations. The proposed meta-optimizer explicitly addresses both: it learns an initialization and an update rule tuned for a fixed, small number of steps.
2. Motivation and placement. The LSTM-as-optimizer perspective is well justified by the algebraic equivalence between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent. The method is well situated relative to Andrychowicz et al. (learning to optimize at scale), Matching Networks and metric learning (few-shot via embedding), and hyperparameter-backprop approaches (Maclaurin et al.). The BN protocol is thoughtfully adapted to the episodic setting to avoid information leakage while maintaining train/test match.
3. Claims and evidence. The experiments on mini-ImageNet (disjoint train/val/test classes; 1- and 5-shot; fixed numbers of inner-loop steps; confidence intervals reported) support the main claims: learned initialization + update rule enable fast adaptation and outperform fine-tuning and nearest-neighbor baselines. Visualizations of gates provide qualitative evidence that the optimizer learns nontrivial dynamics. While more ablations could deepen insight (see below), the presented results are sound and consistent.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not strictly part of the accept decision)
- Concern 1: Input redundancy and ablations. The meta-learner ingests loss, gradients, parameters, and prior gate values. This is potentially overparameterized. Please add ablations that:
  - Remove loss from inputs (gradients often suffice as a proxy).
  - Remove parameters from inputs (only gradient and loss).
  - Use only gradient sign and magnitude separately (already partially addressed via preprocessing), versus raw gradients.
  - Fix ft=1 (pure additive update) to quantify the value of learned forgetting/weight decay versus learned initialization alone.
  - Remove learned initialization (c0 random) to quantify its contribution.
  These ablations would clarify which signals are essential and whether simpler inputs/architectures suffice.
- Concern 2: Learnable architectural components. The results suggest the LSTM can learn per-parameter learning rates and implicit regularization via gates. It would be valuable to discuss whether similar meta-learning could handle:
  - Per-parameter weight decay/momentum (explicitly parameterized and learned).
  - BN affine parameters (gamma/beta) and even per-episode BN momentum.
  - Activation function parameters (e.g., PReLU) or unit types (e.g., choosing between ReLU/ELU) as part of the meta-state.
  - Structural choices (e.g., number of channels, layer selection) through lightweight controllers, or at least per-layer optimizer types.
  Author insight into what is feasible with the current framework, and what would require architectural extensions, would strengthen the broader impact.
- Concern 3: Related work depth. The historical arc of meta-learning deserves more thorough coverage. Please cite and position relative to early “learning to learn” work, including:
  - Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1993) and related works on learning local update rules (Bengio et al., 1990; 1995).
  - Schmidhuber’s early contributions on self-modifying networks/fast weights and meta-levels (Schmidhuber, 1992; 1993; Schmidhuber et al., 1997).
  - Thrun and broader learning-to-learn literature beyond the few papers already cited.
  This will better contextualize the LSTM-optimizer line within the decades-long trajectory of meta-learning.
- Clarify the gradient-independence assumption. You follow Andrychowicz et al. in ignoring second-derivative terms through the learner when computing meta-gradients. A small experiment comparing with truncated or occasional second-order updates (e.g., finite differences, implicit gradients) would help validate this approximation in the few-shot regime.
- Generalization across mismatch. You note the benefit of matching the number of inner steps at meta-train and meta-test. It would be informative to quantify robustness when the number of classes, shots, or inner steps changes at meta-test time.
- Efficiency and scalability. Please report compute/memory overhead and wall-clock relative to standard training and to gradient-based meta-learners (e.g., MAML-style), including sensitivity to unroll length and gradient clipping.

Questions for the authors
1. Which inputs are truly necessary for the meta-optimizer? Can you provide ablations removing loss and/or parameter inputs, and fixing ft=1?
2. How robust is performance when the number of inner-loop steps at test time differs from training, or when N and K change? Does the learned optimizer extrapolate?
3. What is the quantitative impact of the batch-normalization strategy? Have you tried alternatives (e.g., group norm or instance norm) to avoid per-episode statistics?
4. How sensitive is training to the gradient-independence assumption? Any evidence that including second-order information changes outcomes?
5. Could your framework be extended to meta-learn other architectural or training components (BN gamma/beta, per-parameter weight decay/momentum, activation parameters)? What are the main obstacles?
6. Can you comment on failure modes (e.g., overfitting in 1-shot) and how the learned initialization versus the learned update rule ameliorate them?

Overall, this is a strong and well-presented paper with a crisp idea, solid engineering, and convincing results. It should be of broad interest to the ICLR community.
