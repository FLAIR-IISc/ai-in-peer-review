Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns an optimizer to train a target neural network in few-shot settings. The core idea draws an appealing connection between Robbins–Monro-style stochastic updates and the LSTM state update, enabling a decomposition between fast per-task adaptation (via input/forget gates) and slower cross-task transfer (via learned initialization). The method reuses effective ingredients from prior work (e.g., coordinate-wise parameter sharing and input normalization from Andrychowicz et al. 2016) and introduces well-justified design choices, notably a careful batch-normalization scheme that avoids inter-episode leakage while matching train/test conditions. The paper is clearly written, and the experimental results on Mini-ImageNet are competitive with strong metric-learning baselines, particularly strong in 5-shot.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) Clear, principled formulation with novel and well-motivated design that unifies optimization and meta-learning intuitions; (2) Convincing empirical evidence and careful experimental setup. The topic is broadly relevant to ICLR.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The Robbins–Monro/LSTM analogy is insightful and technically meaningful, justifying the gating roles as adaptive step-size and decay. The work is well positioned relative to learned optimizers and few-shot methods, and the BN treatment is a practical contribution for episodic meta-learning.
- Correctness and rigor: The training procedure, including the gradient-independence simplification, is carefully described and aligns with prior art. Experiments are thorough for Mini-ImageNet, with competitive 1-shot and superior 5-shot results, and qualitative gate analyses supporting the adaptation narrative.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The presentation is lucid, architecture/training details are sufficient, and the code availability further increases credibility.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Input redundancy ablations: Please provide ablations on the meta-learner inputs. Specifically compare: (i) gradients only (with sign/norm decomposition), (ii) {gradient, loss}, (iii) {gradient, parameters}, and (iv) full {loss, gradient, parameters}. This will clarify whether feeding θ and L adds value beyond ∇θL.
- Scope of “learned components”: Discuss whether the same LSTM update view could be extended to learn other architectural elements (e.g., per-layer neurons/activations, normalization parameters, optimizer unit types) and what constraints are needed for stability.
- Related work depth: Expand the historical context of meta-learning and learned rules. In addition to the cited items, please cover early work such as Samy Bengio’s thesis (1989/1993), Bengio et al. (1994/1995 on genetic programming/learned rules), and Schmidhuber’s early meta-learning/fast-weights line, and clarify precise distinctions.
- Robustness: Comment on sensitivity to the number of update steps (train/test mismatch), computational overhead, and the effect of the gradient-independence assumption via a small second-derivative check on shallow models.

Questions for authors
1) Which of {loss, gradient, parameters} is most critical? Any performance drop when removing θ or L?  
2) How well does a meta-learner trained for T steps generalize to T±k at test time?  
3) Can your approach jointly learn BN affine parameters and activation choices under the same meta-optimizer?  
4) Did you try gradient-sign-only or norm-clipped inputs to improve robustness?  
5) What is the meta-learner’s compute/memory overhead versus MAML-like baselines?
