Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles few-shot learning by framing optimization itself as a learnable process. It proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner whose cell state represents the classifier’s parameters and whose gates learn to produce parameter updates from gradients, losses, and past gate values.
- The approach jointly learns (a) an initialization of the learner network beneficial across tasks and (b) a task-adaptive update rule tuned for a fixed, small number of steps, matching the few-shot regime.
- To scale to deep nets, the meta-learner shares parameters across parameter coordinates and preprocesses gradients/losses. It also carefully handles batch normalization to avoid cross-episode leakage while keeping training and testing conditions aligned.
- Empirically, on Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the method is competitive with Matching Networks and clearly outperforms sensible baselines (nearest neighbor over pretrained embeddings, and fine-tuning a pretrained network).
- The paper offers practical insights (gate initialization, per-episode BN stats, fixed-update training matching test-time steps) that are broadly useful.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Original and well-motivated meta-learning formulation that learns both initialization and an update rule, with a clear mapping from SGD to gated updates.
- Competitive empirical performance on a challenging benchmark with reasonable baselines, and concrete, actionable training recommendations.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and significance: The paper addresses a central barrier to few-shot learning with deep nets: standard optimizers and random initializations require many steps. Casting optimization as a learned, task-adaptive process is a compelling and timely direction for transfer/meta-learning.

2) Motivation and relation to literature: The work is well placed among meta-learning and few-shot methods. It thoughtfully builds on Andrychowicz et al. (learning to optimize) but targets the few-shot regime with fixed, small T, and complements metric-based methods (e.g., Matching Nets). The paper also acknowledges the close relationship to GRUs, and to earlier “learning to learn” works.

3) Empirical support and rigor: Results on Mini-ImageNet show strong performance, especially in 5-shot where the approach exceeds Matching Networks in the authors’ reimplementation. The episode construction, fixed-update training, and per-episode BN are sensible design choices that increase ecological validity. That said, more thorough ablations (see below) would strengthen the claims about what the meta-learner actually learns (e.g., contribution of gates vs. learned initialization). The gradient-independence assumption follows prior art but should be discussed vis-à-vis potential biases or limitations.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the recurrent analogy: While the LSTM cell update is a natural mapping, the forget/input gating behavior is arguably closer to a GRU-style update. Please motivate the choice of LSTM versus GRU experimentally (e.g., a GRU meta-learner baseline) and expand the qualitative discussion that appears briefly in Section 3.1.
- Data split clarity: The description of meta-training/validation/test class splits and episode construction could be easier to follow. A compact schematic showing class splits and per-episode sampling (k-shot support vs. query/test) would greatly help.
- Ablations on it and ft: Provide analyses isolating the roles of the gates. Examples:
  - Fix ft = 1 (no forgetting) to quantify the necessity of the forget gate.
  - Fix it to a learned scalar per-layer or per-parameter group to assess the value of coordinate-wise gating.
  - Remove learned initialization (random c0) to quantify its contribution.
  - Vary the number of unrolled steps T at test time versus the T used in training to show robustness to mismatch.
- Figure 2: As currently presented, the computational graph brings limited incremental insight. Consider replacing or augmenting it with (i) the proposed episode/BN pipeline, and (ii) clearer gate-behavior visualizations tied to optimization progress (loss vs. steps overlaid with mean gate values).
- BN protocol: The per-episode BN approach is a useful practical tip. Please report performance when using only running statistics computed on the support set versus batch stats on the query set, and confirm no leakage across episodes. A short ablation would increase confidence.
- Robustness and generality: If possible, add results on an additional few-shot benchmark (e.g., Omniglot) or show cross-architecture generalization (train meta-learner on a 4-layer CNN, test on a slightly different learner) to support claims of broader applicability.
- Reproducibility: The release of code is excellent; please include the exact Mini-ImageNet class splits, episode generation scripts, and all hyperparameters to facilitate apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Minor: Section 3.2 has a typo (“it” → “its”).

Questions for the authors
1) LSTM vs. GRU: Did you try a GRU-based meta-learner (with an update gate mapping to the learning rate)? How do performance, stability, and parameter count compare?
2) Contributions of components: What is the relative gain from (a) learned initialization c0, (b) learned it, and (c) learned ft? Can you provide a table of ablations quantifying each?
3) Fixed number of steps: How sensitive is performance to the number of unrolled updates at test time? Does training with a distribution over T improve robustness?
4) Gradient-independence assumption: Did you attempt second-derivative-aware training on smaller models to assess whether ignoring these terms impacts performance or stability?
5) Parameter sharing granularity: Have you explored sharing gates per-parameter group (e.g., per-layer) rather than per-coordinate? Does this reduce variance or improve sample efficiency?
6) BN details: Can you confirm that query/test statistics are computed without mixing with other episodes? What happens if you freeze BN parameters entirely at meta-test time?
7) Computational cost: What are the training and inference costs relative to standard fine-tuning and to Matching Networks? Any memory-saving tricks for longer unrolls?
8) Generalization across tasks: If the meta-learner is trained on 5-way tasks, how well does it transfer to 20-way without retraining? Similarly, does a meta-learner trained for 1-shot degrade gracefully on 5-shot?

Overall, this is a strong and influential paper with a clear idea, solid empirical performance, and practical insights. With the suggested clarifications and ablations, it will be even more compelling.
