This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize another neural network for few-shot classification. The core contribution is to parameterize the learner’s parameter updates as the cell-state dynamics of an LSTM, exploiting the close correspondence between gradient-descent/Robbins–Monro-style updates and LSTM updates. The meta-learner outputs per-coordinate update gates (input and forget) that depend on the current gradient, loss, parameter value, and previous gate values, and it also learns a task-common initialization for the learner. The approach incorporates parameter sharing across coordinates to keep the meta-learner compact, preprocessing of gradients/losses for stability, an episode-aware handling of batch normalization to avoid leakage while matching train/test conditions, and a practical training regime that directly optimizes post-update test performance under a fixed budget of updates. On Mini-ImageNet, the method outperforms strong baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks (and superior for 5-shot), with additional visualizations providing insight into the learned optimization behavior.

Decision: Accept. Reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated idea with clear technical formulation that is well-placed relative to prior learned-optimizer work yet tailored to the few-shot regime; (2) Convincing empirical results with careful design choices that matter for reproducible performance.

Supporting arguments:
- Problem significance and framing: Few-shot generalization with limited updates is both practically important and conceptually aligned with meta-learning. The paper cleanly formulates meta-training, validation, and testing using episodic datasets with disjoint classes, ensuring proper evaluation.
- Methodological novelty and clarity: Casting the learner’s parameter trajectory as the LSTM cell state and learning per-coordinate input/forget gates is elegant and provides a mechanism to encode both fast within-task adaptation and slower, transferable knowledge via the learned initialization. The explicit dependence of gates on gradient, loss, parameters, and prior gate values is reasonable and interpretable.
- Design choices: Parameter sharing across coordinates and the gradient/loss preprocessing from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) are appropriate and necessary to scale. The episode-wise BN protocol is practical and addresses a real leakage pitfall while maintaining distributional consistency between meta-train and meta-test. The “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring second derivatives) is a standard, pragmatic choice and is acknowledged.
- Empirical validation: On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot, the method substantially improves over nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines; in 5-shot it clearly surpasses Matching Networks and is competitive in 1-shot (overlapping confidence intervals), using comparable backbone capacity. The analysis of gate values suggests the meta-learner is not just learning a fixed schedule, but adapts across episodes and k-shot settings. The paper is well written and easy to follow, and the released code aids reproducibility.

Points to strengthen (not blockers):
- Input redundancy and ablations: The meta-learner receives loss, gradient, parameters, and previous gates. It would help to ablate these inputs (e.g., no loss, no parameter, fixed ft=1, no recurrent gate history, or using only normalized gradients) to quantify what is essential. Similarly, ablate the episode-wise BN scheme and the preprocessing p to show their contribution.
- Initialization vs. update rule: Separate the benefits of learned initialization and learned update dynamics (e.g., learned θ0 + standard optimizer vs. random θ0 + learned optimizer, and both).
- Generalization across mismatch in T, N, k: The paper trains explicitly for a fixed number of updates; report how performance degrades or holds when testing with different numbers of steps, different N-way settings, or different k to assess robustness.
- Related work depth: While several classic meta-learning works are cited, the discussion could better situate this paper within early lines of research on learning rules (e.g., Bengio’s dissertation and follow-ups using genetic programming/simulated annealing for local learning rules) and Schmidhuber’s early self-modifying/fast-weights work. Tightening citations and clarifying the distinctions will help position the contribution.
- Practicality: Briefly quantify meta-training cost (time/memory) and sensitivity to network size, as learned optimizers can be resource-intensive.

Questions for the authors:
1) Which inputs to the meta-learner are most critical? Please provide ablations removing (a) loss Lt, (b) parameters θt−1, (c) previous gates, and (d) the forget gate (ft=1), as well as a variant that uses only normalized gradients.
2) How much of the gain comes from the learned initialization versus the learned update rule? Can you report results for combinations of learned θ0 with standard SGD/Adam and random θ0 with the meta-learner?
3) How sensitive is performance to the number of unrolled steps T at test time if it differs from training? Does a model trained for T=5 generalize to T=10?
4) What is the effect of the episode-wise BN protocol? Please compare: (i) global running stats only, (ii) no BN, and (iii) your per-episode scheme.
5) How stable is training across random seeds and meta-train/test splits (given the custom Mini-ImageNet split)? Please report mean±95% CI over multiple seeds.
6) What are the meta-learner’s capacity and overhead (hidden size, parameter count, wall-clock per episode)? Does performance saturate with more meta-learner capacity?
7) Could similar gating ideas be extended to other architectural components (e.g., per-layer or per-filter updates, learning normalization parameters, or meta-learning activation/attention parameters)? Any insights from preliminary experiments?
8) Did you evaluate on another dataset (e.g., Omniglot) to test domain robustness? If not, do you expect the BN strategy and preprocessing to transfer?

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution likely to interest a broad ICLR audience working on meta-learning, optimization, and few-shot learning.
