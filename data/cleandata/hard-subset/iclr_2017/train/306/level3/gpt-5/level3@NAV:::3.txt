1. Summary of contributions
This paper formulates few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem in which each episode is a sequence of training batches followed by a held-out test batch, and proposes an LSTM meta-learner that learns to update a ConvNet classifier. The second-layer LSTM cell state explicitly represents the learner’s parameters; the input/forget gates implement learned update rules closely related to gradient descent. The authors introduce practical design choices—parameter sharing across coordinates (akin to a shared learning rate), gradient-independence to avoid second derivatives, careful gate bias initialization, normalization/rescaling of inputs, and a batch-norm protocol that avoids leakage across episodes. Empirically, the method is competitive with—and in 5-shot Mini-ImageNet clearly outperforms—Matching Networks, offering a distinct alternative to nearest-neighbor/embedding approaches. The insight connecting LSTM memory updates to gradient-based optimization is novel and thought-provoking. After the author rebuttal clarified the episodic task and evaluation protocol, I raised my score to 8.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) a clear, novel formulation with an elegant link between LSTM dynamics and gradient descent; (ii) strong empirical results on Mini-ImageNet, particularly in 5-shot, demonstrating a viable non-metric route to SOTA performance.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem: Learn to rapidly adapt a classifier from very few labeled examples; the paper addresses this by learning both initialization and update rules within an episodic framework.
- Motivation and placement: Well-situated with respect to Matching Networks, memory-augmented models, and learned optimizers (Andrychowicz et al.), while differing in using the LSTM’s cell state as actual parameters of a base learner.
- Evidence/rigor: Experiments on 1- and 5-shot 5-way Mini-ImageNet are sound and competitive; ablations (gate visualizations, training heuristics) provide insight. Some simplifying assumptions (gradient independence, shared per-coordinate LSTM) are acknowledged; despite them, results are convincing.

4. Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Clarify the N-class episodic setup: which classes are seen during meta-training vs. meta-test, how the softmax head is instantiated per episode, and whether class-specific last-layer weights are reinitialized.
- Provide a concrete, end-to-end example (with a schematic) of one episode, showing the sequence of train batches and the final test evaluation.
- Replace or augment Figure 2 with more informative analyses (e.g., training/test loss curves per episode, ablations on parameter sharing, gate bias initialization, and preprocessing).
- Improve exposition around batch norm handling and its effect on performance.

Questions for the authors
- Is the final softmax layer episodic (N-way) and reparameterized per episode? How is it initialized?
- Which classes are included in meta-training vs. meta-test, and are they strictly disjoint?
- How sensitive is performance to the gradient-independence assumption? Any results with second-order terms on smaller models?
- Does sharing LSTM parameters across coordinates limit performance relative to grouping by layer or tensor? Any ablations?
- Can the method handle variable numbers of update steps at meta-test without retraining?
