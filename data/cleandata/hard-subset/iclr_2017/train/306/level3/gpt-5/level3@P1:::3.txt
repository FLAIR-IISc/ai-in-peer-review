The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a base neural classifier for few-shot classification. The key idea is to cast few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem, where each episode consists of a short sequence of training batches followed by a test batch, and to train an LSTM to output parameter updates for the base learner. The second-layer LSTM cell state represents the classifier parameters; its update mimics gradient descent (input gate ≈ learning rate, forget gate ≈ weight decay/reset), which provides a principled, differentiable parametrization of the optimizer. The method shares the LSTM parameters across parameter coordinates to keep the meta-learner compact, learns a common initialization of the base learner, and uses practical tricks (input/gradient preprocessing, gate bias initialization, careful batch norm handling). On Mini-ImageNet, it is competitive with—and in some settings outperforms—Matching Networks, while clearly beating nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines. The conceptual link between LSTM cell updates and gradient descent is simple, elegant, and appears novel, and the paper provides practical guidance for training such meta-learners.

Decision: Accept (8). Primary reasons: (1) a well-motivated and novel formulation that unifies meta-learning and optimization via an LSTM update rule and meta-learned initialization; (2) convincing empirical results on Mini-ImageNet with a base classifier rather than a metric learner, expanding the design space for few-shot learning.

Supporting arguments:
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles few-shot N-way classification where gradient-based training from random initializations is too slow/unstable and standard optimizers are not tailored to a small, fixed number of updates. Framing the task as meta-learning over episodes with matched train/test conditions is appropriate and aligns with recent literature.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on “learning to optimize” (Andrychowicz et al., 2016), but targets few-shot learning explicitly and uses an objective that optimizes test performance after a fixed, small number of inner-loop steps. It contrasts with Matching Networks and other metric-learning approaches by using a learned optimizer over a parametric classifier, and with hypergradient methods (Maclaurin et al., 2015) by avoiding expensive second derivatives via a gradient-independence approximation. The LSTM–GD equivalence is a clear conceptual contribution.
- Claims and evidence: The experimental protocol is in line with episodic training; results on 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet are strong, improving over nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines, and competitive to or better than Matching Networks. The ablations/insights (gate initialization, parameter sharing, preprocessing, batch-norm handling per-episode) are practical and likely to be useful to the community. While the “gradient independence” approximation could in principle bias meta-gradients, the results suggest it suffices in this regime. Visualizations of gates indicate nontrivial, task-dependent behavior.
- Rigor: The training loop is clearly specified, including the mapping from inner losses/gradients to LSTM inputs, shared-parameter design to ensure scalability, and the matching between meta-train and meta-test conditions. Some details (class splits, softmax head size) need clarification, but the core methodology is sound.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical):
- Clarity and accessibility: The task/episode formulation is conceptually dense; include a concrete, step-by-step example of a 5-way 1-shot episode and an annotated diagram that shows the data flow from Dtrain gradients into the meta-learner and back to updated classifier parameters, then evaluation on Dtest.
- Experimental details: Clearly state the N-way setting during meta-train/validation/test (is N fixed at 5?), the output dimensionality of the classifier per episode, and how labels are remapped. Explicitly describe how the final linear layer is handled across episodes and how c0 is shaped when N varies.
- Fair comparison: Ensure parity in backbone capacity across methods; the text mentions different filter counts (32 vs 64) in places—resolve this to avoid confusion. Since you created a new Mini-ImageNet split, please release the exact class splits and episode generation code to facilitate replication.
- Figures and analysis: The gate plots (Figure 2/3) are hard to interpret. Consider summarizing gate statistics across layers and steps, and add ablations: (i) ft fixed to 1 vs learned, (ii) learned c0 vs random initialization, (iii) with/without preprocessing, (iv) varying the number of inner updates T at meta-test to assess robustness.
- Limitations and scope: Discuss sensitivity to mismatch between meta-trained T and test-time T, and to k and N. The method currently trains separate models for 1-shot and 5-shot; comment on training a single meta-learner robust across k and N.

Questions for the authors:
- Episode setup: Is N fixed to 5 during meta-training and meta-testing? How is the classifier head parameterized when sampling new label sets—do you reinitialize a 5-way softmax each episode with a meta-learned c0 of fixed shape?
- Batch normalization: Please detail the exact per-episode statistics handling at meta-train vs meta-test, momentum used for running averages, and whether BN affine parameters are part of c0 and updated by the meta-learner.
- Gradient independence: Did you evaluate the impact of ignoring second derivatives (e.g., truncated higher-order gradients) on performance or stability? Any empirical comparison to a second-order variant on smaller models?
- Robustness: How sensitive is performance to the number of inner updates at test time, to learning rate initialization (input-gate bias), and to the preprocessing hyperparameter p?
- Compute and scalability: What is the wall-clock and memory overhead per episode versus SGD/Adam? How many meta-parameters does the LSTM have, and how does training scale with base model size?
- Generalization: Does a meta-learner trained on 5-way tasks generalize to 20-way (keeping the same backbone) without retraining? Similarly, can a model trained on 1-shot transfer to 5-shot with improved performance by simply running more steps?
- Ablations: Can you report performance with ft fixed to 1 (pure additive updates), and with c0 not learned, to quantify the contribution of each component?

Overall, this is an interesting and well-motivated contribution with solid empirical results and useful practical insights. With clearer exposition and a few additional details, it will be a strong paper.
