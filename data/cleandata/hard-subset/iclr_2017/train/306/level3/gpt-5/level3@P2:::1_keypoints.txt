- Frames few-shot learning as meta-learning an LSTM-based optimizer: learner parameters are LSTM cell state, input gate as learned learning rate, forget gate for escaping bad minima; jointly learns initialization and update rule for a fixed small number of steps.
- Reuses learned-optimizer design choices (parameter sharing, gradient/loss preprocessing) and introduces a test-after-T training objective plus an episode-safe batch-normalization protocol.
- Empirically outperforms natural baselines and is competitive with or superior to Matching Networks on 5-way Mini-ImageNet (1-/5-shot); gate visualizations give qualitative insight.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason 1 for accept: elegant, well-motivated LSTM-as-optimizer formulation that unifies learned initialization and optimizer dynamics.
- Reason 2 for accept: strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark with thoughtful training/testing protocol choices.
- Problem addressed: standard optimizers and random initializations fail in few-shot settings; goal is to learn an optimizer and init that yield high test performance after few updates.
- Positioning: builds on learned-optimizer/meta-learning literature and adapts prior ideas to episodic meta-learning with appropriate design justifications; related work could be deeper.
- Evidence: experiments support claims with consistent gains and appropriate evaluation, though some ablations are missing (e.g., input contributions, init vs update rule).
- Ablation recommendations: test input subsets (gradient/loss/params/gates), fixed forget gate (ft ≡ 1), decouple learned c0 vs learned gates, and sensitivity to preprocessing and parameter-sharing granularity.
- Generalization/robustness recommendations: evaluate mismatch in adaptation step count, transfer across N-way and k-shot settings, and dependence on batch-norm by testing without BN or with alternatives.
- Fairness & reproducibility recommendations: clarify architectural parity between methods (e.g., filter counts), release exact Mini-ImageNet splits/seeds, and report wall-clock training/inference time and memory overhead.
- Theory & clarifications: discuss the gradient-independence assumption, compare to second-order training where feasible, and expand historical related work on learned update rules and fast-weight ideas.
- Questions for authors: quantify each input’s contribution to the meta-learner; ablate learned initialization vs gating; test robustness to different adaptation step counts; evaluate transfer to other N-way/k-shot settings; report computational/memory overhead; assess extending framework to other learned components; provide results with ft fixed and without loss input; confirm BN protocol does not leak across episodes and provide sensitivity analysis; commit to releasing splits and seeds.
