1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a meta-learning approach that interprets the sequence of SGD updates as a gated recurrent computation with trainable parameters. Concretely, the learner’s parameters are identified with the LSTM cell state; the gradient and loss at each step serve as inputs; and the meta-learner learns the input and forget gates (it and ft) as functions of local signals to produce parameter updates. The meta-learner also learns the learner’s initialization (c0), enabling rapid adaptation in few steps. Parameter sharing across coordinates keeps the meta-learner compact, and careful preprocessing plus BN handling make the method practical. Training matches test-time conditions by optimizing the learner’s test loss after a fixed number of updates per episode. On Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot tasks, the method is competitive with Matching Networks and outperforms strong baselines; for 5-shot it surpasses Matching Networks on the authors’ split. The paper also offers useful practitioner guidance (gate bias initialization, gradient independence simplification, per-episode BN treatment, matching update counts).

2. Decision
Accept. The paper offers an original and influential framing of meta-learning as learning an optimizer with gated recurrent updates and a learned initialization, and it demonstrates competitive performance with appropriate baselines while providing practical insights useful to the community.

3. Supporting arguments
Answering the three key questions:
- What problem is tackled? Few-shot learning, specifically how to train a classifier to generalize after very few labeled examples by learning both an initialization and an update rule optimized for a small, fixed number of steps.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It builds on meta-learning ideas and on learned optimizers (e.g., Andrychowicz et al.) but adapts them to few-shot learning with a task-aligned objective (optimize test loss after T updates) and a clear gated-update parametrization. It situates itself relative to metric-learning baselines and prior meta-learners.
- Do results support the claims? Largely yes. The method is competitive with state of the art on Mini-ImageNet and beats strong baselines. The comparisons are appropriate, confidence intervals are reported, and the implementation details are sound. However, some empirical analysis is incomplete (e.g., limited ablations of gate roles and hyperparameters), and parts of the experimental setup (meta-set splits) are hard to parse.

Pros:
- A novel and feasible meta-learning formulation: SGD-as-gated-RNN with learned gates and initialization.
- Originality and importance for transfer/meta-learning; the idea can influence optimizer design and transfer initialization.
- Competitive results with proper baselines (nearest neighbor, fine-tuning) and Matching Networks.
- Practical recommendations (gate biasing, preprocessing, BN treatment, matching update counts) are valuable for real systems.

Cons:
- The update analogy is conceptually closer to a GRU than a full LSTM (no output gate; coupled gated additive updates), and this nuance could be acknowledged/clarified.
- The description of meta-training/meta-validation/meta-testing class separation and episode generation is hard to follow; a clear schematic would help.
- Experimental evaluation is only partially satisfying: the roles and sensitivity of it and ft, number of updates T, and other design choices deserve deeper analysis/ablations.
- Figure 2 adds limited insight given the text; it could be replaced by a clearer per-episode dataflow schematic.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify the gated-update analogy: explicitly discuss why the proposed update may be better viewed as a GRU-like gated additive rule, what was empirically gained by using separate it and ft not constrained to sum to 1, and whether an output gate or hidden/output pathway was explored.
- Meta-set splits and episode construction: include a compact visual showing class disjointness across meta-train/val/test, sampling of N classes, K support examples, and query sets. This will address potential confusion and ensure reproducibility.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Gate roles: report performance with ft fixed to 1 (pure learned learning rate), with tied gates (ft=1−it), and with loss/gradient-only inputs to the gates to quantify which signals matter.
  - Update horizon: vary T at meta-train and meta-test to study robustness to mismatch and whether the learned optimizer generalizes beyond the trained number of steps.
  - Preprocessing parameter p and inclusion/exclusion of loss as an input.
  - BatchNorm strategy: compare per-episode running stats vs. global stats and quantify any leakage risks and performance impact.
- Visualizations: your gate visualizations are helpful; add distributions over it and ft per layer and over time with corresponding loss trajectories to better connect dynamics to outcomes.
- Broaden evaluation if possible: report results on Omniglot or tieredImageNet for cross-dataset validation; include different N-way settings and a stronger fine-tuning baseline with regularization (e.g., weight decay, cosine classifier).
- Complexity: provide memory/time overhead versus standard SGD for typical learner sizes; comment on scalability to deeper learners.
- Minor: fix the typo in Section 3.2 (“it” → “its”).

Questions for the authors
- Gate design: Did you evaluate ft=1 and/or tying ft and it? How sensitive is performance to the presence of the forget gate? Why call the model “LSTM-based” rather than “GRU-like” given the absence of an output gate?
- Inputs to gates: Which inputs (gradient, loss, parameter value, previous gate value) are most important? Do you see degradation when removing the loss input?
- Update horizon: How well does a meta-learner trained for T steps perform when allowed T’≠T at test time? Is there graceful degradation or overfitting to a specific horizon?
- BN handling: Please confirm that running statistics used at test time are computed only from the support (train) set within each episode, not from queries, to avoid leakage. Any performance drop if query stats are forbidden entirely?
- Data splits: Are meta-train/val/test class sets strictly disjoint, and will you release the exact lists to ensure comparability?
- Reproducibility: Will you release the full evaluation code, dataset split files, and training scripts (with random seeds) to facilitate fair comparison? This would be a major plus.

Overall, this is a strong and impactful paper that I recommend accepting, with suggested clarifications and ablations to further strengthen it.
