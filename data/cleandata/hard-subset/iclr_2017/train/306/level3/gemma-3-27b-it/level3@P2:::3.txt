This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner to address the challenge of few-shot learning. The authors frame the problem as a sequential learning task, where each episode consists of a series of training batches followed by a test batch. The LSTM’s memory cells are cleverly used to model the updated parameters of a base learner (a convolutional neural network), and the update rules are inspired by gradient descent. The approach demonstrates competitive performance against, and even outperforms, existing methods like Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset.

**Decision: Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and insightful approach to few-shot learning with strong empirical results. The connection between LSTM memory cells and gradient descent updates is particularly interesting and offers a fresh perspective on optimization within a meta-learning framework.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of formulating few-shot learning as a sequential meta-learning problem is well-motivated. The limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in low-data regimes are clearly articulated, and the paper persuasively argues for the need to learn an effective initialization and update strategy. The LSTM-based meta-learner is a compelling solution, capable of capturing both short-term knowledge within a task and long-term knowledge shared across tasks. 

The experimental results on Mini-ImageNet are strong, demonstrating competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods. The fact that the approach outperforms Matching Networks, particularly in the 5-shot setting, is significant. Furthermore, the method utilizes a distinct architecture (a convolutional network for the base learner) compared to the embedding-based approaches common in this field, showcasing a different and viable route to achieving strong few-shot learning performance.

The authors also provide valuable practical insights for training LSTM meta-learners, including recommendations for initialization, weight sharing, and normalization/rescaling of gradients and losses. These details are crucial for reproducibility and potential adoption by other researchers.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the technical contributions are substantial, the presentation of the paper could be significantly improved for clarity and accessibility. The initial description of the meta-learning task is abstract and difficult to grasp. A concrete example, perhaps with a small, illustrative dataset, and a clearer diagram (beyond Figure 1) would greatly enhance understanding. Specifically, illustrating the flow of data through the LSTM meta-learner during a single episode would be beneficial.

The details of the N-class few-shot setup are somewhat unclear. It would be helpful to explicitly state which classes are seen during meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing, and how the softmax output size of the base learner is handled when presented with novel classes during testing.

Figure 2 is largely uninformative. The plots themselves are difficult to interpret, and the accompanying discussion lacks insightful analysis. What specific patterns or behaviors are the authors observing in the gate values? Does the variability in the input gate values across different datasets suggest that the meta-learner is adapting its learning rate based on the characteristics of the task? Providing more concrete interpretations of these visualizations would strengthen the paper.

The writing style is often opaque, making it challenging to follow the authors’ reasoning. While the technical details are present, streamlining the language and providing more intuitive explanations would make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more concrete example of a single episode in the meta-training process, illustrating the flow of data from the training batches through the LSTM meta-learner to the base learner, and ultimately to the calculation of the loss used to update the meta-learner? A diagram accompanying this example would be highly beneficial.

2.  How are the classes partitioned into meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets? Are the same classes seen across all episodes within a given meta-set? How is the softmax output size handled when the base learner encounters novel classes during meta-testing?

3.  Can you elaborate on the insights gained from the visualization of the LSTM gate values (Figure 3)? Are there specific patterns or behaviors that suggest the meta-learner is adopting particular optimization strategies under different conditions?

4.  The paper mentions that the fine-tuned baseline performs worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline. Is there a specific reason why this is occurring? Does the learned initialization provided by the meta-learner effectively address the overfitting issue encountered by the fine-tuned baseline?
