## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper presents a novel approach to few-shot learning by framing it as a sequential meta-learning problem. The authors propose an LSTM-based meta-learner designed to learn the optimization algorithm for a base learner neural network classifier, specifically targeting scenarios with a limited number of updates. The model learns both a beneficial initialization for the classifier and a dynamic update rule tailored for rapid convergence in few-shot settings. The results demonstrate competitive performance against, and in some cases outperformance of, existing state-of-the-art methods like Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet dataset.

**Decision: Accept**

The core contribution of this work – the formulation of few-shot learning as a learned optimization algorithm via an LSTM meta-learner – is both novel and compelling. The empirical results provide strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The demonstrated performance, alongside the insightful connection between LSTM memory cell updates and gradient descent, makes this a valuable contribution to the field. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully tackles the challenge of few-shot learning, a critical area with significant potential impact. The motivation is clearly articulated: gradient-based optimization struggles with limited data, and a learned optimization strategy could provide a robust solution.  The choice of an LSTM to model the optimization process is well-justified by the analogy between LSTM cell state updates and gradient descent. This connection is a particularly strong aspect of the work. 

The experimental setup, while using a standard benchmark (Mini-ImageNet), provides a solid foundation for evaluating the proposed method. The reported results are competitive and demonstrate an improvement over baseline methods and comparable performance to Matching Networks.  The ablation studies, presented through the visualization of the meta-learner’s gate values, offer some insight into its learned behavior, highlighting its adaptability to different datasets. The practical insights shared regarding LSTM meta-learner training (initialization, weight sharing, and normalization) are particularly valuable for future research in this area. 

The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of meta-learning and few-shot learning, referencing relevant prior work and clearly delineating the contributions of this paper. The inclusion of a code repository further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the research.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the core ideas and results are strong, the presentation could be significantly improved to enhance clarity and accessibility. The initial description of the meta-learning task is rather abstract. Providing a concrete, step-by-step example of how a single "episode" unfolds – from dataset selection to parameter updates and evaluation – would be highly beneficial. A clear diagram illustrating this process would also dramatically improve understanding.

Further clarification is needed regarding the specifics of the N-class few-shot setup. It's unclear which classes are seen during meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing.  Also, what is the size of the softmax output layer for the learner network?  Is it fixed to the number of test classes in a given episode, or is it larger?

Figure 2, depicting the computational graph, is currently uninformative. The visualization lacks sufficient detail to convey meaningful insights. A more detailed and annotated figure, coupled with a more in-depth discussion of the key components and data flow, would substantially improve its value.  

The phrasing throughout the paper is often dense and opaque. While the concepts are theoretically sound, the writing style hinders accessibility.  Rewriting sections with a focus on clarity and conciseness would be greatly appreciated.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of 12 and 5 updates for the 1-shot and 5-shot tasks respectively? Was this determined through hyperparameter optimization, or is there a theoretical justification for these numbers?
2.  What is the distribution of classes across the meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing splits?  Specifically, are the test classes completely disjoint from the training classes, or is there some overlap?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of preprocessing method for the gradients and losses?  Have you explored alternative normalization techniques?
4.  The assumption of gradient independence is a significant simplification. Could you discuss the potential impact of this assumption on the learned optimization strategy and perhaps explore methods to mitigate its effects?
5.  You mention the fine-tuning baseline overfits. Would a regularization technique (e.g., dropout, weight decay) improve its performance and potentially bring it closer to the meta-learning LSTM’s results?
