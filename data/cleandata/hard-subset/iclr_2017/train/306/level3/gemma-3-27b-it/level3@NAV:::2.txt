## Review of “LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning”

This paper proposes a novel and intriguing meta-learning approach that frames SGD updates as a gated recurrent model – specifically an LSTM – with trainable parameters. The core idea of learning an optimization algorithm tailored to the few-shot setting is original and potentially impactful for transfer learning research. The paper is generally well-structured and clearly articulates the motivation behind the work, highlighting the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in low-data regimes. The experimental results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods like Matching Networks, and the authors provide useful recommendations regarding parameter choices and training strategies. Releasing the evaluation code, as stated, would be a significant contribution to the community.

I recommend **Acceptance** of this paper, with minor revisions. The novelty of the approach and promising results outweigh the current limitations. The work presents a compelling alternative to existing few-shot learning techniques and offers a fresh perspective on optimization within a meta-learning framework. 

The primary reason for acceptance is the originality of the proposed method. Interpreting optimization as a learnable recurrent process opens up interesting avenues for research. Furthermore, the empirical results demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the approach, showcasing its competitiveness with established techniques.

However, a few points require attention. While the LSTM analogy is clever, the authors acknowledge it’s closer to a GRU than a traditional LSTM. A brief discussion on why this difference doesn’t fundamentally impact the approach would be beneficial. The description of data separation within the meta-sets is difficult to follow and would greatly benefit from a visual illustration – a diagram showing how classes are split across training, validation, and test sets would significantly enhance clarity. 

The experimental evaluation, while solid, feels somewhat incomplete. A more detailed analysis of the impact of key parameters like *it* and *ft* is needed. How does varying these parameters affect convergence speed and final performance? Is there an optimal range for these values? Additionally, Figure 2 lacks substantial value – it mostly depicts a standard computational graph and doesn't offer unique insight into the proposed model. Finally, a minor typo exists in Section 3.2 ("it" should be "its").

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing an LSTM architecture specifically, and how the design choices were made to accommodate the gradient-based optimization framework?
*   What specific strategies were employed to prevent the meta-learner from overfitting, given the limited data available in the few-shot setting?
*   Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the observed behavior of the *it* and *ft* gates, as shown in Figure 3, and how these dynamics contribute to the overall performance of the meta-learner?
