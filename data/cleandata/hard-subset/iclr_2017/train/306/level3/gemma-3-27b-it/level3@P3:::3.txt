## Review of “LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning”

**1. Summary of Claims and Contributions:**

This paper presents a novel approach to few-shot learning by framing it as a sequential meta-learning problem. The core idea is to use an LSTM meta-learner to *learn* the optimization algorithm used to train a base learner (a convolutional neural network classifier).  Specifically, the LSTM’s cell state represents the learner's parameters, and updates are designed to mimic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate that this meta-learner can learn a good initialization for the learner and an effective update strategy, leading to competitive performance – and improvements over Matching Networks – on 1- and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet. They also provide valuable practical insights into training such LSTM meta-learners, including initialization strategies, weight sharing techniques, and normalization methods.  The work highlights a fascinating connection between LSTM memory cells and gradient descent updates, offering a fresh perspective on optimization within a meta-learning context.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept.** The paper presents a compelling and novel approach to few-shot learning with strong empirical results. Despite presentation issues, the core idea is insightful, and the results suggest a significant contribution to the field.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

The primary reason for acceptance is the originality and effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The formulation of few-shot learning as a sequential optimization problem tackled by an LSTM meta-learner is a clever and promising direction.  Importantly, the method *differs* from prevalent metric-learning approaches (like Matching Networks), indicating a distinct and valuable route to achieving state-of-the-art performance. The experimental results on Mini-ImageNet, particularly the outperformance of Matching Networks in the 5-shot setting, provide strong evidence supporting the claims. 

The insights into training LSTM meta-learners are also a significant contribution. The details regarding initialization, weight sharing (analogous to a shared learning rate), and normalization are practically useful for researchers exploring this area. The authors' observation regarding the relationship between LSTM memory cells and gradient descent is genuinely novel and offers a potentially fruitful area for future investigation.  The reviewer's initial score was increased after the authors clarified the nature of the meta-learning task, demonstrating the importance of clear communication and the authors’ responsiveness.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While I recommend acceptance, the paper suffers from clarity issues that detract from its impact. 

*   **Task Description:** The description of the meta-learning task (Section 2) is abstract and difficult to grasp without a concrete example. A visual diagram illustrating the sequence of training batches, test batch, and the interplay between the meta-learner and base learner would dramatically improve accessibility.
*   **N-Class Setup Clarification:**  The details of the N-class few-shot setup are unclear. Specifically, how are classes selected for meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing? Is there any overlap?  What is the size of the softmax output layer in the base learner for each task?
*   **Figure 2:** Figure 2 is largely uninformative. The plots do not reveal any clear patterns, and the discussion around them lacks insightful interpretations.  Consider revising the figure to highlight key behaviors of the meta-learner or providing a more detailed explanation of what the plots are intended to show.
*   **Writing Style:**  The writing is often opaque, making it challenging to follow the authors’ reasoning.  Rewriting sections with greater clarity and conciseness would substantially improve the paper’s readability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Task Example:** Could you provide a specific example illustrating a complete "episode" of meta-training? Showing exactly how the data flows between the base learner, the meta-learner, and the loss calculation would greatly clarify the process.
2.  **Class Selection:** What strategy is used to select the N classes for each dataset (episode) during meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing? Are the classes completely disjoint between these sets, or is there some overlap?
3.  **Softmax Size:** What is the size of the softmax output layer in the base learner classifier for each task (i.e., for each set of N classes)?
4.  **Figure 2 Interpretation:** Could you provide a more detailed explanation of what the plots in Figure 2 are intended to convey and highlight any key insights revealed by the visualization? Are there any specific patterns in the gate values that correspond to successful or unsuccessful learning episodes?
5. **Gradient Independence Assumption:** You mention the gradient independence assumption. Has any ablation study been performed to measure the impact of this assumption on performance? Would including second-order gradients significantly improve results, even with the computational cost?
