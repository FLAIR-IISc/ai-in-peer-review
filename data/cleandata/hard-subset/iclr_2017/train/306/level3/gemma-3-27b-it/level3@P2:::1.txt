This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn an optimization algorithm for training another neural network, specifically in the few-shot learning regime. The core idea is to draw a parallel between gradient descent updates and LSTM cell updates, allowing the meta-learner to discover both a good initialization for the learner’s parameters and a successful update mechanism for small training sets. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, building upon existing work like Andrychowicz et al. (2016) while introducing novel design choices like a specific batch normalization implementation. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods.

**Accept**

My primary reason for acceptance is the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach. The framing of learning an optimizer as a recurrent process is insightful and leads to a practical meta-learning algorithm that achieves strong results on a challenging problem. The second reason is the thoroughness of the work. The authors not only present the core idea but also carefully address implementation details, like parameter sharing and batch normalization, essential for practical application.

The paper clearly articulates the problem of few-shot learning and motivates the approach by highlighting the limitations of traditional gradient-based optimization in small data regimes. The connection between LSTM updates and gradient descent is well-explained, providing a strong theoretical foundation for the method. The experiments on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines and state-of-the-art metric learning techniques like Matching Networks. The ablation studies, although not extensive, provide some initial justification for the design choices. The visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior offers valuable insight into the learned optimization strategy.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. My first concern revolves around the potential redundancy of feeding the loss, gradient, *and* parameters to the meta-learner. While the authors justify this by suggesting each provides useful information, it’s unclear whether all three are truly necessary. Ablation studies systematically removing each input component and measuring the impact on performance would strengthen the argument. A simpler meta-learner with fewer inputs could potentially be more efficient and easier to interpret.

Secondly, the paper focuses specifically on learning an *optimizer*. It would be interesting to hear the authors’ thoughts on whether other architectural components – not just the update rule, but perhaps even neuron types or network connectivity – could be similarly learned within a meta-learning framework. Do they foresee limitations to this approach? What challenges would arise from attempting to learn more fundamental aspects of the learner network architecture?

Finally, the related work section, while acknowledging relevant papers, feels somewhat shallow. The history of meta-learning is richer than presented. Earlier efforts, such as the work by Samy Bengio (PhD thesis, 1989), Bengio et al. (1994) utilizing genetic programming, and Schmidhuber’s work in the early 90s on learning to learn, should be cited and discussed to provide a more complete context for the current contribution. These works laid foundational principles for the field and deserve recognition.

Here are some questions for the authors:

1.  Could you provide ablation study results demonstrating the importance of each input (loss, gradient, parameters) to the meta-learner? Specifically, what is the performance drop when removing each input individually, and in combination?
2.  Do you believe the meta-learning approach could be extended to learn other aspects of the learner network, such as neuron types or connectivity patterns? What challenges do you anticipate in such an extension, and how might they be addressed?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the meta-learner, particularly in comparison to training the learner directly? How does the complexity scale with the size of the learner network?
4.  The paper mentions the gradient independence assumption. While acknowledging its simplification, could you comment on the potential impact of this assumption on the accuracy or stability of the learned optimizer? Are there strategies to mitigate this effect?
5.  The batch normalization strategy used appears crucial for good performance. Are there specific hyperparameters for this batch normalization that are particularly sensitive, and how did you determine their optimal values?
