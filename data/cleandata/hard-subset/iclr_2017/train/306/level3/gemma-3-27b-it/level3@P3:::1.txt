This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn an optimizer for another neural network, specifically targeting the few-shot learning regime. The core idea, drawing a parallel between Robbins–Monro updates and LSTM updates, is insightful and well-motivated. The authors skillfully reuse beneficial techniques from prior work (Andrychowicz et al. 2016), such as parameter sharing and normalization, while also introducing a well-justified batch-norm implementation tailored for meta-learning. The presentation is exceptionally clear and the paper is well-written. Experimental results demonstrate competitive performance with deep metric-learning techniques, making this a strong contribution.

**Accept**

My primary reason for acceptance is the novelty and potential impact of the approach. Learning an optimizer, rather than relying on hand-designed algorithms, is a promising direction, and the LSTM formulation appears to be a viable path forward. The paper convincingly demonstrates the method’s efficacy in a challenging few-shot learning scenario. Secondly, the clarity of the presentation significantly aids understanding and appreciation of the technical details.

The approach is well-motivated. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in low-data regimes and positions meta-learning as a natural solution. The historical context provided, referencing earlier work in meta-learning (although, see concerns below), helps situate the contribution within the broader field. The connection to both Robbins–Monro updates and LSTM dynamics provides a strong theoretical underpinning. The reuse of techniques from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) is sensible and effectively builds upon existing research.

The paper supports its claims with convincing experimental results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset. The comparisons to strong baselines, including Matching Networks, are thorough, and the ablation studies, while limited (see concerns), offer some insight into the importance of the design choices. The visualization of the learned optimization strategy, though qualitative, adds to the understanding of the meta-learner’s behavior.

**Additional Feedback & Questions for the Authors:**

While I recommend acceptance, several points could strengthen the paper further.

1. **Input Redundancy:** The authors feed the loss, gradient, and parameters to the meta-learner. While this provides maximal information, it also introduces significant redundancy. I am concerned about the computational cost and potential for the meta-learner to focus on irrelevant features. *Could the authors perform ablation studies to investigate the impact of removing the parameters or the loss from the meta-learner’s input?* Does performance degrade significantly, or is the meta-learner effectively ignoring this information?

2. **Architectural Component Learning:** The paper focuses on learning an *optimization algorithm*. However, the success of the learner network also depends on its architecture (number of layers, neurons per layer, activation functions, etc.). *Do the authors believe that other architectural components could be similarly learned through a meta-learning approach?* For example, could an LSTM meta-learner be trained to predict the optimal number of layers for a given task? Any insights on the potential (or limitations) of this direction would be valuable.

3. **Related Work:** The related work section is somewhat shallow. While the recent work on metric learning and meta-learning is cited, the paper overlooks crucial earlier efforts. *I strongly recommend citing Samy Bengio’s 1989 PhD thesis and the subsequent work by Bengio et al. (1990, 1994) on learning update rules using genetic programming.* Furthermore, the significant contributions of Jürgen Schmidhuber’s research group on meta-learning in the 1990s (e.g., Schmidhuber 1992, 1993) should be acknowledged. A more comprehensive review of the literature would strengthen the paper's positioning.

4. **Batch Normalization Detail:** The described batch normalization strategy during meta-testing is good, but further clarity would be useful. *Could the authors elaborate on the specifics of how the running averages are updated – specifically, what weighting scheme is used to prioritize later values?*

5. **Computational Cost:** *Could the authors provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of training the meta-learner, compared to training a standard neural network on the same tasks?* This information would help assess the practical feasibility of the approach.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of meta-learning. Addressing the concerns outlined above would further enhance its impact.
