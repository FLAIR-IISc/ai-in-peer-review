This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn an optimization algorithm tailored for few-shot learning. The core idea is compelling: leveraging the structural similarities between gradient-based updates and LSTM cell state updates to enable rapid adaptation in low-data regimes. The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. The authors effectively build upon prior work, incorporating established techniques like parameter sharing and normalization while introducing thoughtful innovations, such as their specific batch normalization implementation. The experimental results demonstrate strong performance, competitive with state-of-the-art methods, making this a valuable contribution to the field.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to resubmit after addressing the concerns detailed below. While the paper presents a promising approach, some crucial aspects require further clarification and investigation before acceptance. The primary concern revolves around the potential redundancy of the information fed into the meta-learner, and the limited exploration of the broader applicability of this approach.

The motivation for the approach is well-articulated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of gradient-based optimization in few-shot learning scenarios and position their work within the broader context of meta-learning, highlighting the need for both rapid adaptation and transferable knowledge. The analogy drawn between Robbins-Monro updates and LSTM updates is insightful and forms a strong foundation for the proposed model. Furthermore, the reuse of techniques from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) demonstrates a solid understanding of the existing literature and a pragmatic approach to implementation. The experiments conducted on the Mini-ImageNet dataset are convincing, showing competitive results against established baselines like Matching Networks. The visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior provides valuable qualitative insight into its operation.

However, the paper lacks sufficient analysis to fully justify the complexity of the meta-learner’s input. Feeding the loss, gradient, and current parameters to the LSTM seems potentially redundant. It's unclear whether all three are necessary for optimal performance.  Ablation studies systematically removing each input component would be crucial to demonstrate the contribution of each and potentially simplify the model. This is especially important given the computational cost associated with processing this information.

Furthermore, the authors focus exclusively on learning an optimizer for a classifier. It would be valuable to understand their perspective on whether this approach could be generalized to learn other aspects of the learner network, such as its architecture (number of layers, type of neurons, etc.).  Could the meta-learning framework be extended to discover optimal network components, rather than just update rules?  What are the limitations of applying this method to components beyond the optimization algorithm itself? The authors' insights on these questions would strengthen the paper considerably.

Finally, while the related work section acknowledges relevant contributions, it feels somewhat superficial. The authors should expand this section to include earlier and more foundational work in meta-learning. Specifically, citing works like Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989) and subsequent publications (e.g., Bengio et al., 1994) on genetic programming for learning learning rules, as well as Schmidhuber’s early work on meta-learning, would demonstrate a more comprehensive understanding of the field’s history and position this work more accurately within the broader landscape.  Acknowledging this history is vital for establishing the novelty and impact of the current research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** As mentioned above, conduct ablation studies to assess the importance of each input component (loss, gradient, parameters) to the meta-learner.
*   **Generalization Discussion:** Elaborate on the potential to learn other aspects of the learner network beyond the optimizer. Discuss the challenges and opportunities associated with extending this approach.
*   **Expanded Related Work:** Include citations to earlier and foundational work in meta-learning (Bengio, Schmidhuber, etc.).
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with training and running the meta-learner.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the model's performance to different hyperparameters.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide ablation study results demonstrating the contribution of each input component (loss, gradient, parameters) to the meta-learner's performance?
2.  What are your thoughts on extending this meta-learning framework to learn other aspects of the learner network (e.g., architecture, neuron types)? What challenges do you anticipate?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training and deploying this meta-learner compared to other few-shot learning methods?
4.  Were there any specific challenges encountered in implementing the batch normalization strategy designed to prevent information leakage between datasets during meta-testing? Can you share any insights into choosing the running average weighting scheme?
