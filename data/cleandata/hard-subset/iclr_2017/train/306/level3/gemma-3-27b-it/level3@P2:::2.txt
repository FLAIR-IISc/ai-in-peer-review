This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learner to learn the optimization algorithm used to train another neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The core idea is to interpret gradient descent updates as gated recurrent cell updates, allowing the meta-learner to discover a beneficial initialization and update rule for fast adaptation to new tasks with limited data. This is a promising and original approach to few-shot learning, addressing the challenges of gradient-based optimization in low-data scenarios.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling approach to meta-learning, with clear motivation, sound methodology, and competitive results. While there are areas for improvement, the overall contribution is significant and warrants acceptance to ICLR 2017.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation behind this work is well-articulated. The paper clearly identifies the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot learning and positions the meta-learning approach as a potential solution. The analogy between gradient descent updates and recurrent cell updates is insightful and provides a solid foundation for the proposed model. The experimental results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrate that the proposed meta-learner achieves competitive performance compared to existing state-of-the-art methods, including Matching Networks. Furthermore, the paper provides practical recommendations, such as the importance of explicitly training the meta-learner for a specific number of updates, which are valuable for researchers and practitioners. The release of the code, as noted by the authors, would further strengthen the reproducibility and impact of this work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, several points could be addressed to improve clarity and strengthen the analysis. 

1. **LSTM vs. GRU:** The authors acknowledge the closer resemblance to GRUs than LSTMs. Exploring the implications of this distinction, or explicitly framing the model as a GRU-based approach, might provide a more accurate representation.

2. **Data Separation Visualization:** The description of how data is split into meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets is somewhat difficult to follow. A visual illustration (e.g., a diagram showing the class separation across the meta-sets) would greatly improve understanding.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the meta-learner's parameters, particularly the input (`it`) and forget (`ft`) gates. Examining their learned values across different datasets and tasks could provide valuable insights into the optimization strategies being discovered.  Are there consistent patterns? How do they vary based on task difficulty?  Showing examples of learned `it` and `ft` values would strengthen the paper significantly.

4. **Figure 2 Revision:** Figure 2, depicting the computational graph, currently has limited value. It could be significantly enhanced by illustrating the flow of gradients and parameters in more detail, clarifying the interaction between the meta-learner and the learner network.

5. **Minor Typo:** In Section 3.2, “it” should be “its” (“Thus as in Andrychowicz et al. (2016), we share parameters across the coordinates of the learner gradient. This means each coordinate has *its* own hidden and cell state values…”).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of LSTM architecture (specifically the number of layers and hidden units) and the sensitivity of performance to these choices?
2.  The paper mentions that the meta-learner learns a task-common initialization. Can you provide examples of how this initialization differs from random initialization, and how it contributes to faster convergence? Visualizing the weight distributions could be helpful.
3.  Regarding the gradient independence assumption (Section 3.3.1), did you experiment with including these second-order gradient terms, and if so, what were the results? What was the computational cost associated with including these terms?
4.  You state that training the meta-learner for a specific number of updates during meta-training improves performance.  Is there an optimal number of updates, and how does performance degrade if the number of updates at meta-test time differs from the number used during meta-training?
5.  The results show competitive but not drastically superior performance compared to Matching Networks. What are the key limitations of the current approach, and what directions are you exploring to further improve performance?
