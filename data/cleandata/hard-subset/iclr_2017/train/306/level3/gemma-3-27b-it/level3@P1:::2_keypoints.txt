- Decision: Accept.

- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm to train a classifier in few-shot settings by treating gradient updates as LSTM cell-state updates.

- Novel and well-motivated approach addressing few-shot learning limitations of standard gradient-based optimizers.

- Technical formulation is sound and presents a compelling framing of meta-learning the optimizer and initialization.

- Experimental evaluation on Mini-ImageNet shows competitive performance vs. state-of-the-art (e.g., Matching Networks) and superiority over simple baselines.

- Comparisons to nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines demonstrate benefits in avoiding overfitting in few-shot regimes.

- Visualization of learned gate values (it and ft) provides preliminary insight into the discovered optimization strategy.

- Authors’ code release is commendable and supports reproducibility.

- Analogy to LSTMs may be misleading since the gates behave more like GRUs; authors should acknowledge and discuss implications.

- Data split description (meta-train/val/test) and construction from class sets is unclear and would benefit from a visual illustration.

- Experimental analysis should be expanded to study distributions/sensitivities of gate values and perform hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.

- Figure 2 (computational graph) is too abstract and should be elaborated or replaced with a more informative visualization.

- Minor typo: “it” appears instead of “its” in Section 3.2.

- Question: Why choose LSTM specifically over other recurrent models (e.g., GRU), given the similarity?

- Question: Please detail differences in dataset construction and Matching Networks implementation to explain result discrepancies with Vinyals et al. (2016).

- Question: Clarify whether meta-learner fine-tuning was used during meta-testing and its impact on performance.

- Question: Provide computational cost and scalability comparisons to other few-shot methods like Matching Networks.

- Question: Explain choice of preprocessing/normalization and whether alternatives were explored beyond Andrychowicz et al. (2016).
