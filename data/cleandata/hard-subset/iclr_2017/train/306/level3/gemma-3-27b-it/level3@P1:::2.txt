## Review of "Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent"

This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learner designed to learn the optimization algorithm used to train another neural network classifier in the few-shot learning regime. The core idea is to interpret gradient descent updates as analogous to cell state updates within an LSTM, allowing the meta-learner to discover effective parameter updates and a beneficial initialization for the learner network. This is a compelling approach to addressing the challenges of learning with limited data, where standard gradient-based optimization often struggles.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach to a significant problem in machine learning – few-shot learning. While some areas could benefit from further clarification and analysis, the originality of the idea, the competitive results achieved, and the clear presentation of the method warrant acceptance. The approach tackles a core limitation of gradient-based methods in low-data settings and offers a promising path towards more effective meta-learning strategies.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for this work is clearly articulated. The paper convincingly argues that existing gradient-based optimization algorithms are not well-suited for few-shot learning due to their sensitivity to initialization and limited convergence speed with minimal examples. Framing the problem as a meta-learning challenge, where the learning *algorithm* itself is learned, provides a strong foundation. The proposed LSTM-based meta-learner is a clever and innovative solution. Representing optimization steps as gated recurrent updates is insightful and allows for the capture of both short-term and long-term dependencies crucial for effective learning.

The experimental evaluation on the Mini-ImageNet dataset demonstrates that the proposed approach achieves competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods like Matching Networks and establishes its superiority over simpler baselines. The comparison to both a nearest-neighbor baseline and a fine-tuning baseline highlights the benefits of the meta-learning strategy, particularly its ability to avoid overfitting in the few-shot setting.  The visualization of the learned gate values (it and ft) provides some initial insight into the optimization strategy discovered by the meta-learner, further strengthening the argument for its effectiveness.  The authors’ decision to release the code (as indicated in the paper) is commendable and will significantly contribute to the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the core ideas are strong, several areas could be improved with further development. The analogy to LSTMs, while insightful, seems closer in form to GRUs, as the forget and input gates are not constrained to sum to one.  The authors might benefit from acknowledging this and discussing the implications of this difference.

The description of the data separation into meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets, and how these are constructed from the underlying class sets, is somewhat difficult to follow. A visual illustration of this process (e.g., a diagram) would greatly enhance clarity. 

The experimental section could be expanded with more detailed analysis of the learned parameters within the meta-learner. Specifically, exploring the distributions and sensitivities of the input (it) and forget (ft) gate values across different datasets and tasks could provide a deeper understanding of the optimization strategy the meta-learner is employing. A sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters would also be beneficial. 

Figure 2, depicting the computational graph, has limited value in its current form. It is highly abstract and doesn’t provide much concrete information about the model's architecture or data flow. Either elaborating on the figure or replacing it with a more informative visualization would be helpful.

I noticed a minor typo in Section 3.2, where “it” appears instead of “its.”

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose an LSTM architecture specifically, as opposed to other recurrent models (e.g., GRUs)?  Acknowledging the closer similarity to GRUs and explaining the rationale behind the LSTM choice would be appreciated.
2.  In Section 5.1, you mention that the results do not exactly match Vinyals et al. (2016) due to your own dataset creation and implementation of Matching Networks. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the differences in dataset construction and implementation choices to help understand the discrepancies in performance?
3.  The paper mentions that the meta-learner can be fine-tuned using the training set during meta-testing. How did you determine whether or not to include this fine-tuning step during the experimental evaluation, and what were the observed impacts on performance?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with training and using the meta-learner, and how does this compare to other few-shot learning approaches like Matching Networks?  Understanding the scalability of this method is important.
5.  Regarding the preprocessing step described in Section 3.2, did you explore alternative normalization techniques, and why did you ultimately choose the method proposed by Andrychowicz et al. (2016)?
