## Review of "LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning"

This paper proposes an intriguing LSTM-based meta-learning framework designed to learn an optimizer tailored for another neural network, specifically targeting the challenging few-shot learning regime. The core idea of drawing a parallel between Robbins–Monro updates and LSTM updates is well-motivated and offers a compelling approach to balancing quick adaptation with slower, transferable learning. The authors leverage successful techniques from prior work like parameter sharing (Andrychowicz et al. 2016) and introduce a sensible batch-norm implementation, indicating thoughtful design choices. The experimental results presented are convincing, demonstrating competitive performance compared to deep metric-learning techniques. Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field.

I recommend **Acceptance** of this paper. The approach is novel and well-executed, and the results are promising. The clarity of the writing and presentation further strengthens the paper’s contribution. The core idea is a very clever approach to a difficult problem, and the experimental validation is a significant achievement.

However, a key aspect that needs further exploration is the information provided to the meta-learner. While the inclusion of loss, gradient, *and* parameters seems comprehensive, it raises questions about potential redundancy. I believe strong ablation studies are necessary to demonstrate the necessity of each input component; specifically, investigating performance with only loss and gradient, or even just the gradient, would provide valuable insights. 

Furthermore, the authors briefly touch upon the potential for learning other architectural components beyond the optimization algorithm. Expanding on this point – discussing the feasibility, challenges, and potential benefits of learning elements like neuron types or layer connectivity via a similar meta-learning approach – would greatly enhance the paper. What insights do the authors have regarding why this approach may be more or less suitable for these different components?

Finally, the related work section feels somewhat shallow. While key references are included, seminal contributions to meta-learning, such as Samy Bengio’s PhD thesis (1989), earlier work by Bengio et al. on genetic programming (1994), and the extensive work of Jürgen Schmidhuber on meta-learning in the 90s, are noticeably absent. A more comprehensive review of the literature would strengthen the paper's positioning within the broader research landscape.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the design choices behind the specific batch-norm implementation? What alternatives were considered, and why was this approach chosen?
*   What were the computational costs associated with training the meta-learner compared to training the learner network directly?
*   Regarding the redundancy of inputs to the meta-learner, do you anticipate that different input combinations might be beneficial for different types of tasks or network architectures?
