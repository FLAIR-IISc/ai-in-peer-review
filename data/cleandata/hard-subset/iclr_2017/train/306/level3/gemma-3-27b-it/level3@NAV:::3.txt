## Review of “LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning”

This paper presents an interesting approach to few-shot learning by formulating it as a sequential meta-learning problem. The core idea – using an LSTM meta-learner to *learn* an optimization algorithm for a base learner – is novel and well-motivated, particularly given the challenges of gradient-based optimization in low-data regimes. The connection drawn between LSTM memory cells and gradient descent updates is insightful, offering a fresh perspective on how meta-learning can be implemented. The empirical results are competitive, outperforming Matching Networks on 1- and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet, which demonstrates the potential of this approach. The practical insights provided regarding LSTM meta-learner training (initialization, weight sharing, normalization) are valuable contributions.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. While the writing could be significantly improved, the underlying idea is strong, the results are promising, and the approach represents a distinct and worthwhile direction in few-shot learning research. The novelty of framing the optimization process itself as something to be learned, combined with the competitive results, makes this work a valuable addition to the conference.

My assessment is based on addressing the three key questions: 1) The paper tackles the problem of effective learning with limited data, specifically focusing on the challenges of gradient-based optimization in few-shot scenarios. 2) The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of existing methods and builds upon prior work in meta-learning and optimization algorithms. 3) The paper supports its claims with empirical results demonstrating competitive performance on a standard benchmark dataset. The improvement in score after clarification of the meta-learning task further strengthens this conclusion.

However, the presentation needs substantial work. The task description is abstract, and a concrete example illustrating the sequence of training batches and the subsequent test batch would greatly enhance understanding. The details of the N-class setup – how classes are selected for each task and the size of the softmax output – are unclear and require elaboration. Figure 2 is uninformative and should be revised or removed. Furthermore, the discussion surrounding the visualized gate values in Figure 3 lacks sufficient insight. 

**To further strengthen this work, I request the authors address the following:**

*   Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of how weight sharing across the second-layer LSTM cells relates to the concept of a shared learning rate?
*   What is the specific class selection strategy used during meta-training and meta-testing? Are the same classes used across episodes, or are they sampled anew for each episode?
*   How does the performance of the meta-learner change with different numbers of LSTM layers or hidden unit sizes?
*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned optimization algorithm? For example, does the learned update rule resemble any well-known optimization algorithm (e.g., Adam, SGD with momentum)?
