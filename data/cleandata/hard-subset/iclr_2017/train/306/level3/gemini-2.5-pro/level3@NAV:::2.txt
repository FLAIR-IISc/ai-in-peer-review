**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and interesting meta-learning approach for few-shot learning. The core contribution is to frame the optimization process itself as a learning problem by interpreting the standard SGD update rule as a gated recurrent model, akin to an LSTM or GRU. This "meta-learner" LSTM is trained to optimize a separate "learner" network. The approach allows the model to learn not only an effective update rule tailored for a small number of gradient steps but also a beneficial parameter initialization for the learner, which is crucial for transfer learning. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves results that are competitive with, and in some cases superior to, state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for two primary reasons. First, the central idea of modeling the optimizer as a learned recurrent network is highly original and provides a significant conceptual contribution to the meta-learning literature. Second, the method is supported by strong empirical results that demonstrate its practical viability against well-established baselines.

**Supporting Arguments**

The originality of this work lies in its clever analogy between gradient-based updates and recurrent cell state updates. This reframing opens up new avenues for designing task-specific optimizers. The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated, positioning itself effectively within the context of meta-learning and few-shot learning research. The experimental evaluation is sound, with proper comparisons against a fine-tuning baseline and a strong state-of-the-art method (Matching Networks). The results validate the authors' claims, showing that their meta-learner can outperform these methods, particularly in the 5-shot setting. Furthermore, the paper provides useful practical insights, such as how to properly handle batch normalization statistics in a meta-learning context.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision:

*   **Clarity:** The description of how classes are separated into meta-training, validation, and test sets is difficult to follow. A diagram or visualization would significantly improve clarity here.
*   **Analysis:** The experimental evaluation, while showing strong performance, feels partially unsatisfying. A more in-depth analysis of the learned gate parameters (`it` and `ft`) would be highly beneficial. The current Figure 2 has limited value, and the visualizations in Figure 3 could be expanded to provide deeper intuition into the learned optimization strategy.
*   **Framing:** The model's update rule bears a stronger resemblance to a GRU than an LSTM, as the authors briefly note. It would be more precise to frame the analogy around GRUs throughout the paper.
*   **Code:** The promise to release the evaluation code is a major plus and will be a valuable contribution to the community.
*   **Typo:** There is a minor typo in Section 3.2 ("it" should be "its").

**Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned behavior of the input (`it`) and forget (`ft`) gates? For instance, how do their values correlate with the magnitude of the gradient or loss, and does this behavior differ significantly across layers of the learner network?
2.  Could you elaborate on the choice of a 2-layer LSTM for the meta-learner? Have you explored simpler architectures, and what was the impact on performance?
