This paper proposes a novel and compelling approach to few-shot learning by framing it as a sequential meta-learning problem. The core contribution is an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm for a base convolutional neural network classifier. The paper makes an insightful connection between the LSTM cell state update mechanism and the gradient descent update rule, using the LSTM’s memory to model the base learner’s parameters. This formulation allows the meta-learner to learn both a good parameter initialization and an effective update strategy tailored for rapid convergence from few examples.

I recommend that this paper be accepted. The primary reasons are the novelty of the approach and the strength of the empirical results. The formulation of learning an optimizer via an LSTM offers a distinct and promising alternative to the dominant metric-learning paradigms in few-shot learning. Furthermore, the method is shown to be highly effective, achieving results that are competitive with, and in the 5-shot case superior to, a strong contemporary method like Matching Networks on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clever formulation and convincing experimental validation. The insight that an LSTM’s architecture can be adapted to mimic and learn a gradient-based update rule is both elegant and powerful. By using a standard ConvNet classifier as the base learner, the work demonstrates that meta-learning an optimization process is a viable path to state-of-the-art performance, broadening the scope of successful few-shot learning techniques. The practical training details provided, such as weight sharing and initialization strategies, are also a valuable contribution for future work in this area.

To improve the paper for publication, I strongly urge the authors to focus on clarity and presentation. The writing is often opaque, and the description of the meta-learning task is highly abstract. A concrete, step-by-step example of a single training episode, perhaps accompanied by a diagram, would significantly improve the paper's accessibility. The plots in Figure 2 are uninformative; they should be revised to provide clearer insights into the learned optimization strategy or replaced entirely.

To finalize my assessment, I would appreciate clarification on the following:
*   Could you elaborate on the N-class few-shot setup? Specifically, how is the final softmax layer of the base learner handled when N can vary between meta-training and meta-testing, or even between episodes?
*   During meta-training, are the classes seen by the learner disjoint from those used for meta-testing? A clearer explanation of the class splits would strengthen the claims of generalization.
