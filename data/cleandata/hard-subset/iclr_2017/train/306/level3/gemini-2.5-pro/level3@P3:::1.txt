### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a novel meta-learning framework for few-shot image classification. The core contribution is an LSTM-based "meta-learner" that learns an optimization algorithm for a separate "learner" network (a standard CNN classifier). The key insight is to draw an analogy between the gradient-based update rule (θ_t = θ_{t-1} - α * ∇L) and the cell state update of an LSTM, allowing the meta-learner to dynamically compute updates for the learner's parameters. By training across a distribution of few-shot learning tasks, the meta-learner learns two crucial components: 1) a good shared initialization for the learner's parameters (represented by the LSTM's initial cell state), which provides a strong starting point for any new task, and 2) an efficient update rule that allows the learner to converge to a good solution in a small number of steps. The paper is well-written, the model is clearly presented, and the experimental results on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark demonstrate that this approach is highly competitive with state-of-the-art metric-learning methods.

### Decision and key reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents an elegant and effective approach to the challenging problem of few-shot learning. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Insightful Formulation:** The central idea of framing the optimization process itself as a sequence modeling problem, and using an LSTM to learn an update rule, is both novel and compelling. The parallel drawn between Robbins–Monro updates and LSTM cell state updates provides a principled foundation for the model. This moves beyond hand-designed optimizers like Adam or SGD and proposes a data-driven method for discovering optimizers tailored to the few-shot regime.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thoughtful Design:** The method is shown to be competitive with, and in some cases superior to, strong metric-learning baselines on a challenging benchmark. The authors demonstrate careful design by incorporating successful techniques from prior work (e.g., parameter sharing) and introducing well-motivated new ones, such as their specific handling of batch normalization statistics to prevent information leakage between meta-learning episodes.

### Supporting arguments

The paper's strengths are numerous. The model is conceptually clean and well-motivated by the goal of achieving rapid adaptation (short-term learning within a task) while simultaneously accumulating knowledge across tasks (slower, transferable learning). The writing and presentation are exceptionally clear, making the method easy to understand.

The experimental evaluation is convincing. The authors compare their model against appropriate baselines, including a standard fine-tuning approach and a nearest-neighbor classifier built on a pre-trained feature extractor, demonstrating the significant benefits of their meta-learning formulation. The comparison with Matching Networks, a state-of-the-art method, shows that this optimization-centric approach is a powerful alternative to the dominant metric-learning paradigm in this domain. The visualization of the learned gate values provides some initial insight into the learned optimization strategies, revealing that the meta-learner adopts dynamic, task-dependent update rules rather than a fixed schedule.

However, the paper could be strengthened in a few areas. First, the related work section is somewhat shallow. While it covers recent contemporary work, it largely overlooks the foundational history of meta-learning. Citing earlier efforts from the 1990s would better contextualize the paper's contribution within the long-standing goals of the field. Second, the work would benefit from ablation studies to justify the complexity of the meta-learner's input. The model is fed the learner's loss, gradient, and previous parameters, and it is not clear if all three are necessary for good performance. An analysis of simpler input configurations would provide valuable scientific insight.

Despite these minor weaknesses, the paper's core contribution is significant, and its empirical success makes it a valuable addition to the ICLR community.

### Additional feedback for improvement

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

1.  **Strengthening the Related Work Section:** I highly recommend expanding the related work section to include foundational work on learning learning rules. For instance, Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1993) and related papers (Bengio et al., 1990; 1995) explored learning local, biologically plausible update rules, and Schmidhuber (1992, 1993) investigated networks that learn to modify their own weights. Citing these would provide a richer historical context and better situate your contribution.
2.  **Ablation Study on Meta-Learner Inputs:** As mentioned above, an ablation study on the inputs to the meta-learner LSTM ([∇L, L, θ, i_{t-1}]) would be very insightful. How does the model perform with just the gradient ∇L as input? This would clarify what information the learned optimizer is leveraging and whether the added complexity is justified.
3.  **Discussion on Broader Applicability:** It would be interesting to see a brief discussion on the potential of this framework to learn not just parameter updates, but other aspects of a network. This is a powerful idea, and the authors' perspective on its future extensions would be a welcome addition.

### Questions for the authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions during the rebuttal period to clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment.

1.  The meta-learner LSTM is provided with the learner's loss, gradient, and previous parameter values at each step. Have you performed any experiments with a simpler set of inputs (e.g., using only the gradient and loss, or just the gradient)? An ablation study here would be very helpful to understand the contribution of each input component.
2.  The paper successfully learns an optimizer and a parameter initialization. Do you have any insights or opinions on whether a similar meta-learning framework could be extended to learn other architectural components of the learner, such as activation functions, network connectivity, or the number of neurons per layer?
3.  Regarding the Batch Normalization strategy: you mention using batch statistics for the training set and running averages for the test set during meta-testing. Could you clarify if the running averages are computed only on the *k*-shot training set of the current episode, or if they are carried over from the entire meta-training phase? The former seems more consistent with preventing information leakage across test episodes.
