- Proposes a novel meta-learning method that models gradient descent updates as a gated recurrent model, with an LSTM-based meta-learner optimizing a learner network for few-shot classification.  
- Key insight: the update θt = θt-1 - αt∇θt-1Lt resembles an LSTM cell-state update, enabling learning of task-specific update rules and an optimal initialization.  
- Empirical results on Mini-ImageNet show competitive performance, particularly strong in 5-shot and competitive in 1-shot settings.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; the approach is conceptually novel and valuable compared to metric learning and pre-training methods.  
- Motivation and literature placement are solid, with appropriate connections to prior work (e.g., Andrychowicz et al., Vinyals et al.).  
- Experiments are rigorous, comparing to two baselines and Matching Networks with sensible statistical analysis.  
- Evidence that the meta-learner learns task-specific update strategies is provided (gate values in Figure 3).  
- Performance gains highlight benefits of end-to-end meta-training versus fine-tuning baselines that can overfit without regularization.  
- Issue: the LSTM analogy is weaker than claimed—the model more closely resembles a GRU; this should be clarified.  
- Issue: description of meta-set partitioning (Section 2) is confusing and needs a clear visualization of dataset splitting.  
- Issue: insufficient systematic analysis of how gate parameters (it and ft) affect performance across datasets and tasks.  
- Figure 2 (computational graph) adds limited value and should be improved or removed to avoid distraction.  
- Minor typo in Section 3.2: "it" should be "its" regarding parameter sharing.  
- Suggestion: provide more detailed analysis of adaptation between 1-shot and 5-shot settings and compare learned initialization to standard schemes (Xavier, He).  
- Suggestion: authors should consider releasing code to improve reproducibility and community impact.  
- Claim about extending to a "full spectrum of settings" is promising but currently lacks concrete experimental evidence.  
- Key question: How do meta-learned initialization weights differ from standard initialization methods and how do those differences drive performance gains?  
- Key question: Can the meta-learner be extended to learn update rules for other optimizers (e.g., Adam, RMSProp) beyond gradient descent?  
- Key question: What is the computational cost of using the meta-learner during inference compared to standard fine-tuning approaches?
