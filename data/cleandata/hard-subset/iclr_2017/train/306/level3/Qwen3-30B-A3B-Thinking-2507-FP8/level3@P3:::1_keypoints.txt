- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns an optimizer for few-shot learning by using the LSTM cell state to represent learner parameters.  
- Draws an insightful parallel between Robbins–Monro stochastic approximation updates and LSTM cell updates to enable fast within-task adaptation and slower cross-task transfer.  
- Learns both a task-specific update rule and a transferable initialization for the learner network.  
- Introduces a well-motivated batch-normalization strategy to avoid information leakage during meta-testing.  
- Well-written paper with clear presentation and thoughtful design choices building on Andrychowicz et al. (2016).  
- Recommendation: Accept — paper is strong, novel, and well-executed with convincing results.  
- Addresses a meaningful problem where standard gradient methods struggle due to limited updates and poor initialization.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation: outperforms baselines on Mini-ImageNet and is competitive with Matching Networks.  
- Visualization of learned gate values offers interpretable insight into how the optimizer adapts across tasks and shot settings.  
- Practical implementation details are carefully considered and validated.  
- Suggestion: perform ablation on meta-learner inputs (loss, gradient, parameters) to test whether simpler inputs (e.g., gradients only) suffice.  
- Suggestion: discuss whether other architectural components (neurons, unit types, activation functions) could be learned via meta-learning and the associated challenges.  
- Suggestion: broaden related work to include earlier meta-learning efforts (e.g., Bengio 1989/1994, Schmidhuber 1992/1993) to contextualize the contribution historically.  
- Question: provide ablation results testing simpler input configurations to the meta-learner (e.g., gradient-only).  
- Question: can architectural components beyond the optimizer be learned via meta-learning, and what challenges would that entail?  
- Question: clarify the relationship between this work and earlier meta-learning literature by Schmidhuber and Bengio.
