

# Review of "Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel meta-learning approach for few-shot classification by framing the problem as a sequential optimization task. The authors propose an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns how to optimize a base learner (a ConvNet classifier) for few-shot learning scenarios. The key insight is that the LSTM's cell state update rule (ct = ft·ct-1 + it·c̃t) can be made to resemble gradient descent updates (θt = θt-1 - αt·∇θt-1Lt), where the LSTM's gates learn to control the learning rate (it) and parameter forgetting (ft). The meta-learner simultaneously learns an optimal initialization for the base learner and a task-specific optimization strategy. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art metric-learning approaches like Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet, while offering a distinct approach based on gradient-based optimization rather than embedding similarity. The paper also provides valuable practical insights for training LSTM meta-learners, including weight sharing strategies, initialization techniques, and normalization methods.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the meta-learning literature with a novel and well-motivated approach to few-shot learning, supported by convincing empirical results. The core insight about the relationship between LSTM memory cells and gradient descent updates is both novel and interesting, and the method delivers state-of-the-art performance in the few-shot learning domain.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Question 1: Specific problem tackled**  
The paper addresses the fundamental challenge of few-shot learning, where standard gradient-based optimization fails due to limited data. It proposes a meta-learning framework where an LSTM meta-learner learns to optimize a base classifier, effectively learning both an optimal initialization and a task-specific update rule. This directly addresses the paper's stated motivation: "a systematic way to learn a beneficial common initialization that would serve as a good point to start training."

**Question 2: Well-motivated and placed in literature**  
The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from prior work on meta-learning (Santoro et al., 2016; Andrychowicz et al., 2016) and few-shot learning (Vinyals et al., 2016). The authors effectively position their work as bridging the gap between traditional optimization algorithms and meta-learning for few-shot scenarios, with a clear justification for why gradient-based methods fail in this regime.

**Question 3: Support for claims**  
The empirical evidence is strong and convincing. The authors demonstrate competitive performance with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet for both 1-shot and 5-shot learning, with their method outperforming Matching Networks on the more challenging 5-shot setting. The ablation studies (particularly the comparison between their meta-learner and finetuning baselines) provide compelling evidence that meta-training the initialization is beneficial. The visualization of the meta-learner's gate values (Figure 3) offers interesting insights into how the learned optimization strategy varies across tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper makes a strong contribution, several presentation improvements would significantly enhance its clarity and impact:

- **Concrete example needed**: The task description is abstract. A concrete example illustrating how the meta-learner processes a 5-shot task would greatly improve accessibility. For instance, showing how the meta-learner processes the first 5 examples of each class and then makes updates before testing would clarify the sequential nature of the approach.

- **Clarify N-class setup**: The paper needs to explicitly state which classes are used during meta-training and how the softmax output size relates to the number of classes seen during meta-training. It's unclear whether the meta-learner is trained on a fixed set of classes or whether it generalizes to novel classes during meta-testing.

- **Improve Figure 2**: The current Figure 2 plots are uninformative. A better visualization showing the optimization trajectory or the relationship between meta-learner outputs and base learner performance would be valuable.

- **Clarify writing**: The paper's writing is often opaque. The connection between the LSTM cell state and gradient descent should be made more explicit in the text. For example, explaining why the meta-learner's cell state is the learner's parameters rather than the learner's loss would help readers understand the architecture.

- **Clarify batch normalization handling**: The paper states "we found that a better strategy was to collect statistics for each dataset D ∈ D during Dmeta-test, but then erase the running statistics when we consider the next dataset." This needs clearer explanation, as it's a crucial detail for reproducibility.

## Questions for Authors

1. In the N-class few-shot setup, are the classes seen during meta-training the same as those seen during meta-testing, or is the meta-learner trained to generalize to novel classes? This is critical for understanding the few-shot learning setup.

2. Could you provide more insight into why the meta-learner's input gate values (it) are harder to interpret than the forget gate values (ft)? What does the variability in input gate values across different datasets suggest about the learned optimization strategy?

3. In Figure 3, the paper states "the meta-learner seems to adopt a simple weight decay strategy." Could you elaborate on how this weight decay strategy differs from traditional weight decay methods and why it's beneficial for few-shot learning?

The paper's core contribution is strong and well-supported, and with the suggested presentation improvements, it would make a valuable contribution to the meta-learning literature. The reviewers' raised score to 8 after clarification of the meta-learning task is justified, as the paper presents a compelling approach with convincing results.
