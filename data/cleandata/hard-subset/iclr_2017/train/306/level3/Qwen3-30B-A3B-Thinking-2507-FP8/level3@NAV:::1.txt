

This paper proposes an innovative LSTM-based meta-learning framework that learns an optimizer for training neural network classifiers in few-shot learning scenarios. The authors draw a compelling parallel between Robbins-Monro stochastic optimization updates and LSTM cell state updates, enabling the meta-learner to simultaneously learn both an effective optimization algorithm and a task-common initialization for the learner network. The approach effectively addresses two key challenges in few-shot learning: the need for rapid adaptation with limited examples and the benefit of an optimal starting point for training.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The primary reasons are: (1) the paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound contribution that directly addresses a significant challenge in few-shot learning, and (2) the experimental results convincingly demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines and state-of-the-art metric learning methods on Mini-ImageNet.

The paper's strengths include a clear presentation of the core idea, effective reuse of prior techniques (like parameter sharing and normalization from Andrychowicz et al. 2016), and the introduction of a well-motivated novel design choice (the specific batch-norm implementation). The experiments are thorough and properly contextualized, with appropriate ablation studies on the number of updates and clear visualization of the meta-learner's learned behavior.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest addressing three points: (1) Conduct ablation studies to test whether simpler input representations (without feeding loss, gradient, and parameters) would suffice, as the current design might be redundant; (2) Include the authors' perspective on whether other architectural components (neurons, unit types, etc.) could be learned similarly; and (3) Expand the related work section to cite earlier meta-learning efforts, including Samy Bengio's PhD thesis (1989) and Bengio et al. (1994) on genetic programming, as well as Schmidhuber's foundational work.

I'd appreciate clarification on whether the authors have considered the potential redundancy of their input representation, and whether they believe their approach could generalize to learning other architectural components beyond the optimization algorithm. Additionally, I'm curious about their perspective on why these earlier meta-learning works were not included in the related work discussion.
