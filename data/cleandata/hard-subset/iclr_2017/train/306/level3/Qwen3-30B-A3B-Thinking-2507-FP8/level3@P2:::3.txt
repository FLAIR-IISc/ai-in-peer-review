

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses the few-shot learning problem, where a classifier must generalize quickly after seeing very few examples per class. The authors propose an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm to train a learner neural network classifier specifically for the few-shot regime. The key insight is that the LSTM's cell state can be used to represent the parameters of the learner network, with the LSTM's gates learning how to update these parameters. This approach simultaneously learns a good initialization for the learner network and an effective update rule for the specific few-shot setting. The method is evaluated on Mini-ImageNet, demonstrating competitive results with state-of-the-art metric-learning approaches like Matching Networks.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper makes a compelling contribution to few-shot learning by framing it as a meta-learning problem where the optimization algorithm itself is learned. The key insight that the LSTM cell state can be mapped to the learner's parameters, with the gates learning the update rules, is novel and well-motivated. The empirical results are convincing, showing that the proposed method outperforms baselines and is competitive with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet for both 1-shot and 5-shot learning. The paper provides practical insights for implementing LSTM meta-learners, including initialization strategies and preprocessing techniques that make the approach feasible for large neural networks.

The approach is well-placed in the literature, building on recent work in meta-learning while addressing specific limitations of gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work by focusing on learning the optimization procedure rather than just the initialization or embedding space.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the technical contributions are strong, the paper would benefit from significant improvements in clarity and presentation:

- The task description is abstract and would be greatly improved by a concrete example and diagram illustrating how the meta-learning process works with a specific few-shot example.
- The description of the N-class few-shot setup (which classes are seen during meta-training, how softmax output size is determined) is unclear and needs clarification.
- Figure 2 (the computational graph) is uninformative and lacks explanatory text that would help readers understand the key components of the model. The discussion around it provides little insight.
- The writing is often opaque; more precise language would help readers understand the relationship between the meta-learner and the learner network.
- The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why the proposed approach differs from previous meta-learning methods that also learn optimization procedures.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify the exact relationship between the meta-training classes and the classes seen during meta-testing? Specifically, how are the classes selected for each episode, and how does the model handle cases where a class appears in both meta-training and meta-testing sets?

2. The paper mentions that the meta-learner makes 12 updates for 1-shot and 5 updates for 5-shot tasks. Could you explain why the number of updates is specifically tied to the shot number in this way, and whether this was a hyperparameter that was tuned?

3. The paper acknowledges that the gradient independence assumption is a simplification. Could you provide more insight into how this assumption might affect performance, and whether you've considered alternative approaches to handle the gradient dependencies?

4. The authors state that their method is competitive with Matching Networks. Given that Matching Networks use a different approach (metric learning vs. learning optimization), could you elaborate on the specific advantages of your approach in terms of interpretability or flexibility for different few-shot scenarios?

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the few-shot learning literature. With some improvements to clarity and presentation, it would be an excellent addition to ICLR.
