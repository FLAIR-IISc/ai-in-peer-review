

This paper proposes a novel meta-learning approach that interprets SGD updates as a gated recurrent model (LSTM-based) with trainable parameters, enabling the learning of both optimal initialization and update rules for few-shot classification. The core insight—leveraging LSTM gates to dynamically control learning rates and parameter forgetting—addresses critical limitations of gradient-based optimization in small-data regimes. The method demonstrates competitive results against state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques (e.g., Matching Networks) on Mini-ImageNet, with statistically significant improvements in 5-shot settings. The paper is well-structured, clearly motivated by the gap between standard optimizers and few-shot constraints, and provides actionable recommendations for practical deployment.

**Decision: Accept (with Major Revisions)**  
The paper’s core contribution is original, well-motivated, and empirically validated. However, two critical issues require resolution before acceptance: (1) the model’s structural similarity to GRUs (not LSTMs, as gates do not sum to one), which misrepresents the analogy; and (2) insufficient analysis of key hyperparameters (e.g., *it* and *ft*), with no exploration of their impact on performance.  

**Supporting Arguments**  
The competitive results and clear problem framing justify acceptance, but the GRU-LSTM confusion undermines technical precision. The experimental evaluation lacks parameter sensitivity analysis (e.g., how *it*/*ft* thresholds affect convergence), and the data-split description in Section 2 is ambiguous without visualization. While Figure 2’s limited value is noted, the absence of parameter analysis is a more severe methodological gap. The gradient independence assumption (Section 3.3.1) is acknowledged but insufficiently defended for scientific rigor.  

**Additional Feedback**  
1. Clarify the GRU-like nature of the model (e.g., "resembles a GRU with untied gates") and justify the LSTM naming choice.  
2. Add a parameter sensitivity study (e.g., ablation on *it*/*ft* values) and visualize data splits (e.g., Figure 1).  
3. Address the minor typo in Section 3.2 ("it" → "its").  
4. **Crucially, release evaluation code**—this would significantly strengthen reproducibility and align with the paper’s practical focus.  

**Author Questions**  
1. Why does the model’s structure align more closely with GRUs than LSTMs, and how does this impact its theoretical grounding?  
2. Can you provide a sensitivity analysis of *it* and *ft* hyperparameters across tasks (e.g., 1-shot vs. 5-shot)?  
3. Would a visualization of the meta-set data separation (e.g., class distributions in Dmeta-train) clarify the methodology?
