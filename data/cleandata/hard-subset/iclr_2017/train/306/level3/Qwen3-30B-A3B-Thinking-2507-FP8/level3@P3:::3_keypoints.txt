- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that frames few-shot classification as a sequential optimization task.  
- Shows LSTM gate updates can mimic gradient descent, letting the meta-learner control learning rates and parameter forgetting.  
- Meta-learner jointly learns an optimal initialization for the base ConvNet and task-specific optimization strategies.  
- Demonstrates competitive results with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet, outperforming on 5-shot.  
- Provides practical training insights: weight sharing, initialization, and normalization strategies for LSTM meta-learners.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant, novel contribution to meta-learning with convincing empirical support.  
- Addresses the core few-shot problem by learning both initialization and per-task update rules to overcome limited data.  
- Well-motivated and well-placed in literature, bridging traditional optimization and meta-learning approaches.  
- Empirical support is strong: competitive performance, ablation studies showing benefit of meta-trained initialization, and gate-value visualizations.  
- Presentation needs a concrete example illustrating the sequential 5-shot processing to improve accessibility.  
- Paper should explicitly clarify the N-class setup and whether meta-training uses the same classes as meta-testing.  
- Figure 2 is uninformative; recommend visualizations of optimization trajectories or relationships between meta-learner outputs and base performance.  
- Writing is often opaque; clarify the LSTM cell state vs. gradient-descent parameter interpretation.  
- Batch normalization handling needs clearer explanation for reproducibility (how running stats are collected/erased across datasets).  
- Questions for authors: clarify whether meta-training classes are the same or novel at meta-test, explain interpretability of input vs. forget gate values, and elaborate how the learned weight-decay differs from traditional methods and why it helps.  
- Reviewer raised score to 8 after clarification of the meta-learning task, reflecting confidence in the paper’s contribution.
