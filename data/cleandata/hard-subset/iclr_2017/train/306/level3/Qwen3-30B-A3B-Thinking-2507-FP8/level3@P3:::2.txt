

# Review of "Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a novel meta-learning approach that interprets SGD updates as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. The authors propose an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a classifier network specifically for few-shot learning scenarios. The meta-learner simultaneously learns an optimal initialization for the classifier network and a task-specific optimization strategy that can converge rapidly with only a few training examples. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings, and the authors demonstrate competitive results against state-of-the-art metric learning methods on Mini-ImageNet. The paper makes a significant contribution to the meta-learning literature by framing optimization as a learnable process that can be adapted to specific few-shot learning constraints.

## 2. Decision
Accept.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a compelling contribution to meta-learning research with a novel approach that effectively addresses the few-shot learning problem. The core idea is original and important for transfer learning research, as it systematically learns beneficial initialization and optimization strategies rather than relying on random initialization or pre-trained networks. The experimental results are competitive with state-of-the-art metric learning techniques (Matching Networks), and the paper provides useful practical recommendations for implementation. The model structure is clearly explained, and the authors thoughtfully address important considerations like parameter sharing and batch normalization in the meta-learning setting.

The primary reason for acceptance is that the paper successfully demonstrates a feasible meta-learning approach that outperforms natural baselines and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art methods. The second key reason is the strong theoretical motivation for the approach, which clearly articulates the limitations of standard optimization algorithms in few-shot settings and positions their work as a systematic solution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
The paper would benefit from several clarifications to strengthen its contribution:

- The analogy to LSTMs is somewhat misleading, as the proposed model is structurally closer to GRUs (with gates not tied to sum to one). Clarify this distinction early in the paper to avoid confusion.

- The description of data separation in meta sets (Section 2) is difficult to follow without visual aids. Consider adding a clear figure illustrating the meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing splits.

- The experimental evaluation would be more convincing with deeper analysis of parameter sensitivity, particularly for the it and ft gates. A thorough investigation of how these parameters affect performance would strengthen the claims.

- Figure 2 (the computational graph) has limited value as it appears to be a standard LSTM diagram. Consider replacing it with a more informative visualization that illustrates the meta-learning process.

- Correct the minor typo in Section 3.2 ("it" → "its").

- Releasing the evaluation code would be a major plus for the community, as it would allow for reproducibility and further development.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you clarify why the proposed model is more LSTM-like than GRU-like, given that the gates aren't tied to sum to one? How does this model differ from a standard GRU implementation?

2. The paper states that the meta-learner is "explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing." Could you provide more detail on how this affects the training procedure and why it's necessary?

3. In Figure 3, the input gate values appear to have high variability across different datasets. Could you provide more interpretation of what this variability might indicate about the meta-learner's learning strategy?

4. The paper mentions that the fine-tuned baseline performed worse than the nearest-neighbor baseline. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, particularly with respect to overfitting in the few-shot setting?

5. The paper suggests future work should consider meta-learners that work across a spectrum of training examples and classes. What are the main technical challenges to addressing this more general scenario?
