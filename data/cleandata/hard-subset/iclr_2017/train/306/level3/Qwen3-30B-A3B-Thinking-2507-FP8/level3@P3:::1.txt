

# Review of "Learning to Learn by Gradient Descent by Gradient Descent" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contribution
This paper proposes an elegant LSTM-based meta-learning framework to learn an optimizer for another neural network, specifically addressing the few-shot learning problem. The authors draw a compelling parallel between Robbins-Monro stochastic approximation updates and LSTM cell state updates to satisfy the dual requirements of quick adaptation within a task and slower transferable learning across tasks. Their key insight is that by using the LSTM's cell state to represent the learner's parameters, the meta-learner can simultaneously learn both an effective parameter update rule tailored to the few-shot setting and a beneficial initialization for the learner network. The paper is well-written with clear presentation, and the authors effectively build on prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016) while introducing well-motivated novel design choices, particularly in their batch-normalization implementation for the meta-learning context.

## 2. Decision
Accept. This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to meta-learning, with convincing experiments and clear presentation. The core idea is both novel and well-executed, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in machine learning—few-shot learning—where traditional gradient-based optimization fails due to the constraint of limited updates and lack of good initialization. The proposed LSTM meta-learner effectively bridges this gap by learning both a task-specific optimization algorithm and a task-common initialization. The authors' parallel between Robbins-Monro updates and LSTM updates is insightful and well-justified, providing a solid theoretical foundation for their approach.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. On Mini-ImageNet, the meta-learner outperforms strong baselines (nearest-neighbor and fine-tuned models) and achieves competitive results with the state-of-the-art Matching Networks approach. The visualization of the meta-learner's learned gate values provides valuable insight into how the optimizer adapts across different tasks and shot settings. The authors also demonstrate careful consideration of practical implementation details, such as the batch-normalization strategy that prevents information leakage between different datasets during meta-testing.

The paper's strengths include its clear writing, elegant theoretical connection between optimization and LSTM, and convincing empirical results. The approach is both principled and practical, addressing a real limitation in current deep learning systems.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong overall, these suggestions would further improve it:

1. **Input Simplification Ablation**: The meta-learner receives loss, gradient, and parameters as input. An ablation study testing simpler input configurations (e.g., using only gradient information) would strengthen the argument for the current input design and help determine if the additional inputs provide meaningful improvements.

2. **Architectural Learning Scope**: The authors should comment on whether other architectural components (neurons, unit types, activation functions) could be similarly learned through meta-learning. This would provide broader context for the approach's potential and limitations.

3. **Related Work Expansion**: The related work section is somewhat shallow. The authors should cite earlier meta-learning efforts, including Samy Bengio's 1989 PhD thesis, Bengio et al. (1994) on genetic programming for learning rules, and Schmidhuber's foundational work on learning to learn (1992, 1993). This would better position their contribution within the historical context of meta-learning.

## Questions for Authors
1. The input to the meta-learner includes loss, gradient, and parameters. Could you provide results from an ablation study that tests a simpler input configuration (e.g., just gradient information) to determine if the additional inputs are necessary?

2. Beyond learning the optimizer, could other architectural components (neurons, activation functions, etc.) be similarly learned through meta-learning? If so, what would be the challenges in extending this approach?

3. How do you view the relationship between your work and the broader meta-learning literature, particularly the earlier works by Schmidhuber and Bengio that explored similar concepts in the 1990s?
