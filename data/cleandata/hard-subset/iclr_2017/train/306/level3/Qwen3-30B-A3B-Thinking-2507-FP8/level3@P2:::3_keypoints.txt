- Proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns an optimization algorithm for few-shot learning by using the LSTM cell state to represent learner network parameters.  
- Simultaneously learns a good parameter initialization and update rule; evaluated on Mini-ImageNet with competitive 1-shot and 5-shot results versus Matching Networks.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Novel framing: treats optimization itself as a learnable component, which is a compelling meta-learning contribution.  
- Key technical insight: LSTM gates can learn parameter-update rules by storing parameters in the cell state.  
- Empirical support: outperforms baselines and is competitive with state-of-the-art metric-learning approaches.  
- Provides practical implementation details (initialization strategies, preprocessing) that make LSTM meta-learners feasible for large networks.  
- Good literature placement: distinguishes itself by focusing on learned optimization rather than only initialization or embeddings.  
- Clarity issue: task description is abstract and needs a concrete example and diagram of the meta-learning process.  
- Experimental setup unclear: the N-class few-shot setup and how softmax output size is determined require clarification.  
- Figure 2 is uninformative and lacks explanatory text; needs better explanation of the computational graph.  
- Writing is often opaque; the relationship between meta-learner and learner network should be explained more precisely.  
- Need clearer explanation of how this approach differs from prior methods that also learn optimization procedures.  
- Question: clarify how meta-training classes and meta-testing classes are related, how classes are selected per episode, and handling of overlapping classes.  
- Question: explain why the meta-learner makes 12 updates for 1-shot and 5 updates for 5-shot, and whether this was tuned.  
- Question: discuss the impact of the gradient independence assumption on performance and possible alternatives.  
- Question: elaborate on specific advantages over Matching Networks in terms of interpretability or flexibility for different few-shot scenarios.
