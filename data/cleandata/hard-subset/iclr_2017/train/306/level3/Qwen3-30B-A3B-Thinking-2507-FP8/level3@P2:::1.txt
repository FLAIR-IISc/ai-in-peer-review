

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel LSTM-based meta-learner framework for few-shot learning, where the meta-learner learns an optimization algorithm to train a classifier network. The authors draw a compelling parallel between standard gradient update rules and LSTM cell state updates, leveraging this insight to design a model that learns both a task-specific optimization strategy and a beneficial initialization for the learner network. The approach is well-motivated, with clear explanations of why standard optimization fails in few-shot settings and how their meta-learning framework addresses these limitations. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on Mini-ImageNet, outperforming natural baselines and achieving competitive performance with state-of-the-art metric learning methods. The writing is clear and the presentation of ideas is exceptionally well-organized.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The core idea is novel and well-executed, and the experimental results provide strong evidence for the approach's effectiveness in few-shot learning scenarios.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a critical limitation of deep learning in few-shot scenarios. By framing the optimization process as an LSTM update, the authors create a model that learns both an effective initialization for the classifier and a task-specific optimization strategy, addressing two key challenges in few-shot learning. The experimental validation is thorough, with careful attention to the meta-learning setup and appropriate baselines. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms simple fine-tuning baselines, which is particularly important given the overfitting issues that arise with few updates.

The technical execution is strong, with thoughtful implementation details such as the specialized batch normalization procedure for meta-learning settings. The authors also effectively reuse and extend existing techniques from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) while introducing their own well-justified innovations. The results on Mini-ImageNet are convincing, with the model achieving competitive performance with Matching Networks while addressing a different aspect of few-shot learning.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions for improvement that would enhance its impact:

1. The paper could benefit from ablation studies to evaluate the necessity of feeding loss, gradient, and parameters to the meta-learner. It's unclear whether all these inputs are truly necessary, and a study comparing simpler input configurations would strengthen the design choices.

2. The paper would be more complete with a more comprehensive related work section. It would be valuable to cite earlier meta-learning efforts, such as Samy Bengio's 1989 PhD thesis on learning learning rules, Bengio et al. (1994) on genetic programming for learning rules, and Schmidhuber's foundational work on meta-learning.

3. The paper briefly mentions the potential to learn other architectural components beyond the optimizer (e.g., neuron types, layer types). A more detailed discussion of this possibility would provide interesting insights into the broader potential of the framework.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How would you compare the performance of your meta-learner when using only gradient information (without loss and parameter values) versus the full input? Would this simpler input configuration still yield competitive results?

2. In your experiments, you observed that the meta-learner learns different strategies for 1-shot versus 5-shot settings. Could you elaborate on how these strategies differ and why they are appropriate for each scenario?

3. The paper mentions that "the meta-learner could also be fine-tuned using this data" for Matching Networks. How would you incorporate this fine-tuning into your meta-learning framework, and what impact would it have on the results?

This paper makes a significant contribution to meta-learning and few-shot learning, with a well-executed approach that could inspire future work in the field. The ideas presented are novel, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are compelling. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to a wide ICLR audience.
