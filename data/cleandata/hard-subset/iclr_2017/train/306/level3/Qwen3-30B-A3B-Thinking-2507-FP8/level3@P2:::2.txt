

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper proposes an innovative meta-learning approach that interprets gradient descent updates as a gated recurrent model with trainable parameters. The authors design an LSTM-based meta-learner that simultaneously learns a beneficial initialization for the classifier network and an optimization strategy specifically tailored for the few-shot learning regime. The key insight is that standard optimization algorithms aren't designed for scenarios with a fixed number of updates, and the meta-learner can learn both a task-common initialization and an effective update rule. The paper demonstrates competitive results with state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques on the Mini-ImageNet dataset, showing significant improvements over baselines.

## 2. Decision
I recommend rejection. While the core idea is novel and well-motivated, the paper has significant issues with experimental evaluation and presentation that undermine confidence in the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is interesting and addresses a genuine limitation in few-shot learning. However, several critical issues prevent acceptance:

First, the paper's claim of using an LSTM is misleading. The model's structure (with input and forget gates that aren't tied to sum to one) is more closely aligned with GRUs than LSTMs. This fundamental mischaracterization needs to be addressed, as it affects how the model should be understood and evaluated.

Second, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient depth. The paper shows competitive results with Matching Networks but fails to provide meaningful analysis of how the meta-learner's parameters (particularly the input and forget gates) affect performance. The visualization in Figure 3 of gate values doesn't provide clear insights into the meta-learner's strategy, and the paper doesn't analyze how these gates vary across different tasks or classes.

Third, the description of the meta-set construction (Section 2) is confusing and lacks clarity. The paper states "we split the list of all classes in the data into disjoint sets" but doesn't explain the process or provide a visualization of how classes are allocated to meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets. This is critical for understanding the experimental setup.

Fourth, Figure 2 (computational graph) has limited value and doesn't significantly enhance understanding of the model. The paper could have provided a clearer visualization that better illustrates the meta-learning process.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve this paper, I recommend:

1. Clarify the LSTM vs. GRU confusion by either revising the model description to match the actual architecture or explaining why the model can still be considered LSTM-based.

2. Provide a clear visualization of the class splitting process for meta-sets, showing how classes are allocated to training, validation, and testing.

3. Conduct a more thorough analysis of the meta-learner's parameters, including ablation studies on the input and forget gates to demonstrate their importance.

4. Improve Figure 2 to better illustrate the computational graph and the flow of information between the meta-learner and the learner.

5. Fix the minor typo in Section 3.2 ("it" should be "its").

6. Release the evaluation code, which would be a major plus for the community and help verify the results.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims to use an LSTM, but the description of the gates (it and ft) suggests it's more like a GRU. Can you clarify this discrepancy and explain why the model is still called an LSTM?

2. Could you provide a clear visualization of how classes are split into meta-training, meta-validation, and meta-testing sets? The current description is confusing and doesn't adequately explain the experimental setup.

3. How did you determine the specific number of updates (12 for 1-shot, 5 for 5-shot) used during training? Was this based on empirical analysis or heuristic?

4. The paper states that the meta-learner learns a task-common initialization, but it's not clear how this differs from transfer learning. Could you elaborate on the benefits of this approach compared to standard transfer learning techniques?

While the paper presents an interesting idea with potential, the current version lacks sufficient rigor in its experimental evaluation and presentation, which prevents me from recommending acceptance. With the suggested improvements, this could become a strong contribution to the field.
