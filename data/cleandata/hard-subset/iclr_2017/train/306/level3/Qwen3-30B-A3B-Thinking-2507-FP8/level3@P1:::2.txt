

The paper proposes a novel meta-learning approach that interprets gradient descent updates as a gated recurrent model, where an LSTM-based meta-learner learns to optimize a learner neural network for few-shot classification tasks. The key insight is that the update rule θt = θt-1 - αt∇θt-1Lt resembles an LSTM cell state update, allowing the meta-learner to learn both a task-specific update rule and an optimal initialization for the learner network. The authors demonstrate competitive performance against state-of-the-art metric learning approaches on Mini-ImageNet, particularly excelling in 5-shot learning while remaining competitive in 1-shot learning.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to meta-learning by introducing a conceptually novel approach that directly learns an optimization algorithm for few-shot learning, rather than relying on pre-trained networks or metric learning. The motivation for the problem is well-justified, and the approach is well-placed within the literature of meta-learning, with appropriate connections to related work like Andrychowicz et al. (2016) and Vinyals et al. (2016).

The results are scientifically rigorous and support the paper's claims. The authors conduct thorough experiments on Mini-ImageNet, comparing against two baselines and Matching Networks, with appropriate statistical analysis. The key finding that the meta-learner learns task-specific update strategies (evidenced by the gate values in Figure 3) provides valuable insight into how the model operates. The performance gains over baselines, particularly the fine-tuned baseline that overfits due to insufficient regularization, demonstrate the benefit of end-to-end meta-training of the classifier initialization.

However, several issues need addressing before acceptance. First, the LSTM analogy is weaker than claimed; the model more closely resembles a GRU than an LSTM, as the input and forget gates aren't tied to sum to one. This should be clarified in the paper to avoid misleading readers. Second, the description of how the meta-sets are partitioned (Section 2) is confusing and would benefit from a clear visualization of the dataset splitting process. Third, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient analysis of how the gate parameters (it and ft) affect performance. The paper mentions variations between datasets but doesn't systematically analyze how these parameters are tuned for different tasks.

Figure 2, which shows the computational graph, has limited value as it doesn't clearly convey the key innovations of the model. The authors should either improve this figure or remove it to avoid distracting from the main contribution. Additionally, there's a minor typo in Section 3.2 ("it" should be "its" when referring to the parameter sharing).

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend the authors provide a more detailed analysis of how the meta-learner adapts its update strategy for different numbers of shots (1-shot vs. 5-shot) and how the learned initialization compares to standard weight initialization schemes. The paper's conclusion about extending to a "full spectrum of settings" is promising but currently lacks concrete experimental evidence.

The authors should also consider releasing their code, as this would significantly benefit the community and is a major plus for reproducibility. While the paper's core contribution is strong, these clarifications and improvements would make it even more impactful.

Key questions for the authors:
1. How do the meta-learned initialization weights differ from standard initialization methods (e.g., Xavier, He), and how do these differences contribute to the performance gains?
2. Could the meta-learner's approach be extended to learn update rules for different optimization algorithms (e.g., Adam, RMSProp) rather than just gradient descent?
3. What would be the computational cost of using the meta-learner during inference compared to standard fine-tuning approaches?
