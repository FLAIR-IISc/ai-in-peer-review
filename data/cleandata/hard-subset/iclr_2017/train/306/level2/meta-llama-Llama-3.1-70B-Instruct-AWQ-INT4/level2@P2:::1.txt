This paper proposes a novel LSTM-based meta-learner model for few-shot learning tasks. The model is trained to learn an optimization algorithm that can quickly generalize to new classification tasks with only a few examples. The authors demonstrate that their approach is competitive with state-of-the-art deep metric-learning techniques for few-shot learning.

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason being that the authors have properly substantiated their claimed contributions through thorough experiments and analysis. The paper provides a clear and well-motivated approach to addressing the challenge of few-shot learning, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have done a great job in providing a clear and concise description of their approach. The related work section is thorough, and the authors have properly positioned their work relative to previous research on meta-learning and few-shot learning.

One of the strengths of the paper is that the authors have provided a detailed analysis of the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner. The visualization of the gate values in Equation 2 provides valuable insights into how the meta-learner updates the learner during training.

However, there are a few minor issues that the authors may want to address. For example, the authors mention that they use a fixed number of updates for fine-tuning the pre-trained network in the baseline-finetune model. It would be helpful to provide more details on how this number was chosen and whether it was optimized for each task.

Additionally, the authors may want to consider providing more discussion on the limitations of their approach. For example, how does the performance of the meta-learner degrade as the number of classes increases? Are there any specific challenges or limitations that the authors encountered during the training process?

Overall, this is a well-written and well-motivated paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of few-shot learning. With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the conference.

Additional feedback:

* The authors may want to consider providing more details on the computational resources required to train the meta-learner. This would be helpful for other researchers who may want to replicate the results.
* The authors mention that they use a 2-layer LSTM for the meta-learner. It would be helpful to provide more details on why this architecture was chosen and whether other architectures were explored.
* The authors may want to consider providing more discussion on the potential applications of their approach. For example, how could this approach be used in real-world scenarios where data is limited?
