This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner model to learn the exact optimization algorithm used to train another learner neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The authors claim that their approach is competitive with deep metric-learning techniques for few-shot learning.

I recommend accepting this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper tackles a well-defined and interesting problem in the few-shot learning setting, and the proposed approach is well-motivated and grounded in the literature. Secondly, the experimental results demonstrate that the proposed approach is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of an LSTM-based meta-learner model that learns to optimize a learner neural network classifier in the few-shot regime. The authors argue that this approach is more effective than traditional gradient-based optimization methods, which require many iterative steps over many examples to perform well. The meta-learner model is trained to learn a beneficial common initialization that serves as a good starting point for fine-tuning, and the authors demonstrate that this approach is competitive with deep metric-learning techniques.

The experimental results are thorough and well-presented, with a clear comparison to baseline methods and state-of-the-art approaches. The authors also provide a visualization of the optimization strategy learned by the meta-learner, which helps to understand how the model is updating the learner during training.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of their approach, particularly in comparison to traditional gradient-based optimization methods. Additionally, the authors could provide more discussion on the potential limitations of their approach, such as the requirement for a large meta-training set.

To improve the paper, I would suggest the following:

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed approach, particularly in comparison to traditional gradient-based optimization methods.
* Discuss the potential limitations of the approach, such as the requirement for a large meta-training set.
* Consider providing additional experimental results, such as an evaluation of the approach on other datasets or a comparison to other state-of-the-art methods.
