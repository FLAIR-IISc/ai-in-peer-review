Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot N-way classification by learning an optimizer and an initialization that enable a standard neural network classifier to adapt to a new task with very few gradient steps. The key idea is to parameterize per-parameter updates via an LSTM whose cell state holds the learner’s parameters; the input and forget gates are functions of current loss, gradient, parameters, and previous gate values, enabling a learned, coordinate-wise update rule tailored to a fixed, small number of updates. The meta-objective is the loss on a held-out test set after T updates on the support set, aligning training with the few-shot evaluation protocol. The model also meta-learns an initialization for the learner’s parameters. With parameter sharing across coordinates, gradient/loss preprocessing, and careful handling of batch normalization within episodes, the approach achieves performance competitive with Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet and outperforms reasonable baselines, notably in 5-shot settings. The paper offers qualitative visualizations of the learned gates and releases code.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper offers a clear, well-motivated meta-learning formulation that learns both an optimizer and initialization specifically for few-shot adaptation, with competitive empirical results on a challenging benchmark and careful design choices to match train/test conditions.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The few-shot regime exposes weaknesses of standard gradient-based training. Framing meta-learning to optimize post-adaptation test loss directly addresses this gap. The paper is well-placed in the literature, contrasting with metric-learning (Matching Networks) and LSTM-as-optimizer work (Andrychowicz et al.) while focusing on the few-shot objective and learned initialization.
- Novelty and positioning: While LSTM-based optimizers exist, the use of the LSTM cell state to represent learner parameters, the explicit conditioning of gates on loss/gradient/parameter history, and the end-to-end objective targeting rapid generalization after a fixed number of steps in episodic few-shot tasks differentiate the work. Learning the initialization within this framework is important and distinct from prior optimizers trained on large-scale tasks.
- Technical soundness: The training procedure (unrolled updates, meta-loss on held-out query set, coordinate-wise parameter sharing, gradient/loss preprocessing) is coherent and follows prior best practices. The “gradient independence” approximation (ignoring second derivatives) is standard in related work, and the authors justify it empirically.
- Empirical support: On Mini-ImageNet, the approach is competitive with Matching Networks and stronger on 5-shot, surpassing two baselines (nearest neighbor on fixed embeddings; fine-tuning a pre-trained classifier). The protocol (class splits, many test episodes, confidence intervals) is appropriate, and the ablation that training to the same number of updates used at test time improves results supports the design. The batch-norm handling to avoid cross-episode leakage while matching training/testing conditions is thoughtful and practically important. Code availability aids reproducibility.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify the update sign: In Sec. 3.1, you map gradient descent to an LSTM with c̃t = −∇θLt but then state you set c̃t = ∇θLt. Since it and ft are sigmoid-gated (non-negative), please clarify how descent vs ascent is enforced (e.g., an explicit negative sign, or different parameterization of c̃t). A precise equation of the final update would improve clarity.
- Fairness and capacity: The learner used by the meta-learner uses 32 filters per conv layer, whereas baselines/Matching Nets use 64. Please either align architectures across methods, report parameter counts, or include a control where baselines use the same 32-filter CNN to ensure fair comparison.
- Baseline strength: The fine-tuning baseline might benefit from standard regularization for few-shot (weight decay, early stopping, LayerNorm/BatchNorm adaptation strategies). Consider adding these to ensure the gap is not due to underpowered baselines.
- Ablations: It would strengthen the paper to dissect:
  - Learned initialization vs learned optimizer: evaluate (i) learned init + standard SGD/Adam; (ii) random init + learned optimizer; (iii) both.
  - Effect of gate inputs: remove loss inputs or previous-gate inputs to test necessity.
  - Robustness to T: train with a distribution over update counts and test at different T to gauge generalization beyond a fixed number of steps.
- Generality across datasets: Adding Omniglot or tiered-ImageNet results would show broader applicability, or at least discuss expected behavior.
- Complexity and stability: Report memory/compute overhead vs SGD/Adam during meta-training (unroll length, batch sizes), and any stabilization techniques beyond gate bias initialization and gradient clipping.
- Clarify the two-layer LSTM: Explain the role of the first LSTM layer vs the modified second layer, and how inputs are batched per-coordinate. A brief schematic or more explicit equations would help.
- Batch norm details: Specify the decay/momentum used for per-episode running averages, and quantify the impact of your BN handling via an ablation.

Questions for the authors
1. Update sign and gating: Is an explicit negative sign applied to gradients in the update, or can the model produce negative effective learning rates? Please provide the exact update equation implemented.
2. Final classification layer: Since N varies across episodes, how are the final layer parameters handled? Are they included in the LSTM-updated parameter vector and re-initialized per-episode from the learned c0?
3. Coordinate-wise sharing: Are gates computed independently for each parameter coordinate (with their own hidden states), or is there any coupling across coordinates (e.g., via the first-layer LSTM)?
4. Sensitivity to number of updates: How does performance change if you run more/fewer updates at test time than the T used in meta-training?
5. Generalization across tasks: If you meta-train on 5-way tasks, how well does the learned optimizer/initialization transfer to 10-way tasks without re-meta-training?
6. Baseline tuning: What regularization (if any) was used for the fine-tuning baseline? Did you try Adam or per-parameter learning rates?
7. Failure modes: Do you observe instability or divergence for certain episodes? Any patterns in gate visualizations correlating with success/failure?

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and practically useful approach to few-shot learning that learns to optimize for rapid adaptation, with competitive results and clear potential for impact.
