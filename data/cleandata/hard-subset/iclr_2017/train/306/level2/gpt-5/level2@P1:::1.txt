Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot supervised classification by learning an optimizer that can quickly adapt a base learner to a new N-way, k-shot task with only a fixed, small number of parameter updates. The key idea is to parameterize the update rule with an LSTM whose cell state is identified with the learner’s parameters; input and forget gates produce per-coordinate learning-rate and decay-like updates as functions of the current gradient, loss, parameter value, and previous gate values. The meta-learner also learns the initialization of the learner’s parameters. Training is episodic: for each task, the meta-learner observes gradients/losses on the task’s small training set for T steps and is trained to minimize the learner’s loss on the corresponding test set. The model shares optimizer parameters across coordinates, uses gradient/loss preprocessing, and proposes a careful batch normalization strategy to avoid information leakage across episodes while matching train/test conditions. Experiments on Mini-ImageNet 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot show performance substantially better than nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and competitive with (and better on 5-shot than) Matching Networks. The paper also offers diagnostics of gate behaviors across steps and tasks.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Well-motivated meta-learning formulation that directly optimizes the objective of interest (generalization after a fixed, small number of updates), and a clear, elegant LSTM parameterization that jointly learns both an initialization and an update rule.
- Empirical results on a challenging benchmark are strong: clear improvements over standard transfer baselines and competitive to a state-of-the-art metric learning approach, with careful attention to episodic training and batch normalization.

Supporting arguments
1. Problem and motivation: The work addresses a concrete and important limitation of standard gradient-based training in low-data regimes, and it is appropriately framed within the literature on meta-learning and few-shot learning. The distinction from prior LSTM-optimizer work (Andrychowicz et al., 2016) is clear: the focus here is not a general-purpose optimizer for large-scale training, but a meta-optimizer specialized for few-shot episodic adaptation with a fixed budget of updates and a learned initialization.

2. Approach and placement in literature: The mapping between gradient descent updates and LSTM cell updates is intuitive and technically sound. The episodic objective that uses the query/test set loss after T updates aligns training with evaluation, following recent few-shot practice. The paper is positioned relative to Matching Networks and memory-augmented approaches, as well as earlier work on learned update rules.

3. Evidence and rigor: The paper reports 5-way 1-shot and 5-shot Mini-ImageNet results with confidence intervals, competitive with a reimplementation of Matching Networks and outperforming sensible baselines. The experimental setup mirrors test conditions (e.g., full-batch updates on tiny support sets), and the BN handling is thoughtfully designed to prevent leakage. The visualization of gate values provides qualitative support that the learned optimizer is not merely a fixed schedule.

4. Practicality and clarity: The method is implementable (code link provided). The role of gate initializations, gradient preprocessing, parameter sharing, and training stability is discussed. The simplifying “gradient independence” assumption (ignoring second derivatives through learner gradients) is clearly stated and justified by precedent.

Areas for improvement and questions (to help strengthen the paper; not all impact my decision)
- Ablations to isolate contributions:
  - Learned initialization vs. learned update rule: What is performance if you only learn c0 (meta-initialization) and then use vanilla SGD/Adam with tuned hyperparameters for T steps? Conversely, fixing c0 randomly but learning the gates?
  - Forget gate: What if ft is fixed to 1 (no decay/forget)? Does most of the gain come from learning per-coordinate learning rates it?
  - Preprocessing: How sensitive are results to the log/sgn preprocessing and the choice of p=10?
  - Number of LSTM layers: Is the first “regular LSTM” necessary, or does a single meta-update layer suffice?

- Capacity and fairness:
  - The learner used by the meta-optimizer has 4 conv layers with 32 filters, while Matching Networks baselines have 64 filters. Please report parameter counts and, if feasible, match capacities across methods or provide a sensitivity study to confirm conclusions are not driven by model size.

- Generalization across tasks and T:
  - How robust is the meta-optimizer to a mismatch between the number of updates seen during meta-training and used at test time? Can it generalize to variable T or N (e.g., 20-way)?
  - Does a model trained on 1-shot transfer to 5-shot (or vice versa) with minor degradation?

- Scalability:
  - Memory/time costs of per-parameter LSTM states can be high. Please quantify training and inference overhead versus standard fine-tuning and Matching Networks, and discuss applicability to larger learners (e.g., more filters or deeper networks).

- Batch normalization details:
  - Clarify the exact momentum/exponential averaging scheme “giving higher preference to later values,” and confirm no statistics are shared across episodes at meta-test time. Did you evaluate instance/layer normalization as an alternative?

- Evaluation breadth and reproducibility:
  - Results on an additional benchmark (e.g., Omniglot) or reporting 20-way accuracy would help position performance relative to prior work. Since you created new Mini-ImageNet splits, providing the exact class lists/splits and seeds would further aid reproducibility.

- Gradient independence assumption:
  - A small-scale study that compares training with and without second-derivative terms (e.g., truncated backprop through gradients) would help validate this assumption’s impact on performance.

- Analysis:
  - Beyond gate traces, could you report distributions of it and ft across layers and steps, or correlations with gradient magnitudes, to better interpret the learned strategy? Do different layers exhibit systematically different schedules?

Questions for the authors
1. What is the performance of a “learned initialization only” variant (meta-learn c0, standard optimizer for T steps), and how does it compare to the full model and to a tuned fine-tuning baseline?
2. How sensitive are results to the exact number of updates T at test time relative to the value used in meta-training?
3. Could you provide parameter counts and training/inference time comparisons for all methods?
4. Does the method retain its advantage when capacities are matched (e.g., 64 filters for the meta-learner’s CNN)?
5. Are results improved by allowing transductive test-time adaptation (e.g., using the test batch statistics for BN) under a protocol that avoids label leakage?
6. How do results change if ft is fixed to 1 or if the first LSTM layer is removed?
7. Can you share the exact Mini-ImageNet class splits to facilitate comparison?

Overall, this paper offers a clear, principled meta-learning approach with solid empirical support and good insight into practical details. With the suggested ablations and clarifications, it would make a strong contribution to ICLR.
