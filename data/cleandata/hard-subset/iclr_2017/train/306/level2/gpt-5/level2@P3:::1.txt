Summary and contributions
The paper tackles few-shot classification by learning an optimizer that is explicitly tailored to make a small, fixed number of updates on new tasks. The central idea is to cast parameter updates of a learner network as the cell-state update of an LSTM, with the learner’s parameters stored in the LSTM cell state. The meta-learner computes input and forget gates per parameter coordinate as functions of the current loss, gradient, parameter value, and previous gate value, thereby learning both a task-common initialization and a coordinate-wise update rule. Training matches test-time conditions by optimizing the learner on each episode’s training set for T steps and backpropagating through these steps to minimize the episode’s test loss. To keep the meta-learner compact, parameters are shared across coordinates with log-scaling preprocessing of losses/gradients. Practical considerations include an initialization scheme for stable training, a “gradient independence” approximation to avoid second derivatives, and an episode-safe strategy for BatchNorm statistics. Experiments on Mini-ImageNet (5-way, 1-shot and 5-shot) show performance markedly stronger than nearest-neighbor and fine-tuning baselines and competitive with, or better than, Matching Networks. Visualizations of gate values suggest the meta-learner discovers interpretable strategies (e.g., weight decay-like forget behavior) while adapting across tasks.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated, and novel formulation that unifies learned initialization and learned update rules in an LSTM framework matched to the few-step regime; (2) empirical results that are competitive with strong metric-learning baselines on a challenging benchmark, accompanied by careful practical design (BatchNorm handling, preprocessing) and release of code.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper directly addresses a core limitation of off-the-shelf optimizers in few-shot learning: they are not tuned to a fixed, small number of steps nor to rapid adaptation from limited data. Matching the meta-objective to test loss after a fixed number of updates is principled and appropriate.
- Methodology: Mapping θt updates to an LSTM cell update is elegant and sensible. The coordinate-wise parameter sharing keeps the optimizer compact and scalable across learner sizes. The gating functions use informative signals (gradient magnitude/sign, loss, prior gates), enabling adaptive step sizes and forgetting.
- Soundness: The training procedure backpropagates through unrolled optimization and uses a reasonable approximation (ignoring higher-order terms in the dependency of gradients on meta-parameters) aligned with prior work. The BatchNorm treatment explicitly avoids information leakage across episodes while keeping train/test conditions consistent—an important and often overlooked detail.
- Empirics: Results on Mini-ImageNet (with 64/16/20 class split) demonstrate clear gains over nearest-neighbor and simple fine-tuning; 5-shot performance surpasses Matching Networks, and 1-shot is competitive within overlapping confidence intervals. The finding that naïve fine-tuning overfits in 1-shot underscores the value of end-to-end meta-training of both initialization and update rule. Gate visualizations provide insight beyond raw numbers.

Suggestions to improve the paper (aimed at strengthening clarity and evidence; prioritize items 1–3 as essential)
1) Report full quantitative results (means and 95% CIs) for all methods in Table 1 directly in the text, and clarify the number of meta-test episodes used. This is essential for assessing statistical significance.
2) Ensure architectural parity across methods. The learner uses 32 filters per conv layer, while baselines/Matching Nets use 64. Either match capacities across all methods or include a control showing the meta-learner’s performance with 64 filters. This is essential for fair comparison.
3) Provide ablations isolating the roles of (a) learned initialization c0, (b) learned input/forget gates (e.g., ft fixed to 1), (c) loss/gradient preprocessing, and (d) the second LSTM layer. This would clarify which components drive gains and is essential for understanding the mechanism.
4) Strengthen the fine-tuning baseline by adding standard regularization (weight decay, dropout, data augmentation) and modest hyperparameter search on learning rates and schedules under the same T-step constraint. This would help calibrate how much of the meta-learner’s advantage is due to better regularization.
5) Analyze sensitivity to the number of updates T. The paper notes that matching T in meta-training and meta-testing helps; a small study on generalization to T′ ≠ T would broaden applicability.
6) Report computational cost: unroll length, memory footprint, wall-clock time per episode, and overall training time. This helps position the approach versus metric-learning methods in practice.
7) Discuss limits of coordinate-wise sharing. Brief experiments with per-tensor or per-layer features (e.g., layer index, parameter scale, BN statistics) could indicate whether modest context improves performance.
8) Include results on an additional standard few-shot benchmark (e.g., Omniglot) for completeness, or briefly justify focusing solely on Mini-ImageNet.

Questions for clarification
- What are the exact hidden sizes of the LSTM layers and the number of meta-learner parameters relative to the learner’s? Any signs of overfitting of the meta-learner across episodes?
- How many episodes are used for meta-training and meta-testing, and what is the variance across different random class splits?
- How sensitive is performance to the “gradient independence” approximation? Were small-scale experiments with higher-order gradients attempted?
- Does the meta-learner trained for T steps generalize gracefully to fewer/more steps at test time, or is performance brittle outside the trained horizon?
- How were Matching Networks hyperparameters tuned (including FCE and dropout), and were the same augmentation and preprocessing pipelines used across methods?
- How does performance change if BatchNorm is removed or replaced with group/layer norm within the learner?
- Are there failure modes where the forget gate tends to shrink parameters excessively (catastrophic forgetting within an episode)? Any safeguards?
- How does the approach scale to deeper learners (e.g., more filters or residual blocks)? Any observed degradation in stability or speed?

Overall, the paper presents a timely, well-argued contribution that advances meta-learning for few-shot classification with a simple and effective optimizer-as-LSTM formulation, supported by solid empirical evidence and practical insights. With the suggested clarifications and ablations, it would be a strong addition to the conference.
