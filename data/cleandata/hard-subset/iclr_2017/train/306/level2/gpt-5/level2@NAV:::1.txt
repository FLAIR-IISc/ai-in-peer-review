Summary and claims
The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a base classifier for few-shot N-way classification. The key claims are: (1) the meta-learner learns both an update rule (via LSTM gates that take gradients, loss, and parameter state as inputs) and a task-common initialization, enabling rapid convergence within a fixed, small number of steps; (2) the approach is trained end-to-end to minimize the post-adaptation test loss of each episode, aligning train/test conditions; (3) with parameter sharing across coordinates and careful preprocessing, the method is competitive with deep metric-learning baselines on Mini-ImageNet, outperforming them in 5-shot. The paper also proposes a practical per-episode BatchNorm strategy to avoid leakage.

Decision: Reject (insufficient empirical support and limited novelty)
- The empirical evaluation is too narrow and not on standard splits, weakening the strength and generality of the claims.
- The novelty relative to prior “learned optimizer” and meta-learning work is incremental and not sufficiently demonstrated by rigorous comparisons and ablations.

Supporting arguments
- Empirical scope: Results are only on a custom Mini-ImageNet split; no evaluation on widely used splits/datasets (e.g., tiered-ImageNet, CIFAR-FS, CUB, Omniglot), hindering comparability. Matching Networks are reimplemented, and fine-tuning is disabled, making the comparison potentially unfavorable.
- Missing baselines: Absent comparisons to strong few-shot learners (e.g., Prototypical Networks, Relation Networks) and gradient-based meta-learning (e.g., MAML/Meta-SGD), which target the same setting and would directly test the paper’s central claim.
- Statistical rigor: The paper references confidence intervals but does not present numbers here; reproducibility hinges on releasing the custom splits and seeds. Few ablations are provided (no analysis of learned initialization vs learned updates, gate roles, preprocessing, or BN strategy).
- Methodological concerns: The approach is specialized to a fixed update horizon and to separate 1-shot vs 5-shot models, raising questions about generality. The gradient-independence assumption removes second-order terms without empirical justification of its impact. Memory/time scaling with per-parameter LSTM states for larger backbones is not addressed.
- BatchNorm handling during meta-training uses batch statistics on test batches, which does not match meta-test conditions and risks subtle leakage/artifacts.

Additional feedback to improve (constructive, not decisive)
- Evaluate on standard benchmarks and splits; release data splits, code, and seeds. Include stronger baselines (ProtoNets, RelationNets, MAML/Meta-SGD) and report 95% CIs over many episodes.
- Provide ablations: remove learned init vs updater; fix ft=1; vary T; effect of preprocessing p; BN strategy alternatives; with/without gradient-independence (or partial second-order).
- Report wall-clock, memory, and parameter counts; test larger backbones to assess scalability; study generalization across N,k and to variable update counts.
- Consider fine-tuning Matching Nets as reported in prior work for a fair comparison.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the number of update steps T and to training the meta-learner for a different T than used at test time?
- What is the quantitative impact of the gradient-independence assumption? Did you try truncated second-order?
- Can you provide results on standard Mini-ImageNet splits and on tiered-ImageNet/CIFAR-FS with ProtoNets/MAML comparisons?
- What are the memory and time costs per episode as model/backbone size grows?
- Could you clarify the BN protocol precisely and provide ablations showing it does not inflate performance?
