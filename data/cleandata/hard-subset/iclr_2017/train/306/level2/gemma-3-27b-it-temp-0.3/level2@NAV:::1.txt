## Review of "LSTM-based Meta-Learner for Few-Shot Learning"

This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner to learn an optimization algorithm specifically tailored for few-shot learning. The core idea is to train an LSTM to predict parameter updates for a learner network, effectively learning a good initialization and a fast adaptation strategy when presented with limited data. The authors motivate this approach by highlighting the limitations of gradient-based optimization in low-data regimes and the need for a systematic way to learn a beneficial common initialization.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel and well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in the field of few-shot learning. The results demonstrate competitive performance against established methods like Matching Networks, indicating a promising direction for research.

The paper clearly articulates the problem of few-shot learning and positions the proposed method within the broader context of meta-learning. The LSTM-based meta-learner is a creative solution, leveraging the sequential nature of optimization to learn an effective update rule. The experimental setup, using the Mini-ImageNet dataset, is standard and allows for a fair comparison with existing techniques. The ablation study and visualization of the learned optimization strategy (forget and input gate values) provide valuable insights into the model's behavior. The authors also acknowledge the gradient independence assumption and its potential impact, demonstrating a responsible approach to research.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification and elaboration. The preprocessing step for gradients and losses, while referenced, could be explained in more detail regarding its impact on performance. Additionally, while the visualization of gate values is insightful, a more quantitative analysis of the learned optimization strategy would be beneficial. It would be helpful to understand *what* specifically the meta-learner is learning – are there discernible patterns in the learned update rules? Finally, the computational cost of the meta-learning process compared to other methods isn't discussed. 

I would like to ask the authors: 
1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the gradient and loss preprocessing step, and how it affects the learning dynamics of the meta-learner?
2. Are there any specific hyperparameters of the LSTM meta-learner that were particularly sensitive and required extensive tuning?
3. What is the computational overhead of the meta-training phase compared to training a standard classifier on a large dataset?
