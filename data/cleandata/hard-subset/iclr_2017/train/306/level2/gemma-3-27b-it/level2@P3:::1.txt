This paper proposes a meta-learning approach using an LSTM to learn an optimization algorithm for few-shot learning. The core idea is to train an LSTM to predict parameter updates for a learner network, effectively learning a good initialization and update strategy tailored to few-shot scenarios. The work frames the problem as learning to optimize, rather than directly learning a classifier, and presents competitive results on the Mini-ImageNet dataset.

The decision is to **reject** with a request for major revisions. While the idea of learning an optimization algorithm via meta-learning is interesting and well-motivated, the presentation lacks sufficient detail and clarity to fully evaluate the contribution and robustness of the proposed approach. The experimental validation, while showing competitive results, feels somewhat limited and the analysis is not sufficiently in-depth.

The primary concern is the lack of ablation studies and detailed analysis of the learned optimization strategy. The paper mentions visualizing the LSTM gates (Figure 3), but the interpretation provided is superficial. It would be significantly more informative to analyze which components of the input (gradient, loss, previous learning rate, etc.) are most influential in determining the updates, and whether the learned strategy genuinely resembles known optimization algorithms or represents a novel approach. Furthermore, the claim of competitiveness with Matching Networks requires a more rigorous comparison, including hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for both methods. The initialization strategy is mentioned as being beneficial, but its impact isn’t quantified – a comparison to a carefully tuned pre-trained network, as a baseline, would be valuable. 

The description of the experimental setup also lacks crucial details. Specifically, the exact data split used for Mini-ImageNet is not standard, and the implementation details of the Matching Networks baseline (specifically the convolutional network architecture and dropout rate) should be explicitly stated for reproducibility. The effect of the gradient independence assumption, mentioned in section 3.3.1, is not explored; an investigation into its impact on performance would strengthen the justification for its use. Also, the effect of the Batch Normalization strategy, and the choice of running averages, needs more justification. Why is using running averages better than not using batch statistics at all, as suggested in the text? 

Additional feedback:

*   The paper would benefit from a clearer definition of the problem setting and a more precise formulation of the meta-learning objective.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost associated with the LSTM-based meta-learner, especially compared to other meta-learning approaches.
*   Explore the potential for applying this approach to different learner architectures (e.g., ResNets) or datasets beyond Mini-ImageNet.
*   The connection between the LSTM’s cell state update and gradient descent is presented, but a deeper discussion of the analogies and differences could be insightful.
*   Consider a comparison to Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML), which is a prominent meta-learning algorithm.

Questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned optimization strategy, including examples of the updates generated by the LSTM for different inputs?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters for both the meta-learner and the learner networks?
*   What is the computational overhead of the LSTM-based meta-learner compared to training a standard neural network from scratch?
*   How does the performance of the method change when the gradient independence assumption is removed?
*   What is the impact of different Batch Normalization strategies on the overall performance?
