- Tackles few-shot learning by using an LSTM-based meta-learner to learn both initialization and update rules for a learner network.  
- Framing optimization as a sequence learning problem solvable by an LSTM is novel and well-motivated.  
- Recommendation: Reject.  
- Results are competitive but not clearly superior to metric-learning methods like Matching Networks.  
- Paper lacks sufficient analysis to justify the complexity of the LSTM-based meta-learner.  
- Experimental setup (custom Mini-Imagenet and reimplementation of Matching Networks) introduces potential inconsistencies that weaken comparisons.  
- Visualizations of the meta-learner are interesting but do not convincingly demonstrate a superior learned optimization strategy.  
- Reported confidence intervals suggest improvements may not be statistically significant compared to Matching Networks.  
- Using the entire training set at each update step is a restrictive design choice with unclear justification and potential performance drawbacks.  
- The gradient independence assumption is used but not adequately discussed; implications and remedies are missing.  
- Discussion of batch normalization is confusing and needs simplification.  
- Needs ablation studies to separate effects of learning initialization versus learning the update rule.  
- Should explore hyperparameter sensitivity (learning rate, LSTM size, etc.) to assess robustness.  
- Should compare to more recent and stronger few-shot methods beyond Matching Networks.  
- Requires error analysis and a discussion of computational cost to justify added complexity.  
- Must clarify Mini-Imagenet construction details and ideally release code for reproducibility.  
- Questions for authors include sensitivity to LSTM size, impact of gradient independence assumption, rationale for full-set updates, requests for more thorough statistical analysis, and limitations when task distributions differ between meta-training and meta-testing.
