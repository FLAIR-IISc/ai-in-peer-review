This paper tackles the problem of few-shot learning, specifically addressing the challenges of gradient-based optimization in scenarios with limited data. The authors propose a meta-learning approach using an LSTM-based meta-learner to learn the optimization algorithm itself for a learner network. This meta-learner aims to discover both a beneficial initialization for the learner and an effective update strategy for quick convergence with few examples. The core idea – framing the optimization process as a sequence learning problem solvable by an LSTM – is novel and well-motivated.

**Reject**

While the approach is interesting and the motivation is sound, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advantage over existing methods, particularly metric-learning techniques like Matching Networks. The results, while competitive, are not clearly superior, and the analysis lacks the depth needed to justify the complexity of the proposed LSTM-based meta-learner. The fact that performance is only "competitive" despite the intricate design raises concerns about the practical benefits of this approach.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The introduction effectively establishes the problem and motivates the use of meta-learning. The description of the model is detailed, and the connection to LSTM update rules is cleverly explained. However, the evaluation section feels somewhat cursory. The experimental setup, using a created version of Mini-Imagenet and reimplementing Matching Networks, introduces potential inconsistencies and makes direct comparison difficult. The authors acknowledge this regarding Matching Networks, but it still weakens the claims. The visualization of the meta-learner’s behavior, while insightful, doesn’t offer strong evidence of a superior learned optimization strategy. The reported confidence intervals also warrant closer examination; a closer intersection with Matching Networks suggests the improvement might not be statistically significant. The choice of using the entire training set in each update step feels restrictive and potentially hinders performance. It's not clear why this decision was made, and it could be a bottleneck for the model. The gradient independence assumption, while common in similar work, should be discussed more thoroughly – what are the potential implications of ignoring these dependencies, and how might they be addressed? Finally, the discussion of batch normalization is convoluted and could benefit from simplification.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be extremely helpful to include ablation studies to dissect the contribution of different components of the meta-learner. For example, what is the impact of learning the initial parameters versus just learning the update rule?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the meta-learner to various hyperparameters (learning rate, LSTM size, etc.). This will provide insight into the robustness of the method.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** Include a comparison to more recent and robust few-shot learning techniques beyond Matching Networks.
*   **Justification for Design Choices:** Provide a more detailed rationale for key design choices, such as the use of the entire training set in each update step and the gradient independence assumption.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct an error analysis to understand the types of examples where the meta-learner performs well and where it fails.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of training the meta-learner compared to other methods. Is the increased complexity justified by the performance gains?
*   **Clarify Dataset Details:** Provide precise details on the construction of the Mini-Imagenet dataset used, and ideally release the code used for this process.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How sensitive is the performance of the meta-learner to the size of the LSTM?
*   What is the impact of the gradient independence assumption on the learned optimization strategy? Have you experimented with methods to approximate the dependencies?
*   Why was the decision made to use the entire training set in each update step, and how does this affect the learned optimization algorithm?
*   Can you provide more detailed statistical analysis of the results, including confidence intervals and p-values, to support the claim of competitiveness with Matching Networks?
*   Could you comment on the potential limitations of this approach when dealing with drastically different task distributions during meta-testing compared to meta-training?
