This paper presents a novel and compelling meta-learning approach for few-shot classification. The core contribution is an LSTM-based model, termed a "meta-learner," which learns an optimization algorithm for a separate "learner" classifier network. By drawing an elegant analogy between the gradient descent update rule and an LSTM's cell state update, the model learns not only a dynamic, task-specific update mechanism but also an optimal set of initial weights for the learner. This dual approach is designed to enable rapid convergence on new classification tasks when presented with very few labeled examples.

I recommend this paper for **Acceptance**. The primary reasons are its significant novelty and strong empirical validation.
1.  The conceptual framing of few-shot learning as a problem of "learning an optimizer" is a highly innovative contribution. It moves beyond the dominant paradigm of metric learning and directly addresses two fundamental challenges of few-shot optimization: poor initialization and the unsuitability of generic optimizers for a small, fixed number of updates.
2.  The experimental results on the Mini-ImageNet benchmark are strong. The proposed model substantially outperforms well-conceived baselines, including a fine-tuning approach, and demonstrates performance competitive with Matching Networks, a state-of-the-art method. This validates the practical utility of the proposed architecture.

The paper is well-written and clearly situated within the relevant literature, properly distinguishing its contributions from prior work on learning optimizers for large-scale tasks. The model details, including parameter sharing and input preprocessing, are thoroughly explained. The visualization of the learned gate values provides a valuable, albeit preliminary, insight into the learned optimization strategy.

For improvement, the following points could be considered:
*   The discussion on the "gradient independence assumption" is brief. While justified by prior work and practical success, a short ablation or discussion on its potential impact would strengthen the paper.
*   The batch normalization strategy for the meta-learning setting is crucial and non-trivial. An ablation study demonstrating its importance relative to simpler strategies would be highly informative.
*   The conclusion mentions extending the work to a wider spectrum of settings (more classes/examples). It would be interesting to see a brief discussion on the anticipated challenges, such as the scalability of the meta-learner.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:
1.  The number of update steps (T) was set differently for the 1-shot and 5-shot experiments. How sensitive is the model's performance to this hyperparameter, and what was the process for selecting these values?
2.  Could you provide some insight into the computational overhead of training the meta-learner compared to training a metric-learning model like Matching Networks on the same meta-training dataset?
