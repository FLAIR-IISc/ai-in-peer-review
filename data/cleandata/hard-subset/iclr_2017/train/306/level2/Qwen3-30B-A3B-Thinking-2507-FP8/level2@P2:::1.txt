

Summary: The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner that learns to optimize a classifier neural network specifically for few-shot learning tasks. The authors frame optimization as an LSTM update rule, allowing the meta-learner to learn both a good initialization for the classifier and an effective update strategy for the few-shot setting. The approach is evaluated on Mini-ImageNet, showing competitive results with baseline methods and state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques.

Decision: Reject. The paper has significant issues with experimental validation and proper positioning in the literature that undermine the main claims.

Supporting arguments: The primary issue is the lack of fair comparison with prior work due to the authors creating their own Mini-ImageNet dataset rather than using the standard benchmark from Vinyals et al. (2016). The authors acknowledge this in the paper ("It is interesting to note that the numbers do not match the ones provided by Vinyals et al. (2016) simply because we created our version of the dataset"), yet they still claim their method is "competitive with deep metric-learning techniques for few-shot learning." This creates a fundamental problem with the empirical validation of their main claim.

Second, the paper fails to properly position itself against the most relevant recent work. The paper was submitted to ICLR 2017, but fails to cite or compare with Finn et al. (2017) "Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning for Fast Adaptation of Deep Networks" (MAML), which was published in ICML 2017 (though the paper was submitted before ICML 2017). MAML addresses the exact problem of few-shot learning through a meta-learning approach, and would have been a crucial benchmark for the authors' work. The omission of this critical recent work severely weakens the paper's contribution claim.

Third, the paper's claim about learning a "task-common initialization" lacks proper validation. The Baseline-finetune comparison uses a pre-trained network (which already provides a good initialization), making the comparison to the meta-learned initialization unfair. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the meta-learned initialization outperforms standard transfer learning approaches, which is the main benefit they claim.

The paper also makes a strong claim about 5-shot performance ("we are able to do much better than Matching Networks"), but fails to provide the actual numbers or statistical significance testing for this claim. The 1-shot results show overlapping confidence intervals with Matching Networks, which undermines the claim of being competitive.

Additional feedback: To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Re-run experiments on the standard Mini-ImageNet benchmark used by Vinyals et al. (2016) to enable fair comparison.

2. Include and compare against MAML (Finn et al., 2017), which is the most relevant recent work in the area.

3. Provide clear statistical significance testing for all performance claims.

4. Re-evaluate the comparison to baselines to ensure the meta-learned initialization is compared against proper baselines (not just random initialization).

5. Clarify the 5-shot results with actual numbers and statistical analysis.

Questions for authors: 
1. How do you justify claiming your method is "competitive with Matching Networks" when the results are not directly comparable due to different dataset splits?

2. Why was MAML (Finn et al., 2017) not included in the related work or comparison, given it addresses the same problem with a similar approach?

3. Can you provide evidence that the meta-learned initialization outperforms standard transfer learning approaches (not just random initialization)?

4. What is the statistical significance of the performance difference between your method and Matching Networks in the 5-shot setting?

5. How does your approach differ from the recently proposed MAML method, and why would you expect your method to outperform MAML in the few-shot learning setting?
