

This paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner model that learns an optimization algorithm specifically designed for few-shot learning scenarios. The key claim is that the model can learn both appropriate parameter updates for a fixed number of training steps and a beneficial initialization for the learner network, outperforming baselines and achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art metric-learning techniques.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses a significant challenge in few-shot learning where gradient-based optimization struggles with limited data. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of existing optimization algorithms and transfer learning methods, and the LSTM meta-learner formulation cleverly borrows from the cell state update mechanism in LSTMs. The experimental results on Mini-ImageNet demonstrate that the method significantly outperforms two baselines and achieves competitive results with Matching Networks, particularly for 5-shot learning.

The support for claims is generally strong, with appropriate experimental design using standard few-shot learning metrics. The paper provides sufficient technical details for reproducibility, including model architecture, training procedures, and hyperparameters. However, the comparison to Matching Networks could be more thorough, as the authors acknowledge their dataset split differs from Vinyals et al. (2016), potentially affecting direct comparison.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1) Providing more detailed analysis of why the method performs better than baselines, especially regarding the overfitting issue in the fine-tuning baseline
2) Including more comprehensive comparisons with recent few-shot learning methods beyond Matching Networks
3) Clarifying the relationship between the meta-learner's learned optimization strategy and its performance on different tasks
4) Discussing potential limitations in scaling to larger datasets or more complex tasks

I would appreciate clarification on whether the authors have tested their approach on other few-shot learning benchmarks beyond Mini-ImageNet, and whether the learned optimization strategy generalizes across different network architectures. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the meta-learning literature, and with these minor improvements, it would be a strong addition to the conference proceedings.
