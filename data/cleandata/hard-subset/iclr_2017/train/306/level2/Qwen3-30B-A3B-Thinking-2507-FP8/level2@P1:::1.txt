

Review:

The paper proposes an LSTM-based meta-learner model that learns the exact optimization algorithm for training a classifier in few-shot learning scenarios. The key insight is recognizing that gradient descent updates resemble LSTM cell state updates, allowing the authors to use an LSTM to learn both the optimization algorithm and a beneficial initialization for the learner network. This approach is well-motivated by the limitations of standard gradient-based optimization in few-shot settings, where both the optimization algorithm and initialization are suboptimal. The authors demonstrate competitive performance against baselines and Matching Networks on Mini-ImageNet.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to meta-learning by specifically addressing the few-shot learning problem through an LSTM-based meta-learner. The motivation is strong, clearly explaining why standard optimization algorithms fail in few-shot settings and how the proposed approach addresses these limitations. The connection between gradient descent updates and LSTM cell states is insightful and well-explained. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with appropriate baselines and a clear comparison to Matching Networks. The visualization of the learned optimization strategy (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into how the meta-learner adapts to different tasks.

The main strength of the paper is its novel application of meta-learning to few-shot learning through an LSTM-based optimization algorithm, which is well-placed within the literature. The authors effectively distinguish their work from Andrychowicz et al. (2016) by focusing on the few-shot setting rather than large-scale classification, which is a significant contribution.

For improvement, the authors should:
1) Provide more detailed comparison to recent state-of-the-art few-shot learning methods beyond Matching Networks (e.g., Prototypical Networks, Relation Networks, or MAML). The current comparison is limited to two baselines and one metric-learning method.
2) Clarify the exact differences between their approach and Andrychowicz et al. (2016), particularly why their method is better suited for few-shot learning rather than general optimization.
3) Provide more details about their Mini-ImageNet dataset construction, including how it differs from the version used by Vinyals et al. (2016) and why this matters for their results.

The paper would benefit from addressing these points to strengthen its contribution and positioning within the literature.

Questions for the authors:
1) How would your approach compare to more recent few-shot learning methods like Prototypical Networks (Snell et al., 2017) or Relation Networks (Sung et al., 2017), which have achieved state-of-the-art results since the time of this paper?
2) In Figure 3, the authors note that "input gate values are harder to interpret." Could you provide more specific insights into how the meta-learner uses the input gate to adapt to different tasks, particularly for the 1-shot vs. 5-shot settings?
3) The paper mentions that "better performance for each task was attained if the meta-learner is explicitly trained to do the set number of updates during meta-training that will be used during meta-testing." Could you elaborate on why this specific training strategy is necessary and whether the meta-learner could generalize to different numbers of updates without retraining?
