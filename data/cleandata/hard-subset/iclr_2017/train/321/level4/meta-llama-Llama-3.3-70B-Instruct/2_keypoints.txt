- Paper proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks with an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to learn diverse sub-policies.  
- Main result: combining intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards (strong baseline) fails to solve tasks, while the hierarchical approach succeeds, implicating long-term credit assignment and hierarchical abstraction.  
- Alternative explanation: baseline may lack the explicit diversity requirement during sub-policy training, which could explain its poor performance and suggests ways to improve baselines or design end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Visualizations provided by the paper are commendable.  
- Concern: diversification capacity of sub-policies is limited by the concatenation and bilinear integration mechanisms, which may be insufficient for tasks needing highly diverse sub-policies (e.g., manipulation).  
- Suggestion: evaluate method on more challenging, non-locomotion domains to test diversification limits.  
- Limitation: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time scales, forcing reliance on highly effective intrinsic rewards and learned sub-policies.  
- Suggestion: discuss and present results addressing end-to-end hierarchical policy learning and variable sub-policy durations.  
- Concern: intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific or effectively supervised, which may be unavoidable but should be acknowledged.  
- Meta: reviewer raised score from 6 to 7.  
- Questions needing clarification: why the strong baseline in Sec.7.3 performs poorly, and whether the "CoM maze reward" is that baseline.  
- Questions needing clarification: does the baseline account for the extra experience pre-trained policies received?  
- Request clarification: distinction between multi-policy and stochastic neural networks (SNNs).  
- Clarification sought on Fig.1: whether concatenation means different biases per categorical value and bilinear integration means different weight matrices connecting observations to the first hidden layer.
