The paper introduces a hierarchical reinforcement learning method leveraging stochastic neural networks, specifically incorporating an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as a reward to encourage a diverse set of sub-policies. A key strength is the comparison to a strong baseline that combines intrinsic and sparse rewards, demonstrating that simply smoothing the reward signal is insufficient for solving these tasks. While the authors attribute this to challenges in long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, an alternative explanation could be the diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training – a feature absent in the baseline. Exploring this difference could offer valuable insights for improving existing baselines and informing the development of end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, such as hierarchical REPS or option-critic methods. The visualizations are well-executed and informative.

This work represents a promising research direction, and could be further strengthened by considering the following points:

1) **Sub-policy Diversity:** The current concatenation and bilinear integration methods offer limited differentiation between sub-policies, relying primarily on adjustments to the first hidden layer weights. While this is sufficient for the tested locomotion tasks, which require relatively similar policies, this limitation may become more apparent in domains demanding more diverse sub-policies – for example, manipulation tasks where the optimal approach can vary significantly depending on the initial state. Evaluating the method on such harder, non-locomotion domains would be insightful.

2) **Hierarchical Policy Limitations:** The manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy durations. This necessitates highly effective, pre-trained sub-policies and intrinsic rewards to successfully solve downstream tasks. Further discussion and/or results addressing scenarios where this isn’t the case, and potentially connecting to end-to-end hierarchical learning, would be beneficial.

3) **Intrinsic Reward Dependence:** The reliance on intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appears somewhat domain-specific, potentially stemming from the fixed sub-policy constraint described above.
