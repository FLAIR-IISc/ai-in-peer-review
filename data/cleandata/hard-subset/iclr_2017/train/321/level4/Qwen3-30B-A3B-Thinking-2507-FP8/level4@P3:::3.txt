

**Summary of Contribution**

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), then leverages these skills for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach combines intrinsic motivation with hierarchical RL, using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn interpretable, multi-modal skills. The authors demonstrate that their method significantly improves exploration and learning performance across a range of challenging tasks with sparse rewards, including locomotion and maze navigation.

**Decision**

Reject. While the paper presents an interesting approach with promising results on specific tasks, the technical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the novelty claims are overstated. The key reasons for rejection are: (1) the paper lacks evidence that the approach generalizes to more complex embodiments, and (2) the claim that the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge" is unsubstantiated and misleading.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution—using SNNs with an information-theoretic regularizer for skill learning—shows promise on the Swimmer robot. However, the paper fails to adequately address the limitations of the approach when applied to more complex embodiments. While the authors briefly mention testing on a 5-link "Snake" robot, the results are limited and do not convincingly demonstrate robustness to increased embodiment complexity. The paper would benefit from a thorough analysis of the method's performance on robots with higher-dimensional action spaces and more intricate dynamics, including a discussion of failure modes as embodiment complexity increases.

The paper's claim that "the design of which requires very minimal domain knowledge" is problematic. The proxy rewards used (e.g., speed for mobile robots, successful grasping for manipulators) are not as generic as claimed. These rewards are specifically designed for the locomotion and manipulation tasks studied and would require substantial domain knowledge to adapt to other tasks. For instance, the paper does not explain how the proxy reward would be designed for a humanoid walking on uneven terrain. The comparison with Heess et al. (2016) is also inaccurate—their goal-based rewards could be adapted to various tasks, while the authors' proxy rewards appear equally task-specific.

The analysis of the information-theoretic regularizer (Section 5.3) is insufficient. The authors discretize the state space for calculating mutual information but provide no justification for this discretization or analysis of its sensitivity. The paper would be stronger with a detailed discussion of how the discretization parameters affect the results.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

1. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the method's limitations. Include a detailed discussion of failure modes and how the approach might be extended to handle more complex embodiments.

2. Revise the comparison with Heess et al. (2016) to acknowledge the similarities in their approaches and clarify the specific advantages of the SNN approach.

3. Provide concrete examples of how the proxy reward would be designed for new tasks without substantial domain knowledge. Explain precisely what "very minimal domain knowledge" entails.

4. Include a thorough analysis of the discretization in Section 5.3. Explain why discretization is necessary, provide sensitivity analysis to discretization parameters, and discuss what would happen if discretization were not used.

**Questions for Authors**

1. In Section 5.3, the authors discretize the state space for calculating mutual information. What is the rationale for this discretization, and how sensitive are the results to the discretization parameters? If the state space were not discretized, what would happen to the skill diversity and learning performance?

2. The paper claims the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge." Can the authors provide a concrete example of how the proxy reward would be designed for a completely different task (e.g., a humanoid walking on uneven terrain) without substantial domain knowledge?

3. The paper tests on a 5-link "Snake" robot but provides limited analysis of how the method scales to more complex embodiments. How does performance change as robot complexity increases, and what specific challenges arise with more complex embodiments?

4. The paper's comparison with Heess et al. (2016) claims the approach is more general because it doesn't require specifying goals. However, the proxy rewards used appear equally task-specific. How does the authors' approach truly differ in terms of generality, and what specific advantages does it offer over goal-based methods?
