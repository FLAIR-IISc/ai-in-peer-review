

I appreciate the problem formulation in this paper but have significant concerns regarding its technical claims and experimental scope. My specific criticisms are:

(1) What are the failure modes of this approach? As the complexity of behaviors increases, I expect this method to struggle with discovering diverse skills due to its current design.

(2) In Section 5.3, the statement "let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated" implies state discretization. Is the state space discretized? If so, what is the rationale for this choice, and how would the approach perform without discretization?

(3) Regarding the first point, does the method generalize to more complex embodiments? For instance, would it work with a 5-link swimmer instead of the 2-link model used? This assessment is critical to evaluate the approach's broader applicability.

(4) The authors claim: "Recently, Heess et al. (2016) have independently proposed to learn a range of skills in a pre-training environment that will be useful for the downstream tasks, which is similar to our framework. However, their pre-training setup requires a set of goals to be specified. In comparison, we use intrinsic rewards as the only signal to the agent during the pre-training phase, the construction of which only requires very minimal domain knowledge." I disagree with this characterization. The intrinsic rewards proposed in this work are not domain-agnostic but rather specifically engineered for a relatively narrow set of control tasks.
