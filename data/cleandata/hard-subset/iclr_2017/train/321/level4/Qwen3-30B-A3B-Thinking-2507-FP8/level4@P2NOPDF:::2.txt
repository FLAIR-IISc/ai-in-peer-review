The paper introduces a novel approach for hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) using stochastic neural networks, leveraging an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward to encourage diversity in sub-policy learning. A key strength is the compelling comparison against a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards; this baseline fails to solve tasks despite its "smooth" reward structure. Beyond the authors' arguments regarding long-term credit assignment and hierarchical abstraction, the failure likely stems from the baseline's lack of explicit diversity enforcement in sub-policy training—a critical distinction. This observation suggests potential avenues for improving existing baselines and advancing end-to-end hierarchical policy learning (e.g., extending concepts from hierarchical REPS or option-critic frameworks). The paper presents a promising direction, and its impact could be further enhanced by addressing the following points:

1.  **Limited Sub-Policy Diversification:** The proposed integration methods (concatenation and bilinear) constrain sub-policy expressiveness primarily through the first hidden layer's weights. While this suffices for the locomotion tasks tested (where sub-policies require minimal differentiation), it may prove inadequate for domains demanding highly diverse sub-policies. For instance, in manipulation tasks, solving a problem from one state (e.g., object grasp) often necessitates fundamentally different sub-policies than from another state (e.g., object placement). The authors should demonstrate the method on non-locomotion domains where such diversity is essential, as the current scope limits the perceived generality of the approach.

2.  **Rigidity in Hierarchical Policy Structure:** The manager network is trained while sub-policies remain fixed, and sub-policy durations are predetermined. This creates a significant dependency: the intrinsic rewards and pre-trained sub-policies must be exceptionally well-suited to downstream tasks for the manager to succeed. The paper lacks discussion or results on handling scenarios where this condition isn't met. Exploring integration with end-to-end hierarchical learning methods (e.g., jointly optimizing manager and sub-policies) would strengthen the framework's applicability and address a key limitation compared to approaches like hierarchical REPS.

3.  **Intrinsic Reward Dependency:** The reliance on pre-trained sub-policies (point 2) inherently makes the intrinsic reward *domain-specific* and effectively supervised, as it assumes the pre-training has captured task-relevant sub-behaviors. This is a direct consequence of the fixed sub-policy constraint. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss its implications for broader applicability beyond the tested domains.

**Clarification Requests (for Authors):**
*   The strong baseline in Section 7.3 (CoM Maze) fails completely. Could the authors elaborate on the *intuition* for this failure? Specifically, why does the combination of sparse reward and intrinsic reward (which appears smooth) fail where the proposed method succeeds? Does the baseline benefit from the same pre-training experience as the method under comparison?
*   What is the precise definition of "multi-policy" in the context of the baseline comparison? How does it differ from the SNN-based sub-policy approach?
*   Regarding Figure 1: Is it correct that (a) concatenation assigns distinct biases to the first hidden layer based on the categorical option value, and (b) bilinear integration employs distinct weight matrices connecting the observation to the first hidden layer?

The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing diversity as a key objective in hierarchical RL and providing strong empirical evidence for its necessity. The well-executed visualizations effectively support the core claims. Addressing the points above, particularly the need for evaluation on more diverse tasks and discussion of the fixed-sub-policy constraint, would significantly bolster the paper's impact and generalizability. The revised score reflects the paper's strong foundation and promising direction, with clear pathways for enhancement.
