The paper introduces a method for hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks, leveraging an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward to promote diverse sub-policy learning. A key strength is the comparison with a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this "smooth" reward approach fails to solve tasks—likely due to the absence of the diversity requirement in sub-policy training, which the authors' argument on long-term credit assignment and hierarchical abstraction does not fully address. This observation may inform improvements to baselines and new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning frameworks (e.g., hierarchical REPS or option-critic). The visualizations are also well-executed.  

The paper presents a promising direction and could be strengthened by addressing the following:  
1) **Limited sub-policy diversification**: Both concatenation and bilinear integration permit minimal sub-policy differentiation (via the first hidden weight), which is acceptable for locomotion tasks requiring similar policies but may fail in domains demanding highly diverse sub-policies (e.g., manipulation tasks where solutions from different states vary significantly).  
2) **Hierarchical policy limitations**: The manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time steps, necessitating highly effective intrinsic rewards and pre-trained sub-policies for downstream tasks. More discussion or results on handling this, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical learning, would be valuable.  
3) **Domain-specificity of rewards**: Due to (2), intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific, resembling supervised rewards.
