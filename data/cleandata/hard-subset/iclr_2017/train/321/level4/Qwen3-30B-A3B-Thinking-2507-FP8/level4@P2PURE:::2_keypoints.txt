- Reviewer raised score from 6 to 7.  
- Paper proposes hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic reward to promote option identifiability/diversity.  
- Strong empirical comparison shows a baseline that smoothly mixes intrinsic and sparse rewards fails to solve tasks.  
- Alternative explanation: baseline may fail because it lacks an explicit diversity requirement during sub-policy training.  
- Suggests this insight could improve baselines and inspire end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Visualizations in the paper are effective.  

- Limited sub-policy diversification: concatenation and bilinear integration only vary first-layer weights/biases, limiting policy diversity—could fail on tasks needing fundamentally different sub-policies (e.g., manipulation).  
- Constraints on hierarchical learning: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed durations, making success hinge on pre-trained intrinsic rewards and sub-policies; lacks end-to-end learning.  
- Intrinsic rewards are domain-specific due to the above constraints and may require supervised design for best performance.  

- Clarification requested: why the CoM maze/smooth-reward baseline performs poorly, and whether it accounts for extra experience from pre-trained policies.  
- Clarification requested: what exactly distinguishes "multi-policy" from "SNN" in the experiments.  
- Clarification requested: confirm implementation details in Fig.1—(1) concatenation uses distinct biases per categorical code in first hidden layer, and (2) bilinear integration uses distinct weight matrices from observations to first hidden layer.
