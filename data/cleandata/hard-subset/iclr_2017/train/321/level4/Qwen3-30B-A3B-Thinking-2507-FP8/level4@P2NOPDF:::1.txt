

This paper presents an interesting contribution to hierarchical control, building on concepts from Heess et al. The experimental results are robust, successfully completing benchmarks that prior work failed to achieve. However, the analysis of these results is relatively limited.  

(1) The term "intrinsic motivation" is used inappropriately, as it carries a well-established meaning in reinforcement learning (e.g., curiosity-driven exploration). Pre-training locomotion skills via speed rewards (or grasping for manipulators) is inherently task-specific and aligned with downstream objectives, making "intrinsic" misleading. This aligns with Heess et al.’s approach, which similarly pre-trains on task-relevant behaviors. The authors should clarify their terminology to avoid confusion with standard RL usage.  

(2) The Mutual Information regularization is elegantly designed and effective in simpler settings. However, it fails to improve performance in the more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3), as noted by the authors. A deeper analysis of this discrepancy—e.g., why the regularization is ineffective in challenging environments—would strengthen the paper.  

(3) The factorization between the agent state space (S_agent) and residual state space (S_rest) requires clearer exposition. While Duan et al. detail S_agent, S_rest is not explicitly defined for replicability. The paper must specify how S_rest is constructed (e.g., as a function of environment state or action history) or clarify whether this detail was omitted.  

(4) Additional analysis of the agent’s switching behavior would be valuable. For instance: How do switching decisions correlate with task phases? What failure modes emerge during hierarchical transitions? How does the timing of state switching impact overall performance? Such insights would deepen the understanding of the method’s operational dynamics.  

Overall, the work demonstrates significant empirical progress. Addressing these points—particularly clarifying terminology, explaining the MI regularization anomaly, defining S_rest, and expanding the behavioral analysis—would substantially improve the paper’s impact and rigor. The core contribution is compelling, but the current analysis does not fully leverage the promising results.
