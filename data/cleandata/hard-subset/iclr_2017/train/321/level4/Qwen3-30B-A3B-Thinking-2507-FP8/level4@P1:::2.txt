

**Summary of Contributions**  
The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons. It introduces a two-stage approach: first, pre-training a diverse set of skills in a generic environment using a single proxy reward (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and second, leveraging these skills through a high-level policy to solve downstream tasks. The key innovation lies in using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a broad span of interpretable skills efficiently. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly improves exploration and learning performance across multiple challenging tasks, including maze navigation and object gathering, while maintaining sample efficiency.

**Decision**  
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to hierarchical RL with a well-motivated approach that effectively addresses a significant challenge in the field. The core innovation—using SNNs with mutual information regularization for skill diversity—provides a principled solution to a persistent problem in sparse-reward learning.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper excels in three key areas. First, it clearly identifies a critical gap in existing approaches: while hierarchical RL reduces search space complexity, it typically requires domain-specific engineering; intrinsic motivation methods lack transferability across tasks. The authors' solution bridges this gap by learning skills in a pre-training environment with minimal supervision, then reusing them across diverse tasks. Second, the experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The results demonstrate that their method consistently outperforms strong baselines—including one that adds the same proxy reward directly to downstream tasks—across multiple maze and gather environments. The ablation studies (e.g., Fig. 4 and 5) convincingly show how SNNs with bilinear integration and the MI regularizer yield broader skill diversity than alternatives. Third, the technical foundation is sound. The use of SNNs to model multimodal policies, combined with the MI-based regularizer to encourage diversity, is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective. The implementation details (e.g., TRPO optimization, latent code integration) are well-justified and reproducible.

**Additional Feedback**  
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following points:

1. **Broader Task Scope**: While the experiments focus on locomotion tasks (Swimmer, Snake), the authors should explicitly discuss how their framework might generalize to non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation, where skills may require more diverse representations). This would address a reviewer's concern about whether the method scales beyond simple motion patterns.

2. **End-to-End Training**: The current work freezes low-level policies during downstream training. Discussing how end-to-end training (e.g., via straight-through estimators) might further improve performance would align with recent advances in hierarchical RL and strengthen the paper's relevance.

3. **Skill Transfer Analysis**: In Appendix C.4, the paper notes high variance in performance depending on pre-trained SNNs. A deeper analysis of *why* certain SNNs fail (e.g., missing critical skills like backward motion) would provide valuable insights for practitioners.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. In Section 7.3, the baseline with Center of Mass (CoM) speed reward performs poorly despite being a strong intrinsic motivator. Is this primarily due to the lack of skill diversity (as the MI regularizer enables) rather than the reward structure itself? Could the baseline be improved by adding a diversity component?

2. The paper compares "Multi-policy" (training six separate policies) to SNNs. How does the sample efficiency of the SNN approach scale with the number of skills (K)? Specifically, does the SNN method maintain its efficiency advantage as K increases beyond six?

3. In Figure 1, the bilinear integration is described as changing "all first hidden layer weights," while concatenation changes only the bias. Is this interpretation accurate? Could the authors clarify how these architectural choices directly impact the expressiveness of skill representations?
