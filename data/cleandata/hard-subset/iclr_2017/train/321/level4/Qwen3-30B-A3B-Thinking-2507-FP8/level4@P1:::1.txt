

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal, then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors combine stochastic neural networks with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a wide span of interpretable skills in a sample-efficient manner. The approach demonstrates strong empirical results on challenging benchmark tasks that previous methods could not solve, showing improved exploration and performance across multiple downstream tasks.

## Decision
Reject. The paper's primary issue is the misapplication of the term "intrinsic motivation," which conflicts with established RL terminology and creates significant conceptual confusion. The authors also fail to adequately address limitations in their approach, particularly regarding the inconsistent effectiveness of their mutual information regularization across different tasks.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's use of "intrinsic motivation" is fundamentally problematic. Pre-training with rewards directly tied to downstream task capabilities (speed for locomotion, grasping success for manipulation) is not truly intrinsic to the agent's exploration—it is explicitly designed to match downstream task requirements. This misnomer misleads readers about the nature of the approach and conflates it with established intrinsic motivation methods in RL that do not require task-specific reward design. The similarity to Heess et al.'s pre-training approach further highlights this conceptual confusion.

Additionally, while the mutual information regularization is elegant and generally effective, it fails to improve performance on more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3), as the authors acknowledge in their results. The paper lacks any meaningful analysis or explanation for why this regularization works well in some cases but not others, which is a significant limitation given the central role of this component in the proposed approach.

The paper also lacks clarity on the critical factorization between Sagent and Srest. While Duan et al. explicitly define Sagent, the authors do not clearly specify Srest, making replication difficult. The factorization is essential to understanding how the skills learned in the pre-training environment transfer to downstream tasks, yet it remains underspecified.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following revisions:

1. **Reconsider the use of "intrinsic motivation"**: Either redefine this term specifically for their approach (with clear justification) or use a different terminology to avoid confusion with established RL literature. The current usage misrepresents the nature of their pre-training reward.

2. **Provide deeper analysis of the mutual information regularization**: The inconsistent results across different mazes (particularly mazes 1, 2, and 3) deserve more thorough analysis. Why does the regularization help in some tasks but not others? What specific characteristics of the maze structure or task dynamics determine this?

3. **Clarify the Sagent/Srest factorization**: Explicitly define what constitutes Srest in the paper. For example, in the maze tasks, what specific elements of the state space are included in Srest versus Sagent? This is essential for reproducibility.

4. **Expand analysis of switching behavior**: The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of the skill-switching mechanism. How do different switching times affect performance? What are the failure modes when switching between skills, particularly for more unstable robots as mentioned in Appendix D?

5. **Provide more context on the pre-training task design**: While the paper mentions that the proxy reward requires minimal domain knowledge, it would help to clarify exactly what that minimal knowledge entails in different domains (e.g., for the Swimmer robot, what specific knowledge is required to set the speed reward?).

These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper's conceptual clarity and empirical support, addressing the most critical limitations that prevent this work from making a compelling contribution to the field.
