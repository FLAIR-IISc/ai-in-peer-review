

The paper presents an interesting approach to skill discovery using intrinsic rewards, but several critical concerns require clarification before the work can be fully appreciated.  

(1) The paper lacks a thorough analysis of failure modes as behavioral complexity increases. Specifically, it is unclear how the method handles the diversity of skills when the environment demands richer behavioral repertoires. Without empirical evidence on scalability to more complex tasks, the claimed robustness remains unverified.  

(2) Section 5.3 defines a random variable $X$ representing the agent’s "grid," but the discretization of the state space is not justified. Is the grid an inherent property of the environment, or an implementation choice? Crucially, the paper does not address the impact of using a continuous state space instead. If discretization is merely a simplification, how does this affect the method’s applicability to continuous-control domains where grid-based representation is unnatural?  

(3) The generality of the approach hinges on its ability to scale beyond simple embodiments. The authors evaluate only a 2-link swimmer; would the method work for more complex morphologies, such as a 5-link swimmer or a quadruped? This is essential for assessing whether the technique is broadly applicable or limited to specific, low-dimensional tasks. A brief ablation study on a more complex agent would significantly strengthen the claims.  

(4) The comparison with Heess et al. (2016) requires refinement. The authors assert that their pre-training phase "uses intrinsic rewards as the only signal" while Heess requires goal specification, implying their method is more domain-agnostic. However, the proposed intrinsic rewards (e.g., forward velocity, orientation consistency) are highly task-specific and require significant engineering. For instance, the reward for the ant task depends on carefully tuned coefficients to balance locomotion efficiency and stability—a process demanding substantial domain knowledge. Thus, the claim that "very minimal domain knowledge" suffices is misleading; the rewards themselves are hand-crafted and narrowly tailored to the evaluated tasks.  

Overall, the core idea is promising, but the current presentation lacks depth in addressing scalability, implementation assumptions, and the true generality of the reward design. Addressing these points would substantially improve the paper’s contribution.
