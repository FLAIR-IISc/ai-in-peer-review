

This work presents an interesting approach to hierarchical control, building on the framework of Heess et al. The experimental results are strong, successfully completing benchmarks that prior methods struggled with. However, the experimental analysis is somewhat limited.

(1) The term 'intrinsic' motivation appears misleading in this context, as it conflicts with standard usage in reinforcement learning (RL). Pre-training locomotion by rewarding speed (or grasping for manipulation) is strongly task-specific and directly prepares the agent for downstream objectives. This aligns closely with Heess et al.'s pre-training approaches, making the use of 'intrinsic' potentially confusing.

(2) The Mutual Information regularization is elegant and effective in simpler settings, but it fails to provide benefits in the more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3). The authors note this limitation—could they provide further insight into why the regularization does not generalize to these harder environments?

(3) The factorization between the agent policy (S_agent) and the residual policy (S_rest) requires clearer exposition. While Duan et al. specify S_agent, the paper does not sufficiently detail S_rest. For reproducibility, the exact formulation of S_rest should be explicitly stated—was this detail omitted, or is it covered elsewhere?

(4) A deeper analysis of the agent's switching dynamics would strengthen the paper. Specifically, examining how the agent transitions between subpolicies (e.g., switching triggers, timing, and failure modes) would provide valuable mechanistic insight. Additionally, investigating how switching frequency or timing impacts overall performance would be highly beneficial. Such analysis would complement the strong empirical results and offer a more complete understanding of the hierarchical control strategy.
