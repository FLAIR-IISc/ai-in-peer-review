- Paper proposes hierarchical RL that pre-trains diverse skills with a proxy reward and uses them for faster learning on sparse-reward downstream tasks.  
- Empirically strong on several challenging benchmarks, improving exploration and downstream performance.  
- Decision: Reject due to conceptual confusion around terminology and insufficient analysis of limitations.  
- Major flaw: Misuse of the term "intrinsic motivation" — pre-training rewards are task-aligned, not true intrinsic signals.  
- Pre-training rewards appear explicitly designed to match downstream capabilities (e.g., speed, grasping), misleading readers about generality.  
- Method is conceptually similar to prior pre-training approaches (e.g., Heess et al.), which the paper does not sufficiently distinguish from.  
- Mutual information regularization is elegant but shows inconsistent effectiveness, failing on several complex mazes (1, 2, 3).  
- Paper lacks analysis explaining when and why the mutual information regularizer helps or fails.  
- Critical factorization between Sagent and Srest is underspecified (Srest not clearly defined), hindering reproducibility and understanding of transfer.  
- Skill-switching behavior is underanalyzed — impact of switching times and failure modes (especially with unstable robots) is not explored.  
- Pre-training task design needs clearer explanation of what “minimal domain knowledge” actually entails in different domains.  
- Recommended fixes: rename/redefine “intrinsic motivation,” analyze mutual information effects, explicitly define Srest, expand switching analysis, and clarify pre-training design.
