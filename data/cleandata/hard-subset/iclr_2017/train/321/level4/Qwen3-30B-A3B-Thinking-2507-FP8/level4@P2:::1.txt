

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward (requiring minimal domain knowledge), then leverages these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors combine Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a wide span of interpretable skills efficiently. Their approach significantly improves exploration in downstream tasks, enabling the solution of challenging environments that previous methods could not handle. The experiments demonstrate strong empirical results across multiple benchmark tasks, showing clear advantages over existing approaches.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with strong empirical validation of its claims.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a critical challenge in reinforcement learning—solving tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons—through a well-motivated approach that effectively combines elements of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods. The key strengths include:

1. The framework's practicality: The pre-training environment requires only a simple proxy reward (e.g., robot speed for locomotion tasks), avoiding the need for complex reward engineering or domain-specific knowledge.

2. The SNN approach with MI regularization: This elegantly solves the problem of learning diverse skills without training multiple independent policies, reducing sample complexity while maintaining interpretability.

3. Comprehensive empirical validation: The method successfully solves challenging benchmark tasks (Maze 0-3, Gather) that previous approaches could not, with clear improvements in sample efficiency and performance.

The paper's contributions are well-placed in the literature, building on hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation work while making a distinct contribution with the SNN-based skill learning framework. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the empirical results are rigorously presented and scientifically rigorous.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper makes a strong contribution, the following improvements would enhance its impact:

1. Clarify the use of "intrinsic motivation": The proxy reward used in pre-training is task-specific (e.g., rewarding robot speed for locomotion tasks), which differs from the standard meaning of intrinsic motivation in RL (which typically refers to reward signals not tied to the task objective). The authors should carefully distinguish their approach from standard intrinsic motivation methods.

2. Expand analysis of MI regularization: The paper notes that the MI regularization doesn't consistently improve performance in Mases 1-3 (Section 7.3). A deeper analysis of why this occurs would strengthen the paper—does the regularization work better for certain skill types? How do the learned skills differ between Maze 0 (where MI helps) and Mases 1-3 (where it doesn't)?

3. Explicitly define the Sagent/Srest factorization: The paper assumes a factorization of the state space into agent-specific (Sagent) and task-specific (Srest) components. While Duan et al. (2016) specify Sagent for their benchmarks, the paper should explicitly detail how Srest was defined for each task to ensure replicability.

4. Include more analysis of switching behavior: The paper briefly mentions that the switching time T doesn't critically affect performance for static tasks, but more detailed analysis of how switching behavior impacts performance would be valuable. How do failure modes manifest when switching between skills, particularly for unstable agents like Ant?

## Questions for Authors
1. The MI regularization appears to benefit Maze 0 but not Mases 1-3. Can you provide an interpretation for this discrepancy? Is it related to the specific navigational requirements of these mazes?

2. For replicability, please explicitly specify how Srest was defined for each task (e.g., for the Maze tasks, what exactly constitutes the task-specific state components beyond the robot's position and velocity?).

3. In Appendix D, the paper discusses failure modes with the Ant robot due to instability during skill switching. Could you elaborate on how this might be mitigated, and whether the framework could be extended to handle such unstable agents more robustly?

4. The paper demonstrates that performance varies significantly across different pretrained SNNs (Section C.4). How might this variability be reduced, and what would be the most effective way to select a high-performing pre-trained SNN for a new task?
