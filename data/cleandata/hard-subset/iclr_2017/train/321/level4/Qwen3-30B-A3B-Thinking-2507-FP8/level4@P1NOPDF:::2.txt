The paper introduces a novel hierarchical reinforcement learning approach using stochastic neural networks, leveraging an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward to encourage diverse sub-policy learning. A key strength is its comparison with a strong baseline that combines intrinsic and sparse rewards; the baseline fails to solve tasks despite its "smoother" reward structure. Beyond the authors’ arguments regarding long-term credit assignment and hierarchical abstraction, this failure may stem from the diversity requirement explicitly enforced during sub-policy training—a component absent in the baseline. This observation raises the question of whether the baseline could be improved by incorporating similar diversity constraints, potentially enabling new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods (e.g., extending hierarchical REPS or option-critic frameworks). The visualizations are also commendable.  

While the work presents a promising direction, it could be strengthened by addressing the following points:  

1. **Limited sub-policy diversification**: Both concatenation and bilinear integration mechanisms rely solely on minor variations in the first hidden-layer weights (via bias or weight matrices), which suffices for locomotion tasks requiring similar policies. However, this constraint becomes problematic in domains demanding highly diverse sub-policies (e.g., manipulation tasks where solving a task from one state may differ significantly from solving it from another). The authors should demonstrate the method’s efficacy on non-locomotion tasks to validate its broader applicability.  

2. **Rigidity in hierarchical policy structure**: The manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies, and sub-policy durations are predetermined. This dependency critically hinges on the quality of the intrinsic rewards and pre-trained sub-policies for downstream task success. The authors should discuss or provide results for scenarios where these components are suboptimal, ideally linking to end-to-end hierarchical learning frameworks.  

3. **Domain-specific nature of intrinsic rewards**: Due to the fixed sub-policy assumption (point 2), the intrinsic rewards appear inherently domain-specific rather than generalizable. Clarifying this limitation would strengthen the paper’s contribution.  

To aid revision, the authors should clarify:  
- Why the strong baseline (CoM-maze) in Section 7.3 fails catastrophically (e.g., does it leverage extra experience from pre-trained policies, and how does "multi-policy" differ from the SNN approach?).  
- Whether Figure 1’s concatenation mechanism uses distinct bias vectors for the first hidden layer based on the categorical option index, and whether bilinear integration employs distinct weight matrices connecting observations to the first hidden layer.  

The core technical contribution is well-motivated and rigorously evaluated. Addressing the above points would further solidify the paper’s impact.
