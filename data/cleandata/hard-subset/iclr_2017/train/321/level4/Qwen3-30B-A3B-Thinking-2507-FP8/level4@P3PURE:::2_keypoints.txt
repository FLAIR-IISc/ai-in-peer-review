- Introduces hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Provides a strong baseline combining intrinsic and sparse rewards and shows this “smooth” reward approach fails to solve the tasks.  
- Suggests the baseline’s failure may stem from lack of explicit diversity enforcement during sub-policy training, not only long-term credit assignment or abstraction benefits.  
- Visualizations are effective and well-executed.  
- Limited sub-policy diversification: concatenation and bilinear integrations only differentiate sub-policies via first hidden-layer weights, which may be insufficient for non-locomotion tasks.  
- Recommends evaluation on harder, more diverse domains (e.g., manipulation) to assess generalizability.  
- Hierarchical policy constraints: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and predetermined durations, imposing strong dependence on pre-training and intrinsic-reward quality for downstream success.  
- Authors should discuss or demonstrate end-to-end hierarchical learning links (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic) or strategies to relax fixed sub-policy/duration assumptions.  
- Intrinsic rewards appear domain-specific due to the above constraints; a deeper discussion of this limitation is needed.  
- Request clarification why the strong baseline in Section 7.3 fails and whether “CoM maze reward” is the referenced baseline and accounts for extra experience from pre-trained policies.  
- Ask for clarification of what distinguishes “multi-policy” from SNN in the ablation study.  
- Ask for clarification of Figure 1: whether concatenation acts as option-specific biases to the first hidden layer and bilinear integration corresponds to option-specific weight matrices.
