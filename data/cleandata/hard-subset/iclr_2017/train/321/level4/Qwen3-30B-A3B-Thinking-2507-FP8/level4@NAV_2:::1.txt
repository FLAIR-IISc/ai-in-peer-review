

The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, and then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach effectively combines intrinsic motivation with hierarchical methods, requiring minimal domain knowledge for the pre-training proxy reward. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn interpretable skills efficiently and significantly improve performance across a range of challenging tasks with sparse rewards.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing a general framework that reduces sample complexity for sparse reward tasks through skill transfer, and the experimental results are compelling.

The paper's key strengths include the well-motivated approach to skill learning using SNNs with mutual information regularization, the strong empirical results showing improved exploration and learning performance across multiple benchmarks, and the clear demonstration that the pre-training phase can be conducted with minimal domain knowledge. The experiments effectively show that the framework outperforms baselines on challenging sparse reward tasks where previous methods struggled.

The primary area for improvement is the analysis of the mutual information regularization's effectiveness. The paper notes that the MI bonus does not consistently boost performance in more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3), yet provides limited analysis of why this occurs. A more thorough investigation of the conditions under which the MI regularization provides benefits would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a clearer formalization of the state space factorization between Sagent and Srest, which is crucial for the method's applicability but not sufficiently detailed for replicability.

I have two specific questions that would help clarify the work:
1) In the complex mazes (1, 2, and 3), the MI regularization appears to provide no performance benefit. Is this due to the specific maze structures making diverse skills unnecessary, or is there a fundamental limitation in the MI approach for these particular environments?
2) The paper mentions "the switching between skills for unstable agents" as a limitation (Section 8), but does not provide detailed analysis of the switching behavior in the experiments. Could you provide more insight into how the switching time T affects performance across different tasks and what optimal T values might be for various environments?
