

# Review for "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## Summary
This paper presents a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards and long horizons. The authors propose a two-stage approach: first, learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal; second, leveraging these skills to accelerate learning in downstream tasks. The key technical contribution is the use of Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage diverse skill acquisition. The method learns interpretable skills without requiring domain knowledge for the downstream tasks, and the authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms strong baselines, including one that directly incorporates the proxy reward into the downstream tasks. The experiments are well-designed, with thorough ablation studies and insightful visualizations of learned skills.

## Decision
Accept

## Key Reasons for Decision
The paper makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by addressing a critical gap in the literature: how to learn diverse skills without requiring task-specific supervision. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method outperforms strong baselines, particularly in sparse reward settings where naive exploration strategies fail. The technical approach is well-motivated, with a clear explanation for why the baseline using the same proxy reward directly in downstream tasks fails—primarily due to the lack of skill diversity and the inability to handle long-horizon credit assignment.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main strength lies in its clear articulation of the problem and the elegant solution. The authors correctly identify that existing approaches either require domain-specific knowledge (hierarchical methods) or fail to transfer knowledge across tasks (intrinsic motivation methods). Their solution bridges this gap by learning skills in a pre-training environment with minimal supervision. The experimental validation is particularly strong, with multiple tasks (Mazes and Gather), ablation studies on architectural choices (concatenation vs. bilinear integration, MI bonus), and thorough analysis of the learned skills. The most compelling evidence is the comparison with a baseline that uses the same proxy reward directly in the downstream tasks—the authors show that this baseline fails to solve the tasks despite having the same reward signal, which underscores the importance of their hierarchical structure and skill diversity.

The technical contribution of using SNNs with an MI regularizer is well-executed and innovative. The authors provide a clear explanation of how this approach differs from existing methods (e.g., HiREPS, Option-critic) and why it's particularly suited for continuous control tasks. The visualizations of learned skills (visitation plots) effectively demonstrate the interpretability and diversity of the acquired skills.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Expand the discussion of limitations and potential extensions. The paper mentions that the approach has limitations with unstable robots (e.g., Ant), but a more detailed analysis of failure modes and potential solutions would be valuable. This could include discussing how to incorporate end-to-end training with straight-through estimators, as referenced in the paper.

2. Provide more concrete examples of how the method might apply to non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation). While the authors mention this as a future direction, a brief discussion of potential applications to these more complex domains would strengthen the paper's broader impact.

3. Clarify the relationship between the "multi-policy" approach and the SNN approach. The paper mentions "multi-policy" as a baseline that trains six independent policies, but it's not entirely clear how this differs from the SNN approach beyond implementation details. A more detailed comparison would help readers understand the advantages of the SNN approach.

## Clarifying Questions
1. The baseline that uses the proxy reward directly in the downstream task (referred to as "CoM maze reward" in Section 7.3) performs very poorly. Could the authors provide more insight into why this baseline fails? Is it primarily due to the lack of skill diversity, or are there other factors (e.g., long-horizon credit assignment issues)?

2. In Figure 1, is it correct that concatenation of latent variables with observations modifies the bias of the first hidden layer, while bilinear integration modifies the weight matrix connecting observations to the first hidden layer? This is an important technical detail for understanding the architecture.

3. The paper mentions "multi-policy" as a baseline that trains six separate policies. How does this compare to the SNN approach in terms of sample efficiency and skill diversity? The paper states that SNNs achieve similar or better skill diversity with the same sample size, but a more detailed comparison would be helpful.

This paper presents a promising direction for hierarchical reinforcement learning that is well-motivated, technically sound, and experimentally validated. With minor revisions addressing the points above, it would be an excellent contribution to the field.
