- Paper proposes a two-phase hierarchical RL framework using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic (MI) regularizer to pre-train diverse skills and solve sparse-reward downstream tasks.  
- Empirically outperforms baselines on sparse-reward benchmarks (maze navigation, object gathering).  
- Recommendation: Reject.  
- Primary reason 1: Calling the proxy pre-training reward "intrinsic motivation" is misleading — it is task-specific, not standard intrinsic motivation.  
- Primary reason 2: Insufficient analysis for claims about the MI regularizer, especially its failure to help on mazes 1–3.  
- Strength: Addresses a meaningful RL problem and situates work relative to prior hierarchical and intrinsic motivation methods.  
- Weakness: Mislabeling the proxy reward weakens the contribution and could mislead readers about generality.  
- Weakness: No analysis explaining why the MI regularizer fails in some mazes — undermines the claim that it “consistently yields a large span of skills.”  
- Weakness: Unclear specification of state-space factorization (Sagent vs. Srest) for benchmark tasks, hindering replication.  
- Weakness: Limited discussion of skill-switching failure modes (e.g., unstable robots/Ant experiments).  
- Suggested fix 1: Replace "intrinsic motivation" with a precise description of the task-specific proxy reward.  
- Suggested fix 2: Provide detailed analysis (e.g., visualizations, comparisons) explaining when and why the MI regularizer helps or fails.  
- Suggested fix 3: Explicitly define Sagent and Srest for each benchmark (include examples like Maze 1).  
- Suggested fix 4: Expand discussion and analysis of skill-switching behavior and sensitivity to switch time T.  
- Questions for authors: How does the proxy reward generalize to different downstream domains (e.g., manipulation)? Why exactly does MI fail on mazes 1–3? How are Sagent and Srest defined for Maze 1? How sensitive is performance to switch time T?
