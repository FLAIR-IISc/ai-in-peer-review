

Reviewer Comments:  
I find the paper’s setting compelling, but I have several concerns requiring clarification:  

(1) The paper does not sufficiently address failure modes of the proposed approach. As behavioral complexity increases, I expect challenges in discovering diverse skills—particularly when the range of target behaviors becomes more nuanced. The authors should explicitly discuss where and why the method might fail under such conditions.  

(2) In Section 5.3, the authors define *X* as a random variable representing the agent’s grid position. Is the state space discretized? If so, what is the rationale for discretization, and how would the method perform if the space remained continuous? This choice significantly impacts scalability and generalizability.  

(3) A critical test of the framework’s generality would involve more complex agent morphologies. For instance, would the approach work with a 5-link swimmer (vs. the 2-link agent in the experiments)? Assessing performance on such a system would better demonstrate whether the method transcends specific embodiment constraints.  

(4) The authors claim that their pre-training framework differs from Heess et al. (2016) because it relies *solely* on intrinsic rewards (requiring minimal domain knowledge), whereas Heess et al. require specified goals. This distinction requires refinement. The intrinsic rewards proposed here are highly task-specific (e.g., designed for locomotion tasks in the experiments), not domain-agnostic. Thus, the claim that their approach avoids "hand-crafted" rewards is overstated—their reward design still depends on deep task-specific insight. The comparison to Heess et al. would be stronger if the authors clarified how their reward structure *actually* reduces domain knowledge requirements versus prior work.  

Overall, the core idea is promising, but these gaps undermine confidence in the method’s robustness and broad applicability. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper.
