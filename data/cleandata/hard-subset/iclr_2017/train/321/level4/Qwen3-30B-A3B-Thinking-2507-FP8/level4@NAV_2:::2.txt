

The paper introduces a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn diverse skills in a pre-training environment. The authors demonstrate that this approach effectively learns interpretable skills with minimal domain knowledge, which can then be leveraged to significantly improve exploration and learning performance in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. A notable contribution is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly incorporates the same proxy reward into the downstream tasks, showing that this approach fails to solve the tasks despite having a more "smooth" reward signal. This failure appears to stem from the lack of diversity in the skills learned, suggesting that the diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training is crucial for effective hierarchical learning.

The paper presents a promising direction for hierarchical RL, which could be strengthened by addressing the following points:
1) Limited diversification of sub-policies: The current architecture using concatenation or bilinear integration primarily modifies the first hidden layer's weights or bias, which may be insufficient for tasks requiring substantially different sub-policies. It would be valuable to test this approach on non-locomotion domains where ideal sub-policies are expected to be more diverse.
2) Limitation on hierarchical policies: The manager network is trained while sub-policies remain fixed, and the duration of each skill application is fixed. This creates dependency on the quality of pre-trained skills. More discussion or results regarding end-to-end training approaches would strengthen the framework.
3) Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards: The pre-training reward appears to require domain-specific construction, which contradicts the claim of minimal domain knowledge. Further discussion of this limitation would be helpful.

Additional questions:
1) What is the intuition for why the strong baseline in section 7.3 (with CoM proxy reward) performs so poorly? Does it fail due to the long time-horizon and credit assignment issues?
2) Does the baseline account for the extra experience gained by the pre-trained policies, or is it simply the same reward signal applied to the downstream task without the pre-training phase?
3) What distinguishes the Multi-policy architecture from the SNN approach in terms of implementation and performance?
