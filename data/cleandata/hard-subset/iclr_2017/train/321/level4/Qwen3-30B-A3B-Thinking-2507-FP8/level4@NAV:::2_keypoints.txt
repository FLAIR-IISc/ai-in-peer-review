- Proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to promote diverse sub-policies.  
- Strong baseline comparison shows combining intrinsic and sparse rewards ("smooth" reward) fails to solve tasks, likely because it lacks an explicit diversity requirement; authors' long-term credit-assignment/abstraction argument is incomplete.  
- This finding could inform improved baselines and new end-to-end hierarchical policy methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Visualizations are well-executed.  
- Limited sub-policy diversification: concatenation and bilinear integration enable only minimal differentiation (via first hidden layer), which may be insufficient for tasks needing highly diverse sub-policies (e.g., manipulation).  
- Hierarchical policy limitations: manager is trained with fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time steps, forcing reliance on very effective intrinsic rewards; more discussion/results on end-to-end learning needed.  
- Domain-specificity of rewards: intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific and resemble supervised rewards because of the fixed-subpolicy setup.  
- Clarification requested: why does the strong baseline (Section 7.3, "CoM maze reward") fail so severely, and does it account for extra experience from pre-trained policies?  
- Clarification requested: what is the distinction between "multi-policy" and SNN?  
- Clarification requested (Figure 1): does concatenation imply different biases to the first hidden layer based on categorical value, and does bilinear integration imply different weight matrices connecting observations to the first hidden layer?
