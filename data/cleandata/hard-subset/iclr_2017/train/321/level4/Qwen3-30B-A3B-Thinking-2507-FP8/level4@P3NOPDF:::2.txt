

Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning using stochastic neural networks (SNNs), with a novel contribution in leveraging an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward to encourage diverse sub-policy learning. The authors demonstrate a compelling empirical case: their method outperforms a strong baseline that naively combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, despite the latter's apparent smoothness. Beyond the authors' argument regarding long-term credit assignment and hierarchical abstraction, the failure of the baseline likely stems from the absence of explicit diversity requirements during sub-policy training—a key insight that could inform future work on end-to-end hierarchical policy learning (e.g., extending frameworks like REPS or Option-Critic). The paper’s visualizations effectively illustrate policy diversity and task progress, adding significant clarity.  

The work is well-positioned to advance hierarchical RL, though it could be strengthened by addressing the following points:  

1. **Limited Sub-Policy Diversity Mechanism**: The paper employs concatenation and bilinear integration to differentiate sub-policies via minor weight variations (e.g., first-layer biases or weights). While sufficient for locomotion tasks requiring minimal policy variation, this approach may falter in domains demanding fundamentally distinct sub-policies (e.g., robotic manipulation, where solving a task from one state may require entirely different strategies than from another). The authors should test their method on non-locomotion tasks (e.g., block stacking or multi-object navigation) to validate its scalability to harder, more diverse problems. Without this, the generality of the diversity mechanism remains unproven.  

2. **Rigidity in Hierarchical Policy Training**: The manager network is trained while sub-policies remain fixed, and sub-policy durations are predetermined. This creates a dependency on pre-trained sub-policies being near-optimal for downstream tasks—a significant constraint. The authors should discuss how to relax this assumption, such as through end-to-end training (e.g., jointly optimizing manager and sub-policies) or adaptive duration scheduling. A brief ablation study comparing fixed vs. adaptive sub-policy lengths would strengthen the argument for the method’s practicality.  

3. **Domain-Specificity of Intrinsic Rewards**: Due to the fixed sub-policy limitation (point 2), the intrinsic rewards appear heavily tailored to the specific environments tested (e.g., locomotion mazes). The authors should clarify whether these rewards generalize beyond the current domains or require task-specific tuning. If domain-specific, this limits the method’s applicability—suggesting a need for more universal diversity incentives (e.g., unsupervised objectives that adapt to task structure).  

**Clarifications Requested**:  
- In Section 7.3, the strong baseline (labeled "CoM maze reward") fails completely. Could the authors elaborate on *why* this baseline underperforms relative to their method? Specifically, does the baseline leverage pre-trained sub-policies (as in the SNN approach), or is its failure inherent to the reward design?  
- The term "multi-policy" in Section 7.3 appears to refer to a baseline combining multiple intrinsic rewards. How does this differ from the SNN approach (which uses a single SNN with diverse sub-policies)?  
- In Figure 1, does "concatenation" imply sub-policies are selected via categorical values that modify the first hidden layer’s bias (e.g., one bias per option), while "bilinear integration" uses distinct weight matrices connecting observations to the first layer? A brief figure caption clarification would aid reproducibility.  

These points are not critical flaws but opportunities to deepen the paper’s impact. The core contribution—using identifiability to enforce diversity—is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results are robust. Addressing the limitations above would position this work as a stronger foundation for future hierarchical RL research, particularly in bridging the gap between modular sub-policy learning and end-to-end optimization. The method’s simplicity and effectiveness make it a valuable addition to the field, and the proposed extensions (e.g., adaptive durations, broader domain testing) are highly feasible.  

**Final Recommendation**: **7 (Strong Accept)**. The paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to hierarchical RL. With minor clarifications and expanded validation, it would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
