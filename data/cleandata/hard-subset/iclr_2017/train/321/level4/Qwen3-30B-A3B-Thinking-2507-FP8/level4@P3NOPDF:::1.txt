

This paper presents a hierarchical control approach for robotic tasks, building on prior work by Heess et al. The core contribution lies in the use of mutual information (MI) regularization to encourage disentangled state representations across hierarchical levels. The experimental results are compelling: the method achieves state-of-the-art performance on challenging benchmarks (e.g., complex maze navigation and manipulation tasks) that previous hierarchical RL approaches struggled to solve. However, the paper’s analysis and exposition require significant strengthening to fully justify its contributions.

The most significant concern pertains to terminology. The authors employ the term "intrinsic motivation" to describe pre-training with task-specific rewards (e.g., locomotion speed or grasp success). This usage is misleading and conflicts with the established RL literature, where "intrinsic motivation" typically refers to curiosity-driven exploration (e.g., intrinsic rewards for novelty). Pre-training with task-specific objectives is fundamentally distinct from intrinsic motivation; it is task-oriented and directly aligned with downstream goals, as demonstrated in Heess et al.’s work. The authors should replace this term with a precise description (e.g., "task-specific pre-training" or "supervised pre-training") to avoid confusion and align with community standards.

The MI regularization is elegantly designed and effective in simpler settings, but its performance degrades in the more complex mazes (1–3). The paper notes this limitation but lacks a thorough analysis of *why* the regularization fails in these scenarios. For instance: Is the failure due to insufficient representational capacity in the agent’s state space? Does the complexity of the mazes overwhelm the disentanglement objective? Or is the MI term’s hyperparameter configuration suboptimal for high-dimensional environments? A dedicated discussion of these failure modes would significantly enhance the paper’s contribution and provide actionable insights for future work. Without this, the claim that MI regularization "works generally well" is overstated.

Replicability is also a concern. The paper specifies the state space for the agent level (S<sub>agent</sub>) following Duan et al., but omits a clear definition of the rest state space (S<sub>rest</sub>). S<sub>rest</sub> is critical for the hierarchical decomposition, yet its construction (e.g., whether it aggregates states from the agent’s history, environmental context, or other components) remains ambiguous. The authors should explicitly define S<sub>rest</sub> in the methodology section, including how it is constructed from the raw observation space. This omission hinders reproducibility and makes it unclear whether the approach relies on domain-specific assumptions.

Beyond these points, the paper misses an opportunity to deepen its analysis of the hierarchical mechanism. Specifically:  
1. **Switching behavior**: How does the agent decide when to switch between high-level policies? The paper provides no analysis of the switching frequency, conditions, or correlation with task difficulty. A visualization of switching events (e.g., in maze navigation) alongside task progress would clarify the controller’s dynamics.  
2. **Policy robustness**: What are the common failure modes of the hierarchical policy? For example, does the agent struggle with specific maze configurations or object placements? Understanding these limitations would guide future improvements.  
3. **Switching timing**: How does the duration of high-level policy execution affect performance? A sensitivity analysis of the switching interval hyperparameter would strengthen the practical utility of the approach.  

These analyses would transform the paper from a demonstration of feasibility into a rigorous study of the hierarchical control paradigm’s strengths and limitations.

In summary, the paper’s experimental results are strong and address a meaningful gap in hierarchical RL. However, the terminology misalignment, lack of analysis for the MI regularization’s failure in complex environments, insufficient detail on S<sub>rest</sub>, and limited exploration of the controller’s behavior collectively weaken the paper’s impact. Addressing these points would significantly elevate the contribution. The authors should: (1) replace "intrinsic motivation" with precise terminology; (2) provide a detailed analysis of MI regularization’s failure in mazes 1–3; (3) explicitly define S<sub>rest</sub>; and (4) include analysis of switching behavior and failure modes. With these revisions, the paper would make a substantial contribution to the field.
