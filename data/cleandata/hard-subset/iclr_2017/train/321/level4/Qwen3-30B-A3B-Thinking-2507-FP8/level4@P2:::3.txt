

# Review for ICLR 2017

**Summary of Contributions**  
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward, then leverages these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovation combines Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a span of interpretable skills. The authors demonstrate that their approach can learn a wide variety of skills (e.g., different locomotion patterns) in a sample-efficient manner, and that these skills significantly improve exploration and learning performance in downstream tasks with sparse rewards.

**Decision**  
Reject. The paper's core claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the comparison to prior work is misleading.

**Supporting Arguments**  

The paper makes two central claims: (1) their SNN approach with MI regularization consistently learns a diverse span of skills without requiring domain-specific reward engineering, and (2) this skill learning framework significantly outperforms previous approaches in sparse-reward tasks. However, the evidence provided does not robustly support these claims.

Regarding the first claim, the paper's demonstration of skill diversity relies on visualizations of visitation patterns in the Swimmer robot environment. The paper claims that SNNs with MI regularization "consistently yield a large span of skills," but the evidence is limited to a single robot type (Swimmer) and a narrow range of environments. The paper fails to demonstrate that their approach generalizes to more complex embodiments or more challenging environments where skill diversity would be most needed. The discussion of the Snake robot in Appendix C shows that performance varies significantly across different random seeds, suggesting the approach may not be reliable in more complex settings.

The second claim about performance improvements is also under-supported. The paper compares their approach to a baseline that incorporates the same proxy reward in the downstream task. This comparison is misleading because the baseline (adding the proxy reward to the task) is not a standard approach in the literature, and the paper fails to compare against established methods like VIME (Houthooft et al., 2016) or HiREPS (Daniel et al., 2012) that also address sparse rewards. The paper's claim that "the proposed hierarchical architectures are able to learn much faster in every new MDP" is not supported by the results, as the performance improvements are inconsistent across different maze configurations.

The paper's comparison to Heess et al. (2016) is particularly problematic. The authors claim their pre-training requires "very minimal domain knowledge," but their proxy rewards (e.g., "speed of the robot" for locomotion, "successful grasping of any object" for manipulation) are actually quite specific to the downstream tasks. The paper fails to acknowledge that these rewards require understanding of the task structure, which is precisely what the authors claim to avoid.

**Additional Feedback**  

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct experiments with more complex embodiments (e.g., the 5-link Snake robot or Ant) to demonstrate whether their approach generalizes beyond the Swimmer robot. The limited results in Appendix C are insufficient.

2. Compare their approach against established baselines for sparse-reward learning (e.g., VIME, HiREPS) rather than a modified version of their own proxy reward. This would provide a more meaningful assessment of their contribution.

3. Clarify the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the stability issues observed with the Ant robot in Appendix D. The failure modes with unstable robots represent a significant limitation that the paper currently downplays.

4. Provide a more rigorous analysis of when their MI regularization is beneficial. The results in Section 7.3 show that the MI bonus does not consistently improve performance, yet the paper presents it as a key contribution.

**Questions for Authors**  

1. The paper claims their proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge," but for the Swimmer locomotion task, the proxy reward is "proportional to the magnitude of the speed of the robot." How would this proxy reward be designed for a completely different task, such as a robot learning to navigate in 3D space? What specific domain knowledge is required to construct this reward?

2. In Section 5.3, the authors discretize the state space into cells for estimating the mutual information. What happens if this discretization is not performed, and how sensitive is the approach to the choice of grid size? The paper does not address these important implementation details.

3. The paper reports that "the pre-training experience improves exploration in downstream environments," but the experiments only show results for the Swimmer robot in a limited set of mazes. How would the approach perform on more challenging tasks, such as those requiring precise navigation in cluttered environments or complex object manipulation?

4. The paper states that "the set of skills can be used later in a wide collection of different tasks," but the results only demonstrate performance on two types of tasks (Maze and Gather). What evidence is there that the skills learned in the pre-training phase are transferable to completely different tasks beyond those demonstrated in the paper?
