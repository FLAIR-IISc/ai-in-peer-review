

# Review for "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward signal, then leverages these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors introduce Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to efficiently learn a wide span of interpretable skills. Their approach combines the benefits of intrinsic motivation (requiring minimal domain knowledge for the proxy reward) and hierarchical methods (reducing sample complexity by reusing learned skills across tasks). The paper demonstrates that this method significantly improves exploration and performance on challenging sparse-reward tasks like mazes and object gathering, while maintaining sample efficiency.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's core claim that the framework requires "very minimal domain knowledge" is undermined by the specific hand-crafted nature of the proxy rewards, and the paper fails to adequately demonstrate generalization to more complex environments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's key claim—that their pre-training approach requires "very minimal domain knowledge" because it uses a single proxy reward—appears overstated. The proxy rewards described (e.g., speed for locomotion, successful grasping for manipulators) are highly task-specific and require significant understanding of the downstream tasks to design. This directly contradicts their claim of minimal domain knowledge. In contrast, Heess et al. (2016) use a more general reward structure that can be applied across diverse tasks without modification, making their approach more aligned with the stated goal of minimal domain knowledge.

Furthermore, the paper lacks sufficient evidence for generalization to more complex embodiments. While they test on a 2-link swimmer, they provide no results for more complex robots (e.g., a 5-link swimmer or Ant) beyond brief mentions in appendices. The failure modes of their approach with unstable robots like Ant are clearly documented in Appendix D, yet the main paper does not address these limitations or discuss how the framework could be adapted. The claim that "the framework can be applied to the more challenging scenario" is not supported by evidence, as the experiments are limited to relatively simple environments.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
- More carefully qualify their claim about "very minimal domain knowledge" by acknowledging that proxy rewards still require specific task understanding, and compare their approach more precisely with Heess et al. (2016) regarding the actual domain knowledge required.
- Provide results for more complex robot embodiments (e.g., 5-link swimmer, Ant) to demonstrate the framework's generalization capabilities beyond simple locomotion tasks.
- Include a thorough analysis of the failure modes with unstable robots (as documented in Appendix D) in the main paper, discussing potential solutions or limitations.

## Key Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "the construction of [the proxy reward] only requires very minimal domain knowledge." Could you elaborate on what specific domain knowledge is required to design the proxy reward for a new task, and how this compares to the domain knowledge required for Heess et al.'s (2016) goal-based pre-training approach?

2. In Section 5.3, the authors discretize the state space for computing mutual information. What happens if the state space is not discretized? How does this discretization affect the scalability of the approach to more complex environments with higher-dimensional state spaces?

3. The paper shows promising results with a 2-link swimmer but doesn't demonstrate the approach on more complex robots. Could you provide results for a 5-link swimmer (as mentioned in Appendix C) or the Ant robot (Appendix D) to better assess the framework's generalization capabilities?

4. The paper claims that "the span of skills learned by the SNN policy" is "clearly interpretable as the latent code is varied." However, the visitation plots in Figure 4(b) show that some skills (e.g., the "backward" skill) are not consistently learned across different random seeds. How does the framework ensure consistent learning of diverse skills across different random initializations, and what are the implications for reliability in real-world applications?
