

I appreciate the novel setting presented in this paper, but I have several substantive concerns that require clarification:

(1) What is the failure model for this approach? As behavioral complexity increases, I anticipate challenges in discovering diverse skills. The paper does not sufficiently address the limits of skill diversity this method can achieve.

(2) In Section 5.3, the statement "let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated" implies state discretization. Is the state space discretized? If so, what is the rationale for this choice, and what would happen if discretization were omitted?

(3) Regarding generalizability, does the approach scale to more complex embodiments? Specifically, would it work with a 5-link swimmer (or similarly complex agent) instead of the 2-link swimmer used in experiments? This assessment is critical for evaluating the framework's broader applicability.

(4) I disagree with the authors' characterization of their approach relative to Heess et al. (2016). While acknowledging their similarity in framework, the claim that "their pre-training setup requires a set of goals to be specified" while "we use intrinsic rewards as the only signal [...] requiring minimal domain knowledge" is inaccurate. The intrinsic reward functions proposed in this work are highly hand-crafted and tailored to a narrow range of control tasks, contradicting the assertion of minimal domain knowledge requirements.
