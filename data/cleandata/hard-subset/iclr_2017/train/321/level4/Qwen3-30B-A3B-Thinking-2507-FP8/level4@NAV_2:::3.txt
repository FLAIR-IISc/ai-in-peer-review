

This paper presents a compelling framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons. The authors propose a method to first learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward signal requiring minimal domain knowledge, then leverage these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks. The approach effectively combines intrinsic motivation with hierarchical reinforcement learning, using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn interpretable and diverse skills. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in exploration and learning performance across multiple challenging environments, providing strong empirical evidence for the approach's effectiveness.

Reject. The paper's claim regarding the novelty of their pre-training approach compared to Heess et al. (2016) is not sufficiently supported, and the generality of the method to more complex embodiments remains unverified.

The authors' assertion that their pre-training setup requires "very minimal domain knowledge" while Heess et al. (2016) requires "a set of goals to be specified" overlooks the fact that the proxy rewards they propose (e.g., proportional to robot speed for locomotion tasks) are equally domain-specific and hand-crafted for the particular task settings. This undermines the paper's key claim about the minimal domain knowledge requirement. Additionally, the paper's experiments are limited to relatively simple robotic embodiments (Swimmer and Ant), and there is no evidence that the approach generalizes to more complex embodiments such as a 5-link swimmer, which would be crucial for establishing the method's broader applicability.

The authors should provide a more nuanced comparison with Heess et al. (2016) that acknowledges the similarities in their pre-training approaches and more clearly articulates the specific advantages of their information-theoretic regularizer. They should also extend their experiments to more complex robotic embodiments to demonstrate the method's generalizability.

1. The paper claims that their proxy reward "requires very minimal domain knowledge" while Heess et al. (2016) requires specifying goals. However, the proxy rewards they use (e.g., speed for locomotion, grasping success for manipulators) are still quite domain-specific and require careful design for each task type. Can the authors provide examples of proxy rewards that truly require minimal domain knowledge across a wider range of tasks?

2. The paper demonstrates success with the Swimmer robot but does not test the approach on more complex embodiments like a 5-link swimmer. How would the method perform on a more complex embodiment where the relationship between the proxy reward and the downstream tasks becomes less straightforward?

3. The paper states that "the construction of which only requires very minimal domain knowledge" for their proxy reward. Given the specific examples provided (speed for locomotion, grasping success for manipulators), can the authors clarify how these proxy rewards would generalize to completely different task domains without significant re-engineering?
