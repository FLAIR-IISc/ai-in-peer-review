

The paper presents a compelling framework for skill discovery using intrinsic rewards, offering a promising approach to pre-training agents for downstream tasks. While the core idea is well-motivated and the empirical results are generally convincing, several critical issues require clarification and further validation to establish the method's robustness and generality. Below, I outline key concerns that must be addressed before the paper can be considered for acceptance.

First, the paper lacks a thorough analysis of failure modes as behavioral complexity increases. The authors demonstrate success in relatively simple environments (e.g., 2D navigation, 2-link swimmer), but the claim that "intrinsic rewards enable discovery of diverse skills" assumes scalability to richer behavioral repertoires. The draft does not investigate *when and why* the approach might fail under higher complexity—e.g., whether skill diversity plateaus, if certain behaviors become inaccessible, or if reward sparsity hinders exploration. A systematic failure analysis (e.g., measuring skill coverage or success rates across increasingly complex tasks) is essential to validate the method’s limits and guide future improvements.

Second, Section 5.3 states: "Let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated." This implies a discretized state space, but the paper does not explicitly confirm whether the grid is discretized or how the discretization is performed. Crucially, the authors must clarify: (1) *Why* discretization is necessary for their formulation, and (2) *What happens* if the state space is not discretized (e.g., in continuous control settings). If the method relies on discretization, this could limit applicability to environments where natural state representations are continuous (e.g., high-dimensional robot control). The absence of this discussion undermines the method’s claimed generality.

Third, the paper’s generalizability to more complex embodiments is untested. The authors focus on a 2-link swimmer, but the approach’s viability hinges on scalability to systems with higher degrees of freedom (e.g., a 5-link swimmer or a humanoid). Such a test would critically assess whether the intrinsic reward mechanism adapts to increased physical dynamics complexity or if the method collapses due to the "curse of dimensionality" in exploration. Without this evaluation, the claim that the framework is broadly applicable remains speculative. I strongly recommend including a comparison on a more complex embodiment to validate this aspect.

Finally, the authors’ comparison to Heess et al. (2016) requires stronger justification. They assert that "our pre-training setup requires minimal domain knowledge" because it uses "intrinsic rewards as the only signal," contrasting with Heess et al.’s need for specified goals. However, the intrinsic rewards proposed (e.g., sparse goal-reaching rewards, distance-based shaping) are not domain-agnostic; they are explicitly designed for specific tasks (e.g., navigation, swimming). For instance, the reward function in Equation 3 depends on predefined goal positions and distance metrics, which constitute non-trivial domain knowledge. This contradicts the claim of "minimal domain knowledge," as the rewards remain highly task-specific. The authors should either: (a) refine their comparison to clarify *how* their rewards are less hand-crafted (e.g., by deriving them from a universal principle), or (b) acknowledge that their approach still requires task-specific reward engineering, albeit simpler than Heess et al.’s goal specification.

In summary, while the paper’s core contribution is valuable, these gaps limit its impact. The failure analysis, discretization justification, scalability test, and reward-handcraftedness claim must be resolved. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the paper’s technical rigor and broaden its relevance. I recommend rejection pending substantial revisions to these points. The work has potential but currently lacks the necessary evidence to support its generalization claims.
