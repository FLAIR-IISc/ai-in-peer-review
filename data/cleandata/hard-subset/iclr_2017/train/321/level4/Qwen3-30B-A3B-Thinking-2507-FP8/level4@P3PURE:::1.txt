

This paper presents an interesting contribution to hierarchical control, building upon the work of Heess et al. The experiments are strong and successfully complete benchmarks that prior methods failed to address. However, the analysis of the results is insufficient.

(1) The use of the term 'intrinsic' motivation is problematic, as it conflicts with its established meaning in reinforcement learning (RL). Pre-training robots for locomotion by rewarding speed (or grasping for manipulation arms) is explicitly task-oriented and designed to facilitate later task performance. This approach bears significant similarity to pre-training procedures in Heess et al., further undermining the appropriateness of the term.

(2) The mutual information regularization is elegant and effective in simpler settings, but fails to provide significant improvement in the more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3). While the authors note this limitation, they do not provide any interpretation or analysis of why the regularization fails in these scenarios. A deeper investigation into this discrepancy would strengthen the paper.

(3) The factorization between the agent-specific state space \(S_{\text{agent}}\) and the residual state space \(S_{\text{rest}}\) requires clearer specification in the paper. Duan et al. detail \(S_{\text{agent}}\), but for replicability, \(S_{\text{rest}}\) must be explicitly defined. The current description appears incomplete—was this detail omitted or is it present in the supplementary material?

(4) Additional analysis of the agent's switching behavior would be valuable. More broadly, the paper would benefit from deeper exploration of the learned policies, including failure modes and the impact of switching timing on overall performance. Such analysis would provide critical insights into the hierarchical control mechanism.
