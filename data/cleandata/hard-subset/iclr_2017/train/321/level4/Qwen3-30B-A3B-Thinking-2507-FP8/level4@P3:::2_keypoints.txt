- Two-stage HRL: pre-train diverse skills with a proxy reward, then leverage them to speed learning on downstream tasks.  
- Main technical novelty: use of Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a mutual-information regularizer to encourage diverse, interpretable skills without domain knowledge.  
- Method significantly outperforms strong baselines, particularly in sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks.  
- Experiments are well-designed with thorough ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, MI bonus) and informative visualizations (visitation plots).  
- Baseline that injects the proxy reward directly into downstream tasks fails, underscoring the need for hierarchical skills and diversity.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key strength: fills a gap by learning transferable, diverse skills without task-specific supervision, bridging hierarchical and intrinsic-motivation approaches.  
- Technical claim: SNN+MI is well-motivated, distinct from HiREPS/Option-critic, and suitable for continuous control.  
- Limitation: approach struggles with unstable robots (e.g., Ant); requires more analysis of failure modes and remedies (e.g., end-to-end training with straight-through estimators).  
- Recommendation: provide concrete discussion of applicability to non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation) to broaden impact.  
- Clarification needed: better explain differences between the “multi-policy” baseline and SNNs in terms of implementation, sample efficiency, and skill diversity.  
- Question: why exactly does the CoM maze reward baseline perform poorly—solely lack of skill diversity or also long-horizon credit-assignment issues?  
- Question: confirm architectural detail—does concatenation alter the first-layer bias while bilinear integration alters the input weight matrix?  
- Question: provide a more detailed comparison of multi-policy vs SNN regarding sample complexity and achieved diversity.
