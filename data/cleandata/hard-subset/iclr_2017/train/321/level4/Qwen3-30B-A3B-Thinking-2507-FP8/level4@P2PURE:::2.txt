The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning method using stochastic neural networks, introducing an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward to promote diversity in the learned mixture of sub-policies. A notable contribution is the comparison with a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this "smooth" reward approach fails to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors' argument regarding long-term credit assignment challenges and hierarchical abstraction benefits, an alternative explanation for the baseline's failure may stem from the diversity requirement enforced during sub-policy training—this requirement is absent in the baseline. This observation could inform improvements to the baseline and inspire new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, such as hierarchical REPS or option-critic methods. The visualizations are effective.

The paper presents a promising direction and could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

1)  **Limited sub-policy diversification:** Both concatenation and bilinear integration mechanisms permit only minimal differentiation in sub-policies, achieved solely through variations in the first hidden layer's weights. While this limitation is not problematic for the tested locomotion tasks (which require similar policies with minimal diversity), it would become evident in more complex domains where ideal sub-policies necessitate greater diversity (e.g., manipulation tasks where solving a task from different states requires fundamentally distinct strategies).

2)  **Constraints on hierarchical policy learning:** The manager network is trained while sub-policies remain fixed, and sub-policy durations are predetermined. This necessitates that the intrinsic rewards and learned sub-policies are highly effective for downstream tasks. The paper would benefit from deeper discussion or results addressing these constraints, particularly in relation to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning frameworks.

3)  **Domain-specific nature of intrinsic rewards:** Due to the limitations in point (2), the reliance on intrinsic rewards appears inherently domain-specific, potentially requiring supervised reward design for optimal performance.
