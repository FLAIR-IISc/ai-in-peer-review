

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that effectively addresses challenges in sparse reward and long-horizon tasks. The authors propose a two-phase approach: first, learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, and second, leveraging these skills through a high-level policy to solve downstream tasks. The key innovation lies in using a single proxy reward during pre-training that requires minimal domain knowledge, while the MI-based regularizer ensures diversity in the learned skills. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves significantly better exploration and sample efficiency compared to baselines, including approaches that directly incorporate intrinsic rewards into sparse tasks. The work is well-motivated, with strong empirical validation across multiple challenging environments, and the results are clearly presented with insightful visualizations of skill diversity and exploration.

## Decision
Accept (7/10)

## Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a compelling contribution to hierarchical RL by effectively combining skill learning with efficient exploration in sparse reward settings. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. **Strong theoretical motivation**: The paper successfully bridges intrinsic motivation with hierarchical methods by showing how a single proxy reward during pre-training can yield diverse, reusable skills. The information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information is a well-justified and effective technique to ensure skill diversity without requiring domain-specific reward shaping.

2. **Rigorous empirical validation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments across multiple challenging environments (Maze and Gather tasks), demonstrating that their approach significantly outperforms strong baselines, including a direct combination of intrinsic and sparse rewards. The results consistently show improved exploration, faster learning, and better performance across all downstream tasks. The ablation studies (particularly in Section 7.1) clearly demonstrate the value of both the SNN architecture and the MI regularizer.

3. **Novelty and impact**: The framework represents a meaningful advance in hierarchical RL by addressing a key limitation of prior work—requiring minimal domain knowledge for pre-training while still yielding reusable skills. The method is general enough to apply to various robotic tasks and can be extended to more complex scenarios.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, consider addressing the following points:

1. **Skill diversity in non-locomotion tasks**: The current experiments primarily focus on locomotion tasks where the diversity of skills is relatively limited (forward/backward/sideways motion). The method's applicability to more complex domains (e.g., manipulation tasks where skills might be fundamentally different) would significantly strengthen the contribution. Including at least one non-locomotion example would demonstrate the broader applicability of the framework.

2. **End-to-end training**: While the current approach freezes sub-policies during downstream training, a brief discussion of how end-to-end training (using straight-through estimators as mentioned in the paper) might further improve performance would be valuable. The authors briefly mention this as future work, but a short analysis of the potential benefits would be helpful for readers.

3. **Clarify the baseline comparison**: The paper's strong results against the "CoM reward" baseline are compelling, but a more detailed explanation of why this baseline fails despite being a form of intrinsic motivation would strengthen the argument. Specifically, the paper should clarify whether the baseline receives the same pre-training experience as the proposed method.

## Questions for Authors
1. In Section 7.3, the baseline using a Center of Mass proxy reward performs poorly despite having the same intrinsic motivation as the pre-training reward. Could you clarify whether this baseline also benefits from the pre-training experience, or if it's trained from scratch in the downstream task? This would help understand whether the poor performance is due to the reward structure or lack of pre-training.

2. The paper mentions "multi-policy" as a baseline (Fig. 6). Could you clarify how this differs from the SNN approach? Specifically, is multi-policy training six separate policies (one for each skill), and does it use the same amount of pre-training data as the SNN approach?

3. In Figure 1, the description indicates that concatenation modifies the bias term while bilinear integration modifies the first layer weights. Could you confirm this interpretation and explain why bilinear integration leads to better skill diversity?

4. The paper shows that SNNs with MI regularizer improve performance on the Gather task but not consistently on all mazes. Could you elaborate on why this difference exists, and whether this is related to the specific maze structure or the nature of the required skills?

This paper makes a strong contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning, with a well-motivated approach and convincing empirical results. With minor clarifications and extensions to more complex domains, it could become a valuable reference for future work in the area.
