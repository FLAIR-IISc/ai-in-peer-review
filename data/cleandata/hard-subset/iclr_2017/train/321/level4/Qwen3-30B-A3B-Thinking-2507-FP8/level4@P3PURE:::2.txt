

Review:  
The paper introduces a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning using stochastic neural networks, leveraging an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward to promote diversity in learned sub-policies. A notable contribution is the comparison with a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this "smooth" reward approach fails to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ arguments regarding long-term credit assignment and hierarchical abstraction benefits, the failure of the baseline may stem from the absence of diversity enforcement during sub-policy training—a key distinction from the proposed method. This observation may inform improvements to baselines and inspire new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches (e.g., hierarchical REPS or option-critic). The visualizations are effective and well-executed.  

The paper presents a promising direction but could be strengthened by addressing the following points:  
1) **Limited sub-policy diversification**: Both concatenation and bilinear integration mechanisms differentiate sub-policies only through the first hidden-layer weights, which suffices for locomotion tasks requiring minimal policy variation. However, this limitation may become pronounced in non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation tasks), where ideal sub-policies must differ significantly based on state (e.g., solving a task from one state vs. another). The method should be evaluated on harder, diverse domains to assess its generalizability.  
2) **Hierarchical policy constraints**: The manager network is trained while sub-policies remain fixed, and sub-policy durations are predetermined. This imposes strict requirements on the quality of intrinsic rewards and pre-trained sub-policies for downstream task success. The authors should discuss or provide results on handling such cases, ideally linking to end-to-end hierarchical learning frameworks.  
3) **Domain-specificity of intrinsic rewards**: Due to point (2), the reliance on domain-specific intrinsic rewards appears unavoidable. A deeper discussion of this limitation would be valuable.  

Additional questions:  
- Could the authors clarify why the strong baseline in Section 7.3 fails completely? Specifically, is "CoM maze reward" the baseline referenced? Does this baseline account for the extra experience gained from pre-trained policies?  
- What distinguishes "multi-policy" from SNN in the context of the ablation study?  
- Regarding Figure 1: Is (1) concatenation interpreted as applying different biases to the first hidden layer based on categorical options, and (2) bilinear integration as using distinct weight matrices connecting observations to the first hidden layer?
