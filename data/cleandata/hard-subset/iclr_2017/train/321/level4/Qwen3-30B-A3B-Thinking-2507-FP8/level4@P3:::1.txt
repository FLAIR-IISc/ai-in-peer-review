

**Review for "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"**

**Summary of Contributions**  
This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons. The authors introduce a two-phase approach: first, they learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a minimal proxy reward (e.g., locomotion speed for a mobile robot), and second, they leverage these skills through a high-level policy to solve downstream tasks. The key innovation lies in using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to efficiently learn a wide range of interpretable skills. The method demonstrates significant improvements over baseline approaches on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks, including maze navigation and object gathering tasks.

**Decision**  
I recommend *Reject* for the following two key reasons: (1) the use of "intrinsic motivation" misrepresents the pre-training approach, which is more task-specific than standard intrinsic motivation in RL; (2) the paper lacks sufficient analysis to support claims about the MI regularizer's effectiveness, particularly its failure in complex mazes.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in RL: the difficulty of sparse-reward tasks. The authors correctly situate their work in relation to prior hierarchical RL methods and intrinsic motivation approaches, though they mischaracterize their proxy reward as "intrinsic motivation," which conflates with the established RL terminology. As noted in the draft review, this proxy reward (e.g., speed for locomotion) is deliberately designed to align with downstream tasks, making it fundamentally different from standard intrinsic motivation methods that encourage exploration without task-specific guidance. This mislabeling weakens the paper's contribution and could mislead readers.

The empirical results are strong on benchmark tasks, with the proposed method outperforming baselines that fail on sparse-reward environments. However, the paper does not adequately explain why the MI regularizer—which is claimed to enhance skill diversity—fails to improve performance on mazes 1, 2, and 3 (see Section 7.3). The authors note this limitation but provide no analysis of *why* it occurs. For instance, do these mazes require different skill characteristics than mazes 0 or the Gather task? Is the MI regularizer's effect dependent on specific environmental properties? Without this analysis, the claim that the MI regularizer "consistently yields a large span of skills" is undermined.

The paper also lacks clarity on the structural assumption of the state space factorization (Sagent vs. Srest). While Duan et al. (2016) specify Sagent for locomotion tasks, the paper does not clearly define Srest for the benchmark tasks. For replication, the authors should explicitly state how the state space is decomposed for each environment.

**Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
1. Replace "intrinsic motivation" with a more precise description of the proxy reward's role, emphasizing its task-specific design.  
2. Add a detailed analysis of why the MI regularizer works in some environments but not others (e.g., by visualizing skill diversity across tasks or comparing skill representations).  
3. Clarify the state space factorization in Section 4, including explicit definitions of Sagent and Srest for each benchmark task.  
4. Expand the discussion of skill-switching behavior, particularly in unstable robots (Section D), to address the failure modes observed in the Ant experiments.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. In the pre-training phase, how does the proxy reward (e.g., speed) relate to the specific downstream tasks? Would the same reward work for a different task domain (e.g., manipulation instead of locomotion)?  
2. Why does the MI regularizer fail to improve performance on mazes 1–3 despite enhancing skill diversity in the pre-training phase? Could this be due to task-specific skill requirements or limitations in the MI estimation?  
3. For the state space factorization (Sagent vs. Srest), could you provide a concrete example of how these components are defined for Maze 1 (e.g., what constitutes Srest in this environment)?  
4. How sensitive is the framework to the switch time T? The paper notes mild performance variations with T, but a systematic analysis of this hyperparameter's impact would strengthen the results.
