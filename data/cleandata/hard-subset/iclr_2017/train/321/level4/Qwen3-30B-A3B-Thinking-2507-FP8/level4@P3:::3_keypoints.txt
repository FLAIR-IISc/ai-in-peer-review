- Proposes hierarchical RL that pre-trains diverse skills using a single proxy reward and SNNs with an information-theoretic regularizer, then uses those skills for sparse-reward downstream tasks.  
- Decision: Reject — interesting and promising but technical claims under-supported and novelty overstated.  
- Primary rejection reasons: (1) insufficient evidence of generalization to more complex embodiments, (2) claim that the proxy reward requires “very minimal domain knowledge” is unsubstantiated and misleading.  
- Empirical support is mainly on the Swimmer; the 5-link Snake results are limited and do not convincingly show robustness to higher embodiment complexity.  
- Paper lacks analysis on scaling to higher-dimensional action spaces, more intricate dynamics, and failure modes as embodiment complexity increases.  
- Proxy rewards used (e.g., speed, successful grasp) are task-specific and would require domain knowledge to adapt to other tasks (contradicts “very minimal” claim).  
- The claim that this approach is more general than Heess et al. (2016) is inaccurate; goal-based rewards may be similarly adaptable.  
- Analysis of the information-theoretic regularizer is insufficient: state discretization for mutual information is unexplained and sensitivity to discretization is not analyzed.  
- Recommend adding a comprehensive limitations section detailing failure modes and extensions for complex embodiments.  
- Recommend revising comparison with Heess et al. (2016) to acknowledge similarities and clearly state specific advantages of the SNN approach.  
- Recommend providing concrete examples of proxy-reward design for new tasks and precisely defining “very minimal domain knowledge.”  
- Recommend thorough sensitivity analysis and justification for the discretization in Section 5.3 and discussion of outcomes without discretization.  
- Questions for authors: rationale and sensitivity of state discretization and its effect on skill diversity; concrete proxy-reward design for novel tasks (e.g., humanoid on uneven terrain); how performance scales with robot complexity and specific arising challenges; how the approach is truly more general than goal-based methods and what concrete advantages it offers.
