This paper presents an approach to hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks. It proposes the idea of using the information-theoretical measure of option recognition as an additional reward for learning a diverse mix of sub-policies. A key finding of this paper is the comparison with a strong baseline, which directly combines the intrinsic reward and the scattered reward, and shows that this smooth reward cannot solve the tasks. In addition to the authors' arguments about the disadvantages of long-term credit determination/benefits from hierarchical abstraction, a possible explanation could be the requirement of diversity imposed on sub-policy training, which is assumed to be absent in the baseline case. Consider whether it could propose new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning such as improving the baseline and hierarchical REPS/option-critic, etc. Excellent visualization.

This paper presents a promising direction and can be further strengthened by addressing the following issues:

1) Limited diversification of sub-policies: Both concatenation and bilinear integration only allow for slight differences in sub-policies through the first hidden layer, which is not a problem in tested tasks because they mainly require slight diversification with the same locomotion policies, but such limitations may be more pronounced in other tasks where the ideal sub-policies are more diverse. Therefore, it is interesting to apply it in difficult, non-locomotion domains where the ideal sub-policies are not very similar, such as for manipulation, where solving some tasks from one state may be very different from solving them from another state.

2) Limitations of hierarchical policies: The manager network is trained while the sub-policies are fixed. Furthermore, the time steps for the sub-policies are fixed. For this, the "intrinsic" reward and their learned sub-policies must be very good for solving down-stream tasks. It would be good to see some more discussions/results on managing such situations, which are ideally connected to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3) Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem like domain-specific/supervised rewards: (2) Due to this, it seems unavoidable.
