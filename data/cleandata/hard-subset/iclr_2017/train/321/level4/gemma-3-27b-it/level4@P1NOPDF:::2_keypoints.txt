- Introduces an information-theoretic option identifiability reward to encourage diverse sub-policies in hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks.  
- Empirical result: strong baseline that combines intrinsic and sparse rewards fails, showing smoother reward signals alone are insufficient.  
- Possible explanation: baseline lacks an explicit diversity requirement during sub-policy training, which the proposed method enforces.  
- Suggestion: explore whether adding diversity requirements can improve the baseline and inspire end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Visualizations are well-done and informative.  
- Limitation: concatenation and bilinear integration provide limited sub-policy diversification (mainly via first-layer weight/bias changes), which may be adequate for locomotion but not for more diverse tasks.  
- Recommendation: evaluate on more challenging, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) where optimal sub-policies vary widely.  
- Limitation: manager is trained with fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and a predetermined sub-policy time horizon, requiring strong intrinsic rewards and well-learned sub-policies.  
- Recommendation: discuss and provide results for cases where pre-trained sub-policies or intrinsic rewards are insufficient, and connect to end-to-end hierarchical learning.  
- Concern: current approach appears reliant on domain-specific or supervised intrinsic rewards due to the above limitations.  
- Questions for authors: clarify intuition for poor baseline performance in Sec. 7.3 (CoM maze reward), whether the baseline accounts for experience from pre-trained policies, and what “multi-policy” means versus the SNN approach.  
- Clarification request for Fig. 1: confirm concatenation corresponds to categorical-dependent biases on the first hidden layer and bilinear integration corresponds to categorical-dependent input weight matrices.
