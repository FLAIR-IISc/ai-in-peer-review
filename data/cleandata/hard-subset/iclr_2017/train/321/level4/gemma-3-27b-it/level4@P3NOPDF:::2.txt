## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

**Score: 7/10**

This paper presents a novel method for hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) leveraging stochastic neural networks (SNNs). The key contribution lies in the introduction of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability, used as an additional reward signal to encourage the learning of a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A particularly compelling result is the comparison against a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards; the authors demonstrate that this seemingly smoothed reward structure is insufficient to solve the tested tasks. This finding challenges the assumption that simply mitigating sparse rewards is enough for effective hierarchical RL, and supports the authors’ argument regarding the difficulties of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction. The visualizations provided are clear and informative.

Overall, the paper outlines a promising research direction, though several avenues for further investigation could strengthen the work.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel application of information theory to promote option diversity.
*   Strong empirical validation demonstrating the limitations of simpler reward shaping techniques.
*   Clear presentation and illustrative visualizations.

**Weaknesses & Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Limited Sub-policy Diversification:** The proposed methods for combining sub-policies – concatenation and bilinear integration – appear to allow only limited differentiation between them, operating primarily through modifications to the first hidden layer weights. While this seems adequate for the tested locomotion tasks, where optimal sub-policies may be relatively similar, it’s unclear how well this approach will scale to domains requiring more diverse strategies. I encourage the authors to explore the method’s performance on more challenging, non-locomotion tasks – for example, manipulation tasks – where the optimal policy can vary drastically depending on the initial state. Investigating this limitation would provide valuable insight into the method’s generalizability.

2.  **Hierarchical Policy Constraints:** The current framework trains the manager network while holding the sub-policies fixed, and also enforces a fixed time horizon for each sub-policy. This inherently places a high demand on the quality of the learned intrinsic rewards and the corresponding sub-policies. Addressing scenarios where the initial sub-policies are suboptimal is crucial. The authors should expand the discussion to consider end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, drawing connections to related work such as hierarchical REPS or option-critic algorithms. Exploring strategies for jointly optimizing the manager and sub-policies would significantly enhance the method's robustness.

3.  **Intrinsic Reward Dependency:** The reliance on intrinsic rewards, while effective, appears somewhat domain-specific and potentially akin to supervised learning. Given the fixed sub-policy training (point 2), this dependency may be unavoidable in the current setup. However, acknowledging and discussing this limitation is important.

I have a few specific questions regarding the experimental results:

*   The baseline in Section 7.3 (“CoM maze reward”, if I understand correctly) performs surprisingly poorly. What is the authors’ intuition for this result, beyond the difficulties of long-term credit assignment? Does the baseline account for the additional experience gained by the pre-trained policies? 
*   What is the distinction between “multi-policy” and the proposed SNN approach? Clarifying this distinction would be helpful.
*   Regarding Figure 1: Is it correct that (1) “concatenation” modifies the bias term of the first hidden layer based on the categorical value of the chosen sub-policy, and (2) “bilinear integration” introduces a different weight matrix connecting the observation to the first hidden layer, dependent on the selected sub-policy?



In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of hierarchical RL. The novel use of information theory to encourage option diversity and the insightful comparison with existing methods are particularly noteworthy. Addressing the limitations outlined above, especially regarding sub-policy diversification and the constraint of fixed sub-policies, would further strengthen the work and broaden its applicability.
