- Introduces hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks with an information-theoretic option-identifiability intrinsic reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Shows that directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards fails on tested tasks, highlighting benefits of hierarchical abstraction and diverse skills.  
- Suggests alternative explanation: baseline lacks the diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training, so adding diversity to the baseline could improve it.  
- Recommends exploring connections to end-to-end hierarchical policy methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Visualizations are clear and informative.  
- Recommendation: accept (score 7/10); approach is promising and experiments, though limited, support effectiveness.  
- Limitation: concatenation and bilinear integration mainly modulate the first hidden layer, potentially limiting sub-policy differentiation for diverse skill domains (e.g., manipulation).  
- Limitation: manager relies on fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and fixed execution horizons; adapting sub-policies end-to-end or learning termination could improve robustness.  
- Limitation: intrinsic rewards appear domain-specific (e.g., speed for locomotion); generalizability to domains without obvious intrinsic signals needs discussion.  
- Question: Why does the "CoM maze reward" baseline perform poorly — does it leverage pre-training or is it trained per task independently?  
- Question: Please clarify the distinction between “multi-policy” and the SNN-based approach in terms of training and implementation.  
- Question: Confirm Figure 1 interpretation — concatenation varies the first-layer bias by the latent variable, while bilinear integration varies the observation→first-layer weight matrix.
