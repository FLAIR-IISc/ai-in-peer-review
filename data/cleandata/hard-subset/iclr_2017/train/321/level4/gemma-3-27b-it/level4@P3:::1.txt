## Review of STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

This paper presents a compelling framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn reusable skills. The approach addresses the challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons by pre-training a diverse skill set and then utilizing these skills in downstream tasks. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating performance on benchmark tasks where previous methods have struggled.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical RL, offering a novel and effective approach to skill discovery and transfer. The strong experimental results and clear presentation of the method justify acceptance despite some areas for improvement.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of combining SNNs with mutual information maximization for skill learning is well-motivated and innovative. SNNs provide a natural mechanism for representing multi-modal policies, crucial for learning a diverse skill set. The MI regularizer effectively encourages exploration and prevents the SNN from collapsing to a single mode. The experimental validation on challenging benchmark tasks is particularly convincing, demonstrating a clear performance advantage over existing approaches. The authors successfully tackle sparse reward environments, a longstanding challenge in RL. The code availability is also a significant strength, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is generally well-written, several aspects could be strengthened to enhance its impact. The framing of the pre-training reward as "intrinsic motivation" is potentially misleading, as correctly pointed out. The pre-training tasks are clearly designed to facilitate performance in the downstream tasks, making it more akin to targeted pre-training rather than purely intrinsic exploration. Rephrasing this aspect to emphasize the proxy reward and its design relative to downstream tasks would be beneficial.

The observation that the MI regularization doesn’t consistently improve performance in the more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3) is noted but not thoroughly investigated. Providing further analysis or hypothesizing why this might be the case would add valuable insight. Is it related to the state representation, the complexity of the maze, or the specifics of the learned skills?

The factorization of the state space into *S<sub>agent</sub>* and *S<sub>rest</sub>* is a key assumption of the framework. While *S<sub>agent</sub>* is outlined, a more precise definition of *S<sub>rest</sub>* for each environment would improve clarity and replicability. Specifically detailing which elements of the state space constitute *S<sub>rest</sub>* would be helpful.

Expanding the analysis of the learned policies would strengthen the paper. Exploring the switching behavior between skills—how often switches occur, the circumstances leading to switches, and the consequences of switching—would provide a deeper understanding of the algorithm's dynamics. Investigating the impact of the switching time *T* on performance in more detail, and potentially adapting *T* dynamically, could also be fruitful. Including an ablation study to analyze the contribution of each component (SNN, MI regularization, hierarchical structure) would provide further insights.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the design principles behind the choice of the proxy reward in the pre-training environment? What considerations are important in ensuring the proxy reward generalizes well to downstream tasks?
2.  Why does the mutual information regularization appear to have a less significant impact on performance in the more complex maze environments? Do you have any hypotheses as to the underlying reasons?
3.  Can you provide a more concrete definition of *S<sub>rest</sub>* for each environment used in the experiments (e.g., explicitly listing the components of the state space that constitute *S<sub>rest</sub>*)?
4.  Did you observe any undesirable behaviors associated with frequent skill switching, and how does the choice of the switching time *T* impact the overall performance and stability of the agent?
