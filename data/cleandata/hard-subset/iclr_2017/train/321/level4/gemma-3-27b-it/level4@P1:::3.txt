This paper tackles the problem of learning policies for a collection of tasks with sparse rewards, a significant challenge in deep reinforcement learning. The authors propose a framework that first pre-trains a diverse set of skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer, then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks via a hierarchical policy structure. This approach combines the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, requiring minimal domain knowledge for skill acquisition. The paper is well-motivated and addresses a critical issue in RL.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. While the core idea is promising and the results are encouraging, the experimental validation lacks sufficient depth, and some claims regarding novelty appear overstated. The paper needs a more thorough analysis of failure modes and a more convincing demonstration of generalizability.

Here are my supporting arguments:

Firstly, the experimental evaluation primarily focuses on a limited set of tasks (Mazes and Gather) with the Swimmer robot. While the results show improvement over baselines, they don’t convincingly demonstrate robustness or scalability. The authors acknowledge the variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs (Section 7.3 and Appendix C.4) but don’t fully address why this occurs or how to mitigate it. The experiments with the more complex “Snake” robot (Appendix C) are a good step, but a more comprehensive evaluation with different robots and environments is crucial.

Secondly, the claim of minimal domain knowledge required for the proxy reward seems overstated. While it’s true the reward isn't task-specific in the downstream sense, the choice of a speed-based reward (or grasping for the manipulator arm, mentioned in the introduction) is still a design decision requiring some understanding of what constitutes useful behavior for locomotion or manipulation. This nuance should be acknowledged. The comparison to Heess et al. (2016) is also not entirely accurate, as the authors downplay the design considerations inherent in their proxy reward.

Thirdly, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of potential failure modes. The brief mention of instability with the “Ant” robot (Appendix D.2) hints at a significant issue. A more systematic analysis of these failures, and potential solutions (e.g., modifications to the SNN architecture, regularization techniques, or hierarchical control structure), would significantly strengthen the paper.

Here’s additional feedback to improve the paper:

*   **Clarify the discretization in Section 5.3.** The description of discretizing the state space for the mutual information calculation is a bit vague. Explain *why* discretization is necessary, what the impact of the grid resolution is, and whether alternative approaches were considered.
*   **Expand the analysis of the switch time T.** The discussion in Appendix C.3 suggests that T has a limited impact, but this seems somewhat surprising. Provide more detailed results and analysis, perhaps exploring adaptive switching strategies.
*   **Provide more details about the hyperparameters.** While Appendix A lists the hyperparameters, a more detailed justification for their choices would be helpful.
*   **Address the variance in performance.** The high variance observed across different pre-trained SNNs is a concern. Explore techniques to reduce this variance or provide guidance on selecting the best SNN for a given task.
*   **Discuss limitations.** A dedicated section discussing the limitations of the approach would be valuable.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   What specific modifications to the SNN architecture or training procedure would be most effective in addressing the instability issues observed with more complex robots like Ant?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the proxy reward? Could a more diverse set of proxy rewards lead to a wider range of learned skills?
*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the SNNs compared to training multiple independent policies?
*   Regarding the discretization of the state space for MI calculation, have you considered using kernel density estimation or other non-parametric methods to avoid the limitations of grid-based discretization?
