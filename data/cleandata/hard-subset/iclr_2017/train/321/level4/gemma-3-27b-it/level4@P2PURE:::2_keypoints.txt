- Introduces hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Compelling empirical result: a strong baseline combining intrinsic and sparse rewards fails, suggesting simple reward smoothing is insufficient.  
- Possible reason for baseline failure: it lacks an explicit diversity requirement during sub-policy training, unlike the proposed method.  
- Visualizations are well-executed and informative.  
- Limited sub-policy diversification: concatenation and bilinear integration only alter the first hidden layer, which may fail in domains needing highly distinct sub-policies.  
- Evaluation is restricted to locomotion tasks; performance on more diverse domains (e.g., manipulation) should be explored.  
- Manager training constraints: the manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and preset sub-policy durations, requiring high-quality intrinsic rewards and well-learned primitives.  
- Need discussion/results for scenarios with poorly learned sub-policies or variable durations, and connection to end-to-end hierarchical learning.  
- Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific due to the fixed sub-policy training procedure.  
- Suggest exploring whether the diversity insight can improve the baseline or inspire end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Questions about the strong baseline: why did the “CoM maze reward” baseline perform poorly, and does it incorporate pre-training experience?  
- Clarification requests: how does “multi-policy” differ from SNNs, and is figure 1’s interpretation correct — (1) concatenation implements different biases, and (2) bilinear uses different weight matrices for the first hidden layer?
