This work presents an interesting contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning, building on ideas related to those explored by Heess et al. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating successful navigation of benchmarks where previous approaches have struggled. However, the analysis of these results could be strengthened.

(1) I share the concerns of other reviewers regarding the use of the term ‘intrinsic’ motivation. Given the established meaning of ‘intrinsic motivation’ within the reinforcement learning community, applying it to a pre-training phase that explicitly rewards behaviors directly beneficial to downstream tasks (such as speed in locomotion or successful grasping) feels potentially misleading. The pre-training objectives, while not identical, share similarities with those used by Heess et al., and appear strongly task-directed rather than arising from inherent curiosity or novelty-seeking. 

(2) The application of Mutual Information regularization is a particularly elegant aspect of this work and generally improves performance. However, the authors acknowledge that it does not extend this benefit to the more complex maze environments (1, 2, and 3). I would appreciate any further insight or analysis regarding this observation. Understanding *why* the regularization fails to improve performance in these specific cases would be valuable.

(3) The paper clearly defines the ‘Sagent’ component of the hierarchical structure, following the approach of Duan et al. However, for the sake of complete replicability, a more detailed specification of ‘Srest’ is necessary. While I may have missed it, the paper would benefit from explicitly outlining how ‘Srest’ is defined and implemented.

(4) A more in-depth analysis of the agent’s policy switching behavior would enhance the understanding of its operation. Specifically, exploring the frequency and context of switches between sub-policies could provide valuable insights. More generally, further analysis of the learned policies – including their failure modes and the impact of switching time on overall performance – would be a welcome addition to the paper. This could illuminate how the hierarchy enables robustness and adaptability in different environments.
