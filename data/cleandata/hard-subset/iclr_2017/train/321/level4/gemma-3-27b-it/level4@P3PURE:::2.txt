The paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning using stochastic neural networks. A key contribution is the introduction of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward, encouraging the learning of a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A particularly compelling result is the comparison to a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this seemingly smoother reward structure is insufficient to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ argument regarding difficulties in long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, a potential explanation for the baseline’s failure lies in the lack of a diversity requirement during sub-policy training – a component explicitly included in the proposed method. It would be insightful to explore whether this observation can inform improvements to the baseline and potentially inspire new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, such as those found in hierarchical REPS or option-critic frameworks. The visualizations are well-executed and informative.

The paper presents a promising direction and could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1) **Limited Diversification of Sub-Policies:** Both concatenation and bilinear integration allow for only minimal differentiation between sub-policies, achieved through modulation of the first hidden layer’s weights. While this limitation is not critical in the tested locomotion tasks – which largely require variations on a core set of policies – it could become more pronounced in domains demanding more diverse sub-policies. For example, in manipulation tasks, the optimal approach to solving a task can vary significantly depending on the initial state. Investigating the method’s performance on such harder, non-locomotion domains would be valuable.

2) **Limitations in Hierarchical Policy Learning:** The manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies, and the duration of each sub-policy is also fixed. This places significant demands on the quality of the learned intrinsic rewards and sub-policies to effectively solve downstream tasks. Further discussion and/or results concerning scenarios where these initial components are suboptimal would be beneficial, ideally connecting the work to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods.

3) **Domain Specificity of Intrinsic Rewards:** Due to the constraints outlined in point (2), the intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear somewhat domain-specific, resembling supervised signals. This seems inherent to the current setup.

What distinguishes a “multi-policy” approach from the use of stochastic neural networks (SNNs)?
