This paper presents an interesting framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn reusable skills. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating successful performance on challenging benchmarks where previous methods have struggled. However, the analysis of these results could be strengthened, and some aspects of the methodology warrant further clarification.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem in reinforcement learning – sample efficiency in sparse reward environments – and proposes a novel and effective approach. The experimental validation is strong enough to warrant acceptance, despite some areas for improvement in the presentation and analysis.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core contribution – combining SNNs with a mutual information-based regularization for skill learning, followed by hierarchical policy learning – is well-motivated. The approach effectively addresses the challenges of exploration in sparse reward settings by pre-training a diverse set of skills. The experimental results across multiple environments (locomotion + mazes & gathering) convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of this framework, achieving performance beyond prior methods. The code release is a significant positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper’s strength in experimental results is somewhat offset by a lack of in-depth analysis. Specifically, the authors acknowledge the limited benefit of the mutual information regularization in more complex maze environments, but do not provide a clear interpretation for this observation. This is a key area for revision. Furthermore, while the SNN architecture is well-described, the analysis of *how* these networks learn diverse skills beyond the MI regularization could be expanded. Finally, further discussion of potential failure modes and the impact of the switching time parameter would improve the overall understanding of the method’s limitations and sensitivities.

**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Terminology:** The use of "intrinsic motivation" may be misleading. While the proxy reward encourages exploration, it is directly tied to task-relevant behaviors (speed or grasping). Framing this as a pre-training strategy focused on learning useful primitives rather than "intrinsic motivation" might be more accurate.
*   **Mutual Information Analysis:** Elaborate on why the MI regularization does not consistently improve performance, particularly in the more complex mazes. Is the information signal less effective in these environments? Are there alternative regularization strategies that could be explored?
*   **State Space Factorization:** The factorization of the state space into *Sagent* and *Srest* is crucial to the approach. While *Sagent* is mentioned, a clearer and more precise definition of *Srest* for each task (maze and gather) is needed for replicability. A detailed specification of what comprises the task-specific state components would be valuable.
*   **Switching Behavior & Policy Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the agent's skill switching behavior. How frequently does the agent switch skills? Is the switching behavior consistent across different environments or tasks? Discussing potential failure modes, particularly when switching between skills, would also strengthen the analysis. Expanding on the influence of the switch time parameter *T* on performance could be insightful.
*   **Ablation Studies**: While the paper shows results for different designs (SNN vs. Multi-policy), a formal ablation study to isolate the effect of MI bonus, and the bilinear integration might be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasons why the mutual information regularization appears less effective in the more complex maze environments?
2.  What specific components constitute *Srest* (the task-specific state space) for each of the maze and gather environments, to ensure clarity and replicability?
3.  Can you provide some visualizations or statistics regarding the agent’s skill switching behavior during the downstream tasks? How does switching frequency vary across different tasks and environments?
4.  Have you explored any alternative regularization strategies beyond mutual information, and if so, what were the results?
5.  What are the primary failure modes observed when using this hierarchical approach, and what steps could be taken to mitigate them?
