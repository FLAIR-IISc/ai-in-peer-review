This paper presents an interesting approach to unsupervised skill discovery through intrinsic motivation. The premise – learning diverse behaviors without external task-specific rewards – is compelling and addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning. While the experimental results demonstrate promising performance on the presented environments, several questions and potential limitations warrant further consideration.

My primary concern revolves around the scalability and robustness of the proposed method. As the complexity of achievable behaviors increases, I anticipate difficulties maintaining the diversity of learned skills. What are the expected failure modes of this approach? Specifically, how does performance degrade as the agent’s action space and the richness of possible behaviors expand? A discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper.

Section 5.3 introduces the notation “let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated.” This phrasing suggests a discretization of the state space. If this is the case, the authors should explicitly state this, and justify the discretization. What are the implications of using a discrete representation, and how might the approach be adapted to operate in a continuous state space? Conversely, if the space is *not* discretized, clarifying this point is crucial for understanding the methodology.

The generality of the proposed approach is also unclear. The experiments focus on relatively simple environments – a 2-link swimmer and gridworlds. Expanding on this, would the method generalize to more complex embodied agents? Investigating performance with a higher-dimensional agent, such as a 5-link swimmer, would provide valuable insight into the limitations and potential scalability of the framework. This is particularly important given the claim of learning generalizable skills.

The authors draw a comparison to the work of Heess et al. (2016), highlighting the advantage of their intrinsic reward-based approach over pre-training with specified goals. However, I disagree with the assertion that the proposed rewards are minimal in terms of domain knowledge. While the rewards do not explicitly specify goals, they are nonetheless carefully designed and tailored to the specific control tasks investigated. The novelty detection component, for example, requires careful tuning and implicitly encodes assumptions about what constitutes interesting or unexplored states. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging the inherent design choices and assumptions within the intrinsic reward function would be beneficial. It would be helpful to articulate what makes these rewards ‘minimal’ compared to goal-specification methods, providing concrete examples of how the reward structure could be readily transferred to different environments with minimal modification.

Despite these concerns, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised skill learning. The experimental results are well-presented, and the methodology appears sound. Addressing the points raised above – specifically, discussing failure modes, clarifying the state space representation, evaluating performance on more complex agents, and providing a more balanced comparison to related work – would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
