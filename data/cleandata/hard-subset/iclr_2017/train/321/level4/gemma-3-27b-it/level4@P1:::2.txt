This paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning using stochastic neural networks. The approach introduces the use of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward, encouraging the learning of a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A key result is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this seemingly smoothed reward signal is insufficient to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ argument regarding the difficulty of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, a possible explanation for this failure could be the lack of diversity enforced during sub-policy training, which is absent in the baseline. It would be interesting to investigate whether this insight can inform improvements to the baseline and inspire new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, such as those found in hierarchical REPS or option-critic papers. The visualizations are particularly helpful.

The paper presents a promising direction and could be further strengthened by addressing the following points.

1) **Limited diversification of sub-policies:** Both concatenation and bilinear integration offer only limited differentiation in sub-policies, primarily through modifications to the first hidden layer weights. While this isn't a significant issue in the tested locomotion tasks, where ideal sub-policies are relatively similar, this limitation might become more apparent in other domains—particularly those involving manipulation—where optimal sub-policies can vary drastically depending on the state. Exploring the performance of this method on more challenging, non-locomotion domains would be valuable.

2) **Constraints on hierarchical policies:** The manager network is trained while the sub-policies remain fixed, and the duration of sub-policy execution is predetermined. This places a strong reliance on the quality of the intrinsically-rewarded, learned sub-policies for successful downstream task performance. Further discussion and results addressing scenarios where these initial policies are suboptimal would be beneficial, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning techniques.

3) **Nature of intrinsic/unsupervised rewards:** The reliance on pre-defined proxy rewards, while minimizing domain knowledge, somewhat resembles supervised learning in its requirement for thoughtful reward design. This is perhaps unavoidable given the fixed sub-policies, but acknowledging this point is important.

I have a few clarifying questions:

*   What is the underlying reason for the poor performance of the baseline in Section 7.3 (specifically, the “CoM maze reward” baseline)?
*   Does the baseline leverage any of the experience gained during pre-training of the policies, or is it trained from scratch on each downstream task?
*   What distinguishes "multi-policy" from the SNN-based approach?
*   Regarding Figure 1, is my understanding correct that (1) concatenation modifies the bias term of the first hidden layer based on the categorical value, and (2) bilinear integration modifies the weight matrix connecting the observation to the first hidden layer?



**Decision:** Accept (with minor revisions)

**Key Reasons:**

The paper tackles an important problem – exploration in sparse reward environments – with a novel approach that combines stochastic neural networks with hierarchical reinforcement learning. The results are compelling, demonstrating improved performance over strong baselines and highlighting the benefits of learning diverse skills. While some aspects could be strengthened, the core contribution is significant and the paper is well-written and presented.
