The presented setting is promising, however, I have several criticisms and questions regarding the work:

(1) What failure modes has this approach exhibited? As the complexity and diversity of achievable behaviors increase, I anticipate challenges in discovering a broad range of skills. Understanding the limitations in skill diversity is crucial.

(2) In Section 5.3, the definition of X as a random variable representing the agent’s grid location raises a question regarding spatial discretization. Is the environment discretized, and if so, what is the justification for this choice? Furthermore, what are the implications if the space is *not* discretized?

(3) Building upon the first point, I am interested in understanding the scalability of this approach to more complex morphologies. Specifically, would the method generalize effectively to a more articulated embodiment, such as a 5-link swimmer, rather than the simpler 2-link example? Evaluating performance with increased embodiment complexity is essential for assessing the generality of the proposed framework.

(4) The authors contrast their work with Heess et al. (2016), highlighting the lack of specified goals in their pre-training phase. While I acknowledge the distinction, I believe the claim of minimal domain knowledge is overstated. The intrinsic rewards proposed in this paper appear to be carefully designed and tailored to a relatively narrow set of control tasks, suggesting a significant degree of hand-crafting.
