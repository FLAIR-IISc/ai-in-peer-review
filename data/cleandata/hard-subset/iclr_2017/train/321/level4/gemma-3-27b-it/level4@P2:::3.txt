This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that leverages stochastic neural networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment. These skills are then used to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach aims to combine the benefits of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, requiring minimal domain knowledge for the proxy reward design. The experimental results demonstrate effective skill learning and improved performance across a range of tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea is promising and the experimental results are encouraging, the paper suffers from a lack of thorough analysis regarding failure modes and a somewhat overstated novelty claim concerning the minimal domain knowledge required. Additionally, certain aspects of the method, like the discretization in the information-theoretic regularizer, lack sufficient justification.

The core idea of using SNNs for skill discovery and leveraging those skills in a hierarchical framework is well-motivated and aligns with current research trends in addressing sparse reward and long-horizon challenges in RL. The combination of SNNs with the mutual information regularizer is a reasonable approach to encourage diversity in learned skills. The experiments on locomotion and maze tasks provide initial evidence of the framework’s effectiveness. The paper is generally well-written, and the organization is logical.

However, several key concerns prevent a positive assessment. First, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of failure modes. The authors acknowledge instability issues with more complex robots like the Ant in an appendix, but this is not adequately addressed in the main body of the paper. Understanding *when* and *why* the approach fails is crucial for assessing its robustness and practical applicability. Specifically, what conditions lead to the SNN collapsing into a limited set of skills, or what prevents successful transfer to new tasks?

Second, the discretization of the state space in Section 5.3 (“let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated”) requires further justification. Why is discretization necessary, and how does the choice of grid resolution impact performance? What are the limitations of this approach, particularly in high-dimensional state spaces? The authors should discuss potential alternatives to discretization.

Third, the claim of requiring “minimal domain knowledge” compared to Heess et al. (2016) is debatable. While the proxy reward doesn’t require specifying *goals*, choosing a proxy reward like “proportional to the magnitude of speed” for a robot still represents significant domain knowledge – it's a pre-conception about what constitutes useful behavior. The authors should acknowledge that the proxy reward design requires careful consideration and isn’t entirely domain-agnostic. 

Finally, the experiments, while positive, are limited. While the authors include results for a more complex robot (Snake) in the appendix, a more comprehensive evaluation across diverse embodiments and tasks would strengthen the paper’s claims.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (SNNs, MI regularizer, hierarchical structure) to the overall performance.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the MI regularization coefficient (αH) and the number of latent variables (K).
*   **Visualization of Skills:** Provide more visual examples of the learned skills, perhaps through animations or visualizations of the SNN's latent space.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** Compare the performance to more recent and advanced skill discovery methods, beyond VIME and Heess et al. (2016).
*   **Address limitations**: Discuss the limitations of the framework, such as the computational cost of training SNNs, and potential solutions to mitigate these limitations.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the failure modes of your approach, particularly concerning instability with more complex robots? What strategies could be employed to mitigate these failures?
2.  What is the rationale behind discretizing the state space for the mutual information calculation? How does the grid resolution affect the performance, and have you explored alternative approaches without discretization?
3.  Do you believe the choice of proxy reward is truly domain-agnostic, or does it require some level of prior knowledge about the task environment? Could you discuss the impact of different proxy rewards on the learned skills?
4.   How does the computational cost of training the SNN scale with the dimensionality of the state and action spaces? What techniques could be used to improve the scalability of the approach?
5.  What experiments have you done to analyze how the learning curves vary depending on the pre-trained SNN used? Can you identify properties of the pre-trained SNN that lead to better performance in downstream tasks?
