This paper presents interesting work on hierarchical reinforcement learning, building upon and extending related approaches such as those of Heess et al. The core contribution lies in a novel framework for learning hierarchical policies with a focus on disentangling agent-centric and environment-centric dynamics through mutual information regularization. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating successful navigation of benchmarks that have proven challenging for previous methods. However, the analysis of these results could be strengthened.

Overall, the paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in reinforcement learning – scaling to complex, long-horizon tasks. My primary concerns relate to the framing of the pre-training phase and the depth of analysis surrounding the observed experimental results.

Here’s a more detailed breakdown of my comments:

(1) The terminology of “intrinsic motivation” feels somewhat misapplied in this context. While the authors frame the pre-training phase as driven by intrinsic reward, the chosen pre-training objectives – maximizing speed for locomotion and grasping success for manipulation – are demonstrably *extrinsic* to the ultimate downstream tasks. These objectives directly relate to successful performance in the environments the agent will eventually operate within, and share similarities with the task-relevant pre-training used in Heess et al. The use of “intrinsic” currently implies a reward signal independent of environmental goals, which is not the case here. I recommend reconsidering this terminology to avoid potential confusion.

(2) The mutual information regularization is an elegant and generally effective technique. However, the authors acknowledge that it does *not* consistently improve performance across all environments, specifically noting a lack of benefit in the more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3). This is a crucial observation, and I believe a deeper analysis of this result is warranted. What characteristics of these mazes might explain the failure of mutual information regularization to provide further gains? Is it related to the complexity of the state space, the density of the optimal trajectory, or perhaps limitations in the capacity of the learned representations? Exploring potential explanations would significantly strengthen the paper.

(3) The paper clearly defines the S<sub>agent</sub> subspace, representing the agent’s controllable dynamics. However, the definition of S<sub>rest</sub> – encompassing the remaining, environment-determined dynamics – is less explicit. While the agent’s dynamics are specified in Duan et al., complete replicability requires a precise and unambiguous description of how S<sub>rest</sub> is defined and implemented. Did I miss this detail within the paper, or would clarification regarding its construction be beneficial? A clear specification of S<sub>rest</sub> is essential for others to reproduce and build upon this work.

(4) Understanding the agent’s decision-making process in a hierarchical setting is crucial. It would be highly valuable to include an analysis of the agent's *switching behavior* between the high-level and low-level policies. How frequently does the agent switch? What cues trigger these switches? Furthermore, a more general analysis of the learned policies would be welcome. What are the common failure modes observed during training and execution? How does the timing of switches – for example, switching more frequently or less frequently – impact overall performance? Investigating these questions would provide deeper insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach. 



In summary, this is a strong paper with a compelling contribution. Addressing the points raised above – particularly regarding the terminology of “intrinsic motivation,” the analysis of the mutual information regularization in complex environments, the explicit definition of S<sub>rest</sub>, and the analysis of policy and switching behavior – would substantially enhance the paper’s clarity, impact, and reproducibility.
