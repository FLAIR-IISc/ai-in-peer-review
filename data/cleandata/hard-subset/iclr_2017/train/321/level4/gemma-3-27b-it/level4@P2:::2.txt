This paper introduces a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging stochastic neural networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn diverse skills for improved exploration in tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea – pre-training a diverse set of skills and then composing them with a high-level policy – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in RL. A key contribution is the use of mutual information to encourage skill diversity during pre-training, and the demonstration that this approach can effectively learn interpretable skills and boost performance across a range of downstream tasks. The visualizations are particularly helpful in understanding the learned skills.

**Decision:** Accept (with minor revisions)

The primary reason for acceptance is the novel and effective combination of SNNs with an information-theoretic regularizer for skill learning. This addresses a crucial aspect of hierarchical RL – ensuring the learned skills are diverse and useful – and demonstrably improves performance on challenging sparse reward tasks. The comparison to a strong baseline, which directly combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, is insightful and highlights the benefits of hierarchical abstraction.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of SNNs, coupled with a mutual information objective, to hierarchical RL is a novel approach. This moves beyond traditional hand-engineered hierarchies or purely reward-based skill discovery.
*   **Effectiveness:** The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines, particularly on tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The ablation studies (although limited) suggest the importance of both the SNN architecture and the information-theoretic regularizer.
*   **Interpretability:** The visualization of learned skills, linked to the latent code of the SNNs, provides a degree of interpretability that is often lacking in deep RL methods.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The paper follows a rigorous experimental setup, comparing against relevant baselines and providing clear reporting of results.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Limitations of Sub-Policy Diversity:** While the current approach works well for locomotion tasks, the limited differentiation allowed by the concatenation and bilinear integration methods might become a bottleneck in more complex domains (e.g., manipulation) where ideal sub-policies are inherently more diverse. Exploring more expressive integration techniques could be beneficial.
*   **Hierarchical Policy Flexibility:** Fixing the sub-policies and the time steps for each skill limits the adaptability of the hierarchical structure. Discussing the potential benefits of end-to-end training, where both the sub-policies and the manager network are jointly optimized, would strengthen the paper. This could involve referencing related work on hierarchical REPS or option-critic methods.
*   **Domain Specificity of Rewards:** The reliance on proxy rewards during pre-training inevitably introduces a degree of domain specificity. Acknowledging this limitation and exploring ways to minimize it (e.g., learning more general reward functions) would be valuable.
*   **Clarify Baseline Performance:** Provide a deeper analysis of *why* the “CoM maze reward” baseline performs so poorly. Is it purely due to long-term credit assignment, or does it lack the benefit of the diverse skill set pre-trained by the proposed method? Acknowledging if the baseline utilized the experience from the pre-trained policies would also be helpful.
*   **Clarify Multi-Policy vs. SNN:** Briefly define "Multi-policy" in the text, explaining its distinction from the SNN approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   In Figure 1, your description of concatenation and bilinear integration appears accurate. However, could you confirm that the bilinear integration effectively results in a different weight matrix connecting the observation to the first hidden layer, dependent on the latent variable?
*   Could you elaborate on the intuition behind the poor performance of the “CoM maze reward” baseline? Did it also incorporate any learning from the pre-training phase, or was it trained independently?
*   The paper highlights the benefits of skill diversity. Could you discuss how the proposed method might be extended to dynamically adapt the skill set during the learning process, rather than relying on a fixed set of pre-trained skills?
