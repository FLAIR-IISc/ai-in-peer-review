- Introduces a novel hierarchical RL method using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results versus a baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse environmental rewards, arguing reward smoothing alone is insufficient.  
- Authors attribute success to improved long-term credit assignment and hierarchical abstraction; reviewer notes enforced diversity during sub-policy training may also drive gains.  
- Suggests further exploration of how diversity enforcement affects baseline performance and connections to end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., Hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Visualizations are clear and informative.  
- Concern: sub-policy differentiation via concatenation and bilinear integration (mainly affecting first hidden layer) may not scale to tasks requiring substantially different sub-policies (e.g., complex manipulation).  
- Concern: manager is trained with fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and fixed durations, which may limit applicability and necessitates stronger discussion or experiments on end-to-end hierarchical learning.  
- Concern: reliance on intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appears domain-specific due to fixed sub-policy constraint and should be acknowledged.  
- Question: why does the baseline perform poorly in the CoM maze reward setting, and does the baseline account for pre-training experience of evaluated policies?  
- Question: please clarify the distinction between the “multi-policy” baseline and the proposed SNN approach.  
- Question about Figure 1: confirm whether (1) concatenation modifies bias terms of the first hidden layer based on the categorical latent, and (2) bilinear integration implements different input-to-first-layer weight matrices for each latent.
