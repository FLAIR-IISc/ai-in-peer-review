The paper introduces a hierarchical reinforcement learning method leveraging stochastic neural networks, and notably incorporates an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as a reward signal to encourage a diverse set of sub-policies. A key strength of the work is the compelling comparison to a strong baseline – combining intrinsic and sparse rewards – which demonstrates that simply smoothing the reward signal is insufficient for solving these tasks. Beyond the authors’ explanation relating to long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, the observed performance difference may be attributable to the diversity constraint applied during sub-policy training, a feature absent in the baseline. Investigating this further could provide valuable insights for improving existing baselines and informing the development of end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, such as hierarchical REPS or option-critic methods. The visualizations are well-executed and effectively communicate the results. 

This paper presents a promising research direction, and could be further strengthened by addressing the following considerations:

1) **Sub-policy Diversification:** The concatenation and bilinear integration methods currently allow for limited differentiation in the learned sub-policies, operating primarily on the first hidden layer weights. While sufficient for the current locomotion tasks which require relatively similar policies, this limitation may become more apparent in domains demanding more diverse sub-policies – for example, manipulation tasks where optimal strategies vary significantly based on the initial state.  Exploring the method's performance in such more complex, non-locomotion environments would be insightful.

2) **Hierarchical Policy Limitations:** The manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy timescales. This places a substantial burden on the quality of the intrinsic rewards and the learned sub-policies to effectively address downstream tasks. Further discussion and results regarding the method's robustness to imperfect sub-policies, and potential connections to end-to-end hierarchical learning, would be valuable.

3) **Domain Specificity of Intrinsic Rewards:**  The current approach to intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appears somewhat domain-specific, likely a consequence of the fixed sub-policies described in point (2).
