- Proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic identifiability reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Strong empirical comparison shows smoothing rewards alone (intrinsic + sparse) is insufficient; hierarchy + identifiability outperforms.  
- Performance gap may stem from the diversity constraint on sub-policy training, which the baseline lacks.  
- Suggest investigating how diversity constraint, rather than hierarchy alone, drives improvements to inform baselines and end-to-end hierarchical methods.  
- Visualizations are clear and effectively communicate results.  
- Limitation: concatenation and bilinear integration only differentiate sub-policies via first-layer biases/weights, which may limit diversity for complex tasks (e.g., manipulation).  
- Recommend evaluating on non-locomotion domains to test the need for richer sub-policy differentiation.  
- Limitation: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed timescales, making success heavily dependent on intrinsic reward quality and pre-trained sub-policies.  
- Suggest analyzing robustness to imperfect sub-policies and connections to end-to-end hierarchical learning (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Concern: intrinsic/unsupervised rewards may be domain-specific due to fixed sub-policies.  
- Clarification requested: intuition for poor baseline performance in Section 7.3 and whether baseline accounts for pre-training experience.  
- Clarification requested: distinction between "multi-policy" and the SNN approach, and confirmation that concatenation biases the first hidden layer while bilinear integration uses different input-to-first-layer weight matrices.
