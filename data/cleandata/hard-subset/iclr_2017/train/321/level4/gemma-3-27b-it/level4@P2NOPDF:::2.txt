This paper introduces a novel method for hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) utilizing stochastic neural networks (SNNs). A key contribution is the incorporation of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward signal, encouraging the learning of a diverse set of sub-policies. The results are particularly compelling in comparison to a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse environmental rewards, demonstrating that simply smoothing the reward signal is insufficient for solving these tasks. The authors attribute this to difficulties in long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction; however, the diversity requirement enforced during sub-policy training – which is absent in the baseline – may also play a significant role. Exploring this further could offer insights for improving existing baselines and informing the development of new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, such as those found in the Hierarchical REPS or option-critic literature. The visualizations included are clear and informative.

Overall, this work presents a promising direction for hierarchical RL. Several areas could potentially strengthen the paper further:

1) **Limited Sub-policy Diversity:** The proposed method utilizes concatenation and bilinear integration to differentiate sub-policies, operating primarily through modifications to the first hidden layer’s weights. While this appears sufficient for the tested locomotion tasks – which largely require variations on a similar underlying policy – it’s unclear whether this approach will scale to domains demanding more substantial diversity in sub-policies. Investigating performance in more complex domains, such as manipulation, where optimal strategies can vary significantly based on the initial state, would be insightful. For example, a manipulation task might require drastically different approaches depending on the object’s starting position or orientation.

2) **Constraints on Hierarchical Policy Learning:** The manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy durations. This necessitates highly effective, pre-trained sub-policies to succeed in downstream tasks. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion – and ideally, supporting results – addressing the limitations of this approach and potential connections to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods.

3) **Domain Specificity of Intrinsic Rewards:** The current reliance on intrinsic, or unsupervised, rewards appears somewhat domain-specific, stemming from the fixed sub-policy constraint described above. While perhaps unavoidable given the current architecture, acknowledging this limitation would be beneficial.
