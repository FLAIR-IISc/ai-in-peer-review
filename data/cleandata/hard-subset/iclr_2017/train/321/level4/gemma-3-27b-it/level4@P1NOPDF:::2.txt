The paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning using stochastic neural networks. A key contribution is the introduction of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward, encouraging the learning of a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A particularly strong result is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this seemingly smoother reward signal is insufficient to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ argument regarding the difficulties of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, a possible explanation for the baseline’s failure is the lack of a diversity requirement during sub-policy training – a requirement that *is* present in the proposed method. It would be interesting to explore whether this insight could improve the baseline performance and inspire new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, as seen in works like hierarchical REPS or option-critic methods. The visualizations are well-done and informative.

The paper presents a promising direction and could be further strengthened by addressing the following points.

1) **Limited diversification of sub-policies:** Both concatenation and bilinear integration allow for only limited differentiation in sub-policies, primarily through adjustments to the first hidden layer’s weights. While this isn't problematic in the tested locomotion tasks – which largely require similar policies with minimal variation – this limitation could become more apparent in domains where optimal sub-policies are more diverse. Therefore, it would be valuable to see the method applied to more challenging, non-locomotion domains such as manipulation, where the optimal approach to solving a task can vary significantly depending on the initial state.

2) **Limitations in hierarchical policy learning:** The manager network is trained while the sub-policies remain fixed, and the time horizon for the sub-policies is predetermined. This necessitates highly effective “intrinsic” rewards and well-learned sub-policies to successfully solve downstream tasks. Further discussion and results on addressing cases where this isn’t true would be beneficial, ideally connecting the work to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3) **Domain-specificity of intrinsic rewards:** Due to the limitations outlined in (2), the current approach seems reliant on domain-specific, or potentially supervised, intrinsic rewards. This appears unavoidable given the current framework.
