This paper presents a compelling framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, effectively combining skill learning via Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a pre-training approach guided by a proxy reward. The core idea of leveraging SNNs to learn a diverse set of skills and subsequently utilizing these skills for faster adaptation in downstream tasks with sparse rewards is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in the field. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating performance improvements over existing methods on benchmark tasks, including those previously considered intractable.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel, technically sound, and the results are convincing. The use of SNNs and the information-theoretic regularizer represent a thoughtful and effective combination for skill discovery.

The paper clearly articulates the problem of sparse rewards and long horizons in reinforcement learning, and positions the proposed approach within the existing literature on hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation. The framework is well-described, and the experimental setup appears rigorous. The results consistently demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method, particularly in tasks where naive exploration strategies fail. The ablation studies, showing the impact of the bilinear integration and the MI bonus, provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the different components of the approach.

Several points could further strengthen the paper. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the proxy reward as a form of “intrinsic motivation,” a more nuanced discussion of this point would be beneficial. Specifically, elaborating on how the proxy reward inherently encodes prior knowledge related to the downstream tasks, as opposed to being purely intrinsic, would clarify the approach’s positioning. The observation that the MI bonus does not consistently improve performance across all maze environments (specifically 1, 2, and 3) warrants further investigation. Exploring potential reasons for this discrepancy—perhaps related to the specific maze geometries or the required skills—would be valuable. A more detailed description of the state space factorization into *S<sub>agent</sub>* and *S<sub>rest</sub>* would enhance reproducibility. While *S<sub>agent</sub>* is mentioned, a clearer definition of *S<sub>rest</sub>* for each task would be helpful. Finally, a more in-depth analysis of the policy switching behavior, including potential failure modes and the influence of the switching time *T*, would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the system’s dynamics.

Specifically, I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific characteristics of the proxy reward that make it suitable for the downstream tasks, and how this differs from a truly intrinsic reward signal that would be agnostic to the task structure?
2.  What potential explanations do you have for the lack of consistent improvement observed with the MI bonus in mazes 1, 2, and 3? Were any alternative regularizers considered?
3.  Can you provide a more precise definition of *S<sub>rest</sub>* for each of the experimental tasks (Locomotion + Maze, Locomotion + Food Collection)?
4.  What were the observed failure modes related to the policy switching behavior, particularly in more complex environments? Did varying *T* mitigate these issues, and if so, how?
