The paper introduces a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning leveraging stochastic neural networks. A key contribution is the use of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward signal, encouraging the learning of a diverse set of sub-policies. A compelling result is the comparison to a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards; the paper demonstrates that this seemingly smoother reward structure fails to solve the tasks, suggesting that simply smoothing the reward is insufficient. Beyond the authors’ argument concerning difficulties with long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, a possible explanation for the baseline’s poor performance is the lack of a diversity requirement during sub-policy training – a component present in the proposed method. It would be insightful to explore whether this observation can inform improvements to the baseline or inspire new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, such as those found in hierarchical REPS or option-critic architectures. The visualizations are well-executed and informative.

The paper presents a promising research direction, and could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1) Limited sub-policy diversification: Both the concatenation and bilinear integration methods allow for only limited differentiation between sub-policies, achieved through modifications to the first hidden layer weights. While this is not problematic in the tested locomotion tasks – which primarily require variations on a core locomotion policy – this limitation may become more apparent in domains where optimal sub-policies are considerably more diverse. Exploring the method’s performance on more challenging, non-locomotion domains, such as manipulation tasks where the optimal approach varies significantly depending on the starting state, would be valuable.

2) Constraints on hierarchical policy learning: The manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies, and the durations of sub-policy execution are predetermined. This necessitates high-quality, “intrinsic” rewards and well-learned sub-policies to successfully complete downstream tasks. Further discussion and results regarding scenarios where this is not the case, ideally connected to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning, would be beneficial.

3) Domain specificity of intrinsic rewards: The current reliance on intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appears domain-specific, a consequence of the fixed sub-policy training described in (2).

Regarding some last-minute questions: What is the intuition behind the poor performance of the strong baseline in section 7.3 – specifically, the “CoM maze reward” baseline? Does this baseline account for the experience gained during pre-training of the policies? Furthermore, how does “multi-policy” differ from the use of stochastic neural networks (SNNs)?

Concerning figure 1, is it accurate that (1) concatenation implements differentiation by applying different biases to the first hidden layer based on the categorical value, and (2) bilinear integration implements differentiation by using different weight matrices to connect the observation to the first hidden layer?
