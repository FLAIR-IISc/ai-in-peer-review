This work presents an interesting approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning, leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill acquisition and a mutual information regularizer to encourage skill diversity. The experiments demonstrate strong performance, achieving results on challenging benchmarks that have previously posed difficulties for other algorithms. While the core ideas are promising, the analysis of the experimental results could be strengthened.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem – learning effective exploration strategies for sparse reward tasks – and presents a novel and well-motivated approach. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully combines the benefits of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical RL, creating a framework that first learns reusable skills in a pre-training environment and then leverages those skills for faster learning in downstream tasks. The use of SNNs allows for efficient representation of multi-modal policies and facilitates weight sharing among different skills. The mutual information regularization is a clever addition, promoting diversity in the learned skills. The empirical results are particularly strong, consistently outperforming baseline methods and achieving successful performance on challenging benchmarks, including those from Duan et al. (2016).

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

The term “intrinsic motivation” might be misleading. While the proxy reward guides skill learning, it’s inherently tied to downstream task success (e.g., rewarding speed for locomotion is directly relevant to navigating mazes). Framing it as a form of pre-training focused on useful behaviors, rather than purely intrinsic motivation, might be more accurate. 

The observation that the mutual information regularization doesn’t consistently improve performance in all maze environments (specifically mazes 1, 2, and 3) is noted, but deserves further investigation. Exploring potential reasons for this – perhaps the specific maze geometries don’t benefit from the increased skill diversity – would strengthen the analysis.

The paper mentions a factorization between *S<sub>agent</sub>* and *S<sub>rest</sub>*, as inspired by Konidaris & Barto (2007). While *S<sub>agent</sub>* is clearly defined as the robot itself, a more precise description of *S<sub>rest</sub>* would improve reproducibility. Specifically, detailing what constitutes the task-specific state components for each environment would be helpful.

Further analysis of the policy behavior would be valuable. Investigating the switching frequency between skills, potential failure modes associated with switching, and the impact of the switching time (*T*) on performance could provide deeper insights into the learned hierarchical structure. An analysis of the learned skills themselves—what specific behaviors correspond to each latent code—would also enrich the paper.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasons why the mutual information regularization doesn't consistently improve performance across all maze environments? Are there specific characteristics of the mazes where it’s less effective?

2.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the *S<sub>rest</sub>* component of the state space for each environment (maze and gather)? Specifically, what information is included in *S<sub>rest</sub>* beyond the robot's state?

3.  Did you observe any patterns in the skill switching behavior? For instance, are there specific situations where the agent struggles to switch to the appropriate skill, and if so, what causes these difficulties?

4.  Could you provide examples of the behaviors associated with specific latent codes learned during pre-training? Visualizing or describing these skills would help clarify their interpretability and usefulness.
