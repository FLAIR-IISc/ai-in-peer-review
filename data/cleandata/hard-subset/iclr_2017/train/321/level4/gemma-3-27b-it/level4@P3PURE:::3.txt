I find the setting presented in this paper appealing, but I have several criticisms and questions.

(1) What are the failure modes of this work? As the richness of achievable behaviors increases, I anticipate this approach will struggle with discovering a diverse set of skills.

(2) In Section 5.3, the statement “let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated” raises a question about the state space. Is the environment discretized? If so, why was discretization chosen, and what are the implications of using a continuous state space instead?

(3) Expanding on the first point, does this approach scale to more complex embodiments? For example, would it perform effectively with a 5-link swimmer rather than the 2-link example presented? Assessing this is important for understanding the generality of the method.

(4) The authors claim that “Recently, Heess et al. (2016) have independently proposed to learn a range of skills in a pre-training environment that will be useful for the downstream tasks, which is similar to our framework. However, their pre-training setup requires a set of goals to be specified. In comparison, we use intrinsic rewards as the only signal to the agent during the pre-training phase, the construction of which only requires very minimal domain knowledge.” I disagree with this characterization. The intrinsic rewards proposed in this paper are also quite hand-crafted and appear specific to a limited set of control tasks.
