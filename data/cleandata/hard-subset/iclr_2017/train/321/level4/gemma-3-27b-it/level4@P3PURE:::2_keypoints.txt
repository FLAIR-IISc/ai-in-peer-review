- Introduces hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic identifiability reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Strong empirical result: combining intrinsic and sparse rewards as a baseline fails, showing that smoother reward shaping is insufficient.  
- Possible reason for baseline failure: it lacks an explicit diversity requirement during sub-policy training, which the proposed method includes.  
- Suggestion: investigate whether adding diversity requirements can improve the baseline and inspire new end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Visualizations are well-executed and informative.  
- Limited diversification of sub-policies: concatenation and bilinear integration only minimally differentiate sub-policies via first-layer modulation, which may be insufficient for non-locomotion tasks requiring more diverse behaviors.  
- Recommendation: evaluate on harder, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) where optimal sub-policies vary with initial state.  
- Hierarchical learning limitation: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed option durations, which forces reliance on the quality of pre-trained components.  
- Recommendation: discuss/experiment with scenarios where intrinsic rewards or sub-policies are suboptimal and connect to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches.  
- Intrinsic rewards appear domain-specific and akin to supervised signals given the current fixed-subpolicy setup.  
- Clarification requested: intuition for the poor performance of the strong baseline in Section 7.3.  
- Clarification requested: does the "CoM maze reward" baseline incorporate prior pre-training experience?  
- Clarification requested: what exactly distinguishes a “multi-policy” approach from using stochastic neural networks (SNNs)?
