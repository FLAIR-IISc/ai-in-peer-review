This paper presents a compelling framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn reusable skills. The core idea – pre-training a diverse skill set with a minimal proxy reward and then utilizing these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in RL, particularly with sparse rewards and long horizons. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, showcasing improved performance compared to existing methods.

I recommend **accepting** this paper, with minor revisions. The technical soundness of the approach is strong, and the results are promising. The combination of SNNs with the mutual information regularization is a novel and effective way to encourage skill diversity.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed method. The authors provide a solid grounding in related work, correctly identifying the limitations of existing approaches. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed framework across multiple tasks. The provided code and videos further enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the work.

However, several points could be clarified to further strengthen the paper. First, a discussion of potential failure modes, particularly with more complex embodiments, would be valuable. While the results with the simpler swimmer robot are promising, it’s important to address the limitations of the approach when applied to more challenging systems. Specifically, how does the method perform when the learned skills are insufficient for the downstream task, or when the switching between skills introduces instability? This is partially addressed in the appendix with the Ant robot, but expanding on this would be beneficial.

Second, the discretization of the state space in Section 5.3 for the mutual information calculation warrants further explanation. Why is discretization necessary, and what impact does it have on the performance? Are there alternative approaches to estimating mutual information that avoid discretization? 

Third, the claim regarding the minimal domain knowledge required for the proxy reward compared to Heess et al. (2016) is debatable. While the proposed reward is simpler than specifying goals, it still requires careful design to encourage useful behaviors (e.g., using speed for the swimmer robot). A more nuanced discussion acknowledging this trade-off would be appropriate.

Finally, exploring the sensitivity of the results to the switch time *T* in the hierarchical policy, and perhaps considering learned switching policies, would be a valuable addition. The current fixed *T* might be suboptimal for some tasks, and a more adaptive approach could potentially further improve performance.

Specifically, I would like the authors to address the following questions:

*   What strategies could be employed to mitigate instability issues when switching between skills, particularly in more complex robots like the Ant?
*   How does the choice of discretization resolution in Section 5.3 impact the performance of the MI regularization? Were different resolutions explored?
*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the design choices involved in defining the proxy reward, and how these choices might affect the learned skill set?
*   How does the performance vary with different values of *T*? Is there a systematic way to determine an optimal *T* for a given task?
