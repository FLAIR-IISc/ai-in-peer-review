## Review of “Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of sparse reward reinforcement learning by proposing a hierarchical framework that pre-trains a diverse set of skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer, then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks. The core idea of learning reusable skills with minimal domain knowledge is compelling and addresses a significant limitation of traditional RL approaches. The authors present a well-motivated approach, placing it within the existing literature on hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation, and demonstrate promising results on several benchmark tasks.

**Decision:** Reject. While the approach is interesting and the paper is well-written, the experimental results, particularly the variability and limited gains over strong baselines, are not sufficiently convincing to warrant acceptance at this time. Additionally, the claim of minimal domain knowledge required for the proxy reward is debatable.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary concern stems from the experimental validation. While the ablation studies (Figs. 5, 6, 7) demonstrate the benefits of SNNs and the MI bonus, the improvement over the baseline with a simple CoM-based intrinsic reward is often marginal, especially in the Maze tasks (Fig. 6a-c). The authors acknowledge the high variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs (Sec. 7.3, Appendix C.4), indicating a lack of robustness and consistent benefit. The performance gains on the Gather task (Fig. 6d) are more substantial, but it’s unclear if this difference is consistently observed, given the variability.  

Furthermore, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *why* the proposed approach succeeds or fails in different scenarios. The discussion of failure modes is limited (particularly in the main text), and the supplemental material on the Ant robot highlights significant instability issues. A more thorough investigation of these failure cases is crucial for understanding the limitations of the method and guiding future research.

The authors' claim regarding minimal domain knowledge is also questionable. While the proxy reward (e.g., speed for locomotion) is relatively simple, the choice of *which* proxy reward to use still requires domain-specific insight. For example, rewarding speed is a reasonable choice for locomotion but would be inappropriate for a manipulation task. The argument that this is less domain knowledge than specifying a full hierarchy is valid, but it doesn't eliminate the need for informed reward design.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Detailed Failure Analysis:** Expand the discussion of failure modes, specifically addressing the instability observed with more complex robots like Ant. Provide an analysis of the situations where the learned skills are ineffective and why.
*   **Clarify Discretization:** In Section 5.3, clarify the rationale behind discretizing the state space for the MI calculation. What impact does the grid resolution have on performance? What are the drawbacks of not discretizing the space?
*   **Robustness and Generalization:** Investigate the robustness of the approach to different initialization conditions and hyperparameters. Present results on a wider range of tasks and robot morphologies to demonstrate generalization.
*   **Comparison to Alternative Baselines:** Compare the proposed approach to more recent and sophisticated intrinsic motivation methods beyond VIME.
*   **Ablation Study on Proxy Reward:** Conduct an ablation study to assess the sensitivity of the results to the choice of proxy reward.
*   **Visualize Learned Skills:** Include videos or visualizations that clearly demonstrate the diversity and interpretability of the learned skills. While visitation plots are helpful, they don't fully capture the dynamic behavior of the skills.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the instability observed with the Ant robot (and mentioned in Appendix D.2), what modifications to the architecture or training procedure could mitigate this issue? Specifically, could incorporating a safety layer or a more stable control policy help prevent the robot from falling over during skill switching?
2.  The MI bonus relies on discretizing the state space. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of grid resolution? What are the potential downsides of using a very fine or very coarse grid?
3.  You claim minimal domain knowledge in the reward design. How would your approach adapt to a fundamentally different task, such as object manipulation, where a speed-based reward would be inappropriate? Would the search for an appropriate proxy reward become more challenging?
4.  The variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs is significant. What factors contribute to this variability, and are there strategies to reduce it and ensure more consistent performance?
