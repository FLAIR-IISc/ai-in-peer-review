## Review for ICLR 2017 - STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

This paper presents a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment. These skills are then utilized by a high-level policy to improve learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach effectively combines the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, requiring minimal domain knowledge for pre-training. The results demonstrate significant performance improvements across a range of challenging tasks.

**Decision:** Accept (with minor revisions)

**Reasons:**

The paper tackles a significant challenge in reinforcement learning – exploration in sparse reward environments. The proposed approach is well-motivated, building upon existing work in both intrinsic motivation and hierarchical RL, but offering a compelling new combination using SNNs. The core idea of learning a reusable set of skills, guided by a simple proxy reward and encouraged to diversify via the information-theoretic regularizer, is both intuitive and effective, as evidenced by the experimental results. The paper demonstrates a clear performance gain over strong baselines, suggesting a valuable contribution to the field.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, utilizing established benchmarks and providing insightful visualizations of the learned skills. The ablation studies, particularly regarding the impact of the MI bonus and the different integration methods within the SNN, are well-executed and help to justify the design choices. The comparison against the baseline using a direct intrinsic reward (CoM speed) is particularly strong, highlighting the benefits of hierarchical abstraction and skill diversification. The thoroughness of the experiments and the accompanying visualizations contribute to the credibility of the claims. The code availability is also a significant positive aspect, enabling reproducibility and further research. 

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is strong, several areas could benefit from further exploration. 

1. **Sub-policy Diversity:** The current integration methods (concatenation and bilinear pooling) primarily affect the first layer of the network. While sufficient for the locomotion tasks tested, this might become a limitation in domains requiring more diverse sub-policies (e.g., manipulation tasks with varying state-dependent requirements). Investigating more sophisticated integration techniques or network architectures to enable greater sub-policy differentiation could strengthen the approach.

2. **Hierarchical Policy Flexibility:** The current framework trains the high-level policy while keeping the sub-policies fixed, and also employs a fixed time horizon for skill execution. This reliance on well-defined, pre-trained skills may limit adaptability. Exploring end-to-end training or incorporating mechanisms for dynamic skill selection and variable time horizons could improve robustness and performance, potentially linking the work to existing approaches like hierarchical REPS or option-critic methods.

3. **Domain Specificity of Rewards:** While the proxy reward minimizes domain knowledge, its effectiveness still relies on its appropriateness for the pre-training environment. A discussion on how to adapt or design these rewards for different types of tasks would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The baseline using the CoM speed reward performs surprisingly poorly in Section 7.3. Could you elaborate on the specific reasons for this performance, considering that the pre-trained policies have extra experience? Does the baseline also utilize a hierarchical structure, or is it a single policy attempting to learn directly from the sparse reward?

2. Could you clarify the difference between "Multi-policy" and the SNN-based approach? Is "Multi-policy" simply training separate policies independently and switching between them, while the SNN represents a unified network capable of representing multiple policies within its weights?

3. Regarding Figure 1, your description of concatenation and bilinear integration seems accurate. However, is it fair to say the bilinear integration offers a more expressive representation due to its ability to model interactions between the observation and the latent variable, beyond just a bias shift or weight modification?
