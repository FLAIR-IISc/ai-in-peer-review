The paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning utilizing stochastic neural networks. The key contribution lies in introducing an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward, encouraging the learning of a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A notable result is the demonstration that directly combining intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards – a seemingly smoother approach – fails to solve the tested tasks, highlighting the benefit of hierarchical abstraction and diverse skill learning. While the authors attribute this to difficulties in long-term credit assignment, another potential explanation is the diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training, absent in the baseline. Exploring whether incorporating diversity into the baseline could yield improvements, and investigating connections to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods like hierarchical REPS or option-critic, would be valuable avenues for future work. The visualizations provided are clear and informative.

I recommend accepting this paper, with a score of 7/10. The approach is promising and offers a compelling combination of techniques to address the challenges of sparse reward reinforcement learning. The experimental validation, though limited in scope, provides evidence of the method’s effectiveness.

Several points could strengthen the paper further. 
1) **Limited Sub-Policy Diversification:** The current integration methods – concatenation and bilinear integration – primarily modulate the first hidden layer weights, potentially limiting the differentiation achievable between sub-policies. While sufficient for the locomotion-focused tasks evaluated, this limitation might become more apparent in domains requiring a wider range of skills, such as manipulation tasks where optimal sub-policies can vary significantly based on state. Investigating the application of this approach to such domains would be insightful.
2) **Fixed Sub-Policies and Time Horizons:** The manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed execution time horizons. This reliance on pre-trained, high-quality sub-policies could be a bottleneck. Exploring methods for adapting the sub-policies during downstream task learning, perhaps through end-to-end training with techniques like straight-through estimators, or allowing for learned termination conditions, would enhance the framework’s robustness.
3) **Domain Specificity of Intrinsic Rewards:** The intrinsic rewards used appear somewhat domain-specific, requiring knowledge of relevant signals (e.g., speed for locomotion). Further discussion on the generalizability of this approach to settings where defining such intrinsic rewards is challenging would be beneficial.
