This paper presents an interesting approach to unsupervised skill discovery, and I appreciate the framework proposed. However, I have several concerns and questions regarding its limitations and the novelty of its contributions.

First, I am curious about the potential failure modes of this method. As the complexity and richness of possible behaviors increase, I anticipate challenges in discovering a truly diverse and useful set of skills. Specifically, how does the approach perform when faced with environments demanding a wider range of capabilities than those demonstrated in the experiments?

Second, Section 5.3 introduces “X” as a random variable representing the agent's current grid location. Is the underlying state space discretized? If so, the motivation for this discretization should be clarified, along with a discussion of how the method would perform without it. The impact of discretization on the learned skills and overall performance is an important consideration.

Third, building upon the point about complexity, I question the scalability of this approach to more complex embodiments. While the 2-link robot provides a clear demonstration, it's crucial to assess its generality. Would the approach remain effective with a more complex morphology, such as a 5-link swimmer? This is particularly important for understanding the method’s applicability to real-world robotic systems.

Finally, the authors contrast their work with Heess et al. (2016), claiming their approach requires less domain knowledge due to the use of intrinsic rewards, while Heess et al. require specified goals. However, I disagree with the assertion that the proposed intrinsic rewards are entirely free of hand-crafting. The rewards used in this paper appear quite specific and tailored to the particular control tasks considered, and their design necessitates significant domain knowledge. While avoiding explicit goal specification is a benefit, characterizing these intrinsic rewards as requiring “very minimal domain knowledge” seems an overstatement. A more nuanced discussion of the design choices involved in the reward function, and a comparison to the domain knowledge required by goal-conditioned approaches, would be beneficial.
