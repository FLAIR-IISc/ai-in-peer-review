### Review

#### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve complex tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of low-level skills is learned with minimal supervision, followed by a second phase where a high-level "manager" policy is trained to select from these pre-trained skills to solve specific downstream tasks.

The key contributions are twofold. First, the paper introduces a novel method for learning a rich repertoire of skills. It employs a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent a multi-modal policy, enabling a single network to encapsulate multiple distinct behaviors, which is more sample-efficient than training separate policies. To ensure the learned skills are diverse and interpretable, the authors introduce an elegant information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent skill code and the agent's state. Second, the paper demonstrates that this hierarchical structure, with frozen pre-trained skills, provides a powerful exploration strategy that enables agents to solve challenging benchmark tasks (e.g., locomotion in mazes) that were previously unsolvable by standard RL algorithms.

#### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-executed paper that presents a compelling and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method successfully solves a series of challenging continuous control benchmarks with sparse rewards, where prior methods have failed. The experiments are thorough and convincingly demonstrate that the hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and learning efficiency.
2.  **Novel and Effective Skill Discovery Method:** The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks with a mutual information bonus is a novel and valuable contribution for learning a diverse set of reusable skills. The paper clearly shows how this approach leads to a richer and more interpretable span of behaviors compared to baselines.

#### Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its empirical validation. The authors apply their framework to difficult locomotion and navigation tasks from the benchmark by Duan et al. (2016), which were designed to highlight the limitations of naive exploration strategies. The results, particularly in the maze environments, are impressive. The learning curves in Figure 6 clearly show that the proposed hierarchical architectures vastly outperform a strong baseline that uses the pre-training proxy reward directly on the downstream task. This provides strong evidence for the central claim that composing pre-trained primitives is an effective strategy for shrinking the effective time-horizon and tackling the credit assignment problem in sparse-reward settings.

Furthermore, the methodology for skill learning is well-motivated and sound. Using a single SNN is an efficient way to learn multiple skills by sharing weights. The introduction of the mutual information (MI) regularizer is a particularly elegant solution to the common problem of "mode collapse," where a multi-modal policy might learn redundant or very similar behaviors. The visitation plots in Figure 4 provide a clear and intuitive visualization of the effectiveness of this regularizer and the bilinear integration method, showing they consistently produce a wide and distinct set of skills (e.g., forward, backward, and turning motions) from a simple, generic proxy reward. This unsupervised discovery of interpretable skills is a significant step forward.

#### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept. The experimental results are strong, but the paper could be further improved with deeper analysis and clarification on a few points.

1.  **Analysis of MI Bonus Performance:** The MI bonus clearly helps generate a more diverse set of skills in pre-training. However, in the downstream maze tasks (Figures 6a-c), this diversity does not consistently translate into better performance compared to the SNN without the bonus. The authors note this but do not offer a deep interpretation. Could you hypothesize why this might be the case? Is it possible that for these specific mazes, a simpler set of forward/backward skills is sufficient, and the agent can use collisions with walls to reorient itself, making the turning skills learned via the MI bonus redundant? A more detailed discussion on this would strengthen the analysis.

2.  **Clarity on State Space Factorization:** For reproducibility, it would be beneficial to explicitly define the state space factorization `S_agent` and `S_rest` for the experimental domains. Section 6 mentions that `S_agent` is the robot's state and `S_rest` includes task-specific attributes like walls and goals. A more precise specification (e.g., a list of the sensor readings that constitute `S_rest` for each task) would be helpful for others looking to build on this work.

3.  **Analysis of Manager Policy and Failure Modes:** The paper demonstrates *that* the method works, but a deeper analysis of *how* it works would be very insightful. For instance, what does the learned high-level policy look like? Are there common sequences of skills that the manager learns to solve a particular maze? Additionally, I appreciate the discussion of failure modes and the analysis of the switching time `T` in the appendix. Given its importance, especially for understanding the limitations of the approach on unstable agents like the Ant, I suggest summarizing these key findings in the main body of the paper, perhaps in the Discussion section.

4.  **Framing of "Minimal Domain Knowledge":** The paper claims the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge." While it is certainly less prescriptive than hand-crafting options, a reward for forward speed is quite well-aligned with tasks that require locomotion. It would be helpful to briefly clarify that this reward encodes a general prior about the utility of movement, distinguishing it from more task-agnostic intrinsic motivation signals like prediction error or state novelty. This is a minor point of framing but would help place the work more precisely in the literature.
