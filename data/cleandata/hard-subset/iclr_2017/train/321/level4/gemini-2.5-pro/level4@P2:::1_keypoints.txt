- Proposes a two-stage hierarchical RL framework: pre-train low-level skills then train a high-level manager to solve sparse, long-horizon tasks.  
- Pre-training uses a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) in a simple environment with a proxy reward (e.g., forward speed).  
- Introduces an information-theoretic mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage diverse, distinct skills.  
- Freezes pre-trained skills and uses them as temporally-extended actions for the high-level policy in downstream tasks.  
- Empirical results are strong: method solves challenging locomotion and navigation benchmarks previously unsolved by standard RL.  
- Framework is novel, well-motivated, and effectively addresses exploration in sparse-reward settings.  
- Combination of SNN and MI bonus is an elegant contribution to unsupervised skill acquisition.  
- Using one SNN for multiple skills enables efficient weight sharing and better scalability than separate policies.  
- MI bonus demonstrably increases skill diversity (Figure 4).  
- The impact of MI on final downstream performance is inconsistent (Mazes 1–3), and the paper lacks analysis explaining this.  
- The term "intrinsic motivation" is misleading here because the proxy reward is extrinsic and aligned with downstream tasks; "unsupervised pre-training" or "auxiliary task" is more accurate.  
- Paper needs more qualitative analysis of how the high-level policy sequences and switches skills during successful trajectories.  
- Recommend discussing common failure modes (missing skills vs. poor high-level selection) and summarizing Ant-agent failure analysis in main text.  
- Request clearer reproducibility details: explicitly define S_agent vs. S_rest for each environment and summarize findings on the skill switch time T.  
- Questions for authors: why does MI diversity not consistently aid maze performance (over-specialization hypothesis)? and justification for labeling the proxy reward approach as "intrinsic motivation"?
