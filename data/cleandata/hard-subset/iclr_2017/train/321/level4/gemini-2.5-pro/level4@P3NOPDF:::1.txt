This paper presents a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning where a high-level policy learns to select from a set of low-level, pre-trained sub-policies to solve complex tasks. The experimental results are a key strength of this work. The method successfully completes several challenging locomotion and manipulation benchmarks, including maze navigation tasks that have proven difficult for prior methods. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed hierarchical control framework.

However, the paper could be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the learned policies and by refining some of the conceptual framing and experimental details. Below are my main points of feedback.

1.  **Framing of "Intrinsic Motivation":** While the pre-training phase is effective, the use of the term "intrinsic motivation" seems somewhat misaligned with its common usage in the RL community, where it typically refers to task-agnostic objectives like curiosity or novelty. In this work, the pre-training objectives—such as rewarding forward speed for a locomoting agent or successful grasping for a manipulator—are highly aligned with the downstream tasks. This approach functions more as a form of curriculum learning or task-specific skill pre-training. This is conceptually similar to the skill acquisition methods in related work (e.g., Heess et al.), and framing it as such might provide greater clarity and better position the work within the existing literature.

2.  **Analysis of Mutual Information Regularization:** The mutual information (MI) regularization term is an elegant method for encouraging diverse and predictable sub-policies, and it appears to work well in several domains. However, the authors correctly note that this regularization did not yield performance improvements on the more complex environments (Mazes 1, 2, and 3). This is a significant finding that warrants deeper investigation. Is there a hypothesis for this result? For instance, does the complexity of the navigation task require less distinct, more overlapping skills, making the diversity objective less helpful or even counter-productive? Or does the MI objective conflict with the primary task objective in these specific scenarios? Providing some analysis or interpretation for this negative result would greatly enhance the paper's contribution beyond a simple report of empirical findings.

3.  **Clarity on State Space Factorization:** For the sake of clarity and reproducibility, the factorization of the state space into $S_{agent}$ and $S_{rest}$ should be specified more explicitly. The paper provides a clear definition for $S_{agent}$ (e.g., joint positions and velocities of the agent), but a corresponding definition for $S_{rest}$ seems to be missing for the various environments. A precise description of what constitutes the "rest" of the state for each experimental domain is crucial for others to be able to understand the setup and replicate the work.

4.  **Need for Deeper Policy Analysis:** The paper presents strong quantitative results, but the analysis would benefit from a more qualitative examination of the learned hierarchical policies in action. Such an analysis could provide valuable insights into the behavior of the system. For instance:
    *   **Switching Behavior:** An analysis of the high-level controller's switching behavior would be very insightful. How frequently does the agent switch between sub-policies in different situations? Are the switches logical and interpretable (e.g., activating a "turn left" skill at a corner)?
    *   **Failure Modes:** Discussing the common failure modes of the agent could illuminate the limitations of the current approach. Where does the policy struggle most, and why?
    *   **Parameter Sensitivity:** An exploration of how key parameters, such as the fixed duration ($T$) of sub-policy execution, affect overall performance would be a welcome addition. How sensitive is the final performance to this hyperparameter?

In summary, this is a strong contribution with impressive empirical results on challenging control problems. The core weaknesses lie in the lack of depth in the analysis and some lack of clarity in the experimental description. Addressing the points above would substantially improve the paper and solidify its contributions to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning.
