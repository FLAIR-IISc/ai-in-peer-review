This paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that utilizes stochastic neural networks to represent a mixture of low-level sub-policies. A key contribution is the introduction of an information-theoretic objective that provides an intrinsic reward to encourage the learning of a diverse and identifiable set of sub-policies, or "options." The method is evaluated on a series of challenging continuous control tasks.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Core Idea:** The use of an information-theoretic measure for option identifiability as an intrinsic reward is a novel and principled approach to promoting diversity in skill discovery. This provides a clear objective for the pre-training phase of the low-level policies.

*   **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** A significant strength of the paper is its comparison against a strong baseline that directly combines the intrinsic and sparse extrinsic rewards into a single, dense reward signal. The demonstration that this seemingly simpler approach fails to solve the tasks provides compelling evidence for the benefits of the proposed hierarchical abstraction and the challenges of long-term credit assignment.

*   **Clarity and Visualization:** The paper is well-written and supported by clear visualizations that effectively illustrate the behaviors learned by the different sub-policies. This greatly aids in understanding the results.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper presents a promising direction, and its contributions could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Limited Expressiveness of Sub-Policy Modulation:** The current mechanisms for combining the high-level categorical choice with the low-level policy, namely concatenation and bilinear integration, primarily influence the first hidden layer of the policy network. While this is effective for the locomotion tasks presented—where the optimal sub-policies are often variations of a common gait—this approach may be restrictive for more complex domains. For instance, in manipulation tasks, the optimal sub-policy for approaching an object might be fundamentally different from a sub-policy for grasping it, potentially requiring more substantial architectural differences than can be captured by modifying input weights or biases alone. Evaluating the method on non-locomotion domains where ideal sub-policies are more distinct would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of generality.

2.  **Decoupled and Fixed Hierarchical Structure:** The current training paradigm is staged: sub-policies are trained with an intrinsic reward and then frozen before training a high-level manager policy. Furthermore, the temporal duration of each sub-policy is fixed. This two-stage process places a heavy burden on the quality and generality of the initially learned sub-policies; if they are not well-suited for the downstream task, the overall hierarchy is likely to fail. A discussion of how this framework could be extended towards end-to-end training, where the manager and sub-policies are learned concurrently (akin to methods like the option-critic architecture), would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Domain-Specificity of Intrinsic Rewards:** A direct consequence of the decoupled training process is that the "unsupervised" or "intrinsic" rewards used for pre-training the sub-policies must be carefully engineered to be useful for the final task. This arguably makes them more akin to supervised, task-specific reward shaping rather than truly unsupervised skill discovery. While this is a common challenge in HRL, more discussion on this limitation would be welcome.

**Questions for the Authors**

I have a few clarifying questions that arose during my review:

1.  Regarding the strong baseline in Section 7.3 (I assume this is the "CoM maze reward" policy): What is the authors' intuition for its poor performance? My own hypothesis is that the lack of an explicit diversity objective, which is present in the proposed method's pre-training, prevents it from discovering the necessary range of skills. Is this interpretation correct, or are there other factors at play?

2.  For the baseline comparison, does the baseline model receive a comparable amount of total environmental interaction, including the experience the hierarchical agent gains during the sub-policy pre-training phase?

3.  Could you please clarify the distinction between the "multi-policy" and "snn" models mentioned in the experiments?

4.  In Figure 1, is my understanding of the conditioning mechanisms correct?
    *   (1) **Concatenation:** The categorical sub-policy choice effectively selects a different bias vector for the first hidden layer.
    *   (2) **Bilinear Integration:** The sub-policy choice selects a different weight matrix mapping the observation to the first hidden layer activations.
