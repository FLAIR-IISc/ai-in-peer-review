This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to address tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned using a single, generic proxy reward signal, and second, a downstream phase where a high-level policy is trained to select from these pre-trained skills to solve specific tasks. The authors leverage Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to efficiently learn a wide range of skills within a single model and introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to explicitly encourage the diversity and interpretability of these learned skills. The paper includes strong empirical results and clear visualizations, notably demonstrating that the proposed hierarchical method significantly outperforms a strong baseline that directly combines the proxy reward with the sparse task reward.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are the novel and effective combination of Stochastic Neural Networks with an information-theoretic objective for unsupervised skill discovery, and the compelling empirical evidence showing this approach solves challenging sparse-reward tasks where a strong, non-hierarchical baseline fails. The work provides valuable insights into the benefits of hierarchical abstraction and structured exploration.

**Supporting Arguments:**

While the paper presents a promising direction, its claims could be strengthened by addressing the following limitations:

1.  **Limited Diversification of Sub-policies:** The proposed methods for integrating the latent code—concatenation and bilinear integration—primarily affect the bias and first-layer weights of the policy network. While this proves sufficient for the locomotion tasks presented, where the desired skills are variations of a base policy, this architectural choice might limit the model's ability to learn truly distinct sub-policies. This limitation could become more apparent in domains like manipulation, where the optimal behaviors for different sub-tasks might be fundamentally dissimilar and not representable by small perturbations of a single network.

2.  **Rigidity of the Hierarchical Structure:** The current framework trains the high-level manager policy while keeping the low-level skills frozen. Furthermore, each skill is executed for a fixed number of time steps. This rigid structure places a significant burden on the pre-training phase. The proxy reward must be carefully engineered to produce skills that are not just diverse, but also directly applicable and sufficient for all potential downstream tasks. This dependency reduces the autonomy of the learning process.

3.  **Domain-Specificity of the "Unsupervised" Reward:** A consequence of the rigid hierarchy is that the proxy reward used in the pre-training phase cannot be considered truly unsupervised or domain-agnostic. Because the skills are not adapted later, the proxy reward must implicitly encode prior knowledge about what behaviors will be useful for the target tasks. This seems to be a necessary trade-off in the current framework.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

The following points are suggestions aimed at strengthening the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations mentioned above, particularly the reliance on frozen skills. It would be valuable to connect the proposed method to the broader literature on end-to-end hierarchical RL (e.g., the option-critic architecture). Exploring avenues like fine-tuning the sub-policies during downstream task training or learning a termination condition for each skill instead of using a fixed time horizon would be exciting directions for future work.
*   The authors suggest that the baseline's failure may be due to long-term credit assignment. An alternative hypothesis is that the baseline lacks the explicit diversity-seeking objective that the SNN with the MI bonus has. It would be interesting to see an analysis of whether adding a diversity-promoting regularizer to the baseline could improve its performance, which could provide further insight into why the hierarchical approach succeeds.
