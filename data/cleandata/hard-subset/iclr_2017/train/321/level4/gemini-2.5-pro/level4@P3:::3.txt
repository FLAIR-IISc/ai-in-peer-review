### **Summary**

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of low-level skills in a simple environment with a generic, hand-crafted "proxy reward." This stage leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent a multi-modal policy capable of encoding multiple skills within a single network, which improves sample efficiency. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and varied, the authors introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent skill variable and the agent's state. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as a set of macro-actions for a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on specific downstream tasks. The paper provides empirical evidence on challenging continuous control benchmarks (e.g., Swimmer in maze and gather tasks) to show that this approach improves exploration and leads to significantly faster learning compared to baselines.

### **Decision: Weak Accept**

I am recommending a weak accept. The key reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Positive:** The proposed method of combining Stochastic Neural Networks with a mutual information bonus for unsupervised skill discovery is elegant, well-motivated, and empirically effective. The use of a single, multi-modal policy is a sample-efficient way to learn a skill repertoire, and the visitation plots clearly demonstrate that the proposed regularizer encourages the emergence of a diverse and interpretable set of behaviors.

2.  **Concern:** The paper's claim that the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge" seems overstated. While the framework itself is general, the chosen proxy reward (e.g., center-of-mass velocity) is a strong, hand-engineered prior that is well-suited for locomotion tasks but may not generalize easily to other problem domains, such as manipulation.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its approach to the pre-training phase. The problem of discovering useful skills without task-specific rewards is a central challenge in HRL. The authors' solution is compelling: they parameterize a family of policies using an SNN conditioned on a latent variable and then explicitly incentivize these policies to be distinguishable. The use of a bilinear integration to combine the state and the latent code appears to be a crucial architectural detail for learning separable skills. The results in Figure 4 provide strong evidence that the combination of this architecture and the MI bonus consistently produces a richer set of skills than training separate policies or using a simpler SNN. The subsequent hierarchical application of these skills demonstrates a substantial improvement in exploration (Figure 5) and task performance (Figure 6) on difficult sparse-reward problems.

My main reservation relates to the framing of the proxy reward. The authors contrast their method with prior work (Heess et al., 2016) by stating that their approach avoids specifying a set of goals and instead uses a simple intrinsic reward. However, a reward for maximizing forward speed is, in itself, a form of goal specification. It heavily biases the agent towards learning locomotion primitives. While this is effective for the maze navigation and gathering tasks presented, the paper's suggestion for a manipulation proxy reward ("successful grasping of any object") seems far less "minimal" and would likely require significant engineering (e.g., contact sensors, object detectors) to implement. This makes the approach feel less general than claimed, as the success of the pre-training phase appears highly dependent on the ability to design a simple yet effective proxy reward for a given family of tasks.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Generality and Failure Modes:** I appreciate that the authors included experiments with more complex agents like the "Snake" and "Ant" in the appendix. The discussion of failure modes for the unstable Ant robot (Section D.2) is particularly valuable. This analysis of limitations (i.e., instability when switching between skills learned in different regions of the state space) is important and would strengthen the main paper if briefly discussed in the Discussion or Future Work section. It provides a clear direction for future research, such as learning a transition policy or incorporating skill-switching into the pre-training phase.

*   **MI Calculation Details:** In Section 5.3, the mutual information bonus is calculated by discretizing the agent's 2D coordinate space into a grid. The paper notes that an MLP could be used for higher-dimensional spaces. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the method to the resolution of this grid? Furthermore, for higher-dimensional state representations where gridding is infeasible, would a non-parametric approach like a k-NN based density estimator be a viable alternative to training a separate neural network regressor?
