- Two-stage hierarchical RL: unsupervised/minimally-supervised pre-training of skills, then a high-level manager composes skills for downstream tasks.
- Contribution: Use Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn multiple skills within a single, weight-sharing policy for sample efficiency.
- Contribution: Introduce an information-theoretic mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage distinct, interpretable, and diverse skills.
- Empirical results: Demonstrated on continuous-control benchmarks (maze navigation, object gathering) where the hierarchical agent succeeds and standard RL with naive exploration fails.
- Visualizations: Skill diversity and improved exploration coverage are clearly shown in visitation plots and learning curves.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Novel and principled skill-discovery method—SNN + MI bonus prevents mode collapse and promotes interpretability.
- Key reason 2: Strong empirical validation and a comparison to a strong baseline show gains are due to hierarchical abstraction, not mere reward shaping.
- Strength: Well-designed, task-agnostic pre-training using a simple proxy reward enables a useful behavior basis without per-task supervision.
- Strength: SNNs provide sample-efficiency advantages over training multiple independent policies.
- Strength: MI regularizer is a significant technical contribution that directly addresses lack of diversity in multi-skill learning.
- Empirical support: Visitation plots and learning curves substantiate richer skill sets and improved performance; failure of proxy-reward baseline isolates hierarchy benefit.
- Limitation 1: SNN architectures only modify the first hidden layer (bias/weights), which may limit diversity for more complex domains (e.g., manipulation).
- Limitation 2: Hierarchy is rigid—low-level skills are frozen and skill duration T is fixed; end-to-end fine-tuning and learned termination could help.
- Limitation 3: Pre-training proxy reward (e.g., speed) encodes domain-specific prior knowledge and is not fully “unsupervised.”
- Question 1: Could the baseline fail because it lacks the MI diversity objective rather than solely due to long-term credit assignment?
- Question 2: Does the baseline’s sample complexity account for pre-training samples used by the hierarchical method (i.e., equal total environment interactions)?
- Question 3: Clarify the "Multi-policy" baseline—are K independent policies trained on the proxy reward with the manager selecting among them?
- Question 4: Confirm architecture interpretations: concatenation learns different biases per latent z; bilinear integration learns different observation-to-hidden weight matrices per z.
