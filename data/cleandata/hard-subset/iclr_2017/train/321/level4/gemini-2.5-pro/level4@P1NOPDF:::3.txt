The problem setting presented in this paper is compelling; however, I have several questions and concerns regarding the proposed method:

(1) What are the potential failure modes of this work? As the required richness of behaviors becomes more complex, I expect that this approach might struggle to discover a sufficiently diverse set of skills. A discussion of the method's limitations in this context would strengthen the paper.

(2) In Section 5.3, the authors state, "let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated." This suggests that the state space is discretized. Could the authors clarify if this is the case? If so, what is the motivation for this design choice, and how would the method be affected if the space were continuous?

(3) Expanding on the first point regarding scalability, how does the approach handle agents with more complex morphologies? For instance, would it be effective for a 5-link swimmer instead of the 2-link version used in the experiments? Evaluating the method on agents with higher degrees of freedom would be an important test for assessing its generality.

(4) The authors claim a key advantage over prior work: "Recently, Heess et al. (2016) have independently proposed to learn a range of skills in a pre-training environment that will be useful for the downstream tasks, which is similar to our framework. However, their pre-training setup requires a set of goals to be specified. In comparison, we use intrinsic rewards as the only signal to the agent during the pre-training phase, the construction of which only requires very minimal domain knowledge."

I find this distinction to be overstated. The intrinsic reward functions proposed in this paper also appear to be heavily hand-crafted and tailored to a seemingly limited set of control tasks. The paper would be more convincing if the authors could further justify why their use of domain knowledge is significantly more minimal than that of related methods.
