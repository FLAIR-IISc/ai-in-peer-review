- Paper introduces HRL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability intrinsic reward to promote diverse sub-policies.
- Strong baseline (intrinsic + sparse extrinsic reward) compared; it fails to solve tasks and authors blame credit assignment/hierarchy, but reviewer suggests lack of diversity could explain failure.
- Visualizations are clear and helpful.
- Limited sub-policy diversification: concatenation and bilinear integration only differentiate via first hidden layer weights/biases, which may suffice for locomotion but fail for domains needing fundamentally different sub-policies.
- Hierarchical training not end-to-end: manager trained on fixed, pre-trained sub-policies with fixed durations, placing heavy burden on unsupervised pre-training and reducing flexibility.
- Intrinsic rewards are domain-specific: fixed sub-policies imply the "unsupervised" intrinsic rewards must be engineered to produce task-relevant behaviors (effectively supervised).
- Question: Is the baseline's poor performance due to credit assignment, or could exploration/optimization issues also be responsible?
- Question: Did the baseline receive comparable experience, including data from sub-policy pre-training?
- Question: Please clarify the difference between the "multi-policy" and "snn" models in the results.
- Question: Confirm Figure 1 interpretations: (1) concatenation selects a unique bias vector for the first hidden layer; (2) bilinear integration selects a unique weight matrix for the observation→first hidden layer mapping.
