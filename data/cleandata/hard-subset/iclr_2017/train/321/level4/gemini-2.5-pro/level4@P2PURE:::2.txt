This paper proposes a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that utilizes stochastic neural networks. A key contribution is the introduction of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability, which serves as an intrinsic reward to encourage the learning of a diverse set of sub-policies.

A commendable aspect of the paper is its comparison against a strong baseline that directly combines the intrinsic reward with the sparse extrinsic reward. The authors demonstrate that this baseline, despite having a seemingly smoother reward signal, fails to solve the tasks. The authors attribute this failure to challenges in long-term credit assignment and the lack of hierarchical abstraction. However, an alternative explanation could be the absence of the diversity-promoting intrinsic reward in the baseline. Investigating this could offer insights for improving not only the baseline but also other end-to-end HRL frameworks, such as the option-critic architecture or hierarchical REPS. The visualizations are clear and helpful.

Overall, the paper presents a promising direction. Its contribution could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1)  **Limited Diversification of Sub-policies:** The mechanisms for creating distinct sub-policies appear limited. Both concatenation and bilinear integration only permit differentiation through the first hidden layer's weights or biases. This may be sufficient for the tested locomotion tasks, which require similar underlying skills with minor variations. However, this architectural limitation could become more pronounced in domains where optimal sub-policies are fundamentally different. For example, in complex manipulation tasks, the optimal behavior required from one state might be entirely different from that required in another. It would be valuable to see the method evaluated on such non-locomotion domains to test the limits of its sub-policy diversification.

2)  **Limitations of the Hierarchical Training:** The hierarchical learning process is not fully end-to-end. The manager network is trained on top of pre-trained, fixed sub-policies, and each sub-policy executes for a fixed number of timesteps. This two-stage approach places a significant burden on the unsupervised pre-training phase; the learned sub-policies must be sufficiently useful for solving a range of potential downstream tasks. A discussion on the implications of this design choice, and potential connections or extensions to fully end-to-end HRL, would strengthen the paper.

3)  **Domain-Specific Nature of Intrinsic Rewards:** A direct consequence of the fixed sub-policies is that the "unsupervised" intrinsic rewards used for pre-training seem necessarily domain-specific. To be useful, they must be engineered to produce sub-policies relevant to the eventual tasks, which feels more like a form of supervision.

**Questions:**

*   Regarding the strong baseline in Section 7.3 (presumably "CoM maze reward"): what is the intuition for its poor performance? The authors suggest credit assignment, but could other factors like exploration or optimization challenges be at play?
*   Was the baseline given a comparable amount of experience to the proposed method, including the experience gathered during the sub-policy pre-training phase?
*   Could you clarify the distinction between the "multi-policy" and "snn" models mentioned in the results?
*   Regarding Figure 1, could the authors confirm the following interpretations? 1) Concatenation: The selected sub-policy index corresponds to a unique bias vector for the first hidden layer. 2) Bilinear integration: The sub-policy index selects a unique weight matrix for the mapping from observations to the first hidden layer.
