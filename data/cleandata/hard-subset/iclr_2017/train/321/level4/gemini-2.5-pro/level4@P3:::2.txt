### **Summary**

This paper proposes a general and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to tackle challenging problems with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a simple pre-training environment using a generic, easy-to-design "proxy reward" that encourages useful behaviors (e.g., locomotion). Second, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as primitives by a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on the sparse-reward downstream task.

The authors make several key technical contributions to make this framework effective. They use a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent the entire set of skills in a parameter-efficient manner, where different skills are invoked by conditioning on a latent variable. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, they introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state. The empirical results, demonstrated on a set of challenging continuous control benchmarks, are strong. The visualizations compellingly show that the proposed method learns an interpretable and diverse span of skills, which in turn leads to vastly improved exploration and superior performance on the downstream tasks.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-written and well-executed paper that presents a novel and practical framework for HRL. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **A Well-Motivated and Elegant Framework:** The paper effectively combines unsupervised skill discovery with a hierarchical control structure to address the critical challenge of exploration in sparse-reward environments. The two-stage approach is clean, and the use of a simple proxy reward with an information-theoretic bonus for pre-training is an elegant solution to the problem of discovering useful and diverse skills with minimal supervision.

2.  **Strong Empirical Support and Analysis:** The claims are substantiated by a thorough set of experiments on challenging continuous control tasks. The visualizations are particularly insightful, clearly demonstrating the diversity of the learned skills (Figure 4) and the resulting improvement in exploration (Figure 5). Crucially, the authors compare against a strong and relevant baseline where the proxy reward is directly added to the sparse task reward. The failure of this baseline highlights that simply providing a dense reward is insufficient without the structural benefits of hierarchical abstraction and diverse primitives, which is a key insight of this work.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The proposed method sits at a nice intersection of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical reinforcement learning. Instead of hand-crafting options or relying solely on task-agnostic intrinsic rewards, this work uses a minimally supervised pre-training phase to generate a reusable skill library. The use of an SNN to encode multiple skills in one network is sample-efficient compared to training separate policies.

The introduction of the mutual information (MI) regularizer is a significant contribution. As the authors show, naively training an SNN does not guarantee that different latent codes will produce distinct behaviors. By rewarding the policy for making its latent code identifiable from its state, the method explicitly encourages the emergence of a diverse and non-overlapping set of skills. This is shown to be highly effective in the visitation plots.

The experimental validation is comprehensive. The authors not only show that their final method works well but also provide an ablation-like study of the components. For example, the comparison between concatenation and bilinear integration for the latent variable demonstrates a thoughtful approach to architectural design. The learning curves on the maze and gather tasks convincingly show that the hierarchical methods significantly outperform the flat baseline, which fails to learn altogether. This robustly supports the central claim that the discovered skills enable effective exploration and dramatically speed up learning in sparse-reward settings.

### **Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper further and are not critical to my decision.

1.  **Generalization of Sub-policy Architectures:** The paper shows that bilinear integration is more expressive than simple concatenation, as it allows the latent code to modulate the first layer's weights rather than just its biases. This is a great insight. However, this still limits the differentiation between skills to the initial layers of the network. It would be interesting to discuss how this approach might scale to tasks requiring more fundamentally distinct skills (e.g., complex manipulation), where greater weight sharing might be less beneficial. Exploring architectures that allow for more complex, code-dependent routing through the network could be a valuable direction for future work.

2.  **On End-to-End Training and Fixed Time Horizons:** The framework currently freezes the low-level skills and uses a fixed time horizon `T` for the manager. This simplifies the problem and works well for the tasks presented. The authors rightly point to end-to-end fine-tuning as a potential extension. Another valuable extension, which could be discussed, would be to learn a termination policy for each skill, similar to the options framework. This would allow for more flexible temporal abstraction, which might be crucial for more dynamic tasks where reacting at the right time is important.

3.  **Sensitivity to the Proxy Reward:** The design of the proxy reward (e.g., rewarding the agent's speed) requires some domain knowledge, though minimal. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final performance to the choice of this proxy reward would be insightful. For instance, would a different proxy reward lead to a completely different, but perhaps equally useful, set of skills?

### **Questions for the Authors**

I have a few questions to clarify my understanding of the experimental setup and architecture.

1.  In Section 7.3 and Figure 6, you compare against a "Multi-policy" baseline. Is my understanding correct that this architecture involves pre-training six separate policies from scratch on the proxy reward, and then having the manager network select among them? If so, this would mean the SNN approach offers a ~6x sample complexity improvement during the pre-training phase, which is a very strong selling point.

2.  Regarding the SNN architecture in Figure 1, can you confirm my interpretation? Is it correct that: (a) concatenation effectively learns a different *bias term* for the first hidden layer for each latent code, while (b) bilinear integration allows the model to learn a different *weight matrix* connecting the observation to the first hidden layer for each latent code? This seems to be a key reason for the improved expressiveness, and explicitly stating it this way might improve clarity.
