- Proposes a two-stage hierarchical RL framework: pre-train diverse skills with a generic proxy reward, then train a high-level policy to select skills for downstream tasks.
- Uses Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn many skills within a single model efficiently.
- Introduces an information-theoretic mutual information regularizer to encourage skill diversity and interpretability.
- Presents strong empirical results and clear visualizations showing the hierarchical method outperforms a strong non-hierarchical baseline on sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks.
- Recommendation: accept, citing the novel SNN + MI combination and compelling empirical evidence solving tasks where the baseline fails.
- Limitation: concatenation and bilinear latent integrations mainly affect bias/first-layer weights, potentially limiting the ability to learn fundamentally distinct sub-policies.
- Limitation: rigid hierarchy (frozen low-level skills and fixed execution lengths) places heavy burden on pre-training and reduces adaptability.
- Limitation: the proxy pre-training reward is not truly unsupervised/domain-agnostic because it must encode priors useful for downstream tasks when skills remain frozen.
- Suggestion: explicitly discuss these limitations and relate the approach to end-to-end hierarchical RL (e.g., option-critic).
- Suggestion: explore fine-tuning sub-policies during downstream training and learning skill termination conditions instead of fixed horizons.
- Suggestion: test whether adding a diversity-promoting regularizer to the baseline explains its failure vs. long-term credit assignment issues.
- Question: why does the CoM-reward strong baseline perform poorly in mazes, and does its training budget account for SNN pre-training sample complexity?
- Question: clarify the exact difference between the "Multi-policy" and "SNN" architectures—e.g., is Multi-policy six independently trained networks?
- Question: confirm architecture interpretations—(a) concatenation selects a different bias vector for the first hidden layer, (b) bilinear integration modulates the full observation-to-first-layer weight matrix.
