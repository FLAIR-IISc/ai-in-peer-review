- Novelty/core idea: Introduces an information-theoretic intrinsic reward to encourage identifiable, diverse sub-policies (options) during pre-training.  
- Strong empirical evaluation: Compares against a strong dense-reward baseline and shows hierarchical abstraction outperforms naive reward mixing, highlighting long-term credit-assignment benefits.  
- Clarity and visualization: Paper is well-written and supported by clear visualizations that illustrate sub-policy behaviors.  

- Limited expressiveness of sub-policy modulation: Concatenation and bilinear integration mainly affect the first hidden layer and may be too restrictive for domains where sub-policies require substantially different architectures (e.g., manipulation); evaluate on non-locomotion tasks.  
- Decoupled and fixed hierarchical structure: Two-stage training with frozen sub-policies and fixed durations places heavy reliance on pre-trained skills; discuss or move toward end-to-end/joint training (e.g., option-critic).  
- Domain-specificity of intrinsic rewards: Intrinsic rewards used in pre-training may amount to task-specific shaping rather than true unsupervised skill discovery; discuss this limitation and its implications.  

- Question on baseline failure: Is the baseline’s poor performance primarily due to the lack of an explicit diversity objective, or are other factors responsible?  
- Question on training parity: Did the baseline receive comparable total environment interaction, including the hierarchical agent’s pre-training experience?  
- Question on model distinctions: Please clarify the difference between the "multi-policy" and "snn" models in experiments.  
- Question on conditioning mechanisms (Figure 1): Confirm that concatenation selects a different bias vector for the first hidden layer and bilinear integration selects a different input weight matrix.
