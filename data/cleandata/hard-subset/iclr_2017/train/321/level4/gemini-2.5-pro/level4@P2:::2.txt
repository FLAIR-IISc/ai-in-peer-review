### Summary

This paper presents a well-motivated framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with long horizons and sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, an unsupervised or minimally-supervised pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned, followed by a second phase where a high-level "manager" policy learns to compose these skills to solve downstream tasks.

The key contributions are twofold. First, the authors propose using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) as a sample-efficient method to learn multiple skills within a single policy network, leveraging weight sharing. Second, to ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, they introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent skill variable and the agent's state. This bonus encourages the emergence of an interpretable and diverse skill set.

The authors demonstrate their approach on challenging continuous control benchmarks, including maze navigation and object gathering. The experiments effectively show that the hierarchical agent can solve tasks where standard RL agents with naive exploration fail. The visualizations are particularly insightful, clearly illustrating the diversity of learned skills and the significant improvement in exploration coverage compared to unstructured exploration.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Approach to Skill Discovery:** The paper introduces a compelling and principled method for learning a diverse repertoire of skills. The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks with a mutual information bonus is an elegant solution to the problem of unsupervised skill discovery, preventing the common issue of mode collapse and promoting interpretability.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the framework's effectiveness. Crucially, the authors compare against a strong baseline where the intrinsic proxy reward is directly combined with the sparse task reward. The failure of this baseline highlights that the benefit of the proposed method comes from hierarchical abstraction and improved exploration, not just from reward shaping.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's primary strength lies in its well-designed pre-training phase. By using a simple, task-agnostic proxy reward (e.g., speed), the agent learns a basis of behaviors without needing specific supervision for each downstream task. The use of SNNs for this purpose is a clever way to improve sample efficiency over the naive alternative of training multiple policies from scratch. The information-theoretic regularizer is the most significant technical contribution, providing a clear objective for maximizing skill diversity. This directly addresses a common challenge in multi-skill learning where policies can converge to very similar behaviors.

The empirical evaluation is thorough. The visitation plots in Figures 4 and 5 provide clear qualitative evidence that the SNN with the MI bonus learns a richer set of skills and explores a much larger area of the state space than unstructured policies. The learning curves in Figure 6 show a substantial quantitative improvement on difficult sparse-reward tasks. The failure of the "proxy reward" baseline is a particularly strong result, as it isolates the value of the hierarchical structure itself. It supports the authors' hypothesis that breaking the problem down temporally helps with long-term credit assignment, an insight that is valuable to the community.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper presents a promising direction, and its contribution could be strengthened further by addressing the following points. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from the paper's acceptance.

1.  **Limited Diversity of Sub-Policies:** The proposed SNN architectures (concatenation and bilinear integration) modify only the first hidden layer's bias or weights based on the latent code. While this is sufficient for the locomotion tasks tested, it may be a limiting factor in more complex domains where optimal sub-policies are fundamentally different (e.g., manipulation tasks requiring both reaching and grasping skills). It would be valuable to discuss this architectural limitation and how more expressive SNNs could be integrated in the future.

2.  **Rigidity of the Hierarchy:** The current framework trains the manager network while keeping the low-level skills frozen, and the duration `T` for executing a skill is fixed. This places a significant burden on the pre-training phase to discover a set of skills that is near-optimal for all potential downstream tasks. The paper would benefit from a discussion on the potential for end-to-end fine-tuning of the full hierarchy, possibly by using recently proposed techniques for backpropagating through discrete latent variables (which the authors cite). Similarly, allowing the manager to learn a termination condition instead of using a fixed `T` would align the work more closely with the options framework and could improve performance on more dynamic tasks.

3.  **On the "Unsupervised" Nature of the Proxy Reward:** The paper frames the pre-training as requiring "minimal domain knowledge." However, the choice of proxy reward (e.g., the agent's speed) seems quite specific to the agent's morphology and the nature of the locomotion tasks. This is a pragmatic and effective choice, but it is a form of supervision. Clarifying that this reward encodes important prior knowledge about what constitutes "useful behavior" would make the claims more precise.
