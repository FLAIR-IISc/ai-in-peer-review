### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to tackle tasks with long horizons and sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a simple environment with a generic, task-agnostic "proxy reward"; second, a downstream task-learning phase where a high-level manager policy is trained to select from these pre-trained skills to solve a specific, sparse-reward task.

The authors make several key contributions to enable this framework. To learn multiple skills efficiently, they employ a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy, conditioned on a latent variable, which allows for weight sharing across different skills. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, they introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state. The paper provides compelling empirical evidence on a set of challenging locomotion and navigation tasks (e.g., Swimmer and Snake robots in mazes), demonstrating that their hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency compared to standard RL baselines.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This paper presents a well-motivated and elegant framework that effectively combines ideas from hierarchical learning, intrinsic motivation, and information theory to address the critical challenge of exploration in sparse-reward environments. The proposed method is sound, and the experiments clearly demonstrate its ability to solve complex tasks that are intractable for standard policy gradient methods. However, the claims regarding the generality of the approach and the "minimal domain knowledge" required for the proxy reward are somewhat overstated and should be toned down.

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Effective Solution to a Hard Problem:** The paper successfully tackles the long-horizon, sparse-reward problem by decoupling skill discovery from task-specific learning. The hierarchical structure dramatically improves exploration, as shown convincingly in the visitation plots (Fig. 5), leading to strong performance on challenging benchmark tasks.
2.  **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The use of SNNs for sample-efficient multi-skill learning, combined with a mutual information bonus to explicitly encourage skill diversity, is a clever and novel synthesis of existing concepts. This provides a principled way to generate a useful basis of behaviors without needing to pre-specify a set of goals.

### Supporting Arguments

The main strength of this work lies in its clear and effective formulation. The two-stage approach is intuitive: learn to move first, then learn where to go. The technical execution of this idea is solid. Using a single SNN is more sample-efficient than training K separate policies, and the use of bilinear integration to condition the policy on the latent code is a well-justified architectural choice that the experiments show is crucial for learning distinct skills.

The introduction of the mutual information bonus is particularly noteworthy. While SNNs can represent multi-modal policies, there is no inherent objective to force the modes to be distinct. The MI bonus provides this objective, encouraging the emergence of an interpretable and diverse set of skills by rewarding behaviors that are easily distinguishable based on the agent's location. The ablation studies in Figure 4 clearly support the positive impact of both the bilinear integration and the MI bonus.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the claims about its generality. The assertion that the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge" is debatable. For the locomotion tasks presented, rewarding the speed of the center of mass (CoM) is a very strong and effective prior that directly encourages the agent to learn to move. It is not obvious how to design an equally effective yet simple proxy reward for other domains, such as robotic manipulation, where the desired skills might involve delicate interaction with objects rather than broad movement. This hand-crafting of the proxy reward feels like a form of reward shaping, and while it is indeed more general than shaping for a specific maze, its universal applicability is not well-supported.

Furthermore, the experiments are primarily focused on planar locomotion tasks. The appendix rightfully points out that the approach can fail on more complex or unstable agents like the "Ant," where abrupt switching between skills can lead to failure (e.g., falling over). This is a significant limitation that impacts the generality of the framework and should be discussed more prominently.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the decision.

1.  **Discretization for MI Calculation:** In Section 5.3, the mutual information bonus is estimated by discretizing the agent's (x, y) coordinate space into a grid. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the method to the granularity of this grid? More importantly, how would this approach scale to tasks where the relevant state space for measuring skill diversity is high-dimensional (e.g., the full state of a humanoid robot), making a simple grid-based discretization intractable?

2.  **Generality of the Proxy Reward:** I would encourage the authors to discuss the design of the proxy reward more thoroughly. While rewarding CoM speed works well for the presented tasks, what would be an analogous proxy reward for a manipulation task like stacking blocks or opening a door? A more detailed discussion on the principles for designing these rewards would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of generality.

3.  **Scalability and Failure Modes:** The results with the "Ant" in the appendix are very insightful. It seems the "skill-switching" problem is a major hurdle for applying this method to more complex, dynamically unstable agents. Could you expand on potential solutions? For example, could a transition policy be learned to smoothly interpolate between skills, or could the pre-training objective be modified to encourage skills that are robust to being initiated from a wider range of states?

4.  **Fixed Time Horizon for Skills:** The high-level policy commits to a selected skill for a fixed number of steps `T`. While the appendix shows this is not a critical parameter for the tested static tasks, it could be a limitation in more dynamic environments. Have you considered allowing the high-level policy to learn a termination condition, in the spirit of the options framework (Sutton et al., 1999)? This could enable more flexible and reactive behaviors.
