### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve tasks with long horizons and sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process. First, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a simple "pre-training" environment using a generic proxy reward (e.g., maximizing speed). This skill acquisition is facilitated by a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy, which is encouraged to produce distinct behaviors via an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as a primitive action space for a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on the specific downstream tasks. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating that this approach can solve challenging continuous control benchmarks that were previously unsolved.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend **Accept**. The proposed method is elegant, well-motivated, and empirically effective on challenging problems. The key strengths are the novel use of SNNs with an information-theoretic bonus for unsupervised skill discovery and the demonstration of successful transfer to a variety of sparse-reward tasks. However, this recommendation is made with the reservation that the paper's contribution would be significantly strengthened by a more in-depth analysis of the results.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of the paper lies in its empirical results. The proposed hierarchical framework successfully solves several benchmark tasks (Locomotion + Maze, Gather) where previous methods have failed. The ablation studies in Section 7.1 convincingly show that the combination of SNNs, bilinear integration, and the mutual information (MI) bonus is effective at producing a diverse and interpretable set of locomotion skills. The visitation plots provide clear qualitative evidence of the improved exploration coverage afforded by the hierarchical approach compared to unstructured exploration.

However, the analysis of these strong results is comparatively weak, which limits the insights that can be drawn from the work. For example, the authors note that the MI bonus, while generally helpful, does not improve performance on the more complex mazes (Mazes 1, 2, and 3). This is an interesting finding, but it is presented without any accompanying analysis or hypothesis. Why might encouraging skill diversity not be beneficial in these specific scenarios? A deeper investigation into this would provide valuable insights into the method's properties.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit significantly from an analysis of the learned high-level policy. The manager network is a critical component of the system, yet its behavior is largely unexamined. How does the agent decide when to switch skills? Are certain skills used more frequently in specific parts of the environment? What are the common failure modes? An analysis of the manager's switching behavior and a discussion of the sensitivity to the fixed switching time `T` (partially addressed in the appendix) would add substantial depth to the experimental section.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper.

*   **Framing of "Intrinsic Motivation"**: The use of the term "intrinsic motivation" for the proxy reward might be misleading. In the RL literature, this term often implies a more general, task-agnostic curiosity or novelty-seeking mechanism. Here, the proxy reward (e.g., maximizing speed for a locomotion agent) seems quite tailored to the *class* of downstream tasks. This is similar to the setup in Heess et al. (2016). I would suggest reframing this to more accurately reflect that the pre-training reward is a simple, generic objective for a given agent morphology, rather than a general-purpose intrinsic reward.

*   **State Space Details**: For reproducibility, could you please clarify the exact composition of the task-specific state component, `S_rest`, for each of the downstream environments? While the paper references Duan et al. (2016) for the agent-specific state `S_agent`, the details of `S_rest` seem to be missing from the main text.

*   **Analysis of Skill Usage**: Could you provide some analysis of the manager's policy on a solved task? For instance, a visualization of which skill is active in different parts of the maze would be very insightful and would help readers understand *how* the hierarchy solves the task.

*   **Impact of Pre-trained Skills**: The appendix notes that for the maze tasks, performance depends heavily on the specific pre-trained SNN used (C.4). This is a crucial detail. Does this suggest the pre-training process is not robust, or that only a small subset of randomly initialized pre-training runs yield a skill set suitable for a given downstream task? A brief discussion of this variance in the main paper would be valuable.
