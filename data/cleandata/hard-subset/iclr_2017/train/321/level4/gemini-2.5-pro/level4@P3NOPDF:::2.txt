### Summary

This paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning that uses stochastic neural networks to represent a collection of low-level sub-policies, or "options." A key contribution is the use of an information-theoretic objective during a pre-training phase to encourage the discovery of a diverse and identifiable set of sub-policies. These pre-trained options are then composed by a higher-level manager policy to solve complex, sparsely-rewarded tasks. The method is evaluated on a series of challenging continuous control domains.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Objective for Diverse Skill Discovery:** The paper introduces an elegant and principled approach for learning a diverse mixture of sub-policies by maximizing an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability. This serves as an intrinsic reward signal that encourages exploration and the acquisition of a varied behavioral repertoire.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Baselines:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed hierarchical approach can solve complex locomotion and navigation tasks where standard RL agents with sparse rewards fail. A particularly valuable result is the comparison against a strong baseline that directly combines the intrinsic rewards with the sparse task reward. The failure of this baseline highlights the difficulty of long-term credit assignment and reinforces the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, which is a core motivation for this work.

3.  **Clarity and Visualizations:** The paper is well-written, and the method is explained clearly. The visualizations of the learned sub-policies are excellent and provide strong qualitative evidence of the diverse and meaningful skills discovered by the agent.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

The paper presents a promising direction, but its contribution could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Limited Diversification of Sub-Policies:** The proposed architectures for modulating the policy based on the latent code `z` (concatenation and bilinear integration) primarily affect the first hidden layer of the network. This may be sufficient for the tested locomotion tasks, which essentially require minor variations of a similar core gait. However, this architectural choice might constrain the model's ability to learn truly distinct sub-policies. This limitation could become more apparent in domains (e.g., complex manipulation) where optimal sub-policies for different stages of a task are fundamentally different in structure. It would be compelling to see the method evaluated on such non-locomotion domains.

2.  **Decoupled and Fixed Hierarchy:** The current framework relies on a two-stage training process where sub-policies are first pre-trained with intrinsic rewards and then held fixed while the manager is trained. Furthermore, the time duration for which each sub-policy runs is fixed. This decoupling places a significant burden on the pre-training phase to discover a set of options that is not just diverse, but also directly useful for solving downstream tasks. If the pre-trained skills are not well-suited for the target task, the manager has little recourse. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss this limitation and the potential for extending the framework towards end-to-end training, where sub-policies can be fine-tuned concurrently with the manager, similar to approaches like the option-critic architecture.

3.  **Nature of Intrinsic Rewards:** A consequence of the two-stage training is that the intrinsic rewards for the pre-training phase seem to require careful domain-specific design to ensure the learned skills are relevant. For example, rewarding forward motion is a form of supervised guidance for locomotion tasks. This seems to slightly weaken the claim of fully unsupervised skill discovery and raises questions about the method's applicability to domains where designing such intrinsic rewards is less intuitive.

### Questions for Clarification

1.  Regarding the strong baseline in Section 7.3 ("CoM maze reward"), could the authors provide more intuition for its poor performance? My hypothesis is that the baseline's failure might stem from the lack of an explicit diversity-promoting objective, which is central to the proposed method's pre-training. Does this insight suggest potential avenues for improving non-hierarchical baselines?

2.  In the comparison, does the baseline agent receive a comparable amount of total experience, including the experience the proposed method gains during the sub-policy pre-training phase?

3.  Could you clarify the exact difference between the "multi-policy" and "snn" methods that are compared in the experiments?
