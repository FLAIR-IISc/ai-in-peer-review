This paper proposes an approach for hierarchical reinforcement learning that leverages stochastic neural networks. A key contribution is the introduction of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability, which serves as an auxiliary reward to encourage the learning of a diverse set of sub-policies. A particularly compelling result is the comparison against a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic and extrinsic rewards, demonstrating that this more direct reward signal is insufficient for solving the tasks.

Beyond the authors' arguments regarding long-term credit assignment and hierarchical abstraction, an alternative explanation for the baseline's failure might be the absence of the explicit diversity objective used during sub-policy training. It would be interesting to explore if incorporating this insight could improve the baseline or inspire novel end-to-end hierarchical policy learning frameworks, in the vein of Hierarchical REPS or the Option-Critic architecture. The visualizations provided are clear and effective.

While the paper presents a promising direction, it could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Limited Sub-policy Diversification:** The proposed mechanisms for skill differentiation (concatenation and bilinear integration) primarily modulate the first hidden layer of the network. This may limit the capacity for learning truly distinct sub-policies. While this is sufficient for the evaluated locomotion tasks, which require variations of a core skill set, this limitation could become more pronounced in domains where optimal sub-policies are fundamentally different. Evaluating the method on more complex, non-locomotion tasks, such as robotic manipulation where performing an action from different states can require vastly different behaviors, would be insightful.

2.  **Decoupled Hierarchical Training:** The framework trains the high-level manager policy while keeping the low-level sub-policies fixed. Additionally, the temporal duration of these sub-policies is fixed. This two-stage approach places a significant burden on the initial, unsupervised pre-training phase, as the resulting sub-policies must be highly effective for a range of downstream tasks. A discussion of this limitation and its potential solutions, ideally connecting the work to end-to-end hierarchical learning methods, would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Domain-Specific Intrinsic Rewards:** A consequence of the decoupled training scheme described above is that the "unsupervised" or intrinsic rewards seem necessarily tailored to the specific domain to produce useful behaviors. This appears to be an unavoidable artifact of the current training paradigm.

Finally, I have a few clarifying questions:
*   Could the authors provide further intuition as to why the strong baseline presented in Section 7.3 (presumably "CoM maze reward") performs so poorly and fails to solve the task?
*   Was the baseline's training regimen designed to account for the additional experience gained by the proposed method's sub-policies during their pre-training phase?
*   Could you please clarify the distinction between the "multi-policy" baseline and the proposed "snn" method?
*   Regarding the architecture in Figure 1, is the following interpretation correct? (1) Concatenation modulates the bias of the first hidden layer based on the selected option, and (2) bilinear integration modulates the weight matrix connecting the observation to the first hidden layer.
