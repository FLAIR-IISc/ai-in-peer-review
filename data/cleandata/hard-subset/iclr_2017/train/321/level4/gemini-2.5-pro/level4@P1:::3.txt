### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to tackle tasks with long horizons and sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned in a simple environment with a generic "proxy reward," and second, a fine-tuning phase where a high-level policy learns to select among these pre-trained skills to solve specific downstream tasks. To learn a rich repertoire of skills simultaneously and sample-efficiently, the authors employ a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as the low-level policy, conditioned on a latent variable. They introduce two key technical novelties to improve skill discovery: a bilinear integration of the latent variable with the observations for greater expressiveness, and an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to explicitly encourage diversity among the skills. The experiments on challenging continuous control tasks demonstrate that this approach can discover interpretable, multi-modal skills and significantly accelerate learning on downstream tasks compared to learning from scratch.

### Decision and Key Reasons

I am currently leaning towards rejecting this paper, though I am open to changing my mind based on the authors' rebuttal. My decision is based on two main concerns:

1.  **Generality and Scalability:** The paper's claims of generality are not sufficiently supported by the experiments, which focus on a relatively simple agent. I have concerns about whether the skill discovery process can scale to agents with more complex morphologies and a richer space of potential behaviors.
2.  **Overstated Claims on Supervision:** The authors claim their method requires "very minimal domain knowledge" compared to prior work. I find this claim to be overstated, as the proposed proxy reward is itself a form of hand-crafting that seems specific to a limited class of locomotion tasks.

### Supporting Arguments

1.  **Concerns about Generality and Failure Modes:** The effectiveness of the entire framework hinges on the quality and diversity of the skills discovered in the pre-training phase. The paper demonstrates impressive results for a 2-link swimmer agent, where the primary behaviors are variations of locomotion (forward, backward, turning). However, it is unclear how this approach would fare with more complex agents. For instance, would it work for a 5-link snake or a quadruped, where the space of useful gaits is significantly larger and more complex? As the complexity of the agent's embodiment increases, I expect that simply optimizing a proxy reward with a mutual information bonus might struggle to discover a sufficiently diverse and useful set of skills. The paper does not explore these limitations or discuss the potential failure modes. What happens if a critical skill for a downstream task is not discovered during pre-training?

2.  **Hand-Crafted Nature of the Proxy Reward:** The paper positions its approach as superior to methods like Heess et al. (2016) by arguing that it does not require specifying a set of goals, instead relying on a simple proxy reward. While this is true, I would argue that the choice of proxy reward—in this case, rewarding the agent for its speed—is a strong and task-specific prior. This reward is well-suited for navigation and exploration tasks where movement is key, but it is not obviously applicable to other domains, such as manipulation, where useful skills might involve precise, slow movements or even staying stationary. The distinction between specifying a proxy reward and specifying a set of goals feels more like a matter of degree than a fundamental difference in the level of supervision required. This weakens the paper's claim about requiring only "minimal domain knowledge."

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and clarify my understanding.

1.  **State Space Discretization:** In Section 5.3, the mutual information bonus is calculated by partitioning the agent's (x, y) coordinate space into a grid. Could you elaborate on the motivation for this discretization? What are the potential consequences of using a non-discretized approach, for example, by learning a density estimator or training a neural network to approximate the posterior p(z|c)?

2.  **Experiments with More Complex Agents:** To better assess the generality of this approach, it would be very insightful to see experiments on agents with more complex morphologies, such as a 5-link snake or an ant. How does the quality and diversity of the discovered skills change as the agent's state and action complexity increases? This would provide a clearer picture of the method's scalability and its potential failure modes.

3.  **Designing Proxy Rewards:** Could you provide more insight into the design of the proxy reward? While rewarding speed works well for locomotion, how would one design a similarly "minimal" proxy reward for a different class of tasks, such as object manipulation or tasks requiring static postures? A discussion on this would strengthen the claims of the paper's general applicability.
