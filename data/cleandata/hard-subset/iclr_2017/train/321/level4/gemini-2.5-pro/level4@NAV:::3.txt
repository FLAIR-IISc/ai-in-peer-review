The problem setting presented is compelling; however, I have several questions and concerns regarding the proposed approach:

(1) Could the authors elaborate on the failure modes of this work? As the potential for behavioral complexity increases, I suspect the approach may encounter limitations in its ability to discover a sufficiently diverse set of skills.

(2) Regarding Section 5.3, the description of X as "the grid in which the agent is currently situated" implies a discretized state space. Could the authors clarify if this is the case? If so, what is the rationale behind this design choice, and how would the proposed method be affected by a continuous state representation?

(3) Furthering the first point on scalability, how does the approach generalize to more complex agent embodiments? For instance, would the method be effective for a 5-link swimmer, as opposed to the 2-link agent used in the experiments? Assessing performance on more complex morphologies seems critical for establishing the generality of this framework.

(4) I am not fully convinced by the authors' claim of superiority over Heess et al. (2016). The paper argues that its use of intrinsic rewards requires less domain knowledge than specifying a set of goals. However, the proposed reward functions themselves appear to be carefully hand-crafted and specific to the experimental domains. This level of engineering seems comparable to the domain knowledge needed to define goals in the work being compared against.
