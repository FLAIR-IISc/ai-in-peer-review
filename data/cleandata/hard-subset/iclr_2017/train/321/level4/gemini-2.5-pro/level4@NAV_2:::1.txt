This paper presents a general framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with long horizons and sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a pre-training phase using a generic proxy reward, requiring minimal domain knowledge. To efficiently learn a wide range of skills simultaneously, the authors employ Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for the policy, augmented with an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage behavioral diversity. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as primitives for a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on specific downstream tasks. The empirical results demonstrate that this approach learns a span of interpretable skills and significantly boosts performance on a suite of challenging continuous control benchmarks.

I recommend accepting this paper. The proposed method is elegant and effectively addresses the critical challenge of exploration in sparse-reward settings. The primary strengths are the strong empirical results on difficult benchmark tasks and the well-motivated approach of using information-regularized SNNs to generate a diverse skill repertoire in a sample-efficient manner. However, the work could be improved with a more thorough analysis of the results and a more precise framing of its contributions relative to the literature.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's main strength lies in its empirical validation. The proposed hierarchical framework successfully solves several challenging locomotion and navigation tasks where prior methods that use naive exploration fail. The use of SNNs to learn multiple skills within a single network is a sample-efficient alternative to training separate policies, and the ablation studies clearly show the benefits of the bilinear integration and the mutual information (MI) regularizer in generating a rich and diverse set of behaviors. The visualizations of the learned skills (visitation plots) provide clear, interpretable evidence of the method's effectiveness in the pre-training phase.

Despite the strong results, the analysis could be deepened in a few areas.
1.  The term "intrinsic motivation" is used to describe the proxy reward in the pre-training phase. While the reward is internal to this phase, its design (e.g., rewarding forward speed) is heavily guided by the anticipated nature of the downstream tasks. This framing is slightly misaligned with the common usage of the term in RL, which often refers to more general, task-agnostic curiosity or novelty signals. The approach is perhaps more accurately described as a form of curriculum or auxiliary task learning, similar in spirit to concurrent work by Heess et al. (2016).
2.  The paper notes that the MI bonus, while generally helpful, does not consistently improve performance on some of the more complex mazes. An analysis of why this might be the case is missing but would be valuable. For instance, it might be that the specific skills needed to solve these mazes (e.g., using walls to reorient) are learned even without the regularizer, and the additional skills it encourages are not critical for these specific tasks.
3.  A more detailed analysis of the emergent hierarchical policy's behavior would strengthen the paper. The appendix includes a good analysis of the switching time `T` and failure modes for the "Ant" agent, but incorporating these insights into the main text would provide a more complete picture of the method's properties and limitations.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   For the sake of reproducibility, could you please explicitly detail the factorization of the state space into `S_agent` and `S_rest` for the downstream environments? While `S_agent` is defined in the benchmark paper by Duan et al., a clear specification of `S_rest` (e.g., LIDAR readings, object positions) for each task would be beneficial.
*   Could you offer more intuition on the cases where the MI bonus did not lead to performance gains in downstream tasks? Does this imply a trade-off between the breadth of the learned skill set and its utility for a specific set of problems?
*   It would be interesting to see a qualitative analysis of the manager's behavior. For example, a visualization showing the sequence of skills selected by the agent during a successful trajectory could provide powerful insight into the learned high-level strategy.
