- Two-stage HRL: pre-train diverse low-level skills with a simple proxy reward, freeze them, then train a high-level manager on the sparse downstream task.  
- Use a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent multiple skills via a latent variable, improving parameter and sample efficiency.  
- Introduce a mutual information regularizer to ensure latent codes produce distinct, identifiable behaviors.  
- Strong empirical results: visualizations show interpretable, diverse skills and markedly improved exploration and performance on continuous-control benchmarks.  
- Decision: Accept — framework is well-motivated and elegantly combines unsupervised skill discovery with hierarchical control.  
- Key reason: The two-stage approach plus MI bonus is a simple, effective way to discover reusable skills with minimal supervision.  
- Key reason: Thorough experiments and comparisons (including a dense-proxy baseline) demonstrate that hierarchical abstraction, not just dense rewards, is crucial.  
- SNN is sample-efficient compared to training multiple separate policies for each skill.  
- MI regularizer effectively addresses identifiability; visitation plots confirm diverse, non-overlapping skills.  
- Ablations and architecture studies (concatenation vs bilinear) support design choices and show hierarchical methods outperform flat baselines.  
- Feedback: Discuss limits of bilinear/weight-sharing architectures and how to scale to tasks requiring more fundamentally different skills or code-dependent routing.  
- Feedback: Consider end-to-end fine-tuning and learning per-skill termination policies (instead of fixed horizon T) for more flexible temporal abstraction.  
- Feedback: Provide discussion/analysis of sensitivity to the choice of proxy reward and its influence on the resulting skill set.  
- Question: Does the “Multi-policy” baseline pre-train six separate policies, implying the SNN achieves ~6x pre-training sample-complexity savings?  
- Question: Please confirm that concatenation yields per-code bias shifts while bilinear integration allows per-code first-layer weight matrices, explaining increased expressiveness.
