This paper presents a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that tackles sparse reward problems by pre-training a diverse set of skills. These skills are learned efficiently within a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), and an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information is introduced to encourage diversity and interpretability among the learned behaviors. A high-level manager policy is then trained on top of this fixed set of skills to solve downstream tasks. The paper provides strong empirical results on challenging continuous control environments, demonstrating significant improvements in exploration and sample efficiency.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method of combining Stochastic Neural Networks with an information-theoretic bonus for skill discovery is a novel and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem in reinforcement learning.
2.  The experimental results are compelling. The paper includes a particularly strong baseline comparison that clearly demonstrates the benefits of the hierarchical structure over simply using the pre-training reward as a dense reward signal for the downstream task. The visualizations are also excellent and provide clear qualitative support for the claims.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The introduction of a mutual information bonus to encourage the learning of diverse and identifiable skills is a key strength of this work. It provides a principled way to prevent the multi-modal policy from collapsing to a single behavior and results in a set of clearly interpretable skills, as shown in the excellent visitation plots.

Furthermore, the paper's empirical analysis is thorough. The baseline where the sparse reward is augmented with the dense proxy reward from the pre-training phase is a strong and important comparison. Its failure highlights that the benefits of the proposed method come from true hierarchical abstraction and improved exploration, not just from reward shaping. As the authors note, this is likely due to the difficulty of long-term credit assignment. An additional factor might be the lack of explicit diversity pressure in the baseline's training, unlike in the SNN pre-training. This insight could be valuable for future work on end-to-end hierarchical learning.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**
The paper presents a promising direction, and it could be strengthened further by addressing the following points. These are intended as constructive suggestions for improvement and do not detract from the paper's core contributions.

**Limitations and Suggestions:**
1.  **Limited Diversification of Sub-policies:** The proposed SNN architectures (concatenation and bilinear integration) modulate the policy by affecting only the first hidden layer’s biases or weights. This seems sufficient for the locomotion tasks tested, which primarily require variations of a core behavior. However, this architectural choice might be too restrictive for tasks where optimal sub-policies are more fundamentally distinct (e.g., in complex manipulation, where different skills might have very different structures). It would be insightful to see the method tested in non-locomotion domains to explore the limits of this approach.

2.  **Fixed Hierarchy:** In the current framework, the manager network is trained while the low-level skills are frozen, and the execution time for each skill is fixed. This places a significant burden on the pre-training stage to generate a skill set that is already highly effective for the downstream tasks. It would be beneficial to include a discussion of how the framework could be extended toward end-to-end training, perhaps by fine-tuning the skills or learning a termination condition for each option.

3.  **Nature of the Proxy Reward:** Related to the point above, the need for the pre-trained skills to be useful for specific downstream tasks makes the "unsupervised" proxy reward feel somewhat "supervised" by the anticipated task distribution. Acknowledging this trade-off between generality and task-specificity in the design of the proxy reward would improve the paper.

**Clarifying Questions:**
*   Could you provide more intuition on why the strong baseline in Section 7.3 (using the CoM proxy reward directly on the downstream task) performs so poorly? The paper suggests long-term credit assignment is the issue. I wonder if the lack of an explicit diversity pressure, which is central to the SNN training, is also a key factor.
*   To ensure a fair comparison of sample complexity, does the baseline that adds the CoM proxy reward also benefit from a similar amount of experience as the pre-trained policies?
*   Regarding the SNN architectures in Figure 1, is the following interpretation correct? 1) Concatenation corresponds to selecting a different bias vector for the first hidden layer based on the latent code. 2) Bilinear integration corresponds to selecting a different weight matrix mapping observations to the first hidden layer. Confirming this would aid clarity.
