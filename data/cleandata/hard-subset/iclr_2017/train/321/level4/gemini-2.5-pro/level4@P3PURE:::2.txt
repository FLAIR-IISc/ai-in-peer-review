This paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning using stochastic neural networks. A key contribution is the introduction of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability, which serves as an intrinsic reward to encourage the learning of a diverse mixture of sub-policies.

A particularly strong result is the comparison against a baseline that directly combines the intrinsic and sparse rewards. The authors show that this seemingly smoother reward signal is insufficient for solving the tasks. The authors attribute this failure to challenges in long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction. An alternative explanation could be the lack of a diversity-promoting objective in the baseline, which is central to the proposed sub-policy training. This insight could be valuable for improving similar baselines and might inform the design of end-to-end hierarchical methods, such as those explored in the option-critic or hierarchical REPS literature. The paper is also supported by clear and effective visualizations.

The paper presents a promising direction, and it may be strengthened further by addressing the following points:

1)  **Limited Diversification of Sub-policies:** The proposed architectures (concatenation and bilinear integration) modulate sub-policy behavior primarily through the first hidden layer. This seems to allow for only minor variations between sub-policies. While this is sufficient for the tested locomotion tasks, which require similar underlying skills, this approach may be too restrictive for domains where optimal sub-policies are fundamentally different. It would be insightful to see the method applied to more complex, non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation), where solving the task from different states might require vastly different skills.

2)  **Limitations of Staged Training:** The manager network is trained on top of fixed, pre-trained sub-policies that execute for a fixed number of time steps. This staged approach places a heavy burden on the intrinsic reward design, as the resulting sub-policies must be sufficiently useful for a range of downstream tasks. A discussion on the limitations of this approach and potential connections to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning, where sub-policies can be fine-tuned for the task, would strengthen the paper.

3)  **Domain-Specific Nature of "Intrinsic" Rewards:** A consequence of the staged training (point 2) is that the so-called "unsupervised" or "intrinsic" rewards must be carefully designed for the domain to produce useful skills. This potentially limits the generality of the unsupervised pre-training phase.
