This paper presents an interesting contribution to hierarchical control, with similarities to the work of Heess et al. A key strength of the work lies in its strong empirical results, demonstrating success on benchmark tasks that previous methods could not solve. However, the analysis accompanying these experiments could be substantially improved.

1.  The use of the term 'intrinsic motivation' seems inappropriate given its conventional meaning in reinforcement learning. The pre-training objectives, such as rewarding speed for locomotion or successful grasping for a manipulator, are closely aligned with the downstream tasks the agent will perform. This task-specific pre-training is distinct from the more general, task-agnostic exploration typically associated with intrinsic motivation. The pre-training tasks presented are also conceptually similar to those in Heess et al.

2.  The mutual information regularization is an elegant contribution that appears effective in general. However, as the authors acknowledge, it does not improve performance in the more complex maze environments (1, 2, and 3). The paper would be strengthened by providing some interpretation or analysis for this negative result.

3.  For reproducibility, the factorization of the state space into S_agent and S_rest should be detailed more clearly. While the paper specifies the components of S_agent (referencing Duan et al.), a precise definition of S_rest appears to be missing.

4.  The experimental analysis would benefit from a deeper investigation into the learned policies. For instance, an analysis of the high-level controller's switching behavior between sub-policies would be insightful. Further discussion of common failure modes or the effect of the high-level switching frequency on task performance would also be a welcome addition.
