- Score: Initial 6 → Final 7.

- Summary: Proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks to pre-train diverse, identifiable low-level options via an information-theoretic objective, then composes them with a high-level manager to solve sparse-reward continuous-control tasks.

- Strength 1: Introduces a principled information-theoretic intrinsic objective that encourages discovery of diverse, identifiable sub-policies.

- Strength 2: Strong empirical results showing the hierarchical approach solves tasks where standard RL with sparse rewards fails, and comparisons highlight the benefit of hierarchy for long-term credit assignment.

- Strength 3: Clear writing and excellent visualizations demonstrating qualitatively diverse and meaningful learned skills.

- Weakness 1: Latent-code modulation (concatenation/bilinear) mainly affects the first hidden layer, which may limit ability to learn fundamentally different sub-policies, especially outside locomotion domains.

- Weakness 2: Two-stage, decoupled training with fixed, non-adaptive options and fixed option durations forces heavy reliance on pre-training and prevents fine-tuning of sub-policies by the manager.

- Weakness 3: Intrinsic rewards used in pre-training (e.g., forward-motion bias) can be domain-specific, undermining claims of fully unsupervised skill discovery and raising applicability concerns for other domains.

- Question 1: Why does the strong baseline in Sec. 7.3 perform poorly—does its lack of an explicit diversity objective explain the failure and suggest improvements for non-hierarchical baselines?

- Question 2: Did the baseline receive comparable total experience, including the proposed method's pre-training experience?

- Question 3: What is the exact experimental difference between the "multi-policy" and "snn" methods?

- Question 4: Are the architectural interpretations in Fig.1 correct: (a) concatenation selects a bias vector per z; (b) bilinear integration selects a different input weight matrix per z?
