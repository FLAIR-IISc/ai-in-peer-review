### Summary

This paper proposes a two-stage hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to tackle challenging continuous control tasks characterized by sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a method for pre-training a diverse set of low-level skills, which are then utilized by a high-level "manager" policy to solve downstream tasks efficiently. In the first stage, a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) is trained in a simple environment with a generic proxy reward (e.g., forward speed). To encourage the emergence of a wide range of distinct behaviors, the authors introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the SNN's latent variable and the agent's state. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as a temporally-extended action space for a high-level policy, which learns to select the appropriate skill to solve a specific, sparse-reward task. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, showing that this approach can solve complex locomotion and navigation tasks from the benchmark by Duan et al. (2016), which were previously unsolvable by standard RL algorithms.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed framework is a novel and well-motivated approach to skill discovery and hierarchical control that effectively addresses the critical challenge of exploration in sparse-reward settings. The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks with an information-theoretic bonus for unsupervised skill acquisition is an elegant contribution.
2.  The empirical results are strong and convincing. The method successfully solves several challenging benchmark tasks that prior state-of-the-art methods failed to solve, providing clear evidence of the framework's effectiveness.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper lies in its effective solution to a well-known difficult problem in reinforcement learning. The experiments convincingly show that the hierarchical approach significantly enhances exploration (Figure 5) and enables learning in environments where standard RL algorithms with naive exploration fail entirely. The learning curves in Figure 6 clearly demonstrate a substantial performance gain over a strong baseline that uses the proxy reward directly in the downstream task.

The method for learning a diverse set of skills is well-designed. Using a single SNN to represent multiple skills allows for efficient weight sharing and scales better than training a separate policy for each skill. The introduction of the mutual information (MI) regularizer is a key component that demonstrably encourages the learning of a more diverse and useful set of skills (Figure 4), which is a valuable contribution in its own right.

However, the analysis of the results could be strengthened. For instance, while the MI bonus is shown to be beneficial in the pre-training phase, its impact on the final performance in the downstream maze tasks is inconsistent (Figure 6a-c). The paper notes this phenomenon but does not offer an analysis or hypothesis for why the more diverse skill set does not consistently translate to better performance in these specific tasks. A deeper investigation into this would strengthen the paper's conclusions about the utility of the MI bonus.

Furthermore, the framing of the pre-training phase using the term "intrinsic motivation" is slightly misleading. In the RL literature, this term typically refers to task-agnostic curiosity or novelty-based rewards. Here, the "proxy reward" (e.g., rewarding speed for a locomotion agent) is quite extrinsic and closely aligned with the capabilities required for the downstream navigation tasks. This approach is more accurately described as unsupervised pre-training or learning with an auxiliary task. This is not a fundamental flaw but a point of clarification regarding the paper's positioning in the literature, especially in relation to the work of Heess et al. (2016), which uses a similar pre-training strategy.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Analysis of Hierarchical Policy Behavior:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the method works, but provides little insight into *how* the high-level policy uses the learned skills. It would be highly beneficial to include a qualitative analysis of the manager's behavior. For instance, for a successful maze trajectory, what sequence of skills does the agent employ? Understanding the switching behavior would provide a much clearer picture of the emergent strategy.
*   **Failure Modes:** A discussion of the common failure modes would be instructive. When the hierarchical agent fails to solve a task, is it due to a missing skill in its repertoire, or is the high-level policy failing to select the correct sequence of skills? The analysis of the "Ant" agent in the appendix is excellent in this regard and provides a balanced view of the method's limitations; a brief mention of this in the main text could be valuable.
*   **Reproducibility Details:** For the sake of reproducibility, it would be helpful to explicitly define the state space factorization into `S_agent` and `S_rest` for each experimental environment. While `S_agent` is defined in the benchmark paper (Duan et al., 2016), clearly specifying `S_rest` (e.g., LIDAR readings, goal positions) for each task would make the setup unambiguous.
*   **Switch Time `T`:** The analysis of the switch time `T` in the appendix is appreciated. A brief summary of these findings in the main paper could further strengthen the work by proactively addressing the role of this important hyperparameter.

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their response:

1.  Could you offer an intuition or further analysis for why the MI bonus, despite creating a more diverse set of skills, does not consistently improve performance on Mazes 1, 2, and 3? Does it perhaps create over-specialized skills that are less useful for the "cruder" movements needed to solve those specific mazes (e.g., bumping against walls to reorient)?
2.  Regarding the framing, could you comment on your choice of the term "intrinsic motivation" as opposed to "unsupervised pre-training" or "auxiliary reward shaping," especially in light of its established meaning in the field?
