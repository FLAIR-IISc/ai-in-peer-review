This paper presents a compelling contribution to hierarchical control, drawing parallels to the approach of Heess et al. The empirical validation is a key strength, with the proposed method successfully solving benchmark tasks that have proven challenging for prior approaches. However, the analysis accompanying these strong experimental results could be further developed.

(1) Echoing the concerns of other reviewers, the term 'intrinsic motivation' seems misaligned with its common usage in the RL community. The pre-training objectives, such as rewarding locomotion speed or successful grasping, are highly specific to the downstream tasks. This approach, while effective, shares conceptual similarities with the pre-training strategies used in the work of Heess et al.

(2) The mutual information regularization is an elegant addition that appears effective in many cases. However, it is noteworthy that its benefits are not observed in the more complex maze environments (1, 2, and 3). The authors correctly point this out, but the review would be strengthened by a discussion or hypothesis explaining this specific outcome.

(3) A more precise definition of the state space factorization between S_agent and S_rest is needed. The paper provides details for S_agent, citing Duan et al., but an explicit definition for S_rest appears to be absent. Including this would significantly improve the paper's clarity and aid in the replicability of the results.

(4) The paper could be strengthened by including more in-depth analysis of the agent's emergent behavior. For instance, an investigation into the high-level policy's switching patterns would be insightful. Additionally, a broader analysis of the learned policies, perhaps highlighting common failure modes or exploring the sensitivity of performance to the timescale of the high-level actions, would be highly valuable.
