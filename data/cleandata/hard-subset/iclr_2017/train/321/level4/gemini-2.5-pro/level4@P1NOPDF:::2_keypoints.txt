- Introduces HRL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Strong empirical finding: directly combining intrinsic and sparse task rewards (baseline) fails, showing hierarchical abstraction helps beyond smoother rewards.  
- Alternative explanation: baseline may fail because it lacks an explicit diversity-promoting objective present in the proposed method.  
- Visualizations in the paper are clear and helpful.  
- Limitation 1: Concatenation and bilinear integration only modulate the first hidden layer, likely restricting sub-policy diversity—may be fine for locomotion but problematic for functionally diverse tasks.  
- Suggestion: evaluate on more complex, non-locomotion domains (e.g., robotic manipulation) where sub-policies must be fundamentally different.  
- Limitation 2: Hierarchical training is two-stage (pretrain fixed sub-policies, then train manager with fixed-duration options), placing heavy reliance on pretraining and lacking joint end-to-end adaptation.  
- Suggestion: discuss or experiment with joint training/end-to-end HRL connections (e.g., option-critic, hierarchical REPS).  
- Limitation 3: Fixed pre-trained sub-policies make “intrinsic” rewards effectively domain-specific/supervised, reducing unsupervised generality.  
- Clarification requested: why the strong baseline in Sec. 7.3 performs so poorly and fails to solve the task?  
- Clarification requested: was extra experience from sub-policy pre-training accounted for when comparing baselines?  
- Clarification requested: what is the experimental distinction between the “multi-policy” and “snn” conditions?  
- Clarification requested: confirm Figure 1 interpretations—(1) concatenation selects a different bias for the first hidden layer; (2) bilinear integration selects a different weight matrix for observation→first hidden layer.
