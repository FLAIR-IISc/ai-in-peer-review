This paper presents an interesting and relevant framework for unsupervised skill discovery using intrinsic motivation. The core idea of maximizing the mutual information between skills and states to generate a diverse set of behaviors is compelling. While the setting is well-motivated, I have several major concerns and questions regarding the method's scalability, generality, and core claims.

**1. Scalability and Failure Modes**

A key question regarding the proposed approach is its scalability and potential failure modes as the complexity of the environment and the required behaviors increases. The paper demonstrates success in relatively constrained settings. However, as the space of possible behaviors becomes richer and more complex, one might expect this approach to struggle with discovering a sufficiently diverse and useful set of skills. Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of this method? For example, under what conditions would the mutual information objective fail to produce a varied set of skills? A discussion of the method's failure modes would significantly strengthen the paper by providing a clearer understanding of its scope and applicability.

**2. State Space Discretization**

In Section 5.3, the authors state, "let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated." This phrasing suggests a discretization of the state space. Could the authors clarify if this is indeed the case? If so, it would be beneficial to understand the rationale for this design choice. More importantly, how would the proposed mutual information objective be adapted for continuous state spaces, which are common in control problems? A discussion of the potential implications or challenges of applying this method in continuous domains is crucial for evaluating its broader utility.

**3. Generalization to More Complex Embodiments**

Expanding on the issue of scalability, the experiments are conducted on agents with relatively simple morphologies (e.g., a 2-link swimmer). To better assess the generality of this approach, it would be valuable to understand how it performs on agents with more complex embodiments. For instance, would the method successfully discover a comparable range of skills for a 5-link swimmer? Including such an experiment or, at minimum, a discussion of the anticipated challenges, would provide stronger evidence for the method's applicability beyond the specific domains tested. This is an important step in assessing whether the approach is a general solution for skill discovery or one that is effective for a more limited class of agents.

**4. Claims Regarding Minimal Domain Knowledge**

The authors contrast their work with that of Heess et al. (2016), claiming their own framework is more general because it relies on intrinsic rewards requiring only "minimal domain knowledge," as opposed to a pre-specified set of goals. I find this claim to be overstated. The intrinsic rewards proposed in this paper, which are based on the agent's grid position or specific joint angles, appear to be quite hand-crafted and tailored to the specific locomotion and control tasks presented. It is not immediately clear how this reward formulation would generalize to a broader class of problems (e.g., tasks not defined by location or simple joint configurations) without similar, non-trivial domain-specific engineering. The paper would be more convincing if the authors tempered this claim and provided a more nuanced discussion of the domain knowledge implicitly encoded in their reward design.

In summary, the paper introduces a promising direction for skill discovery. However, its current form leaves several key questions regarding scalability, implementation details, and the central claims unanswered. The evaluation could be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the method's limitations, generalization capabilities, and a more balanced discussion of its reliance on domain knowledge. I am willing to reconsider my evaluation pending the authors' response to these points.
