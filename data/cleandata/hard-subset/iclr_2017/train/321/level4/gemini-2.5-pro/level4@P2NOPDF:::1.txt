This paper presents an interesting and effective approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The experiments are a clear strength of the work, demonstrating successful policies on several challenging continuous control benchmarks, some of which have not been solved by previous methods. While the empirical results are compelling, the analysis and presentation could be strengthened in a few key areas.

1.  **Terminology of "Intrinsic Motivation":** The use of the term "intrinsic motivation" for the pre-training phase could be misleading given its conventional meaning in the reinforcement learning community. Typically, intrinsic motivation refers to task-agnostic objectives like curiosity or empowerment, designed to encourage general exploration. In this work, the pre-training rewards—such as maximizing forward speed for a locomoting agent or rewarding successful grasps for a manipulator—are highly aligned with the objectives of the final downstream tasks. This approach is more analogous to curriculum learning or task-specific skill pre-training, similar in spirit to the methods used by Heess et al. Clarifying this distinction would help to position the contribution more accurately within the existing literature.

2.  **Effectiveness of Mutual Information Regularization:** The mutual information regularization term is an elegant mechanism for encouraging skill diversity. However, as the authors rightly acknowledge, its contribution appears to diminish in the most complex environments (mazes 1, 2, and 3). The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion or analysis of this result. Is there a hypothesis for why this occurs? For instance, perhaps in these long-horizon tasks, the immediate utility of a diverse skill set is outweighed by the need for a few highly optimized primitives. Providing some interpretation would offer valuable insight into the scaling properties and potential limitations of this regularization technique.

3.  **Reproducibility and State Space Factorization:** For the work to be fully reproducible, the manuscript should explicitly define the factorization of the state space into `S_agent` and `S_rest` for each environment. While `S_agent` is specified, a clear definition of `S_rest` seems to be missing. This information is critical for other researchers to accurately implement the model and verify the results. Including a table or detailed description of this state space partition in the appendix or methods section would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Analysis of Learned Policies:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the learned hierarchical policies. An investigation into the master policy's switching behavior would be particularly insightful. For example, providing visualizations or statistics on which skills are selected in different phases of a task could illuminate the emergent structure of the solution. Furthermore, a discussion of common failure modes or a sensitivity analysis on the effect of the switching time hyperparameter on performance would provide a more complete picture of the agent's behavior and the robustness of the proposed method.

In summary, this is a strong paper with impressive empirical results. Addressing the points above, particularly regarding the clarity of the experimental setup and the depth of the analysis, would further enhance the quality and impact of this contribution.
