This paper proposes a general framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to solve tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-phase approach: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) guided by a single, generic proxy reward and an information-theoretic regularizer. This is followed by a second phase where a high-level policy is trained on top of these frozen skills to solve specific downstream tasks. The authors demonstrate that using SNNs with a bilinear integration of latent variables and a mutual information bonus effectively produces a wide range of interpretable skills. These skills are then shown to significantly improve exploration and accelerate learning on a series of challenging continuous control tasks.

I recommend to **Reject** this paper in its current form. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The central claim that the method requires "very minimal domain knowledge" is not fully substantiated. The proposed proxy reward, while simpler than a set of explicit goals, appears to be a form of task-specific engineering that limits the claimed generality of the framework.
2.  The experimental evaluation in the main paper is not sufficient to assess the broader applicability and limitations of the approach. Crucial results on more complex agents and discussion of failure modes are relegated to the appendix, preventing a balanced view of the method's robustness.

**Supporting Arguments:**

Regarding the first point, the paper positions its contribution against prior work like Heess et al. (2016) by arguing that their method avoids the need to specify a set of goals, instead using a single proxy reward. However, the chosen proxy reward—proportional to the magnitude of the agent's speed—is highly suited for the locomotion tasks presented but is not universally applicable. The paper suggests a reward for grasping ("successful grasping of any object") as another example, but this is also a non-trivial, hand-engineered reward signal. This level of reward design appears to be another form of domain-specific knowledge, just at a different level of abstraction from specifying goal locations. The paper would be stronger if it tempered its claims of generality and instead more clearly defined the class of problems for which this proxy-reward pre-training paradigm is most effective.

Regarding the second point, the main experiments are conducted on the "Swimmer" robot, which is a relatively simple agent. The claims of a "general framework" would be far more convincing if the results on the more complex "Snake" and "Ant" robots, currently in the appendix, were integrated into the main body of the paper. More importantly, Appendix D reveals a critical failure mode for the "Ant" robot, where the hard switching between frozen skills leads to physical instability. This is a significant limitation of the proposed hierarchical architecture that directly impacts its applicability to more complex or unstable agents. A transparent discussion of such limitations in the main experimental section is essential for the reader to properly assess the contributions and practical viability of the proposed method.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for Improvement:**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision. The technical approach of using SNNs with a bilinear integration and an MI bonus to generate a diverse skill set is novel and compelling. The visualizations in Figure 4 provide clear evidence for the effectiveness of each component.

I have a few questions that could help clarify my understanding and strengthen the work:
*   In Section 5.3, the mutual information bonus is calculated by discretizing the agent's coordinate space. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the skill learning process to the granularity of this discretization? The paper mentions that an MLP regressor could be an alternative for higher-dimensional spaces; have you explored how such an estimation approach would compare to the discretization method in terms of performance and sample efficiency?
*   The paper identifies instability during skill switching as a primary failure mode for the "Ant" agent. Beyond the future work mentioned (e.g., learning a transition policy), have you considered simpler heuristics to mitigate this issue, such as blending between policies for a short duration or resetting parts of the agent's state (e.g., momentum) upon switching? Addressing this practical issue would significantly increase the robustness of the framework.
*   The choice to use the center of mass (CoM) coordinates for the MI bonus is another form of domain knowledge. How does this choice affect the learned skills? For instance, would including the agent's orientation in addition to its position result in a different or more useful set of behaviors for navigation tasks?
