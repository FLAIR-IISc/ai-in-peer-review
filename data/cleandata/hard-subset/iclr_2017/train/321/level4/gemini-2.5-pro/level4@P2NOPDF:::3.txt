This paper presents an interesting framework for unsupervised skill discovery through intrinsic motivation. While the setting is compelling, I have several questions and criticisms regarding the method's scalability, design choices, and positioning relative to prior work.

1.  **Scalability and Diversity of Discovered Skills:**
    The paper should discuss the limitations and potential failure modes of the proposed approach. As the complexity of the environment and the potential richness of agent behaviors increase, I expect that this method may face challenges in discovering a sufficiently diverse and comprehensive set of skills. What are the anticipated failure modes as the state and action spaces become more complex? A discussion on the scalability of the skill discovery process would significantly strengthen the paper.

2.  **State Space Discretization:**
    In Section 5.3, the text states, "let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated." This phrasing suggests that the state space is discretized. Could the authors confirm if this is the case? If so, please provide the rationale for this design choice. Furthermore, it would be insightful to understand how the method would be adapted for, and perform in, a continuous state space and what challenges that might present.

3.  **Generalization to More Complex Embodiments:**
    Related to the question of scalability, the paper's claims of generality would be strengthened by assessing the method on agents with more complex morphologies. For instance, how would the approach perform with a 5-link swimmer compared to the 2-link version used in the experiments? Such an evaluation is crucial for understanding whether the method can generalize beyond the relatively simple embodiments tested and for properly assessing its applicability to a wider range of problems.

4.  **Hand-Crafting of Intrinsic Rewards:**
    The authors differentiate their work from Heess et al. (2016) by claiming their framework is more general because it "use[s] intrinsic rewards as the only signal... the construction of which only requires very minimal domain knowledge," in contrast to requiring a pre-specified set of goals. I find this distinction to be overstated. The proposed intrinsic rewards, which are based on the agent's grid location, appear to be a form of hand-crafted engineering specific to the presented control tasks. This reliance on a structured, location-based reward function seems comparable in its level of domain-specificity to providing a set of target goals. The authors should either provide a more robust justification for why their method requires significantly less domain knowledge or moderate their claims regarding this distinction.
