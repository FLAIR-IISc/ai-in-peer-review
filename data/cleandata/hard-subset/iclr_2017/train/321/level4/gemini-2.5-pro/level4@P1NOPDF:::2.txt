This paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that uses stochastic neural networks. The authors introduce an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability, which serves as an additional reward signal to encourage the learning of a diverse mixture of sub-policies.

A particularly compelling result is the comparison against a strong baseline that directly combines the intrinsic rewards with the sparse task rewards. The authors show that this seemingly smoother, combined reward signal is insufficient for solving the tasks. The paper attributes this failure to the difficulties of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction. An alternative explanation could be the lack of a diversity-promoting objective in the baseline's training, which is a key component of the proposed method. It would be interesting to see if this insight could be used to improve the baseline or inform the design of new end-to-end HRL algorithms, such as those in the vein of hierarchical REPS or the option-critic architecture. The visualizations provided are clear and helpful.

The paper presents a promising direction, which could be strengthened further by addressing the following points:

1)  **Limited diversification of sub-policies:** Both the concatenation and bilinear integration methods seem to allow for only minimal differentiation between sub-policies, as they only modulate the weights of the first hidden layer. This may not be a significant issue for the locomotion tasks tested, which primarily require variations of the same underlying gait. However, this limitation could become more pronounced in tasks where the optimal sub-policies are more functionally diverse. It would be valuable to see the method applied to more complex, non-locomotion domains, such as robotic manipulation, where the optimal action sequence from one state can be fundamentally different from that of another.

2)  **Limitations of the hierarchical training scheme:** The current approach trains the manager network while keeping the sub-policies fixed. Additionally, the sub-policies operate for a fixed number of time steps. This two-stage process places a heavy burden on the pre-training phase; the intrinsic rewards and the resulting sub-policies must be exceptionally well-suited for solving downstream tasks. It would be beneficial to include more discussion or results on how the framework might handle cases where this assumption does not hold, ideally by drawing connections to end-to-end HRL frameworks where the entire hierarchy is trained jointly.

3)  **Domain-specific nature of "intrinsic" rewards:** Following from the previous point, the reliance on a fixed set of pre-trained sub-policies makes the "intrinsic" or "unsupervised" reward design seem more akin to a supervised, domain-specific process. This may be an unavoidable consequence of the two-stage training scheme.

A few final questions for clarification:

*   Could the authors provide more intuition as to why the strong baseline in Section 7.3 (presumably the "CoM maze reward" condition) performs so poorly and fails to solve the task?
*   When comparing against this baseline, was the additional experience gained during the sub-policy pre-training phase accounted for?
*   What is the distinction between the "multi-policy" and "snn" conditions mentioned in the experiments?
*   Regarding Figure 1, is my understanding correct?

    *   (2) Bilinear integration: The sub-policy index *z* selects a different weight matrix for the connection between the observation and the first hidden layer.
