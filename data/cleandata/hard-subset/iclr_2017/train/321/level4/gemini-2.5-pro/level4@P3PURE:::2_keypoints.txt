- Proposes an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an intrinsic reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Shows a strong empirical result: simply combining intrinsic and sparse rewards in a flat baseline fails, argued to be due to long-term credit assignment and benefits of hierarchical abstraction.  
- Notes an alternative explanation: the baseline lacks an explicit diversity-promoting objective, which may explain its poor performance and suggests improvements for end-to-end hierarchical methods.  
- Provides clear and effective visualizations supporting the results.  
- Limitation: sub-policy modulation (via concatenation and bilinear integration) primarily alters the first hidden layer, likely allowing only minor variations and possibly insufficient for tasks needing fundamentally different skills.  
- Suggests testing on more complex, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) to assess generality of learned sub-policies.  
- Limitation: staged training (fixed, pre-trained sub-policies executed for fixed timesteps) places heavy burden on intrinsic reward design and may limit downstream usefulness.  
- Recommends discussing connections to end-to-end hierarchical learning where sub-policies can be fine-tuned.  
- Limitation: the “unsupervised” intrinsic rewards appear domain-specific, potentially reducing generality of pre-training.  
- Question: Why does the CoM maze reward baseline perform so poorly and fail to solve the task?  
- Question: Does the baseline comparison account for extra experience gathered during sub-policy pre-training?  
- Question: What is the distinction between the “multi-policy” and “snn” architectures?  
- Question/clarification about Fig.1: (1) does “concatenation” apply an option-dependent bias to the first hidden layer? (2) does “bilinear integration” use an option-dependent observation-to-hidden weight matrix?
