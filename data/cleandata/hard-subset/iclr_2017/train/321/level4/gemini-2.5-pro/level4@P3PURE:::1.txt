This paper presents an interesting approach to hierarchical control, with a methodology similar to the work of Heess et al. A key strength of the paper is its strong experimental results, which demonstrate successful completion of benchmark tasks that were intractable for prior methods. However, the analysis of these experiments could be strengthened.

1.  The use of the term 'intrinsic motivation' seems inappropriate, a concern that may be shared by other reviewers. In the context of reinforcement learning, this term typically implies a more general, task-agnostic form of exploration. The pre-training objectives used here, such as rewarding a robot for locomotion speed or a manipulator for grasping, are highly relevant to the downstream tasks. While effective, these pre-training tasks are similar in spirit to those used by Heess et al. and are not truly 'intrinsic' in the conventional sense.

2.  The mutual information regularization is an elegant concept that appears to work well in many cases. However, the authors note that it does not seem to help in the more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3). Could the authors provide any interpretation or analysis for why the regularization fails to provide a benefit in these specific scenarios?

3.  For the sake of replicability, the factorization of the state space into S_agent and S_rest should be detailed more explicitly. The paper specifies the components of S_agent, but a clear definition of S_rest seems to be missing from the text.

4.  The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more in-depth analysis of the learned policies. For instance, an analysis of the high-level controller's switching behavior would be very insightful. More generally, providing details on common failure modes or the effect of the switching time on overall performance would be welcome additions.
