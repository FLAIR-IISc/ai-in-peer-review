While the problem setting presented in this paper is compelling, I have several criticisms and questions regarding the proposed approach:

1.  What are the failure modes of this work? As the complexity of potential behaviors increases, it seems plausible that the approach may struggle to discover a diverse range of skills. Could the authors comment on the limitations and scalability of their method in this regard?

2.  In Section 5.3, the text states, "let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated." This suggests that the state space is discretized. Could the authors confirm this and explain the motivation for this choice? Furthermore, how would the proposed method perform in a continuous state space?

3.  Expanding on the first point about scalability, how does the approach generalize to agents with more complex morphologies? For instance, would it be effective for a 5-link swimmer instead of the 2-link version used in the experiments? Evaluating the method on more complex embodiments seems crucial for assessing its generality.

4.  The authors distinguish their work from Heess et al. (2016) by claiming their intrinsic rewards require "very minimal domain knowledge," in contrast to a pre-specified set of goals. I find this distinction questionable. The intrinsic rewards proposed in this paper also appear to be significantly hand-engineered and tailored to a specific, and seemingly limited, set of control tasks. Could the authors further justify why their reward design is fundamentally less reliant on domain knowledge than the goal-based pre-training they compare against?
