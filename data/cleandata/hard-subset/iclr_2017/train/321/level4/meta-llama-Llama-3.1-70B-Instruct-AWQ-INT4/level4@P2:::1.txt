Interesting work on hierarchical control, similar to the work of Heess et al. Experiments are strong and manage to complete benchmarks that previous work could not. Analysis of the experiments is a bit on the weaker side.

**Summary of the paper's contributions**

The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, where a set of skills is learned in a pre-training environment and then used to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach uses Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies and an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage diversity of behaviors. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in learning a wide range of skills and achieving strong performance on challenging tasks.

**Decision and key reasons**

Based on the paper's contributions and the analysis of the experiments, I decide to **Accept** this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper proposes a novel and effective approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning, which is able to learn a wide range of skills and achieve strong performance on challenging tasks.
2. The experiments are strong and manage to complete benchmarks that previous work could not.

**Supporting arguments**

The paper's approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is a key innovation, and the information-theoretic regularizer is an elegant solution to encouraging diversity of behaviors. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in a variety of settings.

**Additional feedback**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1. The use of the term 'intrinsic' motivation is somewhat misleading, as it implies that the pre-training tasks are not geared towards the downstream tasks. It would be more accurate to describe the pre-training tasks as 'locomotion' or 'manipulation' tasks.
2. The Mutual Information regularization is effective in some cases, but not in others. It would be helpful to provide more analysis of why this is the case.
3. The factorization between Sagent and Srest should be clearly detailed in the paper. While Duan et al specify Sagent, Srest should also be clearly specified for replicability.
4. It would be interesting to provide more analysis of the switching behavior of the agent, including failure modes and the effects of switching time on performance.

**Questions for the authors**

1. Can you provide more analysis of why the Mutual Information regularization is not effective in some cases?
2. Can you clarify the factorization between Sagent and Srest, and provide more details on how Srest is specified?
3. Can you provide more analysis of the switching behavior of the agent, including failure modes and the effects of switching time on performance?
