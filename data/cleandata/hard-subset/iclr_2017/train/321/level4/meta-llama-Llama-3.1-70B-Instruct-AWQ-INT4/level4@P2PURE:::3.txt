I appreciate the setting presented in this paper, but I have several criticisms and questions that need to be addressed.

Firstly, it is unclear what the failure modes of this approach are. As the complexity of behaviors increases, I expect this method to struggle with discovering a diverse set of skills. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations of their approach in handling complex behaviors.

Furthermore, in Section 5.3, the authors define a random variable X to denote the grid in which the agent is currently situated. However, it is not specified whether the space is discretized, and if so, what the motivations behind this choice are. Additionally, the authors should discuss how their approach would be affected if the space is not discretized.

Expanding on my first point, it is essential to assess the generality of this approach by evaluating its performance on more complicated embodiments. For instance, would the method work with a 5-link swimmer instead of a 2-link one? The authors should provide experimental results or theoretical analysis to demonstrate the scalability of their approach.

I also disagree with the authors' claim that their method requires minimal domain knowledge. They argue that their approach differs from Heess et al. (2016) in that it uses intrinsic rewards as the only signal to the agent during the pre-training phase. However, the rewards proposed in this paper appear to be hand-crafted and specific to a limited set of control tasks, which raises questions about the generality of the approach.
