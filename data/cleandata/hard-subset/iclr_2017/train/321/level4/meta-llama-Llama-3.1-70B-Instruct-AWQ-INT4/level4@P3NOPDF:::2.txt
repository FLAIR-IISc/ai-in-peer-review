This paper presents a novel approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) using stochastic neural networks. The authors introduce an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward for learning a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A notable result in the paper is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this supposedly smooth reward signal is insufficient to solve tasks.

The authors provide a compelling argument for the difficulty of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction. However, an alternative explanation for the baseline's poor performance might be the diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training, which is absent in the baseline case. This insight could potentially inform the improvement of the baseline and the proposal of new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods, similar to hierarchical REPS and option-critic.

The paper presents a promising direction, and addressing the following points could further strengthen it:

1. Limited diversification of sub-policies: The concatenation and bilinear integration methods allow for only minimal differentiation between sub-policies through the first hidden weight. While this is not a problem in the tested tasks, which require similar locomotion policies with minimal diversification, this limitation may become more apparent in other tasks that demand more diverse sub-policies. It would be interesting to see the application of this method to more challenging, non-locomotion domains, such as manipulation tasks, where ideal sub-policies can vary significantly depending on the initial state.

2. Limitation on hierarchical policies: The manager network is trained while the sub-policies are fixed, and the time steps for sub-policies are also fixed. This requires the "intrinsic" rewards and their learned sub-policies to be highly effective in solving downstream tasks. It would be beneficial to see more discussions and results on handling such cases, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3. Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem domain-specific/supervised rewards: Due to the limitations mentioned in point 2, this might be unavoidable.

I have some additional questions: What is the intuition behind the poor performance of the strong baseline in section 7.3, specifically the "CoM maze reward" baseline? Does this baseline account for the extra experience gained by the pre-trained policies? Can you clarify the concept of multi-policy in comparison to stochastic neural networks (SNNs)?

Regarding Figure 1, I would like to confirm the following: (1) Does the concatenation method imply that different biases are applied to the first hidden layer based on the categorical value? (2) Does the bilinear integration method involve different weight matrices connecting the observation to the first hidden layer?

The paper includes nice visualizations that effectively illustrate the proposed method.
