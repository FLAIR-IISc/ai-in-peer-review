**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) using stochastic neural networks (SNNs). The authors introduce a method for learning a diverse mixture of sub-policies using SNNs and an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward. The paper presents a promising direction for HRL and provides a comparison with a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards. The authors also provide visualizations of the learned policies and skills.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Based on the paper's content and the reviewer's assessment, I recommend accepting the paper with a score of 7. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper presents a novel and promising approach to HRL using SNNs and information-theoretic measures.
2. The authors provide a thorough comparison with a strong baseline and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
3. The paper includes nice visualizations of the learned policies and skills, which helps to illustrate the concepts and results.

**Supporting Arguments and Feedback**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. However, there are a few areas that could be improved:

1. Limited diversification of sub-policies: The authors should consider applying their approach to more challenging domains where ideal sub-policies are more diverse.
2. Limitation on hierarchical policies: The authors should discuss and explore ways to handle cases where the manager network and sub-policies are not fixed.
3. Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem domain-specific/supervised rewards: The authors should provide more discussion on this point and explore ways to make the rewards more generalizable.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

1. The strong baseline in section 7.3 performs poorly, and it is unclear why this is the case. The authors should provide more insight into this result.
2. The authors should clarify whether the baseline takes into account the extra experience pre-trained policies have got.
3. The authors should explain the difference between the multi-policy and SNN approaches.
4. In figure 1, the authors should confirm whether the interpretation of concatenation and bilinear integration is correct.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more insight into why the strong baseline in section 7.3 performs poorly?
2. Does the baseline take into account the extra experience pre-trained policies have got?
3. What is the difference between the multi-policy and SNN approaches?
4. Can you confirm whether the interpretation of concatenation and bilinear integration in figure 1 is correct?
