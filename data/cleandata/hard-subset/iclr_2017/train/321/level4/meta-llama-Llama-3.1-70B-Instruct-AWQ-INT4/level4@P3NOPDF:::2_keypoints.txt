- Reviewer increased score from 6 to 7.

- Paper proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic reward for option identifiability to learn diverse sub-policies.

- Strong empirical finding: combining intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards (baseline) is insufficient to solve tasks.

- Authors argue long-term credit assignment is hard and hierarchical abstraction helps.

- Alternative explanation: baseline’s poor performance may stem from the absence of an explicit diversity requirement during sub-policy training.

- Suggestion: baseline could be improved and new end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic) could be proposed.

- Concern 1: Concatenation and bilinear integration limit sub-policy diversification (only small bias/weight differences), which may fail on tasks requiring highly diverse skills.

- Recommendation: Test on more challenging, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) where sub-policies must vary significantly.

- Concern 2: Manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed option durations, forcing intrinsic rewards/sub-policies to be highly effective for downstream tasks.

- Recommendation: Discuss and evaluate end-to-end hierarchical learning where sub-policies and manager co-adapt and option durations vary.

- Concern 3: Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards may be domain-specific and act like supervised rewards due to the above limitations.

- Questions for authors: Intuition for poor performance of the "CoM maze reward" baseline; whether the baseline accounts for extra experience from pre-trained policies.

- Question: Clarify the distinction between "multi-policy" and stochastic neural networks (SNNs).

- Figure 1 clarifications requested: (1) Does concatenation apply different biases to the first hidden layer per categorical value? (2) Does bilinear integration use different weight matrices from observations to the first hidden layer?

- Positive note: Visualizations in the paper are effective and helpful.
