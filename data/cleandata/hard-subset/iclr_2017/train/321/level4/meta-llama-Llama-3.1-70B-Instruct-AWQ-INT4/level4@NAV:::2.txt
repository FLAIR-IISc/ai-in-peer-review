This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) approach using stochastic neural networks, incorporating an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an auxiliary reward to promote learning of diverse sub-policy mixtures. A notable contribution is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic and sparse rewards, demonstrating the limitations of this supposedly smooth reward in solving tasks. The authors' argument on the challenges of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction is well-taken, and an alternative explanation for the baseline's poor performance could be the absence of diversity requirements in sub-policy training. This insight may inform improvements to the baseline and the development of novel end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods, akin to hierarchical REPS/option-critic approaches. The visualizations are also commendable.

To further enhance the paper, the authors may consider addressing the following points:
1) The sub-policy diversification appears limited, as both concatenation and bilinear integration methods only allow for minimal differentiation through the first hidden weight. While this may not be an issue in the tested tasks, which require similar locomotion policies, it may become more apparent in tasks with more diverse ideal sub-policies, such as manipulation tasks. It would be interesting to see the approach applied to more challenging, non-locomotion domains.
2) The hierarchical policy learning framework has limitations, as the manager network is trained with fixed sub-policies and time steps. This places a high burden on the "intrinsic" rewards and learned sub-policies to effectively solve downstream tasks. Further discussion and results on handling such cases, potentially connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning, would be beneficial.
3) The intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem domain-specific, which may be unavoidable due to the fixed sub-policies and time steps.
