The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) method using stochastic neural networks, introducing an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward to learn a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A notable result is the comparison with a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this approach fails to solve tasks. The authors argue that long-term credit assignment and benefits from hierarchical abstraction contribute to this failure. Alternatively, the diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training, which is absent in the baseline, may also be a contributing factor. This insight could inform improvements to the baseline and the development of new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods, similar to hierarchical REPS/option-critic approaches. The visualizations are also well-presented.

The paper presents a promising direction, but could be further strengthened by addressing the following points:

1. Limited diversification of sub-policies: The proposed concatenation and bilinear integration methods only allow for minimal differentiation in sub-policies through the first hidden weight. While this is not a concern for the tested tasks, which require similar locomotion policies with minimal diversification, this limitation may be more pronounced in tasks with more diverse ideal sub-policies, such as manipulation tasks. It would be interesting to see the application of this method to more challenging, non-locomotion domains.

2. Limitation on hierarchical policies: The manager network is trained while keeping the sub-policies fixed, and the time steps for sub-policies are also fixed. This requires the "intrinsic" rewards and learned sub-policies to be highly effective in solving downstream tasks. It would be beneficial to discuss and provide results on handling such cases, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3. Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem domain-specific/supervised rewards: Due to the fixed sub-policies and time steps, this limitation appears unavoidable.

I have some final questions: What is the intuition behind the poor performance of the strong baseline in Section 7.3, and its inability to solve the task? Is this baseline the "CoM maze reward"? Does the baseline account for the extra experience obtained by the pre-trained policies? What is the distinction between multi-policy and stochastic neural networks (SNN)? In Figure 1, is it correct that (1) concatenation implies different biases for the first hidden layer based on categorical values, and (2) bilinear integration implies different weight matrices connecting the observation to the first hidden layer?
