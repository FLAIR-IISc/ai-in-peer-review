This paper proposes a general framework for training policies for a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The framework first learns useful skills in a pre-training environment, guided by a single proxy reward signal, and then leverages the acquired skills for learning faster in downstream tasks. The approach brings together some of the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods.

I decide to accept this paper with some reservations. The main reason for my decision is that the paper tackles a significant problem in reinforcement learning, namely, tasks with sparse rewards or long horizons. The proposed framework is well-motivated, and the use of Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and information-theoretic regularizers is a novel and interesting approach.

However, I have some concerns and questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, what are the failure modes of this work? As the richness of behaviors gets complex, I expected this approach to have issues with the diversity of skills that could be discovered. The authors should provide more analysis or discussion on the limitations of their approach.

Secondly, the paper mentions that the space is discretized in Section 5.3, but it is not clear why this is necessary or what happens if the space is not discretized. The authors should provide more explanation on this point.

Thirdly, the authors claim that their approach works with more complicated embodiment, such as a 5-link swimmer, but this is not demonstrated in the paper. I think it is essential to assess the generality of this approach by testing it on more complex robots or environments.

Lastly, the authors claim that their approach is different from Heess et al. (2016) because they use intrinsic rewards as the only signal to the agent during the pre-training phase, which requires minimal domain knowledge. However, I don't entirely agree with this. The rewards proposed in this paper are also quite hand-crafted and specific to a seemingly limited set of control tasks. The authors should provide more discussion on this point and clarify how their approach is different from existing methods.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors provide more analysis on the failure modes of their approach, clarify the discretization of the space, demonstrate the generality of their approach on more complex robots or environments, and provide more discussion on the comparison with existing methods.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more analysis or discussion on the limitations of your approach, particularly on the diversity of skills that can be discovered?
* Why is it necessary to discretize the space in Section 5.3, and what happens if the space is not discretized?
* Can you demonstrate the generality of your approach by testing it on more complex robots or environments, such as a 5-link swimmer?
* How do you respond to the criticism that your rewards are also hand-crafted and specific to a limited set of control tasks, and how does your approach differ from existing methods?
