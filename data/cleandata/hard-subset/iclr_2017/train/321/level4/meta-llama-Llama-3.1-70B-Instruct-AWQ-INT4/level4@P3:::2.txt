**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning using stochastic neural networks (SNNs). The authors introduce an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward for learning a diverse mixture of sub-policies. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several challenging tasks, including maze navigation and object gathering, and show that it outperforms a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend accepting this paper, with a score of 7. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper presents a novel and promising direction for hierarchical reinforcement learning.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on several challenging tasks.
3. The paper includes nice visualizations that help to illustrate the results.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward for learning a diverse mixture of sub-policies. This approach allows the agent to learn a wide range of skills that can be used to solve downstream tasks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several challenging tasks, including maze navigation and object gathering.

One of the strengths of the paper is its thorough evaluation of the proposed approach. The authors compare their method to a strong baseline that combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards and show that it outperforms this baseline. They also provide nice visualizations that help to illustrate the results.

**Additional Feedback**

To further strengthen the paper, the authors may want to consider the following points:

1. **Limited diversification of sub-policies**: The paper's approach may not be suitable for tasks that require more diverse sub-policies. It would be interesting to see how the approach performs on harder, non-locomotion domains.
2. **Limitation on hierarchical policies**: The Manager network is trained while the sub-policies are fixed, and the time steps for sub-policies are fixed. It would be nice to see some more discussions/results on handling such cases, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.
3. **Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem domain-specific/supervised rewards**: The paper's approach may not be suitable for tasks that require more generalizable rewards.
