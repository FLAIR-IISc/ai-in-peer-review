This paper presents an interesting approach to hierarchical control, bearing similarities to the work of Heess et al. The experiments are strong, successfully tackling benchmarks that previous work was unable to complete. However, the analysis of these experiments could be more comprehensive.

One concern I share with other reviewers is the use of the term 'intrinsic' motivation, which seems somewhat misplaced given its current meaning in the reinforcement learning (RL) community. The pre-training of robots with locomotion tasks by rewarding speed, or with manipulation tasks by rewarding grasping, is heavily tailored to the tasks they will later perform. This is reminiscent of the pre-training tasks employed by Heess et al., although not identical. This raises questions about the appropriateness of the term 'intrinsic' in this context.

The Mutual Information regularization is an elegant contribution that generally yields positive results. However, it is noteworthy that it does not appear to provide benefits in the more complex mazes 1, 2, and 3. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a deeper interpretation or analysis of this finding would be valuable.

For the sake of replicability, it would be beneficial to clearly detail the factorization between Sagent and Srest in the paper. While Duan et al. specify Sagent, a clear specification of Srest is also necessary to ensure that the method can be accurately reproduced. I may have overlooked it, but I could not find a detailed description of Srest in the paper.

Lastly, an analysis of the agent's switching behavior would be a welcome addition to the paper. More broadly, further analysis of the policies, including failure modes and the impact of switching time on performance, would provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.
