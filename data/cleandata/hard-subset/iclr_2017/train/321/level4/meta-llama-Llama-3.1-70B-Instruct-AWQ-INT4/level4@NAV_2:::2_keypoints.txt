- Proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to learn diverse sub-policies.  
- Shows a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic and sparse rewards fails, arguing smooth reward alone can't solve tasks.  
- Notes an alternative explanation: baseline lacks enforced diversity in sub-policy training, which may explain poor performance.  
- Suggests this insight could inform improving the baseline and connecting to end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).  
- Praises the paper’s visualizations.  
- Limitation: concatenation and bilinear integration only enable minimal sub-policy differentiation via the first hidden layer—sufficient for locomotion but limiting for more diverse tasks.  
- Recommendation: evaluate on harder, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) where ideal sub-policies differ substantially.  
- Limitation: manager is trained with fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time steps, reducing adaptability.  
- Suggestion: discuss or experiment with end-to-end hierarchical learning where manager and sub-policies (and time scales) are learned jointly.  
- Consequence: intrinsic/unsupervised rewards risk being domain-specific or effectively supervised due to fixed sub-policies.  
- Questions for authors: why does the strong baseline in Sec. 7.3 fail; does the baseline use the extra experience from pre-trained policies; what exactly is “multi-policy” vs SNN?  
- Clarification request: confirm concatenation = category-dependent bias to first hidden layer, bilinear = category-dependent input weight matrix.  
- Decision: Accept — promising direction, clear exposition, strong baseline comparison, and useful visualizations.  
- Suggested improvements: add discussion/results on non-fixed sub-policies/time steps, test broader domains, and provide more intuition for baseline failure.
