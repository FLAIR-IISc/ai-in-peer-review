I appreciate the setting presented in this paper, but I have several criticisms and questions that need to be addressed:

(1) What are the failure modes of this work? As the richness of behaviors becomes increasingly complex, I expect this approach to struggle with the diversity of skills that could be discovered. It would be beneficial to discuss the limitations of the proposed method and how it handles complex scenarios.

(2) In Section 5.3, the authors define "X as a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated." I would like clarification on whether the space is discretized and, if so, why this choice was made. Additionally, it would be helpful to discuss how the approach would perform if the space were not discretized.

(3) Building on my first point, I question whether the approach can handle more complicated embodiments. For instance, would the method work with a 5-link swimmer instead of a 2-link one? I believe this is an important consideration in assessing the generality of this approach.

(4) The authors claim that their framework is distinct from the work of Heess et al. (2016), who independently proposed learning a range of skills in a pre-training environment for downstream tasks. However, the authors argue that their pre-training setup only requires intrinsic rewards as the signal to the agent, which can be constructed with minimal domain knowledge. In contrast, Heess et al. (2016) require a set of goals to be specified. I don't entirely agree with this distinction. The rewards proposed in this paper also appear to be hand-crafted and specific to a limited set of control tasks, which may limit the generality of the approach.

Overall, while I find the setting presented in this paper intriguing, I believe these criticisms and questions need to be addressed to strengthen the work.
