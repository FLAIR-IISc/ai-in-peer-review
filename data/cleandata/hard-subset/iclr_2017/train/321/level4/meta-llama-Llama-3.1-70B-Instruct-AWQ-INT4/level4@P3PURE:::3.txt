I appreciate the setting presented in this paper, but I have several criticisms and questions that I believe need to be addressed.

Firstly, I would like to know what the failure modes of this approach are. As the richness of behaviors becomes increasingly complex, I expect this method to struggle with the diversity of skills that could be discovered. It is crucial to understand the limitations of the proposed approach to assess its robustness.

Secondly, in Section 5.3, the authors introduce a random variable X denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated. I would like to know if the space is discretized and, if so, why this choice was made. Moreover, it would be helpful to discuss what happens if the space is not discretized, as this could significantly impact the results.

Expanding on my first point, I am curious to know whether the approach can handle more complicated embodiments. For instance, would it work with a 5-link swimmer instead of a 2-link one? I believe this is essential to evaluate the generality of the proposed framework.

Lastly, I disagree with the authors' claim that their method differs significantly from Heess et al. (2016). The authors argue that their pre-training setup only requires minimal domain knowledge, as it relies solely on intrinsic rewards. However, I argue that the rewards proposed in this paper are also quite hand-crafted and specific to a limited set of control tasks. This seems to undermine the authors' claim of requiring minimal domain knowledge, as the design of the rewards itself appears to necessitate a certain level of expertise.
