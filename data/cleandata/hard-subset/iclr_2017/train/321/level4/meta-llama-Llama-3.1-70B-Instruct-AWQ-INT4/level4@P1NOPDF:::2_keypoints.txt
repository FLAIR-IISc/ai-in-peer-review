- Reviewer raised score from 6 to 7.

- Paper proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to learn diverse sub-policies.

- Key empirical claim: a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic and sparse rewards fails, showing that smoothing the reward alone is insufficient.

- Alternative explanation: the baseline’s failure may stem from the diversity constraint imposed during sub-policy training, which the baseline lacks.

- Suggestion: this insight could guide improvements to the baseline and inspire end-to-end hierarchical policy methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).

- Limitation 1: concatenation and bilinear integration only allow minimal sub-policy differentiation via first-layer weights; may not scale to tasks requiring highly diverse sub-policies.

- Recommendation 1: evaluate on more challenging, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) where ideal sub-policies differ substantially.

- Limitation 2: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed temporal abstraction, forcing intrinsic rewards/sub-policies to be highly effective for downstream tasks.

- Recommendation 2: discuss and experiment with approaches that relax fixed sub-policies/time-steps and connect to end-to-end hierarchical learning.

- Limitation 3: intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific or effectively supervised due to reliance on fixed sub-policies and timings.

- Questions: why does the strong baseline in Sec. 7.3 perform poorly; does the CoM maze reward baseline account for extra experience from pre-trained policies?

- Questions: clarify the distinction between multi-policy and stochastic neural networks (SNNs).

- Clarification request on Figure 1: confirm that (1) concatenation uses a categorical value to alter first-layer biases, and (2) bilinear integration uses different weight matrices to map observation to the first hidden layer.
