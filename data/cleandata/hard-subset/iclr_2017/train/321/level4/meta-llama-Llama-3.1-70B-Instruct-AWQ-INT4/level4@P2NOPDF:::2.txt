The paper proposes a novel method for hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) using stochastic neural networks. A key contribution of the paper is the introduction of an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward for learning a diverse mixture of sub-policies. One of the notable results in the paper is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this supposedly smooth reward is insufficient to solve tasks. The authors argue that the difficulty of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction are possible explanations for this finding. However, another possible explanation is the diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training, which is absent in the baseline case. This insight may shed light on improving the baseline and proposing new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods, similar to hierarchical REPS/option-critic papers.

The paper presents a promising direction, and addressing the following points may further strengthen it:

1. Limited diversification of sub-policies: The proposed concatenation and bilinear integration methods only allow for minimal differentiation in sub-policies through the first hidden weight. While this is not a problem in the tested tasks, which require similar locomotion policies with minimal diversification, this limitation may become more apparent in other tasks where ideal sub-policies are more diverse. It would be interesting to see the application of this method to more challenging, non-locomotion domains, such as manipulation tasks, where ideal sub-policies can vary significantly depending on the initial state.

2. Limitation on hierarchical policies: The manager network is trained while keeping the sub-policies fixed, and the time steps for sub-policies are also fixed. This requires the "intrinsic" rewards and their learned sub-policies to be very effective in solving downstream tasks. It would be beneficial to see more discussions and results on handling such cases, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3. Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem domain-specific/supervised rewards: Due to the limitations mentioned above, this seems unavoidable.
