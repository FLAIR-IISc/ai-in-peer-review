- Paper introduces hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Strong empirical result: a baseline that directly combines intrinsic and sparse rewards fails where the hierarchical method succeeds.  
- Authors attribute baseline failure to long-term credit assignment and need for hierarchical abstraction.  
- Reviewer proposes an alternative explanation: the baseline lacks the explicit diversity constraint used during sub-policy training.  
- Suggestion: this insight could guide improvements to the baseline and motivate new end-to-end hierarchical policy methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS/option-critic).  
- Limitation 1: Concatenation and bilinear integrations only modify the first hidden layer (bias/weights), yielding limited sub-policy diversification that may fail on more diverse tasks.  
- Recommendation: test on more challenging, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) where sub-policies must be more distinct.  
- Limitation 2: Manager is trained with fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and fixed option durations, requiring very effective intrinsic rewards and sub-policies.  
- Recommendation: discuss and evaluate end-to-end hierarchical training or mechanisms to adapt option durations.  
- Limitation 3: Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards may appear domain-specific or effectively supervised due to the above constraints.  
- Questions for authors: Why does the strong baseline in Section 7.3 perform poorly, and is it equivalent to the "CoM maze reward"?  
- Questions for authors: Does the baseline account for extra experience from pre-trained policies?  
- Question: Please clarify the definition of “multi-policy” versus stochastic neural networks (SNNs).  
- Figure 1 clarification: Does concatenation mean the categorical variable applies different biases to the first hidden layer?  
- Figure 1 clarification: Does bilinear integration mean different weight matrices connect observations to the first hidden layer depending on the categorical value?
