I appreciate the setting presented in this paper, but I have several criticisms and questions that require clarification.

Firstly, the authors do not explicitly state the failure model of their approach. As the richness of behaviors increases in complexity, I anticipate that this method may struggle with the diversity of skills that can be discovered. It is essential to acknowledge and address potential limitations to ensure the robustness of the proposed framework.

In Section 5.3, the authors introduce a random variable X, denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated. However, it is unclear whether the space is discretized and, if so, what the rationale behind this decision is. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to discuss how the approach would be affected if the space were not discretized.

Expanding on my first point, it is crucial to assess the generality of this approach by evaluating its performance with more complicated embodiments. For instance, would the method be effective with a 5-link swimmer instead of a 2-link swimmer? This analysis would provide valuable insights into the approach's ability to generalize across various environments and tasks.

The authors claim that their framework differs from the work of Heess et al. (2016) in that it uses intrinsic rewards as the sole signal to the agent during the pre-training phase, requiring only minimal domain knowledge. However, I partially disagree with this statement. The rewards proposed in this paper appear to be hand-crafted and specific to a limited set of control tasks, which may not be entirely distinct from the approach of Heess et al. (2016). A more detailed comparison and discussion of the differences between these two methods would be beneficial to clarify the novelty and contributions of this work.
