The paper presents a method for hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) using stochastic neural networks. The authors introduce an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward for learning a diverse mixture of sub-policies. One notable result in the paper is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this supposedly smooth reward signal is insufficient to solve tasks. In addition to the authors' argument about the difficulty of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, another possible explanation for this result is the diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training, which is not present in the baseline case. This insight could potentially inform improvements to the baseline and the development of new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods, similar to hierarchical REPS or option-critic.

The paper presents a promising direction and may be strengthened further by addressing some of the following points.

1. Limited diversification of sub-policies: Both the concatenation and bilinear integration methods allow for only minimal differentiation in sub-policies through the first hidden layer's weights. While this is not a problem in the tested tasks, which require similar locomotion policies with minimal diversification, this limitation may become more apparent in other tasks where ideal sub-policies are more diverse. It would be interesting to see the method applied to more challenging, non-locomotion domains where ideal sub-policies are not similar, such as manipulation tasks where solving a task from one state can be very different from solving it from another state.

2. Limitation on hierarchical policies: The manager network is trained while the sub-policies are fixed, and the time steps for sub-policies are also fixed. This requires the "intrinsic" rewards and their learned sub-policies to be very effective in solving downstream tasks. It would be beneficial to see more discussions and results on handling such cases, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3. Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem domain-specific/supervised rewards: Due to the fixed sub-policies and time steps, this limitation appears unavoidable.
