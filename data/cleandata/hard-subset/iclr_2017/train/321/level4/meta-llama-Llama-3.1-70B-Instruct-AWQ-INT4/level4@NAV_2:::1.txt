This paper proposes a general framework for training policies for a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The authors' approach brings together the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, using a single proxy reward to guide the learning of useful skills in a pre-training environment. The skills are then leveraged for learning faster in downstream tasks by training a separate high-level policy for each task on top of the skills. The authors use Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) combined with an information-theoretic regularizer to efficiently pre-train a large span of skills.

The paper claims to contribute a novel framework that can learn a wide range of skills in a sample-efficient way and significantly boost the learning performance uniformly across a wide range of downstream tasks. The authors provide experimental results to support their claims, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach in learning a wide range of skills and achieving strong performance on a set of challenging tasks with long horizons and sparse rewards.

I decide to accept this paper with the following key reasons:

1. The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated framework that addresses the challenges of tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons.
2. The authors provide strong experimental results to support their claims, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach in learning a wide range of skills and achieving strong performance on a set of challenging tasks.

However, I have some concerns and suggestions for improvement:

1. The authors should provide more analysis and interpretation of their results, particularly in the more complex mazes where the Mutual Information regularization does not seem to help.
2. The factorization between Sagent and Srest should be clearly detailed in the paper for replicability.
3. It would be interesting to provide some analysis of the switching behavior of the agent and the effects of switching time on performance.
4. The authors should consider providing more discussion on the limitations of their approach and potential future directions for improvement.

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the relationship between the pre-training task and the downstream tasks. How do the skills learned in the pre-training task transfer to the downstream tasks?
* The authors should consider providing more details on the implementation of the SNNs and the information-theoretic regularizer.
* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the potential applications of the proposed framework in real-world scenarios.

Questions for the authors:
