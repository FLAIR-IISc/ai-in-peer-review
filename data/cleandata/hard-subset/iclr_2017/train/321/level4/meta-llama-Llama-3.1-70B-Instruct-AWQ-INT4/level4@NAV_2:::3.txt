The paper "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning" proposes a novel framework for learning a diverse set of skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and then utilizing these skills in a hierarchical architecture to solve challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The authors claim that their approach can learn a wide range of skills in a pre-training environment, which can be used to improve exploration in downstream tasks.

The authors tackle the specific question of learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment and then using these skills to improve exploration in downstream tasks. The approach is well-motivated, as it combines the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods. The authors also provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from previous work, such as Heess et al. (2016), which requires a set of goals to be specified.

The paper supports its claims through experiments on various tasks, including mazes and gathering tasks. The results show that the proposed hierarchical architecture can learn faster and achieve better performance than a baseline approach that uses a Center of Mass speed intrinsic reward in the task.

However, I have several criticisms and questions about the paper. Firstly, I would like to know more about the failure modes of this approach. As the richness of behaviors gets complex, I expect this approach to have issues with the diversity of skills that could be discovered. The authors should provide more analysis on the limitations of their approach and how it can be improved.

Secondly, I would like to know more about the discretization of the space in Section 5.3. The authors mention that the space is discretized into cells, but they do not provide more details on why this is necessary and what happens if the space is not discretized.

Thirdly, I would like to see more experiments on more complicated embodiments, such as a 5-link swimmer instead of a 2-link swimmer. This would help to assess the generality of this approach and its applicability to more complex tasks.

Lastly, I do not entirely agree with the authors' claim that their approach is more general than Heess et al. (2016) because it uses intrinsic rewards as the only signal to the agent during the pre-training phase. The rewards proposed in this paper are also quite hand-crafted and specific to a seemingly limited set of control tasks. The authors should provide more discussion on the limitations of their approach and how it can be improved.

Overall, I think that this paper provides a novel and interesting approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning. However, I believe that the authors need to provide more analysis and experiments to fully demonstrate the effectiveness and generality of their approach.

I accept this paper, but I would like the authors to address the above criticisms and questions in their final version.
