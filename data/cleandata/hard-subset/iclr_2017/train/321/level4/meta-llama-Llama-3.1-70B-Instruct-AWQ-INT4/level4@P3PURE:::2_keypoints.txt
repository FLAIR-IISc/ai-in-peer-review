- Edited score from 6 to 7; paper proposes hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to learn diverse sub-policies.  
- Strong empirical result: combining intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards (baseline) fails on tasks, suggesting smooth intrinsic reward alone is insufficient.  
- Alternative explanation: advantage may stem from explicit diversity requirement in sub-policy training (absent in baseline), not only from hierarchical abstraction or credit assignment benefits.  
- Suggestion: insights could improve baselines and inspire end-to-end hierarchical policy methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS / option-critic).  
- Visualizations are well-executed and informative.  
- Limitation 1 — limited sub-policy diversification: concatenation and bilinear integration only modulate the first hidden layer, which may suffice for similar locomotion policies but likely fails for domains needing highly diverse sub-policies (e.g., manipulation).  
- Recommendation: evaluate on more challenging, non-locomotion tasks to test diversification limits.  
- Limitation 2 — constrained hierarchy training: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time scales, placing heavy burden on learned intrinsic rewards and sub-policies for downstream success.  
- Recommendation: discuss and experiment with end-to-end hierarchical training or adaptive time scales to handle cases where fixed sub-policies/time steps are insufficient.  
- Limitation 3 — intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific due to reliance on fixed sub-policies and time steps, reducing generality.  
- Question: want intuition for poor performance of the strong baseline in Section 7.3 — why it fails to solve the task.  
- Question: does the baseline (“CoM maze reward”) account for extra experience/pretraining that sub-policies receive?  
- Question: request clarification of “multi-policy” concept vs. stochastic neural networks (SNNs).  
- Clarification request on Figure 1: (1) does concatenation imply different biases to the first hidden layer per categorical option? (2) does bilinear integration imply different weight matrices from observations to first hidden layer per option?
