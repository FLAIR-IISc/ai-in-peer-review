This paper presents a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, leveraging stochastic neural networks to learn a diverse set of skills that can be reused across multiple tasks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a range of challenging tasks with sparse rewards.

I accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the authors propose a well-motivated and well-placed approach in the literature, addressing the challenge of learning skills in tasks with sparse rewards. Secondly, the paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed framework, demonstrating its ability to learn a wide range of skills and improve performance on downstream tasks.

The authors' use of stochastic neural networks as policies is a key contribution of the paper, allowing for the representation of complex action distributions and the learning of multiple skills simultaneously. The introduction of an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage diversity of behaviors is also a notable aspect of the paper. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework, showing improved performance on a range of tasks compared to a baseline approach.

One potential criticism of the paper is that the rewards used in the pre-training phase may be considered hand-crafted and specific to a limited set of control tasks. However, the authors argue that their approach requires minimal domain knowledge and can be applied to a wide range of tasks. Further evaluation on more diverse tasks would be beneficial to fully assess the generality of the approach.

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the failure modes of the approach. As the richness of behaviors gets complex, it is likely that the approach will encounter issues with the diversity of skills that can be discovered.
* The discretization of the state space in Section 5.3 could be clarified. Is the space discretized, and if so, why? What happens if it isn't?
* The authors claim that their approach is more general than that of Heess et al. (2016), but this claim could be more thoroughly evaluated. A more detailed comparison of the two approaches would be beneficial.
* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the potential applications of the proposed framework. How can it be used in real-world scenarios, and what are the potential benefits and limitations?

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the failure modes of the approach? How do you plan to address these issues in future work?
* Can you clarify the discretization of the state space in Section 5.3? Is the space discretized, and if so, why? What happens if it isn't?
* How do you plan to extend the approach to more complex embodiments, such as a 5-link swimmer?
* Can you provide more details on the construction of the rewards used in the pre-training phase? How do you ensure that these rewards are not too hand-crafted or specific to a limited set of control tasks?
