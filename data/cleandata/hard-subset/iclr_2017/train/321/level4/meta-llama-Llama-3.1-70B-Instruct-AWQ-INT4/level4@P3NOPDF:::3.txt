I appreciate the setting presented in this paper, but I have several criticisms and questions that need to be addressed.

Firstly, the failure model of this work is not clearly defined. As the richness of behaviors becomes increasingly complex, I expect this approach to encounter issues with the diversity of skills that can be discovered. It is essential to provide a more in-depth analysis of the limitations of the proposed method and how it handles complex scenarios.

Furthermore, in Section 5.3, the authors introduce a random variable X denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated. However, it is unclear whether the space is discretized and, if so, why this choice was made. Additionally, the authors should discuss what implications this has on the results and whether the approach can be applied to continuous spaces.

Expanding on my first point, it is crucial to assess the generality of this approach by testing it on more complicated embodiments. For instance, how would the method perform with a 5-link swimmer instead of a 2-link one? This would provide valuable insights into the robustness and applicability of the proposed framework.

I also take issue with the authors' claim that their approach differs significantly from the work of Heess et al. (2016). While it is true that the pre-training setup in Heess et al. requires a set of specified goals, the intrinsic rewards proposed in this paper are also hand-crafted and specific to a limited set of control tasks. This raises concerns about the generality and scalability of the proposed method, as it may not be easily adaptable to more complex tasks or domains.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting setting, it requires more thorough analysis and clarification on several key points. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach.
