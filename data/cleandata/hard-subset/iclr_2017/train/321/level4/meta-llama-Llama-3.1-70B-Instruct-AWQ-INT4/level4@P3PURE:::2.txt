The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (RL) approach using stochastic neural networks. It introduces an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an additional reward to learn a diverse mixture of sub-policies. One notable result is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards, demonstrating that this supposedly smooth reward cannot solve tasks. In addition to the authors' argument about the difficulty of long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, another possible explanation for this might be the diversity requirement imposed in sub-policy training, which is absent in the baseline case. This insight could potentially improve the baseline and inform new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods, similar to hierarchical REPS/option-critic papers. The visualizations are also well-done.

The paper presents a promising direction, and it may be strengthened further by addressing some of the following points.

1) Limited diversification of sub-policies: The proposed concatenation and bilinear integration methods allow only minimal differentiation in sub-policies through the first hidden weight, which may not be a significant issue in the tested tasks due to their similar locomotion policies. However, this limitation could become more apparent in other tasks that require more diverse ideal sub-policies, such as manipulation tasks where solving a task from one state can be significantly different from solving it from another state. It would be interesting to see the approach applied to more challenging, non-locomotion domains.

2) Limitation on hierarchical policies: The manager network is trained while keeping the sub-policies fixed, and the time steps for sub-policies are also fixed. This requires the "intrinsic" rewards and their learned sub-policies to be very effective in solving downstream tasks. It would be beneficial to see more discussions and results on handling such cases, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3) Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards seem domain-specific/supervised rewards: Due to the fixed sub-policies and time steps, this limitation appears unavoidable.
