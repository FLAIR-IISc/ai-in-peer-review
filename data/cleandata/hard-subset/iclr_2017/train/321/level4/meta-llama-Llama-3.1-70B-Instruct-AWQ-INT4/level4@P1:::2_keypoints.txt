- Paper proposes hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to learn diverse sub-policies.
- Strong baseline comparison shows directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards fails to solve tasks, supporting need for hierarchy.
- Possible alternative explanation: enforced diversity in sub-policy training (absent in baseline) may explain baseline failure.
- Suggestion to explore insights for improving baselines or developing end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).
- Visualizations in the paper are informative and helpful.
- Concern 1: Concatenation and bilinear integration only allow minimal sub-policy differentiation via first-layer weights, which may fail on tasks needing diverse policies.
- Concern 2: Hierarchy limitation — manager is trained with fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time steps, requiring very good intrinsic rewards/sub-policies.
- Concern 3: Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific or effectively supervised because of reliance on fixed sub-policies.
- Question: Why does the strong baseline (CoM maze reward) perform so poorly; what is the intuition behind its failure?
- Question: Does the baseline leverage the extra experience that pre-trained policies have received?
- Question: Clarify "multi-policy" vs SNN — what distinguishes them in practice?
- Clarification: Confirm interpretation of integration methods — concatenation changes first-layer bias per option; bilinear changes observation-to-first-layer weight matrix per option.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason: Novel approach with significant contribution, promising results, and clear comparisons to a strong baseline.
- Supporting: Introduces a new information-theoretic diversity objective; demonstrates effectiveness across tasks; includes clear explanations and visualizations.
- Additional feedback: Discuss method limitations and improvements (diversification, end-to-end learning), provide implementation/hyperparameter details for reproducibility, and evaluate on harder non-locomotion domains.
