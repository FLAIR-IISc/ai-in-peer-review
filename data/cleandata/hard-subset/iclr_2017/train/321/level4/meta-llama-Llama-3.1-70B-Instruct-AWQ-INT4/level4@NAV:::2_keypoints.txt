- Proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks with an information-theoretic auxiliary reward to encourage identifiable, diverse sub-policies.  
- Compares to a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic and sparse rewards, showing that such a smooth reward can fail on tasks.  
- Argues that hierarchical abstraction helps long-term credit assignment; suggests baseline failure may stem from lack of diversity constraints on sub-policy training.  
- Visualizations in the paper are clear and helpful.  
- Concern: sub-policy diversification seems limited because concatenation and bilinear integration only alter the first hidden layer minimally; this may break down on tasks requiring more diverse sub-policies (e.g., manipulation).  
- Suggest applying the method to more challenging, non-locomotion domains to test diversification limits.  
- Limitation: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed time steps, putting high burden on intrinsic rewards and sub-policies to solve downstream tasks.  
- Recommend discussing/experimenting with approaches for end-to-end hierarchical policy learning (e.g., hierarchical REPS/option-critic) to relax fixed-subpolicy constraints.  
- Observation: intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific, potentially unavoidable given fixed sub-policies/time steps; needs discussion.  
- Question: why does the strong baseline (CoM maze reward) perform poorly — does it account for the extra experience from pre-trained policies?  
- Question: clarify the distinction between “multi-policy” and stochastic neural networks (SNNs).  
- Question: confirm figure 1 interpretations — (1) concatenation yields different biases for the first hidden layer per category, and (2) bilinear integration yields different input weight matrices per category?
