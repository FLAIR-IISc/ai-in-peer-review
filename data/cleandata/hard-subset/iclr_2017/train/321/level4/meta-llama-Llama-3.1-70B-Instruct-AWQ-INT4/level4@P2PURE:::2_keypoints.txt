- Reviewer's score increased from 6 to 7.

- Paper proposes hierarchical RL using stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward to learn diverse sub-policies.

- Key empirical finding: a strong baseline combining intrinsic and sparse rewards fails to solve tasks; authors attribute this to long-term credit assignment and benefits of hierarchical abstraction.

- Alternate explanation: baseline lacks explicit diversity requirement during sub-policy training, which may explain its failure and suggests ways to improve baselines or develop end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS/option-critic).

- Visualizations are clear and well-presented.

- Limitation 1: Concatenation and bilinear integration only differentiate sub-policies via the first hidden layer bias/weights, enabling minimal diversification—sufficient for locomotion but likely inadequate for more diverse tasks (e.g., manipulation).

- Recommendation: evaluate method on more challenging, non-locomotion domains to test diversification limits.

- Limitation 2: Manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time scales, forcing intrinsic rewards and pre-trained sub-policies to be highly effective; needs discussion and results for end-to-end hierarchical training or adaptive time scales.

- Limitation 3: Because sub-policies and time steps are fixed, the intrinsic/unsupervised rewards may effectively be domain-specific or supervised, limiting generality.

- Questions for clarification: why does the strong baseline perform poorly in Section 7.3 and fail to solve the task?

- Clarification requests: is the baseline in 7.3 the "CoM maze reward," and does the baseline account for extra experience from pre-trained policies?

- Request to clarify the distinction between multi-policy and stochastic neural networks (SNN).

- Figure 1 clarification request: confirm that (1) concatenation implies different biases for the first hidden layer based on categorical inputs, and (2) bilinear integration implies different weight matrices connecting observations to the first hidden layer.
