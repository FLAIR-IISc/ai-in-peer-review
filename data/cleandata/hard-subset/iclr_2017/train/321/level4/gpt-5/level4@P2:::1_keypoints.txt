- Paper proposes pretraining diverse low-level skills via a stochastic neural network (SNN) with discrete latents, then training a high-level manager to sequence skills for sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks.  
- Skills use a mutual information (MI) bonus to encourage diversity and a bilinear state-latent integration to improve multimodality over concatenation.  
- Learned low-level skills are frozen; the manager selects a skill every T steps to enable temporally coherent exploration.  
- Experiments on Locomotion+Maze and Gather show interpretable, diverse skills and improved exploration/solving of tasks where baselines fail.  
- MI regularization and bilinear integration often increase skill diversity and sometimes improve downstream performance.  
- The approach is simple and sample-efficient relative to training K separate policies; code is available.  
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).  
- Strong empirical results: solves challenging sparse-reward benchmarks with clear ablations showing value of SNNs and bilinear integration.  
- Well-motivated, broadly applicable framework combining unsupervised skill discovery with hierarchical control and minimal downstream instrumentation.  
- SNNs naturally model multimodal actions; discrete latents plus bilinear integration effectively partition behavior space.  
- MI bonus reduces H(Z|C), encouraging distinguishable and interpretable skills.  
- Visitation plots and learning curves substantiate diverse gait primitives and improved exploration; hierarchical control yields consistent gains.  
- Pretraining one SNN for K skills is more sample-efficient than training K separate policies.  
- Relation to prior work: similar spirit to Heess et al. (2016) but uses a proxy reward; differs from Option-Critic and intrinsic-motivation baselines by focusing on reusable temporal primitives.  
- Concern: “intrinsic motivation” term is overloaded—proxy rewards embed domain priors that may align closely with downstream tasks.  
- MI helps diversity but does not consistently improve performance on complex mazes (1–3); limited analysis on when MI helps or hurts.  
- Structural assumption S = Sagent × Srest underpins reuse but Srest is not specified precisely enough for replication.  
- Limited analysis of switching behavior; need more insight into choice of T, failure modes, and stability (especially for unstable agents like Ant).  
- Suggested terminology change: use “proxy reward” or “unsupervised pre-training reward” instead of “intrinsic motivation.”  
- Requests for clarity/replicability: precisely define Sagent/Srest per task, inputs to manager and low-level policies, MI discretization details, and sensitivity analyses for K, α_MI, and T.  
- Ask for deeper MI analysis: explain when MI aids tasks, quantify skill diversity (MI estimates, visitation overlap, trajectory divergence), and relate diversity to returns.  
- Recommend switching/stability ablations: vary T, test termination policies, examine interaction with agent stability and potential mitigations.  
- Suggest adaptation experiments: report preliminary end-to-end fine-tuning (Gumbel-Softmax/Concrete) to see if low-level adaptation helps in challenging mazes.  
- Scalability/architecture notes: discuss bilinear cost with high-dim observations, learned discriminators for p(Z|C) instead of count grids, and trade-offs with continuous latents.  
- Specific questions to authors on MI effect, precise state factorization, sensitivity to hyperparameters, what skills transfer, switching/termination, p(Z|C) estimation robustness, and handling unstable agents.  
- Overall judgment: well-motivated and empirically strong paper likely to be influential; addressing analysis and clarity points would strengthen the contribution.
