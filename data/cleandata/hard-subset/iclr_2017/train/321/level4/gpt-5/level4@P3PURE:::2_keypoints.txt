- Proposes hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability intrinsic reward to learn diverse sub-policies; strong baseline comparison shows simple reward mixing fails.

- Convincing empirical result: mixing intrinsic and sparse rewards (strong baseline) does not solve tasks, highlighting value of hierarchical structure and explicit diversity.

- Visualizations are clear and effectively illustrate learned behaviors.

- Strength: clear motivation for using option identifiability as an intrinsic reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.

- Strength: strong empirical comparisons demonstrating that explicit diversity and hierarchy matter beyond reward smoothing.

- Strength: effective visualizations that illuminate results.

- Concern: current conditioning (concatenation or bilinear) mainly influences the first hidden layer, limiting sub-policy diversification capacity.

- Concern: limited expressivity may be fine for locomotion but could be a bottleneck for heterogeneous tasks (e.g., manipulation); evaluation on harder non-locomotion domains is suggested.

- Concern: hierarchical setup trains the manager with fixed sub-policies and fixed durations, forcing intrinsic rewards and sub-policies to transfer directly to downstream tasks.

- Concern: recommend discussing or experimenting with relaxing fixed sub-policy/duration constraints and connecting to end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic).

- Concern: intrinsic/unsupervised rewards may become domain-specific under the current protocol, reducing generality and robustness to task/domain shifts; sensitivity analysis is needed.

- Question: Baseline in Sec. 7.3—why does it fail; does “CoM maze reward” refer to this baseline; is data/training time matched to pre-trained sub-policies; could lack of explicit diversity explain failure more than credit-assignment issues?

- Question: Clarify what “multi-policy” means and how it differs from the proposed SNN approach.

- Question: Figure 1 clarifications—does concatenation act as a bias into the first hidden layer, and does bilinear integration correspond to different input-to-hidden weight matrices per categorical variable?
