This paper proposes a hierarchical RL method using stochastic neural networks, introducing an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an intrinsic reward to encourage a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A particularly compelling result is the comparison with a strong baseline that simply adds intrinsic rewards to sparse task rewards; despite the smoother reward signal, this baseline fails to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ explanation—credit assignment over long horizons and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction—another plausible factor is the explicit diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training in the proposed method, which appears absent in the baseline. This difference might explain the performance gap. It could also provide insight into strengthening the baseline or designing new end-to-end hierarchical approaches, as in hierarchical REPS or option-critic. The visualizations are clear and helpful.

The paper points to a promising direction. It could be further strengthened by addressing the following:

1) Limited diversification of sub-policies: With both concatenation and bilinear integration, sub-policies differ primarily through the first hidden layer (via input-dependent bias or weights). This is not problematic in the tested locomotion tasks, which largely require similar movement primitives with minor variations. However, the limitation may become more pronounced in domains where ideal sub-policies must be more distinct. It would be valuable to evaluate on harder, non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation), where solving the task from different states can demand qualitatively different sub-policies.

2) Limitations of the hierarchical policy: The manager (high-level) network is trained while sub-policies are fixed, and the option duration is also fixed. This setup requires the intrinsic objectives and the learned sub-policies to already be well aligned with downstream tasks. More discussion and results on handling cases where this alignment is imperfect would be helpful, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning (e.g., joint training of manager and sub-policies, or adaptive option durations).

3) Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific: As a consequence of (2), the intrinsic rewards that yield useful sub-policies seem partially domain-specific (or at least require designer choices that act like supervision). Please comment on the degree of domain knowledge required and how general these objectives are.

Last-minute questions:
- What is the intuition for why the strong baseline in Section 7.3 performs so poorly and fails to solve the task? I am assuming “CoM maze reward” is this baseline.
- Does the baseline account for the extra experience accumulated by the pre-trained policies (i.e., does it receive comparable data or pretraining)?
- What is “multi-policy,” and how does it differ from SNN in your setup?
- Regarding Figure 1, is my understanding correct that:
  (1) For concatenation, the categorical variable effectively induces different biases to the first hidden layer; and
  (2) For bilinear integration, it induces different weight matrices connecting the observation to the first hidden layer?

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a clear idea and strong empirical evidence against a natural baseline. Addressing the above points—especially broader domains, end-to-end training considerations, and clarity on the baselines—would further strengthen the contribution.
