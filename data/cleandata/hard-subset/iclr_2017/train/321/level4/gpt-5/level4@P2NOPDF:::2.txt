This paper proposes a hierarchical RL framework built on stochastic neural networks, using an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an intrinsic reward to learn a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A particularly compelling result is the comparison to a strong baseline that simply adds intrinsic to sparse task rewards; despite smoothing the reward signal, that baseline fails to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ arguments about long-horizon credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, one plausible contributing factor is the explicit diversity pressure during sub-policy training, which appears absent in the baseline. If so, this insight could guide stronger baselines and inspire new end-to-end hierarchical methods, akin to hierarchical REPS or option-critic. The visualizations are also clear and informative.

The approach is promising. The paper could be strengthened by addressing the following:

1) Limited sub-policy diversification. With both concatenation and bilinear integration, sub-policies differ primarily through the first hidden layer’s parameters. This is sufficient for the evaluated locomotion tasks, which mostly require similar gaits with minor variations, but it may be restrictive in domains where the optimal sub-policies must be more distinct. It would be valuable to test on harder, non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation) where the behavior needed from different initial states can vary substantially.

2) Constraints in the hierarchical policy. The manager is trained while sub-policies are fixed, and the sub-policy duration is also fixed. This places a heavy burden on the intrinsic objectives and learned sub-policies to be broadly useful for downstream tasks. More discussion and/or experiments on relaxing these constraints—e.g., adaptive durations, joint end-to-end training, or limited fine-tuning of sub-policies—would help, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3) Domain specificity of intrinsic rewards. Given (2), the intrinsic objectives may need to be tailored to the target domain, edging toward supervision. Clarifying how general these objectives are, and how sensitive performance is to their design, would be useful.

Questions for the authors:
- What is the intuition for why the strong baseline in Section 7.3 performs so poorly? To confirm, is this the “CoM maze reward” baseline? Does the baseline benefit from the additional experience used to pretrain the sub-policies in your method, or is it trained from scratch with the shaped reward?
- What exactly is “multi-policy,” and how does it differ from the proposed SNN approach (beyond the stochastic latent gating)?
- Regarding Figure 1, please confirm my understanding: (1) With concatenation, the categorical latent effectively induces different biases (or input offsets) to the first hidden layer; (2) With bilinear integration, the latent selects different weight matrices connecting observations to the first hidden layer.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that demonstrates clear empirical advantages of encouraging identifiable, diverse sub-policies and a manager that composes them. Addressing the diversification limits, the rigidity of the hierarchy (fixed sub-policies and durations), and the potential domain specificity of intrinsic rewards would further strengthen the paper and broaden its applicability.
