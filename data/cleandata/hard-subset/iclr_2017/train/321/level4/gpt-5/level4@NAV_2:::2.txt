1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first pretrains a repertoire of skills using a single proxy reward in a minimal environment and then composes these skills via a high-level manager to tackle sparse, long-horizon downstream tasks. The key technical ingredients are: (i) Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) that induce multi-modal low-level policies via a categorical latent code, enabling weight sharing and sample-efficient learning of multiple skills; and (ii) an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position), encouraging diverse and interpretable skills. The method demonstrates that training a manager over these frozen skills substantially improves exploration and solves tasks where combining the proxy intrinsic signal directly with sparse rewards fails. The visualizations are clear and compelling, and the comparisons against a strong “intrinsic + sparse” baseline are particularly informative.

2. Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (1) The MI-regularized SNN approach to learning diverse, reusable low-level skills is well-motivated, clearly presented, and empirically effective; (2) Strong evidence that hierarchical composition of these skills meaningfully improves exploration and learning on sparse-reward benchmarks, outperforming a carefully constructed intrinsic-reward baseline.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse rewards and long horizons make naïve exploration ineffective. Pretraining reusable skills with minimal domain knowledge and composing them hierarchically directly addresses this.
- Motivation and placement in literature: The work neatly connects intrinsic motivation, hierarchical RL, and multi-modal policy representations. It is well positioned relative to HiREPS, option-critic, and recent skill-pretraining efforts, and it articulates why a single proxy reward with MI regularization can yield transferable skills without task-specific goals.
- Empirical support and rigor: Ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear integration; varying MI strength) convincingly show that MI regularization and richer integration help produce more diverse, identifiable skills. Hierarchical control improves exploration coverage and achieves superior performance on mazes and gather tasks compared to both naïve exploration and a strong intrinsic-reward baseline. The visualization of visitation plots and learning curves bolster the claims.
- Insight about the strong baseline: Beyond long-term credit assignment, a plausible contributing factor to the baseline’s weakness is the lack of an explicit diversity pressure on low-level behaviors; the MI regularizer in the proposed pretraining appears to supply precisely this missing ingredient.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not strictly part of the accept decision)
- Diversity limits in the current SNN integrations: Both concatenation and the presented bilinear integration primarily modulate the first hidden layer. This worked well for locomotion, where skills differ by gait/direction, but may be constraining in domains where sub-policies need more substantial specialization (e.g., manipulation with qualitatively distinct strategies from different states). Exploring deeper or more expressive conditioning (e.g., FiLM-like conditioning across layers or hypernetworks) could broaden skill diversity.
- Fixed skills and fixed commitment time: Freezing low-level policies and using a fixed switching period T simplifies training but can be limiting when proxy-trained skills are imperfect for a downstream task or when variable temporal abstraction is needed. It would strengthen the paper to include discussion and possibly preliminary results on end-to-end fine-tuning (e.g., straight-through estimators) and/or learning termination (option-style) to adapt T online.
- On “intrinsic” reward design: Although minimal, the proxy reward remains domain-shaped (e.g., CoM speed for locomotion). A brief discussion on designing more task-agnostic proxies and how robust performance is to proxy misspecification would clarify generality.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline clarity in Section 7.3: What explains the strong baseline’s poor performance most convincingly in your view—credit assignment alone, or also the absence of an explicit diversity constraint on low-level behaviors? Did the baseline benefit from the same amount of environment interaction as the pretraining stage (i.e., any reuse of pretraining experience), or was it trained entirely from scratch on the downstream task?
- Multi-policy vs. SNN: Could you precisely define “multi-policy” in your experiments relative to SNN-based skills (e.g., number of samples used, parameter sharing, and switching mechanism), and summarize their empirical trade-offs?
- Figure 1 interpretation: Please confirm my reading: (1) concatenation produces code-dependent biases in the first hidden layer, whereas (2) bilinear integration produces code-dependent weights between observation and first hidden layer.
- Path toward end-to-end: Given your results, do you expect adding end-to-end adaptation (e.g., as in hierarchical REPS/option-critic with straight-through gradients) to primarily improve sample efficiency, final performance, or robustness across domains? Any pitfalls you anticipate?
