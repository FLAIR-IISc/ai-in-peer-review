This paper proposes a hierarchical RL approach using stochastic neural networks. It introduces an information-theoretic metric of option identifiability as an auxiliary reward to encourage a diverse set of sub-policies. A particularly compelling result is the comparison with a strong baseline that simply combines intrinsic and sparse rewards; despite the ostensibly smoother signal, that baseline fails to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ arguments about long-horizon credit assignment and benefits of hierarchical abstraction, another plausible factor is the enforced diversity during sub-policy training, which the baseline lacks. It would be interesting if this insight could help strengthen the baseline and motivate new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning methods in the spirit of hierarchical REPS/option-critic. The visualizations are clear and helpful.

The paper points to a promising direction and could be further strengthened by addressing the following:

1) Limited sub-policy diversification: Both concatenation and bilinear integration only permit modest differentiation across sub-policies via the first hidden layer’s parameters. This is not problematic for the evaluated locomotion tasks, which largely need similar locomotion behaviors with minor variations, but may become limiting in domains where sub-policies should be more distinct. It would be valuable to see results on more challenging, non-locomotion settings (e.g., manipulation), where solving the task from different states can require substantially different behaviors.

2) Constraints on the hierarchical policy: The manager is trained with sub-policies frozen, and sub-policy durations are fixed. This places a heavy burden on the “intrinsic” rewards and the learned sub-policies to already be well-suited for downstream tasks. More discussion or results on handling such cases would be useful, ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical training.

3) Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific or supervised: Given (2), this seems hard to avoid.
