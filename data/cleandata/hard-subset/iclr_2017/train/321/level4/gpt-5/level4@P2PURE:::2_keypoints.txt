- Proposes hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic identifiability auxiliary reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.
- Key result: a strong baseline that combines intrinsic and sparse rewards still fails, underscoring the benefit of the hierarchical approach.
- The explicit diversity requirement during sub-policy training may explain the advantage over the baseline and suggests directions for improving baselines or for end-to-end hierarchical methods.
- Visualizations are compelling.
- Concern: Concatenation and bilinear integration minimally differentiate sub-policies, which may be inadequate for domains requiring substantially different sub-policies; more challenging (non-locomotion) tests are recommended.
- Concern: Manager is trained with fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and fixed execution durations, placing heavy burden on intrinsic rewards and learned sub-policy quality; more discussion/results on fully end-to-end training needed.
- Concern: The intrinsic/unsupervised rewards may be domain-specific or effectively supervised given the fixed-subpolicy setup.
- Questions: Why does the strong baseline (is this the “CoM maze reward”?) perform so poorly and fail to solve the task?
- Questions: Does the baseline account for the additional experience accumulated by the pre-trained policies?
- Questions: What exactly is “multi-policy” and how does it differ from SNN?
- Questions: Clarify Fig.1: does concatenation induce a different bias to the first hidden layer and bilinear integration induce a different input weight matrix?
