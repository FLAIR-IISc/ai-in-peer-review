- Paper proposes hierarchical RL: pretrain diverse temporally-extended skills via an SNN with categorical latents and MI regularizer, then reuse them with a high-level manager for sparse-reward tasks.  
- Low-level policy uses a Stochastic Neural Network with categorical latent variables and bilinear latent-observation integration to enable multi-modal skills and weight sharing.  
- A mutual-information-based regularizer encourages skill diversity and interpretability; latent treated as part of the observation so TRPO can be applied.  
- Empirical results show broad locomotion skills, improved exploration, and faster learning on challenging sparse-reward mazes and Gather tasks, outperforming baselines and independent-policy training.  
- Decision: Accept — strong empirical gains on hard sparse-reward benchmarks and a well-motivated, clean integration of SNNs, MI regularization, and hierarchical control.  
- Motivation: Targets poor exploration in long-horizon sparse-reward tasks by pretraining task-agnostic, temporally abstract skills, aligning with HRL and intrinsic objective literature.  
- Relation to prior work: Closely related to recent HRL but differs by using a single proxy reward without goal specs and MI to encourage diversity; bilinear integration is an effective architectural choice.  
- Technical soundness: Simple, reproducible MI bonus via discretized center-of-mass counts; ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, MI coeffs) and visitation analyses support claims.  
- Empirical support: Solves difficult exploration tasks with multi-seed reporting and multiple pre-trained SNNs, avoiding cherry-picking and demonstrating meaningful skill composition by the manager.  
- Limitation: MI bonus improves diversity but not always returns on hardest mazes; deeper analysis on when/why MI helps is needed.  
- Terminology feedback: Clarify use of “intrinsic motivation” — pretraining reward is more a task-agnostic proxy than classic intrinsic signals like novelty or prediction error.  
- MI regularization feedback: Quantify when MI improves downstream learning (e.g., MI estimates, coverage vs returns) and explain cases with limited gains despite better diversity.  
- State factorization feedback: Explicitly list Srest components per environment for reproducibility and clarify any coupling with Sagent.  
- Switching behavior feedback: Provide diagnostics on switch outcomes, failure modes, sensitivity to switch time T, and consider learned termination or sensitivity analysis.  
- Sample accounting feedback: Clarify sample complexity across baselines and provide apples-to-apples fixed-budget comparisons, since multi-policy baselines use more pretraining samples.  
- Posterior estimation feedback: Justify the CoM-only posterior approximation and discuss alternatives (learned density models) for higher-dimensional latents.  
- Question: Do you consider the pretraining reward “intrinsic” or a task-agnostic proxy, and how sensitive are results to the proxy choice (speed vs energy vs aligned progress)?  
- Question: Why does MI increase skill diversity but not consistently improve maze returns — is manager reorientation via walls making extra skills unnecessary; any skill-usage stats?  
- Question: Please enumerate Srest for each task variant; are there cross-couplings between Sagent and Srest affecting performance?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to switch time T; are there specific failure modes at switches and would learned termination help?  
- Question: How robust are results to number of skills K and bilinear vs concatenation; any guidance for choosing K?  
- Question: Does the manager always observe both Sagent and Srest; were managers with only Srest tested to enforce role separation?  
- Question: Have you tried parametric p(Z|C) estimation instead of grid counts; does it affect diversity or stability?  
- Question: Do skills pretrained on one proxy (e.g., speed) transfer to qualitatively different downstream objectives (e.g., precise tracking) without retraining the low-level SNN?
