- Proposed method: pretrain a repertoire of skills in a minimal environment, then compose frozen skills with a high-level manager to solve sparse, long-horizon tasks.  
- Key technical ingredients: Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for multimodal low-level policies and an MI regularizer to encourage diverse, interpretable skills.  
- Empirical claim: Training a manager over frozen, MI-regularized skills substantially improves exploration and solves tasks where combining intrinsic rewards with sparse rewards fails.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons for acceptance: MI-regularized SNNs are well-motivated and effective; hierarchical composition meaningfully improves exploration and outperforms a strong intrinsic-reward baseline.  
- Problem significance: Addresses ineffective naive exploration under sparse rewards and long horizons by pretraining reusable, minimal-prior skills.  
- Positioning: Nicely connects intrinsic motivation, hierarchical RL, and multi-modal policy representations; compared appropriately to HiREPS, option-critic, and recent skill-pretraining work.  
- Empirical rigor: Ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, MI strength) and visualizations support claims that MI and richer integration yield diverse, identifiable skills and better downstream performance.  
- Insight on baseline weakness: Beyond credit assignment, baseline likely lacks an explicit diversity pressure on low-level behaviors, which the MI regularizer provides.  
- Feedback — SNN conditioning depth: Current concatenation/bilinear mainly modulate the first hidden layer; deeper or more expressive conditioning (FiLM, hypernetworks) might increase diversity for complex domains.  
- Feedback — fixed skills and commitment time: Freezing low-level policies and using fixed switching period T simplifies training but can limit adaptability; end-to-end fine-tuning or learned termination could help.  
- Feedback — proxy reward design: Proxy rewards are domain-shaped; discuss task-agnostic proxy design and robustness to proxy misspecification.  
- Question — baseline clarity: Was the strong intrinsic baseline trained from scratch on downstream tasks or given pretraining experience, and is its failure due to credit assignment or lack of diversity?  
- Question — multi-policy vs SNN: Define “multi-policy” experimentally (samples, parameter sharing, switching) and summarize empirical trade-offs with SNN-based skills.  
- Question — Figure 1 interpretation: Confirm that concatenation yields code-dependent biases in the first hidden layer, while bilinear integration yields code-dependent observation-to-first-layer weights.  
- Question — path to end-to-end: Would adding end-to-end adaptation chiefly improve sample efficiency, final performance, or robustness, and what pitfalls do authors anticipate?
