Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a clear and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that tackles sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks by (i) pretraining a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy with categorical latent variables to learn a diverse set of reusable skills under a minimal proxy reward, and (ii) training a high-level “manager” to sequence these skills for downstream tasks. Diversity in the learned skills is encouraged with an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between the latent code and a task-relevant subset of the state (e.g., the robot’s CoM position), yielding interpretable behaviors. The manager operates at a slower time scale by selecting a skill and committing to it for T steps, substantially enhancing exploration.

The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, connecting intrinsic motivation, options, and multi-modal policies. The use of SNNs with bilinear integration provides shared representations across skills while retaining multi-modality and sample efficiency. The MI bonus is simple, computationally light, and empirically effective at preventing mode collapse. Experiments convincingly show: (a) pretraining yields a wide span of skills; (b) hierarchical control significantly expands exploration coverage; and (c) on sparse mazes and a long-horizon gather task, the method outperforms strong baselines, including intrinsic-motivation methods and a “proxy reward in-task” baseline. Visualizations and ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, with/without MI, different seeds, varying switch times) support the claims.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated method that combines SNN-based skill learning with an MI regularizer to produce diverse, reusable skills, leading to markedly improved exploration and performance on sparse tasks.
- Thorough empirical evaluation with strong baselines and informative ablations; the framework is simple to implement, sample-efficient, and produces interpretable skills.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse-reward, long-horizon settings are a central challenge in RL. The framework reduces effective horizon via temporally extended skills and improves exploration without task-specific reward shaping.
- Novelty/positioning: Uses SNNs for multi-modal policies with bilinear integration and an MI bonus for diversity, differing from goal-conditioned pretraining and option-learning methods that need external rewards, demonstrations, or handcrafted subgoals.
- Soundness: Treating the latent as part of the observation enables TRPO without altering the optimizer. The MI estimator is practical via discretized state counts. Weight sharing makes learning K skills far more sample-efficient than training K independent policies.
- Empirics: The method learns distinct locomotion behaviors tied to latent codes; random managers over pretrained skills cover significantly more of the state space than Gaussian action noise; hierarchical control solves sparse mazes and gather tasks where standard methods fail. Bilinear integration and MI consistently improve skill diversity and downstream performance (especially in gather), with variance analyses across seeds.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Broaden domains: Current tasks are all locomotion-centric with similar gaits; demonstrate applicability to more diverse settings (e.g., manipulation or tasks where optimal skills differ qualitatively across states).
- Adaptive hierarchy: Explore end-to-end training of the manager and low-level skills (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax/Concrete), and learning a termination policy rather than fixing T. This may address cases where pretrained skills are insufficient or switching needs are state-dependent.
- Proxy reward design: While minimal, the CoM-speed proxy is domain-specific. Discuss general strategies for constructing proxy rewards across domains, and assess robustness when the proxy is imperfectly aligned with downstream needs.
- Unstable agents: Switching issues for Ant are important in practice. Consider pretraining with scheduled switching, learning transition policies, or regularizing skills to be robust to off-manifold states at switch times.
- Comparisons: Include more direct comparisons to option-critic and HiREPS (both in methodology discussion and empirical baselines, if feasible), and clarify differences from goal-conditioned or UVFA-style pretraining.
- Diversity metrics: Report quantitative diversity measures (e.g., MI estimates over training, pairwise trajectory divergence) alongside visitation plots.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline design in Section 7.3: What precisely is the “CoM + task reward” baseline? Is it trained from scratch on the downstream task, or does it benefit from any pretraining experience? Are sample budgets matched to the hierarchical method?
- Multi-policy vs SNN: Please clarify the “multi-policy” baseline: are these K independent feed-forward policies trained with the same proxy reward, then managed by the same high-level controller? Are total pretraining samples K× larger than for SNN pretraining?
- Why the strong intrinsic baseline underperforms: Beyond long-horizon credit assignment, can you quantify how lack of diversity (without the MI regularizer) limits exploration or learning? Any diagnostic comparing exploration coverage or option-identity predictability?
- Fig. 1 interpretation: Is it correct that (1) concatenation effectively varies the bias of the first hidden layer by latent code, and (2) bilinear integration changes the entire first-layer weight matrix as a function of the latent code?
- MI bonus sensitivity: How sensitive are results to α_H, the discretization grid, and the choice of the state subset C? Have you tried continuous density models for p(z|c), and do they change outcomes?
- Hyperparameters K and T: How do performance and stability vary with the number of skills K and the commitment time T across tasks of different sparsity/scale? Any guidance for choosing them?
- End-to-end adaptation: Did you test fine-tuning low-level skills during downstream training? How much additional gain is possible, and does it reduce variance across seeds?
- Skill robustness: Do you observe mode collapse without MI in some seeds? Can you report MI estimates and inter-skill overlap metrics over training?
- Compute/sample budgets: Please provide matched budgets for pretraining and downstream phases across methods to facilitate fair comparison.

Overall, the paper presents a simple, well-justified, and empirically strong approach to hierarchical RL with clear potential for impact. Addressing the above clarifications and extensions would further strengthen the work.
