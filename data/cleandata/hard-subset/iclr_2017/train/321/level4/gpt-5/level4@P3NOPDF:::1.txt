This paper presents a hierarchical control approach closely related to Heess et al., combining pre-trained low-level skills with a higher-level controller and employing mutual information (MI) regularization. The experimental results are strong: the method succeeds on benchmarks that prior work could not, and the implementation appears elegant and effective. However, the analysis accompanying the experiments is comparatively light, and several important clarifications would improve the paper’s clarity and reproducibility.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results that advance the state of the art on several challenging benchmarks.
- Conceptually clean use of hierarchical decomposition with MI regularization to promote diverse, controllable skills.
- Clear acknowledgment of limitations in some tasks (e.g., complex mazes), which lends credibility to the evaluation.

Main concerns and questions
1) Terminology (“intrinsic motivation”): The paper’s use of “intrinsic motivation” seems misaligned with its common usage in RL, where it typically refers to task-agnostic rewards (e.g., curiosity, empowerment) that encourage exploration independent of downstream tasks. Here, the pre-training rewards (e.g., speed for locomotion, grasping for manipulation) are task-directed and tailored to the eventual downstream objectives. This framing is closer to skill pre-training or auxiliary objectives. I recommend revising the terminology to avoid confusion and to better situate the approach relative to prior work (including Heess et al.), whose pre-training tasks are similar in spirit.

2) MI regularization on harder mazes: The MI regularizer is elegant and generally effective, but it appears not to help on the more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3). The paper notes this but does not analyze why. Could the authors provide an interpretation or diagnostic analysis? For example:
- Does MI increase skill diversity without aiding long-horizon credit assignment or exploration in these mazes?
- Are there capacity or coordination bottlenecks between high-level commands and low-level skills (e.g., skill collapse, misalignment between skill repertoire and maze navigation needs)?
- How sensitive is performance to the MI coefficient, the command bottleneck size, or the switching period in these settings?
Some ablations or visualizations (e.g., option usage patterns, entropy/MI over training, state visitation) would make this result more informative.

3) State factorization (S_agent vs. S_rest): For reproducibility, the paper should specify the exact factorization for every environment. While S_agent is described (as in Duan et al.), the definition of S_rest is not clearly detailed. Please enumerate which state components belong to each set, any preprocessing or normalization applied, and how sensory modalities (e.g., proprioceptive vs. exteroceptive inputs) are partitioned. This clarity is essential for replicating the training signal and understanding what information is available to each controller.

4) Analysis of switching behavior and policy dynamics: The hierarchical controller’s switching is a central mechanism, yet the paper provides limited analysis. The following would considerably strengthen the contribution:
- Quantitative analysis of switching frequency and dwell times, and their relation to task phases.
- Sensitivity of performance to the switching period or to allowing the high level to adaptively choose when to switch.
- Failure modes (e.g., premature or delayed switching, oscillatory handoffs) and how they correlate with task difficulty.
- Diversity and reuse of skills during evaluation: how many distinct skills are effectively used, and do they align with interpretable sub-tasks?

Additional suggestions
- Positioning relative to Heess et al.: More explicit contrasts (pre-training objectives, policy parameterization, hierarchical interface, and training stability) would clarify the paper’s novelty.
- Reproducibility: In addition to the state factorization, please provide key hyperparameters (especially MI-related ones), random seed handling, and any curriculum or shaping used. Even brief appendices or a code snippet would help.
- Broader analysis: Where feasible, simple ablations—without MI, without pre-training, different skill library sizes—would contextualize which components drive the observed gains.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and well-executed piece of work on hierarchical control with strong empirical results. Clarifying the terminology around “intrinsic motivation,” providing a precise description of the S_agent/S_rest factorization, and adding targeted analyses (particularly on switching behavior and the MI failure cases in harder mazes) would significantly strengthen the paper’s clarity and scientific insight. I view the contribution positively and believe that addressing these points would make the paper both more interpretable and more reproducible.
