Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first pretrains a low-level policy to acquire diverse skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and a mutual-information (MI) regularizer, then trains a high-level “manager” that selects skills over fixed temporal windows to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. Key design elements include: categorical latent variables fed into the policy via bilinear integration to induce multi-modality; an MI bonus between the latent code and a subset of state (e.g., CoM position) to promote behavior diversity; and TRPO for both phases, with the latent treated as part of the observation. The authors provide ablations (concatenation vs bilinear integration, with/without MI), qualitative “visitation plots,” and downstream results on sparse-reward mazes and a gathering task. They also report on more complex agents (Snake), sensitivity to switch time T, and failure modes on unstable agents (Ant).

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The framework is simple, well-motivated, and empirically improves exploration and sample efficiency in sparse, long-horizon control tasks. The SNN+MI mechanism yields interpretable, reusable skills with strong qualitative and quantitative evidence.
- The paper is clearly written, includes useful ablations and analysis (integration scheme, MI bonus, switch time), and offers code and videos, increasing reproducibility and impact.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and contribution: The paper targets poor exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks by learning a reusable skill basis via unsupervised pretraining (guided only by a proxy reward), and composing skills via a high-level policy. This is a meaningful and timely problem in RL.
- Motivation and placement: The work bridges hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation. Compared to prior skill discovery (e.g., HiREPS, options), the approach is lightweight, uses weight sharing for efficiency, and encourages diversity via MI. The relation to Heess et al. (2016) is appropriate, though the claim of needing only “minimal domain knowledge” would benefit from nuance (see below).
- Evidence and rigor: Results demonstrate that bilinear integration and MI substantially increase skill diversity, which translates into better exploration and faster learning on downstream tasks. The analysis on switch time T and the inclusion of Snake/Ant are valuable. However, the MI estimator relies on discretization, and the downstream tasks are still relatively constrained; additional quantitative analysis of robustness would strengthen the claims.

Additional feedback and questions to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Failure modes and robustness
  - The Ant results show instability when switching skills. Could the authors quantify fall rates vs switch timing, and test mitigations (e.g., warm-starting the next skill with a short blending horizon, learned transition policies, or latent continuity constraints)?
  - How sensitive is performance to the number of skills K and to the MI coefficient αH? Please include curves showing skill diversity metrics and downstream returns as K and αH vary.
  - The paper shows variability across pretrained SNN seeds, especially for maze tasks. Can the manager be augmented (e.g., with recurrence or hindsight relabeling) to reduce dependence on having a specific “critical” skill present?

- MI bonus and estimation details (Sec. 5.3)
  - You estimate p(Z|C) via discretizing CoM (x,y). Why this choice, and how sensitive are results to grid resolution? What happens without discretization (e.g., using a learned density model for p(Z|C) or classifier-based MI bounds)? A brief comparison would clarify robustness.
  - The MI targets a subset of the state. How should practitioners choose this subset in other domains (e.g., manipulation)? Please discuss how the proxy for “behavioral distinctiveness” generalizes beyond CoM.

- Generality across embodiments and tasks
  - The inclusion of the Snake agent is appreciated. Could you provide more quantitative comparisons (skill diversity metrics, downstream sample efficiency) between Swimmer and Snake to substantiate scaling claims?
  - For Ant, beyond qualitative visitation plots, can you report manager performance on downstream tasks and analyze why switching fails (state distribution shift vs controller instability)?
  - The downstream tasks are primarily locomotion-centric. Have you tried alternative proxy rewards (e.g., energy-efficient motion, orientation control) or different task families (e.g., manipulation with contact)? This would better support claims of minimal domain knowledge and broad reusability.

- Relation to prior work and claims
  - On the comparison with Heess et al. (2016): while your proxy reward avoids enumerating goals, it is still hand-crafted for a class of tasks (e.g., speed for locomotion). Please temper the claim of “minimal domain knowledge” and clarify when proxy design might be as involved as specifying a goal set.
  - Could you add a brief discussion comparing MI-based diversity to alternative diversity objectives (e.g., empowerment, DIAYN-like mutual information with state) and why CoM-based MI was chosen here?

- Hierarchical control details
  - The manager uses a fixed commitment time T. You show limited sensitivity in static tasks; can you comment on dynamic tasks or propose learning a termination policy? A short discussion or preliminary result would be useful.
  - Have you tried continuous latents (e.g., Gaussian) or temperature/entropy regularization on the manager to balance exploration vs exploitation over skills?

- Empirical reporting
  - Please include more comprehensive statistics (mean ± std over seeds) for key experiments and a compact metric for skill diversity (e.g., coverage area, pairwise trajectory divergence).
  - Clarify pretraining compute/sample costs vs overall savings in downstream tasks for different K.

Questions for the authors
1) What are the dominant failure modes as skill diversity increases (mode collapse, overlapping behaviors, manager indecision), and which mitigations are most effective?
2) How sensitive are results to the discretization used to estimate MI? What performance do you observe with a continuous estimator (e.g., classifier-based variational MI)?
3) How well does the approach scale to more complex embodiments (e.g., 5-link swimmer, Ant) in terms of learned skill diversity and downstream performance? Please provide quantitative comparisons.
4) Could you clarify and temper the claim of minimal domain knowledge in proxy reward design, and discuss how to construct suitable proxies beyond locomotion?
5) What guides the choice of K, and how does over/under-specifying K affect both skill learning and downstream performance?
6) Did you evaluate end-to-end fine-tuning of low-level skills with the manager (e.g., via straight-through estimators), and if so, does it mitigate switching-related failures?
