Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a hierarchical RL framework that first pre-trains a policy to realize a diverse set of skills and then learns a high-level “manager” to compose these skills for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. Skill diversity is induced by conditioning a low-level policy on a discrete latent variable (a Stochastic Neural Network, SNN) and adding a mutual-information bonus between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position). The manager selects a skill every T steps and keeps it fixed, improving exploration and credit assignment. Empirically, the method learns clearly interpretable, temporally extended skills in locomotion domains and substantially accelerates learning in maze and gather tasks, outperforming strong baselines that directly combine intrinsic and sparse rewards. Visualizations and ablations (e.g., bilinear vs. concatenation integration; MI coefficient) are informative, and comparisons are fair and well executed.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, well-motivated combination of SNN conditioning with an information-theoretic objective to induce diverse, identifiable skills, leading to robust exploration.
- Strong empirical evidence on sparse-reward tasks, with careful ablations showing why and when the approach works (e.g., the MI bonus and bilinear integration matter).

Supporting arguments
- Problem formulation is compelling: pre-train reusable skills with minimal domain knowledge (a single proxy reward) and compose them hierarchically for different tasks sharing the agent space. This directly addresses sparse rewards and long horizons where naive exploration fails.
- Methodological choices are justified and connected to prior work: SNNs for multimodality; an MI-based diversity objective inspired by InfoGAN; hierarchical control akin to options, but with a practical proxy-reward pre-training phase. Related work is covered, including HiREPS, option-critic, and concurrent pre-training approaches.
- The empirical story is cohesive: visitation plots demonstrate skill diversity and identifiability; long rollouts show improved state-space coverage; learning curves show consistent gains over intrinsic-reward baselines, including when those baselines are strengthened by proxy rewards during downstream training.
- The paper is clear, with code and videos. The analysis of switching and failure modes (e.g., Ant) is candid and useful.

Feedback to strengthen the paper (not all critical to the decision)
- Breadth of skill diversity: The current SNN integration modulates only the first hidden layer (concatenation changes biases; bilinear changes first-layer weights). This sufficed in locomotion where useful skills are variations of movement direction/gait, but may be limiting in tasks where optimal sub-policies differ qualitatively (e.g., manipulation where state-conditioned strategies vary widely). Consider reporting on harder, non-locomotion domains, or exploring richer conditioning mechanisms (e.g., FiLM, hypernetworks, or deeper multiplicative conditioning).
- Hierarchical training assumptions: The manager is trained with frozen sub-policies and a fixed switch period T. While this highlights reuse, it places a heavy burden on pre-trained skills and their proxy reward. Please expand results or discussion on end-to-end finetuning (e.g., straight-through estimators), learned terminations, or manager-conditioned low-level adaptation, and relate concretely to hierarchical REPS and option-critic.
- Proxy reward design and generality: Although minimal, the proxy reward is domain-shaped (e.g., speed for locomotion). Provide sensitivity analyses to proxy choice, MI bonus weight αH, the discretization of the CoM grid, and the number of skills K. This will clarify robustness and portability.
- Data budget fairness: Clarify the accounting of pre-training samples versus downstream samples across baselines. Explicit sample-complexity plots (total environment steps) would make the transfer benefit more transparent.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline intuition: In Section 7.3, why does the “CoM + task reward” baseline perform poorly? Beyond long-horizon credit assignment, do you attribute the failure mainly to the lack of enforced behavioral diversity (i.e., no MI-like pressure), leading to poor coverage? Any evidence that adding a diversity term to the baseline improves it?
- Data usage in baselines: Does the strong baseline benefit from the same amount of pre-training experience, or is it trained from scratch on the downstream task? Please state total environment steps for each method.
- “Multi-policy” vs. SNN: Could you precisely define the “multi-policy” baseline? My understanding is that it trains K independent low-level Gaussian policies and a manager that switches among them, whereas the SNN shares parameters and uses a latent code to select modes. If so, how are capacities and training budgets matched?
- Figure 1 integration: Please confirm that (1) concatenation effectively selects different biases for the first hidden layer per latent code; and (2) bilinear integration modulates the entire first-layer weight matrix conditioned on the latent code.
- MI estimation details: How sensitive is performance to the CoM grid resolution used for p̂(Z|c)? Have you tried learning p̂ with a regressor (MLP) for higher-dimensional c, and does it change results?
- Hyperparameters and ablations: How does performance vary with K (number of skills), switch period T, and αH across tasks? Are there cases where skills collapse without the MI term, and does bilinear integration alone reliably prevent collapse?
- Generalization beyond locomotion: Do you anticipate the approach will transfer to manipulation or navigation with partial observability? Any preliminary results or obstacles (e.g., state subset choice for MI, proxy reward design)?
- End-to-end hierarchical learning: Did you attempt joint training with straight-through estimators (Gumbel-Softmax/Concrete) and, if so, how did stability and final performance compare to the frozen-skills approach?
