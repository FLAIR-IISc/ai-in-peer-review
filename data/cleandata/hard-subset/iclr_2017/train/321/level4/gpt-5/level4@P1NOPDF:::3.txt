I like the problem setting and overall direction of the paper, but I have several criticisms/questions:

1) Failure modes and scalability. What are the primary failure modes of the approach? As the richness and complexity of behaviors increase, I would expect difficulties in discovering and maintaining a diverse set of skills. Please characterize when and why the method fails and how performance scales with the diversity and complexity of behaviors.

2) Discretization in Sec. 5.3. In Sec. 5.3 you write, “let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated.” Is the space discretized into grids? If so, why is discretization necessary, how is the grid defined, and how sensitive are the results to this choice? What happens if the space is not discretized (e.g., using a continuous representation)? Please clarify the implications for both training stability and final performance.

3) Generality across embodiments. Extending point (1), does the approach work with more complex embodiments, e.g., a 5‑link swimmer rather than a 2‑link swimmer? This is important for assessing generality. If not already included, please provide experiments or analysis indicating how performance and skill diversity vary with increasing degrees of freedom and more complex dynamics.

4) Comparison to Heess et al. (2016) and “minimal domain knowledge.” You write: “Recently, Heess et al. (2016) have independently proposed to learn a range of skills in a pre-training environment that will be useful for the downstream tasks, which is similar to our framework. However, their pre-training setup requires a set of goals to be specified. In comparison, we use intrinsic rewards as the only signal to the agent during the pre-training phase, the construction of which only requires very minimal domain knowledge.” I do not fully agree. The intrinsic rewards proposed here also appear hand-crafted and tailored to a relatively narrow set of control tasks. Please justify in what sense the domain knowledge required for your intrinsic rewards is “minimal,” and discuss the extent to which these rewards would transfer to qualitatively different domains or embodiments without substantial retuning.

Overall, I appreciate the setting and find the direction promising, but the paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of limitations (e.g., discretization assumptions, scalability to complex behaviors and embodiments) and a more balanced comparison with prior work regarding the amount and type of domain knowledge required.
