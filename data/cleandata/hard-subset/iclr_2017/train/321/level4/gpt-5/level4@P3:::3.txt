Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a two-stage framework for solving sparse-reward, long-horizon RL tasks. First, a pre-training phase learns a diverse set of skills in a generic environment using a proxy (intrinsic) reward. Second, a high-level “manager” policy composes these skills to solve downstream tasks.
- Skills are represented by Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a discrete latent code. A bilinear integration of observations and latent code, combined with a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position), encourages diversity and interpretability of skills.
- A hierarchical controller (manager over frozen low-level skills) substantially improves exploration and learning performance on sparse-reward environments (mazes and a gather task), with analyses showing that bilinear integration and MI regularization yield richer skill repertoires than naive concatenation.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: The paper presents a well-motivated and practical approach to hierarchical RL that combines SNNs and MI-based regularization to learn reusable, diverse skills with minimal task-specific supervision, and demonstrates consistent empirical gains on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse-reward and long-horizon tasks remain challenging; learning reusable skills that improve exploration is a compelling direction with broad applicability.
- Motivation and literature placement: The paper is well positioned relative to options, intrinsic motivation, and recent hierarchical approaches. The distinction from Heess et al. (2016) is clear in spirit (single proxy reward vs. goal-conditioned pre-training), though see feedback below about how “minimal” the domain knowledge is.
- Methodological soundness: Using SNNs yields multi-modal policies with weight sharing, and the MI regularizer is a principled way to avoid mode collapse and promote interpretable diversity. Treating the latent as part of the observation to retain TRPO applicability is clean and practical.
- Empirical evaluation: The paper includes ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear, MI coefficients) and multiple random seeds, plus analyses of exploration coverage and skill diversity via visitation plots. Results show meaningful improvements on multiple mazes and the gather task, with code provided.
- Claims vs. evidence: The core claims—learning diverse skills from a proxy reward, improved exploration via a manager, and better performance on sparse tasks—are supported. The paper also candidly discusses limitations (e.g., instability with Ant, fixed switch time T, frozen sub-policies) and shows additional results (Snake) that suggest scalability.
- Limitations: The approach depends on a designed proxy reward and an MI objective computed on hand-chosen state subsets (e.g., CoM), which introduces domain knowledge. Sensitivity to discretization and MI hyperparameters is not fully quantified. Some baselines (e.g., option-critic, other hierarchical methods) are not directly compared. Performance can heavily depend on the particular skill set learned during pre-training.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all required for acceptance)
- Clarify the degree of domain knowledge: While the proxy reward is not task-specific, it is hand-crafted (e.g., CoM speed for locomotion). Please discuss generality beyond locomotion and quantify how much engineering is needed for other domains (e.g., manipulation).
- Quantify diversity: In addition to visitation plots, report diversity metrics (e.g., MI estimates, coverage, pairwise trajectory divergence) and how they vary with K, MI coefficient α, and integration choice.
- Sensitivity and robustness: Provide sensitivity analyses for the MI grid resolution, α, the number of skills K, and the switch time T in the main text (some are in the appendix). Report training instability (e.g., mode collapse) frequencies across seeds.
- Baselines: Include direct comparisons to other hierarchical/option methods available circa 2016–2017 (e.g., option-critic) and strong intrinsic motivation baselines in the same settings, if feasible.
- Skill quality vs. downstream performance: More explicitly link pre-trained skill repertoires to downstream success (e.g., show how missing or weak “backward” skills correlate with maze failure).
- Failure modes and mitigations: Bring the Ant results into the main paper, analyze switching-induced instability, and discuss mitigation strategies (e.g., smoothing transitions, warm-starting skills, or joint fine-tuning).
- Manager flexibility: Consider an adaptive termination mechanism (learned option termination) and/or a recurrent manager to exploit history, even if only as a discussion with small-scale experiments.

Questions for the authors
- MI estimation and discretization (Sec. 5.3): You discretize CoM into grid cells to estimate p(Z|C). Why prefer discretization over parametric density models? How sensitive are results to grid resolution and α? Can you report quantitative performance/diversity vs. grid size and show results with an MLP-based p̂(Z|C)?
- Choice of state subset for MI: How did you choose the CoM coordinates, and how would this generalize to tasks where CoM is not indicative of skill semantics (e.g., manipulation)? Have you tried other state subsets or learned discriminators?
- Sample efficiency: You claim K-fold efficiency over training K separate policies. Please report actual environment steps and wall-clock times for SNN pre-training vs. K independent policies, including any differences in convergence behavior.
- Scalability to more complex embodiments: The appendix includes Snake and Ant. Could you summarize these results in the main paper and provide more quantitative comparisons (diversity metrics, downstream task performance)? What limits the approach for unstable agents like Ant, and which mitigations were most promising?
- Robustness of transfer: Performance on mazes varies depending on which skills emerge during pre-training. Can the manager compensate via longer training, or is fine-tuning the low-level skills necessary? Have you tried joint training with straight-through estimators or Gumbel-Softmax?
- Comparison to goal-conditioned pre-training (Heess et al., 2016): Could you provide a controlled comparison where both methods use comparable supervision budgets (e.g., one proxy reward vs. a small set of goals), to better substantiate the claimed advantage?

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution to hierarchical RL. With clearer discussion of the proxy reward’s generality, more robust MI estimation/sensitivity analyses, and stronger baselines, the paper will be even more compelling.
