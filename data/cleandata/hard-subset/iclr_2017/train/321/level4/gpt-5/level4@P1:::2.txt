Summary
The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that first pre-trains a diverse set of skills using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with a mutual information (MI) bonus, and then learns a high-level “Manager” policy that selects among these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key idea is to learn multi-modal, interpretable sub-policies guided by a single proxy reward that requires minimal domain knowledge, and to encourage skill diversity via an information-theoretic regularizer. The approach is implemented with TRPO, uses bilinear integration of latent codes and observations to induce multi-modality, and demonstrates strong empirical performance on long-horizon sparse tasks (maze navigation and gather), with compelling visualizations and careful comparisons to baselines, including a “smooth” intrinsic reward baseline that fails to solve the tasks.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- The approach is well motivated and clearly positioned in the literature, combining intrinsic motivation and hierarchy in a principled way. The MI bonus for skill identifiability is simple, effective, and leads to interpretable skills.
- The empirical evaluation is strong: the method substantially improves exploration and sample efficiency on challenging sparse-reward tasks and outperforms credible baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Problem/approach fit: The paper tackles sparse rewards and long horizons by learning temporally extended skills that reduce effective horizon and improve exploration. The use of a single proxy reward during pretraining requires minimal instrumentation and avoids task-specific shaping.
- Technical soundness: SNNs provide an effective mechanism for multi-modal policies with weight sharing. The MI regularizer (measured between latent code and a relevant state subset, e.g., CoM) is a well-founded way to encourage diversity and identifiability of skills. The bilinear integration choice is empirically justified and easy to implement.
- Empirical rigor: The paper evaluates on multiple downstream tasks and mazes, includes ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear, different MI coefficients), and presents clear visitation plots and learning curves. The comparison to a strong intrinsic reward baseline shows that “smooth” reward alone does not address exploration/credit assignment at long horizons; the skill-based hierarchy appears crucial.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not critical to the acceptance decision)
- Limited diversification of sub-policies: With both concatenation and bilinear integration, the latent primarily modulates the first hidden layer, which may be sufficient for locomotion where sub-policies are closely related but may be limiting in domains where the optimal skills are more distinct (e.g., manipulation, where solving the same task from different states can require qualitatively different strategies). It would strengthen the paper to test on harder, non-locomotion domains or to discuss architectures enabling richer differentiation (e.g., deeper conditioning, FiLM-style modulation, or option-specific residual layers).
- Limitations of the hierarchical policy: The Manager is trained while sub-policies are frozen, and the skill duration T is fixed. This makes overall success heavily dependent on the proxy reward and the quality/coverage of learned skills. It would be helpful to include results or discussion on end-to-end training (e.g., straight-through estimators for discrete latents) and/or learning adaptive termination, connecting to hierarchical REPS and option-critic.
- Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards are somewhat domain-specific: Because sub-policies are fixed during downstream training, the proxy reward becomes a key design choice and may encode domain knowledge. A brief discussion on how generic these proxy rewards can be across domains and tasks, and guidance for practitioners on constructing them, would be valuable.

Questions for the authors
- On the strong “CoM maze reward” baseline (Sec. 7.3): What is your intuition for why it performs poorly and cannot solve the tasks, beyond the long-term credit assignment argument? Do you attribute this mainly to the lack of enforced diversity (as in your MI-regularized SNN training)? Did this baseline receive any benefit from the pretraining experience (e.g., initialization, data reuse), or is it trained entirely from scratch on the downstream task?
- Multi-policy vs. SNN: Could you clarify precisely how the “Multi-policy” baseline differs from SNN in the final hierarchy (e.g., separate independently trained unimodal policies vs. a single SNN with K latent codes, and how the Manager’s output is connected in each case)? A concise summary in the main text would aid readers.
- Figure 1 understanding: Please confirm whether the following interpretation is correct: (1) With concatenation, the categorical latent induces different bias terms to the first hidden layer; (2) with bilinear integration, the latent modulates the full weight matrix from observation to the first hidden layer (i.e., effectively different first-layer weights per latent).
- Scope/generalization: Do you expect the current conditioning scheme (and MI measured on CoM) to suffice in tasks where relevant state factors are not purely positional (e.g., contact modes in manipulation)? Any preliminary evidence or guidance on choosing the state subset for the MI term in such settings would be helpful.
- End-to-end training: Have you tried joint optimization of Manager and sub-policies (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax/Concrete relaxations)? If so, how did stability and performance compare to the frozen-skill setup?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and promising contribution to HRL. The method is clearly presented, empirically convincing on sparse tasks, and the visualizations are particularly illuminating. I recommend acceptance.
