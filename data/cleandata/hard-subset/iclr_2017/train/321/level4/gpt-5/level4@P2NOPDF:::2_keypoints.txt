- Edited score raised from 6 to 7.
- Paper proposes hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic intrinsic reward to learn diverse, identifiable sub-policies.
- Strong empirical result: baseline that simply adds intrinsic reward to sparse task reward fails despite smoothing, showing advantage of the proposed method.
- Possible reason for advantage: explicit diversity pressure during sub-policy training, absent in the baseline—could inform stronger baselines and new end-to-end hierarchical methods.
- Visualizations are clear and informative.
- Strength: promising approach demonstrating empirical benefits of identifiable, diverse sub-policies and a manager that composes them.
- Weakness 1: Limited sub-policy diversification—concatenation and bilinear integration only change first hidden layer parameters, which may be restrictive for non-locomotion tasks requiring more distinct behaviors.
- Suggestion: evaluate on harder, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) where sub-policies must differ substantially.
- Weakness 2: Rigid hierarchy—manager trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy durations places heavy burden on intrinsic objectives and may limit downstream usefulness.
- Suggestion: discuss/experiment with adaptive durations, joint end-to-end training, or limited fine-tuning of sub-policies to connect to end-to-end hierarchical learning.
- Weakness 3: Domain specificity of intrinsic rewards—intrinsic objectives might need domain tailoring and edge toward supervision; clarify generality and sensitivity to design.
- Question: Why does the strong baseline in Sec 7.3 perform poorly; is this the “CoM maze reward” baseline and does it benefit from pretraining experience or was it trained from scratch with the shaped reward?
- Question: What exactly is “multi-policy” and how does it differ from the SNN approach beyond stochastic latent gating?
- Question: Confirm interpretations in Fig.1—(1) concatenation induces different biases/input offsets in the first hidden layer; (2) bilinear integration selects different observation-to-first-layer weight matrices.
- Overall recommendation: solid contribution that would be strengthened by addressing diversification limits, hierarchy rigidity, and intrinsic reward domain specificity.
