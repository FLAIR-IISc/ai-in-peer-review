- Strong empirical results: elegant hierarchical approach with MI regularization advances SOTA on challenging benchmarks.

- Terminology issue: “intrinsic motivation” is misleading—pre-training rewards are task-directed; relabel as skill pre-training/auxiliary objectives and compare to Heess et al.

- MI regularizer limitations: MI helps generally but fails on harder mazes; request diagnostic analysis (effect on diversity vs. long-horizon exploration, capacity/coordination bottlenecks).

- Sensitivity analyses needed: test MI coefficient, command bottleneck size, switching period and report option usage, entropy/MI over training, and state visitation visualizations.

- Unclear state factorization: must specify exact S_agent vs. S_rest components per environment, preprocessing/normalization, and sensory modality partitioning for reproducibility.

- Insufficient switching analysis: provide quantitative switching frequency/dwell times, sensitivity to fixed vs. adaptive switching, failure modes, and how many distinct skills are used and reused.

- Positioning relative to prior work: more explicit contrasts with Heess et al. on pre-training objectives, parameterization, hierarchical interface, and stability.

- Reproducibility details missing: share key hyperparameters (especially MI-related), random seed handling, curricula/shaping, and brief appendices or code snippets.

- Ablation studies recommended: evaluate without MI, without pre-training, and with different skill library sizes to identify drivers of performance.

- Overall verdict: promising, well-executed work; addressing terminology, factorization, and targeted analyses (switching and MI failures) will improve clarity and reproducibility.
