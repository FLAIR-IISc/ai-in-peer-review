- Proposes a two-stage hierarchical RL framework: pre-train diverse skills with a proxy reward, then train a high-level manager to compose them for downstream sparse-reward tasks.
- Represents skills with Stochastic Neural Networks (discrete latent codes) using bilinear integration of observations and latent to improve expressivity.
- Uses a mutual information (MI) bonus between latent code and a state subset (e.g., CoM) to encourage diverse, interpretable skills.
- Freezing low-level skills and training a manager substantially improves exploration and task learning on mazes and a gather task.
- Ablations show bilinear integration and MI regularization produce richer skill repertoires than naive concatenation.
- Decision: Accept — method is well-motivated, practical, and shows consistent empirical gains on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks.
- Significance: Tackles important problem of sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks by learning reusable skills to aid exploration.
- Positioning: Well placed relative to options, intrinsic motivation, and hierarchical methods, with a clear distinction from goal-conditioned pre-training.
- Methodological soundness: SNNs provide multimodality and weight sharing; MI regularizer helps avoid mode collapse and promotes interpretability.
- Practical implementation: Treating the latent as part of the observation preserves TRPO applicability and is a clean engineering choice.
- Empirical rigor: Includes ablations, multiple seeds, visitation/skill-diversity analyses, and provides code.
- Claims are supported by evidence, and limitations are candidly discussed (e.g., Ant instability, fixed switch time, frozen sub-policies).
- Limitation: Relies on hand-designed proxy rewards and MI computed on hand-chosen state subsets, introducing domain knowledge.
- Limitation: Sensitivity to discretization and MI hyperparameters is not fully quantified.
- Limitation: Some relevant baselines (e.g., option-critic) are not directly compared.
- Limitation: Downstream performance can heavily depend on the particular skills learned during pre-training.
- Feedback: Clarify how much domain knowledge is required and generality beyond locomotion (e.g., manipulation).
- Feedback: Quantify diversity with metrics (MI estimates, coverage, pairwise divergence) and report how they vary with K, α, and integration choice.
- Feedback: Provide sensitivity analyses for MI grid resolution, α, number of skills K, and switch time T, and report instability frequencies across seeds.
- Feedback: Include comparisons to other hierarchical/option methods and strong intrinsic motivation baselines where feasible.
- Feedback: Explicitly link pre-trained skill quality to downstream success and analyze missing/weak skills’ impact.
- Feedback: Move Ant results into the main paper, analyze switching-induced instability, and discuss mitigation strategies (smoothing, warm-starts, fine-tuning).
- Feedback: Consider manager enhancements like learned termination and recurrent managers, with small-scale experiments if possible.
- Question: Why discretize CoM for MI estimation instead of parametric density models, and how sensitive are results to grid resolution and α?
- Question: How was the CoM chosen as the MI state subset, and does this generalize to domains where CoM isn’t meaningful?
- Question: Provide sample-efficiency comparisons: env steps and wall-clock time for SNN pre-training vs. K independent policies.
- Question: Summarize Snake and Ant results in the main paper and quantify limits/mitigations for unstable agents like Ant.
- Question: Can the manager compensate for missing skills via longer training or is low-level fine-tuning necessary; have joint-training methods (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) been tried?
- Question: Provide a controlled comparison to goal-conditioned pre-training (Heess et al., 2016) using comparable supervision budgets.
