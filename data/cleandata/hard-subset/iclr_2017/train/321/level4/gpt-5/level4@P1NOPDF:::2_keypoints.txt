- Proposes hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability intrinsic reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.  
- Strong empirical finding: a baseline that simply adds intrinsic rewards to sparse task rewards fails to solve tasks, despite smoother reward signals.  
- Additional plausible explanation for baseline failure: proposed method enforces explicit diversity during sub-policy training, absent in the baseline.  
- This difference could inform ways to strengthen baselines or design end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS or option-critic).  
- Visualizations are clear and helpful.  
- Limited sub-policy diversification: concatenation and bilinear integration mainly alter only the first hidden layer, which may suffice for locomotion but may fail where sub-policies must be qualitatively different.  
- Recommendation: evaluate on harder, non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation) to test diversification limits.  
- Hierarchical policy limitation: manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed option durations, requiring pre-aligned intrinsic objectives and sub-policies.  
- Suggest exploring end-to-end joint training of manager and sub-policies or adaptive option durations to handle misalignment.  
- Concern that intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific or require designer choices; request discussion on generality and required domain knowledge.  
- Question: why does the strong baseline in Section 7.3 (presumably the “CoM maze reward”) perform so poorly?  
- Question: does the baseline receive comparable data/pretraining, i.e., account for extra experience from pre-trained policies?  
- Question: what is “multi-policy” and how does it differ from SNN in the experimental setup?  
- Question about Figure 1: confirm (1) concatenation induces different biases in the first hidden layer and (2) bilinear integration induces different weight matrices from observation to first hidden layer.  
- Overall: promising, well-executed paper with clear idea and strong empirical evidence; addressing broader domains, end-to-end training, and baseline clarity would strengthen the contribution.
