Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the challenge of solving sparse‑reward, long‑horizon reinforcement learning tasks by first learning a diverse set of reusable skills in a proxy pre‑training environment, then training a high‑level controller that selects among these skills to solve downstream tasks. The authors propose to model skills as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy indexed by a discrete latent code, and to encourage diversity via a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a small, task‑agnostic subset of state features (e.g., the agent’s CoM position). A bilinear integration of observation and latent code improves expressivity over simple concatenation. The low‑level SNN is frozen and a “Manager” policy learns to switch among skills every T steps. The approach is evaluated on challenging sparse tasks (Locomotion+Maze and Gather), showing substantial improvements in exploration and sample efficiency compared to strong baselines (including proxy rewards and intrinsically motivated exploration). Ablations demonstrate the benefits of bilinear integration and the MI regularizer, and experiments include multiple robots (Swimmer, Snake), with a discussion of limitations on unstable agents (Ant). Overall, the work offers a simple, general, and effective framework that combines hierarchical RL with unsupervised skill discovery.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence that SNN‑based skill pre‑training plus a high‑level manager substantially improves exploration and performance on hard sparse‑reward benchmarks that prior methods struggle to solve.
- A well‑motivated and practical method that integrates ideas from hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation, with clear ablations (bilinear integration, MI bonus) and sensible design choices (using TRPO; freezing skills for stability).

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse rewards and long horizons remain a core challenge for deep RL. The proposed formulation—pre‑train reusable skills with a domain‑light proxy reward, then learn to compose them—addresses exploration and credit assignment without per‑task reward shaping.
- Method motivation and placement: The approach is grounded in options/HRL and recent skill discovery. Relative to Heess et al. (goal‑conditioned pre‑training), the proxy reward and MI bonus avoid task‑specific goal instrumentation and demonstrably induce a broader set of behaviors. The use of SNNs provides multi‑modality and weight sharing across skills; bilinear integration strengthens the interaction between state and latent code; the MI term directly promotes skill identifiability and diversity.
- Empirical support and rigor: The paper evaluates on multiple mazes and Gather variants, includes ablations on integration method and MI strength, analyzes exploration coverage (visitation maps), and tests transfer across two agents. Results consistently show that hierarchical reuse of SNN‑skills improves sample efficiency and success rates versus strong baselines. The paper is transparent about failure modes (Ant), and provides code and implementation details.

Areas that could be strengthened (not decisive for acceptance)
- The use of “intrinsic motivation”: While the proxy reward is task‑agnostic and unsupervised with respect to downstream goals, labeling it “intrinsic” may be misleading given current RL usage. The pre‑training reward (e.g., speed or grasp success) is deliberately aligned with anticipated downstream capabilities and differs from pure novelty/uncertainty bonuses. A short clarification of terminology would avoid confusion and ease comparison to intrinsic motivation work.
- MI effectiveness across tasks: The MI regularizer clearly improves skill diversity and exploration, and helps in Gather; however, benefits are mixed in some mazes. The authors note this; a brief analysis (e.g., when turning/sideways skills matter vs when wall‑contact reorientation suffices) would help interpretability and provide guidance on when to tune MI.
- Factorization clarity: The paper assumes S can be factored into Sagent and Srest and leverages a small subset C for the MI. For reproducibility, please explicitly list Srest per environment (e.g., sensor channels, wall/goal features) and the precise definition of C and its discretization.
- Switching analysis: The paper would benefit from a compact analysis of switching behavior: distribution of dwell times, failure modes when switching in unstable systems, and sensitivity to T (beyond the appendix), possibly with a learned termination policy as a robustness baseline.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Quantify skill diversity: In addition to visitation plots, report simple quantitative metrics (e.g., pairwise trajectory divergence, coverage, mutual information achieved) to systematically compare integration types and MI strengths.
- Sensitivity studies: Briefly summarize in the main text sensitivity to K (number of skills), grid resolution for MI posterior estimation, and choice of C. Point to consistent defaults.
- Baseline positioning: Where possible, include or reference a comparison to option‑critic or goal‑conditioned pre‑training in the main text to clarify novelty and trade‑offs (frozen vs end‑to‑end, stability, sample costs).
- Computational cost: Add a note on training time and memory overhead of SNN vs multi‑policy baselines; emphasize the K‑fold sample efficiency advantage when learning multiple skills jointly.
- Terminology and notation: Tighten the description of “proxy reward” vs “intrinsic reward,” and ensure consistent notation for latent variables and MI estimation. A small schematic clarifying the bilinear integration would aid readers.

Questions for the authors
1) MI bonus and posterior estimation:
- How sensitive are results to the grid resolution used to estimate p(Z|C)? Did you try learned density models instead of discretization, and with what effect?
- How was the subset C chosen per domain? Would using velocity or orientation in C further improve identifiability?

2) Number of skills and selection:
- How sensitive are downstream results to K? Do unused or redundant skills harm the manager’s learning?
- Did you try simple pruning/merging of redundant skills after pre‑training?

3) Switching and commitment time:
- How did you select T in practice, and how robust are results to this choice in the mazes? Could a learned termination policy improve stability for agents like Ant?

4) Manager training details:
- What is the observation input to the Manager (full state or partial), and how do you prevent it from overfitting to SNN idiosyncrasies?
- Did you try recurrent managers to incorporate information gathered during a skill’s execution?

5) Comparison to goal‑conditioned pre‑training:
- Could you discuss the pros/cons versus goal‑conditioned skill learning (e.g., Heess et al.) in terms of instrumentation effort, diversity, and transfer?

6) Fine‑tuning skills:
- Did you attempt end‑to‑end fine‑tuning with straight‑through estimators in any environment? Even a small study (or negative result) would be informative.

Overall, this is a well‑motivated, practically useful contribution with strong empirical results and clear ablations. I recommend acceptance.
