1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes a two-phase framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning aimed at sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks. In a pre-training environment, the method learns a repertoire of skills using a single, minimally engineered proxy reward and a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy. Diversity and interpretability of skills are encouraged via an information-theoretic regularizer (mutual information between a latent code and a subset of the state), with architectural choices (e.g., bilinear integration of the latent with observations) to better separate behaviors. A high-level “Manager” then selects among these frozen skills at a fixed period to tackle downstream tasks, improving exploration and reducing effective horizon. Empirically, the approach learns a broad range of locomotion skills that are visually interpretable and demonstrates faster learning and stronger performance across several sparse downstream tasks compared to strong baselines.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The approach is well-motivated and well-situated in the literature, combining intrinsic motivation and hierarchical control in a coherent framework with concrete algorithmic choices (SNNs with bilinear integration and MI regularization).
- The empirical evidence suggests the method reliably acquires diverse skills in pre-training and that the hierarchical use of these skills yields improved exploration and learning in sparse tasks.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem/goal: The paper targets a central challenge—exploration in sparse reward and long-horizon tasks—by pretraining reusable skills that reduce downstream sample complexity. This decomposes difficult problems while keeping domain knowledge minimal.
- Methodological soundness: The SNN-based policy with discrete latent codes is a reasonable way to capture multimodality with weight sharing; the MI-based bonus aligns with encouraging skill-state identifiability. Treating z as part of the observation enables clean use of TRPO without approximate mixture handling.
- Design choices: Bilinear integration between observations and latent codes is a principled intervention to increase expressiveness over concatenation and is empirically validated to improve skill diversity.
- Empirical support: The visitation analyses and downstream results (mazes and gather) show the skills are distinct and interpretable, and that hierarchical switching enhances coverage and task performance compared to baselines that rely on naive exploration or generic proxy rewards within the task.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily decision-critical)
- Failure modes and scalability of skill diversity: As behavioral richness grows, it is natural to worry about mode collapse or entanglement of behaviors in SNNs, even with MI regularization. A more systematic characterization of when and how diversity degrades (e.g., as K increases, with harder morphologies, or longer horizons) would be valuable. Clarifying which aspects of the architecture or regularizer most prevent collapse would strengthen the claims.
- MI estimation details and alternatives: In Section 5.3, you compute an MI-based bonus by discretizing a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position) into grid cells to obtain p̂(Z|C) via counts. Please clarify the discretization rationale and sensitivity: grid resolution, state subset choice, and how performance changes without discretization (e.g., using a learned density/regressor as you briefly suggest). It would help to see ablations comparing grid vs. learned posterior, and how these choices impact both skill separability and downstream task performance.
- Embodiment complexity: To assess generality, it would be useful to highlight results with more complex embodiments directly in the main text (e.g., a higher-DOF swimmer) and discuss whether the framework’s diversity and stability properties persist as action/state dimensionality increases, not only in appendices. Any differences in αH tuning, K, or the need for different integration schemes would be informative.
- Comparison to related pretraining with goals: The contrast with Heess et al. (2016) hinges on the claim that this work uses only an intrinsic proxy reward with minimal domain knowledge. While true that you avoid explicit goal sets, the proxy rewards (e.g., speed-based for locomotion, grasp success for manipulation) can themselves be fairly task-specific. It would be helpful to refine the positioning: concretely delineate the scope where the proposed proxy design is straightforward vs. domains where proxies become hand-crafted, and discuss how sensitive the method is to proxy misspecification.

Questions for the authors (for clarification and to build confidence in the assessment):
- Failure modes: As K grows or as embodiments become more complex, what failure cases do you observe (e.g., mode collapse, unstable switching, overlapping skills)? How often do such failures occur, and which mitigation (αH tuning, bilinear integration, different latent priors) is most effective?
- MI discretization: Is the space necessarily discretized for the MI bonus? If so, why is discretization preferable, and how robust is performance to grid resolution and boundaries? If not discretized, what learned estimator did you find reliable in practice, and how does it affect computational cost and stability?
- Embodiment generality: Does the approach maintain diversity and downstream gains with a more complex embodiment such as a 5-link swimmer? Are there notable changes in hyperparameters or architecture needed to preserve skill interpretability and utility?
- Positioning vs. goal-conditioned pretraining: Given that your proxy rewards (e.g., speed magnitude) still encode task-specific priors, can you elaborate on the degree of hand-crafting required across domains? Are there domains where designing a single proxy is difficult, and how would the method adapt in those settings?
