- Summary: Proposes hierarchical RL with SNNs that use an information-theoretic identifiability intrinsic reward to learn diverse sub-policies, with a high-level manager selecting options for downstream tasks; shows that simply summing intrinsic+extrinsic rewards fails on some tasks and provides useful visualizations.

- Strength: Well-motivated diversity objective using identifiability, yielding interpretable, specialized sub-policies.

- Strength: Strong empirical comparison showing a simple intrinsic+extrinsic baseline underperforms, highlighting the value of hierarchical abstraction.

- Strength: Approach is practical and easy to implement, with convincing visualizations of behavior specialization.

- Strength: Potential broader impact on baselines and end-to-end hierarchical methods by clarifying principled diversity induction.

- Weakness 1: Sub-policy modulation is limited—concatenation and bilinear conditioning mainly affect the first hidden layer, constraining expressivity.

- Weakness 1 (implication): This limitation may be fine for locomotion but could fail for domains requiring qualitatively different strategies (e.g., manipulation).

- Weakness 2: Fixed, pre-trained sub-policies and fixed option durations burden pretraining and prevent adaptation during downstream learning.

- Weakness 2 (implication): Misaligned intrinsic rewards may harm transfer; joint/alternating training or fine-tuning and learning durations are needed.

- Weakness 3: Intrinsic reward may be domain-specific; identifiability may not align with task-relevant structure in all domains.

- Suggestion: Evaluate on non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation) to test expressive limits of sub-policy parameterizations.

- Suggestion: Compare or connect to end-to-end hierarchical methods (option-critic, hierarchical REPS), including joint/fine-tuning of options and learning option duration.

- Suggestion: Provide ablations/diagnostics on diversity—mutual information estimates, option usage entropy, state coverage, and reward-strength sweeps.

- Suggestion: Analyze gating architecture—compare concatenation vs. bilinear and consider deeper modulation (FiLM, hypernetworks) for greater expressivity.

- Suggestion: Ensure fair baselines—equalize total environment interactions or report sample complexity explicitly.

- Clarification needed: Why the strong baseline fails—role of hierarchy vs. lack of explicit diversity constraint?

- Clarification needed: Does the baseline receive the same total environment interactions including pretraining, and how does sample complexity affect comparisons?

- Clarification needed: Define “multi-policy” relative to SNNs—ensemble of independent policies or shared-parameter variant?

- Clarification needed: Confirm architectural interpretations: concatenation acts like bias shifts; bilinear acts like multiplicative input-weight modulation?

- Clarification needed: Have you explored fine-tuning sub-policies or learning option duration during downstream training, and how sensitive is performance to mismatches between identifiability and task structure?

- Overall assessment: Solid, promising paper with important negative baseline result; recommend acceptance pending clarifications on baselines/experience accounting and discussion or experiments on adaptability and broader domains.
