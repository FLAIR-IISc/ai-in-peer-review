Summary
The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first pre-trains a diverse set of low-level skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and a simple proxy reward, and then learns a high-level “manager” that selects among these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The SNN uses categorical latent variables combined with observations via bilinear integration, and an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information (MI) between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., the robot’s CoM position) to encourage diversity and interpretability of skills. During downstream training, the high-level policy selects a skill and commits to it for T steps, which improves exploration and reduces effective horizon. The method is trained with TRPO. Empirically, the approach learns interpretable locomotion primitives, yields broader state-space coverage under random high-level policies, and achieves strong performance on sparse-reward benchmarks (multiple Swimmer mazes and Gather), often outperforming baselines.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
- Reason 1: Strong empirical results on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks that prior methods struggled to solve, with clear ablations demonstrating the contributions of SNN multimodality, bilinear integration, and the MI bonus.
- Reason 2: The approach is simple, well-motivated, and well-positioned in the literature, combining hierarchical control and skill discovery with a minimal pre-training signal. The learned skills are interpretable and reusable, and the framework appears sample-efficient relative to training separate policies.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets long-horizon, sparse-reward tasks where naive exploration is ineffective. The proposed two-phase approach (proxy-reward pre-training + hierarchical reuse) is coherent and aligns with the literature on options, HRL, and intrinsic motivation. Compared to Heess et al., the pre-training relies on a single proxy reward rather than a set of goals, reducing domain instrumentation.
- Technical approach: Using SNNs to induce multi-modal policies is appropriate; the bilinear integration between latent code and observation is a meaningful design that empirically matters. The MI regularizer is elegant and practical: it encourages skill-state identifiability via a simple count-based estimate on discretized CoM coordinates (or an MLP regressor for higher-dimensional cases). Freezing the low-level layer and training only the manager simplifies optimization and still performs well.
- Empirical support: The paper presents qualitative evidence (visitation plots) that SNNs learn diverse, interpretable skills and that random high-level policies over these skills explore much more broadly than Gaussian noise. Quantitatively, the hierarchical method solves several sparse mazes and the Gather task with improved sample efficiency relative to baselines, including a “proxy reward in-task” baseline. Ablations (concatenation vs bilinear; with/without MI) are informative. The evaluation across multiple seeds and pre-trained SNNs is appreciated and avoids cherry-picking.
- Limitations acknowledged: The paper fairly discusses cases where MI does not boost performance, as well as instability and switching challenges for more complex agents (e.g., Ant).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- On “intrinsic motivation”: As other reviewers note, calling the proxy reward “intrinsic” is potentially misleading in current RL terminology. Rewarding locomotion speed (or generic grasp) is still a shaped signal that biases learning toward downstream task-relevant behaviors. I recommend adopting more neutral terminology (e.g., “proxy reward” or “self-supervised pre-training signal”) throughout to avoid confusion and to better distinguish from exploration bonuses like count-based or information-gain methods.
- MI effects in harder mazes: While MI clearly promotes skill diversity and helps in some tasks, the paper notes it does not consistently improve performance in mazes 1–3. Please provide a deeper interpretation. For example: do the extra turning/sideways skills induced by MI not align with the specific navigation strategies the manager ends up selecting? Is credit assignment still dominated by long-horizon issues when goals require specific contact with walls for reorientation, making MI-induced diversity less impactful?
- Factorization Sagent vs Srest: The factorization assumption is central. For reproducibility, please explicitly specify Sagent and Srest for each environment (Duan et al. define Sagent for Swimmer, but Srest details—e.g., LIDAR-like features, goal/wall sensors, ball indicators, and any normalization—should be enumerated). Clarify exactly what the manager observes and whether observations differ between pre-training and downstream tasks.
- Switching analysis: The paper would benefit from more analysis of switching behavior:
  - Quantify how performance varies with T in the main body (some is in the appendix). Are there consistent trends across task sizes, and how sensitive is learning to T in practice?
  - Describe failure modes during switching (e.g., transient instability, state distribution shift across skills) even for stable agents, and report any mitigation (e.g., smoother transitions, overlap in state regions across skills).
  - Report whether the manager observes the current latent code and how that affects switching decisions.
- Comparisons and baselines: The “proxy reward in-task” baseline is useful; if feasible, additional comparisons to goal-conditioned or option-critic baselines would further position the contribution. Also, a brief discussion contrasting this method and Heess et al. under identical pre-training budgets would be valuable.
- Hyperparameters and implementation details: Please provide the exact αH used per experiment, the discretization grid for MI, and how K (number of skills) is selected. Reporting the effective utilization of skills (e.g., entropy over z usage by the trained manager) would be informative.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you explicitly list Srest for each environment and confirm whether the manager receives the full state (Sagent ∪ Srest) during downstream training? Are any inputs omitted or preprocessed differently than in pre-training?
2) What values of αH were used for each reported result, and how sensitive are the skills and downstream performance to αH and the discretization granularity?
3) For the mazes where MI does not help, do you observe different manager policies (e.g., fewer switches, preference for forward motion against walls)? Any evidence that some MI-induced skills are underutilized?
4) How does performance vary with K? Do additional skills beyond a certain point yield diminishing returns or increase manager learning difficulty?
5) Does the manager observe the currently active skill z? If not, would providing z (and perhaps a short history) help switching decisions?
6) Beyond the appendix analyses, can you share quantitative failure rates during switches (e.g., transient regressions or stalls) for Swimmer/Snake? Any simple techniques (e.g., brief action ramping) that mitigate instability?
7) How consistent are results across seeds for the pre-trained SNNs? You report 10 runs per curve; could you include per-seed performance tables to illustrate robustness?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and empirically strong contribution to hierarchical RL with a practical and interpretable skill-learning stage. With clearer terminology and additional analysis of switching and state factorization, the paper would be even stronger.
