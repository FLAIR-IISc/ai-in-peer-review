Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the challenge of exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon reinforcement learning by (1) pretraining a diverse set of temporally extended skills in a proxy environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with categorical latent variables, and (2) training a high-level “Manager” policy that selects among these skills at fixed intervals to solve downstream tasks. To encourage diversity and interpretability of skills, the authors add a mutual information (MI) regularizer between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position), estimated via discretization and counts. The framework is trained with TRPO by treating the latent as part of the observation. Empirically, SNNs with bilinear integration plus the MI bonus produce a richer span of skills than independent unimodal policies at similar sample cost, and high-level policies built on these skills solve sparse downstream tasks (mazes and gather) more reliably and efficiently than strong baselines. The work is well positioned within HRL and intrinsic motivation, and the qualitative interpretability of skills is compelling.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The approach is well motivated, clearly placed in the literature, and combines known components in a novel, practical way (SNNs + MI regularizer) to yield robust skill diversity.
- Empirical results on challenging sparse tasks, ablations (integration type, MI strength), and open-sourced code provide convincing support that the method improves exploration and sample efficiency.

Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Efficient exploration and credit assignment in sparse, long-horizon RL by reducing the effective search space via reusable skills.
- Motivation and placement: The paper bridges hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation. Unlike many option-learning methods that rely on task rewards or bottleneck discovery (often impractical in high-dimensional continuous domains), this method uses a simple proxy reward and MI-based diversity to produce skills without hand-specifying options. The comparison to Heess et al. (2016) is apt; here, the pretraining signal does not require goal labels.
- Evidence and rigor: The paper provides thorough qualitative analyses (visitation plots, effect of bilinear vs. concatenation, MI weight), and quantitative gains on multiple sparse tasks. The hierarchical policy substantially increases state-space coverage under random switching, which plausibly explains improved learning. The TRPO workaround (treat z as observation) is reasonable. Limitations are discussed (e.g., instability when switching with Ant), though they would benefit from more central emphasis.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all required for acceptance)
- Failure modes and limitations: Please consolidate and foreground a discussion of failure cases in the main text. In particular: instability when switching skills on fragile embodiments (Ant), sensitivity to the switch interval T, and mode collapse or overlap when MI is weak. A short diagnostic checklist (e.g., indicators of skill collapse, recovery strategies) would be valuable.
- MI estimation details: The MI bonus relies on discretizing CoM coordinates. Clarify the rationale, grid resolution selection, and sensitivity. What happens without discretization (e.g., a learned classifier p(z|c) as in InfoGAN or a continuous estimator)? How robust are results to the choice of c (CoM vs. other subsets)?
- Scalability to more complex embodiments: The question of generality is important. If you have results with a higher DoF agent (e.g., a 5-link swimmer/snake) and with unstable robots (Ant), consider promoting at least a subset of those to the main paper, including quantitative metrics of skill diversity and downstream performance.
- “Minimal domain knowledge” claim: While the proxy reward is simple (e.g., speed), it is still hand-designed and tailored to locomotion-like behaviors. Please nuance the claim and discuss how to design proxy rewards for non-locomotion domains, as well as how sensitive the approach is to proxy reward choice.
- Manager design and switching: Include a brief analysis of sensitivity to T and whether a learned termination policy could further help, especially in dynamic tasks. A recurrent Manager might better exploit information gathered during a skill’s execution; a short discussion would strengthen the paper.
- Quantifying skill diversity: Beyond visitation plots, consider reporting coverage metrics (e.g., state visitation entropy, MI estimates, pairwise behavior distances) to make the diversity claim more concrete.
- Hyperparameters and selection of K: Discuss how K is chosen, what happens when K is larger than the number of useful skills (unused/duplicate codes), and whether you observe skill collapse or redundancy.

Questions for the authors
1) Failure modes: As the number and richness of skills increase, what failure modes do you observe (e.g., overlapping skills, unstable switching, non-stationary Manager learning)? How do α for MI, K, and T interact, and what ranges are robust?
2) MI and discretization (Sec. 5.3): You discretize the (x, y) space to estimate p(z|c). Why is discretization preferable to a learned classifier or a continuous-density approach? How sensitive are results to grid resolution, and what happens if you avoid discretization?
3) Scalability to more complex bodies: Does the approach work out-of-the-box for higher-DoF agents (e.g., a 5-link swimmer/snake)? Please detail training stability, sample requirements, and the diversity of learned skills. For unstable agents (Ant), can you quantify failure due to switching and suggest mitigations?
4) Proxy reward design: To what extent is the method dependent on specific, hand-crafted proxy rewards (e.g., speed)? Can you provide guidance or examples for other domains (manipulation, non-geometric tasks) and discuss portability across tasks?
5) Choice of K: How is K selected in practice? Do some latent codes remain unused or collapse to similar behaviors? Do you have a criterion to merge/prune redundant skills?
6) End-to-end fine-tuning: Have you tried jointly fine-tuning the low-level skills and Manager (e.g., with straight-through Gumbel-Softmax)? Any preliminary evidence on benefits vs. stability?
7) Transfer and overfitting: Do skills overfit to the pretraining environment layout? How robust is transfer when downstream environments vary in scale, obstacles, or dynamics? Would mild domain randomization in pretraining help?
