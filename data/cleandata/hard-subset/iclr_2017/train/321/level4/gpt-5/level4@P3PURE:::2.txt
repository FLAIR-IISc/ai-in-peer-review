Summary and overall assessment
The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning approach based on stochastic neural networks (SNNs), introducing an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an intrinsic reward to encourage a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A notable and convincing result is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic and sparse rewards; this “smoothing” of the reward signal still fails to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ arguments about long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, one plausible contributing factor is the explicit diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training, which appears absent in the baseline. This perspective might inform improved baselines and suggests connections to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning, as in hierarchical REPS and option-critic. The visualizations are clear and helpful.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for option identifiability and its use as an intrinsic reward to learn diverse sub-policies.
- Strong empirical comparison showing that simply mixing intrinsic and sparse rewards does not suffice, underscoring the value of hierarchical structure and explicit diversity.
- Effective visualizations that illuminate the learned behaviors.

Concerns and suggestions
1) Limited diversification capacity in sub-policies:
- With the current conditioning mechanisms (concatenation or bilinear integration), the categorical variable primarily affects the first hidden layer, which restricts how much the sub-policies can truly differ.
- This is not an issue in the evaluated locomotion domains, where sub-policies naturally resemble each other, but it may become a bottleneck in tasks requiring substantially different behaviors across modes (e.g., manipulation tasks where the optimal behavior can vary dramatically with the initial state).
- It would be informative to evaluate on harder, non-locomotion domains where ideal sub-policies are more heterogeneous.

2) Constraints in the hierarchical policy:
- The manager is trained while sub-policies are fixed, and the sub-policy duration is also fixed. This setup requires the intrinsic rewards and the learned sub-policies to be strong enough to transfer directly to downstream tasks.
- Additional discussion and results on relaxing these constraints would strengthen the paper—ideally connecting to end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic), where both high-level and low-level components can adapt jointly.

3) Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards may be domain-specific:
- Given point (2), the intrinsic rewards end up being tailored to the downstream tasks to ensure the fixed sub-policies are useful, making them appear domain-specific or even supervised in effect. This seems hard to avoid under the current training protocol, but it would be helpful to discuss how general these intrinsic objectives are expected to be and how sensitive they are to task/domain shifts.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline in Section 7.3:
  - What is the intuition for why the strong baseline performs very poorly and fails to solve the task? I am assuming the “CoM maze reward” corresponds to this baseline.
  - Does this baseline account for the additional experience/interaction that the pre-trained sub-policies receive (i.e., is data or training time matched)?
  - To what extent might the lack of an explicit diversity requirement in the baseline explain its failure, beyond long-horizon credit assignment issues?
- Clarification on “multi-policy” vs. SNN: What exactly is “multi-policy,” and how does it differ from the proposed SNN approach?
- Figure 1 interpretation:
  - For concatenation, is it correct that the categorical variable effectively induces different biases into the first hidden layer?
  - For bilinear integration, is it correct that the categorical variable induces different weight matrices from the observation to the first hidden layer?

Overall, this is a promising direction with clear empirical value. Addressing the above concerns—especially expanding sub-policy expressivity, relaxing the fixed sub-policy and duration constraints, and probing generality beyond locomotion—would further strengthen the contribution.
