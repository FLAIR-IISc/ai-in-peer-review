This paper presents an interesting approach to hierarchical control, closely related to the work of Heess et al. The experimental results are strong and solve benchmarks that prior methods did not, though the analysis of these experiments is comparatively limited.

1) As other reviewers have noted, the term “intrinsic motivation” seems misapplied given its conventional meaning in RL. Pretraining locomotion by rewarding speed (or rewarding grasping for manipulation) is strongly task-oriented, and the pretraining tasks used here are similar in spirit to those in Heess et al. Please clarify the terminology and how it differs from common usage in the literature.

2) The mutual information regularization is elegant and generally effective, but it does not appear to help in the more complex mazes (1, 2, and 3). The paper notes this; could the authors provide an interpretation or additional analysis of why the benefit disappears in these settings?

3) The factorization between S_agent and S_rest should be specified clearly. Duan et al. define S_agent; for reproducibility, S_rest should also be explicitly defined in the paper. If this is already included, please point to the exact section or appendix.

4) An analysis of the agent’s switching behavior would be valuable. More broadly, additional policy analysis—e.g., common failure modes and the effect of the switching interval on performance—would strengthen the contribution.

Overall, the work demonstrates compelling empirical results on challenging benchmarks. Clearer terminology, a more explicit description of the state factorization, and deeper analysis of policy behavior and the mutual information regularizer’s limitations would substantially improve the paper.
