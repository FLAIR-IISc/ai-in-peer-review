Summary
The paper explores pre-training agents with intrinsic rewards to discover diverse skills that can later be reused for downstream tasks. The goal of minimizing task-specific engineering at pre-training time is timely and important. The empirical demonstrations are engaging and the approach appears simple to implement. However, key aspects of the method and its claims require clarification, and several experiments would strengthen the case for generality and robustness.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for unsupervised skill acquisition as a way to accelerate downstream learning.
- Simple, interpretable intrinsic objective that seems to produce recognizable behaviors.
- Promising qualitative results and initial transfer experiments.

Major concerns and questions

1) Failure modes and scalability of diversity
- Please articulate the main failure modes and provide diagnostic evidence. For instance:
  - Does the method suffer from mode collapse, where multiple skills converge to similar behaviors?
  - How sensitive is diversity to the number of skills, entropy/regularization coefficients, or pre-training budget?
  - Are there environments where the discovered behaviors become trivial (e.g., boundary hugging) or repetitive due to the intrinsic objective?
- Provide quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., state-occupancy entropy, coverage of distinct regions, pairwise distances or KL divergence between visitation distributions across skills) in addition to qualitative snapshots.
- Discuss how diversity scales with environmental complexity and horizon length. As the behavior space grows richer, what mechanisms prevent redundancy or premature convergence?

2) Clarification on Sec. 5.3 and discretization
- You write “let X be a random variable denoting the grid in which the agent is currently situated.” Is the state space discretized into a grid?
  - If yes, please justify the choice, explain how grid resolution was selected, and analyze sensitivity to this hyperparameter. Coarse grids can overestimate diversity; fine grids can make learning sparse and unstable.
  - What happens in continuous state spaces without discretization? Can the same intrinsic signal be computed with a learned density model or function approximator? If a classifier or density estimator is needed, what is the computational cost and stability trade-off relative to fixed grids?
  - If discretization is used only for evaluation, please make that explicit and describe the training signal separately.
- If discretization is integral to the method, the paper should discuss the bias-variance trade-off and show results across multiple grid granularities.

3) Generality across embodiments and dynamics
- The current experiments seem to focus on relatively low-DoF systems. How well does the approach scale to more complex embodiments (e.g., a 5-link swimmer instead of 2, or higher-DoF agents with contact dynamics)?
- Please either add experiments on higher-dimensional control tasks or provide a careful analysis:
  - How do training stability, sample complexity, and the diversity of learned skills change as state/action dimensionality grows?
  - Does the intrinsic reward remain informative in larger spaces, or does it become too sparse/diffuse?
- If full experiments are infeasible, a smaller-scale proxy (e.g., a more articulated swimmer or an ant-like agent) and ablations would still be informative.

4) Positioning vs. prior work and the “minimal domain knowledge” claim
- The paper contrasts its approach with pre-training that requires goal specification (e.g., Heess et al., 2016) and claims to rely only on intrinsic rewards with minimal domain knowledge. I find this claim too strong as stated. The intrinsic rewards here appear hand-crafted for a limited class of control tasks, and they rely on design choices (e.g., discretization, shaping terms, termination criteria) that encode domain knowledge.
- Please:
  - Clarify what is meant by “minimal domain knowledge,” and enumerate the engineering decisions required to instantiate the intrinsic reward in each environment.
  - Temper the claim or provide broader evidence that the same objective transfers across substantially different domains without task-specific tuning.
  - Include comparisons to other intrinsic-motivation baselines that also avoid explicit goal sets (e.g., prediction-error-based bonuses, count-based exploration, empowerment-style objectives), to isolate the benefits of your particular formulation.
  - Provide ablations for reward components and hyperparameters to show robustness.

Additional requests and suggestions
- Methodological clarity: Precisely define all random variables, distributions, and training objectives. For Sec. 5.3, specify whether X indexes a grid cell, a learned discrete code, or another abstraction; how it is estimated online; and how it interfaces with policy updates.
- Skill definition and compositionality: How are skills parameterized and selected during transfer? What is the temporal extent/termination condition? Can skills be composed to solve longer-horizon tasks, and how is credit assignment handled during fine-tuning?
- Evaluation protocol: Report both zero-shot performance (using pre-trained skills without fine-tuning) and fine-tuning speed on downstream tasks. Include multiple seeds and confidence intervals.
- Scaling and compute: Provide training curves, wall-clock compute, and sample budgets for pre-training. Discuss how these scale with environment complexity and number of skills.
- Sensitivity analyses: Show results varying the number of skills, pre-training duration, and (if applicable) grid resolution. Include at least one environment where discretization is impractical and demonstrate how the method adapts.

Minor comments
- Clarify notation in Sec. 5.3; “the grid in which the agent is currently situated” is ambiguous.
- If figures depict state coverage or trajectories, ensure consistent color maps and include scale bars/units.
- Include hyperparameters and implementation details in an appendix to support reproducibility.

Overall assessment
This is a promising and well-motivated direction with appealing qualitative results. That said, the paper’s central claims about minimal domain knowledge and generality would be more convincing with: (a) a clear account of discretization and its impact; (b) evaluations on more complex embodiments; (c) quantitative diversity metrics and robustness analyses; and (d) more balanced positioning against related intrinsic-motivation approaches. With these improvements, the work could make a strong contribution.
