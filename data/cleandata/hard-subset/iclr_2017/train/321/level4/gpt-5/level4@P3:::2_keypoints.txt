- Proposes hierarchical RL: pretrain a single SNN with categorical latents to learn diverse skills via a proxy reward, then train a high-level manager to sequence skills.  
- Encourages diversity with a mutual-information (MI) regularizer between latent code and task-relevant state subset, producing interpretable behaviors.  
- Manager commits to a selected skill for T steps (slower timescale), improving exploration for long-horizon sparse-reward tasks.  
- Positions method at intersection of intrinsic motivation, options, and multi-modal policies; uses SNNs with bilinear integration for shared representation and multi-modality.  
- MI bonus is simple, cheap, and empirically prevents mode collapse during skill pretraining.  
- Experiments show pretrained skills span diverse behaviors and that hierarchical control greatly expands exploration coverage.  
- Method outperforms strong baselines (including intrinsic-motivation and proxy-in-task baselines) on sparse mazes and a long-horizon gather task.  
- Visualizations and ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, with/without MI, seeds, switch times) support methodological claims.  
- Decision: Accept. Key reasons: clear, well-motivated method that yields diverse reusable skills and strong empirical gains.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation with strong baselines, informative ablations, sample-efficiency, and interpretable skills.  
- Addresses a significant RL challenge by reducing effective horizon via temporally extended skills and improved exploration without task-specific shaping.  
- Novelty: uses SNNs + bilinear integration + MI bonus, differing from goal-conditioned pretraining and option methods that need external signals.  
- Soundness: treating latent as observation allows TRPO use; MI estimated via discretized state counts; weight sharing enables sample-efficient learning of K skills.  
- Empirics detail: distinct locomotion behaviors per latent, random managers over skills cover more state space than Gaussian noise, bilinear+MI improve diversity and performance.  
- Suggest broadening domains beyond locomotion to test generality (e.g., manipulation, qualitatively different skills).  
- Recommend exploring adaptive hierarchies: end-to-end manager/skill training, learned termination/Gumbel-Softmax to handle state-dependent switching.  
- Discuss proxy reward design: CoM-speed is domain-specific; provide strategies and robustness analysis for imperfect proxies.  
- Address unstable agents (e.g., Ant) by pretraining with scheduled switching, transition policies, or regularizing for robustness at switch times.  
- Include comparisons to option-critic and HiREPS in discussion and ideally empirics; clarify differences from goal-conditioned/UVFA pretraining.  
- Report quantitative diversity metrics (MI over training, pairwise trajectory divergence) in addition to visitation plots.  
- Clarify baseline design: specify whether “CoM + task reward” baseline uses pretraining or is trained from scratch and whether sample budgets are matched.  
- Clarify multi-policy baseline: whether K independent policies are trained with same proxy reward and whether pretraining samples scale with K.  
- Diagnose why strong intrinsic baseline underperforms: quantify how lack of diversity (without MI) limits exploration/learning and provide diagnostics.  
- Confirm Fig.1 interpretation: concatenation changes first-layer bias by latent, bilinear changes the entire first-layer weight matrix per latent.  
- Report MI bonus sensitivity to α_H, discretization grid, and choice of state subset C; evaluate continuous density models for p(z|c).  
- Provide guidance on choosing hyperparameters K and T and report performance/stability variation across tasks of different sparsity/scale.  
- Test fine-tuning low-level skills during downstream training and report gains and effects on variance across seeds.  
- Report skill robustness: occurrence of mode collapse without MI, MI estimates, and inter-skill overlap metrics across seeds.  
- Provide matched compute/sample budgets for pretraining and downstream phases across compared methods for fair comparisons.
