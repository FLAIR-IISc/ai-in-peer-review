- Paper proposes hierarchical RL: pre-train diverse low-level SNN skills with a proxy reward and MI regularizer, then train a high-level manager to select skills for sparse-reward tasks.  
- SNN uses categorical latents with bilinear integration and an MI bonus between latent and part of the state to encourage diverse, interpretable skills.  
- Manager commits to a chosen skill for T steps to improve exploration and reduce effective horizon; training uses TRPO.  
- Empirically, method yields interpretable locomotion primitives, broader state coverage under random managers, and strong performance on sparse mazes and Gather, often outperforming baselines.  
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).  
- Reason 1: Strong empirical results on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks with clear ablations showing contributions of multimodality, bilinear integration, and MI.  
- Reason 2: Approach is simple, well-motivated, literature-aligned, and yields reusable, interpretable skills with apparent sample efficiency.  
- Problem & motivation: Two-phase proxy-reward pre-training plus hierarchical reuse addresses long-horizon sparse-reward exploration without heavy domain instrumentation.  
- Technical approach: SNN multimodality and bilinear latent-observation integration are appropriate and empirically important; MI regularizer is practical via count-based/M LP estimates; freezing low-level skills simplifies manager training.  
- Empirical support: Qualitative and quantitative evidence (visitation plots, maze/Gather results, ablations, multiple seeds) supports claims and avoids cherry-picking.  
- Limitations acknowledged: MI not always helpful; instability and switching issues for complex agents (e.g., Ant) are noted.  
- Terminology feedback: “Intrinsic motivation” is misleading—prefer “proxy reward” or “self-supervised pre-training signal.”  
- MI effects: Need deeper analysis why MI sometimes fails to help in easier mazes and whether MI-induced skills align with manager strategies.  
- Factorization clarity: Must explicitly specify Sagent vs Srest per environment and precisely state manager observations and any preprocessing differences between phases.  
- Switching analysis: Request more analysis on sensitivity to T, failure modes during switching, mitigation strategies, and whether manager observes current latent z.  
- Comparisons/baselines: Recommend additional comparisons to goal-conditioned or option-critic baselines and discussion versus Heess et al. under equal pre-training budgets.  
- Hyperparams/implementation: Ask for exact αH per experiment, MI discretization grid, how K is chosen, and metrics like entropy over z usage.  
- Question 1: List Srest for each environment and confirm manager’s observation set and any preprocessing differences.  
- Question 2: Report αH values used, and sensitivity of skills/downstream performance to αH and discretization granularity.  
- Question 3: For mazes where MI fails, show whether manager policies differ and whether some MI-induced skills are underutilized.  
- Question 4: How does performance scale with number of skills K and are there diminishing returns or added difficulty?  
- Question 5: Does the manager observe active skill z, and would providing z/short history improve switching?  
- Question 6: Provide quantitative failure rates during switches and any mitigation techniques (e.g., action ramping) for Swimmer/Snake.  
- Question 7: Report per-seed pre-trained SNN results and include per-seed performance tables to demonstrate robustness.
