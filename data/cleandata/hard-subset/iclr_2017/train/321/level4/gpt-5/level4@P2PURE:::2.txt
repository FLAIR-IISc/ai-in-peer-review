This paper proposes a hierarchical RL method using stochastic neural networks, introducing an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability as an auxiliary reward to encourage a diverse mixture of sub-policies. A notable result is the comparison with a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic and sparse rewards, showing that this ostensibly smoother reward signal still fails to solve the tasks. Beyond the authors’ points about long-term credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchical abstraction, another possible explanation is the explicit diversity requirement imposed during sub-policy training, which appears absent in the baseline. This perspective might inform improvements to the baseline and motivate new end-to-end hierarchical policy learning approaches, as in hierarchical REPS or option-critic. The visualizations are also compelling.

The paper is promising, and could be strengthened by addressing the following:

1) Limited diversification of sub-policies:
- Both concatenation and bilinear integration introduce only minimal differentiation among sub-policies via the first hidden layer’s parameters. This is acceptable for the evaluated tasks, which largely require similar locomotion behaviors with limited diversification, but the limitation may become pronounced in domains where the ideal sub-policies must differ substantially. It would be valuable to test on more challenging, non-locomotion domains where optimal sub-policies are less alike—for example, in manipulation, solving a task from one state can be very different from solving it from another.

2) Limitations of the hierarchical policy:
- The manager is trained while sub-policies are held fixed, and the duration of sub-policy execution is also fixed. This setup places a strong burden on the intrinsic rewards and the quality of the learned sub-policies to be sufficiently effective for downstream tasks. More discussion and/or results on how to handle such cases would be helpful, ideally connecting to fully end-to-end hierarchical policy learning.

3) Intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific or supervised:
- Given (2), this seems hard to avoid.

Some last-minute questions:
- What is the intuition for why the strong baseline in Section 7.3 performs so poorly and cannot solve the task? I am assuming the “CoM maze reward” is this baseline.
- Does the baseline also account for the additional experience accumulated by the pre-trained policies?
- What is “multi-policy,” and how does it differ from SNN?
- In Figure 1, is it correct that (1) concatenation means the categorical variable induces a different bias to the first hidden layer, and (2) bilinear integration means it induces a different weight matrix from the observation to the first hidden layer?
