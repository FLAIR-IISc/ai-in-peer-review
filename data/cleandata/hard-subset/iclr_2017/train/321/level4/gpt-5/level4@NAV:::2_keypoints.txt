- Proposes hierarchical RL with stochastic neural networks and an information-theoretic option-identifiability auxiliary reward to encourage diverse sub-policies.
- Strong empirical result: outperforms a baseline that combines intrinsic and sparse rewards, which fails despite a smoother signal.
- Enforced diversity during sub-policy training is a plausible reason the baseline fails; this insight could inform stronger baselines or end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS/option-critic).
- Visualizations are clear and helpful.
- Concern: concatenation and bilinear integration only allow modest sub-policy differentiation via the first hidden layer, which may be fine for locomotion but limiting for tasks needing very distinct behaviors.
- Suggestion: evaluate on more challenging, non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation) to test need for stronger diversification.
- Limitation: manager is trained with sub-policies frozen and fixed sub-policy durations, placing heavy burden on intrinsic rewards and pre-learned sub-policies.
- Request: more discussion or results on handling variable durations and end-to-end hierarchical training.
- Concern: intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific or effectively supervised given the frozen-subpolicy setup.
- Question: what is the intuition for why the strong baseline (CoM maze reward) performs poorly and cannot solve the task?
- Question: does that baseline also benefit from the extra experience obtained by the pre-trained policies?
- Question: what exactly is “multi-policy,” and how does it differ from SNN?
