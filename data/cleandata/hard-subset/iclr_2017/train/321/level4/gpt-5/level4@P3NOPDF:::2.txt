Summary
The paper proposes a hierarchical RL framework based on stochastic neural networks (SNNs) that encourages the learning of diverse sub-policies via an information-theoretic measure of option identifiability used as an intrinsic reward. A high-level manager selects among these sub-policies to solve downstream tasks. The paper includes a strong baseline that directly combines intrinsic and sparse task rewards; notably, this “smoother” reward fails to solve certain tasks, which is a valuable and somewhat surprising empirical result. The paper also presents clear visualizations that help interpret the learned behaviors.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for diversity-promoting intrinsic rewards: using an information-theoretic criterion to ensure option identifiability is well-justified and leads to interpretable sub-policies.
- Strong empirical comparison: the baseline that simply adds intrinsic to extrinsic rewards underperforms markedly, underscoring the value of hierarchical abstraction and the way diversity is enforced here.
- Practical contributions: the approach is straightforward to implement and the visualizations convincingly show behavior specialization.
- Potential for broader impact: the findings could inform improved baselines and end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., hierarchical REPS, option-critic), particularly around principled diversity induction.

Weaknesses and limitations
1) Limited diversification capacity in the sub-policy architecture:
- With the proposed concatenation and bilinear integrations, the categorical latent primarily modulates the first hidden layer (e.g., bias shift for concatenation; input weight modulation for bilinear). This can constrain the diversity expressible across sub-policies.
- In the tested locomotion domains, this may suffice because many skills share similar low-level dynamics. However, in domains where sub-policies must be qualitatively different (e.g., manipulation with distinct strategies from different initial states), this limitation could become more pronounced.

2) Fixed sub-policies and fixed temporal abstraction:
- The manager is trained after sub-policies are learned and kept fixed, and the sub-policy duration is also fixed. This places a heavy burden on the unsupervised pretraining stage to produce options that transfer well.
- In settings where the intrinsic reward is misaligned with the downstream task, downstream learning may be handicapped by the inability to adapt the sub-policies or their temporal extent.
- This motivates either joint/alternating training or fine-tuning of sub-policies during downstream learning, and potentially learning the option duration.

3) Domain specificity of intrinsic rewards:
- Because of (2), the choice and design of intrinsic rewards become crucial. In some domains, the proposed identifiability measure may correlate imperfectly with task-relevant structure, raising questions about generality and how to select or adapt intrinsic objectives.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Demonstrate on domains with more diverse, non-locomotion skills (e.g., manipulation or tasks requiring distinctly different strategies from different initial states). This would better test the expressive limits of the sub-policy parameterizations.
- Compare or connect more directly to end-to-end hierarchical methods (e.g., option-critic, hierarchical REPS), including:
  - Joint training or fine-tuning of options during downstream learning.
  - Learning or adapting option duration.
  - Reporting the stability and sample efficiency trade-offs in joint vs. staged training.
- Provide ablations/diagnostics on diversity:
  - Quantify how the identifiability objective affects sub-policy specialization (e.g., mutual information estimates, option usage entropy, state-space coverage).
  - Vary the strength of the identifiability reward and analyze downstream task performance and exploration benefits.
- Analyze the gating architecture:
  - Compare concatenation vs. bilinear integration in terms of expressivity and downstream performance.
  - Consider deeper modulation (e.g., FiLM-style conditioning or hypernetworks) to increase sub-policy diversity while retaining identifiability.
- Ensure a fair baseline:
  - Control for total environment interactions across methods.
  - If pretraining yields additional environment exposure for the hierarchical method, provide variants that equalize experience or report sample complexity explicitly.

Clarifications and questions for the authors
- Baseline in Section 7.3:
  - What is the primary reason the strong baseline (presumably “CoM maze reward”) fails so decisively? Beyond long-horizon credit assignment and the benefits of hierarchy, could the absence of an explicit diversity constraint be the key factor?
  - Does the baseline receive access to the same total environment interactions as the hierarchical method, including the pretraining phase? If not, please discuss how sample complexity differences might affect the comparison.
  - Please clarify “multi-policy” relative to the SNN-based approach. Is “multi-policy” an ensemble of independent policies without a shared latent, or does it share parameters differently than the SNN?
- Architectural interpretation (Figure 1):
  - For the concatenation variant, is it correct that the categorical latent effectively induces different biases into the first hidden layer (i.e., a learned embedding concatenated to observations)?
  - For the bilinear variant, does the latent select or modulate a distinct input weight matrix to the first hidden layer (i.e., multiplicative modulation)?
- Manager training and temporal abstraction:
  - Have you explored fine-tuning sub-policies or learning the option duration during downstream training? If so, how does it affect performance and stability?
  - How sensitive is the approach to mismatch between the identifiability objective and the structure of the downstream task?

Overall assessment
This is a solid and promising paper. The use of an information-theoretic identifiability objective to induce diverse sub-policies is well-motivated and empirically supported, and the negative result for the strong “intrinsic + extrinsic” baseline is both important and instructive. The primary limitations lie in the expressivity of the sub-policy modulation, the reliance on fixed options/durations, and the potential domain specificity of the intrinsic reward when transferring to downstream tasks.

I recommend acceptance after addressing the above points, especially clarifying the baselines and experience accounting, discussing (or demonstrating) applicability to more diverse domains, and outlining how the approach relates to or could be extended toward end-to-end hierarchical policy learning. The visualizations and empirical comparisons are strong, and the work has the potential to influence how we design intrinsic objectives and structure hierarchical RL systems.
