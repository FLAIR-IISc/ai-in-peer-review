1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper introduces a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first acquires a repertoire of temporally extended skills in a pre-training environment using only a proxy reward, and then reuses these skills via a learned high-level “manager” policy to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The low-level skill policy is implemented as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with categorical latent variables, enabling multi-modal action distributions and efficient weight sharing across skills. A mutual-information-based regularizer encourages diversity and interpretability of skills, with a bilinear integration of the latent code and observation shown to be especially effective. The method uses TRPO for optimization and treats the latent code as part of the observation. Empirically, the approach learns a broad set of locomotion skills and demonstrates substantially improved exploration and faster learning on challenging sparse-reward tasks (multiple maze variants and a Gather setting), outperforming baselines and showing favorable sample efficiency compared to training independent policies.

2. Decision
Accept. The main reasons are:
- Strong empirical results on hard sparse-reward benchmarks, showing consistent gains in exploration and learning speed over meaningful baselines.
- A clean, well-motivated integration of SNNs with an MI regularizer and a hierarchical manager, yielding diverse, interpretable skills with good sample efficiency.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper directly targets the well-known limitations of naive exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks by pretraining task-agnostic skills and imposing temporal abstraction at deployment. This is well aligned with the literature on hierarchical RL and intrinsic objectives.
- Relation to prior work: The setup is close in spirit to recent HRL approaches (e.g., Heess et al.), but here skills are learned with a single proxy reward without goal specifications, and diversity is encouraged via MI; the bilinear integration choice is a thoughtful architectural contribution with clear empirical impact.
- Technical soundness: Treating the latent as part of the observation keeps TRPO applicable, and the MI bonus is implemented in a simple, reproducible way via discretized CoM counts. The ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear, MI coefficients) and visitation analyses support the claims about skill diversity and interpretability.
- Empirical support: The method solves maze and gather tasks that are known to be difficult for standard exploration, showing that the learned skills materially improve exploration when composed by a high-level policy. The multi-seed reporting and inclusion of multiple pre-trained SNNs (without cherry-picking) strengthen the empirical evidence.
- Limitations: While the MI bonus generally improves skill spread and interpretability, it does not consistently translate to gains on the most complex mazes, and the analysis of when/why it helps could be deeper. The conceptual use of “intrinsic” motivation is somewhat broader than typical RL usage and could be clarified.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not central to the decision)
- Terminology: Consider clarifying the use of “intrinsic motivation.” Rewarding speed (or grasp success) during pre-training is a pragmatic proxy that is likely aligned with many downstream tasks; this is closer to a task-agnostic proxy reward than to intrinsic rewards in the sense of prediction error, novelty, or empowerment used in exploration literature.
- MI regularization: Provide more analysis on when the MI bonus improves downstream learning. For example, quantify diversity (e.g., MI estimates, coverage metrics) versus downstream returns across seeds and environments, and discuss why certain mazes see limited gains despite better skill diversity.
- State factorization: The assumption Sagent ⊕ Srest is central. For reproducibility, please explicitly list the exact components of Srest (e.g., walls, goal sensors, object positions, lidar features) for each environment, in addition to the Sagent defined by Duan et al.
- Switching behavior: Add diagnostics on switching, e.g., distributions of switch outcomes, failure cases at switch points, and how switch time T affects stability, exploration range, and returns. A short sensitivity analysis (or learned termination) would strengthen the story and help practitioners tune T.
- Sample accounting: Clarify sample complexity across baselines. The multi-policy baseline uses more pretraining samples; an apples-to-apples accounting (and perhaps a fixed-budget comparison) would make the efficiency gains of SNNs even clearer.
- Posterior estimation: Briefly justify the CoM-only posterior approximation for MI and discuss alternatives (e.g., learned density models) for higher-dimensional choices of c.

Questions for the authors
- Intrinsic/proxy reward: In what sense do you consider the pre-training reward “intrinsic”? Would you characterize it as a task-agnostic proxy, and how sensitive are results to the specific proxy chosen (e.g., speed vs. energy efficiency vs. heading-aligned progress)?
- MI impact: Why does the MI bonus improve skill diversity but not consistently improve returns in mazes 1–3? Is this due to the manager’s ability to reorient using walls, making additional turning/sideways skills less critical? Any quantitative evidence (e.g., skill usage statistics) supporting this interpretation?
- State factorization: Could you enumerate Srest explicitly for each task variant, to ensure replicability? Are there any cross-couplings between Sagent and Srest that materially affect training or performance?
- Switching analysis: How sensitive is performance to the switch time T across tasks? Do you observe specific failure modes at switch points (e.g., instability, dead-ends), and would a learned termination policy mitigate them?
- Architecture choices: How robust are results to the number of skills K and to the choice of bilinear versus concatenation? Do you have guidance on selecting K for a new domain?
- Manager inputs: Does the manager observe both Sagent and Srest in all tasks, and did you test managers that observe only Srest to enforce a cleaner separation of roles?
- MI estimation: Have you tried learning p(Z|C) with a parametric model instead of grid counts? If so, does it change skill diversity or stability?
- Generalization: Do skills learned with one proxy (e.g., speed) transfer to qualitatively different downstream objectives (e.g., precise target tracking) without retraining the low-level SNN?
