Summary
The paper tackles long-horizon, sparse-reward reinforcement learning by first learning a diverse set of low-level skills in a proxy pre-training environment and then training a high-level “manager” to select among these skills to solve downstream tasks. Skills are represented by a stochastic neural network (SNN) policy conditioned on discrete latent codes, with a mutual information (MI) bonus to promote behavioral diversity. A bilinear integration of state and latent code improves multimodality over simple concatenation. The learned skills are frozen, and a high-level policy selects a skill every T steps, enabling temporally coherent exploration. Experiments on Locomotion+Maze and Gather tasks show that (1) pre-trained SNN skills are interpretable and diverse, (2) hierarchical control substantially improves exploration and solves tasks that strong baselines fail on, and (3) MI regularization and bilinear integration often increase skill diversity and, in some settings, downstream performance. The approach is simple, sample-efficient relative to training K separate policies, and the code is available.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical results: the method solves challenging sparse-reward benchmarks where prior baselines struggle, with clear ablations demonstrating the value of SNNs and bilinear integration.
- A well-motivated, broadly applicable framework that combines unsupervised skill discovery with hierarchical control, with minimal instrumentation of downstream tasks.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse rewards and long horizons are a core pain point in deep RL; temporal abstraction and exploration via skills are principled remedies.
- Method soundness: SNNs naturally model multi-modal action distributions; conditioning on discrete latents with bilinear integration effectively partitions behavior space. The MI bonus encourages distinguishable, interpretable skills by reducing H(Z|C).
- Empirical support: Visitation plots and learning curves demonstrate diverse gait primitives and improved exploration; hierarchical control yields consistent gains, especially on Gather and some mazes. The sample-efficiency benefit of learning K skills in one SNN rather than K separate policies is compelling.
- Relation to prior work: The approach is close in spirit to Heess et al. (2016), but uses a single proxy reward rather than goal-conditioned pretraining; compared to Option-Critic and count-based/intrinsic-motivation baselines, it focuses on reusable temporal primitives with clear transfer benefits.

That said, several aspects limit the strength of the endorsement:
- The “intrinsic motivation” terminology is somewhat overloaded; the proxy rewards (e.g., velocity, grasp success) embed domain priors that align closely with downstream tasks. The paper notes minimal domain knowledge, but the choice of proxy reward is consequential.
- MI regularization improves skill diversity but does not consistently improve performance on the more complex mazes (1–3), and the paper offers limited analysis of when/why MI helps or hurts.
- The structural assumption S = Sagent × Srest underpins reuse, but Srest is not specified with enough precision for replication.
- Analysis of switching behavior is limited; more insight into switching time T, failure modes, and stability (notably for unstable agents like Ant) would strengthen the claims.

Feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Terminology: Prefer “proxy reward” or “unsupervised pre-training reward” over “intrinsic motivation” to avoid confusion with established RL usage.
- Clarity and replicability: Precisely define Sagent and Srest for each task (what state features go where), and report the exact inputs to the manager and low-level policies. Detail the discretization used for MI (grid resolution, smoothing), and provide sensitivity analyses for K, α_MI, and T.
- Analysis of MI: Explain why MI aids Gather and some mazes but not others (e.g., when sideways/turning skills matter vs. when wall-contact reorientation suffices). Quantify skill diversity (e.g., MI estimates, visitation overlap, pairwise trajectory divergence) and relate it to downstream returns.
- Switching and stability: Provide ablations on T and, if possible, a termination policy; discuss how switching interacts with agent stability (Ant), and whether a brief warm-up or transition policy mitigates falls.
- Adaptation: Report preliminary results or discussion on end-to-end fine-tuning of low-level skills via straight-through estimators (Gumbel-Softmax/Concrete); clarify whether limited adaptation would help in mazes that require infrequent but specific maneuvers.
- Scaling and architecture: Comment on bilinear integration cost with higher-dimensional observations; consider learned discriminators to estimate p(Z|C) instead of count grids; discuss continuous latents and the trade-offs.

Questions for the authors
- MI effect: Why does the MI bonus not improve performance in mazes 1–3? Is it because forward/backward primitives plus wall-based reorientation suffice? Any evidence that MI increases sideways/turning that are underutilized by the manager?
- State factorization: Precisely specify Sagent and Srest for all tasks. Are any Srest components leaked into the low-level policy during pre-training or only into the manager?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive is performance to K (number of skills), α_MI, grid resolution for MI, and T? Is there a regime where too many skills harms manager learning?
- Transfer and reuse: Beyond locomotion directionality, what aspects of the learned skills transfer (speed profiles, stability, turning)? Do manager policies reuse the same subset of skills across tasks?
- Switching: Did you try variable or learned termination? Does a shorter T improve precision in dense regions (e.g., small Gather) and a longer T improve exploration in sparse ones, as suggested by your Appendix?
- Estimation of p(Z|C): How robust is the count-based posterior to grid choices and non-stationary visitation? Did you consider a learned classifier to infer Z from C?
- Unstable agents: For Ant, can brief “handover” durations or overlap in execution mitigate falls when switching? Any preliminary success with joint training of manager and low-level policy?

Overall, the paper is well-motivated, empirically strong, and likely to be influential. Addressing the analysis and clarity points above would further solidify the contribution.
