## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning, coupled with an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity. The core idea – pre-training a diverse set of skills with a proxy reward and then utilizing these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks – is a promising approach to address sparse reward and long-horizon challenges in RL. The authors clearly articulate the motivation for combining intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, and the use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is well-justified.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate a clear benefit of the proposed method over existing techniques, particularly in challenging sparse reward environments. The results on the locomotion + maze and gather tasks are compelling and showcase the effectiveness of the SNN-based skill learning and hierarchical structure.

The paper provides strong support for its claims. The ablation studies and comparisons to existing methods (including a baseline with the proxy reward directly applied to the downstream tasks) convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed framework. The visualizations of learned skills (visitation plots) provide valuable insight into the diversity and interpretability of the SNN-learned behaviors. The authors also provide code and videos, enhancing reproducibility and understanding.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification and elaboration. While the results are strong, the impact of the bilinear integration within the SNN architecture isn’t fully explained – a more detailed discussion of why this integration is superior to simple concatenation would be helpful. Additionally, the discussion of the limitations regarding unstable robots (Ant) feels somewhat abrupt. Expanding on potential solutions or future research directions to address this instability would strengthen the paper. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the mutual information regularizer and why it was more effective than other diversity-promoting techniques?
*   What is the computational overhead associated with using SNNs compared to standard neural networks, and how does this impact scalability to more complex environments?
*   Regarding the Ant robot experiments, could you provide more details on the specific challenges encountered during skill switching and potential strategies for mitigating these issues?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the proxy reward in the pre-training environment? Would different proxy rewards lead to qualitatively different skill sets?
