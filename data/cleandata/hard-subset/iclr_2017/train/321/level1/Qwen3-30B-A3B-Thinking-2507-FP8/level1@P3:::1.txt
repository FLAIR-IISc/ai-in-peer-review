

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary

The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards and long horizons by first learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), then leveraging these skills for downstream tasks. The key technical innovation is the use of Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to learn a wide span of interpretable skills efficiently. The authors demonstrate that their approach learns diverse, interpretable skills, significantly enhances exploration in downstream tasks, and achieves better performance on challenging sparse-reward environments like Mazes and Gather tasks compared to baselines.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a significant problem in RL with a well-motivated and novel approach that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing a well-defined problem with clear motivation. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches: hierarchical RL methods typically require domain knowledge for skill specification, while intrinsic motivation methods don't provide transferable skills across tasks. Their proposed framework bridges this gap by learning skills with minimal supervision in a pre-training environment, then using them as reusable building blocks for downstream tasks.

The methodology is sound and well-justified. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is appropriate, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of different architectural choices (concatenation vs. bilinear integration). The information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information is a clever and well-motivated addition that effectively encourages diversity in the skills learned. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with clear ablation studies showing the impact of different design choices (e.g., bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI regularizer strength). The results on the Maze and Gather tasks are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines, including the strong baseline of using the same proxy reward in the downstream task.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with careful consideration of related work. The authors properly position their work relative to hierarchical RL (Parr & Russell, 1998; Sutton et al., 1999; Dietterich, 2000), intrinsic motivation (Schmidhuber, 1991; Houthooft et al., 2016), and skill discovery (Mnih et al., 2016; Daniel et al., 2013; Heess et al., 2016). The comparison to Heess et al. (2016) is particularly strong, highlighting their key innovation of using a single proxy reward rather than requiring goal specification.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, I offer the following suggestions to enhance it:

- The theoretical justification for the mutual information regularizer could be strengthened. While the empirical results support its effectiveness, a more formal argument connecting MI maximization to skill diversity and downstream performance would strengthen the contribution.

- The paper claims "uniform improvement across a wide range of downstream tasks" but the evaluation is limited to two specific environments (Maze and Gather). Expanding the evaluation to more diverse tasks would better support this claim.

- The limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the instability issues with the Ant robot (Section D.2), could be discussed more thoroughly. The authors should address why the framework struggles with unstable agents and what modifications might resolve this.

- The paper should clarify the computational cost of the MI regularizer, especially for larger state spaces. The current implementation uses a 2D grid for the CoM coordinates, but how would this scale to higher-dimensional state spaces?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper uses a 2D grid for computing mutual information (based on CoM coordinates). How would the approach scale to environments where the relevant state space is higher-dimensional (e.g., 3D robot navigation)? Would the computational cost become prohibitive?

2. In Section 7.3, the authors note that the MI regularizer doesn't always boost performance. Can you provide more detailed analysis on when the regularizer is beneficial versus when it's not, and what factors determine this?

3. The paper mentions that the hierarchical structure with SNNs outperforms the Multi-policy baseline despite using the same number of samples for pre-training. However, the Multi-policy baseline actually trains six separate policies (using six times more samples). How does the paper's approach achieve equivalent or better performance with the same number of samples?

4. The authors observe that the performance of the hierarchical approach depends strongly on the specific skills learned in pre-training (Section C.4). Given that the pre-training is unsupervised, how can one ensure that the learned skills will be useful for a particular downstream task without task-specific knowledge?
