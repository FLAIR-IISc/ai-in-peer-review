

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards and long horizons. The key contribution is a two-phase approach: first, learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and second, leveraging these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks. The authors introduce Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with bilinear integration and an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage skill diversity. The framework is evaluated on several robotics tasks (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) with sparse rewards, demonstrating that their approach learns a wider span of interpretable skills, provides better exploration, and achieves faster learning compared to baselines.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a compelling contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with well-motivated approach and scientifically rigorous empirical evaluation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses an important and well-motivated problem in reinforcement learning: the challenge of sparse rewards and long horizons. The authors clearly identify limitations in existing approaches (hand-engineered hierarchies requiring domain knowledge, intrinsic motivation methods lacking transfer across tasks) and position their work as a synthesis of these approaches.

The approach is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough review of related work on hierarchical RL, skill learning, and intrinsic motivation. The paper correctly identifies the gap they address and provides a clear comparison to relevant prior work.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The experiments cover multiple environments (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) and tasks (Mazes, Gather), with multiple random seeds (10 runs per curve) to demonstrate statistical significance. The authors provide detailed ablation studies on key components (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus with different αH), showing consistent improvements across different settings. The results clearly demonstrate that their approach learns a wider span of skills (as shown in visitation plots), provides better exploration (Fig. 5), and achieves faster learning in downstream tasks (Fig. 6) compared to baselines, including a strong intrinsic motivation baseline (VIME) and a hierarchical baseline with independently trained skills.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of computational cost, particularly how the MI bonus scales with the number of skills (K). The current implementation uses a grid-based approach for estimating the posterior, which may become computationally expensive for large K or high-dimensional state spaces.

- The paper mentions that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" (Section 7.3), but doesn't clearly explain when the bonus is most beneficial. A more detailed analysis of the conditions under which the MI bonus provides the most improvement would strengthen the paper.

- The connection between the MI bonus and skill diversity could be more theoretically grounded, even if not fully proven. While the empirical results support the approach, a brief theoretical justification would add depth to the contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the Ant experiments (Appendix D), the authors note that switching between skills is challenging due to instability. How might the framework be modified to handle unstable agents more robustly, and what would be the computational cost of such modifications?

2. The paper states that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" (Section 7.3). Can the authors elaborate on the specific conditions under which the MI bonus is most beneficial, and when it might not be necessary?

3. The authors use a fixed switch time T in their hierarchical policy. How would the framework adapt if the optimal switch time varied across different downstream tasks, and what would be the implications for the learning process?
