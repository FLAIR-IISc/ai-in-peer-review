

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons. The authors propose a two-phase approach: first, learning a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward signal that requires minimal domain knowledge; second, leveraging these skills to solve downstream tasks through a hierarchical policy architecture. The key technical contributions include: (1) using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing; (2) introducing an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage skill diversity; and (3) demonstrating that this approach significantly improves exploration and learning performance in sparse reward environments. The paper shows that their method effectively learns interpretable skills (e.g., forward/backward/sideways locomotion) and achieves superior performance compared to baselines across multiple benchmark tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing a well-motivated, sample-efficient framework for learning transferable skills with minimal supervision. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the problem is clearly identified and well-motivated within the literature; (2) the approach demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple challenging tasks; and (3) the method provides a principled way to learn diverse skills without requiring domain-specific knowledge for each downstream task.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a critical problem in reinforcement learning: the poor performance of standard algorithms in sparse reward settings. The authors provide a compelling motivation by positioning their work between two existing approaches: hierarchical RL (which requires domain knowledge) and intrinsic motivation (which lacks transferability). Their framework bridges these by learning skills in a pre-training environment with a single proxy reward, then using those skills for downstream tasks.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. The authors demonstrate that their SNN approach with mutual information regularization learns a significantly larger span of interpretable skills compared to standard methods (Fig. 4). The hierarchical use of these skills (Fig. 5) shows dramatically improved exploration in downstream tasks. Most importantly, the method consistently outperforms baselines on challenging sparse reward tasks (Fig. 6), including mazes and gather tasks where standard methods fail.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough related work section that clearly distinguishes their approach from prior work on skill learning. The comparison to Heess et al. (2016) is particularly relevant, as their method requires goal specification while the authors' approach uses a single proxy reward signal. The experiments with multiple robots (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) and different environments provide strong evidence for the generalizability of the approach.

The theoretical justification for the mutual information regularization is somewhat limited, but the empirical results sufficiently support the claim that it improves skill diversity. The paper acknowledges this limitation in the discussion and suggests future work on stronger theoretical foundations.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the theoretical basis for the mutual information regularization**: While the empirical results support the approach, a more detailed theoretical justification (e.g., how the MI bonus relates to exploration efficiency) would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Provide statistical significance analysis**: The paper reports average returns but doesn't include error bars or statistical significance tests. Adding confidence intervals or p-values would make the comparisons more robust.

3. **Detail the hyperparameter sensitivity**: The paper mentions that the MI coefficient αH affects performance (Fig. 4), but doesn't discuss how sensitive the approach is to this and other hyperparameters. A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.

4. **Expand the discussion of limitations**: The paper mentions issues with unstable robots (Ant) but doesn't fully explore how these limitations might affect broader applicability. A more detailed discussion of when the approach might fail would be helpful.

5. **Clarify the "sample efficiency" claim**: The paper claims "sample-efficient" learning, but doesn't provide clear evidence of sample complexity reduction compared to training separate policies. A direct comparison of sample usage would strengthen this claim.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the number of samples used to train a SNN is the same as a single feed-forward policy from Fig. 4(a)". Could you clarify exactly how the sample complexity compares when learning K skills? Specifically, is the total sample complexity for learning K skills approximately equal to the sample complexity for learning one skill (as the authors claim), or does it scale with K?

2. In Section 7.3, the paper states that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance." Can you elaborate on when the MI bonus is most beneficial, and whether there are specific task characteristics that make the MI bonus particularly helpful or detrimental?

3. The paper uses a fixed switch time T in the hierarchical architecture. How sensitive is the approach to this choice, and what would be a principled way to determine T for new tasks? The paper mentions this in Section C.3, but a more formal discussion would be valuable.

4. The paper mentions that "the span of skills learned can be greatly improved by using Stochastic Neural Networks as policies." Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the representational capacity of SNNs versus other approaches for multi-modal policy learning?

5. In Appendix D.2, the paper describes failure modes with the Ant robot. How might the approach be modified to handle unstable robots more robustly, and what would be the computational cost of such modifications?
