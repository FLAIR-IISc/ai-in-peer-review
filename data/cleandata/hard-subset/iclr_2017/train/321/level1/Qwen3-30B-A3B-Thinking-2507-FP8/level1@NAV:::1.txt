

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards and long horizons in RL tasks. The authors introduce a method to learn a diverse span of interpretable skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, then leverage these skills for efficient downstream task learning. The key contribution is a general approach that requires minimal domain knowledge for skill acquisition through a single proxy reward, enabling transfer across multiple tasks without task-specific reward engineering.

I recommend acceptance. The paper clearly identifies the gap between hierarchical methods (requiring domain knowledge) and intrinsic motivation (lacking transferability), and provides a well-motivated solution. The empirical results are strong and scientifically rigorous: the method consistently outperforms baselines across multiple environments (Swimmer, Snake, Ant robots) on challenging sparse-reward tasks like mazes and object gathering. The ablation studies demonstrate the importance of bilinear integration and the MI regularizer, and the authors properly acknowledge limitations (e.g., instability with Ant robot).

The framework's key strengths are its sample efficiency (learning multiple skills simultaneously with SNNs) and interpretability (clearly mapping latent codes to distinct behaviors). The pre-training phase with minimal supervision is particularly compelling for real-world applications where reward engineering is difficult.

For improvement, the authors should clarify the relationship between their MI regularizer and prior work (e.g., InfoGAN) in the related work section. Additionally, they should provide more detail on how the proxy reward is designed for different domains in the main text (not just the appendix).

Questions for authors: How would you adapt this framework for continuous task distributions where the optimal skills vary smoothly rather than discretely? What are the theoretical guarantees regarding the diversity of skills learned with the MI regularizer?
