

# Review for "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses challenges in sparse reward and long-horizon tasks. The core contribution is a method to learn a diverse set of interpretable skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer. The approach requires only a simple proxy reward (e.g., robot speed for locomotion) during pre-training, which needs minimal domain knowledge about downstream tasks. These learned skills are then leveraged in a hierarchical architecture to improve exploration and learning in downstream tasks. The authors demonstrate that their method effectively learns a wide span of skills, significantly outperforms baselines on Maze navigation and Gather tasks, and provides interpretable skill representations.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning that is well-motivated and scientifically rigorous. The authors clearly articulate the problem: existing hierarchical methods require domain-specific knowledge for skill specification, while intrinsic motivation approaches don't enable knowledge transfer across tasks. Their solution bridges these gaps by using a single proxy reward for pre-training that requires minimal domain knowledge, then reusing the learned skills across multiple downstream tasks.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough review that situates their work between hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation approaches. The use of SNNs with a mutual information regularizer is novel and well-justified, addressing the key challenge of ensuring diverse skill learning. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with multiple ablation studies (bilinear vs. concatenation integration, MI bonus effect), careful visualization of learned skills (visitation plots), and evaluation across multiple tasks (Maze and Gather). The authors also acknowledge limitations (e.g., instability with Ant robot) and discuss future work thoughtfully.

The most compelling evidence is the demonstration that their method consistently learns a wide span of interpretable skills (Fig. 4) that significantly improve exploration (Fig. 5), leading to better performance on sparse reward tasks (Fig. 6). The pre-training approach with SNNs reduces sample complexity by a factor equal to the number of skills learned, addressing a key efficiency concern in hierarchical RL.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more formal theoretical justification for why the MI regularizer effectively encourages diverse skill learning. While the empirical results support this, a brief theoretical argument would strengthen the paper.
- The discussion of "minimal domain knowledge" could be more concrete. Providing specific examples of what this minimal knowledge entails for different task types (e.g., navigation vs. manipulation) would help clarify this important claim.
- The paper mentions end-to-end training as future work but doesn't discuss potential benefits or challenges. A brief analysis of how joint optimization might affect performance would be valuable.
- The analysis of the impact of switch time T (Sec. C.3) is good, but the paper could explicitly state whether T should be tuned for specific task types.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper claims the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge." Could you provide specific examples of what this minimal knowledge entails for different task domains (e.g., navigation tasks vs. manipulation tasks), and how this compares to the domain knowledge required by prior approaches?
- In the experiments, the MI bonus (αH = 0.01) improves skill diversity in pre-training but doesn't always boost downstream performance (Fig. 6). Could you provide more insight into why this occurs, and what factors determine whether the MI bonus is beneficial for downstream tasks?
- The paper reports failure modes with the unstable Ant robot (Sec. D.2). Could you elaborate on why this particular robot is challenging for the framework, and what specific modifications would be needed to handle such unstable agents?
