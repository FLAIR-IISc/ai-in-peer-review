1) Summary of what the paper claims to do/contribute
- Addresses sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by first learning a diverse set of reusable skills in a proxy pre-training environment and then learning high-level task policies that select among these skills.
- Uses stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with discrete latent codes to represent multi-modal, skill-conditioned policies with weight sharing. Introduces bilinear integration of observation and latent code to increase expressivity over simple concatenation.
- Encourages diversity via an information-theoretic regularizer: a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of state variables (e.g., CoM position), computed with simple on-policy posterior estimates via discretization.
- Demonstrates that SNN-based pre-training produces interpretable, temporally coherent skills that substantially improve exploration and downstream learning on sparse tasks (locomotion mazes, gather) against strong baselines, with code/videos provided.
- Provides ablations on integration method and MI strength, and reports results across multiple seeds and agents (Swimmer, Snake), with a discussion of failure modes on Ant.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Well-motivated and simple framework that combines intrinsic pre-training and hierarchy to address sparse rewards, with a clear and novel use of SNNs plus an MI bonus to learn diverse skills.
- Strong empirical evidence that learned skills improve exploration and performance on challenging sparse tasks, along with informative ablations and open-source code.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse rewards and long horizons are central RL challenges. Hierarchical policies and intrinsic motivation are natural remedies; this paper bridges them by learning reusable skills off a task-agnostic proxy reward, reducing downstream sample complexity.
- Method clarity and placement: The approach is conceptually clean: pre-train a latent-conditioned SNN policy with a proxy reward and MI diversity, then freeze it and train a manager over skills. It sits well among HRL (options, skill discovery) and intrinsic motivation, and the MI regularizer is well-motivated by InfoGAN-style objectives. The contrast to goal-conditioned pre-training (e.g., Heess et al. 2016) is appropriate; here no goals are needed, just a weak proxy reward and a simple choice of state subset for MI.
- Technical choices: Treating the latent as input avoids intractable mixture marginals in TRPO. Bilinear integration meaningfully enlarges the set of behaviors compared to concatenation. Sampling a latent per rollout yields temporally coherent skills. The MI posterior estimation by discretized visitation counts is simple and effective.
- Empirical support:
  - Skill diversity: Visitation plots show interpretable, distinct locomotion skills; MI and bilinear integration reduce mode overlap and increase coverage. Training a single SNN achieves diversity at the cost of one policy, not K, improving pre-training sample efficiency.
  - Exploration: Random manager rollouts cover much larger areas than Gaussian action noise and than collections of independently trained policies, consistent with richer skills.
  - Downstream performance: On multiple mazes and gather tasks, hierarchical policies over SNN skills outperform strong baselines (including adding the proxy reward during task learning) and previously reported intrinsically motivated exploration (VIME) in benchmark settings. Ablations show bilinear > concat, and MI helps especially where turning/sideways motion is valuable (gather). Results are reported across seeds and different pre-trained SNNs, with honest discussion of variance.
- Limitations acknowledged but reasonable: Fixed switch period T and frozen skills; high variance across pre-trained seeds on some tasks; instability on Ant when switching skills. These are typical for HRL and do not undermine the main contributions.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Quantify diversity: Report an explicit diversity metric across skills (e.g., achieved MI, pairwise trajectory divergence, coverage area) to complement visitation plots, and correlate with downstream performance.
- Sample efficiency accounting: Provide end-to-end sample counts (pre-training + downstream) and compare against training from scratch and multi-policy baselines to substantiate the claimed uniform sample efficiency gains.
- Sensitivity analyses: Expand ablations on K (number of skills) and MI coefficient; include more discussion on how K is chosen and how redundancy or collapse manifests when K is large.
- MI estimation: Consider a variational lower bound with a learned discriminator/posterior (InfoGAN-style) instead of discretization for higher-dimensional C, and discuss robustness to the choice of state subset C. Report negative results or guidance for domains where C is less obvious than CoM.
- Stronger baselines: Where feasible, compare to option-critic, feudal architectures, or goal-conditioned policies trained with hindsight/UVFA (as of the time) to further situate the gains.
- Transition robustness: For unstable agents (Ant), explore skill blending/smoothing at switch time, short warm-up phases, or robust fine-tuning of skills under off-distribution states to mitigate falls. Learning a termination/transition policy could also help.
- Manager improvements: Investigate a recurrent manager and/or learned termination. You partially analyze T; adding adaptive termination could reduce variance and improve responsiveness.
- Generality beyond locomotion: Provide at least a small-scale manipulation pre-training example with a different proxy reward and state subset C to support claims of minimal domain knowledge.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of the state subset C used in the MI bonus? In domains without a natural 2D CoM, what guidance can you offer for choosing C, and did you try full-state MI with a learned posterior?
- How do you select K in practice? What happens when K is overestimated—do you observe unused skills, and does this harm learning (e.g., via manager exploration dilution)?
- Can you report total sample counts (pre-training plus downstream) and wall-clock training time relative to training K separate policies (multi-policy) and a from-scratch baseline?
- Did you try entropy regularization on the manager’s skill distribution and/or a usage prior to avoid premature collapse to a few skills?
- How does the approach behave with much shorter switching periods T on the Swimmer tasks? You provide an analysis for Snake; analogous results for Swimmer would help.
- For Ant, did you try interpolation between old and new latent codes during a short transition window, or conditioning skills on the previous skill to improve stability?
- Is the per-z policy unimodal in practice? How would this compare to a mixture-of-experts policy with a learned gating network trained end-to-end without freezing?
- Could the skills be fine-tuned with a small KL constraint during downstream learning to maintain diversity while adapting, and do you expect this to reduce variance across seeds?
