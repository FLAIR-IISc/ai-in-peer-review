Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a two-phase framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning on sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks. Phase 1 pretrains a policy to discover a diverse set of skills in a proxy environment via a single proxy reward (e.g., speed), using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a discrete latent variable z to induce multimodality and weight sharing across skills. A key design is a bilinear integration of z with observations to increase coupling between latent and state. Diversity is further encouraged by an information-theoretic regularizer: a mutual information (MI) bonus between z and a selected subset of state variables (e.g., center-of-mass (CoM) position), estimated via discretized visitation counts. Phase 2 freezes the low-level SNN and trains a high-level “Manager” that selects a latent code every T steps to form a temporally extended action. Using TRPO for both phases, the authors show: (i) SNNs with bilinear integration and MI bonus reliably yield interpretable, distinct locomotion skills; (ii) hierarchical control over these skills greatly improves exploration; and (iii) this leads to strong performance on Swimmer/Snake Maze and Gather tasks with sparse rewards. Ablations cover concatenation vs bilinear integration, effect of the MI coefficient, switching period T, and multi-policy baselines.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) The combination of SNN-based multimodal policies with an MI bonus over a targeted state subset is simple, well-motivated, and empirically effective for skill discovery; (2) the hierarchy demonstrably improves exploration and achieves better learning on sparse tasks, with informative ablations clarifying which design choices matter.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work targets a central open issue in deep RL—exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon settings—by leveraging reusable skills learned without task-specific rewards. The “single proxy reward + MI for diversity” is a pragmatic alternative to goal-conditioned pretraining, reducing domain knowledge required. The hierarchical design (temporal commitment to a skill) is well aligned with the credit assignment and exploration bottlenecks.

- Positioning w.r.t. literature: The approach sits at the intersection of HRL (options/skills), intrinsic motivation, and multimodal policy modeling. It is distinct from Option-Critic (learns options end-to-end from task reward) and from methods relying on demonstrations or bottleneck discovery. Compared to concurrent pretraining approaches (e.g., Heess et al., 2016), it reduces instrumentation by using one proxy reward and MI rather than a set of goals. The use of a discrete latent SNN with bilinear integration to realize skill families is a concrete architectural contribution. The MI objective inspired by InfoGAN is adapted to RL via online count-based estimation over a state subset—simple and effective.

- Methodological soundness: Treating z as part of the observation restores tractability for TRPO. The MI bonus I(Z;C) is approximated by minimizing H(Z|C), estimated via discretized CoM counts collected on-policy; while coarse, the estimator is stable and computationally cheap. Sampling z per episode promotes temporally coherent skills. The hierarchical Manager uses categorical actions over K skills and commits for T steps—standard and justified. The paper is careful to separate pretraining and downstream phases and provides code.

- Empirical support: The ablations convincingly show: (i) bilinear integration > concatenation for inducing distinct skills; (ii) MI bonus reduces mode overlap and yields more diverse locomotion (e.g., sideways/turning); (iii) exploration coverage improves dramatically under hierarchical control; (iv) downstream learning on sparse mazes and Gather improves vs strong proxy-reward baselines and vs multi-policy (which is more sample-hungry in pretraining). Sensitivity analyses for T, and transfer to a more complex agent (Snake), add credibility. The Ant case study highlights current limitations (instability upon switching).

- Limitations: Evaluation is confined to locomotion-like tasks in MuJoCo. The MI bonus relies on privileged, shaped state subsets (CoM xy), which may be unavailable or less indicative for other domains (manipulation, vision). Some baselines are missing or only partially covered (e.g., Option-Critic, macro-action learning), and fairness in total sample counts (pretrain + downstream) could be quantified more explicitly. The approach freezes low-level skills and uses fixed T; although the paper discusses end-to-end training and learned termination as future work, this limits adaptivity. Success depends on careful selection of MI target variables and discretization.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines: Include comparisons to Option-Critic and to end-to-end macro-action methods (e.g., Mnih et al., 2016) on the same sparse tasks. Where possible, compare against training K separate policies with explicit diversity regularizers (e.g., mutual exclusion or diversity via state-visitation distance) to isolate the benefit of SNN weight sharing.

- Sample accounting: Report total sample budgets (pretraining + downstream) and net gains over training from scratch under equal total samples. Since the value proposition is “pretrain once, reuse many,” show cumulative savings across multiple downstream tasks.

- MI estimation: Provide sensitivity to grid resolution, α_H, and the choice of state subset C. Consider continuous-density estimators or learned discriminators for p(z|c) to reduce discretization artifacts in higher dimensions.

- Robustness and generalization: Show results with varying initial orientations and start positions to test whether skills encode absolute vs body-centric directions. Discuss or demonstrate transfer to qualitatively different downstream tasks (e.g., manipulation with object interactions).

- Adaptivity: Explore learned termination and/or recurrent Managers; report whether end-to-end fine-tuning of low-level skills materially improves performance or stability for unstable agents (Ant).

- Analysis: Quantify achieved MI and inter-skill diversity metrics (state/action occupancy overlap, KL between skill-conditioned policies). Clarify how K affects diversity, learning stability, and Manager’s performance.

Questions for the authors
1) MI target and estimation: How sensitive are results to the choice of C and discretization? Have you tried learning p(z|c) with a classifier instead of counts? Do you have curves of I(Z;C) over training?

2) Sample efficiency: What is the exact pretraining cost (in environment steps) relative to training one unimodal policy? How does total cost amortize when using the same SNN across the four mazes and Gather?

3) Choice of K and α_H: How were these selected? What happens when K is larger than the “natural” number of distinct skills? Do redundant modes collapse or split the data?

4) Orientation and invariance: If the agent’s initial heading is randomized, do the learned skills still cover diverse directions? Is the diversity mostly tied to world-frame or body-frame movement?

5) Manager design: Did you try learned termination/variable T or recurrent Managers? Any signs of over-commitment or oscillatory switching with different T?

6) Beyond locomotion: For manipulation, which state subsets would you target for MI? Have you tried vision-only observations, where CoM xy is not directly available?

7) Unstable agents: For Ant, did per-skill state initialization (e.g., skill-specific warm starts) or gradual mixing during switching reduce falls? Any success with joint fine-tuning to mitigate distribution shift at switch time?
