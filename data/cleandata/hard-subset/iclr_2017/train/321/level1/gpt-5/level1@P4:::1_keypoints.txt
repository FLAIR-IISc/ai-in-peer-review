- Two-phase HRL: pretrain multi-skill low-level SNNs with a proxy reward and MI regularizer, then train a high-level Manager to select skills at fixed timescale.  
- Key techniques: bilinear integration for z-conditioning, MI bonus I(Z;C) estimated via batch counts on CoM, and treating z as part of observation to keep TRPO valid.  
- Empirical claim: learns diverse locomotion skills, greatly improves exploration, and yields faster, more successful learning on sparse tasks (mazes, gather); transfers to Swimmer/Snake; Ant has stability limits.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons: technically sound, simple, well-motivated unification of skill discovery and hierarchical control; consistent empirical gains with clear ablations.  
- Clarity: method, MI estimation, training details, pseudocode, hyperparameters, and code/videos are provided; bilinear vs concat studied.  
- SNN/TRPO soundness: SNN parametrization and treating z as observation preserve TRPO tractability.  
- MI bonus soundness: uniform skill prior + per-batch count-based p(z|c) is an appropriate empirical MI shaping term; using CoM yields interpretable skills.  
- Hierarchical design: fixed commit time T and discrete skill selection is a standard, stable choice for sparse tasks; sensitivity to T is analyzed.  
- Pretraining evidence: visitation plots and ablations show diverse, interpretable directional/gait skills; bilinear > concatenation.  
- Sample-efficiency claim: one SNN with K skills plausibly cheaper than K independent policies (claimed, not fully quantified).  
- Exploration evidence: random Manager rollouts with SNN skills achieve much larger coverage than Gaussian-noise or independent-policy baselines.  
- Downstream performance: hierarchical policies with MI-regularized pretraining learn reliably/faster across multiple seeds; variance and multiple pretrained SNNs reported.  
- Transfer: method scales to Snake; Ant failures are candidly analyzed with suggested mitigations.  
- Novelty: meaningful combination of SNN-conditioned skills, bilinear integration, MI on informative state subset, and a simple Manager—distinct from goal-conditioned pretraining.  
- Suggestion: add quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., mutual information estimates, occupancy divergence, coverage area) alongside visitation plots.  
- Suggestion: broaden baselines to include option-critic, feudal methods, and other skill-discovery approaches where feasible.  
- Suggestion: report absolute sample budgets to substantiate the “K×” pretraining efficiency claim.  
- Suggestion: include sensitivity analyses for K, MI grid resolution, MI coefficient αH, and commit time T in the main text.  
- Suggestion: explore other proxy rewards (energy, contact diversity) and discuss their impact on learned skills and transfer.  
- Suggestion: for Ant, try learned terminations, short warm-up rollouts when switching, or switching-aware regularization to reduce falls.  
- Q1: Provide quantitative diversity measures (e.g., estimated I(Z;C), coverage) and correlate them with downstream performance.  
- Q2: How does performance scale with number of skills K; is there diminishing returns or interference?  
- Q3: How sensitive is MI estimation to CoM grid resolution and choice of C; have higher-dimensional C or function-approximation estimators been tried?  
- Q4: Report absolute pretraining and downstream sample counts across methods; does pretraining overfit to the proxy reward?  
- Q5: How sensitive is Manager training to T and initial skill distribution; would learned terminations or entropy regularization improve stability?  
- Q6: Have you attempted direct comparisons to option-critic or goal-conditioned pretraining (e.g., Heess et al., 2016) in these environments?  
- Q7: Can the pretrained skills transfer to richer tasks (narrow passages, moving goals) or partial observability?  
- Q8: Do end-to-end fine-tuning experiments with straight-through estimators for z help reduce variance or improve performance; what optimization issues arise?
