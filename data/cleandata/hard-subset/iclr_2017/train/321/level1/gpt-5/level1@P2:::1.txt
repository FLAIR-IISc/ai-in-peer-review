Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles exploration and sample-efficiency in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by (i) pretraining a diverse set of reusable low-level skills with only a simple proxy reward and (ii) training a high-level “Manager” to select among these skills in downstream tasks. The approach aims to combine the benefits of hierarchical abstraction and intrinsic motivation without hand-coding options per task.
- It models the low-level policy as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with a discrete latent code z. A bilinear integration of observation and z increases expressivity and enables multi-modal, interpretable behaviors (different z yield different gaits/directions).
- To avoid mode collapse and encourage diversity, it adds a mutual-information (MI)-based bonus between z and a subset of state variables (e.g., CoM (x,y)). The MI term is implemented via a simple empirical conditional entropy penalty using discretized visitation counts.
- A high-level policy operates over these skills, committing to a selected skill for T steps. Training uses TRPO throughout. The methodology is simple to implement, and code/videos are provided.
- Experiments on Swimmer and Snake in Maze and Gather tasks show: (a) SNN+MI learns a wide span of interpretable skills; (b) hierarchical use of pre-trained skills substantially expands effective exploration; (c) on sparse downstream tasks, the approach markedly outperforms strong baselines (including intrinsic proxy reward in-task), with ablations showing the importance of bilinear integration and (often) MI.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Clear, important problem with a simple, well-motivated, and novel combination (SNN skills + MI regularization + hierarchical control) that addresses sparse-reward exploration; (2) Careful empirical evidence and ablations showing consistent gains and interpretability, with publicly released code.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled?
- The paper addresses sample-efficient learning in sparse-reward, long-horizon continuous-control tasks by reusing a set of skills learned once in a proxy pretraining environment. This directly targets the exploration bottleneck (naive noise is ineffective and scales poorly with horizon).

2) Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
- Hierarchical RL reduces effective horizon but usually needs hand-engineered options; intrinsic motivation helps exploration but lacks reuse across tasks. The authors propose to learn a multi-modal skill policy using SNNs (to share parameters and avoid per-skill training cost) and to induce diversity via an MI-based bonus akin to InfoGAN. The factorization assumption (agent-state shared across tasks) is standard and reasonable for locomotion/manipulation.
- Related work is adequately discussed: options/HiREPS/option-critic; intrinsic motivation (VIME, count-based); prior multi-skill pretraining that requires goal labels. The paper’s proxy-reward-only pretraining and the simple MI regularizer are a distinctive, pragmatic contribution.

3) Do the results support the claims and are they rigorous?
- The paper convincingly shows: (a) the SNN with bilinear integration and MI learns diverse skills (visitation plots per z, clear qualitative differences); (b) hierarchical controllers using these skills explore much farther than Gaussian action noise or single-policy baselines; (c) downstream performance improves substantially on multiple mazes and the Gather task, with multiple seeds and no cherry-picking of pre-trained policies.
- Ablations isolate the roles of concatenation vs bilinear integration and the MI coefficient; they report variance across pretrains and discuss failure modes (e.g., unstable Ant, sensitivity of mazes to presence of backward gait).
- While there is no theoretical analysis of sample complexity or MI estimation bias, empirical methodology is careful, hyperparameters are documented, and batchwise MI estimation is transparent. The scope of environments (Swimmer/Snake; Mazes/Gather) is reasonable for ICLR 2017 and matches prior benchmarks.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify sample-efficiency accounting: report total environment interactions including pretraining, and compare to learning from scratch on each task (even if infeasible) to quantify amortization benefits when many tasks are considered.
- Sensitivity analyses: (1) number of skills K; how does performance and skill diversity scale with K? Are redundant/unused skills common? (2) MI coefficient αH; provide curves showing diversity/performance vs αH and failure modes when it’s too small/large. (3) Discretization granularity for CoM; does it materially affect skill separation?
- Choice of state subset C for MI: currently handpicked (CoM). Discuss generalization to other domains (manipulation) and whether C can be learned (e.g., via a discriminator or information bottleneck) to reduce manual instrumentation.
- Manager policy details: input features, commitment time T, and sensitivity to T are partly in the appendix; consider adding main-text guidance and an adaptive termination mechanism (learned option termination) to improve robustness in dynamic tasks.
- Comparisons: include or discuss results vs option-critic and HiREPS on overlapping domains if feasible; or explain incompatibilities. For intrinsic motivation baselines, ensure matched budgets and elaborate on why VIME underperforms here.
- MI estimation: the current approach is an empirical conditional entropy on a discretized space. Consider reporting NMI or alternative MI lower bounds (e.g., InfoNCE-style) and discuss stability/training-time overheads.
- Stability with unstable robots: the Ant analysis is valuable; adding a simple safety/transition policy or pretraining with skill switches could mitigate falls. Briefly report preliminary success/failure rates to quantify the issue.

Questions for the authors
- How many downstream tasks are needed to “break even” on pretraining cost? Can you provide a rough amortization analysis?
- How does varying K (e.g., 4, 8, 16) affect (i) skill diversity, (ii) manager learning speed, and (iii) final performance? Do you observe mode collapse or unused skills for large K?
- Can the MI be computed on features learned end-to-end (e.g., a small encoder of Sagent) rather than handpicked coordinates, and does it still yield interpretable skills?
- How sensitive is performance to the bilinear integration vs more general multiplicative interactions? Did you try FiLM-like conditioning or low-rank bilinear forms?
- When low-level policies are frozen, do you observe systematic suboptimality in tasks that require skills not present in the repertoire? If so, how effective is fine-tuning the SNN jointly with the Manager?
- For Mazes, performance depends on presence of backward/turning gaits. Could you add a simple diversity coverage metric during pretraining to ensure coverage of canonical directions and reduce variance across seeds?
