Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a two-phase framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning in sparse-reward, long-horizon domains: (i) pretrain a multi-skill low-level policy using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) conditioned on a discrete latent code and a proxy reward, with an information-theoretic mutual information (MI) regularizer to promote diverse, interpretable skills; (ii) learn a high-level “Manager” that selects skills at a fixed timescale to solve downstream tasks.
- Key technical elements: (a) conditioning the policy on latent variables via bilinear integration (shown to be more effective than simple concatenation); (b) an MI bonus I(Z; C) between the latent and a subset of the state (center-of-mass (CoM) position), implemented via a tractable, batch-based count estimator; (c) treating z as part of the observation to retain TRPO applicability.
- Empirically, the approach learns a broad set of distinct locomotion skills in a simple pretraining environment with a single proxy reward (speed), improves exploration substantially, and yields higher success and faster learning on sparse downstream tasks (mazes and gather) compared to baselines (including intrinsic CoM-speed reward and VIME in the benchmark setting). The method transfers across agents (Swimmer, Snake), and limitations on unstable agents (Ant) are analyzed.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Technically sound, simple, and well-motivated framework that unifies skill discovery and hierarchical control using SNNs with an MI regularizer; (2) Consistent empirical benefits on challenging sparse tasks, with clear ablations (integration type, MI coefficient) and analysis supporting the claims.

Supporting arguments
- Clarity: The method is clearly described, including the pretraining procedure, MI estimation (via discretized CoM counts, with uniform skill prior and per-batch aggregation), training details (TRPO, z fixed per rollout), and hierarchical execution (Manager sampling z every T steps). Algorithm pseudocode and hyperparameters are provided; code and videos are linked. The architectural choice (bilinear vs concatenation) and its impact are explicitly studied.
- Technical soundness: 
  - The SNN parametrization is standard; conditioning on z and treating it as part of the observation preserves tractability with TRPO. 
  - The MI bonus is a well-defined shaping term for pretraining: with uniform sampling over skills and consistent batching, the count-based estimator for p(z|c) is an appropriate empirical approximation. The use of a subset of the state (CoM) is justified by the locomotion focus and yields interpretable directional skills.
  - The hierarchical control design (fixed commit time T, discrete skill selection) is a standard and stable instantiation for sparse tasks; the paper also analyzes sensitivity to T and discusses extensions (learned terminations, end-to-end training).
- Empirical support and rigor:
  - Pretraining: Visitation plots demonstrate that SNNs with bilinear integration and MI bonus produce diverse, interpretable skills covering different directions and gaits; ablations show simple concatenation is inferior. Training K skills in one SNN plausibly reduces sample cost vs K independent policies.
  - Exploration: Long rollouts with random Manager show dramatically larger coverage than Gaussian noise, and broader coverage than multi-policy baselines trained independently.
  - Downstream tasks: On multiple mazes and gather, hierarchical policies with SNN skills learn reliably and faster than baselines; MI-regularized pretraining often improves consistency and performance, especially in gather. Results report multiple seeds and multiple pretrained SNNs, without cherry-picking, with variance explicitly discussed. Transfer to a more complex agent (Snake) shows scalability; limitations on Ant are candidly analyzed.
- Novelty: While related to prior HRL and skill discovery (options, HiREPS) and to InfoGAN-like MI objectives, the combination of (i) SNN-conditioned skills with bilinear integration, (ii) MI between skill and informative state subset for unsupervised skill discovery from a single proxy reward, and (iii) a simple, effective hierarchical controller that exploits these skills for sparse tasks, is a meaningful and practically useful contribution at the time of writing. The dependence only on a task-agnostic proxy reward (no goal set) distinguishes it from contemporaneous goal-conditioned pretraining.

Feedback to improve the paper (non-blocking)
- Quantify diversity: Add quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., skill-conditioned state occupancy divergence, mutual information estimates, coverage area) alongside visitation plots.
- Broaden baselines: Include comparisons to option-critic (where feasible in continuous control), feudal-style methods, or other concurrent skill-discovery approaches. Even if adaptations are needed, partial comparisons will strengthen the empirical case.
- Report sample budgets: Explicitly compare total sample counts for SNN vs K independent policies to substantiate the “K×” pretraining efficiency claim.
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide sensitivity to K (number of skills), grid resolution for MI estimation, MI coefficient αH, and commit time T on downstream performance in the main text (some are in appendices).
- Generality of proxy rewards: Discuss and, if possible, demonstrate proxy choices beyond CoM speed (e.g., energy use, contact diversity) and their impact on the learned skill span and transfer usefulness.
- Unstable agents: For Ant, include initial experiments with learned terminations or short warm-up rollouts when switching skills to mitigate falls, or switching-aware regularization in pretraining.

Questions for the authors
1. Diversity metrics: Can you report quantitative measures of skill diversity (e.g., estimated I(Z;C), state coverage, pairwise behavioral distances) and correlate them with downstream performance?
2. Number of skills K: How does performance scale with K? Is there a point of diminishing returns or interference among skills?
3. MI estimation: How sensitive are results to the (x, y) grid resolution and to the choice of C? Have you tried higher-dimensional C (e.g., position plus heading) with function approximation rather than counts?
4. Sample complexity: What are the absolute pretraining and downstream sample counts across methods (SNN vs multi-policy vs baselines)? Does pretraining ever overfit to the proxy reward in ways that hurt transfer?
5. Manager training stability: How sensitive is Manager learning to T and to the initial distribution over skills? Would a simple termination mechanism (learned or entropy-regularized) materially improve the results?
6. Comparisons: Have you attempted option-critic or goal-conditioned pretraining (e.g., Heess et al., 2016) in the same environments for a head-to-head comparison?
7. Transfer breadth: Beyond locomotion mazes/gather, can the same pretrained skill set transfer to tasks with richer constraints (e.g., narrow passages, moving goals), or to partial observability?
8. End-to-end fine-tuning: Do preliminary experiments with straight-through estimators for z during downstream training improve performance or reduce variance across seeds? What are the optimization challenges observed?
