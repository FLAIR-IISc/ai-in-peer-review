1. Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the challenge of learning in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by first pretraining a diverse set of reusable skills with minimal supervision and then composing them via a high-level manager for downstream tasks. The core idea is to use Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a categorical latent code held fixed per rollout to represent multi-modal skills, augmented with a mutual information (MI) bonus (computed on a low-dimensional subset of the state, e.g., CoM (x, y)) to encourage diversity. A bilinear integration of latent code and observation improves mode separation over simple concatenation. Skills are then frozen and selected by a manager policy at a fixed period T. Empirically, the approach yields interpretable skills, significantly improved exploration, and consistent gains on sparse tasks (multiple mazes and Gather) with Swimmer and Snake. Ablations (latent integration, MI strength, switch time) are informative, and code/videos are provided.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) A well-motivated, simple, and general framework that unifies intrinsic motivation and hierarchy with a novel SNN+MI skill learning mechanism; (ii) Empirical evidence that the learned skills markedly enhance exploration and improve performance on challenging sparse tasks, with clear ablations supporting design choices.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance is high; prior HRL and intrinsic motivation methods often require task-specific goals or hand-designed options. Using a single proxy reward plus MI is compelling and minimally domain-dependent. 
- The method is placed in relevant literature (options, option-critic, intrinsic motivation, InfoGAN); the use of MI over a task-agnostic state subset is a neat practical adaptation.
- Empirical support: visitation plots show distinct, interpretable modes; manager-driven exploration covers space dramatically better than Gaussian noise; downstream learning curves outperform strong baselines (including intrinsic CoM reward) across tasks, with consistent benefits from bilinear integration and often from MI. Robustness to switch-time T for static tasks is demonstrated; Snake results show scalability. Limitations (e.g., Ant instability, variance across seeds) are candidly discussed.

4. Feedback and questions (to help improve; not all affect acceptance)
- Provide stronger baselines: option-critic, hierarchical goal-conditioned policies, FeUdal/HIRO-style planners, and compare to VIME under identical horizons.
- Report sample complexity wall-clock/interaction counts for pretraining vs downstream, and quantify transfer efficiency across tasks.
- Analyze sensitivity to K, MI weight αH, and the discretization used for p(Z|c); consider continuous density models for p(Z|C).
- Explore learning termination and joint fine-tuning of skills with straight-through estimators; could this mitigate Ant failures?
- How does performance change with different proxy rewards or with purely unsupervised skill discovery (no speed bonus)?
- Can the MI target use richer state subsets (e.g., orientation) to shape more nuanced skills? Any results from visual inputs?
- Clarify whether the manager uses Sagent vs full state and how this impacts transferability.
