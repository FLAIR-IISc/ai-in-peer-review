This paper proposes a general and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a pre-training phase using a single, generic proxy reward that requires minimal domain knowledge. To efficiently learn a rich repertoire of skills, the authors employ a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as the policy, augmented with a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage behavioral diversity. Second, for downstream tasks, a high-level policy is trained on top of these pre-trained, frozen skills, effectively simplifying the exploration problem and enabling rapid learning.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for this decision are the novel and well-motivated method for unsupervised skill discovery and the strong, comprehensive empirical evidence supporting the framework's effectiveness on challenging continuous control problems.

The paper’s main strength lies in its elegant pre-training methodology. Combining SNNs for multi-modal policy representation with an information-theoretic bonus is a clever approach to learn a diverse and interpretable set of skills in a sample-efficient manner. This avoids the need to train multiple policies independently or to hand-craft specific rewards for each desired skill. The claims are substantiated by a thorough experimental evaluation. The visitation plots provide clear qualitative evidence of diverse skill learning. Furthermore, the results on the benchmark maze and collection tasks demonstrate that the hierarchical approach significantly outperforms a relevant baseline, confirming that the learned skills provide a powerful basis for exploration and accelerate learning on difficult downstream tasks. The authors’ rigor in testing across multiple environments, seeds, and even different robot morphologies strengthens the paper’s conclusions.

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision:

The use of a fixed time `T` for skill execution is a potential limitation. While the appendix analyzes its impact, discussing the integration of a learned termination condition, akin to the options framework, in the main body could provide a more complete picture. The failure modes for the unstable "Ant" robot are an important finding; a brief mention in the main discussion section would offer a more balanced perspective on the method's robustness.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
*   How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to the design of the proxy reward? Could you provide more intuition on what constitutes a "good" proxy reward?
*   The MI bonus relies on discretizing the state space. How does the grid resolution for this discretization affect the diversity of the resulting skills?
*   Have you performed any preliminary experiments on fine-tuning the low-level SNN policy jointly with the high-level manager on the downstream task? It would be interesting to know if this provides benefits over keeping the skills frozen.
