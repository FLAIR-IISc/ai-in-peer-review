### **Summary**
This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve challenging tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, a diverse set of reusable skills is learned in a simple environment with a single, generic "proxy reward" that requires minimal domain knowledge (e.g., rewarding an agent for moving quickly). To learn a wide span of distinct skills efficiently, the authors employ a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as the policy, conditioned on a discrete latent variable. Two key technical contributions are introduced to encourage skill diversity: (1) a bilinear integration of the latent variable with the state observation, which gives the latent code more influence over the policy, and (2) an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state, which explicitly incentivizes different latent codes to produce distinguishable behaviors.

In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as a set of temporally-extended actions for a high-level "manager" policy. This manager is then trained on specific downstream tasks (e.g., navigating a maze), where it learns to select the appropriate skill at set intervals. The experiments, conducted on challenging continuous control benchmarks, convincingly demonstrate that this approach learns a wide span of interpretable skills, drastically improves exploration, and significantly accelerates learning on sparse-reward tasks compared to strong baselines.

### **Decision: Accept**
I recommend accepting this paper for two key reasons:
1.  **Novel and Effective Method for Unsupervised Skill Discovery:** The paper presents an elegant and well-motivated solution to the problem of automatic skill discovery. The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks with a mutual information-based objective is a novel and powerful way to generate a diverse skill set from a single, simple reward signal, reducing the need for extensive human engineering.
2.  **Strong and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by extensive experiments that are well-designed and clearly presented. The qualitative results (visitation plots) provide compelling visual evidence for the diversity of learned skills and the resulting improvement in exploration, while the quantitative results demonstrate significant performance gains on difficult sparse-reward tasks.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The problem of exploration in sparse-reward settings is a critical bottleneck for deep RL. This paper makes a significant contribution by effectively combining ideas from hierarchical RL, intrinsic motivation, and information theory into a practical and scalable framework.

The unsupervised pre-training phase is the main strength of the work. By using a simple proxy reward and an information-theoretic objective to enforce diversity, the method sidesteps the common HRL requirement of manually defining sub-goals or options. This is a more general and scalable approach than competing methods that might require specifying a set of goals for pre-training (e.g., Heess et al., 2016). The inspiration from InfoGAN is well-placed and effectively adapted to the RL context.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides strong support for the authors' claims.
*   **Ablation of Technical Contributions:** The results in Figure 4 clearly demonstrate the impact of the core technical ideas. The comparison between simple concatenation and bilinear integration, and the incremental effect of adding the mutual information bonus, convincingly shows that these components are crucial for learning a rich and separated set of skills.
*   **Demonstration of Improved Exploration:** Figure 5 provides a powerful visualization of the method's primary benefit. The exploration coverage of the proposed hierarchical agent is orders of magnitude larger than that of a naive exploration strategy and appears qualitatively superior to the "Multi-policy" baseline, which required six times the pre-training data.
*   **Performance on Downstream Tasks:** The learning curves in Figure 6 show that the improved exploration translates directly into successfully solving tasks that standard RL algorithms fail on. The SNN-based method is competitive with or superior to the Multi-policy baseline, confirming the sample-efficiency benefits of learning skills jointly within a single network.
*   **Scientific Rigor:** The authors demonstrate rigor by evaluating on multiple random seeds, testing on different robots (Swimmer, Snake, Ant), and honestly reporting failure modes (e.g., instability of the Ant robot when switching skills), which strengthens the credibility of the work.

### **Additional Feedback**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   The calculation of the mutual information bonus relies on discretizing the agent's (x, y) coordinates. While effective here, this may not scale to tasks where the relevant state for distinguishing skills is higher-dimensional or less obvious. The paper briefly mentions the alternative of using a neural network to estimate the posterior `p(Z|c)`; a short discussion on the potential challenges or benefits of this approach would strengthen the paper's discussion of generality.
*   The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The appendix shows that performance is not overly sensitive to this hyperparameter for the tasks studied, but for more dynamic tasks, a fixed commitment time could be a limitation. Highlighting this and briefly mentioning potential solutions (like learning a termination condition, as noted in the future work section) in the main text would be beneficial.
*   The choice of which state variables to use for the MI calculation (`C`) seems to require some domain insight (e.g., the center of mass coordinates). It would be interesting to know how the method's performance degrades if a less informative or noisier subset of the state is used.

### **Questions for Authors**
1.  The posterior `p(Z|c)` for the MI bonus is estimated using visitation counts from only the current batch of trajectories. This means the bonus landscape is non-stationary. Did you observe any training instabilities as a result? Have you considered more stable estimators, such as using a running average of counts over multiple batches?
2.  The low-level skills are trained in an open environment, conditioned only on the agent's proprioceptive state and the latent code `z`. The high-level manager, however, selects skills based on the full state, including task-specific sensor readings (e.g., LIDAR). Does this mismatch cause issues? For example, does a skill trained to move forward in an open space behave predictably when executed near a wall?
3.  The paper argues that the proxy reward should "encourage the existence of locally optimal solutions." For locomotion, rewarding forward speed is intuitive. Could you provide some intuition on how one might design a good proxy reward for a qualitatively different domain, such as robotic manipulation, where the goal isn't just movement?
