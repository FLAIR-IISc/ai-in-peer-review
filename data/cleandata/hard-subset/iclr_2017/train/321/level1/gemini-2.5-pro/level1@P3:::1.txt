### **Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a general and principled framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a task-agnostic manner, and second, a downstream phase where a high-level policy is trained to select among these pre-trained skills to solve specific tasks.

The key innovations lie in the pre-training stage. To learn multiple skills efficiently, the authors employ a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) where different skills are conditioned on a discrete latent variable, `z`. This allows for weight sharing and is significantly more sample-efficient than training separate policies for each skill. To ensure that the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the authors introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer. This regularizer provides an intrinsic reward bonus proportional to the mutual information between the latent skill variable and the agent's state, effectively encouraging behaviors that are clearly distinguishable based on where they take the agent. The authors show this can be implemented effectively by penalizing the conditional entropy `H(Z|C)`, which encourages predictability of the skill given the agent's location. The paper provides strong empirical evidence on challenging continuous control benchmarks (locomotion in mazes and a gathering task), demonstrating that this framework learns interpretable skills, significantly enhances exploration, and leads to faster learning on sparse-reward tasks compared to relevant baselines.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novel and Effective Skill Discovery Mechanism:** The combination of SNNs for sample-efficient multi-skill learning with a mutual information-based objective for encouraging diversity is both elegant and highly effective. This provides a practical solution to the long-standing problem of automatic skill discovery in HRL, moving beyond hand-engineering or complex discovery heuristics by framing it as a representation learning problem.
2.  **Compelling and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's claims. The qualitative visualizations of learned skills and exploration patterns provide excellent intuition, while the quantitative results on difficult sparse-reward tasks demonstrate a substantial performance gain over non-hierarchical methods and a strong multi-policy baseline. The rigor is enhanced by testing over multiple seeds for both pre-training and downstream tasks.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, situating itself clearly within the literature on HRL and intrinsic motivation. It correctly identifies the limitations of prior approaches—the manual effort required for traditional options and the lack of explicit skill transfer in many intrinsic motivation methods. The proposed framework offers a compelling synthesis, using an intrinsic-like "proxy reward" to guide skill learning but structuring it within a hierarchical framework for direct reuse across tasks.

The technical approach is sound. The use of an SNN with bilinear integration of the latent variable is a well-justified architectural choice for expressivity. The core idea of maximizing mutual information `I(Z;C)` is inspired by successful work in unsupervised representation learning (e.g., InfoGAN) and is cleverly adapted to the RL setting. The practical implementation, which involves estimating `p(z|c)` from batch statistics, is simple and effective. The choice to treat the latent code `z` as part of the observation allows for the straightforward application of powerful off-the-shelf policy gradient algorithms like TRPO, which is a significant practical advantage.

The experimental results are impressive. Figure 4 provides a clear visual demonstration that the combination of bilinear integration and the MI bonus is crucial for learning a diverse set of interpretable skills (e.g., forward, backward, turning motions). Figure 5, showing the exploration coverage of a random high-level policy, is a powerful illustration of how the learned skills provide a much better basis for exploration than primitive actions. The learning curves in Figure 6 show that this improved exploration translates directly into superior performance on sparse-reward tasks, outperforming a well-chosen baseline (proxy reward shaping) and being more sample-efficient than a multi-policy approach. The ablation-style presentation of results effectively isolates and validates each of the paper's core contributions. The inclusion of results for more complex agents like the "Snake" in the appendix further strengthens the claims of generality.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision.

*   **Scalability of the MI Estimator:** The current MI bonus calculation relies on discretizing the 2D coordinate space into a grid. While effective for the tasks presented, this approach may not scale gracefully to higher-dimensional state spaces for `C` (e.g., if one wanted to include orientation or full joint configurations). The paper briefly mentions that an MLP could be used to estimate the posterior `p(z|c)`, which is a great direction. Expanding slightly on the potential challenges of this (e.g., stability of training the auxiliary classifier, computational overhead) would add depth to the discussion.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter `T`:** The switch time `T` is a fixed hyperparameter. The paper rightly identifies learning a termination condition as future work. However, the performance of the current method could be sensitive to this choice. A brief sensitivity analysis of `T` in the main text (similar to the excellent one for the Snake agent in Appendix C.3) would be valuable for readers looking to apply this method.
*   **Generalizing the Proxy Reward:** The concept of a simple, generic proxy reward is very appealing. For the locomotion tasks, rewarding speed is a natural choice. It would be beneficial to discuss in more detail how this concept might be instantiated in other domains, such as manipulation. The paper suggests rewarding "successful grasping of any object," which is a good start. A short discussion on the properties a good proxy reward should have (e.g., encouraging diverse state visitation, being dense and easy to compute) would make the framework feel more general.
*   **Discussion of Failure Modes:** The appendix honestly discusses the failure mode for the "Ant" agent, where switching between dynamically distinct skills can lead to instability. This is a crucial point for HRL in general. Briefly mentioning this limitation and the challenge of ensuring safe state transitions between skills in the main paper's discussion would provide a more complete and balanced perspective.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

*   The mutual information bonus is calculated with respect to the 2D Center of Mass (CoM) coordinates `C=(x,y)`. Have you experimented with including other state variables in `C`, such as the agent's orientation or CoM velocity? How do you hypothesize this would affect the nature of the learned skills?
*   In the downstream task, the manager policy selects a skill `z` every `T` steps based on the current state. Is the previously chosen skill, `z_{t-1}`, also part of the manager's input? It seems this could be valuable context for selecting the next skill, particularly for planning sequences of behaviors.
*   Appendix B shows that your method outperforms VIME, a state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation method from that time. Could you elaborate on the conceptual difference? VIME encourages visiting states where the agent's dynamics model is inaccurate (i.e., novelty-seeking), whereas your method encourages behaviors that are distinguishable from each other. Do you see these two forms of intrinsic reward as complementary? For instance, could a VIME-style bonus be used as the proxy reward in your pre-training phase?
