### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a general and sample-efficient hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve a collection of tasks characterized by sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training stage, the agent learns a diverse set of reusable skills using only a single, generic "proxy reward" that requires minimal domain knowledge (e.g., rewarding forward speed). To learn a span of skills efficiently from this single objective, the authors propose using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as the policy, conditioned on a discrete latent variable. Two key technical innovations are introduced to make this skill learning effective: (1) using a bilinear integration of the latent variable and the state to improve the expressiveness of the SNN, and (2) adding an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and the agent's state to explicitly encourage the learned skills to be distinct and diverse.

In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as primitives in a hierarchical architecture to tackle downstream tasks. A high-level "manager" policy is trained to select which skill to execute for a fixed time interval, effectively reducing the complexity and time horizon of the new task. The paper demonstrates through a series of experiments on challenging continuous control environments that this framework learns a wide range of interpretable skills, dramatically improves exploration, and significantly accelerates learning on sparse-reward tasks compared to non-hierarchical baselines.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are:
1.  The paper presents a very well-motivated and elegant framework that effectively combines several powerful ideas—hierarchical RL, intrinsic motivation, and information-theoretic unsupervised learning—to address the critical challenge of exploration in sparse-reward settings.
2.  The claims are substantiated by strong and comprehensive empirical evidence. The experiments include excellent visualizations that provide clear intuition, compelling quantitative results on benchmark tasks, and insightful ablation studies that validate the necessity of the core technical contributions (bilinear integration and the MI bonus).

### **Supporting Arguments**

The problem of sparse rewards is a fundamental obstacle for deep RL, and the proposed solution is both novel and practical. While skill discovery is a classic topic in HRL, this paper’s method for acquiring skills is particularly clever. By using a single SNN, conditioned on a latent variable and regularized by an MI objective, the framework can learn an entire library of distinct skills from a simple, generic reward signal. This is a significant improvement in sample efficiency and generality over methods that require training separate policies for each skill or rely on hand-designed, task-specific rewards.

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing.
*   **Skill Diversity:** The visitation plots in Figure 4 provide clear, qualitative proof of the method's success. They convincingly show that the combination of bilinear integration and the MI bonus is crucial for learning a rich and diverse set of behaviors (e.g., forward, backward, and turning motions), whereas simpler SNN architectures fail to do so. This directly supports the core technical claims of the paper.
*   **Improved Exploration:** Figure 5 demonstrates the practical benefit of the learned skills, showing that a random high-level policy can explore a vastly larger portion of the state space than standard naive exploration. This provides a strong mechanistic explanation for why the method succeeds on the downstream tasks.
*   **Downstream Performance:** The learning curves in Figure 6 show that the hierarchical agent significantly outperforms a strong baseline on several challenging sparse-reward tasks. The fact that the SNN-based agent (trained on 1x samples) performs comparably or better than the "Multi-policy" baseline (trained on 6x samples) is a powerful testament to the sample efficiency of the proposed approach.

Furthermore, the authors' rigor is commendable. They run experiments with multiple random seeds, report variance, and include an extensive appendix with results on more complex agents ("Snake," "Ant") and an analysis of failure modes. This transparency and depth add significant credibility to the findings.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   **Connection to Options Framework:** The manager-skill architecture is functionally a policy over options. It would be beneficial to make this connection more explicit and briefly discuss how the proposed pre-training approach differs from and potentially complements end-to-end option discovery methods like the Option-Critic architecture.
*   **MI State Discretization:** The MI bonus calculation relies on discretizing a subset of the state space (the agent's `(x, y)` coordinates). This may not scale easily to tasks where the relevant state information is higher-dimensional or less obvious. The paper mentions the possibility of using a neural network to estimate the posterior, and a brief comment on the potential trade-offs of this approach would be valuable for future work.
*   **Fixed Switching Time `T`:** The paper acknowledges that the fixed switching time `T` is a limitation. While learning a termination condition is rightly left as future work, it might be interesting to comment on whether the performance is highly sensitive to the choice of `T`, based on the analysis in the appendix. A brief mention in the main text could help readers understand the robustness of this hyperparameter.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to deepen my understanding of the work.
1.  In Section 7.3, you note that the MI bonus does not always boost performance on the maze tasks, hypothesizing that simpler "wall-bumping" strategies are sufficient. Could you elaborate on this? Is it possible that the greater diversity of skills learned with the MI bonus creates a more complex control problem for the manager, which might hinder learning if those specific skills are not strictly necessary?
2.  The failure mode for the "Ant" agent, where switching skills leads to instability, is an important finding. You suggest learning a "transition policy" as a potential solution. Could you expand on what this might entail? Would this be a short policy that bridges the state distributions of two skills, and how might one train such a policy without external supervision?
3.  How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to the specific choice of proxy reward? For instance, with the Swimmer agent, would a proxy reward based on maximizing angular velocity produce a different but equally useful set of skills for navigation tasks?
