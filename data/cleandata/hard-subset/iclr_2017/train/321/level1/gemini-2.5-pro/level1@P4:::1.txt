### 1. Summary

This paper proposes a general framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage approach: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of reusable skills is learned in a simple environment with minimal supervision, and second, a downstream learning phase where a high-level policy is trained to select from these skills to solve complex tasks.

To achieve sample-efficient learning of a diverse skill set, the authors employ Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs), where a latent categorical variable is provided as input to the policy network, allowing a single network to represent a multi-modal policy. To explicitly encourage diversity and prevent the skills from collapsing to a single behavior, the authors introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer. This regularizer provides an intrinsic reward bonus proportional to the mutual information between the latent variable (the skill) and the agent's state, effectively rewarding the agent for producing behaviors that are clearly distinguishable for each skill. The paper demonstrates empirically on challenging continuous control benchmarks that this framework can learn a wide range of interpretable skills and leverage them to significantly improve exploration and achieve strong performance on sparse-reward tasks where standard RL algorithms fail.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Method for Unsupervised Skill Discovery:** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated method for learning a repertoire of skills without task-specific rewards. The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks for multi-modal policy representation and an information-theoretic objective for encouraging skill diversity is a significant and novel contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning.

2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Validation:** The claims are substantiated by extensive experiments on challenging benchmark environments. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed framework dramatically improves exploration and solves sparse-reward tasks intractable for baseline methods. The inclusion of insightful ablations and qualitative visualizations (e.g., visitation plots) effectively validates the importance of each component of the proposed method (bilinear integration, MI bonus).

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's approach is well-placed within the existing literature, successfully bridging ideas from hierarchical reinforcement learning, intrinsic motivation, and representation learning. It addresses a key limitation of prior HRL work, which often required manual specification of skills, by proposing a principled and data-driven method for skill discovery that requires only a generic "proxy reward" with minimal domain knowledge.

The technical approach is sound. The use of an SNN with a latent variable to parameterize different skills is an elegant way to achieve weight sharing and sample efficiency compared to training multiple policies independently. The introduction of the mutual information bonus, inspired by recent work in unsupervised learning (InfoGAN), is a clever way to formalize the objective of learning distinct and interpretable skills. The practical implementation, which treats the latent code as part of the observation to enable the use of off-the-shelf policy gradient algorithms like TRPO, is simple and effective.

The experimental evaluation is rigorous and convincing.
*   **Clarity of Results:** The visitation plots in Figure 4 provide clear, qualitative evidence that the SNNs, particularly with bilinear integration and the MI bonus, learn a diverse set of interpretable locomotion skills (e.g., forward, backward, turning).
*   **Demonstrated Impact on Exploration:** Figure 5 effectively illustrates the primary benefit of the learned skill hierarchy: a random high-level policy explores a vastly larger state space than random low-level actions, pre-empting the exploration problem in the downstream tasks.
*   **Performance on Challenging Tasks:** The learning curves in Figure 6 show that the hierarchical architectures substantially outperform a strong baseline (training from scratch with the proxy reward) on several sparse-reward maze and collection tasks. The proposed SNN-based hierarchy is shown to be competitive with, and often superior to, a more sample-intensive multi-policy baseline.
*   **Honesty and Thoroughness:** The authors' inclusion of experiments on more complex agents and a discussion of failure modes (e.g., instability of the Ant agent) in the appendix adds to the scientific credibility of the work.

### 4. Additional Feedback

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Scalability of MI Estimation:** The mutual information bonus is estimated by discretizing the 2D coordinate space. This approach is effective here but may not scale well to tasks where the relevant state-space for distinguishing skills is higher-dimensional. While the paper briefly mentions using a function approximator (e.g., an MLP) as an alternative, expanding on the potential challenges and solutions for scaling this component would strengthen the paper.
*   **Fixed Time Abstraction:** The high-level policy commits to a selected skill for a fixed number of timesteps `T`. The appendix shows that performance is reasonably robust to the choice of `T` in these static tasks. However, for more dynamic tasks, a fixed `T` could be suboptimal. It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss this limitation in the main body and mention the possibility of learning termination conditions, as is done in the options framework.
*   **Frozen vs. Fine-tuned Skills:** The low-level skills are frozen during downstream training. This simplifies the problem for the high-level policy but prevents skills from being adapted to the specifics of a new task. A brief discussion of the trade-offs of this design choice would be welcome.

### 5. Questions for Authors

*   The mutual information bonus `I(Z;C)` is calculated using the agent's `(x, y)` coordinates for `C`. How was this choice of state variables made? Have you experimented with other state variables, such as including orientation or joint velocities, and how did this affect the diversity and nature of the learned skills?
*   Section 7.3 notes that the performance variance on maze tasks is high because some pre-trained SNNs may lack the specific skill needed to solve a particular maze. Does this suggest an inherent limitation of unsupervised pre-training, or could the MI objective be modified to encourage a more "complete" or "covering" set of skills over the state space?
*   The comparison against the "Multi-policy" baseline highlights the sample efficiency of the SNN approach (1x vs 6x pre-training samples). For a more direct comparison of representational capacity, did you ever try training the SNN for a comparable number of samples (e.g., 6x) as the multi-policy baseline? It would be interesting to see if this leads to a richer set of skills or if performance plateaus.
