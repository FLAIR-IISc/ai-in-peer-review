- Decision: Accept.

- Proposed method: A two-stage hierarchical RL framework that pre-trains diverse reusable skills in a simple environment and trains a high-level policy to sequence those skills for downstream sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks.

- Skill representation: Uses Stochastic Neural Networks with a latent categorical input to represent a multi-modal policy in a single network, enabling weight sharing and sample efficiency.

- Diversity objective: Introduces an information-theoretic mutual information (MI) bonus as an intrinsic reward to encourage skills that produce distinguishable behaviors.

- Implementation detail: Treats the latent code as part of the observation so standard policy-gradient algorithms (e.g., TRPO) can be used off the shelf.

- Empirical claims: Demonstrates on challenging continuous-control benchmarks that the approach learns interpretable skills and substantially improves exploration and performance on sparse-reward tasks where baselines fail.

- Experimental rigor: Strong experimental validation with ablations, qualitative visitation plots, and learning curves that substantiate the necessity of components (bilinear integration, MI bonus).

- Visitation/skill clarity: Visitation plots show SNNs (with bilinear integration and MI bonus) learn diverse locomotion skills (e.g., forward, backward, turning).

- Exploration impact: Random high-level controllers using learned skills explore a much larger state space than random low-level actions, addressing exploration bottlenecks.

- Performance vs baselines: Hierarchical SNN approach outperforms training-from-scratch baselines on maze and collection tasks and is competitive with a more sample-intensive multi-policy baseline.

- Scientific credibility: Paper includes experiments on more complex agents and discusses failure modes (e.g., Ant instability) in the appendix.

- Scalability concern: The MI bonus is estimated by discretizing 2D coordinates, which may not scale to higher-dimensional or more complex distinguishing state spaces.

- Time abstraction limitation: High-level actions commit to a fixed timestep T, which may be suboptimal for dynamic tasks; learning termination conditions could be beneficial.

- Skill adaptation trade-off: Freezing low-level skills during downstream training simplifies high-level learning but prevents task-specific fine-tuning of skills; trade-offs should be discussed.

- Question — MI state choice: Why was (x,y) chosen for MI estimation, and how do other state variables (orientation, velocities) affect skill diversity?

- Question — coverage limitation: Does variance in downstream performance (some pre-trained SNNs lacking needed skills) indicate a limitation of unsupervised pre-training, or can the MI objective be modified to encourage more complete coverage?

- Question — sample-efficiency comparison: Was the SNN ever trained with comparable pre-training samples to the multi-policy baseline (e.g., 6x) to test whether more samples yield richer skills or plateauing performance?
