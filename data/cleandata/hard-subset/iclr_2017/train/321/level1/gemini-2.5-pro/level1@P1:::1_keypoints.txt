- Proposes a two-stage hierarchical RL framework: pre-train diverse skills with a proxy reward using an SNN conditioned on a discrete latent, then freeze skills and train a high-level manager to compose them for downstream tasks.
- Key technical innovations: bilinear integration of latent and state to increase SNN expressiveness, and a mutual-information (MI) regularizer to encourage distinct, diverse skills.
- Decision: Accept — framework is well-motivated and effectively combines hierarchical RL, intrinsic motivation, and information-theoretic unsupervised learning.
- Empirical claims are well-supported by strong visualizations, quantitative results, and ablation studies.
- Skill diversity evidence: visitation plots show bilinear + MI are crucial for learning diverse behaviors; simpler SNNs fail to achieve this diversity.
- Improved exploration: a random high-level policy over learned skills explores much larger portions of state space than naive exploration.
- Downstream performance: hierarchical agent significantly outperforms strong baselines on sparse-reward tasks and is notably sample-efficient (SNN trained with 1x samples vs Multi-policy with 6x).
- Experimental rigor: multiple seeds, variance reporting, extensive appendix with results on complex agents and failure-mode analysis.
- Suggest making the connection to the Options framework explicit and contrasting with end-to-end option discovery methods like Option-Critic.
- Concern about MI calculation: it currently discretizes (x,y) coordinates and may not scale to higher-dimensional or less obvious state representations; discuss neural posterior estimation trade-offs.
- Limitation: fixed skill switching time T; paper should comment on sensitivity/robustness to T (analysis partly in appendix).
- Question: Why does the MI bonus sometimes fail to improve maze performance — could increased skill diversity complicate manager learning when simple behaviors suffice?
- Question: For Ant instability when switching skills, expand on the proposed "transition policy" idea — would it bridge state distributions and how could it be trained without supervision?
- Question: How sensitive are learned skills to the choice of proxy reward (e.g., angular velocity vs forward speed) and how does that affect downstream task utility?
