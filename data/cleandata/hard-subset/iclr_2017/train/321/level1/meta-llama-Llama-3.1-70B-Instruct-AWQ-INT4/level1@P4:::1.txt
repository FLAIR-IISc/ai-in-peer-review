**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper proposes a general framework for training policies for a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The framework first learns a span of skills in a pre-training environment using a proxy reward signal that requires minimal domain knowledge. The skills are learned using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) combined with an information-theoretic regularizer. The pre-trained skills are then used as basic building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards by training a high-level policy that operates on top of the skills. The authors claim that their framework can learn a wide span of interpretable skills in a sample-efficient way and significantly boost the learning performance uniformly across a wide range of downstream tasks.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I decide to accept this paper with minor revisions. The two key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and novel approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning, combining the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods.
2. The authors provide strong empirical evidence to support their claims, demonstrating the effectiveness of their framework in learning a wide span of skills and improving the learning performance on downstream tasks with sparse rewards.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's approach is well-motivated by the challenges of tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The use of SNNs and an information-theoretic regularizer is a novel contribution that addresses the limitations of previous hierarchical reinforcement learning methods. The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of their framework, including the pre-training environment, the SNN architecture, and the high-level policy.

The empirical results presented in the paper are strong and convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the framework in learning a wide span of skills and improving the learning performance on downstream tasks. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, including ablation studies and comparisons to baseline methods.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

While the paper is well-written and clear, there are a few areas that could be improved:

* The authors could provide more details on the implementation of the SNN architecture and the information-theoretic regularizer.
* The paper could benefit from more theoretical analysis of the framework, including a discussion of the convergence properties of the SNNs and the high-level policy.
* The authors could provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of skills and the switch time T.

Some questions that I would like the authors to answer:

* How do the authors plan to extend their framework to more complex tasks and environments?
* How do the authors plan to address the issue of switching between skills for unstable agents, as reported in the appendix?
* Can the authors provide more details on the computational resources required to train the SNNs and the high-level policy?
