## Review of Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning and an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity. The core idea of pre-training skills with a simple proxy reward, then utilizing those skills for faster learning in downstream sparse-reward tasks is sound and addresses a critical challenge in RL. The authors successfully demonstrate improved performance across several benchmark tasks.

**Decision: Accept** – The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to HRL, and the empirical results demonstrate a clear benefit from the proposed method. While some aspects could be further clarified, the overall contribution is significant and warrants acceptance. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Problem Tackled & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of sparse rewards and long horizons in RL, and positions the proposed work within the existing literature on hierarchical methods and intrinsic motivation. The motivation for combining these approaches – leveraging intrinsic motivation for skill discovery and hierarchy to reduce exploration complexity – is compelling and logically presented.

2. **Approach & Literature Placement:** The proposed approach, combining SNNs, an information-theoretic regularizer, and a two-level hierarchical policy structure, is well-defined. The authors appropriately acknowledge and contrast their work with related approaches like option learning, intrinsic motivation methods (e.g., VIME), and skill discovery techniques. The justification for using SNNs (multi-modal policies, weight sharing) and the MI regularizer (encouraging skill diversity) is reasonable. 

3. **Results & Rigor:** The experiments demonstrate substantial improvements over existing methods and a well-established baseline. The ablation studies (Fig. 6, Fig. 7) highlighting the benefits of the MI bonus and the SNN architecture are convincing. The results are reported with clear comparisons across different environments and robot morphologies.  The provided code and video links enhance reproducibility and understanding. The analysis of pre-trained skill variation (Fig. 4) is valuable in illustrating the effectiveness of the MI regularizer.  While a more exhaustive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis would be beneficial (discussed in feedback), the results presented are sufficiently robust to support the claims.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not affecting the decision):**

* **Mutual Information Calculation:** The discretization of the state space for MI calculation seems somewhat ad-hoc. While functional, the sensitivity of the results to the grid size/discretization scheme should be discussed. Is there a principled way to determine the optimal level of discretization?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more thorough investigation of the hyperparameters (learning rates, MI coefficient, network architecture) would strengthen the conclusions. While the paper mentions hyperparameters, a table detailing their values across different experiments would be helpful.
* **Ablation Study - SNN Architecture:** Beyond bilinear integration, exploring other potential SNN architectures (e.g., different integration methods mentioned in the text) in a more systematic manner would provide deeper insights into the efficacy of the SNN structure.
* **Switching Cost:** The framework assumes a fixed switch time (T).  In more complex environments, a variable switch time or learning a policy for *when* to switch skills might be beneficial. The paper briefly mentions this in the discussion, but a preliminary exploration could be valuable.
* **Clarification of "Skill" Interpretation:** While the visitation plots provide qualitative insight into the learned skills, a more formal quantification of skill diversity (e.g., based on the distance between learned policies in the action space) would be helpful. 
* **Ant Robot Discussion:** The discussion about instability with the Ant robot (Appendix D) is important. Providing a brief example of the failure modes in the main body of the paper would strengthen the overall narrative.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the MI calculation, what is the justification for the chosen discretization level? Did you explore different levels, and how did they impact performance?
2.  The paper mentions the potential for end-to-end training of the high-level and low-level policies. What are the major challenges you anticipate in implementing this, and how might they be addressed?
3.  For the Gather task, why does the MI bonus not consistently improve performance? Is this related to the task's specific characteristics?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the SNN approach compared to training separate policies? Specifically, how does the training time scale with the number of skills?
