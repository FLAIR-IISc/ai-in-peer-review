## Peer Review for ICLR 2017 Submission: Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery and transfer. The core idea is to pre-train a diverse set of skills using a proxy reward, then use these skills as building blocks for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors combine this with an information-theoretic regularizer to promote skill diversity and demonstrate promising results on benchmark robotic control tasks.

**1. What is the specific question/problem tackled by the paper?**

The paper tackles the challenge of sparse reward and long horizon reinforcement learning problems. Specifically, it investigates how to effectively learn and reuse skills across multiple tasks to accelerate learning and improve sample efficiency. It addresses the limitations of both intrinsic motivation (lack of knowledge transfer) and traditional hierarchical RL (reliance on domain expertise) by proposing a pre-training framework that automatically discovers a diverse set of skills with minimal domain knowledge.

**2. Is the approach well motivated, including being well-placed in the literature?**

Yes, the approach is well motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches to sparse reward RL and effectively position their work within the broader landscape of hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation. The introduction provides a solid overview of related work and justifies the need for a method that combines the strengths of both paradigms. The use of SNNs is also logically connected to the goal of learning a diverse skill set, and the information-theoretic regularizer is appropriately introduced as a means to enhance skill diversity. The literature review is comprehensive and accurately reflects the state-of-the-art.

**3. Does the paper support the claims? This includes determining if results, whether theoretical or empirical, are correct and if they are scientifically rigorous.**

Generally, yes, the paper supports its claims, but with some caveats. The empirical results on the locomotion + maze/gather tasks are compelling, demonstrating that the proposed approach outperforms baselines and achieves comparable or better performance than state-of-the-art methods. The ablation studies, specifically the investigation into the impact of the bilinear integration and the MI bonus, are helpful in understanding the contribution of each component. However, the presentation of the results could be more rigorous.

*   **Statistical Significance:** The paper would benefit from explicitly reporting statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for the performance comparisons. While the learning curves suggest improvements, quantifying the statistical significance would strengthen the claims.
*   **Variance across Runs:** The authors acknowledge a high variance in performance depending on the pre-trained SNN. While they report results over multiple seeds, a more detailed analysis of *why* certain SNNs perform better than others would be valuable. What characteristics of the learned skill space correlate with downstream performance?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to the MI bonus coefficient (alpha) is not thoroughly explored. A more systematic investigation of this hyperparameter would provide further insights into the effectiveness of the proposed regularizer.
*   **Baselines:** While comparisons to existing methods are made, more comprehensive benchmarking against a wider range of recent algorithms for sparse reward RL would further validate the approach.

**Decision: Accept with Minor Revisions**

The paper presents a novel and promising approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning with significant potential for addressing the challenges of sparse reward environments. The combination of SNNs and an information-theoretic regularizer is a clever idea, and the experimental results are encouraging. The concerns regarding the rigor of the evaluation can be addressed through minor revisions.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity of Notation:** The notation could be slightly more consistent. For example, the meaning of 'z' sometimes refers to a single latent variable and other times to the entire latent distribution.
*   **Visualizations:** While the visitation plots are helpful, additional visualizations of the learned skills (e.g., trajectories or action distributions) would further improve understanding.
*   **Computational Cost:**  A discussion of the computational cost associated with training SNNs compared to standard neural networks would be useful.
*   **Scaling to more complex environments:** The experiments presented are on relatively simple environments. Exploring the scalability of the approach to more complex, high-dimensional environments would be a valuable direction for future work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide statistical significance tests for the performance comparisons presented in the figures?
2.  What specific characteristics of the pre-trained SNNs correlate with better performance in the downstream tasks? (e.g., diversity of learned skills, specific latent code activations)
3.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the MI bonus coefficient (alpha)? Could you provide a plot showing performance as a function of alpha?
4.  Regarding the high variance observed across different pre-trained SNNs, what steps could be taken to reduce this variance and ensure more consistent performance?
5.   How does the computational cost of training SNNs scale with the dimensionality of the state and action spaces, compared to training standard neural network policies?
