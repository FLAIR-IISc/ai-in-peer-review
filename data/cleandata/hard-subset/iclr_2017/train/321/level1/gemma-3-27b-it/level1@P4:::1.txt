This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework combining Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer for pre-training skills, and then leveraging those skills in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a minimal proxy reward, and then reuse these skills across multiple tasks by training a high-level policy on top of them.

**Reject.** The paper presents a complex approach with several novel components (SNNs + MI regularization for skill learning, combined with hierarchical RL). However, the experimental validation is not sufficiently compelling to justify acceptance. While the results demonstrate some improvement over baselines, the gains are often marginal, and the experiments lack the rigor needed to confidently claim a significant contribution. The descriptions of the experiments and ablation studies are not detailed enough to fully understand the effect of each component.

The primary reasons for rejection are: 1) **Lack of strong empirical evidence:** The performance improvements observed in the experiments are modest and the results are sensitive to random seeds. 2) **Insufficient clarity and detail in the experimental setup:** The experiments are described at a high level and lack sufficient detail to enable reproduction. The impact of key hyperparameters (e.g., MI coefficient) isn’t thoroughly explored, and the analysis is limited.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of SNNs, MI regularization, and hierarchical RL is a novel approach. However, novelty alone isn’t sufficient for acceptance; the approach must deliver significant performance improvements.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundations of the approach seem reasonable. The use of SNNs for multi-modal policy representation and MI regularization for skill diversity are well-motivated. However, the choice of bilinear integration for the SNNs and the justification for specific architectural decisions aren't fully elaborated.
* **Empirical Support:** The experimental results on the locomotion + maze and gather tasks are not convincing. While the proposed method sometimes outperforms baselines (including a CoM reward baseline), the differences are not substantial and the variance across runs appears high (evident in the Gather task results). The reported results depend heavily on pre-trained SNNs which are sensitive to random seeds. The impact of the MI bonus isn't consistently positive, suggesting it may require careful tuning or is not universally beneficial.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (particularly the mathematical formulations and details of the experiments) could be more clear and concise. The explanation of the rationale behind the architectural choices (e.g., bilinear integration) could be strengthened.
* **Reproducibility:** The provided code is appreciated, but the experimental details are insufficient for easy reproduction. Critical hyperparameters and the random seeds used for different runs are not fully specified.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Perform more thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of each component (SNNs, MI regularization, bilinear integration). For example, compare the performance of SNNs with and without MI regularization, and compare bilinear integration with simpler integration methods.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Systematically explore the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters, such as the MI coefficient, the learning rate, and the network architecture.
* **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate that the observed performance differences are statistically significant and not due to chance.
* **More Detailed Experimental Description:** Provide a more detailed description of the experimental setup, including the specific hyperparameters used, the random seeds used for different runs, and the training procedure.
* **Error Analysis:** Provide an error analysis to understand the failure cases and identify potential areas for improvement.
* **Visualization of Learned Skills:** Provide more visual examples of the learned skills. Show how the latent variables in the SNNs correspond to different behaviors. This would help to build intuition for what the SNNs are learning.
* **Justification of Bilinear Integration:** Explain more precisely the advantages of bilinear integration over simpler integration methods.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The results are highly sensitive to the pre-trained SNN. What strategy did you use to select the best SNN for the downstream tasks? How do you address the issue of variability in performance across different SNNs?
2.  The impact of the MI bonus appears mixed. Under what conditions does the MI bonus consistently improve performance? What are the potential drawbacks of using the MI bonus?
3.  Why did you choose bilinear integration over other integration methods? Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the benefits of this approach?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to other hierarchical RL methods?
5.  Given the limited gains on the tested environments, how do you envision this approach scaling to more complex and realistic scenarios? What modifications would be necessary to address the challenges of such scenarios?
