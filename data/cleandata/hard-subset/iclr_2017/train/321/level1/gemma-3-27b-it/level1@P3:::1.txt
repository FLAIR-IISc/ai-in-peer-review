This paper tackles the problem of sparse reward reinforcement learning, specifically in scenarios where a single agent needs to perform a variety of tasks. The authors propose a hierarchical framework where a set of reusable skills are first learned in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage diversity. These skills are then leveraged by a high-level policy for faster learning in downstream tasks. This is a strong and relevant contribution, as sample efficiency in sparse reward settings remains a significant challenge in RL.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is well-motivated, builds upon existing work in hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation, and the empirical results are promising, demonstrating significant performance gains over existing baselines. The combination of SNNs with the mutual information regularization appears to be a particularly effective way to learn a diverse and reusable skill set.

The paper's strength lies in its effective integration of several ideas. The use of SNNs is a clever way to represent multimodal policies, and the MI regularization directly addresses the potential for collapse within the SNN. The pre-training/fine-tuning framework is a logical way to tackle the multi-task problem and improve exploration. The experimental evaluation, while not exhaustive, provides convincing evidence that the proposed method works well in a variety of challenging environments. The ablation studies (particularly Figure 7) are especially valuable, demonstrating the importance of both the SNN architecture *and* the MI regularization. The extensive visualizations (visitation plots) are also very helpful for understanding what skills the SNN is actually learning.

However, there are areas where the paper could be improved. First, the explanation of *why* the bilinear integration is beneficial compared to simple concatenation is somewhat limited. More intuition or perhaps a theoretical justification would be helpful. Second, while the results are strong, the comparison to other skill discovery algorithms is limited. A more thorough comparison, including more recent methods like those based on behavioral cloning or inverse reinforcement learning, would strengthen the paper. Third, the failure case analysis with the Ant robot (Appendix D.2) highlights a potential weakness of the approach – instability when switching skills. Addressing this, even if only theoretically, would be beneficial. Finally, the hyperparameter sensitivity is not fully explored. Reporting a sensitivity analysis of the MI bonus coefficient (alpha) would be helpful.

Here are some specific questions for the authors:

1. **Bilinear Integration:** Could you elaborate on *why* bilinear integration leads to better skill diversity compared to simple concatenation? Is there a connection to representational capacity or optimization landscape?
2. **Alternative Skill Discovery Algorithms:** Have you considered comparing your method to more recent skill discovery algorithms that leverage imitation learning or inverse reinforcement learning? If not, why? What are the key differences that make your approach unique?
3. **Skill Switching & Stability:** The Ant robot example suggests that frequent skill switching can destabilize the agent. Have you considered any techniques to mitigate this issue, such as smoothing the skill transitions or learning a “recovery” skill?
4. **MI Bonus Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of the MI bonus coefficient (alpha)? Can you provide a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of different values of alpha on skill diversity and downstream task performance?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational overhead associated with using SNNs compared to standard neural networks? Does the training time scale significantly with the number of latent variables (skills)?
6. **Generalization to More Complex State Spaces:** The current experiments focus on relatively low-dimensional state spaces. How well do you expect the approach to generalize to tasks with very high-dimensional state spaces (e.g., image-based inputs)? Would any modifications be necessary?
7. **Downstream Task Reward Shaping:** While the paper focuses on sparse rewards, could the pre-trained skills be further leveraged by shaping the rewards in the downstream tasks? Would reward shaping amplify the benefits of your hierarchical approach?
