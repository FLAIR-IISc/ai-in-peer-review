## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning in a pre-training environment, followed by training a high-level policy on top of these skills for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea – combining the benefits of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods through SNNs and an information-theoretic regularizer – is compelling and addresses a significant challenge in RL. The authors clearly articulate the motivation and position their work within the existing literature on HRL and intrinsic motivation.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate a clear benefit of using SNNs within a hierarchical framework, particularly in challenging sparse reward environments. The inclusion of an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity is a valuable contribution.

The paper provides a strong justification for its approach. The experiments on locomotion + maze and food collection tasks show significant performance improvements compared to baseline methods, including those utilizing intrinsic rewards. The ablation studies analyzing the impact of the bilinear integration and MI bonus in the SNN pre-training phase add credibility to the claims and provide insight into the method’s components. The authors also acknowledge limitations and potential avenues for future work. The inclusion of code and videos further strengthens the reproducibility and understanding of the research.

However, some points could be clarified to further enhance the paper. The explanation of why bilinear integration outperforms simple concatenation in the SNN architecture could be expanded. While the authors state it improves expressiveness, a more intuitive explanation would be beneficial. Additionally, the sensitivity of the MI bonus coefficient (αH) to performance isn't fully explored; a more systematic analysis of its impact would be valuable. Finally, while the paper demonstrates success with the Swimmer robot, further experiments with more complex and unstable robots (like the Ant) could strengthen the generalizability of the results.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the intuition behind why bilinear integration leads to better skill diversity compared to concatenation in the SNN?
*   How does the performance of the system vary with different values of αH, and is there a systematic way to tune this parameter?
*   The results for the Ant robot show instability. What specific modifications to the architecture or training procedure would be necessary to address this issue and achieve stable learning with more complex robots?
*   What is the computational overhead of using SNNs compared to standard neural networks for skill learning and policy optimization?
