

The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses challenges in sparse reward and long horizon tasks. It introduces a two-phase approach: first, learning a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, and second, leveraging these skills to enhance exploration and learning in downstream tasks. The key innovation lies in using a single proxy reward for pre-training that requires minimal domain knowledge, while the SNN architecture with mutual information regularization ensures diverse, interpretable skills. The framework demonstrates significant improvements over baselines across multiple challenging tasks, including mazes and object gathering scenarios.

The paper should be accepted. The approach is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning. The experimental results robustly support the claims about the effectiveness of the proposed framework, particularly the combination of SNNs with the mutual information regularizer.

The work makes a strong contribution by effectively combining elements from intrinsic motivation and hierarchical reinforcement learning. The pre-training phase with a single proxy reward is particularly compelling, as it requires minimal domain knowledge compared to previous approaches. The SNN architecture with bilinear integration and mutual information regularization successfully learns a diverse set of interpretable skills, which the authors demonstrate through thorough ablation studies. The experimental results across multiple tasks consistently show that the hierarchical approach with pre-trained skills significantly outperforms baselines, including the baseline of directly applying the proxy reward to downstream tasks. The paper provides clear evidence that the skills learned in pre-training directly transfer to downstream tasks, reducing sample complexity and enabling faster learning.

The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive. The authors evaluate multiple design choices (concatenation vs. bilinear integration, with and without MI regularization) and demonstrate how these affect skill diversity and downstream performance. The inclusion of multiple random seeds and analysis of variance across different pre-trained SNNs adds credibility to the results. The paper's focus on interpretability, where different latent codes correspond to distinct, meaningful skills, is a significant advantage over many previous hierarchical RL approaches.

To improve the paper, the authors should provide more detailed analysis of the mutual information regularizer's theoretical underpinnings. While the empirical results support its effectiveness, a more rigorous justification of why this specific regularizer is beneficial would strengthen the contribution. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of the relationship between the pre-training environment and the downstream tasks. While the paper acknowledges that the proxy reward should encode prior knowledge about useful behaviors, a more systematic exploration of how different proxy rewards affect skill diversity and transfer would be valuable.

The paper would also benefit from a clearer explanation of the relationship between the switch time T and task complexity. While the authors acknowledge that T affects performance in some tasks, the analysis in Appendix C.3 is somewhat limited. A more thorough exploration of how to optimally set T for different task characteristics would be helpful for practitioners.

The authors should also address the failure modes with unstable robots (like Ant) mentioned in Appendix D more thoroughly. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, providing a brief analysis of why switching between skills is challenging for unstable agents and suggesting potential mitigation strategies would strengthen the work's practical applicability.

The paper's approach to skill learning using SNNs with mutual information regularization is a significant step forward in hierarchical reinforcement learning. The framework effectively bridges the gap between intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, requiring minimal domain knowledge while enabling skill reuse across tasks. The experimental results are convincing, and the proposed method is both theoretically sound and practically applicable to a range of challenging reinforcement learning problems.

The paper's main contribution is the demonstration that a single proxy reward in pre-training, combined with SNNs and MI regularization, can effectively learn a diverse set of reusable skills. The experiments provide strong evidence that this approach significantly improves exploration and learning in sparse reward environments compared to existing methods. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in hierarchical reinforcement learning, particularly in developing more sample-efficient methods for complex tasks.
