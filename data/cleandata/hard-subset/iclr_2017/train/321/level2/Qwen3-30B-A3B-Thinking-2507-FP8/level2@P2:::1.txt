

**Review**

This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first pre-trains a diverse set of skills using a simple proxy reward, then leverages these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovation is using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a wide span of interpretable skills efficiently. The authors claim that this approach significantly improves exploration and learning efficiency across multiple tasks without requiring extensive domain knowledge.

**Decision**: Reject

**Key reasons for rejection**: The paper fails to adequately substantiate its central claims regarding the generality and efficiency of the proposed method, and the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor to support the claimed improvements.

**Supporting arguments**:

The paper's main claim is that their framework enables learning a diverse set of skills with minimal domain knowledge (via a proxy reward), which then significantly boosts performance on downstream tasks. However, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate that the "proxy reward" truly requires minimal domain knowledge. For instance, for the swimmer robot, the proxy reward is "proportional to the magnitude of the speed of the robot, without constraining the direction of movement." This is not trivial domain knowledge - it requires understanding that speed is a useful metric for locomotion skills. The paper fails to compare against approaches that might use more intuitive or less domain-specific proxy rewards, making the claim of "very minimal domain knowledge" unsubstantiated.

The experimental results, while showing improvement over baselines, suffer from significant methodological issues. The paper reports results for Maze 0-3 and the Gather task, but the evaluation lacks statistical significance testing. The high variance in the results (particularly noted in Appendix C.4) is not properly addressed - the paper doesn't explain why some pre-trained SNNs work well while others fail completely, and it doesn't provide sufficient analysis to determine whether the observed improvements are consistently significant across multiple seeds.

The paper's claim that "the combination is effective in learning a wide range of interpretable skills in a sample-efficient way" is not properly supported. The sample efficiency claim is based on training a single SNN versus training multiple independent policies, but the paper doesn't provide a fair comparison of the total sample complexity. The SNN approach may require similar or more samples per skill than training multiple policies, as the paper doesn't show that the SNN learns the same number of diverse skills in the same number of samples as the multi-policy baseline.

The paper also fails to adequately address the limitations of the approach. While Appendix D discusses issues with unstable robots like Ant, the main paper doesn't integrate these limitations into the core argument. The fact that the approach fails for unstable agents without clear solutions is a significant drawback that should be addressed in the main text.

**Additional feedback to improve the paper**:

1. Clarify what "minimal domain knowledge" actually means in practice. Provide examples of proxy rewards that truly require no domain knowledge, and demonstrate that these are sufficient for a variety of tasks.

2. Address the high variance in results. Explain why some pre-trained SNNs work well while others fail, and provide statistical significance tests to demonstrate that the improvements over baselines are consistently significant.

3. Provide a more rigorous comparison of sample efficiency. Show how the total sample complexity compares between the SNN approach and training multiple independent policies, rather than just comparing the number of samples used per skill.

4. Integrate the limitations discussed in Appendix D into the main paper. Explain when the approach is likely to fail, and what modifications might address these limitations.

5. Include ablation studies to better understand the contribution of each component (SNN architecture, bilinear integration, MI regularization).

**Questions for authors**:

1. Could you provide a concrete example of a proxy reward that requires absolutely no domain knowledge for a complex task, and explain why it would work across different domains?

2. How does your approach scale to tasks where the proxy reward is not obvious or where multiple proxy rewards might be needed?

3. Given the high variance in results (as shown in Appendix C.4), how do you recommend selecting which pre-trained SNN to use for a downstream task, and what is the expected performance impact of this selection?

4. How do you account for the fact that the MI regularization sometimes doesn't boost performance (as noted in Section 7.3), and what are the conditions under which it is most effective?
