

Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward, and then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors introduce Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a wide span of interpretable skills efficiently. Their approach combines the benefits of intrinsic motivation (requiring minimal domain knowledge for the proxy reward) and hierarchical methods (reducing sample complexity through skill reuse), demonstrating significant improvements in exploration and learning performance across multiple challenging environments.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem in reinforcement learning with a well-motivated and innovative approach that makes a clear contribution to the field. The empirical results are strong, showing consistent improvements across multiple tasks, and the ablation studies provide valuable insights into the impact of different design choices.

The key strengths of the paper include:
1. A clear and well-motivated problem statement addressing the challenge of sparse rewards in RL
2. A novel combination of SNNs with an information-theoretic regularizer for skill diversity
3. Comprehensive experiments demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple tasks
4. Good ablation studies showing the importance of different architectural choices

The paper could be strengthened by:
1. Providing more theoretical justification for the mutual information bonus, beyond the empirical results. While the empirical evidence is strong, a more formal argument would strengthen the contribution.
2. Expanding the discussion of limitations, particularly regarding the stability issues with more complex robots (mentioned in Appendix D). The paper currently notes these issues but doesn't adequately discuss their implications for the framework's applicability.
3. Clarifying the relationship between this work and prior skill discovery methods like HiREPS (Daniel et al., 2012) and Option-critic (Bacon & Precup, 2016), particularly regarding how their approach differs in terms of skill reuse across tasks.

The paper has excellent empirical results, but some figures (particularly the visitation plots in Figure 4) would benefit from clearer labeling to better illustrate the distinct skills learned. The current presentation makes it slightly challenging to fully appreciate the diversity of skills achieved.

I have two specific questions to help clarify the paper:
1. The paper mentions that the MI bonus "yields less overlapped skills and new advancing/turning skills" - could the authors provide specific examples of how the learned skills differ between the MI bonus and non-bonus cases, particularly regarding the "new advancing/turning skills"?
2. The paper states that "the MI bonus does not always boost performance" in some mazes. Could the authors elaborate on why this occurs and whether there's a way to predict when the bonus will be beneficial?

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning. The framework is well-constructed, the experiments are thorough, and the results clearly demonstrate the value of their approach for learning in sparse reward environments. The minor suggestions for improvement would help strengthen the paper's impact and clarity. The work is particularly valuable because it addresses a critical limitation of current RL methods while requiring minimal domain knowledge for implementation.
