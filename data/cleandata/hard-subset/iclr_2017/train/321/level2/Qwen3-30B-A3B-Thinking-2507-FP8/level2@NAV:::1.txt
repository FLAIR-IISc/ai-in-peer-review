

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards and long horizons through skill pre-training using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) combined with an information-theoretic regularizer. The main contributions are: (1) a general framework that learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and (2) a hierarchical architecture that leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks. The authors demonstrate that SNNs with bilinear integration and mutual information regularization can learn a wide range of interpretable skills, significantly improving exploration and performance on sparse reward tasks.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning, addressing a significant challenge with a well-motivated approach. The experimental results are compelling, showing consistent improvements across multiple maze and gather tasks compared to baselines, including the important demonstration that the approach works with minimal domain knowledge. The ablation studies on integration methods and MI bonus provide valuable insights into the design choices.

The main concerns are related to reproducibility and presentation. The paper would benefit from more detailed description of the MI bonus implementation (particularly how the discretization of the state space affects results) and a clearer explanation of why the MI bonus doesn't consistently improve performance across all tasks (as noted in Section 7.3). Additionally, while the authors acknowledge limitations with unstable robots like Ant, a more concrete discussion of when their approach might fail in practice would strengthen the paper.

To further improve the paper, I suggest adding a paragraph explicitly comparing the computational efficiency of their approach versus training multiple separate policies (beyond the sample complexity discussion in Section 5.2). Also, providing a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the different integration methods (concatenation vs. bilinear) would help readers understand when to choose one over the other.

I would be interested to know: How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the proxy reward in the pre-training environment, and what constitutes "minimal domain knowledge" for different types of tasks?
