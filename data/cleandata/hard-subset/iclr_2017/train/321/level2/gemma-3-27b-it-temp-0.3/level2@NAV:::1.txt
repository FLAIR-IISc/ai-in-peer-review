## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery and subsequent policy learning in sparse reward environments. The core idea is to pre-train an SNN with a simple proxy reward to learn a diverse set of skills, then train high-level policies on top of these skills to tackle downstream tasks. The authors motivate this approach by highlighting the challenges of exploration in sparse reward settings and the limitations of existing methods requiring domain knowledge or struggling with sample efficiency.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in RL. The combination of SNNs with an information-theoretic regularizer appears to be a novel and effective way to learn a diverse and interpretable set of skills. The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods on challenging benchmark tasks, showcasing the benefits of the proposed framework.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology. The use of SNNs is justified, and the inclusion of the mutual information regularizer is a valuable contribution. The experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple tasks and providing comparisons to relevant baselines. The ablation studies (e.g., impact of the MI bonus) provide insights into the effectiveness of different components of the proposed approach. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the potential instability when applying the framework to more complex robots like Ant.

However, some areas could be improved. While the paper provides a good overview, a more detailed explanation of the bilinear integration used in the SNN architecture would be beneficial. Additionally, the impact of the switch time (T) on performance could be explored more thoroughly, particularly for more complex tasks. It would also be helpful to discuss the computational cost associated with training SNNs compared to traditional policies. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the bilinear integration over other integration methods for the latent variable?
*   How does the performance of the proposed framework scale with the dimensionality of the state and action spaces?
*   What are the practical considerations for tuning the hyperparameters, particularly the MI bonus coefficient (αH)?
*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training the SNNs, including memory requirements and training time?
