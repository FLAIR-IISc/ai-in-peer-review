### Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper presents a novel and well-motivated framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training stage, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a simple environment with only a generic "proxy reward" signal that requires minimal domain knowledge (e.g., a reward for movement speed). To learn these skills sample-efficiently, the authors propose using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) where different skills are conditioned on a latent categorical variable. To encourage the learned skills to be distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, they introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state. In the second stage, these pre-trained and frozen skills are used in downstream tasks. A high-level manager policy is trained on top of the skills to select which one to execute for a fixed period, effectively simplifying the exploration problem and reducing the time horizon for credit assignment in the sparse-reward setting. The authors provide compelling empirical evidence on challenging continuous control benchmarks, showing that their method learns interpretable skills, significantly improves exploration, and achieves strong performance on tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to an important problem area in reinforcement learning. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:
1.  The proposed framework is elegant and effective, cleverly combining ideas from skill discovery, information theory, and hierarchical RL. The method of using a single SNN with a simple proxy reward and an MI bonus to learn a diverse skill set is a practical and novel approach to unsupervised pre-training.
2.  The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The paper includes strong qualitative results that provide intuition for why the method works (visualizations of learned skills and exploration patterns) and quantitative results on challenging benchmark tasks that demonstrate significant performance gains over a relevant baseline. The ablation studies effectively justify the main components of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision:**

The paper addresses the critical challenge of exploration in sparse reward settings. The proposed two-stage approach is a well-reasoned solution. By decoupling the learning of basic motor skills from the solving of a specific task, the agent can first build a foundation of useful behaviors in a simple, unsupervised manner.

The technical contributions are solid. Using an SNN to represent a multi-modal policy that captures different skills is a sample-efficient alternative to training multiple policies independently, a point well-supported by the experiments (Fig. 4). The introduction of the mutual information bonus, inspired by recent work in generative modeling, is a key contribution that demonstrably encourages the discovery of a more diverse and useful set of skills. The paper clearly shows the positive effect of both the bilinear integration in the SNN and the MI bonus.

The experimental results strongly support the paper's claims. Figure 5 provides a powerful visualization of how the hierarchical structure dramatically improves exploration over naive noise. The learning curves in Figure 6 show that this improved exploration translates directly into the ability to solve complex, sparse-reward tasks much more efficiently than a strong baseline (standard RL augmented with the same proxy reward). The comparison against the "Multi-policy" baseline is particularly important, as it shows the SNN approach achieves comparable or better performance with significantly fewer samples during pre-training. The inclusion of experiments on more complex agents in the appendix further strengthens the paper's claims of generality.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further improving the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Choice of Switch Time (T):** The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The appendix provides a good analysis of the sensitivity to `T`, but the main paper could benefit from a brief summary of these findings. It would be useful to discuss the intuition behind the choice of `T=500` and acknowledge that while a fixed `T` works for these static tasks, learning a termination condition would likely be necessary for more dynamic environments.
*   **Scalability of the MI Bonus Calculation:** The MI bonus is calculated by discretizing the 2D coordinate space. This approach is simple and effective here, but it may not scale gracefully to higher-dimensional state spaces. The paper mentions that an MLP could be used to estimate the posterior, which is a good point. A brief discussion in the main text about the potential limitations of the discretization approach and this possible extension would be valuable for readers interested in applying the method to other domains.
*   **Stability for Complex Agents:** The appendix honestly reports failure modes for the unstable "Ant" agent, where switching between skills can lead to the agent falling. Acknowledging this limitation in the main discussion section would strengthen the paper, as it highlights an interesting open challenge for this style of hierarchical control: ensuring smooth and stable transitions between skills, especially when they are learned independently.

**Questions for the Authors:**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the discussion period:

1.  How was the set of skills used in the "Multi-policy" baseline in Figure 4 generated? Were they selected as the 6 most diverse policies out of a larger set of trained policies, or are they simply 6 policies trained with different random seeds?
2.  In Section 7.3, you note that the performance on mazes can be highly dependent on the pre-trained SNN, as some may fail to learn a crucial skill (e.g., backward motion). This suggests that the pre-training does not guarantee a complete set of primitives. Have you considered any mechanisms to explicitly encourage a more comprehensive coverage of behaviors during pre-training, perhaps by rewarding visitation to novel regions of the agent's state space in addition to the MI bonus?
3.  Could you elaborate on the role of the task-specific state information `S_rest` (e.g., LIDAR readings) for the manager policy? The low-level skills are trained without this information. How does the manager learn to map from these sensory inputs to an appropriate motor primitive? For example, does it learn an intuitive mapping like "if a wall is detected on the left, select the 'turn right' skill"?
