### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the significant challenge of reinforcement learning in environments with sparse rewards and long horizons. The authors propose an elegant and general two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning. In the first stage, a diverse set of skills is learned in a "pre-training" environment with a simple, generic proxy reward that requires minimal domain knowledge (e.g., rewarding an agent for its speed). In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as primitives by a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on a specific downstream task with sparse rewards.

The core technical contributions lie in the pre-training stage. The authors propose using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent the entire skill repertoire, where different skills are invoked by conditioning the policy on a discrete latent variable. This allows for efficient learning via weight sharing. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and useful, they introduce an information-theoretic regularizer that adds a reward bonus proportional to the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state. This encourages the emergence of diverse and interpretable behaviors. The paper provides strong empirical validation on a set of challenging continuous control tasks, demonstrating that the proposed framework significantly improves exploration and accelerates learning compared to strong baselines.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a clear, well-motivated, and effective solution to a key problem in reinforcement learning. My decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Effective Framework for HRL:** The paper proposes a clean and practical framework for skill discovery and reuse. The decoupling of unsupervised skill acquisition (using a simple proxy reward) from task-specific hierarchical control is a powerful idea. It elegantly combines the benefits of intrinsic motivation (unsupervised exploration) and hierarchical RL (temporal abstraction), leading to substantial performance gains on difficult sparse-reward tasks.

2.  **Strong Technical Contributions and Empirical Support:** The use of SNNs regularized by a mutual information objective to learn a diverse skill set is a novel and well-executed contribution. The experiments are thorough and convincing. The visualizations of the learned skills (visitation plots) and the resulting exploration patterns provide compelling qualitative evidence for the method's effectiveness. The quantitative results on the downstream tasks clearly demonstrate a significant improvement over relevant baselines, and the ablation studies successfully justify the key design choices (e.g., bilinear integration, MI bonus).

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its thoughtful combination of existing ideas into a cohesive and practical framework. While HRL and skill discovery are not new, this work makes them significantly more applicable by removing the need for hand-engineering skills or specifying explicit sub-goals. The pre-training phase is guided by a single, generic proxy reward, making the approach widely applicable. The subsequent hierarchical training effectively reduces the problem's horizon, allowing standard policy gradient methods to succeed where they would otherwise fail due to the credit assignment problem, as clearly shown in the learning curves in Figure 6.

The technical novelty is also significant. Using an SNN to compactly represent multiple skills is more sample-efficient than training separate policies. The key insight is the introduction of the mutual information bonus, inspired by work like InfoGAN. This provides a principled way to prevent "mode collapse" (where the policy ignores the latent code) and explicitly encourages the skills to be behaviorally distinct. The ablation study presented in Figure 4 is excellent, clearly demonstrating that both the bilinear integration and the MI bonus are crucial for learning a rich and diverse set of skills. The paper's empirical methodology is sound, reporting results averaged over multiple pre-training and downstream training runs, which adds confidence in the conclusions and transparently shows the variance of the method.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for further strengthening the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Scalability of MI Estimation:** The mutual information bonus is estimated using a count-based method on a discretized 2D grid of the agent's (x, y) coordinates. This is effective for the locomotion tasks presented but may not scale to tasks where the relevant state components (`C`) are higher-dimensional or less obvious. A brief discussion on the scalability of this estimation, perhaps elaborating on the mentioned alternative of using an MLP regressor to approximate the posterior `p(Z|C)`, would be valuable for readers interested in applying the method to other domains.
*   **Choice of State Subset `C`:** The MI regularizer depends on a chosen subset of the state, `C`. In this work, the center-of-mass coordinates are a natural choice. It would be helpful to briefly discuss the sensitivity to this choice and provide some intuition on how one might select `C` in other environments where the choice is less clear (e.g., manipulation tasks).
*   **Comparison with Concurrent Work:** The paper appropriately cites Heess et al. (2016) as a similar, concurrent approach. A slightly more detailed comparison could be beneficial. For example, while the proposed method is more general by not requiring pre-specified goals, does the goal-conditioned approach of Heess et al. offer finer control over the learned skills? A sentence or two on such trade-offs would further contextualize the work.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  The posterior `p(Z|c)` is estimated via discretization. How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to the granularity of this discretization? Have you experimented with the alternative of training a neural network to predict `z` from `c`, and if so, how did it compare?
2.  The results in Appendix C.4 show that the performance on the Maze task can vary significantly depending on the set of skills learned during pre-training. This suggests that while the method produces diverse skills, it doesn't guarantee the emergence of a specific skill that might be critical for a particular downstream task. Do you have any ideas on how to encourage a more "complete" set of skills, perhaps by adapting the proxy reward or the regularizer during pre-training?
