This paper presents a novel and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) aimed at solving complex tasks with sparse rewards. The primary contribution is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of reusable skills is learned with minimal supervision, followed by a second phase where a high-level policy learns to compose these skills to solve downstream tasks. The skill discovery phase cleverly employs a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy, which learns multiple behaviors simultaneously. To ensure these behaviors are distinct and varied, the authors introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information, a key component that encourages interpretability and diversity.

I recommend to **Accept** this paper. The proposed method is well-motivated, addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning, and the claims are supported by strong and convincing empirical results.

The key strength of this work is its elegant approach to unsupervised skill discovery. The combination of SNNs for sample-efficient multi-skill learning and an information-theoretic bonus for encouraging diversity is novel and powerful. The experiments clearly validate the paper's claims. The visualization of the agent's trajectories (visitation plots) provides compelling qualitative evidence that the proposed method, particularly with bilinear integration and the mutual information bonus, learns a much richer and more distinct set of skills compared to simpler baselines. Quantitatively, the learning curves on challenging sparse-reward benchmark tasks (Mazes and Gather) demonstrate a significant performance improvement over non-hierarchical methods, proving the practical utility of the learned skills for enhancing exploration and accelerating learning.

For potential improvement, I offer the following suggestions. The main text would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the method’s limitations. For example, the instability encountered with the "Ant" robot when switching skills, detailed in the appendix, is an important finding that merits a brief mention in the main discussion. Additionally, the calculation of the mutual information bonus relies on discretizing a low-dimensional subset of the state space. A short discussion on the potential challenges and solutions for applying this to higher-dimensional or less-obvious state representations would strengthen the paper.

I have a couple of questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
1. The mutual information regularizer was applied to the 2D coordinates of the agent’s center of mass. How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to this specific choice of state features, and what might be a general heuristic for selecting these features in new environments?
2. The paper notes high variance on some tasks, caused by certain pre-trained policies lacking a crucial skill. It seems that fine-tuning the low-level skills during downstream training could alleviate this. Could you elaborate on the potential challenges of this end-to-end training that led you to leave it as future work?
