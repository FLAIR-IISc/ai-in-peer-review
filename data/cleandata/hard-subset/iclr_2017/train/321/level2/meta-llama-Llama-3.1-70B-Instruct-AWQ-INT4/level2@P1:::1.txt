**Summary**

The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment. The skills are then used as basic building blocks to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several tasks, including maze navigation and object gathering, and show that it outperforms state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods.

**Decision**

I decide to **Accept** this paper, with the following key reasons:

1. The paper tackles a challenging problem in reinforcement learning, namely, learning to solve tasks with sparse rewards. The proposed framework is well-motivated and provides a novel solution to this problem.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on several tasks, including maze navigation and object gathering. The results show that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper provides a clear and well-written introduction to the problem of HRL with sparse rewards. The authors provide a thorough review of the existing literature and motivate the need for a new approach. The proposed framework is well-explained, and the experimental results are thorough and well-presented.

The use of SNNs to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment is a novel and effective approach to HRL. The authors demonstrate that the skills learned in the pre-training environment can be used to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards, and that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods.

**Additional Feedback**

One potential limitation of the proposed approach is that it requires a large amount of data to train the SNNs in the pre-training environment. The authors could explore ways to reduce the amount of data required, such as using transfer learning or meta-learning techniques.

Additionally, the authors could provide more analysis on the interpretability of the skills learned by the SNNs. While the paper provides some visualizations of the skills, it would be helpful to have a more in-depth analysis of the types of skills that are learned and how they are used to solve downstream tasks.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to extend the proposed approach to more complex tasks, such as tasks with multiple agents or tasks with dynamic environments?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the implementation of the SNNs, including the architecture and training procedure?
3. How do the authors plan to address the potential limitation of requiring a large amount of data to train the SNNs in the pre-training environment?
