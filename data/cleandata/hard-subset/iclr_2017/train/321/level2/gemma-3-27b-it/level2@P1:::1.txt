This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning, coupled with an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity. The core idea is to pre-train a policy represented by an SNN on a simple task using a proxy reward, and then utilize the learned skills as building blocks for solving more complex, sparse-reward tasks by training a high-level policy on top.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea and demonstrates promising initial results. However, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient rigor and clarity, and the contributions, while potentially valuable, are not substantial enough for acceptance at ICLR given the current presentation. The primary concern is the lack of convincing demonstration of the benefits of the proposed approach over well-established baselines in a controlled manner.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Baselines & Comparison:** The comparisons are weak. While the paper acknowledges previous work, the comparison to baselines (particularly intrinsic motivation methods like VIME) is insufficient. It is stated that VIME is outperformed, but only in an appendix, and without detailed comparisons. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t compare against simpler hierarchical RL methods or skill discovery algorithms that do not involve SNNs, making it difficult to isolate the contribution of the SNN component. The paper argues that some baselines are difficult to apply directly (e.g., discrete skill learning in continuous spaces), but a thorough discussion of why alternative approaches were not considered would strengthen the justification.

2. **Experimental Setup & Reproducibility:** The experimental setup feels somewhat ad-hoc. The choice of hyperparameters is not sufficiently justified, and the impact of key components (like the MI regularization strength, αH) isn't thoroughly analyzed. The claim that the same hyperparameters work across different robots and tasks seems suspect. The paper mentions the large variability in performance depending on the pre-trained SNN, but doesn’t offer a clear explanation or mitigation strategy. The reliance on random seeds and the variation in performance raise concerns about reproducibility and the robustness of the proposed method.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper presents a complex system (SNNs, bilinear integration, MI regularization, hierarchical structure). It's unclear which component contributes the most to the observed performance. A systematic ablation study removing or modifying individual components is crucial to understand the interplay of these elements. For example, how much does the bilinear integration improve performance compared to simple concatenation of the latent variable? What is the impact of removing the MI regularization?

4. **Unclear Motivation for SNNs:** While the paper highlights the benefits of SNNs for representing multi-modal policies, the rationale for choosing them over other methods for learning diverse policies (e.g., mixture-of-Gaussian policies, variational autoencoders) isn't clearly articulated. Why are SNNs particularly well-suited to this problem?

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify the Role of the Proxy Reward:** The proxy reward is described as requiring "minimal domain knowledge". However, even choosing a simple proxy like robot speed still involves *some* domain knowledge. A more precise description of the assumptions underlying the choice of proxy reward would be helpful.
*   **Provide More Detail on the MI Regularization:** The MI regularization is a key contribution. The paper should provide a more in-depth explanation of its theoretical underpinnings and how it encourages diversity. It would be beneficial to show visualizations of the learned skills (e.g., trajectories, action distributions) to demonstrate the effect of the MI regularization.
*   **Address the Instability Issue with Ant:** The paper acknowledges that the method struggles with unstable robots like Ant. This is a significant limitation, and the discussion of potential solutions (e.g., learning a transition policy) should be expanded.
*   **Address the Switching Time (T):** The discussion of the switch time `T` and its influence on performance is relevant but feels incomplete. More detailed analysis and potentially adaptive selection strategies for `T` could be explored.
*   **Improve Visualization and Presentation:** Some figures are difficult to interpret. Clearer visualizations of the learned skills, the impact of the MI regularization, and the performance of different baselines would greatly enhance the paper's clarity.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed comparison to strong baselines (e.g., VIME, hierarchical RL methods) on the tasks presented?
*   What are the key hyperparameters, and how were they selected? What sensitivity analysis was performed?
*   How does the performance vary across different random seeds, and what steps can be taken to mitigate this variability?
*   What is the computational cost of training and using SNNs compared to standard neural network policies?
*   What is the justification for using bilinear integration instead of simpler alternatives for incorporating the latent variable?
*   Could you provide more detail on why SNNs are preferred over other methods for learning diverse policies, like mixture-of-Gaussian policies or variational autoencoders?
