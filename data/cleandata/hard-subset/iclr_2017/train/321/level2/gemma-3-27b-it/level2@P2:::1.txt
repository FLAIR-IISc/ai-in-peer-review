This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that learns skills in a pre-training environment and then leverages them for faster learning in downstream tasks. The core idea is to use Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) combined with a mutual information (MI) regularizer to efficiently learn a diverse set of skills, and then train a high-level policy on top of these skills. The authors demonstrate promising results on locomotion and maze navigation tasks with sparse rewards.

**Decision: Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of convincing evidence that the proposed method consistently outperforms strong baselines, especially given the experimental setup. While the paper shows improvements over some baselines, the performance gains are often modest, and the variability across different random seeds is high, particularly in the more complex environments. The paper relies heavily on ablation studies and visualizations to justify design choices, but lacks rigorous statistical analysis to support the claims of significant improvement. The benefits of SNNs and the MI regularizer are not clearly demonstrated in a consistently impactful manner.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains:** The results in Fig. 6 demonstrate only marginal improvements over the baseline using a proxy reward in the downstream tasks. The standard deviations are relatively large, which suggests the performance is unstable and sensitive to the initialization or pre-training process.  The Gather task shows more noticeable improvement, but the authors acknowledge this is partly due to the task setup favoring their approach.

2. **High Variance and Lack of Statistical Significance:** The significant variability in performance across different random seeds (as illustrated in Fig. 12 and discussed in Section C.4) raises concerns about the robustness and reliability of the proposed method. The paper doesn't present any statistical tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to determine if the observed differences are statistically significant, making it difficult to confidently claim that the improvements are not due to chance. 

3. **Unclear Justification for SNNs:** The authors argue that SNNs are beneficial for learning diverse skills. However, the justification primarily relies on visualization of the learned skills and comparison with independently trained policies. While these visualizations are helpful, they don’t provide concrete evidence that SNNs consistently learn *better* skills than alternative approaches, or that the multimodal nature of SNNs is crucial for the observed performance. A more rigorous comparison with a similarly sized deterministic neural network would be valuable.

4. **MI Regularizer’s Impact is Not Consistent:** The MI regularizer is presented as a key component for encouraging skill diversity. However, the results show that its impact is not consistent across different tasks, and sometimes doesn’t lead to significant improvements. The authors don't provide a detailed analysis of *why* the MI regularizer is helpful in some cases but not in others.



**Additional Feedback:**

* **Stronger Baselines:** The comparison with existing HRL methods is limited. Including more recent and competitive baselines, such as those based on option discovery or meta-learning, would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Ablation Study Depth:** While the ablation studies are helpful, they could be more thorough. For example, a study that systematically varies the size of the latent space in the SNNs could provide more insight into the optimal configuration.
* **Detailed Analysis of Skill Interpretability:** The authors mention the interpretability of the learned skills. A more detailed qualitative analysis of what each latent code represents and how it contributes to solving the downstream tasks would be beneficial.  Providing examples of successful trajectories generated by different latent codes would strengthen this claim.
* **Clarify Role of Proxy Reward:** The design of the proxy reward is crucial. More discussion on how to choose a suitable proxy reward for different tasks would be helpful.
* **Addressing Robot Instability:** Section D discusses the challenges with unstable robots like Ant. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the issues and potential solutions, even if they are not fully implemented.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide statistical analysis (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate the statistical significance of the performance improvements over the baselines?
* How sensitive is the performance to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, discount factor, and the MI regularizer’s weight?
* Could you compare the performance of the SNN-based approach with a deterministic neural network of similar size and complexity?
* Can you provide examples of trajectories for different latent codes in the SNN, illustrating the skills that are learned?
* How would you recommend choosing a suitable proxy reward for a new downstream task?
