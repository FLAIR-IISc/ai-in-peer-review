## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning and a high-level policy for downstream task execution. The core idea is to pre-train a diverse set of skills using a proxy reward, then utilize these skills to accelerate learning in sparse-reward environments. The use of SNNs, combined with a mutual information regularizer, is intended to learn a richer, more interpretable skill set than traditional methods.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach addresses a significant challenge in RL – exploration in sparse reward settings – with a novel and well-motivated combination of techniques. The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods, especially in complex tasks, showcasing the benefits of the proposed framework.

The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the problem, proposed solution, and experimental setup. The motivation for using SNNs and the inclusion of the MI regularizer are convincingly explained. The experiments on locomotion and maze tasks are thorough and the results consistently favor the proposed approach. The ablation studies and analysis of the learned skills further strengthen the findings, demonstrating the interpretable nature of the learned skills. The extensive supplementary material is particularly helpful, providing detailed experimental settings and further results.

However, the paper could benefit from further discussion of the computational cost associated with SNNs, especially concerning scalability to even higher-dimensional state/action spaces. While the results on Ant are presented as a failure case, more detailed analysis of *why* the approach struggles with unstable robots (beyond simply falling over) would be valuable. Further exploration of the optimal switch time ‘T’ could also yield insights into the interaction between the high- and low-level policies. 

Finally, it would be helpful to clarify how sensitive the performance is to the specific choice of proxy reward during pre-training. Could a poorly designed proxy reward negate the benefits of the SNN and MI regularizer? Addressing these points would further strengthen an already compelling contribution to the field.
