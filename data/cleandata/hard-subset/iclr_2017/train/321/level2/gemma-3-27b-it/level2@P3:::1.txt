This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning and an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity. The learned skills are then used as building blocks for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea of pre-training a diverse set of skills with minimal domain knowledge and then transferring them to various tasks is promising, and the experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing methods, particularly in sparse reward settings.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The work addresses a challenging problem in reinforcement learning – sparse rewards and long horizons – with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The combination of SNNs and the mutual information regularizer appears to be effective in learning interpretable and reusable skills. The experimental evaluation on multiple tasks supports the claims made in the paper.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the proposed method. However, several areas could benefit from further clarification and refinement. 

Firstly, the justification for choosing bilinear integration in the SNN architecture could be elaborated upon. While the paper mentions its superiority, a more detailed explanation of *why* it works better than simple concatenation would be valuable. Is it due to increased expressiveness, improved gradient flow, or other factors?

Secondly, the impact of the MI regularization coefficient (αH) is shown in Figure 4, but more analysis of its sensitivity and optimal range would be beneficial. A discussion of how this parameter might be tuned for different environments or tasks is warranted.

Thirdly, the paper states that the Manager Network (high-level policy) is trained with TRPO without modification, but doesn't detail how the discrete action space created by the SNN’s latent variables is handled within the TRPO framework. Clarifying this implementation detail would strengthen the technical rigor.

Fourthly, the ablation studies, while present, could be expanded. Specifically, comparing the performance of the full framework against just using an SNN *without* the MI regularization, and against standard HRL approaches *without* SNNs, would further isolate the contributions of each component.

Finally, the discussion of failure modes with the Ant robot is valuable, but a more detailed exploration of potential solutions (e.g., incorporating a recovery mechanism) would be a useful addition.

Specific questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the bilinear integration compared to concatenation, and how this impacts scalability?
*   What strategies could be employed to automatically tune the MI regularization coefficient (αH) based on the characteristics of the pre-training environment?
*   Can you provide more detail on how the discrete action space resulting from the SNN’s latent variables is handled within the TRPO algorithm?
*   What specific limitations of the current method prevent it from being effectively applied to tasks requiring extremely precise timing or rapid responses?



These suggestions are aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not considered critical for acceptance. The current work represents a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning and has the potential to advance research in sample-efficient learning for complex tasks.
