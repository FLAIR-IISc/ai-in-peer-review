Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a two-phase framework for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks. In a pre-training environment, a stochastic neural network (SNN) policy is trained to produce a diverse set of “skills” using a simple proxy reward (e.g., center-of-mass speed) and an information-theoretic mutual information (MI) bonus between a latent skill code and a relevant subset of the state (e.g., CoM position). A high-level “manager” then learns task-specific policies by selecting among these skills at a fixed temporal granularity.
- Key technical elements: (i) SNN policies with categorical latent variables realized via joint embeddings; (ii) a bilinear integration of latent and observation inputs that significantly improves the diversity and separability of skills over simple concatenation; (iii) an MI-based regularizer estimated via discretized posteriors p(z|c) that encourages skills to be distinguishable from state; and (iv) freezing low-level skills and training a manager with TRPO, treating z as a discrete action with commitment time T.
- Empirically, the approach learns interpretable, multimodal locomotion skills in pretraining and yields substantial exploration improvements and faster learning on downstream sparse-reward tasks (multiple mazes and a gather task) primarily with Swimmer and also with a higher-dimensional Snake robot. The paper includes careful ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear; with/without MI), extensive visitation plots, and multiple random seeds. The method fails on Ant due to instability when switching, which is discussed transparently as a limitation.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- The combination of SNN-based multimodal policies with an MI bonus is simple, well-motivated, and empirically effective at discovering diverse, reusable skills with minimal task-specific supervision.
- The hierarchical reuse of these skills measurably improves exploration and learning on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks, supported by ablations and qualitative analyses that clarify why the components matter.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Sparse rewards and long horizons are a recognized challenge; the proposed pretrain-then-reuse structure is a sensible compromise between intrinsic motivation and hierarchical control. The “shared agent-space” assumption is clear and reasonable for many control domains.
- Methodological clarity: The architecture and training procedures are clearly described. Treating z as part of the observation avoids intractable mixture policy issues with TRPO. The MI bonus is well-defined and computationally simple (count-based posterior on a discretized subspace).
- Evidence: The paper shows that bilinear integration and MI yield more diverse, interpretable skills than concatenation or no MI. Exploration plots with a random manager show markedly broader coverage than Gaussian action noise or multi-policy baselines trained separately. Learning curves across multiple maze layouts and gather tasks show consistent gains; the authors report multiple seeds (including variability across different pretrained SNNs), and provide code and videos.
- Scope and limitations: The approach is transparent about not jointly adapting skills during downstream training, the fixed switch time T, and limitations on unstable agents (Ant). The discussion proposes reasonable remedies (learned termination, straight-through estimators, transition-aware switching).

Areas for improvement (not blocking acceptance)
- Baselines: While there is an appendix comparison to VIME, stronger baselines in the main text would help (e.g., Option-Critic, HiREPS, or other option discovery methods), and a non-hierarchical goal-conditioned policy if feasible. Clarify whether the multi-policy baseline equalizes total samples (including its higher pretrain cost) when comparing.
- Accounting for pretraining cost: The paper targets many downstream tasks, but the reported learning curves focus on per-task training. Please quantify end-to-end sample savings over multiple tasks, including the one-time pretraining budget, to substantiate the “uniformly reduce sample complexity” claim.
- Generality of the proxy reward and MI target: The proxy (speed) and MI subset (CoM) are well matched to locomotion/navigation. The paper would benefit from either (i) a discussion on selecting proxies/MI targets in other domains (e.g., manipulation), or (ii) an additional non-locomotion domain demonstrating transfer.
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide more systematic sensitivity to K (number of skills), α_H (MI weight), grid resolution for p(z|c), and T in the main text (some is in the appendix). Report how often skills collapse without MI and how bilinear+MI scales with K.
- Stability and switching: For unstable agents, simple mitigations could be evaluated: warm-start blending between skills, short-term joint adaptation at switch time, or a learned transition policy.

Questions for the authors
- MI estimation: How sensitive are results to the choice of the state subset C and the discretization granularity? Did you try continuous density models for p(z|c), and if so, how did they affect training stability and skill diversity?
- Choice of K: How does performance scale with K? Do you observe unused or redundant skills, and could a sparsity or selection regularizer help?
- Sample accounting: Do the downstream learning curves include the pretraining cost? For a suite of N tasks, what is the net sample efficiency vs. training each task from scratch with intrinsic motivation?
- Manager training details: Did you use entropy regularization on the manager’s policy? Does encouraging manager exploration (e.g., via MI between z_t and future state) further help?
- Termination and duration: You study fixed T; did you try simple heuristics (e.g., value-based or variance-based termination) or a parametric termination head? Any evidence that adaptive durations would particularly help mazes vs. gather?
- Robust switching: For Ant, did you try short overlap blending or a brief low-gain handover to reduce discontinuities at switch time? Alternatively, pretraining with stochastic z switches to expose skills to off-nominal states?
- Generalization beyond locomotion: If the proxy reward is not “speed,” what guidance would you give for designing proxies/MI targets that induce multiple useful local optima in other domains?

Overall, this is a timely, well-executed contribution that advances practical skill discovery and reuse for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings. The paper is clear, the ablations are informative, and the empirical gains are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
