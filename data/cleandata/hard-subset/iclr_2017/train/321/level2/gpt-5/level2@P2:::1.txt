Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles sample-efficient learning in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by first discovering a diverse set of reusable low-level skills in a proxy pre-training environment, then learning a high-level “manager” that composes these skills to solve downstream tasks.
- Skills are represented by a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy conditioned on a categorical latent variable z; this induces multi-modality with shared parameters, enabling learning K skills with roughly the cost of one policy.
- To prevent mode collapse and encourage interpretable, distinct behaviors, the pre-training adds a mutual information bonus I(Z;C) between the latent z and a chosen subset of state features (e.g., CoM (x, y)), implemented via a simple posterior estimator p̂(Z|cell(x, y)).
- A high-level controller selects a skill every T steps (z fixed for T) and is trained with TRPO on sparse downstream tasks, while the low-level SNN is frozen.
- Empirically, the method learns clearly distinct locomotion skills (directions/gaits), dramatically improves exploration (visitation coverage) under a randomly initialized manager, and yields strong gains on hard sparse tasks (several mazes and a gather task) relative to baselines, including a multi-policy hierarchy trained at K× sample cost and intrinsic-motivation methods. Ablations show the importance of bilinear integration and the MI bonus; analysis covers switch time T and transfer to a larger “Snake” agent. Code is provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Simple, well-motivated synthesis of SNNs and an MI bonus yields a practical, general pre-training recipe for skill discovery with minimal domain knowledge, leading to markedly better exploration and performance on challenging sparse-reward tasks.
- Solid empirical evidence with ablations, transfer across tasks and agents, and provided code; the approach is novel relative to contemporaneous HRL and intrinsic motivation work and addresses a recognized bottleneck.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper squarely addresses sparse rewards and long horizons by decoupling low-level skill acquisition (guided by a proxy reward) from task-specific learning, which is compelling and broadly applicable when Sagent dynamics are shared across tasks.
- Placement in literature: The work bridges intrinsic motivation (proxy reward) and HRL (temporal abstraction), differs from GPS/trajectory-centric and option-critic lines, and is distinct from contemporaneous pretraining methods requiring goal instrumentation. The use of SNNs plus MI for skill diversity is a novel and well-placed contribution.
- Method soundness: The SNN architecture with bilinear integration is a good design choice for coupling latent codes and observations; keeping z fixed per rollout induces temporal coherence. The MI regularizer is simple to implement and aligns the learned skills with differentiated state-space footprints. Treating z as part of the observation permits unmodified TRPO. The hierarchical training is straightforward and stable by freezing skills.
- Empirical support: 
  - Skill diversity is convincingly demonstrated via visitation plots and qualitative behaviors; the MI bonus and bilinear integration materially improve distinctness and coverage.
  - Exploration coverage with a random manager is drastically better than Gaussian action noise and better than a multi-policy baseline that requires K× samples.
  - On mazes and gather tasks, hierarchical control improves sample efficiency and final performance; MI helps especially where fine navigation is needed (gather), and analyses explain when and why. Sensitivity to T is modest, and results scale to a more complex agent (Snake).
  - Limitations with unstable agents (Ant) are honestly discussed, with plausible remedies outlined; this does not detract from the main contributions demonstrated on stable locomotion.

Areas for improvement (non-blocking, constructive)
- Baselines and breadth: A direct empirical comparison with option-critic or other HRL baselines from 2016–2017 would strengthen claims. Including a non-hierarchical latent-conditioned policy (z resampled every T without a manager) could help isolate the manager’s contribution.
- Quantitative diversity metrics: Beyond visitation plots, report quantitative measures of skill separability/diversity (e.g., MI estimates, state-space coverage, skill-conditioned trajectory clustering purity, pairwise divergence of policy actions across shared states).
- MI estimator details and robustness: The grid-based p̂(Z|c) is simple but may be sensitive to discretization. Consider learning a density/classifier p̂(Z|C) and analyze sensitivity to grid resolution, batch size, and αH. Report training stability with the non-stationary reward bonus.
- Generality beyond locomotion: The proxy reward and choice of C are tailored to locomotion. A demonstration on a manipulation pretrain (e.g., generic interaction reward) and downstream tasks would substantiate generality.
- Adaptive hierarchy: Fixed T can be suboptimal; a learned termination policy or options-like termination could help, especially for dynamic tasks. Joint fine-tuning of low-level skills with straight-through estimators (as noted) would be valuable to mitigate mismatch and instability on some agents.
- Sample accounting: Provide wall-clock/sample counts separating pre-training and downstream learning to substantiate the “K×” pretraining efficiency and net gains across multiple tasks.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the MI coefficient αH and the number of skills K? Is there a practical heuristic for selecting them, and does the manager naturally ignore redundant skills?
- How is p̂(Z|c) updated over training—per-batch only or with a running estimator? How sensitive are results to the grid resolution and the chosen subset C? Did you experiment with learned p̂(Z|C) (e.g., a small classifier) instead of counts?
- Can you report quantitative MI values and coverage metrics during training to correlate with eventual downstream performance?
- In the multi-policy baseline, do the independently trained policies end up less diverse than SNN skills because of lack of MI regularization, or primarily due to sample inefficiency? What happens if you add the same MI bonus to each independent policy?
- Did you try entropy regularization or intrinsic bonuses at the manager level to encourage broader skill usage early in training?
- For unstable agents (Ant), did you attempt warm-starting the next skill with a brief blending period or learning a transition policy? Any preliminary results?
- To what extent does freezing the low-level policy limit asymptotic performance in downstream tasks? Do small amounts of joint fine-tuning help without destroying skill interpretability?
