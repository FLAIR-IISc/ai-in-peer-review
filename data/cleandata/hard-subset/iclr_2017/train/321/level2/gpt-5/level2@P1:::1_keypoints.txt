- Proposal: two-phase hierarchical RL — pretrain SNN skills with a proxy reward + MI bonus, then train a high-level manager that selects skills at fixed temporal granularity.
- Technical: categorical latent variables via joint embeddings to represent discrete skills.
- Technical: bilinear integration of latent and observation inputs improves skill diversity and separability vs. concatenation.
- Technical: MI-based regularizer estimated from discretized posteriors p(z|c) to encourage distinguishable skills.
- Technical: freeze low-level skills and train a manager with TRPO, treating z as a discrete committed action of duration T.
- Empirics: pretraining yields interpretable, multimodal locomotion skills and substantially improves exploration and downstream learning on sparse-reward mazes and a gather task.
- Empirics: results shown primarily for Swimmer, also for a higher-dimensional Snake; method fails on Ant due to instability when switching.
- Evaluation: careful ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear; with/without MI), visitation plots, multiple seeds, and provided code/videos.
- Decision: recommend acceptance.
- Primary reason: SNN + MI is simple, well-motivated, and effective at discovering diverse, reusable skills with minimal task supervision.
- Primary reason: hierarchical reuse of skills measurably improves exploration and learning on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks, supported by ablations and qualitative analyses.
- Motivation: sparse rewards and long horizons justify the pretrain-then-reuse structure; shared agent-space assumption is reasonable for control domains.
- Clarity: architecture and training procedures are clearly described; treating z as observation avoids TRPO mixture issues.
- MI implementation: computationally simple (count-based posterior on discretized subspace) and well-defined.
- Evidence: bilinear + MI yields more diverse, interpretable skills than alternatives; exploration plots outperform Gaussian noise and separately trained multi-policy baselines.
- Scope/limitations: no joint adaptation of skills during downstream training, fixed switch time T, and instability for some agents (Ant); authors discuss remedies.
- Improvement: stronger baselines (Option-Critic, HiREPS, goal-conditioned policies) should appear in main text and clarify equalized sample budgets.
- Improvement: quantify end-to-end sample savings across multiple downstream tasks including the pretraining cost.
- Improvement: discuss or demonstrate proxy reward and MI-target selection beyond locomotion (e.g., manipulation).
- Improvement: more systematic sensitivity analyses for K, α_H, grid resolution for p(z|c), and T in main text; report frequency of skill collapse without MI.
- Improvement: evaluate mitigations for instability/switching (warm-start blending, short-term adaptation at switches, learned transition policies).
- Question: sensitivity of results to choice of state subset C and discretization granularity for MI estimation; did continuous density models for p(z|c) help or harm?
- Question: how does performance scale with K, and are there unused/redundant skills — would sparsity/selection regularizers help?
- Question: do downstream learning curves include pretraining cost; for N tasks, what is net sample efficiency vs. training from scratch?
- Question: manager training details — was entropy regularization used and would manager exploration incentives (e.g., MI with future states) help?
- Question: termination/duration — any experiments with heuristics or learned termination and whether adaptive durations help specific tasks?
- Question: robustness for Ant — were blending, low-gain handovers, or stochastic z switches during pretraining tried to reduce switching discontinuities?
- Question: guidance for designing proxies/MI targets in non-locomotion domains to induce multiple useful local optima?
