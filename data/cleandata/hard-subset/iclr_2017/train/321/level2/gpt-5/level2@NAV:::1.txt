Summary and contributions: The paper proposes a practical framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that pretrains a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy with categorical latent codes and bilinear observation–latent integration, coupled with a mutual-information (MI) regularizer to induce diverse, interpretable skills using only a proxy reward. A downstream “Manager” policy operates over these frozen skills with a fixed commitment horizon, substantially improving exploration and sample efficiency on sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks. Claims include: (i) learning K distinct skills at roughly the cost of training one policy via weight sharing; (ii) MI-regularized SNNs reliably produce diverse, directionally distinct locomotion skills; and (iii) these skills uniformly accelerate learning on several maze and gather tasks. Code and implementation details are provided.

Decision: Accept (weak accept). Key reasons: (1) Clear empirical gains on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks with careful ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, MI strength) and multiple seeds; (2) A simple, general, and reproducible method that combines unsupervised skill discovery and HRL in a coherent way.

Supporting arguments: The paper is well-motivated within the HRL and intrinsic motivation literature and positions itself appropriately against HiREPS, Option-Critic, and goal-conditioned pretraining. The MI bonus over a meaningful state subset (CoM) is sensible and easy to implement. Evidence includes visitation plots demonstrating skill diversity, enhanced random exploration under the Manager, and improved learning curves across multiple mazes and gather settings; sensitivity to switch time T is analyzed; limitations on unstable agents (Ant) are candidly discussed. While the “K skills at the cost of one” claim is intuitively supported, a more direct wall-clock/sample accounting would strengthen it. Comparisons to stronger HRL baselines (e.g., option learning, feudal methods) are limited, but the provided baselines and ablations are still informative.

Additional feedback (to help improve, not core to decision):
- Quantify sample efficiency: report total environment steps and training time vs single-policy baselines and multipolicy training.
- Provide comparisons to Option-Critic/FeUdal/PPO-based options on the same tasks.
- Analyze sensitivity to K, αH, grid resolution for MI, and choice of state subset C; discuss guidance for proxy reward design.
- Consider adaptive commitment/termination and joint fine-tuning to mitigate switching failures on unstable agents.
- Discuss scalability with high-dimensional observations (images) and alternative MI estimators.

Questions for the authors:
1) How does performance scale with K (under/over-specification) and αH? 2) Can you report exact sample/time savings vs training K separate policies? 3) How sensitive are results to the CoM grid resolution and to the choice of C beyond CoM? 4) How does the method compare to Option-Critic or goal-conditioned policies on the same sparse tasks? 5) What failure rates arise from switching-induced distribution shift, and can adaptive termination reduce this?
