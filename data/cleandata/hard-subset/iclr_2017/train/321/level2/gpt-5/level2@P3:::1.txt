Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a two-stage framework for solving sparse-reward, long-horizon reinforcement learning problems. Stage 1 (pre-training) learns a diverse set of temporally extended skills in a task-agnostic environment using a simple proxy reward (e.g., locomotion speed). Diversity is achieved by parameterizing a policy as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with a discrete latent variable and encouraging latent–state dependence via a mutual information (MI) bonus computed on a low-dimensional, task-relevant state subset (e.g., CoM x–y). A bilinear integration of latent and observation features further improves skill separability compared to concatenation. Stage 2 (downstream tasks) freezes the low-level skill policy and trains a high-level “manager” that selects a skill every T steps to address sparse tasks (mazes and gather). The framework reduces exploration burden by committing to meaningful primitives. Empirically, the method learns interpretable and multi-directional locomotion skills, yields substantially broader exploration than Gaussian action noise, and improves sample efficiency and success on sparse maze and gather tasks relative to strong baselines. Ablations show the benefits of SNN multimodality, bilinear integration, and MI regularization.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated framework that combines SNN-based multimodal policies with an MI regularizer to reliably learn diverse, interpretable skills from minimal supervision; (2) convincing empirical results on challenging sparse-reward control tasks, including ablations that isolate the roles of SNNs, bilinear integration, and MI.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: The work targets a recognized weakness of deep RL—exploration in sparse/long-horizon settings. It bridges hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation by pretraining generally useful skills from a single proxy reward and reusing them across tasks. Relative to prior skill discovery and hierarchy methods, the approach avoids goal instrumentation (cf. Heess et al., 2016), uses a simple, scalable MI objective (inspired by InfoGAN) to prevent mode collapse and induce interpretability, and exploits weight sharing via SNNs to reduce pretraining sample cost compared to training K separate policies.
- Methodological clarity: The design choices are well motivated: discrete latent codes for multimodality, bilinear integration to modulate first-layer weights by the latent, a practical MI bonus computed via discretized posteriors over a small state subset, and TRPO with the latent treated as part of the observation to sidestep mixture complexities.
- Empirical evidence: 
  - Skill diversity: Visitation plots demonstrate distinct, interpretable behaviors per code (forward, backward, sideways), with bilinear integration and MI producing broader, less-overlapping skills.
  - Exploration: A random manager switching among pretrained skills explores orders of magnitude more space than Gaussian action noise; SNN-based hierarchies cover more directions than multi-policy baselines composed of separately trained policies.
  - Downstream performance: On multiple mazes and gather, hierarchical policies outperform a strong “proxy-reward-in-task” baseline and an intrinsic motivation baseline (VIME) under benchmark settings; MI is especially helpful in the longer-horizon gather.
  - Sensitivity and robustness: The paper reports variability across seeds and analyzes the effect of switch time T (Appendix), with limited sensitivity in static tasks. Limitations with unstable agents (Ant) are surfaced transparently.
- Relevance and impact: The proposal is simple, general, and practical (code available). It advances a compelling template for decoupling skill acquisition from task learning with minimal domain knowledge.

Suggestions for improvement (to help strengthen the paper; not all are essential)
Essential or high priority
- Comparative baselines: Include stronger hierarchical comparisons where feasible, such as option-critic (Bacon & Precup, 2016) trained on the same tasks, and the HiREPS-style mixture (Daniel et al., 2012) if practical. Clarify differences to Heess et al. (2016) with an empirical comparison where goals can be instrumented.
- Sample complexity accounting: Report pretraining cost and downstream learning cost separately and as amortized cost per downstream task. This will substantiate the claim of uniform gains across tasks and the K-fold efficiency from weight-sharing SNNs.
- MI estimator details and sensitivity: Provide a more thorough analysis of the MI hyperparameter αH, grid resolution for the posterior, and an alternative regressor-based estimator when state is higher-dimensional. Report diversity metrics (e.g., empirical I(Z;C), state coverage, pairwise behavioral distances) alongside visitation plots.
- Clarify assumptions: Make the Sagent/Srest factorization assumptions explicit in the main text and discuss how violations affect performance. Indicate what information the manager receives in each task, and whether it requires privileged state.

Nice-to-have (if time/resources allow)
- Ablations on K and latent structure: Study the effect of the number of skills K and alternatives to discrete latents (e.g., continuous latents with clustering at manager time).
- Broader tasks and modalities: Demonstrate transfer to tasks where the proxy reward is less aligned with downstream goals (e.g., tasks requiring precise turning or manipulation), and consider observations with learned embeddings from pixels.
- Ant robustness: Evaluate a termination/transition model or pretraining with switching to reduce instability when changing skills; even small-scale experiments would strengthen the narrative on unstable systems.
- Manager learning analysis: Quantify the relative contributions of temporal abstraction vs. skill diversity by comparing to a hierarchical agent with randomly reinitialized low-level policies matched for temporal commitment.

Questions for clarification
- Proxy reward design: How robust is performance to mis-specified proxy rewards? Are there guidelines for choosing a proxy that generalizes across a family of tasks beyond locomotion?
- MI target state: How was the CoM subset chosen, and how would performance change if a different subset or a learned embedding were used? Does maximizing I(Z;C) risk over-emphasizing directional diversity at the expense of other useful behaviors (e.g., gaits, stability)?
- Manager training: What is the impact of freezing low-level policies vs. allowing end-to-end fine-tuning with a straight-through estimator in terms of sample efficiency and stability?
- Variance across seeds: Can the variance in downstream performance be predicted from pretraining diversity metrics, enabling selection or aggregation of pretrained SNNs without cherry-picking?
- Skill duration T: Beyond the static tasks studied, how would an adaptive termination policy affect performance and stability, especially in dynamic environments?

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated, practical contribution with clear empirical benefits on sparse-reward control and a thoughtful analysis of design choices. The framework is likely to be influential and useful to the community.
