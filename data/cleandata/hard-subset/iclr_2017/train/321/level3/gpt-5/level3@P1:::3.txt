Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by (i) pretraining a set of reusable skills in a proxy environment using a single proxy reward, (ii) representing multi-modal action distributions with Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) where discrete latent variables select skills, (iii) encouraging diversity via a mutual-information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of state variables, and (iv) learning a high-level “Manager” that switches among frozen skills to solve downstream tasks. 
- Empirically, the method learns diverse, interpretable skills with a single pretraining run (vs training many separate policies), and the resulting hierarchy improves exploration and sample efficiency on challenging sparse-reward downstream tasks (several mazes and a gather task). 
- The paper includes thoughtful architectural choices (bilinear integration of latent code and observation) and ablations, code and videos, and useful analyses of failure modes (especially for unstable agents).

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) The framework is timely and well-motivated, combines ideas from intrinsic motivation and HRL in a novel, practical way (SNNs + MI bonus + hierarchical reuse), and demonstrates clear empirical gains on sparse tasks. (2) While some design choices (proxy reward, MI discretization) reduce generality, the paper is honest about limitations and provides ablations and analyses that make the approach and its scope understandable and reproducible.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Improving exploration and sample efficiency for sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks by learning reusable, temporally extended skills that a high-level controller can sequence.
- Is the approach well placed in the literature? Yes. It situates itself among HRL/options (Parr & Russell; Sutton et al.; Dietterich; Option-Critic), intrinsic motivation (Schmidhuber; VIME; count-based), and skill discovery (HiREPS; goal-conditioned pretraining), highlighting differences: pretraining with a single proxy reward, multi-modality via SNNs, and MI-based behavioral diversity inspired by InfoGAN.
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. 
  - Skill diversity: visitation plots show multiple distinct locomotion behaviors; bilinear integration and MI bonus matter (good ablation). 
  - Transfer to sparse tasks: hierarchical use of skills yields markedly better exploration and learning than strong baselines (including adding the proxy reward to the downstream task). 
  - Generality: some evidence beyond the 2-link Swimmer (Snake, and Ant in appendix with failure analysis). 
  - Limitations are acknowledged: instability with Ant when switching skills; sensitivity to MI coefficient; variable performance across pretrained SNNs, especially in mazes.
- Methodological clarity: Treating z as part of the observation to keep TRPO simple is sensible. The Manager’s fixed commitment horizon T is analyzed; end-to-end fine-tuning is discussed as future work.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all part of the decision)
- On “minimal domain knowledge”: The proxy reward and MI target require choosing “what to encourage” (e.g., speed magnitude; CoM (x,y) for MI). This is a mild but real form of hand-design. Please temper the claim and clarify the assumptions: one must know a task-invariant agent subspace and which state coordinates render skills distinguishable.
- Diversity as behavior richness grows: As the agent acquires many modes, skill collapse or entanglement is a risk. Consider adding diagnostics (e.g., entropy of p(z|c), pairwise trajectory divergence) and mechanisms to prevent redundancy (e.g., repulsive regularizers among skills or posterior coverage constraints).
- MI estimation and discretization: The grid-based posterior is simple but may not scale. A learned density estimator (e.g., classifier p(z|ϕ(c))) with appropriate regularization could handle higher-dimensional c, reduce binning artifacts, and provide uncertainty estimates. Please analyze sensitivity to grid resolution and batch size.
- Baselines and scope: Including Option-Critic and a goal-conditioned policy baseline (Heess et al., 2016) would strengthen positioning. Even if not identical settings, a qualitative comparison on similar setups would help. 
- Stability for unstable morphologies: The Ant results suggest the need for switch-aware training (e.g., pretrain with stochastic mid-episode switches, or learn per-skill entry policies). A termination policy or a short transition controller could mitigate falls.
- Skill economy: Discuss how to select K, prune redundant skills, and avoid negative transfer. A simple selection phase (e.g., policy distillation or sparsity in the Manager’s logits) could reduce overheads when K is large.

Questions for the authors
1. MI targeting and discretization
   - How sensitive are results to the choice of c for MI (e.g., CoM vs richer pose features)? Do you have results using a learned p(z|c) classifier instead of grids? How do grid resolution and αH affect skill diversity and downstream returns?
2. Proxy reward design
   - In domains where “speed magnitude” is unhelpful or unsafe (manipulation, contact-rich tasks), what proxy rewards do you anticipate? Can you provide guidance or examples beyond locomotion to support the “minimal domain knowledge” claim?
3. Scalability and embodiment
   - Beyond Swimmer and Snake, what performance should we expect for more complex robots when skills are switched mid-episode? Did you try pretraining with random switch times to improve robustness? Any evidence that end-to-end joint training (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) mitigates Ant’s failures?
4. Skill redundancy and K
   - How do you choose K in practice? Do you observe diminishing returns or skill collapse as K increases? Any metrics or procedures to merge or prune skills post hoc?
5. Sample efficiency accounting
   - Could you quantify total samples across pretraining and all downstream tasks, and contrast against training from scratch per task (with/without intrinsic motivation)? This would substantiate the “uniform sample efficiency gains” claim.
6. Manager policy and commitment horizon
   - How robust are results to T across tasks? Would a learned termination improve performance in dynamic tasks? Any preliminary results with an option-termination head?
7. Negative transfer
   - Do certain pretrained skill sets hinder learning on specific mazes (e.g., missing a backward mode)? Can the Manager learn to ignore unhelpful skills, or is a skill-selection/pruning step needed?

Overall, the paper offers a compelling and practical HRL recipe that shows clear benefits on sparse-reward benchmarks, with thoughtful analyses. Clarifying the degree of hand-design, the MI estimation details, and scalability to more complex embodiments would strengthen it further.
