Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a hierarchical RL framework that (i) pre-trains a low-level, multi-modal policy using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) under a simple proxy reward and a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage diverse skills, and (ii) trains a high-level “manager” that switches among these skills to solve sparse-reward downstream tasks. The technical choices are simple and elegant: categorical latents, bilinear integration of latents with observations to induce skill-specific dynamics, and a lightweight MI bonus computed via discretized CoM visitation. Empirically, the approach solves challenging sparse-reward locomotion mazes and a gather task that prior baselines struggled with, and demonstrates interpretable, reusable skills. The framework is broadly applicable and requires minimal task-specific shaping signals at pre-training.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- The hierarchical control contribution is interesting, practical, and shows strong empirical results on benchmarks that prior work failed to solve; the SNN+MI design is simple, effective, and well-motivated.
- However, the experimental analysis is shallow in critical places (role of MI on harder mazes, switching behavior and failure modes, and the precise factorization of state spaces), which limits scientific clarity and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: The paper targets long-horizon, sparse-reward tasks by amortizing exploration via reusable skills shared across tasks—concretely, sharing Sagent and actions while varying Srest. This is a pertinent and important problem.
- Motivation and relation to prior work: The approach to pre-training skills and then stacking a manager aligns with hierarchical RL and is closely related to Heess et al. (2016): both learn reusable low-level motor primitives. The bilinear latent–state integration and the MI bonus are well-placed in the literature (InfoGAN-style regularization; multi-modal policies). One concern: calling the proxy reward “intrinsic motivation” is misleading. Speed/grasp rewards are task-aligned priors akin to goal-conditioned or proxy-reward pre-training (as in Heess et al.), not task-agnostic curiosity or novelty signals.
- Empirical support: The framework robustly learns diverse locomotion skills and, with a manager, solves sparse mazes and gather. The MI regularization generally improves skill diversity and downstream performance (notably in Gather), but the paper also reports that MI does not improve performance on the more complex mazes (1–3). This is an important negative result that lacks interpretation—e.g., whether the extra diversity yields harder switching, misalignment with wall-following strategies, or manager credit assignment issues.
- Rigor and analysis gaps:
  - The factorization Sagent vs Srest is central yet underspecified for reproducibility. While Sagent follows Duan et al., Srest (e.g., exact LIDAR channels, goal encodings, walls) isn’t clearly itemized per task.
  - Switching behavior is under-analyzed: how the fixed commitment horizon T, the skill set, and the manager’s policy interact; failure modes (e.g., switching-induced instability for Ant) are acknowledged but not probed quantitatively.
  - The MI estimator’s discretization (grid size, bias/variance trade-offs) and sensitivity are not studied; likewise, ablations on K, bilinear vs concatenation beyond visuals, and fair sample-budget comparisons vs multi-policy baselines need strengthening.
  - Diversity is mostly shown via visitation plots; quantitative metrics (e.g., coverage, pairwise policy divergence, option duration distributions, MI measured on held-out rollouts) would bolster claims.

Evaluation against review criteria
1) Specific question: How to learn a diverse set of reusable, temporally extended motor skills that improve exploration and sample efficiency on sparse, long-horizon tasks via hierarchical control.
2) Motivation and placement: Strong. Clear connection to HRL, multi-modal policies, and InfoGAN-style MI; closely related to Heess et al. skill pre-training with noteworthy architectural simplifications.
3) Claims vs evidence: Largely supported for solving selected benchmarks and demonstrating interpretable skills. Analysis is weaker on when/why MI helps or hurts, on switching dynamics, and on the exact state factorization necessary to reproduce results.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Terminology: Reframe “intrinsic motivation” as “proxy reward pre-training” or “self-supervised skill pre-training”; clarify how this differs from curiosity/novelty bonuses (e.g., VIME).
- MI regularization:
  - Provide sensitivity to grid resolution, αH, and the choice of state subset C; report actual MI or conditional entropy reductions across training and tasks.
  - Analyze why MI helps Gather but not Mazes 1–3: e.g., does diversity induce frequent turning not aligned with long corridor traversal? Does the manager overfit to a few skills?
- Reproducibility: Precisely list Sagent and Srest for each task (state indices, normalizations, sensor specs), K, bilinear layer details, and how the manager observes and conditions on Srest.
- Switching analysis:
  - Report the empirical distribution of dwell times, switching frequency, and transition matrices between skills; add ablations over T with manager learning curves and final performance.
  - Quantify failure modes (instability after switches, especially for Ant); consider smoothing transitions or brief warm-up periods after switches.
- Fairness and ablations: Normalize pre-training sample budgets across multi-policy vs SNN and report performance per unit sample. Include ablations on K, concatenation vs bilinear, and manager architecture (FF vs recurrent).
- Baselines: Include Option-Critic and goal-conditioned pre-training (Heess et al.) baselines if feasible, or at least stronger discussion on differences and trade-offs.

Questions for the authors
- State factorization: For each environment, what are the exact components of Sagent and Srest (indices, dimensions, preprocessing)? Does the manager see both in full? Any task-specific masking?
- MI bonus details: How sensitive are results to the CoM grid resolution and αH? Did you try alternative C choices (e.g., velocities or headings)? Can you report estimated MI over training?
- Skill count and integration: How does performance vary with K and with concatenation vs bilinear integration quantitatively (beyond visitation plots)?
- Switching: What is the impact of T on manager learning and final performance across all mazes and gather? Do you observe specific failure modes tied to switching times or skill incompatibilities?
- Sample budgets: When comparing to the multi-policy baseline (trained with ~6× samples), how do results compare if the SNN receives the same total pre-training budget?
- Ant instability: Did you test transition smoothing, action interpolation across skills, or brief no-op periods to reduce falls? Any metrics on fall rates pre/post-switch?
- Manager policy: Did you try recurrent managers to use within-skill observations for the next switch? If so, does it mitigate the need for longer T or improve performance on mazes 1–3?
