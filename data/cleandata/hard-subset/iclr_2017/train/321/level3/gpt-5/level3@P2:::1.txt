Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a two-phase hierarchical RL framework for sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks. In a pre-training environment, a policy parameterized as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with a discrete latent variable learns a diverse set of temporally extended skills using only a simple proxy reward (e.g., CoM speed). Diversity is encouraged via an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between the latent and a task-relevant state subset (e.g., CoM position), with a practical discretized estimator. A “Manager” policy is then trained on downstream tasks to select among frozen skills at a fixed timescale T, greatly improving exploration. Key technical choices include bilinear integration of the latent with observations (improving skill separability over concatenation) and TRPO-based training that treats the latent as part of the observation. Experiments on the Swimmer (and Snake in the appendix) across multiple mazes and a Gather task show: (i) pretraining yields interpretable, multi-directional locomotion skills; (ii) hierarchical control markedly increases state space coverage; and (iii) downstream learning succeeds where standard exploration fails. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical evidence that the proposed SNN+MI skill pretraining combined with a simple high-level manager solves challenging sparse-reward benchmarks that defeated prior standard methods; (2) A clean, general, and practical method (including MI regularization and bilinear integration) that advances hierarchical control and sample-efficient exploration.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem: The paper tackles efficient learning across a family of tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons by learning reusable skills that improve exploration and reduce effective planning depth. This is an important and timely problem in deep RL.

2) Motivation and placement: The approach is well-motivated at the intersection of hierarchical RL and skill discovery. The use of SNNs for multi-modal low-level policies and an MI regularizer to induce interpretable, distinct skills is elegant and grounded in prior work (e.g., InfoGAN). The pretraining-with-proxy-reward setup is conceptually close to recent pretraining/goal-conditioned work (e.g., Heess et al. 2016). One caveat: branding the approach as “intrinsically motivated” is potentially misleading—rewards like speed or grasp success are proxy but task-aligned shaping signals rather than curiosity/novelty bonuses in the classical sense.

3) Do results support claims? The paper provides:
- Ablations showing bilinear > concatenation for skill diversity, and MI generally improves skill separation and exploration.
- Clear qualitative evidence (visitation plots, videos) that skills are distinct and interpretable, and that hierarchical control improves coverage dramatically.
- Learning curves showing consistent gains across mazes and Gather, outperforming strong baselines (including shaped CoM reward and prior intrinsic-motivation methods in the provided settings).

However, the analysis is thinner where results are mixed. Notably, the MI regularizer does not translate into better performance on the more complex mazes (1–3), and the paper offers limited interpretation or diagnosis. Sensitivity analyses (K, T, α_H, discretization grid), switching behavior, and failure modes are only partially addressed (some appear in the appendix). Reproducibility would benefit from a clearer definition of the factorization S_agent vs. S_rest per task.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive, not decisive)
- Terminology: Reframe “intrinsic motivation” as “proxy-reward skill pretraining” to avoid conflating with curiosity/uncertainty-based methods.
- Analyze when MI helps downstream performance: Provide skill-usage statistics in the mazes, representative trajectories, and a hypothesis for why added skills (e.g., sideways/turning) didn’t help maze 1–3. Are skills misaligned with corridor geometry or manager state inputs?
- Sensitivity and robustness: Include sweeps for K, switch time T, α_H, and MI grid resolution; report means ± CI across seeds for both pretrain diversity metrics and downstream returns. Clarify if Laplace smoothing is used for p̂(Z|c) to avoid zero counts.
- Factorization clarity: Explicitly list S_agent and S_rest for each environment (what sensors/variables go where) and the exact observation interface for the manager. This will aid reproducibility and interpretation of transfer.
- Switching analysis: Quantify switching-induced instabilities, dwell-time effects, and how T trades off exploration vs. reactivity. Consider a simple termination policy or learned switching as an ablation (even a small-scale experiment).
- Baselines and related work: Provide a more direct comparison/positioning to Heess et al. (goal-conditioned pretraining), and, if feasible, a baseline where pretraining uses multiple goal directions rather than MI, to disentangle diversity mechanisms.
- Metrics for skill diversity: Report MI estimates, inter-skill KLs over action distributions, or spatial coverage metrics to complement visitation plots.
- Unstable agents: The appendix notes Ant failures when switching. Summarize mitigation attempts (e.g., warm-start transitions, smoother interpolation) in the main text and clarify how general the limitation is.

Questions to the authors
- Exact factorization: For each task, what variables constitute S_agent and S_rest? Please provide a table listing all observation components (proprioception, LIDAR/goal sensors, etc.) and which are input to the low-level vs. the manager.
- Sensitivity: How do performance and skill diversity vary with K, T, α_H, and grid resolution for MI? Any evidence of over-diversification (skills that move but are hard to control for managers)?
- MI estimator details: Do you use smoothing for p̂(Z|c)? How do you handle rarely visited cells and batch-to-batch nonstationarity? Did you try learned density models instead of counting?
- Tradeoffs: Does the MI bonus reduce locomotion efficiency (speed) during pretraining? Please report proxy reward vs. MI across α_H.
- Maze performance: Why does MI not improve mazes 1–3? Are managers overusing a small subset of skills? Can you show skill-selection histograms along successful trajectories?
- Robustness across seeds: You mention no cherry-picking of pretrained SNNs. Could you include per-pretrain-seed results in the main paper and quantify variance reductions, especially for Gather?
- End-to-end adaptation: Did you try brief fine-tuning of the low-level SNN during downstream training (e.g., with straight-through estimators)? Does it alleviate maze failures or improve Ant stability?
- Generality: How portable is the approach to tasks where S_rest strongly interacts with S_agent (e.g., manipulation with contact-rich dynamics)? What proxy rewards would you use there, and how would MI be defined?
