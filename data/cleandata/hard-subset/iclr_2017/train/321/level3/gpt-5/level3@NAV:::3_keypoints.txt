- Paper proposes pretraining skills with stochastic neural networks + MI bonus and a high-level manager; technical choices and implementation are clearly presented, with code/videos provided.  
- Empirically shows interpretable locomotion behaviors and improved downstream learning on maze and gather tasks.  
- Decision: Reject (borderline).  
- Main rejection reason: Claims of minimal domain knowledge and broad generality are overstated—intrinsic rewards and MI targets are hand-crafted for locomotion.  
- MI estimation relies on a discretized CoM grid (Sec. 5.3), raising scaling and robustness concerns for continuous/high-dimensional settings.  
- Experiments are limited to a few locomotion domains with tailored proxy rewards (e.g., speed) and CoM-based MI, limiting evidence of generality.  
- Diversity evaluation is largely qualitative (visitation plots); improvements are not uniformly consistent and failure modes (Ant instability, skill overlap) emerge with richer behaviors.  
- Unclear sensitivity to grid mesh density and how discretization extends beyond 2D CoM or to continuous MI estimators.  
- Main narrative centers on simple morphologies; robustness to complex bodies and non-locomotion tasks is not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Reward and MI target choices require domain knowledge (speed, CoM, grasp success), contradicting the “very minimal domain knowledge” claim.  
- Suggestions: use continuous MI estimators (e.g., variational MI/MINE) and report sensitivity to representation and αH.  
- Suggestions: quantify skill diversity (state coverage, option dissimilarity, MI achieved) and report sensitivity to K and switch time T.  
- Clarify whether any state discretization occurs during training vs. only for MI estimation, and behavior in continuous/high-dimensional states.  
- Test performance when CoM is unavailable or irrelevant (manipulation) and evaluate alternative choices for C.  
- Strengthen experiments on complex morphologies (Ant/Humanoid) and non-locomotion tasks, and compare to option-critic and goal-conditioned baselines.  
- Explore end-to-end fine-tuning with learned terminations to mitigate instability and skill-switching failures.
