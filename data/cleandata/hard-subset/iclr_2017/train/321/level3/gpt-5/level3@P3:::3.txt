1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a two-stage framework for solving sparse-reward, long-horizon RL tasks. In pre-training, a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy conditions on a discrete latent code to learn a diverse set of skills using a single proxy reward. Diversity is encouraged by an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between the latent code and a subset of state variables. In downstream tasks, a high-level “manager” selects among the frozen skills at a fixed period, greatly improving exploration. Key technical elements are: (i) SNNs to represent multi-modal action distributions via a latent code; (ii) bilinear integration of latent and observation features to strengthen conditioning; (iii) an MI bonus on selected state components to discourage mode collapse and increase interpretability; and (iv) a simple hierarchical composition with TRPO. Empirically, the approach learns a visibly diverse repertoire of locomotion behaviors and significantly improves learning on sparse-reward mazes and gather tasks. Ablations show the importance of bilinear integration and MI, and appendices report results on more complex robots.

2. Decision
Weak accept, for a practical and well-motivated HRL approach that produces interpretable skills and materially improves exploration on sparse tasks, with clear ablations and open-sourced code. However, several claims (minimal domain knowledge, generality, and robustness) are stronger than the evidence presented, and key design choices (state discretization for MI, choice of proxy/MI signals, fixed switching) need clarification and/or further evaluation.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper targets efficient learning across a family of sparse-reward tasks by pretraining reusable motor skills and composing them hierarchically.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. It bridges intrinsic motivation and HRL, relates to options, HiREPS, Option-Critic, and recent pretraining-for-transfer work. The use of SNNs for multi-modality and an MI regularizer (InfoGAN-inspired) is sensible and novel in this context. The architectural choice of bilinear integration is well argued and empirically justified.
- Do results support the claims?
  • Positive: Clear qualitative and quantitative evidence that SNN+MI yields more diverse locomotion behaviors than single policies; random manager with pretrained skills dramatically enlarges exploration; hierarchical training solves sparse mazes and gather tasks where standard baselines struggle. Ablations on concatenation vs bilinear integration and MI coefficient are helpful.
  • Concerns: (i) Failure modes grow with behavioral richness and morphology complexity. As K and embodiment complexity increase, the MI on a small state subset (CoM x,y) may not be sufficient to prevent overlapping or redundant skills; switching can destabilize agents (Ant) and cause rollouts to terminate, and the manager may only exploit a subset of robust skills. (ii) Discretization in Sec. 5.3 for MI estimation introduces nontrivial design choices (grid size, coverage, bias) and may limit applicability to higher-dimensional c; continuous estimators are mentioned but not evaluated. (iii) Generality: While Snake results in the appendix are encouraging, the Ant case exposes fragility. Evidence that the approach scales with morphology and larger action/state spaces is mixed. (iv) Minimal domain knowledge claim seems overstated: choosing a speed-based proxy reward and selecting the CoM (x,y) for the MI objective are hand-designed and tailored to translational locomotion; for manipulation, a “grasp any object” proxy still requires instrumentation and task-specific sensing. (v) Fixed commitment time T and frozen low-level skills may be suboptimal; sensitivity analyses are limited (some in Appendix C.3) and no learned termination is demonstrated.

4. Additional feedback and questions for the authors (to improve the paper; not all affect the decision)
- Discretization and MI estimation:
  • Please clarify precisely what is discretized in Sec. 5.3 (only the CoM (x,y) used in the MI bonus, not the RL state/action spaces), and report sensitivity to grid resolution and domain bounds. How does grid choice affect the diversity metric and downstream performance?
  • You mention fitting an MLP for higher-dimensional c. Could you report results with a continuous estimator (e.g., classifier-based MI lower bound) and compare to the grid? Does continuous estimation improve stability/diversity as K grows?
  • How do you handle coverage bias when many cells have low counts? Any smoothing or priors?

- Failure modes as behavior richness increases:
  • How does skill diversity and downstream performance scale with K? Do you observe saturation or collapse beyond some K? Any regularization (e.g., entropy over skills used by the manager) to avoid mode under-utilization?
  • Can you quantify diversity with a metric (e.g., inter-skill trajectory distances or coverage) rather than only visitation plots?

- Generality and embodiment:
  • The Snake results are helpful; could you include main-text figures summarizing performance vs Swimmer to support generality claims?
  • For Ant, can you mitigate instability by (i) skill-specific warm starts or short transition controllers; (ii) learning a termination/switching policy; or (iii) training with randomized initializations to increase skill robustness to off-manifold starts? Any preliminary evidence?
  • How does training time/sample efficiency grow with state/action dimension across embodiments?

- Hand-design of rewards and “minimal domain knowledge”:
  • The proxy reward (speed magnitude) and MI state choice (CoM (x,y)) encode strong locomotion priors. Please nuance the claim of minimal domain knowledge and discuss how to select proxy/MI signals in other domains (e.g., manipulation with orientation, contact events). Can the subset c be learned (e.g., via attention) rather than chosen?
  • Have you tried alternative, less task-specific proxies (e.g., state coverage/novelty) and compared transfer performance?

- Hierarchy and training details:
  • Sensitivity to the switch time T is partly in Appendix C.3; could you expand to Swimmer and include variance bars? Have you tried learned termination?
  • The manager trains with sparse rewards only. Any shaping on the manager side (e.g., skill-usage entropy) that improves exploration?
  • How often does the manager use each skill during downstream training? Reporting usage distributions would help assess whether diversity translates into utility.

- Comparisons and scope:
  • Option-Critic or other hierarchical baselines with learned terminations would strengthen the empirical position.
  • Please report statistical significance for the main curves and include per-seed results in the supplement; some tasks show high variance.

Overall, the paper is timely and practical, with clear empirical wins and a simple recipe practitioners can adopt. Tightening the claims around domain knowledge, clarifying the MI estimator and discretization, and expanding evidence on embodiment/generalization would make the contribution stronger.
