Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a hierarchical RL framework that pre-trains a multi-modal low-level policy using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with a mutual information (MI) regularizer, and then trains a high-level manager to switch among these skills with a fixed commitment time. The approach is well-engineered: bilinear integration of latent codes improves skill separability, and weight sharing in SNNs yields sample-efficient skill acquisition. Empirically, the method solves sparse-reward locomotion-with-mazes and gather tasks that prior baselines could not, and shows transfer to larger agents (Snake). The contribution is interesting and complementary to prior hierarchical control and pre-training (e.g., Heess et al.), with strong empirical performance.

Decision: Accept
Reason: Strong empirical results on previously unsolved sparse tasks with a simple, general hierarchical pre-train/transfer pipeline; the MI-regularized SNN skill learning is elegant and practically useful.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem: How to acquire reusable, diverse low-level skills in a minimal pre-training environment and leverage them to efficiently solve long-horizon, sparse downstream tasks via hierarchical control. This is a concrete and relevant question.
2) Motivation and placement: The work is well positioned relative to options, skill discovery, and recent pre-training approaches. However, calling the proxy-reward pre-training “intrinsic motivation” is misleading; speed/grasp rewards are task-aligned and closer to prior goal-conditioned pre-training (Heess et al.) than curiosity/novelty bonuses.
3) Evidence and rigor: Results are compelling overall and reproducible code is provided. Yet the experimental analysis is comparatively weak where it matters: MI often helps skill diversity but does not improve performance on harder mazes (1–3); the paper notes this but offers little interpretation. Analysis of switching dynamics (timing T, failure modes, instability on Ant) is limited and mostly anecdotal. Reproducibility also suffers from an underspecified factorization between S_agent and S_rest—Duan et al. define S_agent, but S_rest needs precise documentation.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Clarify and formalize the S = S_agent ⊕ S_rest factorization for each domain; list state variables in each part to enable replication.
- Provide a deeper analysis of MI: report MI/entropy metrics, posterior estimation details (grid resolution, counts smoothing), and sensitivity to α_H; explain why MI does not help mazes 1–3.
- Analyze switching behavior: distributions over skill usage, sensitivity to T (beyond appendices), per-skill success/failure, and failure cases on unstable agents; consider termination policies or transition-aware training.
- Calibrate terminology: avoid “intrinsic motivation” unless using task-agnostic curiosity/novelty rewards.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely what variables constitute S_rest in each task? Any interactions with S_agent beyond “weak coupling” that could violate assumptions?
- How sensitive are results to K, α_H, grid resolution for MI, and bilinear vs. concatenation?
- Why does MI not help on mazes 1–3? Is it due to over-specialized lateral/turning skills or manager credit assignment?
- Can you report quantitative switching statistics (skill dwell times, usage entropy) and correlate them with success?
- Have you tried a learned termination policy or end-to-end fine-tuning to mitigate switching-induced instability (e.g., Ant)?
