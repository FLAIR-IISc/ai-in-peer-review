1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by first learning a diverse set of reusable low-level skills in a proxy pre-training environment and then training a high-level “manager” to sequence these skills for downstream tasks. The key technical ideas are: (i) modeling low-level policies as Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) conditioned on a categorical latent variable to induce multimodal action distributions while sharing parameters across skills; (ii) a mutual-information (MI) regularizer between the latent code and a small subset of the state (e.g., CoM (x, y) for locomotion) to encourage behaviorally distinct, interpretable skills; (iii) a simple hierarchical policy that commits to a chosen skill for T steps to improve exploration and credit assignment under sparse rewards. Empirically, on locomotion+maze and gather benchmarks where standard exploration fails, the learned skills substantially widen exploration and enable learning; the MI term reliably increases skill diversity and interpretability. The framework is simple, sample-efficient (learns K skills for the cost of one policy), and comes with code and clear engineering details.

2. Decision
Accept (weak accept), primarily because:
- The hierarchical pretrain-and-reuse framework with SNNs is simple and effective, solving challenging sparse-reward benchmarks that prior methods could not.
- The MI regularizer is an elegant addition that consistently improves skill diversity and interpretability, and often improves downstream performance.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Enabling efficient exploration and learning on sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks by discovering and reusing low-level skills that are agent-centric and task-agnostic.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It is squarely within HRL and skill discovery, relates to options, HiREPS, and recent pretraining-for-transfer (notably Heess et al.). Using MI to bind latent codes to behavior echoes InfoGAN-style disentanglement. The work is differentiated by (a) learning with a single proxy reward rather than task-specific goals; (b) SNN-based multimodality with explicit weight sharing; and (c) a simple, effective manager over frozen skills.

Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The paper demonstrates:
- Skill learning: SNN+MI yields diverse locomotion behaviors (directions and gaits), with bilinear integration outperforming concatenation. Visitation plots and qualitative analyses support interpretability and mode separation.
- Exploration: Random manager over pretrained skills covers significantly larger areas than Gaussian action noise or single-policy baselines.
- Downstream performance: On multiple mazes and gather, the hierarchical approach outperforms strong baselines (including adding the proxy reward to the downstream task); with longer horizons the hierarchy is especially beneficial.
However, the experimental analysis is thinner than the results deserve. Notably, MI does not boost performance on the harder mazes, and the paper acknowledges this without deeper diagnosis. Variance across seeds can be high on some tasks; more quantitative analysis of skill usage, switching, and failure modes would strengthen the empirical case. The “intrinsic motivation” framing is also somewhat misleading: the proxy reward (speed/grasping) is extrinsic and task-aligned, closer to prior pretraining (e.g., Heess et al.) than curiosity/novelty-based intrinsic motivation.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Clarify terminology and positioning. Calling the proxy reward “intrinsic motivation” is misleading; it is a designer-specified extrinsic proxy encouraging agent-centric capabilities. Please discuss the distinction and relate more directly to goal-conditioned pretraining (Heess et al., 2016) and option-critic.
- Analyze why MI helps diversity but not downstream performance in Mazes 1–3. Hypotheses to test: (a) manager can exploit wall-aided reorientation, making sideways/turning skills less critical; (b) skill sets learned with high MI may over-specialize to pretrain dynamics; (c) commitment horizon T interacts with skill set. Provide quantitative diagnostics: skill selection frequencies, manager policy entropy, option dwell times, and success conditioned on having specific directional skills.
- Factorization Sagent/Srest. For reproducibility, explicitly list the exact observation components assigned to Sagent and Srest for each environment (including all LIDAR/sensor channels). This matters for the manager’s inputs and for defining the MI target subset.
- Switching analysis. Add systematic studies of T on the main Swimmer tasks (analogous to the Snake appendix), and report sensitivity to T and to the manager’s switching policy. Provide ablations with learned termination versus fixed T, and report failure modes (e.g., instability upon switching for Ant) with quantitative metrics (fall rate after switches, success rate vs. switch frequency).
- Skill diversity metrics. Beyond visitation plots, quantify diversity (e.g., coverage area, pairwise trajectory divergence, or mutual confusion of a z-classifier). Report MI estimates achieved and overlap between skills.
- Ablations and baselines. Include: (i) SNN without bilinear integration; (ii) end-to-end fine-tuning vs. frozen skills; (iii) number of skills K; (iv) comparison to option-critic and to goal-conditioned pretraining baselines where possible.
- MI implementation details. Report sensitivity to αH and grid density, and whether using a learned posterior (MLP) versus counting changes results. Discuss whether focusing MI on CoM could bias against other useful behaviors and how to generalize to manipulators.
- Variance and seed handling. Given high variance on mazes, provide success rates across seeds and per-pretrained-SNN diagnostics that predict downstream success.

Questions for the authors
- Please precisely define Sagent and Srest for all tasks. What exact observation vectors are fed to the low-level policy and to the manager?
- How sensitive are results to αH, grid resolution, and the choice of state subset for MI? Did you try learned posteriors instead of counting?
- How was T chosen for Swimmer experiments? Can you show performance vs. T on those tasks?
- Can you report manager behavior statistics (skill selection distribution over time, dwell times, entropy) and correlate them with success?
- Did you try end-to-end fine-tuning of low-level skills during downstream training (e.g., with straight-through estimators)? If so, what changed?
- For Ant, can you quantify the probability of failure after a switch and explore mitigations (e.g., transition policies, warm starts, smoother blending)?
- How does performance scale with K, and is there evidence of diminishing returns or skill redundancy?
- Can you compare directly to Heess et al. (2016)-style goal-conditioned pretraining under matched budgets to isolate the benefit of MI-based diversity?
