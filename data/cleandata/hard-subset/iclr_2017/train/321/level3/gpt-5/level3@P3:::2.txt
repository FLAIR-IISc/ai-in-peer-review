Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by decoupling skill acquisition from task learning: first, learn a diverse set of temporally extended skills in a proxy pretraining environment; then learn a high-level “manager” that composes these skills to solve downstream sparse tasks.
- Skills are parameterized by stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with a categorical latent z; bilinear integration between z and observations yields multi-modal policies while sharing weights.
- An information-theoretic regularizer maximizes mutual information between z and a small, task-agnostic subset of state (e.g., CoM (x, y)), encouraging option identifiability and diversity.
- The hierarchical policy (manager over frozen skills with a fixed switching period T) substantially improves exploration and sample efficiency on mazes and gather, compared to intrinsic-reward baselines.
- Strong empirical observation: simply adding the intrinsic proxy reward to sparse tasks performs poorly, highlighting the value of hierarchical abstraction and/or enforced diversity during skill learning.
- The paper provides clear visualizations (visitation plots) demonstrating interpretable, distinct skills and the impact of design choices (concatenation vs bilinear; MI strength).

Decision
Accept (7/10; increased from 6)
Key reasons: (1) Clean, well-motivated integration of SNNs and MI regularization that yields robustly diverse skills and improved hierarchical exploration; (2) Strong empirical evidence that hierarchy + diversity outperforms direct intrinsic-reward baselines in sparse tasks, with compelling and interpretable visualizations.

Support for decision
1) Problem: The work addresses the core challenge of exploration and credit assignment in sparse-reward, long-horizon control, and the need to amortize exploration across tasks sharing an “agent-space.”
2) Motivation and placement: The approach is well situated relative to options/HRL, intrinsic motivation, and InfoGAN-style MI. Using SNNs to induce multi-modality with shared parameters is intuitive and practical. The MI bonus operationalizes option identifiability in a simple, scalable way.
3) Claims and evidence:
- Diversity: Ablations show bilinear conditioning and MI significantly expand the span of learned locomotion skills beyond concatenation/vanilla SNNs.
- Hierarchical exploration: One-million-step rollouts with random managers show much broader coverage using SNN-based skills than Gaussian noise or multi-policy baselines.
- Downstream performance: On multiple mazes and gather, hierarchical policies outperform the “sparse + intrinsic proxy” baseline, especially at longer horizons. The paper avoids cherry-picking and reports variance across pretraining seeds.
- Rigor: Training details (TRPO, batch sizes), architecture choices, and ablations are documented; limitations on unstable robots (Ant) are candidly discussed.

Supporting arguments and concerns
- Limited diversification capacity: Conditioning via concatenation or a single bilinear layer mostly induces per-option differences in the first layer (bias shift vs input-weight gating). This can suffice for locomotion directions/gaits but may be too restrictive for tasks requiring qualitatively distinct control programs (e.g., dexterous manipulation with contact-specific feedback). Richer conditioning (e.g., deeper multiplicative conditioning/FiLM across layers, hypernetworks, or option-specific low-level modules) could enable more diverse sub-policies without bloating parameters.
- Fixed hierarchy: The manager operates over frozen skills and a fixed time scale T. This makes performance depend heavily on (i) the quality/domain-match of the intrinsic proxy and (ii) prelearned skills. It also limits temporal adaptivity. While the paper mentions straight-through estimators and learned termination as future work, these are important to mitigate brittleness and reduce the apparent domain specificity of “unsupervised” rewards.
- Domain specificity of intrinsic rewards: The MI is computed on CoM (x, y); for locomotion this is natural but makes the “unsupervised” objective tailored to locomotion. For manipulation, a different subset (e.g., end-effector pose, object motion) would be needed—approaching supervised task proxies. This tension is acknowledged, but general solutions (e.g., empowerment/coverage over agent-state subsets learned automatically) would strengthen the claim of minimal domain knowledge.
- Accounting for experience: Pretraining adds interaction steps not available to baselines; fairness hinges on reporting total environment steps to reach performance targets. The paper notes multi-policy uses 6× pretrain samples; clearer normalization across methods would help.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
- Report total environment interactions (pretrain + downstream) to reach success thresholds and compare to intrinsic baselines with equalized total steps.
- Quantify skill diversity beyond plots (e.g., I(Z;C) achieved, pairwise skill occupancy overlap, coverage metrics) and control for parameter count when comparing concatenation vs bilinear integration.
- Explore deeper/multi-layer conditioning of z and/or lightweight option-specific adapters to expand sub-policy diversity without exploding parameters.
- Consider a learned termination policy and variable T; even simple hazard-aware termination might alleviate instability on Ant-like robots.
- Discuss how to select the “relevant subset” C for MI in non-locomotion domains to preserve generality.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline in Sec. 7.3: Please clarify precisely what “sparse + intrinsic” baseline is (is this the “CoM maze reward”?). Why does it perform so poorly—credit assignment, lack of diversity, or both? Did you normalize for the extra pretraining experience the hierarchical methods receive (i.e., compare at equal total environment steps)?
- “Multi-policy” vs “SNN”: Does “multi-policy” denote training K independent Gaussian low-level policies in pretrain and letting the manager select among them, whereas “SNN” trains a single policy with a categorical latent z (shared weights) and the manager sets z at switch times?
- Fig. 1 interpretation: Please confirm that (i) concatenation effectively produces a z-dependent bias to the first hidden layer, while (ii) bilinear integration implements a z-dependent input weight matrix (outer-product gating) for the first layer, with subsequent layers shared.
- Parameter/control: Is bilinear’s benefit due to the form of conditioning or simply more effective capacity? Have you matched parameter counts across concatenation vs bilinear?
- Sample efficiency: Can you report success rates vs total interactions (including pretrain) and show whether the hierarchy still wins when accounting for pretraining cost?
- Generality of MI target: How would you instantiate the MI bonus for manipulation or visual navigation without heavy domain knowledge? Can the “relevant subset” be learned rather than handpicked?

Overall: Promising, well-presented direction with strong visualizations and a clear empirical message about the limits of naïvely mixing intrinsic and sparse rewards.
