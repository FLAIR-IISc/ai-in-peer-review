- Paper decouples skill acquisition from task learning: pretrain diverse temporally-extended skills, then train a manager to compose them for sparse tasks.
- Skills use stochastic neural networks (categorical latent z) with bilinear integration to produce multimodal shared-weight policies.
- An information-theoretic regularizer (MI between z and a small task-agnostic state subset) encourages option identifiability and diversity.
- Hierarchical policy (manager over frozen skills with fixed switch period T) improves exploration and sample efficiency on mazes and gather tasks versus intrinsic-reward baselines.
- Adding the intrinsic proxy reward directly to sparse tasks performs poorly, emphasizing the value of hierarchical abstraction and enforced diversity.
- Clear visualizations (visitation plots) show interpretable, distinct skills and effects of design choices (concatenation vs bilinear; MI strength).
- Decision: Accept (7/10), motivated by clean integration of SNNs and MI and strong empirical evidence for hierarchy + diversity.
- Strength: Addresses exploration and credit assignment in long-horizon, sparse-reward control and amortizes exploration across related tasks.
- Positioning: Well situated relative to options/HRL, intrinsic motivation, and InfoGAN-style MI; using SNNs for multimodality is intuitive and practical.
- Evidence: Ablations show bilinear conditioning and MI increase locomotion skill span versus concatenation/vanilla SNNs.
- Evidence: Random-manager rollouts demonstrate broader coverage from SNN skills than Gaussian noise or multi-policy baselines.
- Evidence: Hierarchical policies outperform “sparse + intrinsic proxy” baselines on multiple tasks and horizons, with variance reported across pretraining seeds.
- Rigor: Training details, architectures, and ablations are documented; limitations (e.g., unstable Ant) are candidly discussed.
- Concern: Conditioning methods may be limited—current schemes mostly alter first-layer behavior and may be insufficient for qualitatively distinct control programs.
- Concern: Fixed hierarchy (frozen skills, fixed T) makes performance sensitive to proxy quality and limits temporal adaptivity.
- Concern: MI computed on CoM (x,y) is domain-specific to locomotion and may not generalize without handpicked subsets for other domains.
- Concern: Pretraining adds environment steps; fairness requires reporting total interactions and normalizing comparisons across methods.
- Suggested improvement: Report total environment interactions (pretrain + downstream) to compare sample efficiency fairly.
- Suggested improvement: Quantify skill diversity (I(Z;C), pairwise overlap, coverage metrics) and control for parameter counts when comparing conditioning methods.
- Suggested improvement: Explore deeper/multi-layer conditioning or option-specific adapters to increase sub-policy diversity efficiently.
- Suggested improvement: Consider learned termination or variable T to improve adaptability and robustness on unstable agents.
- Question: Clarify the exact “sparse + intrinsic” baseline and whether comparisons normalize for pretraining experience.
- Question: Confirm whether “multi-policy” means K independent Gaussian policies pretraining vs single SNN with categorical z.
- Question: Confirm interpretation that concatenation yields z-dependent bias and bilinear yields z-dependent input weight gating in the first layer.
- Question: Is bilinear’s benefit due to conditioning form or simply higher parameter capacity; were parameter counts matched?
- Question: Can success rates be reported versus total interactions (including pretrain) to test if hierarchy still wins when accounting for pretraining cost?
- Question: How to choose or learn the MI target subset C for non-locomotion domains to avoid handcrafting domain knowledge?
