Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by first pretraining a diverse set of temporally extended skills and then learning a high-level “manager” to sequence these skills on downstream tasks. Skills are learned as modes of a single stochastic neural network (SNN) conditioned on a categorical latent z; diversity is encouraged via an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between z and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position).
- A simple but effective bilinear integration between observations and the latent z yields clearly interpretable, multi-modal low-level policies. A manager policy operates at a slower time scale by selecting a z and committing to it for T steps.
- Empirically, on maze and gather tasks with Swimmer (and variants in the appendix), the hierarchical approach substantially improves exploration and learning compared to standard policy learning and to a strong baseline that augments the downstream task with the same proxy (CoM speed) reward used in pretraining. Ablations (concatenation vs bilinear; MI coefficient) support the design choices. Code and videos are provided.
- Overall, the paper offers a simple, well-motivated framework that combines intrinsic motivation and hierarchy, with clear visualizations and solid empirical evidence in settings known to be hard for naïve exploration.

Decision: Accept (7)
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-placed idea that is both novel in its specific combination (SNN skills + MI identifiability + hierarchical manager) and practically effective for sparse-reward control.
- Convincing empirical evidence that pretraining diverse skills and using them hierarchically materially improves exploration and learning on hard tasks where standard baselines fail.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is addressed? Sparse, long-horizon continuous-control problems where naïve exploration fails. The proposed pretraining + hierarchy reduces search complexity and improves directed exploration.
- Is it well motivated and positioned? Yes. The paper engages HRL (options/MAXQ), intrinsic motivation (e.g., VIME), prior skill discovery (HiREPS), end-to-end hierarchy (option-critic), goal-conditioned pretraining (Heess et al.), and interpretable latents (InfoGAN). The differences are clear: single proxy reward for pretraining, multi-modal SNN with weight sharing, and an MI objective targeted at “option identifiability.”
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. Visitation plots demonstrate interpretable, diverse skills; random-manager rollouts show much broader state-space coverage than action-noise baselines; learning curves on multiple mazes and gather show consistent performance gains. Ablations show bilinear > concatenation and provide evidence that MI further de-overlaps modes. Multi-seed reporting and the acknowledgement of variance are appreciated. Limitations are honestly discussed (fixed sub-policies and T; instability with Ant; MI estimated via discretization).
- Caveats: The domain focus is locomotion; it remains unclear how far the learned diversity extends to manipulation-like settings. The “CoM proxy” downstream baseline could be clarified to ensure sample-budget fairness against hierarchical approaches that benefit from pretraining. The MI estimate is heuristic; diversity is mostly argued qualitatively. Comparisons to strong HRL baselines (e.g., option-critic) are not included.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all tied to the accept decision)
- Baselines and fairness
  - Clarify and, if possible, equalize sample budgets across methods (including pretraining experience). Report results both with and without counting pretraining steps. Explicitly discuss how the “CoM proxy in downstream” baseline accounts for the extra experience available to HRL methods.
  - Consider adding a comparison to option-critic or HiREPS, and to goal-conditioned pretraining (e.g., Heess et al.) with similar budgets.
- Diversity quantification
  - Report a quantitative measure of skill identifiability/diversity (e.g., p̂(z|c) classification accuracy, MI estimate, coverage metrics, state-visitation entropy) alongside visitation plots. Include sensitivity to α_H and to the grid resolution used for MI estimation.
  - Analyze sensitivity to K (number of skills) and provide guidance on selecting K.
- Architecture and training details
  - Provide a clearer exposition of concatenation vs bilinear integration: which parameters are effectively conditioned by z (bias only vs full first-layer weight matrix), and implementation details (factorization, parameter count).
  - Include sensitivity to the commitment horizon T and to manager architecture (e.g., adding recurrence), beyond the appendix; consider learning a termination policy.
  - Report environment-steps and wall-clock for pretraining vs downstream to substantiate the sample-efficiency claim (“train K skills for roughly the cost of one policy”).
- Generality and scope
  - Discuss how proxy rewards might differ across domains and how domain-specific they are. Consider a manipulation or navigation-with-obstacles setting where directional skills may be insufficient to test broader sub-policy diversity.
  - Explore continuous latents or learned priors over z; briefly discuss end-to-end fine-tuning with straight-through estimators (acknowledged as future work).
- Robustness
  - For unstable agents (Ant), evaluate mitigations such as transition smoothing, brief warm-up when switching skills, or learning inter-skill transition policies.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline clarity and sample budgeting: In Sec. 7.3, does the “CoM proxy reward” baseline receive additional experience comparable to the pretraining phase used by the hierarchical methods, or is it trained only on downstream data? Are total environment steps matched across methods when comparing learning curves?
- “Multi-policy” vs “SNN”: Please define “multi-policy” precisely. Is it an ensemble of K independently trained unimodal policies selected by the manager, versus a single SNN policy conditioned on z with weight sharing? Are the manager and training protocols otherwise identical?
- Fig. 1 confirmation: Is our understanding correct that (i) concatenation conditions only the first-layer bias (effectively an additive offset per z), and (ii) bilinear integration conditions the entire first-layer weight matrix (i.e., a multiplicative modulation per z)?
- MI estimator details: What grid resolution is used for p̂(z|c)? How sensitive are results to this resolution and to α_H? Do you observe mode collapse or excessive fragmentation at extreme α_H?
- Diversity metrics: Can you report a numeric MI estimate or identifiability accuracy and state-visitation coverage for the skills with/without MI and for concatenation vs bilinear?
- Manager time scale: How sensitive are results to T on Swimmer mazes and Gather when training with identical budgets? Any benefit from a learned termination policy?
- Transfer scope: Are the exact same pretrained skills reused across all downstream tasks without any adaptation? Any evidence on reusing one SNN across significantly different environments (e.g., different maze layouts) without re-pretraining?
- Instability mitigation: For Ant, have you tried transition smoothing or brief “handover” horizons to reduce falls when switching skills? Any quantitative data on fall rates per switch?
