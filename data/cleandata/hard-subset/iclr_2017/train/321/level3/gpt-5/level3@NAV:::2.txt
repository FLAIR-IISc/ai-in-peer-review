Summary and contributions
The paper tackles long-horizon, sparse-reward RL by pretraining a diverse set of temporally extended skills and then composing them via a high-level manager. The key technical ideas are: (i) using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal low-level policies (options), (ii) an information-theoretic option-identifiability reward based on mutual information between latent codes and a subset of state (e.g., CoM) to encourage diversity, and (iii) a hierarchical controller that selects among frozen skills at fixed time intervals. The work is well positioned in HRL and intrinsic motivation literature, delivers clear visualizations of learned skills, open-sources code, and presents ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear integration; MI bonus).

Decision
Accept (score 7; edited from 6) due to a sound, well-motivated approach with strong empirical evidence that hierarchical abstraction and enforced diversity provide clear benefits over directly mixing intrinsic and sparse rewards.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: How to explore effectively under sparse rewards and transfer exploration structure across related tasks. The formulation and assumptions (shared agent-space) are reasonable and clearly stated.
- Motivation and placement: The paper synthesizes SNN-based multi-modality with an MI regularizer for option identifiability, bridging intrinsic motivation and HRL. Related work is covered appropriately for the time.
- Empirical support: The strong and important finding that directly combining intrinsic (CoM) and sparse rewards performs poorly underscores the value of hierarchy/diversity. Ablations show bilinear integration plus MI yields more distinct skills and better exploration; hierarchical training improves sample efficiency across mazes and gather. Results appear rigorous and reproducible.

Additional feedback and questions (to improve/clarify; not blockers)
- Clarify the “strong baseline” in Sec. 7.3: Is it exactly the CoM maze reward? Why does it underperform—credit assignment, lack of diversity, or something else? Does it account for the extra experience that pretraining provides (i.e., is total environment interaction matched)?
- What does “multi-policy” denote relative to “snn”? My understanding: “multi-policy” trains K independent Gaussian policies; “snn” trains one SNN with K latent codes. Please confirm.
- Fig. 1 confirmation: Concatenation makes the categorical code act as a bias selector for the first hidden layer; bilinear integration makes it select a different input weight matrix. Correct?
- Limitations: (i) Skill expressivity is still modest—concatenation/bilinear may suffice for locomotion but may not diversify for manipulation; (ii) fixed skills and fixed commitment time force reliance on very good intrinsic rewards and domain-specific MI (CoM), making “unsupervised” pretraining somewhat task-shaped. Please discuss portability to domains where CoM is not meaningful and sensitivity to α_H and discretization.
- Could you report how many distinct skills the manager actually uses, and option-switch sensitivity to T? Any control experiments that equalize total samples between “multi-policy” and “snn”? Nice direction overall; the visualizations are particularly compelling.
