Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a practical hierarchical reinforcement learning framework for sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks. The core idea is to (i) pre-train a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy conditioned on a discrete latent code to induce a multi-modal, temporally consistent set of “skills,” using only a proxy reward and an information-theoretic regularizer; and then (ii) learn, per downstream task, a high-level manager that selects skills at a fixed temporal granularity. The SNN combines observation and latent code via a bilinear integration to increase expressivity and is trained with TRPO. An MI bonus I(Z; C) between the latent code Z and a small subset of state features C (e.g., the agent’s center-of-mass (CoM) position) encourages diversity and interpretability of skills. Experiments on benchmark locomotion-with-mazes and gather tasks demonstrate broader exploration and improved sample efficiency compared to strong intrinsic-motivation baselines, while amortizing the pre-training cost across tasks. The paper is clearly written, the idea is well-motivated, the learned skills are interpretable, and the empirical evidence is compelling for the domains studied.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated approach that combines multi-modal SNN policies with a simple MI-based diversity bonus to yield reusable, interpretable skills, improving exploration and performance on sparse tasks.
- Solid empirical evidence on multiple downstream tasks, with code and videos, and ablations (e.g., concatenation vs bilinear, MI strength, switch time).

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: How to learn a reusable skill set that improves exploration and learning across a family of sparse-reward RL tasks, while minimizing domain-specific engineering.
- Motivation and placement: The paper positions itself well with respect to hierarchical RL (options, option-critic), intrinsic motivation (e.g., VIME), and recent skill discovery. The novelty lies in (a) leveraging an SNN with a discrete latent to compactly represent multiple skills with weight sharing; (b) a simple, effective MI regularizer applied to behaviorally relevant state features; (c) a practical two-stage pipeline that demonstrably improves sparse-task learning.
- Support for claims: The paper provides qualitative visitation maps showing diverse skills and improved coverage; quantitative learning curves on several tasks with multiple seeds; and ablations demonstrating the contribution of bilinear integration and the MI bonus. The stated claims about enhanced exploration and improved performance on downstream tasks are supported within the chosen domains.
- Sample efficiency and reusability: Training K skills within one SNN yields a K-fold sample saving relative to training K separate policies; downstream managers reuse the same low-level policy without fine-tuning, simplifying transfer across tasks. This is a concrete and valuable contribution.

Concerns and limitations (informing the weak recommendation)
- Diversity scaling: While the MI bonus helps, skill collapse or redundancy may grow with K. The paper focuses on small K (e.g., 6). It remains unclear how diversity scales and whether the approach saturates or degrades as behavior richness increases.
- MI estimation and discretization: The MI is computed with a discretized grid over the CoM position. This discretization choice, its sensitivity to grid resolution, and its applicability to high-dimensional continuous features are not fully explored. The bonus could depend strongly on discretization granularity.
- Generality to more complex morphologies: Results on “Snake” suggest some generality, but the instability issues on “Ant” highlight sensitivity to embodiment and switching. The approach may require additional mechanisms (e.g., termination/transition policies, stabilization during switches) to be robust across complex agents.
- “Minimal domain knowledge”: The proxy rewards (e.g., speed of CoM for locomotion) are hand-crafted and task class–dependent. While appealingly simple, they still encode meaningful domain assumptions; the claim of minimal knowledge should be tempered or better justified across diverse domains (manipulation, vision-based tasks).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Quantify skill diversity: Report metrics such as mutual information estimates, state-space coverage, pairwise distinguishability, or success on a suite of probing tasks as K varies. Show how diversity scales with K and αH, and provide guidance for tuning.
- MI estimation analysis: Provide ablations on grid resolution, alternative estimators (e.g., density models or classifier-based estimators), and demonstrate applicability when C is higher-dimensional or continuous. Clarify computational overhead and stability.
- Robust switching: Explore adding an option-termination policy or a transition model to mitigate instability (especially for Ant-like agents). Include an experiment with adaptive switch times or a learned termination condition.
- End-to-end fine-tuning: Include a small-scale study with straight-through gradients (Gumbel-Softmax/Concrete) to fine-tune skills jointly with the manager, and analyze benefits vs. stability.
- Broader proxy rewards: Demonstrate the method with alternative proxy rewards beyond CoM speed (e.g., energy efficiency, direction-agnostic displacement with penalties, contact-rich interaction counts) and in a non-locomotion domain (e.g., manipulation) to strengthen the minimal-knowledge claim.
- Cost amortization: Report total samples for pre-training plus per-task training, and how many downstream tasks are needed for pre-training to “pay off,” to make the sample-efficiency argument more explicit.

Questions for the authors
- Diversity scaling: How does performance and skill diversity change as K increases (e.g., K=10, 20)? Do you observe mode collapse or redundancy, and how does αH mitigate it?
- MI discretization: Why discretize CoM rather than estimate MI in continuous space? How sensitive are results to grid resolution and count-smoothing? Have you tried classifier-based MI estimators for higher-dimensional C?
- Choice of C: How do results change if C includes heading, velocities, or other behaviorally relevant features? Any guidance for selecting C in domains without obvious low-dimensional summaries?
- Proxy reward generality: Can you show an example outside locomotion where a similarly simple proxy (not tailored to the downstream tasks) yields reusable skills? What design principles should practitioners follow to choose proxy rewards?
- Switching robustness: For unstable agents (e.g., Ant), can you quantify failure modes at switch times and show improvements from simple mitigations (e.g., warm-up phases, restricted switch sets, or transition-aware managers)?
- Amortized sample efficiency: What is the total pre-training budget relative to per-task gains across the suite of downstream tasks? How many tasks are needed before pre-training yields net savings?
- Manager observability: Have you tried a recurrent manager to leverage history gathered within a skill, and does it improve performance on long-horizon navigation?

Overall, the paper presents a practical and well-argued approach to skill discovery and reuse for hierarchical RL, with convincing benefits on challenging sparse tasks. Addressing the questions above would strengthen the generality and robustness claims.
