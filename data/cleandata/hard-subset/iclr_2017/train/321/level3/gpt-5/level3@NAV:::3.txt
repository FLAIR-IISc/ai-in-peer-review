1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by pretraining a repertoire of skills using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with a mutual-information (MI) bonus, then training a high-level manager that selects among these skills. The technical choices—bilinear integration of latent codes, MI regularization inspired by InfoGAN, and TRPO optimization—are clearly presented. Empirically, the method discovers interpretable locomotion behaviors and improves downstream learning on maze and gather tasks. The setting is timely and the framework unifies intrinsic motivation with hierarchical control in a clean, reusable pipeline. Code and videos are provided.

2) Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons: (i) The claims of minimal domain knowledge and generality are overstated; the “intrinsic” rewards and MI targets are hand-crafted for locomotion. (ii) The MI estimation relies on a discretized grid over CoM coordinates (Sec. 5.3), raising scaling/robustness concerns and leaving unclear behavior in continuous/high-dimensional settings.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem/placement: Well-motivated and related to HRL/IM literature. The SNN+MI angle is reasonable and connected to prior work.
- Evidence/rigor: Results are promising but constrained to a few locomotion domains with tailored proxy rewards (speed) and MI on CoM (x,y). The diversity metric is largely qualitative (visitation plots); claims of “uniform” improvements are not consistently borne out across tasks/seeds, and failure modes (e.g., Ant instability, skill overlap) surface as behavior richness increases.
- Discretization: Sec. 5.3 effectively imposes a grid over state projections. It is unclear how sensitive performance is to mesh density, how this extends beyond 2D CoM, or whether the approach still works with continuous MI estimators. This design choice undercuts the claimed generality.
- Embodiment scaling: While Snake results appear in the appendix, the main narrative remains centered on simple morphologies. Robustness to more complex bodies and non-locomotion agent-spaces is not convincingly demonstrated.
- Reward design: The proxy reward and MI target require non-trivial domain knowledge (choosing speed/CoM for locomotion; grasp success for manipulation). This contradicts the “very minimal domain knowledge” claim.

4) Suggestions and questions (to help improve, not all decision-critical)
- Replace grid counting with a continuous MI estimator (e.g., variational MI/MINE) and report sensitivity to representation and αH.
- Quantify skill diversity (e.g., state coverage, option dissimilarity, MI achieved) and report sensitivity to K and switch time T.
- Clarify: Is any part of the state truly discretized during training, or only for MI estimation? What happens if the state is continuous and high-dimensional?
- How does the method perform when CoM is unavailable or irrelevant (manipulation)? What alternative C choices work?
- Provide stronger experiments on more complex morphologies (Ant/Humanoid) and non-locomotion tasks, plus comparisons against option-critic and goal-conditioned baselines.
- Can end-to-end fine-tuning with learned terminations mitigate instability and skill switching failures?
