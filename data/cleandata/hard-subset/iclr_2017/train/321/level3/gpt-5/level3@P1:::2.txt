Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper tackles long-horizon, sparse-reward RL by learning a reusable skill set in a pre-training environment and then training a high-level “manager” that selects among these skills in downstream tasks. The approach is simple and broadly applicable under a mild “shared agent-state” assumption.
- Key technical ideas: (i) Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) parameterized by a categorical latent code to obtain multi-modal low-level policies with weight sharing; (ii) an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position), encouraging diverse, identifiable skills; (iii) a hierarchical controller that freezes the skills and selects a skill every T steps via a manager policy.
- Empirically, the method learns interpretable, diverse locomotion skills, substantially improves exploration, and outperforms strong baselines on Swimmer mazes and Gather, including a “better-than-vanilla” baseline that injects the proxy CoM reward into the downstream task. Ablations on latent integration (concatenation vs bilinear) and MI strength show that bilinear integration and MI help produce more clearly differentiated skills. Visualizations are compelling and reproducible details are provided.

Decision: Accept (7/10)
- Reason 1: Addresses an important challenge (sparse rewards, long horizons) with a simple, well-motivated combination (SNN multimodality + MI regularization + hierarchical manager) and shows clear empirical gains over strong baselines.
- Reason 2: The framework is general, requires minimal domain knowledge for the pre-train reward design, and the resulting skills are interpretable and reusable.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Efficient exploration and transfer for collections of tasks with sparse rewards/long horizons by learning reusable skills independent of specific task rewards.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. It bridges hierarchical RL (options/MAXQ) and intrinsic motivation (e.g., VIME), with a principled diversity objective inspired by InfoGAN. It compares and contrasts with contemporaneous Option-Critic and HiREPS, and with pre-training approaches that require explicit goal sets. Using SNNs for multimodal policies is natural and leverages weight sharing to amortize skill learning.
- Do results support the claims? Largely yes. Visitation plots show diverse skills; random exploration baselines cover tiny regions even with large noise; the hierarchical agent using SNN skills explores widely and solves sparse mazes and Gather where standard algorithms (and even a proxy-reward-in-task baseline) struggle. The paper reports multiple seeds and different pretrained SNNs to avoid cherry picking. It also acknowledges that MI does not uniformly improve all tasks (e.g., some mazes), which adds credibility. Limitations are candidly discussed (e.g., Ant instability, fixed T, frozen subpolicies).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Diversity and generality: The current proxy reward and MI target (CoM) are tailored to locomotion. For broader domains (e.g., manipulation), consider richer proxies and/or conditioning MI on task-agnostic but semantically meaningful subsets of state. Reporting initial manipulation results or a discussion of how to choose c beyond CoM would strengthen generality claims.
- Fixed sub-policies and timescale: Freezing skills and using a fixed T simplifies training, but may constrain performance. A brief ablation with end-to-end fine-tuning (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) and/or a learned termination policy would be valuable, even if only in a small-scale environment.
- Skill diversity vs task needs: The paper notes that MI does not always translate into downstream gains. Consider reporting (i) a correlation between I(Z;C) (or an option identifiability metric) and downstream scores, and (ii) option-usage histograms to show whether the learned manager exploits the full skill set.
- Mutual information estimation: The discretized, batch-based posterior may induce estimator bias and non-stationarity. A short discussion of sensitivity to grid resolution and of alternative estimators (e.g., parametric classifier for p(z|c)) would be helpful.
- Fairness and sample accounting: Please make explicit whether the total number of environment steps (pretrain + downstream) is matched across baselines, and whether baselines receive comparable prior experience or pre-trained features.
- Clarity: Minor typos/formatting (e.g., “signle,” “sparcity”), and some reference formatting issues. Figures are excellent; adding a concise schematic comparing “Multi-policy” vs “SNN” hierarchies would help.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline fairness in Sec. 7.3: Does the “CoM in-task” baseline get the same total environment interaction (pretrain + downstream) as the hierarchical methods? If not, can you report results under matched total samples? Also, is the baseline combining extrinsic sparse reward and CoM proxy (i.e., sum of both), or only the proxy? Please clarify why it performs poorly relative to your method despite having a seemingly informative dense component.
- “Multi-policy” vs “SNN”: Please confirm that “Multi-policy” means K separately trained unimodal low-level policies used by the manager, whereas “SNN” is a single policy with a categorical latent. Are the pretraining sample budgets equal to one policy’s training (thus SNN amortizes K skills for the cost of one), and is Multi-policy trained with K× the samples?
- Fig. 1 interpretation: Is it correct that concatenation effectively gives each category a different bias vector for the first hidden layer, while bilinear integration gives a different input weight matrix (i.e., category-specific first-layer linear mapping)?
- Sensitivity analyses: Can you report sensitivity to K, α_H, and the discretization density for p̂(z|c)? Does larger K yield diminishing returns or degenerate/unused modes?
- Skill usage: Do managers in downstream tasks utilize most skills, or only a subset? Option-usage histograms and switching frequency plots would clarify.
- Unstable robots: For Ant, do you have preliminary results when (i) pretraining includes occasional latent switches, (ii) adding a stabilizing prior or KL between skills, or (iii) using a short warm-start rollout before committing to a new skill?

Overall, this is a promising and well-executed direction with clear empirical benefits and strong presentation. The paper will be of interest to the ICLR community.
