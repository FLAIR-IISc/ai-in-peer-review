## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic reward to learn diverse, reusable skills. The core idea – pre-training a diverse set of skills with a proxy reward and then utilizing them within a hierarchical structure for downstream tasks – is promising, particularly for sparse reward environments. The empirical results demonstrate that directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards performs poorly, highlighting the benefit of hierarchical abstraction or the enforced diversity in sub-policy training. The visualizations of learned skills are also quite insightful.

**Decision: Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper tackles a relevant problem in RL – exploration in sparse reward settings – and presents a novel approach with strong empirical support. The key finding that simple combination of intrinsic and sparse rewards fails is significant, and the SNN-based approach offers a compelling solution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Motivation:** The combination of SNNs with an information-theoretic regularizer for skill diversity within an HRL framework is a novel contribution. The motivation for addressing sparse rewards and long horizons is well-established.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach across multiple environments. The comparison against a strong baseline (combining intrinsic and sparse rewards) clearly shows the benefits of the hierarchical structure and skill diversity.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The ablation studies (varying the integration method and MI bonus) provide valuable insights into the impact of different design choices.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Limited Diversification:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of concatenation and bilinear integration in achieving substantial sub-policy differences. Exploring more sophisticated integration methods could be a valuable direction for future work.
*   **Hierarchy Limitation:** The fixed sub-policy timescales and reliance on pre-learned, high-quality intrinsic rewards are potential bottlenecks. Investigating adaptive timescales or joint training of the manager and sub-policies would be beneficial.
*   **Domain-Specificity of Rewards:** The observation that intrinsic rewards appear domain-specific due to the fixed sub-policy setup is important. Further discussion on how to mitigate this limitation would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding Section 7.3, could you clarify why the strong baseline performs so poorly? Specifically, does it account for any experience gained from the pre-trained policies used in the hierarchical framework, or is it solely the "CoM maze reward" as described?
2.  What is the distinction between a "multi-policy" approach and the SNN-based approach presented in the paper? Is "multi-policy" simply training separate policies independently, as opposed to the shared representation within the SNN?
3.  Could you confirm my understanding of Figure 1? Concatenation corresponds to applying different biases to the first hidden layer, while bilinear integration applies different input weight matrices?
