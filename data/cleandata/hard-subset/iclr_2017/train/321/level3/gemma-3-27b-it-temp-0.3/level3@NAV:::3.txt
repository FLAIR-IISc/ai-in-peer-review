## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery, followed by high-level policy learning on top of these skills. The core idea of pre-training a diverse set of skills with a minimal domain-knowledge proxy reward, and then transferring these skills to downstream sparse-reward tasks, is a promising approach to address challenges in RL. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies and the inclusion of a mutual information regularizer to encourage skill diversity are interesting contributions.

I **reject** the paper in its current form. While the proposed approach has merit, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and rigorous evaluation to convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness and generality. My primary concern is the claim of “minimal domain knowledge” – the proxy reward, while simple, appears carefully hand-crafted and task-specific, undermining this claim. Furthermore, the experiments, while demonstrating promising results, don't adequately address the scalability and robustness of the method.

My assessment is based on the following arguments. First, the paper doesn’t sufficiently justify why the chosen proxy reward requires “very minimal domain knowledge.” For example, rewarding speed in a locomotion task inherently biases the learned skills towards movement, which is a significant prior. A more convincing argument would involve demonstrating performance with a truly generic proxy reward. Second, the experiments primarily focus on a limited set of environments (Swimmer and Ant) and tasks. The generality of the approach to more complex morphologies (e.g., a 5-link swimmer) or significantly different task structures remains unclear. The brief mention of results with the Snake robot doesn't provide sufficient detail to assess this. Third, the paper lacks a thorough ablation study. How much does the MI regularizer contribute? What is the impact of the bilinear integration versus concatenation in the SNN? Finally, the discussion of failure modes with the Ant robot highlights a significant limitation – instability during skill switching – which needs further investigation.

To improve the paper, I recommend the following: 
* **Clarify the “minimal domain knowledge” claim:** Provide a more nuanced discussion and potentially explore more generic proxy rewards.
* **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include results on more diverse environments and morphologies.
* **Conduct a comprehensive ablation study:** Quantify the contribution of each component of the proposed framework.
* **Address the instability issue:** Investigate methods to mitigate the instability during skill switching, particularly for more complex robots.
* **Clarify the discretization:** Section 5.3 mentions a grid. Is the state space discretized? If so, why, and how does this affect the results? What happens if the state space is continuous?



I have several clarifying questions for the authors:
* What alternative proxy rewards were considered, and why were they rejected?
* How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the MI regularization coefficient?
* Could you provide more details on the experimental setup for the Snake robot, including the specific tasks used and the performance metrics?
* What strategies could be employed to address the instability issue observed with the Ant robot?
