## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery during a pre-training phase, followed by learning high-level policies on top of these skills for downstream tasks. The approach combines elements of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, using a proxy reward for pre-training and mutual information regularization to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results on challenging locomotion and maze tasks, achieving performance beyond previous work.

**Reject.** While the approach is promising and the experimental results are impressive in terms of solving benchmarks others cannot, the paper suffers from a lack of rigorous analysis and clarity in key aspects, hindering confidence in the claims. The experimental validation, while strong in *what* it achieves, is weak in *why* it achieves it.

The core strength of the paper lies in the successful application of SNNs to hierarchical RL and the ability to solve complex tasks. The pre-training approach, while framed as “intrinsic motivation,” demonstrably learns useful skills. However, the framing is misleading. The use of speed/grasp rewards is clearly task-aligned pre-training, not a truly intrinsic signal. This is a critical point, as it impacts the interpretation of the results. Furthermore, the mutual information regularization, while elegant, doesn't consistently improve performance and receives insufficient analysis. The authors acknowledge this, but fail to investigate *why* it doesn’t help in certain scenarios. A deeper dive into the learned skills, their transferability, and the impact of the MI regularization would be valuable.

A significant concern is the lack of clarity regarding the factorization of the state space (S_agent and S_rest). While S_agent is defined, S_rest remains vaguely described, impacting reproducibility. Finally, the paper lacks detailed analysis of the switching behavior between skills. Understanding failure modes and the influence of the switching timing (T) on performance is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of the hierarchical structure.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify “Intrinsic Motivation”:** Reframe the pre-training phase as task-aligned pre-training.
*   **Analyze MI Regularization:** Investigate why MI regularization doesn't improve performance on more complex mazes. Is it a hyperparameter issue, or does the regularization become detrimental in certain environments?
*   **Detail State Factorization:** Provide a precise definition of S_rest for reproducibility.
*   **Switching Behavior Analysis:** Include an analysis of the frequency and timing of skill switches. How does the choice of T impact performance? Are there specific situations where switching is detrimental?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (SNNs, MI regularization) to the overall performance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how S_rest is defined for each environment?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the MI regularization coefficient (αH)?
*   Have you investigated the impact of different switching policies (e.g., learning a termination condition) on performance?
*   Can you provide examples of learned skills that are particularly effective or ineffective in specific downstream tasks?
