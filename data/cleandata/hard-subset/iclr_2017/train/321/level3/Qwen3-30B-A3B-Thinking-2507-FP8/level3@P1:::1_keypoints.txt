- Proposes hierarchical RL that pre-trains diverse skills using a single proxy reward and reuses them for efficient downstream learning with sparse rewards, using SNNs plus an information-theoretic regularizer.  
- Claims strong empirical performance and reduced sample complexity on continuous-control sparse-reward benchmarks.  
- Recommendation: reject — approach is interesting with good results but contribution is incremental and poorly contextualized vs prior work (Heess et al., 2016).  
- Major critique: mischaracterizes pre-training as "intrinsic motivation" despite using task-aligned proxy rewards (speed/grasping), which are not true intrinsic objectives.  
- Experimental analysis is shallow, especially lacking explanation for why the mutual information (MI) regularizer does not improve performance on mazes 1–3.  
- Strength: demonstrates SNNs can learn multiple distinct skills simultaneously with sample efficiency; empirical results are convincing on several tasks.  
- Mislabeling as intrinsic motivation misplaces the work in the literature and overstates novelty compared to goal-conditioned pre-training methods.  
- The paper does not analyze whether MI-induced skill diversity is useful or why it sometimes fails, leaving unclear the relationship between diversity and downstream performance.  
- Lacks clarity on the assumed state-space factorization (S_agent vs S_rest) and how this is implemented in experiments, harming reproducibility.  
- Needs deeper analysis of skill switching behavior and failure modes, particularly on unstable agents like Ant.  
- Suggested improvements: correct the intrinsic-motivation claim, provide detailed analysis of MI regularizer effects, clarify state-space factorization implementation, and analyze switching/failure behaviors.  
- Questions for authors: (1) how exactly does your pre-training signal differ from Heess et al. (2016)’s goal specification? (2) why does the MI regularizer fail on mazes 1–3 and what does that imply? (3) define S_agent and S_rest concretely for your experiments and explain how they are identified.
