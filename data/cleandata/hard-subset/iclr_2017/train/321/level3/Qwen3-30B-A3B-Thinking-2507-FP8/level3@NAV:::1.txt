

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first pre-trains a diverse set of skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with mutual information regularization, then leverages these skills for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach demonstrates strong empirical results on challenging benchmarks like mazes and object gathering tasks, where previous methods failed. The method effectively learns interpretable skills that significantly improve exploration and sample efficiency in downstream tasks.

Decision: Reject

Key reasons for rejection:
1. The paper misuses the term "intrinsic motivation" to describe a pre-training process that is actually task-aligned and resembles prior work (Heess et al. 2016). The proxy reward (speed/grasp) is directly designed with minimal domain knowledge about downstream tasks, making it task-aligned rather than true intrinsic motivation as defined in the literature.

2. The experimental analysis is weak, particularly regarding the mutual information regularization. The paper notes that this regularization does not improve performance on mazes 1-3 but provides no interpretation or analysis for this finding, which is critical for understanding the method's limitations.

Supporting arguments:
The paper's contribution builds on prior hierarchical RL work but misrepresents its relationship to the literature through the misleading "intrinsic motivation" claim. This mischaracterization undermines the paper's positioning and makes it difficult to assess how the method relates to existing work on skill acquisition.

The weak experimental analysis is particularly concerning given the paper's emphasis on mutual information regularization as a key component. The authors fail to address why this regularization doesn't help on more complex mazes, which is a significant omission for a paper claiming to solve benchmarks previous work could not.

Additional feedback:
- Clearly specify the factorization between S_agent and S_rest for reproducibility (S_agent is defined in Duan et al., but S_rest is not clearly detailed).
- Provide more analysis of switching behavior between skills, including failure modes and how switching timing affects performance.
- Reconsider the use of "intrinsic motivation" to accurately reflect the method's relationship to prior work.

Questions for authors:
1. Can you clarify why mutual information regularization doesn't improve performance on mazes 1-3, and how this might inform future work on skill diversity?
2. How would you formally define S_rest to ensure reproducibility of your factorization approach?
