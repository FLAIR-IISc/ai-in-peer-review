

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, then leverages these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors claim their approach significantly improves exploration and learning efficiency across multiple tasks while requiring minimal domain knowledge for the pre-training reward signal. The framework demonstrates strong empirical results on maze navigation and object gathering tasks, showing that the learned skills can be reused effectively across different downstream tasks.

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem in RL with sparse rewards, the core claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence. The most critical issue is the lack of theoretical justification for the discretization approach in Section 5.3, which undermines the generality of the proposed method. The authors claim their approach requires minimal domain knowledge, but the proxy rewards (e.g., speed for locomotion, grasping success) appear to be carefully hand-crafted for specific task types, contradicting their main claim. The results also fail to demonstrate scalability to more complex morphologies beyond the 2-link swimmer, limiting the perceived contribution.

The paper's technical approach has several concerning aspects. The discretization of the state space (x,y coordinates) into grid cells for computing mutual information (Eq. 2) is not justified for continuous state spaces, and the authors do not address how this would generalize to higher-dimensional continuous state spaces. The claim that "the design of the proxy reward requires very minimal domain knowledge" is questionable - the speed proxy reward for locomotion assumes knowledge about what constitutes useful locomotion, and the grasping reward assumes knowledge about what constitutes a successful grasp. These are not minimal domain knowledge requirements but rather specific task-relevant knowledge.

Furthermore, the paper's empirical results do not sufficiently demonstrate the method's scalability to more complex morphologies. The experiments are limited to a 2-link swimmer robot, with a brief mention of a "Snake" robot in Appendix C that appears to use the same approach but without meaningful comparison to the main results. The failure modes with the "Ant" robot in Appendix D further highlight the limitations of the approach, as switching between skills causes instability and termination of rollouts. The paper does not provide a clear path forward for addressing these instability issues or demonstrating how the approach would scale to more complex robotic systems.

The paper's contribution to the field is limited by its narrow scope and lack of theoretical grounding for the key methodological choices. While the hierarchical approach shows promise on the specific tasks tested, the methodological choices (particularly the discretization) and the limited evaluation scope prevent the authors from making the strong claims they make about the approach's general applicability and minimal domain knowledge requirements.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Remove the claim about minimal domain knowledge and instead specify precisely what domain knowledge is required for the proxy reward in different task types.
2. Provide a theoretical justification for the discretization approach or propose an alternative method that works with continuous state spaces.
3. Expand the evaluation to include more complex robot morphologies beyond the 2-link swimmer.
4. Address the failure modes with unstable robots like "Ant" and discuss how the approach might be modified to handle such cases.

I would like to clarify the following points to better understand the paper's contributions:
1. In Section 5.3, the authors discretize the state space into grid cells for computing mutual information. How would this approach scale to continuous state spaces without discretization, especially for high-dimensional state spaces like those in visual-based RL? What is the theoretical justification for this discretization?
2. The authors claim the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge," but for the locomotion task, the proxy reward is proportional to speed, which assumes knowledge about what constitutes useful locomotion. What specific domain knowledge is required to design this proxy reward for a new task, and how does this compare to other approaches that use more explicit rewards?
3. The paper demonstrates results only for a 2-link swimmer robot. How does the method scale to more complex morphologies like the 5-link "Snake" robot mentioned in Appendix C, and what changes would be required to maintain performance?
