

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal, then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors introduce a method using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a wide span of interpretable skills efficiently. The approach successfully combines strengths of intrinsic motivation (using a minimal proxy reward for pre-training) and hierarchical methods (using learned skills as building blocks for downstream tasks). The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements on challenging benchmarks like Locomotion + Maze and Locomotion + Food Collection tasks, where previous methods struggled with sparse rewards. The paper makes a valuable contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing a practical, sample-efficient method for skill learning that avoids the need for task-specific hand-engineering.

## 2. Decision

Reject.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

My decision to reject is based on two key weaknesses that undermine the paper's contribution and scientific rigor:

First, the paper misuses the term "intrinsic motivation" in a misleading way. The pre-training with speed or grasp rewards is not truly intrinsic motivation but rather a task-aligned pre-training signal that resembles the approach by Heess et al. (2016). Intrinsic motivation typically refers to rewards that encourage exploration without task-specific knowledge, but here the proxy reward is explicitly designed to encourage behaviors relevant to downstream tasks (e.g., movement for locomotion, grasping for manipulation). This mischaracterization misleads readers about the novelty of the approach and conflates it with prior work that properly implements intrinsic motivation (e.g., Houthooft et al., 2016).

Second, the paper fails to adequately analyze why the mutual information (MI) regularization does not improve performance on the more complex mazes (1-3), despite being helpful in simpler cases. The authors note this lack of improvement but provide no meaningful analysis or interpretation. This represents a significant gap in the scientific rigor of the paper. A proper analysis would have investigated why the MI bonus doesn't help in more complex environments (e.g., whether the skill diversity was already sufficient, or whether the reward structure of the mazes makes the MI bonus less relevant), which would have strengthened the paper's contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the terminology**: Replace "intrinsic motivation" with accurate descriptions of the pre-training approach. The authors should explicitly acknowledge that this is a task-aligned pre-training signal rather than true intrinsic motivation, and clearly distinguish this from prior work on intrinsic motivation.

2. **Expand the analysis of MI regularization**: Provide a thorough analysis of why the MI bonus doesn't help on mazes 1-3. The authors should investigate whether the skills learned without the MI bonus were already sufficiently diverse for these tasks, or if the reward structure of these mazes makes the MI bonus less relevant. This would provide deeper insights into the method's limitations and potential improvements.

3. **Clarify the state space factorization**: The paper needs to explicitly define how the state space is decomposed into S_agent and S_rest for all experiments, especially for the more complex tasks. Without this detail, the work is not reproducible, as Duan et al. (2016) provide clear definitions for S_agent but leave S_rest ambiguous.

4. **Expand analysis of switching behavior**: Provide more detailed analysis of the switching behavior between skills, including failure modes (as noted in the Ant robot experiments) and how the switching time T affects performance across different tasks. This would strengthen the paper's contribution to hierarchical RL.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims the pre-training reward is "minimal domain knowledge" but for locomotion tasks, the proxy reward is proportional to speed, which directly corresponds to the goal of moving toward the maze's goal. How does this differ from simply using a reward that directly encourages the desired behavior (which would not be considered intrinsic motivation)? 

2. The MI regularization does not improve performance on mazes 1-3, despite improving skills diversity. Can you provide any insights into why this might be the case, and how the reward structure of these mazes might interact with the MI bonus?

3. For the maze tasks, how exactly was the state space decomposed into S_agent and S_rest? The paper mentions that S_agent should be the same across tasks, but for the maze environments, it's unclear what constitutes S_agent versus S_rest.

4. The paper mentions failure modes with unstable robots like Ant, but doesn't discuss how to address them. Are there specific modifications to the SNN or the switching mechanism that would help stabilize the learning process in such cases?
