

# Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first pre-trains a diverse set of skills using a proxy reward signal (e.g., speed for locomotion, grasp success for manipulation), then leverages these skills in a hierarchical policy for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovation lies in using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to efficiently learn a multimodal policy representing multiple skills within a single network, combined with a mutual information regularizer to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results across challenging benchmarks (mazes and gather tasks) where previous methods with naive exploration failed, showing significant improvements in sample efficiency and performance. The framework effectively combines aspects of hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation, with the pre-training phase requiring minimal domain knowledge.

## 2. Decision
Weak Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to hierarchical RL with strong empirical results, but requires significant revisions to clarify terminology, improve experimental analysis, and provide better reproducibility details.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main strengths are its clear hierarchical framework and impressive empirical results. The authors successfully demonstrate that their pre-training approach solves benchmarks previously considered intractable with naive exploration strategies (e.g., Duan et al.'s mazes). The SNN-based approach with mutual information regularization effectively learns a diverse span of skills in a sample-efficient manner, as evidenced by the visitation plots in Section 7.1. The comparison with prior work (Heess et al., 2016) is well-motivated, showing how their approach requires only a single proxy reward rather than multiple goal specifications.

However, the paper has critical weaknesses that prevent a strong accept. First, the use of "intrinsic motivation" is misleading. The proxy reward (speed/grasp success) is task-aligned and resembles prior pre-training work (Heess et al.), not true intrinsic motivation which should be independent of the task. This mislabeling undermines the paper's theoretical contribution. Second, the experimental analysis is weak. The paper notes that mutual information regularization does not improve performance on mazes 1-3 but provides no interpretation of why this occurs. The lack of analysis on switching behavior, failure modes (especially for unstable robots like Ant), and how switching timing affects performance (Section 8) is a significant gap. Finally, the factorization between S_agent and S_rest is not clearly specified for reproducibility, which is essential for a method claiming to be generalizable across tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- **Clarify terminology**: Remove "intrinsic motivation" from the title and body, and reframe the pre-training reward as a "task-aligned proxy reward" to accurately reflect the method.
- **Improve experimental analysis**: Add a thorough discussion of why MI regularization fails to improve performance on mazes 1-3, and analyze the relationship between skill diversity and maze complexity. Provide more detailed analysis of switching behavior, including failure modes (as hinted at in Section D.2) and how switching timing affects performance.
- **Specify state factorization**: Clearly define S_rest for each task in the experiments to enable reproducibility. Explain how the factorization was determined for each environment.
- **Expand on failure modes**: Provide a more detailed analysis of the instability issues with the Ant robot (Section D.2), including potential solutions and how they might be integrated into the framework.
- **Include more ablation studies**: Add experiments showing the impact of different MI regularization strengths on skill diversity and downstream performance.

## 5. Clarification Questions
1. The paper claims the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge," but for the maze tasks, does this not require knowing the robot's locomotion capabilities? Could you clarify what constitutes "minimal domain knowledge" in this context?
2. Why does the MI regularization improve performance on Maze 0 but not on Mazes 1-3? Is there a specific aspect of Maze 0's structure that makes it more amenable to the additional skills learned with MI, and how could the framework be adjusted to better handle the more complex mazes?
3. How was the factorization between S_agent and S_rest determined for each task? For the Gather task, what specific state components constitute S_rest versus S_agent, and how were these identified?
4. The paper notes that performance varies significantly across different pretrained SNNs (Section C.4), yet the results in Figure 6 report average performance. Could you provide more detail on how the authors selected which SNN to use in the final experiments, and whether this selection process would be feasible in a real-world scenario without prior knowledge of which SNNs would work best?
