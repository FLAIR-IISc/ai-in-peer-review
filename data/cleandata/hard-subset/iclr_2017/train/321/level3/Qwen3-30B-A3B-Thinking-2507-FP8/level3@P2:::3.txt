

# Review for "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary

This paper proposes an interesting framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, and then leverages these skills for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors demonstrate that their approach can learn a wide range of interpretable skills with minimal domain knowledge (using a single proxy reward signal), and that these skills significantly improve exploration and learning in downstream tasks. The experimental results on maze navigation and food collection tasks are promising, showing clear improvements over baselines that use naive exploration or intrinsic rewards without skill learning. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on hierarchical RL by providing a principled way to learn a span of skills that can be reused across multiple tasks.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper has a strong experimental foundation but contains methodological limitations and misleading claims that undermine the significance of the contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The key reasons for rejection are:

First, the paper's claim that the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge" is misleading and contradicts the experimental setup. The proxy rewards used (e.g., speed for locomotion, grasping for manipulators) require significant task-specific domain knowledge to design. The authors present these as if they are universal, but they are clearly tailored to specific control tasks. For instance, using speed as a proxy reward for locomotion requires understanding that movement is desirable in the environment, which is not "minimal" domain knowledge but rather specific knowledge about the task's dynamics. The paper would be stronger if it demonstrated how a single proxy reward could work across different task domains without modification.

Second, the discretization approach in Section 5.3 is problematic and limits the method's applicability. The authors use a grid to discretize the state space for computing mutual information, but they do not adequately justify why discretization is necessary or how the method would work with continuous state spaces. This discretization is particularly concerning because the paper claims to work with continuous control problems, yet the core technique relies on discretizing continuous state variables. The paper would need to address how their method would work without this discretization step for continuous state spaces.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

- Clarify the discretization approach in Section 5.3. Explain why discretization is necessary for the mutual information calculation, and provide an analysis of how the grid size affects the learned skills and performance. Discuss how the method would work without discretization for continuous state spaces.

- Re-evaluate the claim about "minimal domain knowledge." Either provide evidence that the proxy rewards can be designed with truly minimal domain knowledge (e.g., by showing how the same proxy reward works across different domains), or revise the claim to be more accurate.

- Address the scalability concerns by including more comprehensive experiments with complex morphologies (e.g., the 5-link Snake robot mentioned in the appendix) and discussing the limitations of the current approach for more complex systems.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Section 5.3, you discretize the state space for computing mutual information. Why is this discretization necessary? How would your method work with continuous state spaces where discretization would be intractable? What are the theoretical guarantees for your discretization approach?

2. The paper claims the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge," but the examples provided (speed for locomotion, grasping for manipulators) seem quite specific to the tasks. Can you provide examples of proxy rewards that would work across different domains without modification? How would you design a proxy reward for a completely novel task domain?

3. The Ant robot experiments (Section D) show significant failure modes when switching skills. What are the main challenges with skill switching for unstable agents, and what specific modifications would be needed to address these challenges? How would these modifications affect the sample complexity of the pre-training phase?
