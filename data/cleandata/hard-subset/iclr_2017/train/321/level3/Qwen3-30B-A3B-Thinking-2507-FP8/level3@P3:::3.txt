

# Review for "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Paper's Contribution

This paper proposes an elegant framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and then leverages these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors introduce Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to efficiently learn multiple interpretable skills simultaneously. The approach is well-motivated, addressing the challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons in RL by combining insights from intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in exploration and learning performance across multiple challenging tasks, with skills that are clearly interpretable as the latent code varies. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a principled approach to skill discovery that requires minimal task-specific engineering.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's key claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, primarily due to concerns about the scalability of the approach and the hand-crafted nature of the proxy reward.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong claim that the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge," but this is not substantiated by the evidence. The proxy reward (e.g., speed for locomotion, grasping success for manipulators) is carefully designed to match the specific downstream tasks, effectively requiring substantial domain knowledge about the types of behaviors that would be useful. This contradicts the authors' central claim and weakens the paper's contribution.

Furthermore, the paper's experiments are limited to relatively simple morphologies (2-link swimmer, 3-link snake) and do not demonstrate scalability to more complex morphologies like the 5-link swimmer or the Ant robot (which the authors themselves note has failure modes). The failure modes section (D.2) explicitly shows that the approach fails with more complex robots due to instability during skill switching, raising serious concerns about the method's generality.

The discretization in Section 5.3 is problematic and not adequately addressed. The paper implies a grid-based discretization of the (x,y) coordinate space for computing the MI bonus, but it doesn't clarify whether this discretization is necessary or what happens if the state space is continuous. This is a significant gap, as most real-world RL tasks involve continuous state spaces.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Re-evaluate the claim about "minimal domain knowledge" by providing a more thorough analysis of what constitutes "minimal" and demonstrating how the proxy reward could be designed for a broader range of tasks without task-specific engineering.

2. Expand the experiments to include more complex morphologies (e.g., 5-link swimmer, Ant) and demonstrate how the method handles the increased complexity, particularly addressing the failure modes observed with unstable robots.

3. Clarify the discretization issue in Section 5.3. If the discretization is necessary, explain why a continuous approach wouldn't work. If it's not necessary, provide experiments showing the method works with continuous state spaces.

4. Investigate whether the MI bonus is truly necessary for skill diversity, or if other mechanisms (e.g., different latent variable distributions) could achieve similar results without the discretization step.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge" but provides no examples of how this reward would be designed for a task completely unrelated to the ones tested (e.g., a novel robot morphology or task). Could you provide a specific example of how the proxy reward would be designed for a completely different task, and explain why this would require minimal domain knowledge?

2. In Section 5.3, the authors discretize the state space to compute the MI bonus. How would this approach scale to continuous state spaces with higher dimensions (e.g., 10+ dimensions), where discretization would be computationally intractable? Is there a continuous alternative that could be used instead?

3. The failure modes with the Ant robot (Section D.2) suggest that skill switching is problematic for unstable agents. Could you elaborate on how your framework would need to be modified to handle such unstable systems, and whether these modifications would require additional domain knowledge that contradicts your minimal domain knowledge claim?

4. The paper demonstrates success on Maze 0-3 and Gather tasks, but these tasks are relatively simple with short horizons. How would your approach scale to more complex navigation tasks with longer horizons and more intricate structures, where the diversity of skills would need to be substantially greater?
