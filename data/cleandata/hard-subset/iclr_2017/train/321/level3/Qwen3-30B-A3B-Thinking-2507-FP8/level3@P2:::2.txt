

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards and long horizons. The authors introduce a method to first learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, and then leverage these skills in downstream tasks through a hierarchical architecture. The approach is motivated by the observation that traditional methods either require domain-specific knowledge for hierarchical structures or fail to provide transferable knowledge across tasks. The key innovation is the use of a single proxy reward for pre-training, requiring minimal domain knowledge, combined with SNNs that naturally represent multi-modal policies. The paper demonstrates that this approach can learn interpretable skills and significantly improve learning performance across a range of challenging tasks with sparse rewards.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper makes a strong empirical contribution showing that their method can learn diverse skills and significantly improve performance on sparse reward tasks compared to strong baselines; (2) the framework is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in the literature by combining intrinsic motivation with hierarchical learning in a principled way.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for the benefits of their hierarchical approach. The experimental results are thorough and well-designed, with careful attention to the diversity of skills learned and their impact on downstream task performance. The authors demonstrate that their SNN-based approach with the information-theoretic regularizer can learn a wider span of interpretable skills compared to training separate policies, while maintaining sample efficiency. The results on maze navigation and food collection tasks clearly show that their method outperforms baselines, including the strong baseline of adding the same proxy reward to the downstream task (which surprisingly performs poorly).

The motivation for the approach is well-placed in the literature, correctly identifying the limitations of existing methods (hand-engineered hierarchies, intrinsic reward methods that don't transfer). The connection to related work on hierarchical RL, intrinsic motivation, and skill learning is appropriate and well-justified. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is a natural fit for the problem, and the information-theoretic regularizer provides a principled way to encourage skill diversity.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper makes a promising contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. To further strengthen the work, I would suggest the following improvements:

1. The paper could provide more detailed analysis of the diversity of skills learned. While the visitation plots in Figure 4 are visually compelling, it would be helpful to quantify the diversity (e.g., through metrics like mutual information between skills and behaviors, or through more systematic exploration of the skill space).

2. The authors should clarify the relationship between "multi-policy" and "SNN" approaches in the paper. The distinction between these two methods isn't entirely clear, and this could be confusing for readers.

3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach, particularly regarding the fixed time scales for skill execution. The authors mention this as a limitation in the discussion, but it would be valuable to explore how this might affect performance on more complex tasks or how it could be addressed in future work.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The baseline of adding the CoM speed reward to the downstream task (Fig. 6) performs poorly despite being a strong baseline in the original Duan et al. (2016) benchmark. Could the authors clarify why this baseline performs so poorly for the maze tasks, and whether this accounts for the extra experience from pre-trained policies (i.e., is the baseline using the same number of samples as the hierarchical methods)?

2. The paper states that bilinear integration corresponds to changing all the first hidden layer weights, while concatenation corresponds to changing the bias term. Could the authors confirm this interpretation is accurate, as it's important for understanding the architectural choices?

3. The paper mentions that the MI bonus sometimes fails to boost performance (Fig. 6). Could the authors provide more insight into when the MI bonus is most beneficial, and whether there are specific characteristics of tasks that make it more effective?
