

The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward (e.g., speed for locomotion, grasping success), then leverages these skills for efficient learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovation lies in using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent a multi-modal policy that can learn multiple distinct skills simultaneously, combined with an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity. The approach effectively reduces sample complexity by learning skills once and reusing them across multiple tasks, demonstrating strong performance on benchmark continuous control tasks with sparse rewards that previous methods struggled with.

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting approach with strong empirical results, the contribution is somewhat incremental compared to Heess et al. (2016), and the paper fails to properly contextualize its method within the literature, particularly regarding the misleading use of "intrinsic motivation."

The primary reasons for rejection are: (1) the paper mischaracterizes the pre-training approach as "intrinsic motivation" when it's actually task-aligned (rewarding speed/grasping is not intrinsic but directly related to downstream task requirements), and (2) the experimental analysis lacks depth, particularly regarding why the mutual information regularization fails to improve performance on the more complex mazes despite being designed to enhance skill diversity.

The paper makes an important contribution in using SNNs to learn multiple skills simultaneously with sample efficiency, and the empirical results on the benchmark tasks are convincing. However, the mislabeling of the approach as "intrinsic motivation" is problematic, as true intrinsic motivation would not rely on a proxy reward that is specifically designed to align with downstream tasks. This mischaracterization misplaces the work in the literature and overstates the novelty. The related work section correctly cites Heess et al. (2016), which uses a similar framework but requires specifying goals in the pre-training environment, whereas the proposed method uses a single proxy reward. But the paper fails to clearly distinguish this from intrinsic motivation approaches, which typically don't require any task-specific reward design.

The experimental analysis is another weakness. The paper notes that the MI regularizer doesn't improve performance on mazes 1-3, but provides no meaningful analysis or interpretation of this finding. It's unclear whether the MI regularizer is ineffective in these specific environments, or if the skill diversity it promotes isn't useful for these particular tasks. The paper would benefit from a deeper exploration of when and why the MI regularizer helps or doesn't help, especially since the authors claim it "consistently yields a large span of skills."

The paper also lacks clarity on the structural assumption of state space factorization (S_agent vs S_rest), which is crucial for the approach. While the paper references Duan et al. (2016), it doesn't clearly specify how this factorization is implemented in their experiments, making reproducibility challenging. The paper states that "S_agent should be the same for all MDPs in M" but doesn't define what S_agent consists of in their specific experiments.

The paper could be significantly improved by: (1) correcting the mischaracterization of the pre-training approach as "intrinsic motivation" and properly contextualizing it within the literature, (2) providing deeper analysis of when the MI regularizer is beneficial and when it's not, (3) clarifying the state space factorization assumption with concrete implementation details, and (4) analyzing the switching behavior between skills, particularly the failure modes with unstable robots like Ant.

I would like the authors to address the following questions: 
1. How does your approach differ from Heess et al. (2016) in terms of the pre-training signal? Your paper states you use "a single proxy reward" while they "require a set of goals to be specified," but it's not clear how a proxy reward like speed or grasping is fundamentally different from specifying goals for pre-training. 
2. Why does the MI regularizer fail to improve performance on mazes 1-3 despite improving skill diversity, and what does this imply about the relationship between skill diversity and task performance? 
3. Please provide a clear, concrete definition of S_agent and S_rest for your specific experimental setup, including how these components are identified and separated in the state representation.
