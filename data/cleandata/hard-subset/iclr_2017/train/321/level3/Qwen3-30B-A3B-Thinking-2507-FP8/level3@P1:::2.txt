

The paper "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning" proposes a novel framework that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a single proxy reward signal, and then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors introduce a hierarchical architecture where a high-level policy (Manager) selects from a set of low-level skills learned via Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs). The key innovation is the use of an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage diversity in the skills learned. The paper demonstrates that this approach significantly improves exploration and learning performance on sparse reward tasks like mazes and object gathering, outperforming both standard RL methods and baselines with intrinsic rewards.

Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

The primary reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the challenging problem of sparse reward reinforcement learning, and (2) the proposed framework is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated with strong results across multiple tasks.

The authors provide a compelling argument for the need for a framework that can learn diverse skills without domain-specific reward engineering. Their approach addresses a key limitation of existing hierarchical RL methods that require domain knowledge for skill specification. The use of SNNs with information-theoretic regularization to encourage skill diversity is novel and well-justified. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, showing that the proposed method consistently outperforms baselines across multiple tasks. The ablation studies on integration methods (concatenation vs. bilinear) and the impact of the MI bonus provide valuable insights into the design choices.

The paper's most significant contribution is the empirical demonstration that directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards (as a baseline) fails, which underscores the value of the hierarchical abstraction and the enforced diversity in sub-policy training. The visualizations of skill diversity (visitation plots) are particularly compelling and help demonstrate the interpretability of the learned skills.

I have two main concerns that would be addressed in revisions:

First, while the paper demonstrates that the method works well for locomotion tasks, the limited diversity in the skills learned (particularly with concatenation and bilinear integration) might be insufficient for more complex manipulation tasks. The authors should provide more analysis of the skill diversity in more complex environments, as the current experiments are limited to locomotion and simple mazes. The paper mentions this limitation in the discussion, but it would be stronger to have some experimental evidence.

Second, the hierarchical architecture has limitations in that the manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time scales. This means the approach relies on having very good intrinsic rewards and prelearned sub-policies, which might limit its applicability to more complex tasks. The authors should discuss these limitations more thoroughly and suggest potential extensions.

I have a few specific questions that would help clarify the paper:

1. In Section 7.3, the "CoM proxy reward" baseline performs poorly. However, this baseline is the same reward used in the pre-training environment. Could the authors clarify why this baseline performs worse than their hierarchical approach, especially since the pre-training environment used this same reward? Is there an issue with the baseline not accounting for the extra experience from pre-trained policies?

2. The paper mentions "multi-policy" as a baseline. Could the authors clarify how "multi-policy" differs from "SNN" in their framework? Specifically, is "multi-policy" referring to training multiple separate policies (instead of using an SNN), and if so, how does this differ from the SNN approach?

3. Could the authors confirm the interpretation of Figure 1: Is concatenation equivalent to changing the bias term of the first hidden layer depending on the latent code, and is bilinear integration equivalent to changing all the first hidden layer weights (as suggested in the paper)?

The paper makes a strong contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning and addresses a significant challenge in the field. The experimental results are robust and well-analyzed, and the visualizations of skill diversity are particularly impressive. The authors have provided a promising direction for future work on skill learning and transfer in reinforcement learning. The minor concerns I've raised would be easily addressed with a few additional experiments and clarifications.
