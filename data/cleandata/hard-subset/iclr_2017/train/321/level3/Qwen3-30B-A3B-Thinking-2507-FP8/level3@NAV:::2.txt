

This paper proposes a novel hierarchical reinforcement learning framework using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn diverse skills in a pre-training environment. The key contribution is the use of an MI-based reward to encourage option identifiability, enabling the agent to learn a wide range of interpretable skills without domain-specific supervision. The authors demonstrate that directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards (a strong baseline) fails to solve sparse reward tasks, while their hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and learning efficiency across multiple maze navigation and object gathering tasks.

I recommend acceptance (score 7). The paper addresses a significant challenge in RL with a well-motivated approach that properly situates itself within the literature on hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation. The empirical findings are scientifically rigorous, with strong results showing that the proposed method consistently outperforms baselines across multiple environments, including the challenging maze navigation tasks where standard methods fail. The visualizations effectively demonstrate the learned skills and their hierarchical application.

The paper makes a promising contribution to the field, particularly in its ability to learn diverse skills without requiring task-specific rewards during pre-training. However, the current implementation has limitations: the concatenation and bilinear integration methods only allow minimal sub-policy differences, which may not suffice for more complex manipulation tasks. Additionally, the fixed sub-policies and time scales limit the framework's flexibility, and the intrinsic rewards appear domain-specific due to this fixed setup.

I request clarification on three points: (1) Why does the strong baseline in Section 7.3 (directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards) perform poorly, and does it account for the extra experience from pre-trained policies? (2) What does "multi-policy" denote in comparison to "SNN"? (3) Can the authors confirm that in Figure 1, concatenation corresponds to categorical values giving different bias to the first hidden layer, while bilinear integration gives different input weight matrices?

The paper would be strengthened by more detailed discussion of these limitations and how they might be addressed in future work. The promising direction and excellent visualizations make this a valuable contribution to the field.
