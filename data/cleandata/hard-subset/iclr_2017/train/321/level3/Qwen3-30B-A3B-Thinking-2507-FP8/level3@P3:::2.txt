

# Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary
This paper presents a promising framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that combines unsupervised skill learning with hierarchical policy execution. The authors propose using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills during a pre-training phase guided by a simple proxy reward, without requiring domain-specific knowledge. An information-theoretic regularizer encourages diversity in the learned skills, which are then leveraged by a high-level manager policy to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach is well-motivated by the challenges of sparse reward learning and long-horizon tasks, and the paper makes a strong case for the benefits of hierarchical abstraction through compelling empirical results across multiple environments. The visualizations of skill diversity and exploration patterns are particularly effective in demonstrating the paper's contributions.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with a well-motivated approach that effectively combines unsupervised skill learning with hierarchical policy execution. The key insight that directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards fails (Fig. 6) provides strong empirical evidence for the value of the proposed hierarchical abstraction.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a critical challenge in reinforcement learning: solving tasks with sparse rewards. The proposed framework successfully demonstrates that learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment can significantly improve exploration and learning efficiency in downstream tasks. The key contributions are well-justified and supported by strong empirical evidence. The information-theoretic regularizer effectively encourages diverse skill acquisition, as evidenced by the visitation plots (Fig. 4) showing distinct locomotion patterns for different latent codes. 

The finding that directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards fails (baseline in Sec 7.3) is particularly insightful, suggesting that the hierarchical structure and enforced diversity in sub-policy training provide significant benefits beyond simple reward combination. The paper's empirical results consistently show that the SNN approach outperforms the baseline across multiple tasks, with the MI bonus further improving performance in some environments.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, there are several areas where the authors could improve clarity and completeness:

- The paper could better explain the limitations of the current approach, particularly regarding the limited diversification of sub-policies (concatenation and bilinear integration only allow minimal differences). This is especially important for tasks requiring more diverse sub-policies (e.g., manipulation), where the current approach may fail.

- The hierarchy limitation (fixed sub-policies and fixed time scales) should be more thoroughly discussed, as it forces reliance on very good intrinsic rewards and prelearned sub-policies. The authors should clarify how this limitation might affect the approach's applicability to more complex tasks.

- The claim that "intrinsic/unsupervised rewards appear domain-specific" is intriguing but needs more elaboration. The authors should explain why these rewards would be domain-specific despite being designed with minimal domain knowledge.

## 5. Clarifying Questions
To help clarify my assessment, I would appreciate the authors' responses to the following questions:

1. In Sec 7.3, the strong baseline (adding CoM reward to the sparse reward task) performs poorly. Could the authors clarify why this baseline performs so poorly, and whether it accounts for the extra experience from pre-trained policies (is it the "CoM maze reward" mentioned in the abstract)? The poor performance of this baseline is a key finding, but the explanation in the paper seems insufficient.

2. The paper uses "multi-policy" and "snn" interchangeably in some places but distinguishes them in others (e.g., Sec 7.2). Could the authors clarify what "multi-policy" denotes in comparison to "snn"? Specifically, is "multi-policy" referring to training multiple independent policies (as opposed to the SNN approach), and if so, how does this differ from the SNN approach in terms of implementation and results?

3. Regarding Fig. 1, the paper states "concatenation = categorical value gives different bias to first hidden layer; bilinear integration = categorical value gives different input weight matrix." Could the authors confirm this interpretation? This would help clarify the architectural differences between the two integration methods.

The paper presents a promising direction for hierarchical reinforcement learning with clear empirical validation. The visualizations of skill diversity and exploration patterns are particularly strong, and the proposed framework demonstrates clear benefits over baseline approaches. With the clarification of the above points, the paper would be an excellent contribution to the field.
