

**Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"**

The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that learns diverse skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer. The authors claim that their approach requires minimal domain knowledge (only a single proxy reward) to learn a wide range of interpretable skills, which can then be leveraged for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The paper makes a compelling contribution to the literature by addressing the challenge of skill transfer across tasks while reducing sample complexity.

I recommend rejection. The primary concerns are the method's limited generality and the authors' claim of minimal domain knowledge. First, the paper fails to demonstrate scaling to more complex morphologies beyond the 2-link swimmer. The 5-link "Snake" robot in Appendix C shows improved performance but doesn't conclusively prove the method's robustness to increased morphological complexity, as required by the key point about generality. Second, the "minimal domain knowledge" claim is undermined by the hand-designed intrinsic rewards and discretization choices. The MI bonus in Section 5.3 relies on discretizing the state space (implied by the grid in Figure 4), which requires significant domain knowledge to implement effectively, contradicting the authors' assertion.

The paper's experimental validation is limited to simple tasks with a single robot morphology, leaving critical questions unanswered about failure modes as behavior richness increases. The discretization approach in Section 5.3 requires a grid-based state representation, but the paper doesn't clarify whether this approach would fail in continuous state spaces or how it would scale to more complex environments.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) demonstrate their method on more complex morphologies beyond the 2-link swimmer, (2) provide a thorough analysis of the discretization approach and its implications for continuous state spaces, and (3) clarify the actual domain knowledge required for their proxy reward design.

Questions for authors:
1. How would your method handle continuous state spaces without discretization? What are the theoretical guarantees for this approach?
2. Given your MI bonus relies on discretizing the state space, how would you justify this as "minimal domain knowledge" when the discretization parameters (e.g., grid size) require careful tuning?
3. Can you provide evidence that your method would scale to a 5-link swimmer where the skill diversity requirements are significantly higher than in the 2-link case?
