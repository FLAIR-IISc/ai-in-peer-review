- Proposes a hierarchical RL framework using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic reward to pre-train diverse sub-policies.  
- Core idea: pre-train diverse skills with minimal domain knowledge, then compose them with a high-level policy to tackle sparse-reward tasks.  
- Empirical results show significant performance gains over baselines and validate the benefit of hierarchical abstraction and diversity.  
- Visualizations of learned skills are insightful and support qualitative understanding of behaviors.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — novel, promising approach with solid empirical evidence.  
- Key finding: directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards fails, implying exploration bonuses alone are insufficient without hierarchical structure or enforced diversity.  
- Using SNNs to represent multiple skills within one network is elegant and promotes diversity; the MI regularizer offers a principled encouragement of distinct skills.  
- Experiments and ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, MI bonus effects) are well-designed and clarify component contributions.  
- Robustness demonstrated across different maze configurations, the Gather task, and multiple robot morphologies (Swimmer, Snake, others in appendix).  
- Limitation: manager depends on fixed sub-policies and fixed timescales, which constrains flexibility and future applicability.  
- Limitation: intrinsic rewards become domain-specific under the fixed setup, reducing generalizability of pre-training.  
- Suggestion: include a deeper discussion and comparison with related work on option discovery, meta-learning, and latent-state methods.  
- Question 1: Clarify which strong baseline in Sec 7.3 underperforms and whether it includes experience from pre-trained policies.  
- Question 2: Define “multi-policy” precisely—is it separate policies per skill or something else compared to SNNs?  
- Question 3: Confirm that concatenation acts like a z-dependent bias on the first hidden layer while bilinear integration modifies the input weight matrix based on z.  
- Question 4: Report sensitivity analysis for the MI bonus coefficient (αH) to assess hyperparameter robustness.  
- Question 5: How can this approach scale to much longer-horizon or higher-dimensional tasks given fixed sub-policies, and what modifications are needed?
