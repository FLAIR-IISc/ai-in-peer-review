## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill acquisition, followed by learning high-level policies on top of these skills. The approach utilizes a proxy reward for pre-training and incorporates a mutual information regularization term to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their method on challenging locomotion + maze and food collection tasks, achieving performance beyond previous benchmarks.

**Decision: Reject.** While the work presents an interesting contribution to hierarchical control and achieves strong empirical results, the experimental analysis is significantly lacking and the claims regarding “intrinsic motivation” are misleading. 

The primary concern is the weak experimental analysis. The paper demonstrates strong performance, solving benchmarks others couldn’t, but doesn’t adequately dissect *why*. For example, the mutual information regularization is shown to sometimes *hurt* performance on more complex mazes, and this is simply noted without any attempts at explanation. What is happening when the MI regularization fails? Does it lead to overly specialized skills? Are there hyperparameter sensitivities? The authors should investigate this failure case. Additionally, the impact of different pre-trained SNNs on downstream task performance isn’t clearly analyzed; the variation suggests sensitivity that needs unpacking.

Further, the framing of the pre-training reward as “intrinsic motivation” is inaccurate. Using speed/grasp rewards, while requiring minimal domain knowledge for the downstream task, is fundamentally task-aligned and represents pre-training with shaped rewards, akin to Heess et al. (2016). True intrinsic motivation strives for novelty or competence without direct task relevance. The authors should reframe this aspect of their work.

Finally, the paper lacks clarity on the factorization of the state space between S_agent and S_rest. While S_agent is defined, a concrete description of how S_rest is identified and separated is missing, hindering reproducibility. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Switching Behavior:** Analyze the switching patterns of the high-level policy. How often are skills switched? Are there specific failure cases related to suboptimal switching timing? Visualizations of the skill usage during task execution would be valuable.
*   **Policy Analysis:** Provide a deeper analysis of the learned policies. What behaviors correspond to each latent code in the SNN? Can these skills be visualized or interpreted?
*   **Ablation Study:** A thorough ablation study would help to isolate the contribution of each component (SNNs, MI regularization).
*   **Robustness:** Assess the robustness of the approach to different initial conditions and hyperparameter settings.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a detailed explanation of how S_rest is identified and separated from S_agent in different environments?
*   Why does the mutual information regularization sometimes degrade performance? What factors influence this?
*   What is the distribution of skill usage frequency during task execution? Does the frequency correlate with performance?
*   Have you explored alternative methods for encouraging skill diversity beyond mutual information regularization?
