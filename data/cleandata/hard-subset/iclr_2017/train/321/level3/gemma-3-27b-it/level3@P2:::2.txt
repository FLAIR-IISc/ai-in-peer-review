## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase. These learned skills are then utilized by a high-level policy to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors demonstrate promising results on benchmark locomotion and maze environments, suggesting that their approach effectively combines intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods to improve exploration and sample efficiency. The provided visualizations are particularly helpful in understanding the learned skills.

**Decision:**

**Accept (Weak)**. While the paper presents a compelling approach and demonstrates encouraging empirical results, some limitations and unanswered questions prevent a stronger recommendation. The core idea of utilizing SNNs to learn a diverse skill set is novel and the results suggest a benefit from this approach, but more thorough investigation and clarification are needed.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its attempt to bridge the gap between intrinsic motivation and hierarchical RL. Previous methods often struggle with either requiring significant domain knowledge for hand-designing hierarchies or failing to generalize skills across tasks. This paper addresses these issues by proposing a method for *learning* a diverse set of skills with minimal domain-specific supervision, and then leveraging these skills in a hierarchical fashion. The use of SNNs is a clever way to represent multi-modal policies and the information-theoretic regularizer appears effective in encouraging skill diversity. The empirical results, particularly the demonstration that simply adding an intrinsic reward at the task level does *not* achieve comparable performance, is a strong indicator that the hierarchical structure and skill learning process are providing a significant benefit. The visualizations of the learned skills (e.g., visitation plots) are well-presented and provide intuitive insights into the behavior of the agent.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses. The degree of diversity achievable with the current SNN architecture (concatenation and bilinear integration) seems limited, as noted by the authors. While sufficient for locomotion tasks, it appears to hinder performance on more complex tasks requiring more nuanced skills. The hierarchical architecture is also somewhat restrictive, relying on fixed sub-policies and a fixed time horizon for skill execution. This places a heavy burden on the quality of the pre-trained skills and the appropriateness of the chosen time horizon.  The strong reliance on a well-tuned proxy reward for pretraining also raises concerns about domain specificity, despite the authors’ claims to the contrary.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify Baseline:** Section 7.3 states the baseline "fails," but it isn’t immediately clear *why*. Is this due to the inherent difficulty of the tasks, or is the baseline implementation flawed? Does this baseline leverage any experience gained during the pre-training phase? Explicitly stating this would strengthen the argument for the proposed approach. The term "CoM maze reward" is unclear and should be elaborated.
*   **"Multi-policy" vs. "SNN":** Please clarify what the "Multi-policy" approach represents. Is it training separate, independent policies and selecting between them, or is it a different configuration of the SNN? A clear distinction is needed.
*   **Confirm Architectural Details:** Confirm the interpretation of Fig. 1. Is it correct that concatenation effectively changes the bias term of the first hidden layer based on the categorical latent code, while bilinear integration changes the weights?
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the overall framework, exploring the individual contributions of the SNN architecture (concatenation vs. bilinear integration) and the information-theoretic regularizer in a more controlled manner would be beneficial.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Expand the discussion of limitations, particularly concerning the fixed sub-policies and time horizons. Briefly outlining potential future directions for addressing these limitations (e.g., learning the switch time, adaptive skill selection) would strengthen the paper.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the baseline performance in Section 7.3, could you clarify *why* it performs so poorly? Is it solely due to the sparse rewards and long horizon, or are there other contributing factors?  Does it leverage any information or experience from the pre-training phase?
2.  Could you please elaborate on the differences between the “Multi-policy” architecture and the SNN-based approach? Is the “Multi-policy” simply training separate policies, or is there a more sophisticated mechanism at play?
3.  Is the interpretation of Fig. 1 correct—that concatenation affects the bias and bilinear integration affects the weights of the first hidden layer?
