## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper presents a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to tackle challenges posed by sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is to pre-train a diverse set of skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) guided by a single, easily designed proxy reward. An information-theoretic regularizer, based on Mutual Information (MI), is employed to encourage the learning of distinct skills within the SNN. Subsequently, a high-level policy is trained on top of these pre-trained skills to efficiently solve downstream tasks. The authors demonstrate through experiments that this approach learns interpretable skills and significantly boosts performance across a range of challenging tasks.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach, the experimental results, while positive, don't definitively demonstrate a compelling advantage over simpler baselines, and several aspects of the methodology require further clarification. The reliance on a carefully tuned proxy reward and the limited scope of demonstrable gains raise concerns about the generalizability and robustness of the proposed framework.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper successfully articulates the motivation for hierarchical reinforcement learning and the difficulties associated with sparse reward environments. The introduction of SNNs, combined with the MI regularizer, is a reasonable approach to encourage skill diversity. However, the strength of the claim that this method *significantly* boosts performance is not fully substantiated. 

The experimental results, while showing improvements over naive exploration, are presented in a way that makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed framework. Notably, the baseline of simply adding the proxy reward to the downstream tasks is shown to perform poorly, which *suggests* the benefit of the hierarchical abstraction or the enforced diversity of the sub-policies. However, a more thorough comparison against established intrinsic motivation techniques (like those also benchmarked in the referenced work by Duan et al., 2016) would be necessary. 

Furthermore, the reported results lack sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter sensitivity. The reliance on a well-tuned proxy reward feels somewhat akin to a carefully engineered supervised reward, undermining the claim of minimal domain knowledge. The fact that the "multi-policy" approach (which isn't clearly defined) performs comparably to the SNN-based approach raises questions about whether the complexity of the SNN is truly necessary.

The limitations related to the fixed sub-policy timescales and the inability to adapt skills during downstream task learning are significant. This rigid structure necessitates excellent pre-trained skills and limits the system's ability to recover from unforeseen situations.

**4. Additional Feedback & Questions**

This paper has potential, and addressing the following points would significantly strengthen it:

*   **Clarification of Baselines:** The performance of the baseline with the proxy reward added directly to the downstream task is surprisingly weak. Please clarify why this is the case and whether this baseline accounts for any experience gained during the pre-training phase. Is the "CoM maze reward" the same reward used during pretraining?
*   **Definition of "Multi-policy":**  The term "Multi-policy" is used in the experiments, but it's not clearly defined. How does this approach differ from the SNN-based approach, and why is it included as a baseline?
*   **Detailed Explanation of Integration Methods:**  Confirm my understanding of Figure 1: does "concatenation" mean that the categorical latent variable directly modifies the bias of the first hidden layer, while "bilinear integration" means it modifies the input weight matrix?
*   **Ablation Studies:**  Perform more targeted ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the SNN architecture, the MI regularizer, and the hierarchical structure.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the MI bonus term (alphaH).
*   **Generalization to More Complex Tasks:** Evaluate the framework on tasks with more complex state and action spaces and explore its ability to adapt to changing environments.
*   **Addressing Instability:** Explore mechanisms to mitigate the instability issue observed with the Ant robot. Can adaptive skill switching strategies or online skill refinement address this limitation?



Despite these criticisms, the paper presents a promising direction for HRL research, and the visualizations are well-executed and informative. I encourage the authors to address these points and resubmit a revised version.
