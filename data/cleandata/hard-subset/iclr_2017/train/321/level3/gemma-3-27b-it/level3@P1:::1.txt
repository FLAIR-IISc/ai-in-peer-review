## Review of STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery, followed by high-level policy learning on top of these skills. The approach aims to address challenges in sparse reward and long-horizon tasks by pre-training a diverse set of skills with a simple proxy reward, and then efficiently learning downstream tasks using these pre-trained skills. The experimental results demonstrate strong performance on benchmark tasks, surpassing previous methods.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a compelling approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning with clear potential benefits for sparse reward environments. While the analysis could be more in-depth, the results are strong enough to warrant acceptance. The combination of SNNs with a mutual information regularizer appears to be a particularly effective strategy for learning a diverse and useful skill set, which translates into improved performance on challenging downstream tasks.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles an important problem in reinforcement learning: sample efficiency in sparse reward environments. The proposed approach is well-motivated, building on existing work in both intrinsic motivation and hierarchical RL, but offering a novel combination through the use of SNNs. The key idea of pre-training a diverse set of skills with a minimal domain-specific proxy reward is appealing, as it reduces the need for hand-engineering and allows for knowledge transfer across tasks.

The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms previous methods on the Locomotion + Maze and Locomotion + Food Collection benchmarks. Furthermore, they present ablation studies demonstrating the impact of the different components of their framework, such as the bilinear integration in the SNN and the mutual information regularizer. While the MI regularizer does not consistently improve performance across all tasks, its inclusion often leads to a richer skill set.  The thoroughness of the experiments, including testing on different maze configurations and the Snake robot, strengthens the claims made in the paper. 

The inclusion of code and videos is a significant positive, allowing for easy verification and reproducibility of the results. The detailed hyperparameter settings reported in the appendix also contribute to the transparency and reproducibility of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, several areas could be improved. 

1. **Clarify “Intrinsic Motivation”:** The authors use the term “intrinsic motivation,” but the proxy reward used during pre-training (speed or grasping) feels more akin to task-aligned reward shaping than true intrinsic motivation (e.g., curiosity-driven exploration). Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the framing of the contribution. The relationship to work from Heess et al. (2016) should be more thoroughly discussed and contrasted, highlighting the specific advantages of the proposed approach.

2. **Detailed State Space Factorization:** The paper states a factorization of the state space into S<sub>agent</sub> and S<sub>rest</sub>. However, the definition of S<sub>rest</sub> is not sufficiently detailed for reproduction. Providing a clearer specification of this factorization, particularly in the context of the benchmark tasks, would be helpful.

3. **Analysis of Mutual Information:** The lack of consistent improvement with the mutual information regularizer warrants further investigation. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth analysis exploring the reasons for this observation would be valuable. Is the regularization strength optimal for all tasks? Are there specific task characteristics that influence the effectiveness of the MI bonus?

4. **Switching Behavior & Timing:** The paper briefly mentions challenges with switching between skills, particularly for unstable robots. A more detailed analysis of this behavior is needed. How often do skill switches occur? What is the impact of switch timing (T) on performance? A visualization of the state space and the skills learned could help understand how switching influences exploration and learning.

5. **Failure Mode Analysis:** Expanding the failure mode analysis, especially for the Ant robot, would provide valuable insights. Understanding *why* the agent fails when switching skills could inform future improvements to the framework.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the choice of the proxy reward? What considerations led you to select speed/grasping, and how does this choice impact the types of skills learned?
2. Can you provide a more detailed description of the S<sub>rest</sub> component of the state space for the benchmark tasks? Specifically, what features are included in S<sub>rest</sub> and how are they defined?
3. Why do you believe the mutual information regularizer doesn't consistently improve performance? Have you explored different regularization strengths or alternative information-theoretic measures?
4. How does the choice of the switch time *T* impact the performance of the hierarchical policy, and are there any strategies for adaptively determining an optimal value for *T*?
5. Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the skill switching behavior, perhaps by visualizing the state trajectory during learning and highlighting when switches occur?
