## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery, coupled with an information-theoretic regularizer to promote skill diversity. The core idea of pre-training a diverse skill set and then utilizing these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards is appealing and well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in RL. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is a novel approach, and the authors clearly articulate the rationale behind their design choices.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper in its current form. While the idea is promising, the paper falls short in convincingly demonstrating the generality and robustness of the proposed approach, and the claim of "minimal domain knowledge" feels overstated. A core concern is the potential failure of the method as behavior richness increases – the experiments currently focus on relatively simple robotic morphologies.

My primary concern stems from the limited exploration of morphological generality. The experiments primarily focus on a 2-link swimmer. While results are shown for a 5-link snake, a more thorough investigation into scaling to significantly more complex morphologies (e.g., a manipulator arm) is needed to assess the true generality of the method. Does the learned skill diversity translate effectively to more complex action spaces and state observations? 

Furthermore, the claim of minimal domain knowledge is questionable. The proxy reward, while seemingly simple (e.g., robot speed), is still a *hand-designed* intrinsic reward, specifically tailored to elicit locomotion behaviors. This isn’t necessarily a flaw, but it contradicts the assertion of minimal domain knowledge. A more honest framing would acknowledge this as a form of carefully designed intrinsic motivation. 

Finally, Section 5.3 implies a discretization of the state space to calculate mutual information. The paper needs to explicitly clarify whether this is the case, *why* it's necessary, and how the performance is affected by the granularity of this discretization, or what happens if a continuous state space is considered.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies are needed to isolate the contribution of each component – the SNNs, the MI regularizer, and the hierarchical structure.
*   **Quantitative Comparison:** A more rigorous quantitative comparison to existing HRL and intrinsic motivation methods (beyond the cited VIME) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Failure Cases:** A discussion of failure modes and limitations of the approach, particularly concerning stability and transfer to more challenging tasks, would be valuable.
*   **Clarify Notation:** Improve clarity regarding the use of the latent variable 'z' – is it sampled during pre-training or during downstream task execution?




I would be more confident in this work with a more convincing demonstration of scalability to more complex systems and a more nuanced discussion of the role of domain knowledge.
