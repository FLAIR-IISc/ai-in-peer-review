## Review of Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning

This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that combines stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn useful skills in a pre-training environment. These skills are then leveraged to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach aims to address challenges in sparse reward settings by learning a diverse set of skills with minimal domain knowledge. While the approach is promising and the experiments demonstrate performance improvements, several concerns remain regarding the generality, scalability, and true minimal domain knowledge requirement.

**1. Summary of Claims:**

The paper claims to present a novel and effective method for learning a diverse set of skills that can be transferred to multiple downstream tasks with sparse rewards. By utilizing SNNs and a mutual information-based regularization term, the authors aim to learn interpretable skills with limited prior knowledge about the target tasks. The experiments on locomotion and maze tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in improving sample efficiency and performance compared to baseline methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting approach, the concerns regarding the generality of the method, the level of hand-design required, and the limited exploration of failure modes prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The results, while positive, don't convincingly demonstrate a significant advancement over existing approaches given the level of engineering involved.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

The paper’s strengths lie in its combination of SNNs for multi-modal policy representation and the MI regularization to promote skill diversity. The experimental results show performance gains on the chosen tasks, and the visualizations of the learned skills are compelling. However, the method appears highly sensitive to hyperparameters and the specific design of the proxy reward, casting doubt on its true “minimal domain knowledge” requirement. 

The scaling analysis with the more complex “Snake” robot (Appendix C) demonstrates a decline in robustness and a greater dependency on the quality of the pre-trained SNN. The failure modes identified with the “Ant” robot (Appendix D) – instability during skill switching – highlight a significant limitation of the approach. This instability is not adequately addressed, and the proposed solutions are only briefly mentioned.  

Furthermore, the discretization of the state space for the mutual information calculation (Section 5.3) is a concerning detail. The paper doesn't justify this discretization. If the underlying state space is continuous, arbitrarily discretizing it introduces a strong inductive bias and potentially hinders the learning of truly generalizable skills. The authors need to clarify *why* this discretization is necessary, its impact on the learned skills, and explore alternatives for continuous state spaces.

The claim of “minimal domain knowledge” is also debatable. While the proxy reward doesn't require precise goal specifications, it *does* require careful design to encourage the exploration of useful behaviors. The choice of rewarding speed for the locomotion tasks, or successful grasping, is not trivial and represents a significant engineering decision tied to the specific control problems. This isn’t simply “intrinsic motivation” but a carefully crafted reward function that implicitly guides the agent toward desirable skills.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

*   **Robustness and Scalability:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the method's robustness to different environments and robot morphologies. Scaling to more complex agents and tasks is crucial for demonstrating the generality of the approach.
*   **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies are needed to understand the contribution of each component of the framework, particularly the MI regularizer and the bilinear integration.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** While comparisons to some existing methods are provided, a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art hierarchical RL algorithms would strengthen the paper.
*   **Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper should address the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters and provide guidance on how to tune them for different tasks.
*   **Clarification of Discretization:** The rationale and consequences of state space discretization for the mutual information calculation must be thoroughly discussed. What happens if the space isn't discretized? What if a different discretization scheme is used?
*  **Explain the limitations of the switch time (T)** - The effect of the switch time T on learning appears minimal. This could either be an interesting finding that shows robustness to this parameter or a sign that the system is not particularly sensitive to the quality of the skill switching. Clarification is necessary.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the design choices made for the proxy reward and justify the claim of "minimal domain knowledge"?
*   How does the performance of the framework change with different levels of discretization in Section 5.3? Have you explored alternative approaches for handling continuous state spaces?
*   What modifications would be necessary to address the instability issues observed with the "Ant" robot?
*   Have you explored other methods for promoting skill diversity besides the MI regularizer, and if so, how do they compare?
*   What are the computational costs associated with the SNNs and MI calculation, and how do they scale with the dimensionality of the state and action spaces?
