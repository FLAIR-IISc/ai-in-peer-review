- Paper proposes using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn transferable skills for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward tasks.
- Authors claim the method learns diverse, interpretable skills with minimal prior knowledge and improves sample efficiency on locomotion and maze tasks.
- Recommendation: Reject due to concerns about generality, required hand-design, and unexplored failure modes.
- Strength: Combines SNNs and mutual information (MI) regularization to promote multi-modal, diverse policy representations.
- Experimental results show improvements on chosen tasks and compelling visualizations of learned skills.
- Method appears highly sensitive to hyperparameters and proxy reward design, undermining the “minimal domain knowledge” claim.
- Scaling to more complex agents (e.g., Snake) reduces robustness and increases dependence on pre-trained SNN quality.
- Failure modes (e.g., Ant instability during skill switching) are significant and not adequately addressed.
- Discretization of state space for MI calculation is unjustified and may introduce harmful inductive bias for continuous spaces.
- Proxy reward design (e.g., rewarding speed or grasping) requires task-specific engineering, contradicting minimal domain-knowledge claims.
- Need more thorough robustness and scalability analysis across environments and robot morphologies.
- More ablation studies required to isolate contributions of MI regularizer and bilinear integration.
- Broader comparisons to state-of-the-art hierarchical RL baselines are necessary.
- Paper should discuss hyperparameter sensitivity and provide tuning guidance.
- Clarify rationale and consequences of state discretization and explore continuous alternatives.
- Explain the role and limitations of the skill switch time T and whether its apparent minimal effect is meaningful.
- Questions for authors include justification of proxy reward choices, effect of different discretizations, fixes for Ant instability, alternative diversity methods, and computational/scaling costs.
