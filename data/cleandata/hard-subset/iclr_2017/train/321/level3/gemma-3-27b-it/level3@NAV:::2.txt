## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic reward to learn diverse, reusable skills. The core idea – pre-training a range of skills with minimal domain knowledge and then utilizing them in downstream tasks – is a compelling approach to tackle sparse reward and long-horizon problems. The empirical results demonstrate that directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards is less effective, highlighting the benefits of hierarchical abstraction or the enforced diversity during sub-policy training. The visualizations of learned skills are particularly insightful.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper demonstrates a promising approach to hierarchical RL, presenting a well-motivated idea and supporting it with solid empirical evidence. While there are areas for improvement, the contribution is significant and addresses a key challenge in reinforcement learning. The strong finding regarding the failure of directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards is particularly valuable.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully demonstrates that SNNs, coupled with the information-theoretic regularizer, can learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase. These learned skills then demonstrably improve performance in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The ablation studies highlighting the importance of the bilinear integration and MI bonus are convincing. The fact that the pre-training environment requires minimal domain knowledge is a substantial advantage. The comparative performance against simpler baselines and other intrinsic motivation methods provides a good context for the proposed approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Limited Diversification:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of concatenation and bilinear integration for tasks requiring more diverse sub-policies. Exploring alternative integration methods or more expressive SNN architectures would strengthen the work.
* **Hierarchy Limitation:** The fixed sub-policy timescales and the reliance on well-defined intrinsic rewards during pre-training seem restrictive. Investigating adaptive timescales or more sophisticated intrinsic reward schemes could broaden the applicability of the framework.
* **Clarification of Baseline:** As requested by the supervisor, please clarify why the strong baseline in Sec 7.3 performs poorly, specifically whether it accounts for the extra experience gained during pre-training (is it related to the "CoM maze reward"?).
* **Multi-policy vs. SNN:** Please clarify what "multi-policy" denotes in comparison to the SNN approach. Is it simply training separate policies independently, or is there a specific architectural difference?
* **Fig. 1 Confirmation:** Please confirm the interpretation of Fig. 1: Does concatenation indeed provide a categorical value influencing the bias of the first hidden layer, and does bilinear integration lead to a categorical value influencing the input weight matrix?

Addressing these points would enhance the clarity and impact of the paper. Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field, and I look forward to seeing further developments in this direction.
