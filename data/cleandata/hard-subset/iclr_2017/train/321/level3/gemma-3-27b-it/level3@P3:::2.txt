## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic reward to encourage diverse sub-policies. The core idea—pre-training a diverse set of skills with minimal domain knowledge and then composing them via a high-level policy—is compelling and addresses a critical challenge in sparse reward RL. The empirical results demonstrate the benefit of hierarchical abstraction and diversity, showing significant performance improvements over baseline methods. The visualizations of the learned skills are particularly insightful.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and promising approach to hierarchical RL, backed by solid empirical evidence. While some limitations exist, the contribution is significant and warrants acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The central finding – that directly combining intrinsic and sparse rewards *fails* – is crucial. This strongly suggests that simply adding exploration bonuses isn't enough; hierarchical abstraction or, as demonstrated here, enforced diversity in sub-policy training is essential. This result has implications beyond the specific techniques used in this paper. The use of SNNs to represent multiple skills within a single network is an elegant approach to enabling diversity, and the information-theoretic regularizer provides a principled way to encourage it.

The experiments are well-designed, and the ablation studies (e.g., comparing concatenation vs. bilinear integration, effect of the MI bonus) help to understand the contribution of each component. The robustness of the approach across different maze configurations and the Gather task further strengthens the claims. The fact that the authors demonstrate this with multiple robots (Swimmer and Snake, with results for others in the appendix) is positive.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the results are strong, the limitations surrounding the manager's reliance on fixed sub-policies and timescales are important. The authors acknowledge this, but it’s a critical area for future work. The observation that the intrinsic rewards become domain-specific due to this fixed setup is also concerning, limiting the generalizability of the pre-training phase. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper. 

The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work on option discovery and hierarchical RL. Specifically, comparing the approach to more recent methods like those based on meta-learning or learning latent state spaces would provide valuable context.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Baseline Clarification:** In Section 7.3, you mention a strong baseline performing poorly. Could you clarify exactly which baseline this is (is it the “CoM maze reward”?), and importantly, does it account for *any* additional experience gained from pre-trained policies? This is crucial to understanding why it underperforms the proposed method.

2. **Multi-policy vs. SNN:** What is the precise definition of “multi-policy” as used in your experiments? Is it simply training separate policies for each skill, or is there a more nuanced difference compared to the SNN-based approach?

3. **Integration Details:** Confirming my understanding of Fig. 1, concatenation effectively adds a bias term to the first hidden layer based on the categorical value of *z*, while bilinear integration modifies the input weight matrix based on *z*. Is this an accurate description?

4. **Robustness to Hyperparameters:** The MI bonus coefficient (αH) appears to be important. Was there any sensitivity analysis performed to determine the robustness of the results to variations in this hyperparameter?

5. **Scale to more complex tasks:** Given the limitations of fixed sub-policies, how do you anticipate scaling this approach to much more complex tasks with very long horizons or significant state/action dimensionality? What modifications would be necessary?
