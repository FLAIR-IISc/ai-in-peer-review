This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first pre-trains a set of skills using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) and a mutual information (MI) regularizer, and then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach aims to combine the benefits of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, requiring minimal domain knowledge for the pre-training phase. The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on several challenging tasks, outperforming previous approaches.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning – learning in sparse reward environments. The proposed approach is well-motivated and builds upon existing work in both hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation. The use of SNNs is a novel and interesting idea, allowing for the learning of a diverse set of skills within a single network. The MI regularizer further encourages diversity, potentially leading to a more comprehensive skill set. The empirical results are compelling, showcasing performance improvements across a range of tasks where existing methods struggle. The code availability is a strong positive aspect, facilitating reproducibility and further research. 

However, the presentation could be improved with more thorough analysis, particularly regarding the MI regularization and the impact of the factorization assumption. While the results are positive overall, a deeper understanding of *why* the approach works so well – and when it might fail – would strengthen the contribution.

**Additional Feedback:**

1. **Clarify “Intrinsic Motivation”**: The paper frequently uses the term "intrinsic motivation," but the pre-training reward (speed, grasping) is inherently task-aligned. This is more akin to pre-training with a shaped reward, as seen in Heess et al. (2016), rather than genuine intrinsic motivation which aims for exploration without specific task goals. This distinction should be clarified.

2. **Analyze MI Regularization**: The MI regularizer appears beneficial in some cases, but performance gains are not consistently observed across all mazes. The paper acknowledges this, but lacks a detailed investigation. Why does the MI regularization *not* help on more complex mazes (1-3)? Is it a hyperparameter tuning issue, or does the structure of these mazes negate its benefit? Exploring this would be valuable.

3. **Define S_agent and S_rest**: The paper introduces a factorization of the state space into S_agent and S_rest, crucial for the framework’s applicability. However, the definition of S_rest is vague. While Duan et al. (2016) define S_agent for these environments, a clear explanation of how S_rest is defined in this work is needed for reproducibility. Specific examples would be beneficial.

4. **Switching Behavior Analysis**: The hierarchical framework relies on switching between skills. A more detailed analysis of this switching behavior would be beneficial. How often does switching occur? What triggers a switch? Are there failure cases where frequent switching hinders performance? Investigating these questions would provide insights into the limitations and potential improvements of the approach. Examining the effect of the switch time 'T' in more detail would also be informative, particularly how it relates to the task complexity and robot dynamics.

5. **Failure Modes**:  The reference to the technical report on failure modes for the Ant robot (Appendix D.2) is good. Summarizing the key findings within the main paper would strengthen the discussion of limitations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The MI regularization is tuned with a coefficient αH. How sensitive are the results to the choice of αH? Did you perform a systematic hyperparameter search for this value, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Regarding the state space factorization, can you provide specific details on how S_rest is defined for each environment (locomotion + maze, locomotion + gather)?
3.  Could you elaborate on the challenges encountered with the Ant robot, particularly the instability issues during skill switching? What strategies were considered or explored to mitigate these problems?
4.  The paper mentions a potential for end-to-end training. Have you explored this direction, and if so, what were the results? What challenges did you encounter when attempting to apply straight-through estimators?
5.  The visitation plots in Fig. 4 are helpful for visualizing learned skills. Could you provide similar plots for the Snake robot, demonstrating the impact of the MI bonus and bilinear integration?
