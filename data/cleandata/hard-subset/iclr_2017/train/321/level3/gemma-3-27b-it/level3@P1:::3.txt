## Review of Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework utilizing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for pre-training skills, subsequently used to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach combines intrinsic motivation via a proxy reward with the representational capacity of SNNs, regularized with a mutual information term to encourage skill diversity. The paper demonstrates promising results on locomotion and maze tasks, showing improvements over baseline methods.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting approach and shows some positive results, the limitations and concerns regarding the methodology and claims outweigh the contributions. Specifically, the reliance on hand-designed proxy rewards and the potential instability of the approach with more complex agents raise significant concerns about the practicality and generality of the proposed framework.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary weakness lies in the claim of "minimal domain knowledge." While the proxy reward is simpler than designing specific skills, it is still a *hand-crafted* reward signal. The choice of rewarding speed in the pre-training environment is heavily tailored to the locomotion tasks considered.  It’s not clear this would generalize to other robotic platforms or tasks – a grasping robot would require a very different proxy reward. The authors acknowledge this somewhat in the related work, but the overall presentation suggests a greater degree of automation and generality than is achieved.

Secondly, the experiments show variability in performance depending on the pre-trained SNN. The authors themselves acknowledge that some SNNs are less effective in the downstream tasks, particularly with the more complex “Ant” robot. This instability suggests a sensitivity to initialization or training dynamics that limits the robustness of the framework. The authors identify a potential solution (end-to-end training), but this remains future work and the current instability is a significant drawback. The failure modes with the Ant robot, where skill switching leads to instability, are particularly concerning.

Finally, the discretization mentioned in Section 5.3 regarding the mutual information calculation needs further clarification. Is the state space discretized? If so, why, and how does this affect the theoretical properties of the mutual information maximization? If not, how is MI calculated in a continuous state space? The paper lacks sufficient detail on this crucial implementation aspect.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study exploring the contribution of each component (SNNs, MI regularization, proxy reward design) would be highly valuable. How much of the performance gain comes from each aspect?
*   **Generalization to Morphology:** While experiments with the “Snake” robot are included, further investigation into scalability to even more complex morphologies (e.g., humanoid robots) is necessary to assess the true generality of the approach. Does the framework require significant re-tuning or architectural adjustments for different robot designs?
*   **Reward Shaping Discussion:** A more nuanced discussion regarding the nature of the proxy reward is needed. Acknowledging its role as a form of reward shaping and its potential limitations would strengthen the paper.
*   **Switching Policy:** The authors briefly mention learning a transition policy. Elaborating on this potential direction and discussing its potential benefits and challenges would be helpful.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** While not essential, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the mutual information maximization and its effect on skill diversity would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the claim of “minimal domain knowledge,” can you elaborate on the general principles for designing effective proxy rewards across different robotic platforms and tasks? What strategies can be employed to avoid overly task-specific reward design?
2.  The paper reports variability in performance based on the pre-trained SNN. What steps can be taken to mitigate this variability and ensure more consistent performance across different pre-training runs?
3.  Can you provide more details on the discretization process used in the mutual information calculation? How does the choice of grid resolution affect the results? If the state space is not discretized, please explain how MI is computed.
4.  How does the performance of the proposed framework compare to other hierarchical reinforcement learning approaches that *do* require more explicit domain knowledge, such as those involving hand-designed skill hierarchies? What are the trade-offs between the two approaches?
5.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of applying this approach to tasks with very high-dimensional, continuous state spaces where discretization becomes impractical?
