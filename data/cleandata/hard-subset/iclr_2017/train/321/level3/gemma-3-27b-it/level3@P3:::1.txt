## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

This paper presents a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning, combined with mutual information regularization. The core idea – pre-training a diverse set of skills using a proxy reward, then utilizing these skills via a high-level policy – is promising and builds upon recent work in both intrinsic motivation and HRL (e.g., Heess et al., 2016). The experimental results demonstrate performance improvements on challenging locomotion and maze tasks, exceeding that of previous methods.

**Decision: Reject**

While the approach is interesting and the experimental results show improvements, the weaknesses in experimental analysis and justification for design choices outweigh the contributions. Specifically, the claim of “intrinsic motivation” is misleading, and the lack of detailed analysis regarding the failure of MI regularization on more complex mazes, alongside concerns regarding reproducibility, prevent a positive assessment.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The central claim of utilizing “intrinsic motivation” is problematic. The use of speed/grasp rewards during pre-training is task-aligned and represents a form of pre-training, similar to previous work (e.g., Heess et al., 2015), rather than genuine exploration driven by internal curiosity. Labeling this as intrinsic motivation obscures the fact that the pre-training phase is still guided by an externally defined reward signal. The authors acknowledge similarities to Heess et al. (2016), but downplay the crucial difference of requiring pre-defined goals in that work versus the proxy reward here.

The results are presented with a limited analytical depth. While the experiments demonstrate performance gains, the paper lacks a robust analysis of *why* these gains occur. The impact of the bilinear integration within the SNNs is asserted but not rigorously evaluated against other integration methods. Similarly, the mutual information regularization, while elegant in theory, does not improve performance on the more complex mazes (1-3), and this is simply noted without investigation. Why does MI regularization fail in these scenarios? Is it related to the state space complexity, or a limitation of the MI estimation method used?

Reproducibility is a concern. The factorization of the state space into *S<sub>agent</sub>* and *S<sub>rest</sub>* is critical to the framework, yet the definition of *S<sub>rest</sub>* remains vaguely described. The paper states *S<sub>rest</sub>* contains “task-specific attributes,” but offers little detail on how this is determined or implemented for each task. Clear specification is crucial for others to replicate and extend this work.

Finally, the paper lacks sufficient analysis of the switching behavior between skills. What is the impact of the fixed switching time *T* on performance? Are there failure modes associated with frequent or infrequent skill changes? A deeper understanding of how the high-level policy orchestrates the learned skills is needed to fully evaluate the efficacy of the hierarchical approach.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Refine Terminology:** Avoid using "intrinsic motivation" and instead accurately describe the pre-training phase as guided skill learning with a proxy reward.
*   **Detailed Analysis of MI Regularization:** Investigate why MI regularization fails to improve performance on mazes 1-3. Consider alternative regularization techniques or explore the impact of different MI estimation methods.
*   **Clarify State Space Factorization:** Provide a precise definition of *S<sub>rest</sub>* for each experimental task. Include details on how this factorization is determined.
*   **Switching Behavior Analysis:** Analyze the impact of the switching time *T*. Explore adaptive switching strategies or learn a termination policy. Investigate failure modes related to skill transitions.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components (e.g., bilinear integration, MI regularization) to the overall performance.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Provide a more comprehensive comparison to relevant baselines, including variations of HRL approaches with different skill learning methods.
*   **Code Release:** While code is available, ensuring it’s fully executable and well-documented is vital for reproducibility.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the criteria used to define *S<sub>rest</sub>* for each task? What considerations were made to ensure that *S<sub>agent</sub>* remained consistent across tasks?
2.  Why do you hypothesize that the mutual information regularization fails to improve performance on the more complex mazes? Have you considered alternative explanations or methods for encouraging skill diversity?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the fixed switching time *T*? Have you explored adaptive switching strategies or learned termination policies?
4.  Could you provide more details on the exploration behavior exhibited by the high-level policy? Specifically, what patterns of skill usage emerge during the learning process?
5.  How does the performance vary when using different initialization strategies for the SNN weights, given the potential for local optima?
