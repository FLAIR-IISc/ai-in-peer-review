## Review of “Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning”

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery, followed by training a high-level policy on these learned skills to improve sample efficiency in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach combines intrinsic motivation (via a proxy reward) with hierarchical methods, aiming to minimize the need for domain-specific knowledge. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on locomotion and maze navigation tasks.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting idea and a well-structured approach, concerns regarding the generality of the method and the claim of minimal domain knowledge, coupled with unresolved questions about the implementation details, lead me to recommend rejection. The empirical results, while positive, are not sufficiently compelling to overcome these concerns.

**Supporting Arguments:**

My primary concern lies with the scalability and generality of the proposed method. The experiments primarily focus on a 2-link swimmer robot. While results are shown for a 5-link “Snake” robot in the appendix, it isn’t clear whether the approach would generalize to significantly more complex morphologies. The paper doesn’t adequately address whether the benefits observed with the swimmer and snake would hold for robots with more degrees of freedom, more complex action spaces, or drastically different kinematic structures. Scaling to such scenarios is crucial for broader applicability of HRL methods.

Furthermore, I disagree with the authors’ claim of minimal domain knowledge. The proxy reward, while seemingly simple (e.g., speed for the swimmer), still constitutes a form of domain knowledge. The choice to reward speed implicitly encourages locomotion, directing the skill discovery process. A truly domain-agnostic approach would ideally derive the skills purely from the environment's dynamics, without such pre-defined incentives. While the authors acknowledge needing “very minimal” domain knowledge, the framing seems misleading. The effectiveness heavily relies on this initial, albeit simple, reward shaping.

Finally, the description of the state/space discretization in Section 5.3 is unclear. The paper mentions a grid-based approach for estimating mutual information, but it's not explicitly stated whether the *state space itself* is discretized. If it is, the authors need to justify this choice, especially given that the motivation for using SNNs is to handle continuous action spaces. If not, it remains unclear how the MI is calculated in a continuous space. The impact of discretization resolution on the results should also be discussed.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Failure Modes:** The paper briefly acknowledges potential issues with unstable robots like Ant in the appendix. A more thorough discussion of failure modes and limitations, particularly regarding the robustness of the learned skills to perturbations or changes in the environment, would strengthen the paper. Specifically, how do the learned skills transfer *between* tasks if the dynamics of those tasks differ?
*   **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies would be helpful to understand the contribution of each component of the framework. For example, what is the performance gain achieved by using SNNs compared to training separate policies? How does the MI bonus impact the diversity and transferability of the learned skills?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The influence of key hyperparameters, such as the latent dimension K, the MI coefficient αH, and the switch time T, needs to be further investigated. A sensitivity analysis could reveal potential pitfalls and guide practical applications of the method.
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational cost associated with training and deploying the SNN-based framework would be valuable. How does the computational overhead compare to traditional HRL methods or other skill discovery approaches?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the experiments performed with the Snake robot beyond the visitation plots? Were the performance gains observed on the downstream tasks comparable to those achieved with the swimmer robot?
2.  Regarding the state discretization, is the continuous state space being discretized *before* being fed into the SNN or is the grid used solely for MI estimation? If discretization is used, what is the justification for choosing that particular resolution, and how sensitive are the results to changes in resolution?
3.  How would you characterize the domain knowledge required to design the proxy reward? Can you provide examples of scenarios where a truly domain-agnostic approach (without any proxy reward) might be feasible, and how the proposed method would compare in those scenarios?
4.   What modifications would be necessary to address the instability issues observed with the Ant robot, beyond those mentioned in the appendix? Would incorporating a recurrent policy at the manager level help mitigate these issues, and if so, how?
