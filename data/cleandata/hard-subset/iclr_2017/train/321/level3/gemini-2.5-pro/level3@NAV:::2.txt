This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process: first, a diverse set of low-level skills (sub-policies) are pre-trained in a simple environment using a generic proxy reward. This skill discovery phase uses a stochastic neural network (SNN) policy, where diversity is explicitly encouraged via an information-theoretic regularizer. In the second stage, a high-level manager policy is trained to select among these frozen, pre-trained skills to solve complex downstream tasks. The empirical results on challenging locomotion benchmarks demonstrate significant improvements in sample efficiency and final performance.

I recommend **Accept**. After initial consideration, I have raised my score from 6 to 7. The paper presents a promising and well-executed approach to skill learning in HRL. The key reason for my decision is the strong empirical evidence supporting the value of the proposed hierarchical abstraction. Specifically, the experiments compellingly show that simply combining the intrinsic (proxy) and sparse task rewards fails, whereas the proposed hierarchical method succeeds, highlighting the benefits of structured skill learning and temporal abstraction.

The method's strength lies in its effective combination of SNNs with an information-theoretic objective for unsupervised skill discovery. This allows for sample-efficient learning of a diverse set of interpretable skills, as clearly shown in the visualizations. The paper's ablations on the latent variable integration method (concatenation vs. bilinear) and the effect of the mutual information bonus are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the utility of each component.

However, the framework has some limitations. First, the diversification mechanism, which modulates the first network layer, seems suited for generating variations of a base behavior (e.g., different locomotion gaits) but may be insufficient for tasks requiring more distinct sub-policies, such as robotic manipulation. Second, the hierarchy is rigid: the manager is trained over fixed sub-policies and a fixed time scale. This places a high burden on the pre-training phase to generate a perfect skill set, making the "unsupervised" proxy reward feel somewhat domain-specific and carefully engineered.

The work could be strengthened by exploring ways to relax the rigid hierarchy, for example by fine-tuning the sub-policies during downstream task training or by learning a termination condition for each skill.

Overall, this is a well-written paper presenting a valuable contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The visualizations are excellent and the direction is very promising.
