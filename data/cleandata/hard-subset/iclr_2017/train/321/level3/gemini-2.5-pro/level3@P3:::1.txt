### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. In the first stage, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a pre-training environment using a simple, generic proxy reward (e.g., forward velocity). The core novelty here lies in using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent the entire skill set, with different skills conditioned on a latent categorical variable. To encourage the learned skills to be distinct and interpretable, the authors introduce an elegant information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as primitives by a high-level manager policy, which is trained on specific downstream tasks. The experiments, conducted on challenging continuous control benchmarks involving locomotion and navigation, demonstrate that this approach can solve tasks that were previously intractable for standard RL algorithms and significantly improves sample efficiency.

### Decision: Accept

I recommend accepting this paper. The proposed method presents a novel and effective combination of stochastic latent variable models and information theory for skill discovery in a hierarchical RL context. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating success on challenging benchmark tasks where prior methods fail. While the paper suffers from a lack of in-depth analysis and some overstated claims, the core technical contribution is significant and the positive results are compelling enough to warrant acceptance.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The primary strength of this paper is its effective and practical framework for learning and utilizing skills. By pre-training a repertoire of behaviors, the agent develops a powerful exploration strategy that drastically simplifies solving complex downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The experiments on the maze and gather environments are convincing and clearly demonstrate the method's superiority over non-hierarchical baselines. The use of a single SNN for learning multiple skills is an efficient and elegant approach that enables weight sharing, contrasting favorably with the less efficient method of training multiple separate policies. This contribution is similar in spirit to Heess et al. (2016) but is arguably more general, as it relies on a single proxy reward rather than a set of pre-defined goals.

However, the paper is weakened by a lack of rigorous experimental analysis and some misleading framing. These are the key areas of concern:

1.  **Misleading Use of "Intrinsic Motivation"**: The paper frames the proxy reward used during pre-training as a form of intrinsic motivation. This is inaccurate. Intrinsic motivation typically refers to task-agnostic objectives like curiosity, novelty, or prediction error that drive exploration in the absence of external rewards. Here, the proxy reward (e.g., speed for a locomotion agent) is highly aligned with the expected nature of the downstream tasks. This is better described as a curriculum or a pre-training phase with a useful auxiliary objective, a well-established technique. This framing mispositions the work relative to the true intrinsic motivation literature.

2.  **Weak Analysis of the MI Regularizer**: The mutual information regularizer is a key technical contribution, yet its impact is not fully analyzed. The results in Figure 6 show that for the more complex mazes (Maze 1, 2, 3), the MI bonus provides no benefit and may even slightly degrade performance. The paper acknowledges this observation but offers no interpretation or investigation as to why this occurs. Does the bonus encourage skills that are too complex or specialized for these mazes, where simple forward and turning motions suffice? A deeper analysis is needed to understand the trade-offs of this regularizer and provide guidance on its application.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis of Hierarchical Behavior**: The paper focuses on whether the tasks are solved but provides little insight into the behavior of the high-level manager policy. An analysis of the skill switching strategy would be highly valuable. For instance, visualizing which skills are selected in different parts of the state space, or analyzing common failure modes (e.g., getting stuck, oscillating between skills), would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution. The fixed switching interval `T` is a major hyperparameter, and while Appendix C.3 provides a sensitivity analysis, this critical aspect of the hierarchical design deserves more attention in the main text.

Despite these weaknesses, the core idea is sound and the empirical success is undeniable. The paper introduces a valuable method to the community, and the identified issues can be addressed in future work.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper.

*   **Reproducibility**: For the method to be reproducible, the factorization of the state space into `S_agent` and `S_rest` must be specified precisely for each environment. The paper states that `S_agent` covers the robot's dynamics, but it is unclear what constitutes `S_rest` in the maze and gather tasks. Is it just the LIDAR readings, or does it include other task-specific information? Please clarify this in the experimental setup.
*   **Variance in Pre-training**: Appendix C.4 reveals that performance on downstream tasks can vary significantly depending on the random seed used for pre-training the SNN. This suggests that the pre-training phase is not guaranteed to produce a consistently useful set of skills. This is a critical point that reduces the method's reliability and should be discussed in the main paper. Have you considered ways to make the skill discovery process more robust?
*   **Failure Modes**: Appendix D discusses failure modes for the unstable "Ant" agent, where switching between skills can lead to falling. This is an excellent piece of analysis that highlights a key challenge for this type of hierarchical approach. It would be beneficial to include this discussion in the main paper, as it points to important limitations and avenues for future work, such as learning a safe transition policy between skills.

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their response:
1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the `S_rest` state components for each of the downstream tasks?
2.  Do you have a hypothesis for why the MI regularizer fails to improve performance on Mazes 1-3? For example, does it prioritize diverse but less stable gaits over the simple, robust motions needed to navigate the corridors?
3.  Given the high performance variance tied to the pre-trained SNN, how should a practitioner approach using your method? Does one need to pre-train multiple SNNs and select the best one, and if so, how would "best" be evaluated without access to the downstream tasks?
