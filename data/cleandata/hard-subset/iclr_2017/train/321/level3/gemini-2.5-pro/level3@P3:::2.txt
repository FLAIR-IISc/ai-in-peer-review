### Review

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with long horizons and sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process. First, a diverse set of low-level skills (sub-policies) is learned in a pre-training phase using a generic, intrinsic-like "proxy reward". To efficiently learn a wide range of behaviors, the authors employ a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy, conditioned on a latent categorical variable. Diversity among the skills corresponding to different latent codes is explicitly encouraged via an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent code and the agent's state. In the second stage, these pre-trained, frozen skills are used as temporally extended actions for a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on specific downstream tasks. The experiments, conducted on challenging locomotion and navigation tasks, demonstrate that this hierarchical approach significantly outperforms learning from scratch and a strong baseline where the intrinsic reward is directly added to the sparse task reward. The paper presents a promising direction for skill discovery and includes clear visualizations that aid in understanding the learned behaviors.

**Decision: Accept**

My decision is to accept this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  The proposed framework is a well-motivated and elegant combination of stochastic policies, information-theoretic objectives, and hierarchical control for unsupervised skill discovery.
2.  The empirical results provide strong evidence for the utility of the approach. Crucially, the failure of a strong baseline (directly combining the intrinsic proxy reward with the sparse task reward) convincingly argues for the benefits of either the hierarchical abstraction or the diversity encouraged during pre-training.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The main strength of this paper is its clear demonstration of the value of pre-training a diverse set of skills for solving sparse reward tasks. The comparison in Section 7.3 is particularly compelling. The baseline, which provides the agent with the same proxy reward used for pre-training directly in the downstream task, performs poorly. This is a significant finding, as it suggests that simply providing a dense shaping reward is not enough to solve these long-horizon credit assignment problems. The success of the proposed hierarchical method highlights that the benefits stem from either the temporal abstraction provided by the options-like framework or the explicit diversity of behaviors learned during pre-training, both of which are key contributions of this work. The use of an SNN with an MI-based regularizer is an effective and sample-efficient method for learning these skills compared to training separate policies.

However, the paper has several limitations that temper its impact. First, the mechanisms for skill diversification—concatenating or bilinearly integrating a one-hot latent code—are architecturally shallow. This approach effectively modifies the biases or input weights of the first layer. While this appears sufficient for generating different gaits in locomotion tasks, it may not be expressive enough to produce the fundamentally distinct sub-policies required for more complex domains like robotic manipulation (e.g., reaching, grasping, pushing).

Second, the hierarchical structure itself is quite rigid. The manager policy is trained on top of *fixed* sub-policies and operates on a *fixed* time scale T. This imposes a heavy burden on the pre-training stage: it must discover a set of skills that is both correct and sufficient for all potential downstream tasks. If a crucial skill is missing or suboptimal, the manager has no mechanism to refine it. This reliance on a perfect pre-training phase makes the claim of using "minimal domain knowledge" less convincing, as the design of the proxy reward becomes critical to the success of the entire pipeline, making it feel closer to a supervised signal for behaviors.

**Additional Feedback:**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

-   The paper clearly shows the benefit of using pre-trained skills. However, it would be interesting to disentangle the benefits of the temporal hierarchy from the benefits of the learned skills themselves. An interesting ablation would be to use the learned SNN policy in a non-hierarchical way on the downstream task (e.g., by making the latent code part of the policy's action space).
-   The authors rightly point out that end-to-end training and learning a termination policy are important avenues for future work. Making the hierarchy more flexible would significantly increase the applicability of this framework.
-   The visualizations are excellent and significantly contribute to the paper's clarity.

**Questions for Authors:**

I have a few questions that would help clarify my understanding of the work:

1.  In Section 7.3, the baseline of adding the "CoM maze reward" performs very poorly. Could you elaborate on why you believe this is the case? Is it purely a credit assignment problem, or is there another factor? Also, for a fair comparison of sample complexity, does this baseline account for the number of environment steps used to pre-train the hierarchical policies?
2.  In the experiments (e.g., Figure 6), you compare against a "Multi-policy" architecture. My understanding is that this refers to a hierarchical agent whose sub-policies are K independently trained networks, as opposed to the single SNN that learns K skills simultaneously. Is this interpretation correct?
3.  Regarding Figure 1 and the architectural description in Section 5.2: Can you confirm my understanding that for the concatenation approach, the categorical latent code `z` effectively selects a different bias vector for the first hidden layer, while for the bilinear integration, it selects a different weight matrix for processing the observation `s`?
