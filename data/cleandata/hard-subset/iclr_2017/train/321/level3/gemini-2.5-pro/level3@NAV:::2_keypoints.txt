- Proposes a two-stage hierarchical RL framework: pre-train diverse low-level skills with a proxy reward, then train a high-level manager to select frozen skills for downstream tasks.
- Core technical contribution: use of stochastic neural network (SNN) policies with an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage diverse, interpretable skills.
- Empirical results show significant improvements in sample efficiency and final performance on challenging locomotion benchmarks.
- Ablation studies on latent integration (concatenation vs bilinear) and the mutual information bonus are thorough and convincingly demonstrate each component’s utility.
- Skill visualizations are clear and effectively demonstrate the learned, interpretable behaviors.
- Reviewer recommendation: Accept; score raised from 6 to 7 based on strong empirical evidence for hierarchical abstraction.
- Limitation 1: The diversification mechanism (modulating the first layer) may only produce variations of a base behavior and could be insufficient for tasks needing distinctly different sub-policies (e.g., manipulation).
- Limitation 2: The hierarchy is rigid—manager uses fixed, frozen sub-policies and a fixed timescale—placing heavy reliance on pre-training and making the proxy reward seem domain-specific.
- Suggested improvement: explore relaxing the rigid hierarchy, e.g., fine-tuning sub-policies during downstream training or learning skill termination conditions.
- Question 1: Why does the strong baseline in Sec 7.3 ("CoM maze reward") perform poorly — is it trained from scratch or does it account for the extra pre-training experience hierarchical methods receive?
- Question 2: Confirm interpretation of Figure 1 — concatenation modulates first-layer bias, while bilinear integration modulates the entire input weight matrix?
- Question 3: Please clarify the experimental distinction between the "multi-policy" and "snn" architectures.
