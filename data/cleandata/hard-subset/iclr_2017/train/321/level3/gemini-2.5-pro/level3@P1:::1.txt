### Review

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve complex continuous control tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a "pre-training" environment with a simple, generic proxy reward (e.g., forward velocity). This stage uses a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent a multi-modal policy, where different latent codes correspond to different skills. To encourage diversity and interpretability among these skills, the authors introduce an elegant information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent code and the agent's state. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as a temporally extended action space for a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on specific downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The experiments, conducted on challenging locomotion and navigation benchmarks, demonstrate that this hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and enables the agent to solve tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a complete and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that successfully solves challenging continuous control benchmarks with sparse rewards, a significant problem in the field. The empirical results are strong and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed skill pre-training and hierarchical structure.
2.  The method for discovering a diverse set of skills using Stochastic Neural Networks combined with a mutual information bonus is a novel and technically interesting contribution. This approach provides an efficient way to learn multiple distinct behaviors within a single policy network, substantially reducing the sample complexity compared to training skills independently.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its strong empirical validation on difficult benchmark tasks from Duan et al. (2016). Where prior methods employing naive exploration fail completely, the proposed hierarchical framework learns successful policies. The qualitative results, such as the visitation plots in Figure 4, provide compelling evidence that the SNN architecture, particularly with bilinear integration and the mutual information (MI) bonus, learns a rich and interpretable span of skills. The subsequent exploration plots in Figure 5 clearly show how this skill set, when used hierarchically, leads to far superior coverage of the state space compared to random exploration.

However, the paper's analysis has a few weaknesses that temper the excellent results. A key technical contribution, the MI regularizer, does not consistently improve performance on all tasks. The authors note that it does not boost performance on the more complex mazes (Mazes 1-3), yet they provide no hypothesis or analysis for this outcome. This omission is significant because it leaves the reader questioning when and why this regularizer is beneficial. A deeper investigation into this phenomenon would strengthen the paper's scientific contribution by providing a better understanding of the method's limitations and applicability.

Furthermore, the paper's framing of the proxy reward as a form of "intrinsic motivation" is somewhat misleading. Intrinsic motivation typically refers to task-agnostic curiosity or novelty-driven signals. The proxy rewards used here (e.g., speed) are directly aligned with the general class of downstream locomotion tasks. This is better described as a form of curriculum learning or pre-training with a generic, task-family-specific objective, similar to the approach in Heess et al. (2016). While this is a semantic point and does not diminish the method's effectiveness, more precise positioning within the literature would be beneficial.

Despite these weaknesses in the analysis and framing, the overall framework is well-motivated, the components are thoughtfully designed, and the results are impressive. The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   **Analysis of High-Level Policy:** The paper focuses heavily on the pre-training stage but offers little analysis of the learned high-level manager policy. It would be highly insightful to see an analysis of the sequences of skills the manager learns to employ to solve a task like a maze. Does it learn predictable patterns (e.g., "move forward," "turn left," "move forward")? This would provide a more complete picture of the hierarchical control system.
*   **Sensitivity to Switch Time `T`:** The use of a fixed switch time `T` is a major simplification. The appendix provides a brief analysis for the "Snake" robot, but a similar analysis for the main "Swimmer" experiments would be valuable. Understanding the sensitivity of the performance to this hyperparameter is important for practitioners.
*   **Reproducibility Details:** The paper mentions the factorization of the state space into `S_agent` and `S_rest`. For full reproducibility, it would be helpful to explicitly define the components of `S_rest` for each downstream task (e.g., the exact nature of the LIDAR-like sensor readings).
*   **Failure Modes:** The appendix notes failure modes for the "Ant" robot related to instability when switching skills. Including a brief discussion of this limitation in the main body (perhaps in the Discussion section) would provide a more balanced view of the method's robustness and highlight an important avenue for future work.

**5. Questions for Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
1.  Could you offer a hypothesis for why the mutual information regularizer provides a benefit on Maze 0 and the Gather task, but not on the structurally similar Mazes 1, 2, and 3? Does the "bumping against a wall" strategy you mention make the fine-grained turning skills (encouraged by the MI bonus) redundant in those specific maze layouts?
2.  For the maze and gather tasks, could you please specify the exact composition of the task-specific state component `S_rest` that is provided as input to the high-level manager network?
3.  The appendix shows that the switch time `T` does not critically affect performance for the Snake robot on static tasks. Did you observe similar robustness for the Swimmer robot in the main paper's experiments?
