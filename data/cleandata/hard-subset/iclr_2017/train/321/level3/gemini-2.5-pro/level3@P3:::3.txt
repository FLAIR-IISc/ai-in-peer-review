### **Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"**

**1. Summary**

This paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is to first pre-train a diverse set of low-level skills in an unsupervised manner, and then train a high-level manager policy to select among these skills to solve downstream tasks. The key contributions include: (1) The use of a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), conditioned on a discrete latent variable, to learn a multi-modal policy representing multiple skills within a single network, which improves sample efficiency. (2) The introduction of an information-theoretic regularizer, based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state, to explicitly encourage the learned skills to be distinct and diverse. (3) Demonstrating that this hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and enables solving challenging continuous control tasks with sparse rewards, such as navigating mazes, where standard RL algorithms fail.

**2. Decision: Reject**

While the problem setting is well-motivated and the idea of using information-theoretic objectives to learn diverse skills is compelling, I am recommending rejection for two key reasons:

1.  **The claims of generality and minimal domain knowledge are not sufficiently supported.** The proposed intrinsic rewards appear hand-crafted for locomotion, and the method's effectiveness is not convincingly demonstrated on morphologies or tasks beyond simple planar movement.
2.  **Critical methodological details raise concerns about the approach's scalability and robustness.** The reliance on a 2D spatial grid for the mutual information bonus limits its obvious application to more complex state spaces, and the potential for the method to fail as behavioral richness increases is not adequately addressed.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on the following concerns:

*   **Hand-Design of Intrinsic Rewards:** The authors claim that designing the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge." I disagree with this assessment. The suggested reward for a mobile robot—proportional to its speed—is a highly specific and effective choice for locomotion tasks but represents significant prior knowledge about what constitutes useful behavior. Similarly, the suggestion for a manipulator—"successful grasping of any object"—is not a simple proxy but a complex sub-task that may require its own shaped reward. The success of the pre-training phase seems critically dependent on a well-chosen, domain-specific proxy reward, which undermines the claim of being a general, minimally-supervised method.

*   **Limited Generality and Scalability:** The experiments are conducted on the 2-link Swimmer and 5-link Snake robots, both of which are relatively simple, planar agents. While the extension to the Snake is a positive step, it is not sufficient to demonstrate broad applicability. The appendix reveals that the method struggles with the more complex Ant robot due to instabilities when switching skills. This is a crucial finding that suggests a potential failure mode for agents with more complex dynamics and should be a central part of the discussion, not relegated to the appendix. Without evidence of success on more challenging morphologies or different task families (e.g., manipulation), the claim of a "general framework" is premature.

*   **Concerns about the Discretization for Mutual Information:** The method for calculating the mutual information bonus, a key component for ensuring skill diversity, relies on partitioning the (x, y) coordinate space into a grid (Sec. 5.3). This approach is practical for 2D navigation but does not scale to tasks where the relevant state information is higher-dimensional (e.g., full joint angles, object configurations). The paper mentions that an MLP could be used to estimate the posterior as an alternative, but provides no details or experiments to validate this. As presented, this discretization is a significant limitation that may confine the method's utility to a narrow class of problems.

*   **Potential Failure Modes with Richer Behaviors:** The framework learns a fixed number (K) of skills. While this works for the presented tasks, it is unclear how the method would cope with environments where a much richer and more continuous spectrum of behaviors is required. As the complexity of an agent's morphology and environment increases, the agent's potential behaviors grow exponentially. The current approach may be limited to discovering only a few distinct modes, potentially missing the nuance and diversity needed for more complex downstream tasks. The paper does not explore the sensitivity to the hyperparameter K or discuss how the diversity of skills scales with the agent's capabilities.

**4. Additional Feedback**

Despite my criticisms, I believe the core ideas in this paper are valuable. The following suggestions are offered to help improve the work:

*   I strongly encourage moving the discussion of the Ant robot's failure modes from the appendix into the main body of the paper. A frank discussion of limitations, such as instability during skill switching, is scientifically valuable and would provide a more complete picture of the method's capabilities.
*   The claim regarding "minimal domain knowledge" should be softened. It would be more accurate and useful to reframe this as a discussion on the principles for designing effective proxy rewards for unsupervised skill discovery.
*   The paper would be significantly strengthened by including experiments on a task that is not purely about locomotion (e.g., a simple manipulation task) to better substantiate the claims of generality.
*   The section on the information-theoretic regularizer could be improved by providing more implementation details and analysis of the proposed alternative for high-dimensional states (i.e., using an MLP to estimate the posterior). An ablation study on the grid resolution would also be informative.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  **Discretization:** In Section 5.3, you use a grid to partition the (x,y) coordinate space for the MI bonus. How would this approach scale to higher-dimensional state spaces where gridding is intractable? Have you experimented with the proposed MLP-based posterior estimator, and if so, how does it perform?
2.  **Generality and Embodiment:** The appendix notes that the method fails on the Ant robot due to instability when switching skills. Does this point to a fundamental limitation of freezing low-level skills during downstream training? How do you see this framework scaling to even more complex morphologies or non-locomotion tasks?
3.  **Proxy Reward Design:** Could you provide a concrete example of how you would design a "minimal domain knowledge" proxy reward for a task fundamentally different from locomotion, such as a manipulator learning to stack blocks, where rewarding "any successful grasp" is itself a non-trivial, sparse-reward problem?
4.  **Behavioral Richness:** How does the quality and diversity of the learned skills degrade as the number of desired skills, K, increases? Is there a risk that for agents capable of a very wide range of behaviors, the method will fail to discover a sufficiently rich skill set?
