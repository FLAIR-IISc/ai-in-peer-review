### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process: 1) a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills (low-level policies) are learned using a generic, easy-to-specify proxy reward, and 2) a downstream training phase where a high-level "manager" policy learns to select from these pre-trained skills to solve a specific task. To learn a diverse set of skills efficiently, the authors propose using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) where a latent variable conditions the policy's behavior. To ensure the skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, they introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state. The experiments, conducted on challenging continuous control locomotion tasks, demonstrate that this approach learns interpretable skills and significantly outperforms standard RL algorithms and a strong baseline on sparse-reward maze and gathering tasks.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept (Score: 7)

This is a well-executed paper that presents a novel and effective method for skill discovery and hierarchical control. The primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Strong Empirical Validation of Hierarchical Abstraction:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed hierarchical structure is crucial for solving the challenging sparse-reward tasks. The baseline of simply adding the pre-training proxy reward to the sparse downstream task reward performs poorly, indicating that the benefit comes not just from reward shaping, but from the temporal abstraction and structured exploration provided by the learned skills.
2.  **Novel and Effective Skill Diversification:** The combination of a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with a mutual information-based regularizer is an elegant and principled approach to learning a diverse set of skills in a sample-efficient manner. The visualizations clearly show that this method produces a richer and more varied set of behaviors compared to training multiple policies independently or using a simpler SNN architecture.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its clear demonstration of the value of hierarchical learning. The experiments in Section 7.3 are particularly convincing. The "CoM maze reward" baseline is a strong one, as it provides a dense, seemingly helpful reward signal to a standard flat policy. Its failure highlights the credit assignment problem in long-horizon tasks and shows that simply having a good reward is not enough. In contrast, the hierarchical methods succeed by effectively reducing the horizon for the high-level policy and providing it with temporally extended, meaningful actions. This is a significant finding.

The method for learning the skills is also a key contribution. Using a single SNN is much more sample-efficient than training multiple policies from scratch. The authors identify a potential failure mode where the SNN might learn redundant skills and propose a well-motivated solution: an MI bonus that encourages the agent's state to be predictable from the latent skill variable. The ablation studies in Figure 4 effectively show that both the bilinear integration architecture and the MI bonus are important for generating a rich span of skills. The qualitative results (visitation plots) are excellent and provide strong intuitive support for the method's effectiveness. The work is well-placed in the literature, correctly identifying the challenges of prior HRL methods (requiring hand-engineered skills) and intrinsic motivation methods (not explicitly designed for skill transfer).

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Limited Expressiveness of Skill Conditioning:** The proposed methods for conditioning the policy on the latent code `z` (concatenation and bilinear integration) effectively modify the first layer's biases or weights. While this works very well for locomotion, where skills are variations of a base gait, it might be a limitation for tasks requiring more fundamentally different skills (e.g., manipulation tasks involving reaching, grasping, and pushing). It would be valuable to discuss this limitation and potential future work using more expressive conditioning mechanisms (e.g., FiLM layers or routing mechanisms).
*   **Rigidity of the Hierarchy:** The current framework relies on sub-policies that are frozen after pre-training and a fixed time scale `T` for skill execution. This places a very high burden on the pre-training phase; the proxy reward must be good enough to generate all skills necessary for any potential downstream task. This makes the "unsupervised" pre-training phase feel more supervised in practice, as the quality of the proxy reward is paramount. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the sensitivity to this proxy reward and by exploring possibilities for more flexible hierarchies, such as end-to-end fine-tuning of the skills or learning a termination policy for the options, as the authors briefly mention in the conclusion.

### Questions for Authors

To clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Regarding the baseline in Section 7.3 ("CoM maze reward"): Why does this strong baseline perform so poorly? The text suggests it's due to the credit assignment problem, but given the dense reward, this is surprising. Could you confirm that this baseline is trained from scratch on the downstream task and does not benefit from any pre-training experience? If so, does it receive a comparable number of environment interactions as the hierarchical methods (including their pre-training phase)?
2.  In Figure 6 and throughout the results, you compare "Multi-policy" and "SNN" architectures. To confirm, does "Multi-policy" refer to the baseline approach of training K independent policies from scratch (as shown in Fig 4a-b) and then learning a manager on top of them?
3.  Regarding the SNN architecture in Section 5.2 and Figure 1: Could you confirm if my interpretation is correct? Concatenation integration with a one-hot `z` is equivalent to having a different bias vector for the first hidden layer for each skill, while bilinear integration is equivalent to having a different input-to-hidden weight matrix for each skill.

Overall, this is a promising paper that pushes forward the state of the art in hierarchical reinforcement learning. The approach is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive.
