- Proposes a two-stage hierarchical RL framework: pre-train diverse low-level skills with a proxy reward using a single SNN conditioned on a categorical latent, then train a high-level manager over frozen skills.  
- Encourages skill diversity via an information-theoretic mutual information regularizer between latent code and agent state.  
- Experiments on locomotion and navigation show the hierarchical approach substantially outperforms learning from scratch and a strong dense-reward baseline.  
- Decision: Accept — framework is well-motivated and empirically strong.  
- Key strength: clear demonstration that pre-trained diverse skills aid sparse long-horizon tasks; MI-regularized SNN is sample-efficient compared to separate policies.  
- Important empirical finding: directly adding the proxy reward to the task fails, suggesting hierarchical abstraction or explicit skill diversity is crucial.  
- Limitation: latent-code integration (concatenation/bilinear) is architecturally shallow and may not produce fundamentally distinct skills for complex domains like manipulation.  
- Limitation: rigid hierarchy — frozen sub-policies and fixed time scale T prevent adaptation if pre-training misses necessary skills, increasing reliance on proxy reward design.  
- Concern: claim of “minimal domain knowledge” is weakened because proxy reward design becomes critical and close to supervised behavior specification.  
- Suggested ablation: test the learned SNN non-hierarchically (e.g., make latent code part of action space) to separate benefits of temporal hierarchy vs. learned skills.  
- Suggested future work: enable end-to-end training, learn termination policies, and make the hierarchy more flexible for broader applicability.  
- Positive note: visualizations are excellent and aid clarity.  
- Question 1: Why does the CoM maze baseline perform poorly — pure credit assignment or other factors, and does that baseline account for pre-training environment steps?  
- Question 2: Clarify that “Multi-policy” means K independently trained networks vs. a single SNN learning K skills simultaneously.  
- Question 3: Confirm that concatenation effectively selects different first-layer biases while bilinear integration selects different first-layer weight matrices.
