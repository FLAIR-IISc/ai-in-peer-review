This paper addresses the challenge of reinforcement learning in tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons by proposing a hierarchical framework. The core contribution is a two-stage process: first, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a pre-training phase using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) regularized by a mutual information (MI) bonus. This phase requires only a simple, generic proxy reward (e.g., forward velocity). Second, a high-level manager policy is trained on top of these frozen skills to solve complex downstream tasks. The authors demonstrate that this approach is sample-efficient and capable of solving challenging continuous control benchmarks, such as complex mazes, that were previously intractable for standard RL algorithms.

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The primary reason is that the proposed method achieves strong empirical results on difficult, established benchmarks, demonstrating a clear performance improvement over prior work. However, this recommendation is tempered by the fact that the experimental analysis does not match the strength of the results, leaving some of the core contributions and behaviors of the system underexplored.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's main strength lies in its results. The hierarchical framework successfully enables agents to solve sparse-reward locomotion and maze tasks where previous methods failed. The method of using an SNN with a shared architecture to pre-train a diverse skill set is an elegant and sample-efficient contribution, comparing favorably to training multiple policies independently.

However, the paper is weakened by a lack of deep analysis. First, the term "intrinsic motivation" is used loosely. The proxy rewards for pre-training (e.g., speed) are well-aligned with the downstream locomotion tasks, making this approach more akin to curriculum learning or task-aligned pre-training, rather than a general, task-agnostic intrinsic motivation. Second, a key component, the mutual information regularizer, is shown to not improve performance on several of the maze tasks. The paper notes this but fails to provide a compelling analysis as to why this elegant mechanism for encouraging diversity does not translate to better downstream performance. This is a significant missed opportunity for insight. Finally, the analysis of the emergent hierarchical behavior is limited; there is little investigation into the manager's switching strategy, its failure modes, or how the timing of skill switches impacts performance.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   For reproducibility, the factorization of the state space into S_agent and S_rest should be explicitly defined for each experimental domain.
*   The paper would benefit greatly from a qualitative analysis of the learned high-level policies. Visualizing trajectories and correlating them with the manager’s skill selections, especially in failure cases, would provide valuable insight into the system's dynamics.

**Questions for Authors:**
*   Could you provide more intuition or analysis on why the mutual information bonus, which successfully diversifies skills, fails to translate to better performance on Mazes 1-3? Does it create skills that are too specialized for the manager to sequence effectively?
*   Can you elaborate on the manager's learned switching behavior? For instance, does it learn to use specific skills only in certain parts of the state space, such as using a "turning" skill near corners?
