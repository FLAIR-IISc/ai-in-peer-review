This paper presents a compelling framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage approach: first, a pre-training phase where a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), regularized by a mutual information bonus, learns a diverse set of skills guided by a simple proxy reward. Second, a high-level policy is trained to select from these pre-trained skills to solve complex downstream tasks. The authors demonstrate that this method can discover interpretable skills and significantly improve performance on challenging locomotion and navigation benchmarks. The setting is well-motivated and the idea of using information-theoretic objectives to explicitly encourage skill diversity is appealing.

**Decision: Reject**

My primary reasons for this decision are: (1) The paper’s central claim of requiring "minimal domain knowledge" is overstated, as the intrinsic reward mechanisms appear carefully hand-designed for the tested domains. (2) The generality of the approach is questionable, with concerns about its scalability to more complex embodiments and its reliance on a potentially brittle discretization of the state space.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The claim of using "minimal domain knowledge" is a key selling point, but it does not seem fully supported. The proxy reward, such as maximizing the agent's speed, is a well-established and highly effective choice specifically for locomotion tasks. Similarly, the mutual information bonus requires hand-selecting a "relevant subset of the state" (e.g., the 2D center of mass coordinates), which is a non-trivial design choice that tailors the objective to a specific task family.

Furthermore, the methodology has potential limitations in its generality. Section 5.3 describes discretizing the coordinate space into a grid to estimate the mutual information bonus. This approach seems unlikely to scale to higher-dimensional state representations or tasks where the relevant state variables are not easily identified a priori. The paper demonstrates results on a 5-link "Snake" in addition to the 2-link "Swimmer," but the reported failure modes for the more complex "Ant" agent suggest that the framework may not be robust as behavioral richness and agent instability increase, limiting the diversity of skills that can be effectively utilized.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision.
1. The discretization for the MI bonus is a major concern. Exploring alternative, continuous estimators for the posterior p(z|s) (e.g., using a neural network) could make the method more general and robust.
2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the failure modes. Why exactly does switching between skills cause instability for the Ant? Is it an issue with state distribution mismatch, and could this be mitigated by fine-tuning skills or learning a dedicated transition policy?

To better assess the work, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
*   Could you elaborate on the choice to discretize the state space in Section 5.3? What are the limitations of this grid-based approach, and how would it perform in a continuous setting?
*   How does the method scale as you increase the desired number of skills (K)? Is there a point where the SNN fails to learn distinct behaviors, leading to mode collapse and limiting skill diversity?
