### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve complex tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is a two-phase approach. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of low-level skills using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy. This learning is guided by a simple, generic "proxy reward" (e.g., forward velocity) and an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between a latent skill variable and the agent's state. This regularizer is designed to encourage the emergence of distinct and interpretable behaviors. Second, for downstream tasks, a high-level manager policy is trained on top of the frozen, pre-trained skills, treating them as macro-actions. The paper claims this approach significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency, enabling agents to solve challenging continuous control tasks like navigation in mazes, which are intractable for standard RL algorithms. Key technical contributions include the use of SNNs with bilinear integration for multi-modal skill representation and the MI bonus for explicitly encouraging skill diversity.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **The central claim of requiring "minimal domain knowledge" is not well-supported.** The proxy rewards and the specific state variables used for the mutual information bonus appear to be carefully hand-designed for the locomotion tasks presented, which constitutes a significant form of domain-specific engineering.
2.  **The generality and scalability of the proposed method are questionable.** The experiments are confined to locomotion-style tasks with relatively simple morphologies, and the paper's appendix reveals critical failure modes for more complex, unstable agents, suggesting the approach may not be robust as behavior richness increases.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's motivation is excellent, and the proposed framework is elegant. However, the claims of generality and minimal supervision seem overstated based on the presented evidence.

The proxy reward used in pre-training is described as generic, yet rewarding the "magnitude of the speed of the robot" is a very specific choice that is highly effective for locomotion but may not have a clear or useful equivalent for other problem domains, such as manipulation. Similarly, the MI bonus is calculated between the latent skill and the (x, y) coordinates of the agent's center of mass (CoM). This choice explicitly embeds the prior that spatial coverage is the desired form of diversity. While effective for navigation, it is not obvious how to select the appropriate state variables for tasks where diversity is not defined by spatial location. This reliance on hand-crafting the reward and the MI objective function contradicts the claim of requiring only minimal domain knowledge and brings the method closer to a form of reward shaping.

Furthermore, the experiments primarily involve agents where the main challenge is locomotion in a 2D plane (Swimmer, Snake). The appendix (Sec D.2) reports that the method struggles with the more complex and unstable "Ant" robot, where switching between skills can cause the agent to fall and terminate the episode. This is a significant limitation, as it suggests the framework may not be robust enough for agents with more complex dynamics or for tasks requiring a richer set of behaviors beyond simple locomotion. The fixed-duration execution of skills without a mechanism to handle state transitions between them appears to be a critical flaw for more sensitive agents. This calls into question the general applicability of the framework.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision. I would appreciate clarification on these issues.

*   **State Space Discretization:** In Section 5.3, you state that you "partition the (x, y) coordinate space into cells" to estimate the posterior for the MI bonus. This detail is critical. Could you clarify how this discretization is performed? Is it a uniform grid, and how sensitive is the method to the grid resolution? For high-dimensional or continuous state spaces where gridding is infeasible, you mention that an MLP regressor could be used, but this is not explored. This seems like a major limitation for applying the method beyond simple 2D navigation tasks.

*   **Generality and Embodiment:** The experiments progress from a 3-link Swimmer to a 5-link Snake. How does the method scale to even more complex morphologies? Does the effectiveness of the proxy reward and MI bonus diminish as the state-action space and potential for diverse behaviors grow? The failure case with the Ant suggests that embodiment is a critical factor. It would strengthen the paper to analyze this relationship more deeply.

*   **On the Richness of Discovered Skills:** The experiments show the discovery of skills corresponding to movement in different directions. Is the framework capable of discovering qualitatively different types of skills within the same pre-training setup? For instance, could it learn both locomotion and object interaction skills if the environment supported it? My concern is that the current formulation of the proxy reward and MI bonus may heavily bias skill discovery towards a single class of behaviors (e.g., locomotion), limiting the diversity of the learned skill set.

Addressing these points, particularly by demonstrating the method in a non-locomotion domain or providing a more principled way to design the proxy reward and select MI variables, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of generality.
