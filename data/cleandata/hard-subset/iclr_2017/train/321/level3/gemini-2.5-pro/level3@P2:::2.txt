### **Summary**

This paper proposes a general and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to tackle challenging tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is a two-stage process. First, in a "pre-training" phase, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a simple environment using a generic proxy reward (e.g., rewarding forward velocity for a locomotion agent) that requires minimal domain knowledge. To learn these skills in a sample-efficient manner, the authors employ a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) where a latent categorical variable conditions the policy, allowing for weight sharing across different skills. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state is introduced. In the second stage, for a specific downstream task, a high-level "manager" policy is trained on top of the frozen, pre-trained skills. This manager selects which skill to execute for a fixed period, effectively operating in a temporally-abstracted action space, which significantly improves exploration. The authors provide strong empirical results on challenging continuous control benchmarks, demonstrating that their method can learn a wide span of interpretable skills and solve tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant framework for HRL that effectively combines unsupervised skill discovery with hierarchical control. The proposed method directly addresses the critical challenge of exploration in sparse-reward environments, and the two-stage training paradigm is a practical way to acquire reusable skills.
2.  The empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed hierarchical abstraction. A particularly compelling finding is the failure of a strong baseline that directly combines the proxy (intrinsic) reward with the sparse task reward. This shows that the benefit comes not just from the reward signal itself, but from learning a diverse set of temporally extended skills that enable structured exploration.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary contribution is a novel and practical synthesis of existing ideas—HRL, intrinsic motivation, and information-theoretic objectives—into a coherent and high-performing framework. The motivation is clear: standard RL methods fail on sparse, long-horizon tasks due to inefficient exploration. This work tackles this by first learning a reusable skill repertoire.

The use of an SNN to learn multiple skills within a single network is a sample-efficient alternative to training separate policies. The key technical contribution is the information-theoretic regularizer, inspired by InfoGAN, which explicitly encourages the skills corresponding to different latent codes to be distinguishable. The paper includes a valuable ablation study on the SNN architecture, showing that a bilinear integration of the latent code and observation is more effective at producing diverse skills than simple concatenation. The visualizations provided (e.g., the visitation plots in Fig. 4) are excellent and clearly illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed components in generating a diverse and interpretable set of locomotion gaits.

The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The hierarchical agent demonstrates significantly improved exploration (Fig. 5) and achieves strong performance on difficult maze and gathering tasks where baseline methods fail (Fig. 6). The comparison against the "proxy reward as intrinsic motivation" baseline is crucial; its poor performance highlights that the hierarchical structure and temporal abstraction are essential for solving these long-horizon credit assignment problems. The authors are also transparent about the method's limitations, such as failure modes on unstable agents like the "Ant," which strengthens the paper.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision. The work is a promising step in a valuable direction, and I believe addressing these points could make it even stronger.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Limited Skill Diversity:** The mechanism for creating skills—modifying the first layer's weights/biases via the latent code—seems well-suited for locomotion, where skills are often variations of a central gait. However, this architectural prior might be too restrictive for domains requiring more fundamentally distinct skills (e.g., a manipulation task needing separate "reach," "grasp," and "lift" skills). It would be beneficial to discuss this limitation and speculate on architectures that could support greater diversity.
*   **Rigidity of the Hierarchy:** The current framework freezes the low-level skills after pre-training and uses a fixed time duration `T` for executing them. This makes the system heavily dependent on the quality of the pre-trained skills and the choice of `T`. As noted in the future work section, allowing for end-to-end fine-tuning of the skills or learning a termination condition for the options would significantly increase the framework's flexibility and power.
*   **Generality of the Proxy Reward:** The paper claims the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," which is true for the locomotion examples where "move fast" is a generic and useful objective. However, for other domains, designing a proxy reward that elicits a sufficiently rich set of skills could be as challenging as shaping a reward for the final task. A more nuanced discussion of the role and design of this reward would be valuable.

**Clarifying Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Baseline Performance:** The baseline where the proxy "CoM" reward is added directly to the sparse maze reward performs surprisingly poorly (Fig. 6). Could you clarify how this baseline was trained? Was it trained from scratch on the mixed-reward task, or did it also benefit from a pre-training phase like the hierarchical models? Understanding why this seemingly strong baseline fails is key to appreciating the contribution of the hierarchical structure.
2.  **"Multi-policy" Architecture:** To confirm my understanding, does the "Multi-policy" baseline involve training K separate, independent policies on the pre-training task, which are then used as the skill set for a manager network in the downstream task?
3.  **SNN Architecture Details:** Could you confirm that in your SNN implementation, the "concatenation" integration (Fig. 1a) effectively results in the latent code selecting a different bias vector for the first hidden layer, while the "bilinear" integration (Fig. 1b) results in it selecting a different input-to-hidden weight matrix?
