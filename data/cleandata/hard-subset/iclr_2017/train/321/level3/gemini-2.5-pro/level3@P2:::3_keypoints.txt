- Proposes a hierarchical RL framework: pre-train diverse low-level skills via an SNN with a proxy reward and MI regularizer, then train a high-level manager on frozen skills for downstream tasks.  
- Uses bilinear integration in SNNs for multi-modal skill representation and an MI bonus to encourage distinct, interpretable behaviors.  
- Claims improved exploration and sample efficiency enabling solutions to long-horizon, sparse-reward continuous control tasks (e.g., maze navigation).  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main critique: The claim of requiring “minimal domain knowledge” is unsupported because proxy rewards and MI state variables are hand-designed for locomotion.  
- Proxy reward (forward speed) is domain-specific and likely ineffective outside locomotion (e.g., manipulation).  
- MI computed with the agent’s (x,y) CoM embeds a spatial-coverage prior, not a general diversity objective for other domains.  
- Experiments are limited to locomotion-style tasks with simple morphologies, raising questions about generality and scalability.  
- Appendix shows failure on the more complex/unstable Ant agent, indicating fragility as behavior richness increases.  
- Fixed-duration skill execution and lack of transition handling cause instability (e.g., falling) in sensitive agents.  
- Unclear how the (x,y) discretization for MI posterior is performed, its sensitivity to grid resolution, and how to extend to high-dimensional continuous states.  
- Uncertainty whether method scales to more complex embodiments or larger state-action spaces; effectiveness of proxy reward and MI may diminish with complexity.  
- Doubt that the framework can discover qualitatively different skill types (e.g., interaction vs. locomotion) given current proxy reward/MI design.  
- Recommendation to strengthen the paper: demonstrate in non-locomotion domains or provide a principled method for choosing proxy rewards and MI variables to support generality claims.
