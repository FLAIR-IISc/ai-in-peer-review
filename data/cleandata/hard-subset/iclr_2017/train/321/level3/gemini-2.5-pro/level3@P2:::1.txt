### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve complex continuous control tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of low-level skills is learned efficiently, followed by a second phase where a high-level "manager" policy is trained to select among these skills to solve specific downstream tasks.

The skill acquisition phase is particularly novel. Instead of training multiple policies independently, the authors use a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent a multi-modal policy, where different latent codes correspond to different skills. This allows for efficient weight sharing and reduces the sample complexity of learning K skills by a factor of K. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the authors introduce an elegant information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent code and the agent's state. This pre-training is guided by a simple, generic "proxy reward" (e.g., forward speed), requiring minimal domain knowledge. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating that this approach can solve challenging benchmark tasks (locomotion in mazes, object gathering) that prior methods based on naive exploration could not.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Effective Framework with Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed hierarchical framework is well-designed and demonstrates impressive performance on a set of challenging continuous control tasks with sparse rewards. By successfully solving benchmarks where previous methods failed, the paper makes a significant empirical contribution to the field.
2.  **Novel and Efficient Skill Discovery Method:** The use of Stochastic Neural Networks combined with a mutual information regularizer is a novel and compelling method for learning a diverse set of reusable skills in an unsupervised or minimally supervised pre-training phase. This approach is more sample-efficient than training skills independently and provides an effective mechanism for structured exploration.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper lies in its convincing empirical demonstration. The hierarchical approach dramatically improves exploration, as shown qualitatively in the visitation plots (Fig. 5), and this translates directly to successful policies on sparse-reward tasks where standard RL algorithms fail (Fig. 6). The comparison against a strong baseline (adding the proxy reward directly to the downstream task) effectively highlights the benefit of the hierarchical structure in addressing long-horizon credit assignment problems.

The methodology for skill learning is also a key strength. The combination of SNNs with a bilinear integration and the MI bonus is shown to produce a rich and interpretable set of skills (Fig. 4). The MI regularizer, inspired by InfoGAN, is a well-motivated way to encourage diversity and prevent mode collapse, and its positive effect is clearly shown in the Gather task.

However, the paper's analysis has some weaknesses that temper its impact.

1.  **Misleading Framing of "Intrinsic Motivation":** The paper frames the pre-training phase as being guided by intrinsic motivation. However, the proxy rewards used (e.g., speed for a locomotion agent) are highly aligned with the general capabilities needed for the downstream tasks. This feels less like task-agnostic intrinsic curiosity and more like a well-chosen pre-training objective, similar in spirit to the approach of Heess et al. (2016), who also pre-train skills for downstream tasks. A more precise framing would strengthen the paper's positioning.

2.  **Incomplete Analysis of the MI Bonus:** The MI bonus is a key methodological contribution, yet its empirical benefit is inconsistent. It provides a clear advantage on the Gather task but offers no significant improvement on the more complex mazes (Mazes 1-3). The paper acknowledges this but provides no interpretation or analysis as to why this might be the case. This lack of analysis weakens the claim about the general utility of the MI bonus. For example, it's possible that the turning skills encouraged by the MI bonus are not critical for the mazes, where an agent can reorient itself by simply colliding with walls.

3.  **Lack of Analysis on Key Hierarchical Components:** The behavior of the hierarchical system, particularly the skill-switching mechanism, is under-analyzed. The switch time `T` is a critical hyperparameter, yet its analysis is relegated to the appendix and the conclusion that its impact is "not critical" for static tasks feels premature. Furthermore, the failure modes for more unstable agents like the Ant (also in the appendix) are a crucial part of understanding the limitations of this fixed-time switching approach and deserve more prominent discussion in the main paper.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the decision.

*   **Reproducibility:** The paper mentions the state space is factored into `S_agent` and `S_rest`, following Konidaris & Barto (2007). While `S_agent` is defined by the robot's state in the benchmark (Duan et al., 2016), the exact components of `S_rest` for each downstream task (e.g., LIDAR readings, object positions) should be explicitly detailed for clarity and reproducibility.
*   **Ablation Study:** The impact of the bilinear integration versus simple concatenation is a very interesting architectural detail. A clearer ablation study separating the effects of the integration method from the effects of the MI bonus would strengthen the claims about the importance of each component.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide more intuition or analysis as to why the mutual information bonus significantly improves performance on the Gather task but not on Mazes 1, 2, and 3? Does this suggest a limitation where the diversity encouraged by the MI bonus is only helpful for certain types of navigation challenges?
2.  The term "proxy reward" seems very similar to a pre-training objective. Could you further clarify how your approach differs from the skill pre-training in Heess et al. (2016), and could you elaborate on why you frame this as a form of "intrinsic motivation"?
3.  The analysis of the switching time `T` in the appendix suggests performance is not highly sensitive to this parameter. This is somewhat surprising. Could you elaborate on this finding? Do you expect this insensitivity to hold for more dynamic tasks where timely reactions are crucial?
4.  For reproducibility, could you please explicitly state the components of the observation that constitute `S_agent` and `S_rest` for the Maze and Gather environments?
