- Proposes a hierarchical RL framework: SNN pre-training with a mutual information (MI) bonus to learn diverse skills, then a high-level policy selects skills for downstream tasks.
- Shows interpretable skills and improved performance on locomotion and navigation benchmarks.
- Uses information-theoretic objectives to explicitly encourage skill diversity.
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary reason 1: Claim of “minimal domain knowledge” is overstated — intrinsic rewards appear hand-designed for the tested domains.
- Primary reason 2: Generality is questionable due to scalability concerns and reliance on a brittle state-space discretization.
- Proxy rewards (e.g., maximizing speed) are domain-tailored to locomotion and not generally applicable.
- MI bonus requires hand-selecting a relevant state subset (e.g., 2D center-of-mass), a nontrivial, task-specific choice.
- Grid-based discretization for estimating MI is unlikely to scale to higher-dimensional or unknown relevant state variables.
- Reported failures on the more complex Ant agent suggest the approach may not be robust as embodiment complexity and instability increase.
- Suggestion: Explore continuous estimators for p(z|s) (e.g., neural-network-based posterior) to improve generality and robustness.
- Suggestion: Provide a detailed analysis of failure modes (e.g., skill-switching instability), and consider fine-tuning skills or learning transition policies.
- Questions for authors: justify the discretization choice and its limitations/behavior in continuous settings; how does performance scale with the number of skills K and risk of mode collapse?
