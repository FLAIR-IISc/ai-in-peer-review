This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that utilizes stochastic neural networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a diverse set of skills. The authors demonstrate that these skills can be effectively used to solve challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The key contribution of this work is the development of a general framework that combines unsupervised skill learning with a hierarchical architecture to tackle complex tasks.

I decide to **accept** this paper with one major reason being the strong empirical results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in solving challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, and the results show a significant improvement in performance.

One of the main strengths of this paper is the way it brings together different ideas from the literature, including hierarchical reinforcement learning, stochastic neural networks, and information-theoretic regularizers. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated explanation of their approach, including the design choices they made and the trade-offs involved. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the related work and the limitations of their approach.

The empirical results presented in the paper are strong, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the performance of their approach on different tasks. The results show that the proposed framework can learn a diverse set of skills that can be effectively used to solve challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of the different components of their approach, including the effect of the information-theoretic regularizer and the choice of integration method.

One potential limitation of this paper is that the authors rely on a fixed sub-policy setup, which may limit the diversity of sub-policies that can be learned. Additionally, the manager is trained with fixed sub-policies and fixed sub-policy time scales, which may force reliance on very good intrinsic rewards and prelearned sub-policies. However, these limitations are acknowledged by the authors, and they provide a clear discussion of the potential future directions for this work.

To improve the paper, I would like to see more discussion on the following points:

* Clarify why the strong baseline in Sec 7.3 performs poorly and whether it accounts for extra experience from pre-trained policies.
* Provide more details on what "multi-policy" denotes in comparison to "snn".
* Confirm that Fig.1: concatenation = categorical value gives different bias to first hidden layer; bilinear integration = categorical value gives different input weight matrix.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a promising direction for hierarchical reinforcement learning. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, and the results show a significant improvement in performance. I believe that this paper will be of interest to the ICLR community and will contribute to the development of more effective hierarchical reinforcement learning algorithms.
