**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first learns a diverse set of skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and then utilizes these skills to solve challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The framework consists of two phases: a pre-training phase where the agent learns a span of skills using a proxy reward signal, and a downstream phase where a high-level policy is trained to select and combine the learned skills to solve a specific task. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework on a range of tasks, including mazes and gathering tasks, and show that it outperforms state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods.

**Decision**

Based on the review, I decide to **accept** the paper with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning, and the experiments demonstrate its effectiveness on a range of tasks. However, there are some minor issues with the paper that need to be addressed before publication.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper tackles a specific question/problem in the field of reinforcement learning, namely how to learn a diverse set of skills that can be used to solve challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing methods and how their approach addresses these limitations. The experiments are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on a range of tasks.

However, there are some minor issues with the paper that need to be addressed. For example, the authors claim that their approach is related to intrinsic motivation, but this is misleading as the proxy reward signal used in the pre-training phase is not truly intrinsic. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear interpretation of the results on the more complex mazes, where the mutual information regularization does not improve performance. Finally, the authors do not provide enough details on the factorization between S_agent and S_rest, which is necessary for reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback**

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors provide more details on the factorization between S_agent and S_rest, and clarify the relationship between their approach and intrinsic motivation. Additionally, the authors should provide more analysis of the switching behavior and policies, including failure modes and how switching timing affects performance. Finally, the authors should consider providing more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameters used and the computational resources required.
