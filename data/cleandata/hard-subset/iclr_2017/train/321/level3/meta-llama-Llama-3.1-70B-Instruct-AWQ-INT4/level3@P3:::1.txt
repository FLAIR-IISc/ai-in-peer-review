**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that combines the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods. The framework first learns a set of useful skills in a pre-training environment using a proxy reward signal, and then leverages these skills to learn faster in downstream tasks. The authors use Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a diverse set of skills, and then use these skills as basic building blocks for solving tasks with sparse rewards.

**Decision**

I recommend rejecting this paper, primarily due to the lack of experimental analysis and the misleading use of the term "intrinsic motivation".

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Lack of Experimental Analysis**: While the paper presents some promising results, the experimental analysis is weak. The authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the switching behavior and policies, including failure modes and the impact of switching timing on performance. Additionally, the paper lacks a clear interpretation of the results, particularly with regards to the mutual information regularization.
2. **Misleading Use of "Intrinsic Motivation"**: The authors use the term "intrinsic motivation" to describe their approach, but this is misleading. The proxy reward used in the pre-training environment is not a true intrinsic motivation signal, but rather a task-aligned reward that requires minimal domain knowledge. This is not in line with the traditional definition of intrinsic motivation, which involves encouraging exploration and learning without external rewards.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Clarify the Factorization between S_agent and S_rest**: The paper assumes a factorization between the agent's state space (S_agent) and the rest of the state space (S_rest), but this is not clearly specified. The authors should provide more details on how this factorization is defined and how it affects the results.
2. **Provide More Analysis of Switching Behavior**: The paper presents some results on the switching behavior between skills, but more analysis is needed to understand the impact of switching timing on performance.
3. **Consider Alternative Architectures**: The authors use a simple feed-forward architecture for the Manager Network, but it would be interesting to explore alternative architectures, such as recurrent neural networks, to see if they can improve performance.
4. **Provide More Details on the Information-Theoretic Regularizer**: The paper introduces an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information, but more details are needed on how this regularizer is implemented and how it affects the results.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the factorization between S_agent and S_rest is defined and how it affects the results?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of switching between skills for unstable agents, such as the Ant robot?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the impact of switching timing on performance, including failure modes and success stories?
4. How do you plan to extend this work to more complex tasks and environments?
