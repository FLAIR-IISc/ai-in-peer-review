This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first learns useful skills in a pre-training environment and then leverages these skills for learning faster in downstream tasks. The authors bring together strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, using a single proxy reward to guide skill learning and a high-level policy trained on top of these skills to improve exploration and tackle sparse rewards.

The approach is well-motivated, given the challenges of tasks with sparse rewards or long horizons in deep reinforcement learning. The authors provide a clear and comprehensive overview of related work, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the potential benefits of their proposed framework.

The paper supports its claims through experiments on several tasks, including mazes and a gathering task. The results demonstrate that the proposed framework can learn a wide range of skills and significantly boost learning performance uniformly across a range of downstream tasks. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the impact of different design choices, such as the integration of latent variables and the mutual information bonus, on the quality of the skills learned.

Based on the analysis of the paper, I decide to accept it, with the key reasons being the originality and significance of the contribution, the clarity and comprehensiveness of the writing, and the strength of the experimental results.

However, I would like to see more analysis of the switching behavior and policies, including failure modes and the impact of switching timing on performance. Additionally, the factorization between S_agent and S_rest should be clearly specified for reproducibility. Furthermore, the term "intrinsic motivation" is somewhat misleading, as the pre-training with speed/grasp rewards is task-aligned and resembles prior pre-training rather than true intrinsic motivation.

To improve the paper, the authors could provide more interpretation or analysis of the results, particularly with regards to the mutual information regularization, which does not improve performance on the more complex mazes. They could also consider additional experiments to demonstrate the robustness of their framework to different environments and tasks.

Questions to the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the factorization between S_agent and S_rest, and how it is specified for the different tasks?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of switching behavior and policies, particularly in the context of more complex tasks or environments?
3. Can you provide more interpretation or analysis of the results, particularly with regards to the mutual information regularization and its impact on performance?
4. How do you see your framework being applied to more complex tasks or environments, and what challenges do you anticipate?

Additional feedback:

* The writing is clear and comprehensive, but there are some minor errors and typos throughout the paper.
* The figures and tables are well-organized and easy to understand, but some of the captions could be improved for clarity.
* The related work section is comprehensive, but some of the references could be more clearly connected to the proposed framework.
* The experimental results are strong, but some of the analysis could be improved, particularly with regards to the mutual information regularization.
