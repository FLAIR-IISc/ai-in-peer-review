This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, tackling the challenging problem of tasks with sparse rewards or long horizons. The authors introduce a general framework that first learns useful skills in a pre-training environment and then leverages these skills for faster learning in downstream tasks. The approach combines the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, using a single proxy reward to guide skill learning. The authors employ Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer to efficiently pre-train a large span of skills.

I decide to reject this paper, with the main reasons being the lack of generality and the potential failure modes of the approach.

The paper's main contribution is the combination of SNNs and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a diverse set of skills. However, I am concerned that this approach may fail as the behavior richness increases, limiting the diversity of skills discovered. The authors' claim that the method requires minimal domain knowledge is also disputed, as the intrinsic rewards appear hand-crafted and tailored to specific control tasks.

The authors' experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several tasks, including maze navigation and object gathering. However, I question the generality of the method, particularly when applied to more complex morphologies. The authors' results on the 5-link snake robot are encouraging, but I would like to see more experiments on diverse environments and tasks to assess the method's scalability.

Additionally, I am unclear about the discretization of the state/space in Section 5.3. The authors imply a grid, but it is unclear whether the state/space is discretized, why, and what happens if it is continuous. This clarification is necessary to understand the method's applicability to continuous environments.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, I believe that the method's limitations and potential failure modes need to be more thoroughly addressed. I would like to see more analysis on the impact of the switch time T on the performance of the method, as well as more experiments on diverse environments and tasks.

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors:

1. Provide more analysis on the potential failure modes of the approach, particularly as behavior richness increases.
2. Clarify the discretization of the state/space in Section 5.3 and discuss the implications for continuous environments.
3. Conduct more experiments on diverse environments and tasks to assess the method's scalability and generality.
4. Provide more details on the hand-design of rewards and discuss the implications for the method's applicability to different control tasks.

Questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the discretization of the state/space in Section 5.3 and discuss the implications for continuous environments?
2. How do you plan to address the potential failure modes of the approach, particularly as behavior richness increases?
3. Can you provide more experiments on diverse environments and tasks to assess the method's scalability and generality?
4. How do you respond to the criticism that the intrinsic rewards appear hand-crafted and tailored to specific control tasks?
