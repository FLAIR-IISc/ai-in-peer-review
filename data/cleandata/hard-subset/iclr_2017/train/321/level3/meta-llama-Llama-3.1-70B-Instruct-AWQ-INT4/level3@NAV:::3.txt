**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

The paper proposes a general framework for training policies for a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The framework first learns useful skills in a pre-training environment using a proxy reward signal that requires minimal domain knowledge. Then, it leverages the acquired skills for learning faster in downstream tasks by training a high-level policy on top of the skills. The authors use Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) combined with an information-theoretic regularizer to efficiently pre-train a large span of skills.

**Decision: Reject**

I am rejecting this paper due to two main concerns:

1. **Failure Modes**: I am concerned that the approach may fail as behavior richness increases, limiting the diversity of skills discovered. The authors do not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate the scalability of their method to more complex tasks.
2. **Discretization**: The paper implies a grid-based discretization of the state/space in Section 5.3, but it is unclear whether the state/space is discretized, why, and what happens if it is continuous. This lack of clarity makes it difficult to understand the generality of the approach.

**Supporting Arguments**

While the paper presents an interesting framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, I have several concerns that need to be addressed:

* The authors claim that their method requires minimal domain knowledge, but the design of the proxy reward seems to be hand-crafted and tailored to specific control tasks. This contradicts their claim.
* The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate the scalability of their method to more complex tasks. The experiments are limited to simple tasks, and it is unclear how the method would perform in more challenging environments.
* The use of SNNs and the information-theoretic regularizer is interesting, but the authors do not provide a clear explanation of why these choices are necessary or sufficient for learning a diverse set of skills.

**Additional Feedback**

To improve the paper, I would suggest the following:

* Provide more evidence to demonstrate the scalability of the method to more complex tasks.
* Clarify the discretization of the state/space and explain why it is necessary.
* Provide more details on the design of the proxy reward and explain why it is not hand-crafted.
* Consider using more complex tasks to evaluate the performance of the method.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you provide more evidence to demonstrate the scalability of the method to more complex tasks?
* How do you discretize the state/space, and why is it necessary?
* Can you provide more details on the design of the proxy reward and explain why it is not hand-crafted?
* How do you plan to address the failure modes of the approach, particularly as behavior richness increases?
