This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that tackles the challenges of tasks with sparse rewards or long horizons. The authors introduce a general framework that first learns useful skills in a pre-training environment and then leverages the acquired skills for learning faster in downstream tasks. The approach brings together the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods, using a single proxy reward to guide the learning of useful skills. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework in learning a wide span of interpretable skills in a sample-efficient way and significantly boosting the learning performance uniformly across a wide range of downstream tasks.

I decide to accept this paper with the following key reasons:

1. The paper tackles a significant challenge in reinforcement learning, namely, tasks with sparse rewards or long horizons, and proposes a novel framework that addresses this challenge.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their framework, demonstrating its effectiveness in learning a wide span of interpretable skills and significantly boosting the learning performance in downstream tasks.

However, I have some criticisms and questions that the authors should address to further improve their work:

1. Failure modes: The authors should discuss the potential failure modes of their approach, particularly as the behavior richness increases. How does the approach handle cases where the diversity of skills discovered is limited?
2. Discretization: In Section 5.3, the authors imply a grid-based discretization of the state or action space. They should clarify whether this is indeed the case, why they chose this approach, and what happens if the state or action space is continuous.
3. Generality/embodiment: The authors should assess the generality of their method by applying it to more complex morphologies, such as a 5-link swimmer versus a 2-link swimmer. This would help to evaluate the scalability of their approach.
4. Hand-design of rewards: The authors claim that their approach requires minimal domain knowledge, but the intrinsic rewards used in their experiments appear to be hand-crafted and tailored to specific control tasks. The authors should discuss the potential limitations of their approach in terms of the design of rewards and whether their method can be applied to more general cases.

Additional feedback:

* The authors should provide more details on the neural network architectures used in their experiments, including the number of layers, the number of units in each layer, and the activation functions used.
* The authors should discuss the potential benefits and limitations of using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) in their framework, particularly in terms of the trade-off between expressiveness and computational complexity.
* The authors should provide more details on the policy optimization algorithm used in their experiments, including the choice of hyperparameters and the convergence criteria used.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the discretization scheme used in Section 5.3 and how it affects the performance of your approach?
* How does your approach handle cases where the diversity of skills discovered is limited, and what are the potential failure modes of your approach?
* Can you discuss the potential limitations of your approach in terms of the design of rewards and whether your method can be applied to more general cases?
* How does your approach scale to more complex morphologies, such as a 5-link swimmer versus a 2-link swimmer?
* Can you provide more details on the neural network architectures used in your experiments and the policy optimization algorithm used?
