**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that leverages stochastic neural networks (SNNs) and an information-theoretic regularizer to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment. The framework then utilizes these skills to solve challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on various robotic tasks, including locomotion, maze navigation, and object manipulation.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I decide to accept this paper, with a score of 7. The two key reasons for this decision are:

1. **Strong empirical findings**: The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in learning a wide range of skills and solving challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The results show that the framework outperforms state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods and achieves significant improvements in exploration and learning performance.
2. **Well-motivated approach**: The paper provides a clear and well-motivated approach to HRL, combining the strengths of intrinsic motivation and hierarchical methods. The use of SNNs and an information-theoretic regularizer is well-justified, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the benefits and limitations of their approach.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the benefits and limitations of their approach, including:

* The use of SNNs allows for flexible weight-sharing schemes among different policies and enables the learning of multi-modal policies.
* The information-theoretic regularizer encourages diversity in the skills learned by the SNN and helps to prevent mode collapse.
* The hierarchical structure of the framework enables the reuse of skills across different tasks and reduces the sample complexity required to solve each task.

However, the paper also acknowledges some limitations of their approach, including:

* The need for careful tuning of the hyperparameters, particularly the mutual information bonus.
* The potential for mode collapse in the SNN, particularly when the number of skills is large.
* The limited ability of the framework to handle tasks with very long horizons or highly complex dynamics.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

To further improve the paper, I would like to see:

* More analysis on the impact of the switch time T on the performance of the framework, particularly in tasks with long horizons or highly complex dynamics.
* A more detailed comparison with other HRL methods, including those that use different types of neural networks or regularization techniques.
* More discussion on the potential applications of the framework beyond robotic tasks, such as in game playing or recommendation systems.

I also have some questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the information-theoretic regularizer, including the choice of the mutual information bonus and the discretization of the state space?
* How do you handle the case where the number of skills is very large, and the SNN is prone to mode collapse?
* Can you provide more analysis on the robustness of the framework to different types of noise or perturbations in the environment?
