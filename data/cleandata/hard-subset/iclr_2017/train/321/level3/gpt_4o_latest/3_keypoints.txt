- Paper contribution: Proposes an HRL framework that pre-trains diverse skills using SNNs with intrinsic motivation and an information-theoretic regularizer, improving exploration and downstream task performance (mazes, gathering).
- Decision: Reject — concerns about failure model, scalability, and overstated minimal domain knowledge undermine generality and rigor.
- Failure model concern: Does not adequately address limitations when skill diversity is critical; instability with the Ant highlights switching difficulties in unstable environments.
- Generality and scalability: Strong on simple embodiments (Swimmer, Snake) but not convincingly scalable to more complex/higher-dimensional systems (e.g., 5-link swimmer, Ant).
- Intrinsic rewards critique: Claim of minimal domain knowledge is overstated because proxy rewards and the mutual information regularizer are hand-crafted and task-specific.
- Space discretization: Unclear whether mutual information estimation uses discretization and what its implications are for scalability to high-dimensional state spaces.
- Skill transferability: Lacks thorough evaluation of how well learned skills generalize to tasks with significantly different dynamics or objectives.
- Switching mechanism: Instability during skill switching (Ant) indicates a need for more robust transition mechanisms (e.g., learned transition or termination policies).
- Evaluation metrics: Evaluation is incomplete — should include additional quantitative metrics like sample efficiency, success rate, and robustness beyond visitation plots and learning curves.
- Q1 (mutual information): How does discretization scale with higher-dimensional state spaces and were continuous-space estimation alternatives considered?
- Q2 (dynamics): Can the framework handle dynamic objectives or changing environments, and how would it adapt?
- Q3 (instability mitigation): What strategies can address instability during skill switching for complex robots like the Ant?
- Q4 (proxy reward design): How does the choice of proxy reward affect skill diversity and quality, and are there guidelines for designing effective proxy rewards?
