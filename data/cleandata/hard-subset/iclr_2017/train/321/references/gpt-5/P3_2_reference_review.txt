Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a simple and practical framework for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings. The key idea is to pretrain a single low-level policy that represents a span of skills using a stochastic neural network (SNN) conditioned on a discrete latent code, and then to freeze this policy and train a high-level “Manager” that selects a latent code every T steps to compose skills. Two technical contributions make the low-level skills diverse and interpretable: (1) a bilinear integration of the latent code with observations, which effectively gates the first-layer weights and encourages distinct behaviors per code; and (2) an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between the latent code Z and a subset of state features (here, the robot center-of-mass (CoM) position), implemented via a simple discretized posterior estimator. Policy optimization uses TRPO, treating Z as part of the observation.

Empirically, the authors demonstrate that SNNs with bilinear integration and MI regularization produce clearly different locomotion gaits and directions on Swimmer/Snake during pretraining with a very weak proxy reward (speed magnitude), and that a randomly initialized Manager on top of these skills yields much broader exploration. On sparse downstream tasks (four mazes and Gather), the hierarchical agent learns substantially faster and reaches higher returns than baselines that either use standard exploration or add the same CoM intrinsic reward directly in the downstream task. Ablations compare concatenation vs bilinear integration, and varying MI coefficient αH. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The paper delivers a clear, well-motivated, and easy-to-implement recipe that reliably yields diverse, reusable skills from a minimal proxy reward, and shows that composing them with a high-level policy substantially improves exploration and learning in sparse-reward tasks.
- Although conceptually related to prior work, the combination of SNN conditioning, bilinear integration, and a targeted MI regularizer is novel in this RL context and yields interpretable, practically useful behaviors.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The problem is important—efficient exploration and transfer across sparse-reward tasks. The paper situates itself among options/HRL (Parr & Russell; Sutton et al.), skill discovery (HiREPS), intrinsic motivation (VIME, count-based), and hierarchical architectures (option-critic, feudal). Compared to Heess et al. (2016), which requires goal specifications, the use of a single proxy reward is appealingly minimal. The InfoGAN-inspired MI objective is adapted to RL by conditioning on a relevant subset of state (CoM), which is a reasonable design choice for locomotion.
- Methodological clarity: The architecture and training procedure are clear. Treating Z as part of the observation avoids complications with TRPO. Fixing Z per rollout in pretraining enforces temporally extended behaviors, and fixed-duration commitment during high-level training is a sensible first step. The bilinear integration is a simple but impactful architectural choice, and the paper provides an intuitive explanation (conditioning first-layer weights).
- Empirical support: The visitation plots convincingly show that SNNs with bilinear integration and MI produce multiple distinct gaits/directions from a single pretraining run, with K-fold better pretraining sample efficiency than training K separate policies. The one-million-step exploration traces under a random Manager illustrate significant coverage gains vs Gaussian action noise. On the sparse mazes and Gather, the hierarchical approach consistently outperforms strong baselines that include adding the same CoM proxy reward to the downstream task; the benefit of MI is especially evident in Gather (improved mean and lower variance). Reporting multiple seeds for both pretraining and downstream training is appreciated, and the paper is honest about variance and failure modes (e.g., Ant instability).
- Limitations acknowledged: The paper fairly discusses limitations—fixed T, frozen low-level, sensitivity to the pretraining skill span, and instability when switching skills in Ant—together with plausible future directions (learned terminations, end-to-end fine-tuning with straight-through estimators, recurrent Managers).

Concerns and suggestions (impact not fatal)
- Scope and generality: The MI regularizer uses CoM coordinates; while “minimally engineered,” it is domain-specific and may not transfer to, e.g., manipulation without careful choice of the informative subset. A learned discriminator q(z|s) (as in InfoGAN/DIAYN-style objectives) could generalize beyond CoM and avoid discretization/hand-chosen features.
- Baselines: The most relevant comparisons (beyond “intrinsic reward in downstream”) would include other HRL/skill-discovery methods that use latent conditioning or options: option-critic, mixture-of-experts with a gating network, goal-conditioned policies, or curiosity-driven baselines (e.g., ICM). The multi-policy baseline is stronger than naive exploration but uses 6× more pretraining samples; reporting results at matched total sample budgets would strengthen claims about sample efficiency.
- Metrics: Diversity is primarily assessed via qualitative visitation plots. Quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise trajectory distance, state coverage, MI achieved, entropy over Z given state) would make the case stronger and allow clearer ablation conclusions.
- Sensitivity and ablations: More systematic analysis of K, αH, grid resolution for MI, and switch time T on Swimmer would be helpful. The appendix provides T analysis on Snake; bringing at least one such analysis into the main text would aid readers.
- Stability and transfer: The Ant results highlight that switching can be unsafe. Solutions like training with a switching prior, continuity penalties, or overlap between skill regions (or allowing short “hand-off” phases) could mitigate this. Demonstrating even partial success on Ant would broaden the impact.

Questions for the authors
1. MI estimation: How sensitive are results to the grid resolution and to count smoothing? Did you try learning qφ(z|s) with a classifier/regressor instead of discretization?
2. Generality: For non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation), what subset of state would you use for MI? Can you show results with a learned q(z|s) to avoid manual feature choice?
3. Sample efficiency: Can you report total environment steps for pretraining + downstream training across methods, and include comparisons at matched budgets against multi-policy and curiosity-based baselines?
4. Manager design: Did you try learning a termination policy (variable T) or a recurrent Manager? How does performance change when allowing short “warm-up” phases after switching to reduce instability?
5. Skill usage: During downstream training, what is the entropy of the Manager’s distribution over Z, and how many skills are used frequently? Do different tasks reuse overlapping subsets?
6. Robustness: How sensitive is performance to K and αH? Is there a principled way to set αH without peeking at downstream tasks?

Overall, this is a well-executed piece of work that advances practical HRL with a simple and effective mechanism for discovering reusable skills, supported by solid empirical evidence on sparse tasks. With stronger baselines and more general MI estimation, it would be even more compelling.
