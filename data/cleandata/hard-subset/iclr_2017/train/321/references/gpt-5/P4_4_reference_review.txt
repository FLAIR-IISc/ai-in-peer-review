1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper addresses learning and reusing diverse, temporally extended skills to improve exploration and sample efficiency in sparse-reward reinforcement learning tasks. It proposes:
- Pretraining a single latent-conditioned policy (a Stochastic Neural Network, SNN) in a minimal proxy environment using a generic proxy reward (e.g., center-of-mass speed for locomotion).
- Inducing multi-modality via a discrete latent variable z held fixed during an episode, with bilinear integration of z and state to increase expressivity.
- An information-theoretic regularizer: a mutual information (MI) bonus between z and a selected subset of state features (e.g., CoM position) to encourage diverse, interpretable, and distinguishable behaviors.
- A hierarchical control phase where a high-level “manager” selects skills (latent codes) at fixed intervals T to solve sparse downstream tasks; low-level skills are frozen.
- Empirical results on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks (maze navigation and gather) with Swimmer and Snake robots, ablations on integration type and MI coefficient, exploration analyses, sensitivity to switch time T, and discussion of failure modes on Ant.

The paper reports substantial improvements in exploration and task performance compared to strong baselines (including intrinsic reward baselines and prior intrinsic motivation methods for Gather), with code and implementation details provided.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- The approach is technically sound and well-motivated, combining latent-conditioned policies with an MI-based diversity objective to learn reusable skills with minimal supervision.
- The empirical evidence is convincing, with ablations, multiple environments/robots, analysis of exploration, and consistent gains on sparse tasks where standard methods fail.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity and placement in literature:
- The problem is clearly formulated: reduce sample complexity across sparse-reward tasks by learning a skill repertoire in a proxy environment consistent across tasks’ “agent-space.”
- The method is positioned against hierarchical RL (options, HiREPS), intrinsic motivation (e.g., VIME), and related pretraining works (e.g., Heess et al. 2016). The MI objective is inspired by InfoGAN and adapted to RL skill discovery without downstream task supervision.

Technical soundness:
- The SNN architecture with discrete latent variables and bilinear integration is appropriate for multi-modal control and weight sharing. Holding z fixed per rollout yields temporally coherent skills.
- The MI bonus is implemented as an additional reward term proportional to log p̂(z|c), which corresponds to minimizing H(Z|C) given fixed H(Z), matching the stated objective. Estimation via discretized counts is practical, and treating z as part of the observation allows standard TRPO updates.
- The hierarchical manager over frozen skills is a reasonable and clean design choice; commitment time T controls temporal abstraction.

Empirical rigor:
- The paper includes ablations on integration (concatenation vs bilinear) and MI strength; qualitative visitation plots and quantitative learning curves on multiple mazes and gather; and results for two robots (Swimmer, Snake). It evaluates variance across pretraining seeds and studies sensitivity to T.
- Baselines include adding the proxy reward to downstream tasks, and comparison to VIME in the Gather setting. The method solves tasks that standard exploration fails on, consistent with prior reports.
- The analysis acknowledges limitations (skill switching in unstable agents, fixed T, frozen skills) and provides supporting appendices and videos.

Novelty:
- Using SNNs with a discrete latent, bilinear integration, and an MI-based diversity bonus to pretrain a span of skills for hierarchical control is a coherent and, at the time, novel combination for continuous control. The reliance on a single proxy reward rather than goal sets is a practical contribution.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all part of the decision)
- Quantify diversity: add metrics (e.g., estimated MI, state-space coverage, option distinguishability) in addition to visitation plots.
- Broaden baselines: where feasible, compare to option-critic or HiREPS on at least a subset of tasks, or discuss why direct comparison is nontrivial.
- Sensitivity analyses: report performance vs K (number of skills), grid resolution for p̂(z|c), and α for MI, to guide practitioners.
- Non-stationary reward shaping: discuss the effect of batch-wise p̂ updates on policy gradient bias and stability.
- Generality of C: clarify how to select the subset of state used for MI in domains beyond locomotion (e.g., manipulators), and include a non-locomotion example if possible.
- Manager extensions: consider a learned termination policy (variable T) or recurrent manager, and report preliminary results or expected benefits.
- Robustness to instability: for Ant, explore smoother skill transitions or warm-start phases to reduce falls; include partial positive results if any.
- Reporting: clearly separate pretraining and downstream sample budgets; include compute cost; and provide more details on bilinear integration implementation.

Questions for the authors
- MI estimation: How sensitive are results to the discretization granularity of c? Did you try learned density models (e.g., a classifier p(z|c) with a neural network) and how did they compare?
- Choice of C: Beyond CoM (x, y), what criteria guide choosing C in other domains (manipulation, visual inputs)? Any empirical results outside locomotion?
- Number of skills K: How does performance scale with K? Is there evidence of diminishing returns or mode collapse at larger K?
- Manager training: What is the sample complexity for training the manager vs end-to-end training with adaptability of low-level skills? Did partial fine-tuning of the low-level help?
- Stability: Does the MI bonus ever encourage redundant skills that differ only spatially but not functionally? How do you detect and mitigate that?
- Continuous latents: Did you try continuous z (e.g., Gaussian) and, if so, how did interpretability and manager training compare?
- Exploration vs exploitation: What entropy regularization or scheduling is used for the manager? Any evidence that over-committing to certain skills hampers final performance?
- Ant failures: Did you evaluate skill-switch smoothing (e.g., interpolation) or overlap training between skills to reduce distribution shift at switch time?
