Summary
The paper proposes a hierarchical RL framework aimed at improving exploration and sample efficiency on sparse-reward tasks by (i) pretraining a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy with a discrete latent variable to learn a “span of skills,” encouraged to be diverse via a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., center-of-mass position), and (ii) training a high-level “Manager” that selects among these skills at a fixed temporal granularity to solve downstream tasks. The method uses TRPO throughout, examines different ways to integrate the latent code (concatenation vs bilinear), and argues that the SNN enables multimodal action distributions with weight sharing. Empirically, the paper reports qualitative skill diversity (visitation plots) and improved learning on Swimmer-based Maze and Gather benchmarks, with additional discussion for Snake and Ant. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Empirical validation is limited and does not convincingly establish superiority over strong, established HRL and intrinsic motivation baselines; several claims (e.g., sample complexity benefits) are not rigorously supported. (2) The approach relies on nontrivial hand-crafted design choices (proxy reward; MI on discretized CoM) whose generality and robustness are not demonstrated; performance exhibits high variance and sensitivity to pretraining.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning vs prior work: The idea of learning temporally extended skills/options is well established (e.g., options framework, HiREPS, option-critic). Using a latent variable to gate per-skill behaviors is functionally close to a mixture-of-experts or conditional policies. The MI-based diversity encouragement is inspired by InfoGAN-like objectives and by prior skill discovery ideas. The paper does not include comparisons to strong hierarchical baselines (option-critic, learned macro-actions) or to non-hierarchical strong exploration methods beyond limited references (VIME only in appendix). As a result, it is difficult to assess incremental value over established approaches.

- Methodological concerns:
  - MI bonus design: The MI is computed between Z and a discretized (x, y) of the CoM, estimated by counts within each batch. This encourages separation primarily in position, not necessarily in control-space or task-relevant behavior. It is unclear that this yields reusable, task-agnostic skills beyond directional locomotion; it may bias towards trivial “go different directions” modes.
  - Proxy reward: The method assumes a hand-designed proxy reward that induces multiple local optima. There is no guidance on designing such rewards for other domains (e.g., manipulation), and the approach appears tailored to locomotion where CoM and speed are natural.
  - Claims on sample efficiency: The paper suggests K-fold gains in pretraining efficiency due to weight sharing. However, the core question—amortized sample complexity across multiple downstream tasks—is not empirically quantified. The suite includes only a few closely related tasks, and there is no cumulative sample accounting across many tasks to substantiate the claim.
  - Stability/robustness: Results show large variance across seeds; certain pretrained SNNs lack critical motions and then fail on some mazes. Ant experiments expose instability when switching skills. These undermine the claim of broadly reusable skills.

- Empirical evaluation:
  - Benchmarks are relatively simple (Swimmer mazes, Gather with specific horizons), with limited diversity. The appendix results on Snake/Ant are qualitative or show failure modes.
  - Baselines: The “multi-policy” baseline uses more pretraining samples and is not consistently outperformed; stronger baselines (option-critic, feudal, learned macro-actions, count-based methods) are missing. A non-hierarchical policy with temporally extended actions (frame-skip/macro-actions) or a mixture-of-experts trained end-to-end on downstream tasks would be informative.
  - Metrics: Heavy reliance on visitation plots to assert diversity; no quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., MI values, state coverage, skill discriminability, option termination statistics) are reported. The effect of K and switch time T is only partially explored and largely in appendices.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide rigorous, quantitative evaluation of diversity (estimated MI, coverage, pairwise behavioral distances) and transfer (amortized sample counts across many distinct tasks with different layouts/dynamics).
- Compare against established HRL baselines (option-critic with/without intrinsic rewards; learned macro-actions; feudal methods) and strong exploration baselines (e.g., count-based, pseudo-counts) under matched budgets.
- Clarify and justify MI target features. Using only CoM (x, y) is restrictive; consider learning/choosing a broader set of behavior-descriptive features and a parametric posterior estimator rather than coarse counting.
- Evaluate sensitivity to the proxy reward and show results in domains beyond locomotion to support generality.
- Report ablations on K, latent-code integration (including mixture-of-experts equivalences), and Manager switch time with statistical significance across many seeds.
- Address instability when switching skills (e.g., learned terminations, transition-aware training, or regularization for compatibility across skills).
- If claiming sample-efficiency benefits, include controlled accounting: pretraining cost amortized over N tasks, performance vs N, and cross-task generalization with no (or minimal) fine-tuning.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of proxy reward and to the MI features? Can you demonstrate transfer to qualitatively different downstream tasks (e.g., where directionality is not sufficient or even irrelevant)?
- What is the total pretraining budget, and how does downstream sample efficiency scale as the number of tasks increases? Please provide cumulative sample-complexity comparisons across many tasks.
- How does performance vary with K (under- and overcomplete settings)? Is there any mechanism to prune redundant skills or adapt K?
- Can you compare to an end-to-end mixture-of-experts or option-critic trained directly on the downstream tasks under the same budget, and to a learned macro-action baseline?
- Does computing the MI on CoM incentivize degeneracies (e.g., drifting to specific regions) that make skills less reusable? What happens if MI is computed on richer state features?
- Can the Manager learn variable-duration terminations, and does that mitigate the instability observed on Ant?
- Please provide quantitative diversity metrics and confidence intervals for all main results, and ensure budgets are matched across baselines.
