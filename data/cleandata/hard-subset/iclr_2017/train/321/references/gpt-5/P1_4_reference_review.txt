1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles learning and reusing skills to improve exploration and sample efficiency in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL tasks. The authors propose a two-phase framework:
- Pretraining: Learn a single low-level multi-skill policy using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with a discrete latent variable z. Skills emerge as modes of π(a|s,z). To reliably induce diverse, interpretable behaviors, they (i) use bilinear integration of z with the observation to increase expressivity and (ii) add an information-theoretic regularizer: a mutual-information (MI) bonus between z and a low-dimensional subset of the state (the agent’s center-of-mass (CoM) position), estimated via simple discretization and counts. The pretraining uses a “proxy” reward (e.g., speed magnitude) requiring minimal domain knowledge, and importantly no task-specific reward.
- Transfer: Freeze the SNN skills and train a high-level “Manager” policy that selects a skill (z) every T steps, thereby providing temporally extended actions that greatly improve exploration in downstream sparse-reward tasks.
The paper uses TRPO in both phases and provides code and videos. Empirically, on MuJoCo swimmers and snakes in sparse mazes and gather tasks, the approach learns a wide, interpretable span of locomotion skills, substantially improves exploration, and solves tasks that naïve exploration fails to solve. Ablations show the importance of bilinear integration and the MI bonus; the hierarchical manager with SNN skills outperforms a multi-policy baseline while using far fewer pretraining samples. The paper also analyzes sensitivity to switch time T, discusses failure modes with unstable agents (Ant), and reports multiple seeds.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated, and practical method for skill discovery and reuse that addresses sparse-reward exploration and sample efficiency.
- Strong empirical evidence that SNN-based skills plus an MI bonus produce diverse, interpretable behaviors and enable solving challenging sparse tasks via hierarchical control, with ablations and analyses that support the design choices.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a core challenge—exploration and sample efficiency in sparse reward settings—and leverages HRL to reduce effective horizon. The idea of learning a diverse skill basis once and reusing it across tasks with minimal domain knowledge is compelling and well positioned relative to options/HRL and intrinsic motivation.
- Methodological novelty and clarity: Using an SNN to model a multi-modal low-level policy with a categorical latent z is a neat way to share parameters and amortize pretraining costs. The bilinear integration of z with observations is a simple design that empirically improves the separability of skills. The MI regularizer, computed over CoM coordinates, is easy to implement and demonstrably increases skill diversity without requiring task-specific reward shaping.
- Empirical validation: 
  - Pretraining: Visitation plots convincingly show skill diversity emerging from different z’s; bilinear integration and MI bonus each contribute, together they yield the most distinct skills.
  - Exploration: One-million-step rollouts show that random skill switching dramatically expands the explored region relative to Gaussian action noise or a multi-policy baseline composed of independently trained controllers.
  - Downstream tasks: On multiple mazes and gather, hierarchical policies with SNN skills learn faster and to higher return than baselines using only the proxy reward; results are averaged across multiple pretrains and seeds. The method scales to a harder agent (Snake) and includes sensitivity to T. The Ant experiments candidly expose limitations and suggest future directions.
- Reproducibility: Code and videos provided; hyperparameters and implementation details are reasonably documented.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Baselines: While intrinsic motivation (VIME) is compared on gather in the appendix, including additional HRL baselines (e.g., option-critic, macro-action learning) in at least one setting would strengthen claims about the advantage of the proposed pretrain+manager framework. The multi-policy baseline is helpful, but it uses substantially more pretraining samples; a matched-budget comparison would be informative.
- Generality of MI bonus: The MI is computed with CoM (x,y), which is well-suited to locomotion. Please discuss or demonstrate how to choose the “relevant subset” C for other domains (e.g., manipulation) and how performance degrades if C is misspecified. A small manipulation or non-locomotion proxy task would make the generality claim more convincing.
- Quantifying skill diversity: Beyond visitation plots, consider a quantitative metric (e.g., MI estimate, state-space coverage, direction entropy, or inter-skill policy divergence) to summarize diversity vs. α_H and integration choice.
- Sensitivity analyses: Include sensitivity to the number of skills K and to the grid resolution for p̂(Z|C). Also report the computational overhead (if any) of the MI bonus estimation.
- Fairness and stability: Given the high variance in some tasks (especially mazes), include confidence intervals and, where possible, statistical tests. Clarify whether any early stopping or model selection is applied.
- Manager design: While fixed commitment T is reasonable here, briefly quantify the performance gap (if any) to shorter T in more reactive scenarios; or add a simple termination heuristic baseline to show that fixed T is not a bottleneck in the studied tasks.
- Minor: Provide exact bilinear integration details (dimensionalities, parameterization) and clarify whether any normalization of z or s is used before the outer product.

5) Questions for the authors
- Choice of C for MI: How would you select the subset of state variables C in domains where the “right” factors are less obvious than CoM? Have you tried learning a discriminator qφ(z|c) (as in InfoGAN) instead of count-based estimation to handle higher-dimensional C?
- Number of skills K: How sensitive are results to K? Do too many skills cause redundancy or manager confusion, and too few limit task performance?
- Pretraining budget: How do results change if you match the pretraining sample budget between the SNN and the multi-policy baseline? Does the SNN still outperform?
- Transfer breadth: Have you tried downstream tasks that require qualitatively different behaviors than locomotion direction (e.g., precise turning or stopping), and does the MI encourage such skills without explicit reward?
- Ant instability: Did you try curriculum switching, warm-starting the next skill near states it has seen, or regularizing skills to overlap in state distribution to mitigate falls?
- Manager training stability: Do you observe non-stationarity issues when the manager explores rarely used skills? Any tricks (entropy bonuses, temperature) that proved crucial?
- MI coefficient and grid: How was α_H chosen and how robust are results to its value? What is the trade-off between grid granularity and stability of p̂(Z|C)?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, practical contribution to HRL with convincing empirical evidence and clear ablations. The paper would be further strengthened by additional baselines and a demonstration of generality beyond locomotion-centric state factors for the MI bonus.
