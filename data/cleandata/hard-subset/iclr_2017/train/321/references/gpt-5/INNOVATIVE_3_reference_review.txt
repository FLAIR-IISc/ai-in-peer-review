Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles sparse-reward RL by learning a reusable skill space in a pretraining environment with only a proxy reward, and then training a high-level “manager” to compose those skills on downstream tasks.
- Skills are parameterized by a stochastic neural network (SNN) policy with a discrete latent z; skills are made diverse via an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between z and a subset of state features (e.g., CoM position). A bilinear integration between observation and latent is proposed and shown to matter.
- In downstream tasks, the low-level SNN is frozen; a manager selects a skill every T steps, yielding temporally coherent exploration.
- Experiments on Swimmer (and Snake, with partial results on Ant) show that: (i) SNN+MI produces diverse, interpretable skills; (ii) hierarchical control substantially improves exploration; (iii) the method solves sparse mazes and gather tasks more reliably and efficiently than strong baselines (including VIME in the benchmark setting), with ablations indicating the value of bilinear integration and MI.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Novel and bold direction: unsupervised skill discovery with a minimal proxy reward using an SNN policy and an MI regularizer, then hierarchical reuse. This is a clean, general idea likely to influence subsequent HRL work.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach materially improves exploration and solves challenging sparse tasks, with interpretable skills and useful ablations.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse reward and long horizons are central RL challenges; HRL with reusable skills is a compelling way to reduce search.
- Novelty: Using an SNN to encode multiple skills with weight sharing and a simple MI bonus to enforce behavioral diversity is elegant and differs from prior options/macro-action work that relies on extrinsic reward, bottleneck discovery, or strong supervision. The bilinear integration between observation and latent is a practical architectural detail that clearly improves skill separation.
- Soundness: Treating z as input allows standard TRPO. The MI objective reduces to minimizing H(Z|C) with a simple count-based posterior, which is pragmatic and matches the pretraining signal.
- Evidence: 
  - Clear qualitative visitation plots show distinct, reusable behaviors tied to latent codes.
  - Quantitative improvements on sparse mazes and gather; random-manager rollouts demonstrate vastly better coverage.
  - Ablations: concatenation vs bilinear; with/without MI; switch-time analysis; multi-policy vs SNN weight sharing. The approach scales to a higher-DoF “Snake.”
- Candor about limits: Failures on unstable agents (Ant), fixed T and frozen low-level policies, high variance across pretraining seeds. This transparency is appreciated.

Evaluation against key questions
1) Question tackled: How to learn a reusable, diverse skill set with minimal supervision that enables effective exploration and efficient learning in sparse-reward downstream tasks.
2) Motivation and placement: Well motivated; connects to HRL, intrinsic motivation, multimodal policies. Related work is discussed fairly; the MI angle and SNN multimodality are a distinct synthesis.
3) Support for claims: Empirical claims are supported on standard continuous-control benchmarks with appropriate ablations. Some comparisons are missing (see below), but the evidence is convincing for the stated scope.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to my decision)
- Accounting for samples: Please report total environment steps including pretraining when comparing to baselines (e.g., VIME) to quantify net sample-efficiency gains across tasks.
- Choice of MI features C: The CoM choice is intuitive for locomotion but hand-crafted. Consider learning a discriminator q(z|s) (as in InfoGAN) to reduce dependence on feature selection and discretization; compare to a learned q to assess robustness.
- Sensitivity: Provide sensitivity analyses for K (number of skills), MI weight α, and grid resolution. Do skills degenerate for large K? Is there an automatic way to prune unused skills?
- Baselines: Add comparisons to other hierarchical methods available at submission time (e.g., option-critic macro-actions) or macro-action learning (Mnih et al., 2016), even if not perfectly matched, to position your gains more clearly.
- Manager design: While fixed T works here, a learned termination policy would likely help in dynamic tasks and unstable agents. A short experiment (or simulation) with learned termination would strengthen the story.
- End-to-end fine-tuning: A small-scale experiment with Gumbel-Softmax to fine-tune skills on a downstream task would illustrate the trade-off between reuse and task specialization (even if freezing suffices in your tasks).
- Quantifying exploration: Add a simple coverage metric (unique cells visited, dispersion) to complement the qualitative plots.
- Ant instability: Your diagnosis is plausible; a safety/comfort set or value-penalized skill switch could mitigate falls. A short experiment with conservative switching would be informative.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to the choice of C and its discretization? Did you try learning q(z|s) with a classifier to estimate MI without gridding?
2) What is the impact of K on performance and exploration? Do you observe mode collapse or redundant skills for larger K?
3) How do total sample budgets (pretraining + downstream) compare to non-hierarchical baselines across multiple downstream tasks? Is the amortized cost of pretraining clearly recouped?
4) Why does bilinear integration help over concatenation beyond increased parameterization? Did you control for parameter count?
5) For Ant, did you try state-dependent switch penalties, value-aware switching, or short warm-up periods after switching to reduce falls?
6) Do frozen skills materially limit performance in any downstream task relative to end-to-end fine-tuning?
7) Could the MI objective be extended to include orientation or local sensory features to induce turning/sideways gaits more consistently?

Overall, this is a creative and forward-looking paper that proposes a practical, minimally supervised approach to discovering and reusing diverse skills. Despite some missing comparisons and hand-designed MI features, the idea and evidence are strong enough to merit acceptance.
