1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning in sparse-reward settings that (a) pre-trains a single multi-skill low-level policy using stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with a categorical latent, (b) encourages diversity via a mutual-information (MI) regularizer between the latent and a task-relevant subset of the state (e.g., center-of-mass coordinates), and (c) composes skills at test time with a high-level “Manager” that selects a latent every T steps. The key design choices include bilinear integration of the latent with observations (to increase expressiveness over simple concatenation) and estimating the MI bonus by discretizing the relevant state subspace and using empirical p̂(z|c). Training uses TRPO and treats the latent as part of the observation. Empirically, the approach learns interpretable, directionally distinct locomotion skills in a proxy environment using only a simple speed reward, and then solves long-horizon sparse-reward tasks (several maze variants and a gather task) more efficiently than baselines, including a “multi-policy” baseline (multiple independently trained policies) and direct intrinsic rewards. The method scales to a larger “Snake” agent and analyzes sensitivity to the switching period T. Limitations on unstable robots (Ant) are discussed.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) The approach is simple, well-motivated, and technically sound, yielding diverse, reusable skills via SNNs and an MI bonus with minimal domain knowledge. (2) The empirical results show clear improvements over reasonable baselines on challenging sparse-reward tasks, with ablations that isolate the contributions of bilinear integration and MI regularization.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity: The problem setting, assumptions (Sagent vs Srest), and method are clearly described. The MI regularizer is motivated and implemented transparently, with an explicit estimator and training algorithm. The hierarchical control scheme is straightforward and reproducible; hyperparameters and code availability further support clarity.

Motivation and placement in the literature: The work is well positioned with respect to HRL, intrinsic motivation, and prior skill-discovery methods. Compared to HiREPS and option-critic, this paper targets multi-modal low-level policies via SNNs with a simple, general pretraining signal. The InfoGAN-inspired MI objective is appropriately adapted to RL. The comparison to concurrent pretraining work (Heess et al.) highlights the distinction of not requiring goal labels in pretraining.

Technical soundness: Treating z as part of the observation allows TRPO to be applied to π(a|s,z) without approximating a mixture policy’s marginal. The MI bonus reduces to −H(Z|C), which is estimated in a pragmatic manner (grid counts). The assumption to sample a latent per episode during pretraining yields temporally consistent skills. The hierarchical manager acting every T steps is a standard and reasonable abstraction for sparse tasks. While there is no theoretical sample-complexity result, the claims are empirical and appropriately stated.

Empirical support: 
- Skill learning: visitation plots show multimodal, interpretable behaviors; bilinear integration and MI increase diversity and reduce overlap between skills.
- Exploration: one-million-step rollouts under random managers demonstrate substantially larger state-space coverage versus Gaussian noise and versus multi-policy baselines.
- Downstream tasks: consistent improvements in learning speed and final performance across several mazes and the gather task, with multiple seeds and no pretrain cherry-picking. The Snake experiments and T-ablations increase confidence in robustness. Limitations on Ant are candidly presented.

Novelty: Combining SNN policies with categorical latents, bilinear integration, and an MI bonus targeted to relevant state subspaces for skill discovery, then reusing these skills via a frozen low-level policy with a high-level manager, is a coherent and practically useful contribution. The components are individually known, but their integration and demonstrated effectiveness for sparse long-horizon control represent meaningful novelty.

Caveats: Experiments focus on locomotion; claims of generality to manipulation are not empirically supported. The MI estimator is heuristic and domain-specific (choice of C). Baseline coverage could be broader. The “K-times sample efficiency” statement for pretraining is intuitive but not rigorously validated.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (advisory, not central to the decision)
- Provide quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., mutual information achieved, state coverage entropy, skill confusion rates) in addition to visitation plots.
- Detail sensitivity to K, αH, and grid resolution for p̂(z|c). Include failure/saturation regimes.
- Clarify the bilinear integration implementation (parameterization and computational cost vs concatenation).
- Report wall-clock and environment-step budgets for pretraining vs multi-policy baselines to support sample-efficiency claims.
- Expand baselines where feasible (e.g., option-critic or other unsupervised skill discovery methods) and include success-rate metrics for mazes.
- Discuss how to choose C (the state subset used in MI) for non-locomotion domains and provide at least a small manipulation or non-locomotion demonstration.
- Consider adding a simple termination policy or variable T experiment to show adaptability without relying on walls to reorient.
- Improve reference formatting.

Questions for the authors
1. Sensitivity: How do performance and learned diversity vary with K, αH, and the grid discretization for p̂(z|c)? Are there guidelines for choosing these hyperparameters across domains?
2. MI target choice: How would you select C in manipulation or sensory-rich tasks where CoM is not informative? Have you tried learning C or using learned embeddings?
3. Bilinear integration: What is the exact form and size of the bilinear embedding with one-hot latents? How much does it increase parameters and does it affect stability/training time?
4. Pretraining efficiency: Can you quantify the pretraining sample and wall-clock savings of SNN vs training K independent policies, including convergence behavior and stability?
5. Manager training: How sensitive is downstream performance to T across more dynamic tasks? Have you tried a learned termination policy or variable T?
6. Robustness: For unstable agents (Ant), did you try (a) warm-starting the next skill with a brief overlap/blend, (b) learning transition-specific adapters, or (c) joint fine-tuning of low-level and manager?
7. Generalization: Do skills transfer across materially different downstream tasks or environments (e.g., altered dynamics, obstacles not seen in pretraining)?
8. Theory: Can you provide any bounds or analysis (even simplified) on when MI regularization avoids mode collapse and promotes coverage without sacrificing control performance during pretraining?
