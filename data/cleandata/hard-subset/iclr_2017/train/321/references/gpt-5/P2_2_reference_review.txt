Summary and contributions
The paper tackles exploration and sample-efficiency in sparse-reward reinforcement learning by learning reusable, temporally extended skills without task-specific supervision, and then composing them via a high-level manager to solve downstream tasks. The key ideas are:
- Pre-train a low-level policy as a stochastic neural network (SNN) with a discrete latent code z sampled once per rollout, so that each z induces a distinct “skill.”
- Encourage diversity and interpretability of skills via an information-theoretic regularizer: a mutual information (MI) bonus between z and a relevant, low-dimensional subset of state (e.g., CoM position). This is implemented by adding α log p̂(z|c) computed via discretized visitation counts.
- Use a “bilinear” integration of z with observations, shown to significantly help learn distinct modes versus simple concatenation.
- In downstream sparse tasks, freeze the low-level SNN, and train a high-level “manager” to select a z every T steps (Option-like), using standard TRPO.
Empirical results on MuJoCo-style tasks (Swimmer, Snake) for Maze variants and Gather show: (i) the pre-trained SNN yields clearly distinguishable, interpretable skills that broaden exploration; (ii) the hierarchical policy learns faster and solves tasks that naïve exploration cannot; (iii) SNN-based skills are more sample-efficient and cover more behaviors than training multiple separate uni-modal policies; and (iv) MI regularization improves skill diversity and downstream performance (especially in Gather). Code and hyperparameters are provided; videos illustrate behavior; limitations on unstable agents (Ant) are discussed.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated and clear approach that unifies intrinsic-motivation skill discovery (via MI) with a practical, reusable skill representation (SNN with categorical latent) and a simple hierarchical controller. The method is simple, novel in its combination, and easy to reproduce.
- Empirical evidence supports the main claims: the approach consistently improves exploration and learning on sparse downstream tasks, yielding interpretable skills and better sample efficiency than reasonable baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper is well-positioned within HRL and exploration literature. It addresses a salient challenge—efficient exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon settings—and the need for reusable skills across tasks that share a common agent-space. The use of a single, minimal proxy reward in pre-training is attractive and realistic.
- Novelty: While related to options, policy sketches, and intrinsic motivation, the particular combination—SNN with discrete latent as skill index, bilinear integration to induce distinct modes, mutual-information bonus grounded in InfoGAN-style objectives, and a manager that commits for T steps—appears novel and effective. The “skill span” learned without task goals is a meaningful contribution.
- Methodological soundness: Treating z as part of the observation allows TRPO to be applied unmodified. Fixing z per rollout induces temporally coherent skills. The MI bonus (maximizing log p̂(z|c)) is a reasonable, low-variance proxy for encouraging disentangled, direction-specific skills and is easy to implement via discretized visitation counts. The arguments about sample efficiency (training one SNN vs K separate policies) are plausible and supported by equal sample budgets for SNN vs single policy.
- Empirical support: 
  - Skill diversity: Visitation plots show distinct behaviors across z; bilinear integration and MI bonus materially improve diversity and coverage.
  - Exploration: One-million-step rollouts under random high-level selection demonstrate markedly broader coverage than Gaussian action noise, and broader than a multi-policy baseline trained separately.
  - Downstream tasks: On four mazes and Gather, the hierarchical policy outperforms strong baselines (including adding the same proxy reward during downstream training). The SNN-based hierarchy generally beats the multi-policy baseline and benefits from MI. Variance across seeds is reported; no cherry-picking of pre-trained SNNs. Additional results with Snake validate scaling; analysis of switch time T shows robustness; Ant experiments candidly expose failure modes on unstable agents.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is clearly written, includes algorithmic details, hyperparameters, and code. Limitations and future directions are discussed.

Reservations and limitations
- Scope: Diversity is defined over CoM (x,y); this biases skills to directions of locomotion and may limit generality beyond locomotion-like domains. Evidence beyond locomotion (e.g., manipulation) would strengthen claims of broad applicability.
- Baselines: While multi-policy and intrinsic reward baselines are included (and VIME in the appendix), head-to-head comparisons with other HRL skill discovery frameworks (e.g., option-critic) are missing.
- Sensitivity and metrics: Diversity is primarily visual (visitation plots). Quantitative diversity metrics and sensitivity to binning resolution, α for MI, K, and bilinear vs concatenation would strengthen the case. The variance across seeds can be high in mazes, depending on the pre-trained skill span.
- Fixed skills and option length: Low-level freezing and fixed T simplify training but may underperform in more dynamic settings; end-to-end fine-tuning and learned termination are left to future work.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Add quantitative diversity measures (e.g., mutual information estimates, coverage metrics, pairwise trajectory dissimilarity) and ablations on MI coefficient, grid resolution, K, and integration type.
- Include comparisons to at least one contemporary HRL baseline (e.g., option-critic) on the same tasks, and report success rates/sample complexity.
- Report sensitivity and guidance for choosing T and α; summarize variance across seeds with confidence intervals and success counts.
- Discuss how to choose c (the state subset used in MI) in other domains; consider a learned discriminator p̂(z|c) (as suggested) to scale beyond 2D CoM.
- Explore partial fine-tuning of low-level skills or learned termination to mitigate instability and increase robustness, especially for Ant-like agents.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the discretization of CoM space and to α? Can you provide ablations and practical guidelines?
2) How do results vary with the number of skills K? Do redundant modes emerge, and how does the manager use them?
3) Can you report quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., MI estimates or coverage) and correlate them with downstream performance?
4) How does the method compare to option-critic or other HRL baselines on the same tasks (sample complexity and success rate)?
5) For domains where CoM is not meaningful (e.g., manipulation), what c would you choose, and have you tested this?
6) Does partial end-to-end fine-tuning (e.g., small learning rate on the SNN) help on unstable agents or reduce variance across seeds?
7) What is the training budget equivalence across SNN vs multi-policy baselines (wall-clock and environment steps)? Can you quantify the claimed K× sample-efficiency benefit?
