Summary and contributions
The paper tackles exploration and sample-efficiency in sparse-reward reinforcement learning by learning reusable, temporally extended skills in a pre-training environment and then composing them with a high-level policy for downstream tasks. The key ideas are: (1) use Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a discrete latent variable to represent a multi-modal, shared-parameter policy that encodes multiple skills; (2) encourage diversity and interpretability of skills via a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of state variables (e.g., CoM position); (3) integrate the latent variable with observations using bilinear interactions that effectively modulate first-layer weights; and (4) in downstream tasks, freeze the learned skills and train a high-level “manager” that selects a skill for fixed time windows, improving long-horizon exploration. The work includes careful ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear integration; with/without MI), extensive qualitative analyses (visitation plots, one-million-step rollouts), and quantitative results on sparse locomotion mazes and a gather task with Swimmer and Snake, plus discussion of failure modes on Ant. The approach is simple, well-engineered, and accompanied by code and videos.

Decision: Accept (clear empirical gains on sparse tasks with a simple, well-motivated hierarchical framework)
Key reasons: (1) Strong, targeted empirical evidence that the combination of SNN-based skills and MI regularization yields more diverse, interpretable skills and materially improves exploration and performance on sparse long-horizon tasks; (2) A clean, general framework that is easy to adopt (minimal instrumentation, weight sharing across skills, standard policy optimization), with ablations clarifying design choices (bilinear integration, MI).

Supporting arguments
- Q1: Problem tackled. The paper addresses how to efficiently solve multiple sparse-reward tasks that share an “agent space” by first learning a library of reusable skills with minimal domain supervision. This is a central and difficult question in HRL and exploration.

- Q2: Motivation and placement. The approach is well positioned relative to:
  - Options/HRL (Parr & Russell; Sutton et al.; Option-Critic): learns reusable skills without dense external rewards; contrasts with option-critic’s end-to-end learning by pretraining with a simple proxy reward and enforcing diversity via MI.
  - Intrinsic motivation (e.g., VIME, count-based): differs by producing transferable skills, not just per-task exploration bonuses.
  - Prior pretraining frameworks (Heess et al., GPS-based skill extraction): requires less instrumentation (no goal set, no demos), and emphasizes multi-modality via SNNs with weight sharing.
The choice of SNNs is appropriate: they compactly encode multi-modal behaviors and allow skill reuse with shared parameters. The MI regularizer is natural (InfoGAN-inspired), and the bilinear integration is a pragmatic design to increase expressivity.

- Q3: Evidence and rigor. The claims are supported by:
  - Skill diversity: convincing visitation plots across seeds; ablations showing bilinear integration and MI both matter; interpretability of skills per latent code.
  - Exploration: one-million-step rollouts demonstrating dramatic coverage gains from hierarchical execution compared to Gaussian action noise or independent multi-policy baselines.
  - Downstream performance: learning curves on several mazes and gather tasks where standard RL fails; comparison against an intrinsic CoM-speed baseline; additional results in benchmark settings vs. VIME; scaling to Snake; analysis of switch-time sensitivity; discussion of failure modes on Ant.
  - Reproducibility: code and hyperparameters are provided.
Caveats: Evaluation focuses on locomotion/navigation; the MI estimation uses coarse discretization, and there is sensitivity to αH and K. Statistical reporting is generally good (multiple seeds), but more formal significance tests would strengthen claims.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Experimental breadth and baselines:
  - Include head-to-head comparisons with option-critic and macro-action methods (e.g., STRAW), ideally in identical sparse settings. This would better position the method within HRL.
  - Evaluate on at least one non-navigation domain (e.g., simple manipulation) to test the generality of proxy rewards and the Sagent/Srest factorization.
  - Report sensitivity to K (number of skills) and to the bilinear embedding dimension, and show how manager performance scales with K.

- MI estimation and analysis:
  - Detail how zero counts and smoothing are handled in p̂(Z|c); analyze the impact of grid resolution on training stability and diversity; consider continuous estimators (e.g., parametric classifiers) for higher-dimensional c.
  - Provide quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., Jensen-Shannon divergence over occupancy measures per skill) alongside visitation plots.

- Design choices:
  - Discuss when MI is most beneficial downstream (e.g., gather shows clearer benefits than mazes) and how to pick αH systematically.
  - Explore variable-duration skills via a learned termination policy or conditioning on desired duration; add an ablation on switch time T in all reported tasks (main text, not just appendix).
  - Consider limited end-to-end fine-tuning of low-level skills with straight-through estimators to mitigate the fixed-skill limitation, especially for unstable agents like Ant.

- Theory/justification:
  - While the empirical story is compelling, a brief theoretical perspective on why maximizing I(Z;C) with a domain-relevant C should diversify controllable modes (and its limits) would add depth.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to K? Is there a point where too many latent codes dilute training signal and reduce per-skill quality? Any automatic pruning or merging of redundant skills?
2) How did you choose αH and the CoM grid resolution? Did you try learned discriminators for p̂(Z|c) (e.g., a small classifier), and if so, how did it compare to discretization?
3) How robust is the method to the choice of C? For instance, if CoM coordinates are replaced or augmented with heading or velocity, does skill diversity and downstream performance change?
4) In the manager, did you try learning a termination policy (variable T)? For which tasks did variable durations help or hurt?
5) Could you report manager sample complexity versus training multiple independent policies (Multi-policy) more systematically, including wall-clock and environment steps?
6) For Ant, did you try partial fine-tuning of low-level skills or a “handover” curriculum to reduce falls at switches?
7) Beyond locomotion, what proxy rewards do you expect to work for manipulation tasks, and how would you select C there?

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a clear idea, careful ablations, and tangible gains on challenging sparse tasks. I recommend acceptance.
