1) Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a two-phase framework for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings. In pretraining, a single policy conditioned on a discrete latent variable (termed an SNN) is trained with a proxy reward (e.g., speed magnitude) to induce a set of skills. To prevent skill collapse and encourage diversity, they introduce a mutual information bonus between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM (x, y)), estimated via discretized counts. They also propose bilinear integration of latent codes and observations.
- In downstream tasks, they freeze the learned skills and train a high-level “Manager” policy that selects a discrete skill for T steps, improving exploration and sample efficiency in sparse tasks. TRPO is used throughout.
- Empirically, on Swimmer/Snake variants of Maze and Gather, they show: (i) SNNs with bilinear integration and MI produce more diverse, interpretable locomotion skills than concatenation; (ii) the hierarchical manager over these skills explores better than Gaussian noise or a simple proxy-reward baseline; (iii) they can solve long-horizon sparse tasks that standard methods fail to solve. Code and some ablations are provided.

2) Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Limited novelty and unclear methodological advantage relative to established hierarchical/skill-discovery frameworks; the core is a policy conditioned on a discrete latent, plus an MI-style diversity term akin to InfoGAN, and a standard manager that switches skills for T steps.
- Insufficient empirical validation against strong, established baselines and under matched budgets; evidence is largely limited to simple locomotion environments (Swimmer/Snake), with high variance and known failure on more challenging agents (Ant).

3) Supporting arguments
- Positioning and novelty: The paper’s core components—pretraining skills with a proxy reward, conditioning on discrete latents, and a manager selecting skills—are closely related to options/skills literature (e.g., HiREPS, option-critic) and to contemporaneous work on goal-conditioned pretraining. The MI regularizer is very similar in spirit to InfoGAN-style objectives. The contribution reads as a practical combination rather than a clearly new principle.
- Empirical scope and baselines:
  - Most results are on Swimmer/Snake mazes and Gather; these are relatively toy tasks with state decompositions that favor the proposed MI on CoM position. The demonstrated transfer is narrow (locomotion directionality) and the MI choice is tightly coupled to locomotion; claims of generality (e.g., manipulation) are untested.
  - Comparisons to strong hierarchical or intrinsic exploration baselines (e.g., option-critic, HiREPS, goal-conditioned pretraining) are missing. VIME is only compared in a limited setting (Appendix, short horizon), while the main claims rely on longer horizons where VIME results are not shown.
  - The multi-policy baseline is data-inefficient by construction (6× pretrain data). A fair, compute-matched comparison against other multi-modal policy parameterizations (e.g., mixture policies with shared backbones) is absent.
- Claims vs evidence:
  - The “K× sample efficiency” claim for learning K skills in one SNN is asserted but not rigorously substantiated. K is fixed at 6; scaling experiments, compute/time costs, and convergence behavior as K varies are not reported.
  - The MI estimation relies on discretized CoM counts; sensitivity to grid resolution, estimator bias/variance, and whether diversity persists beyond directional coverage is not thoroughly analyzed.
  - The approach exhibits high variance across seeds; in mazes, some pretrained SNNs lack the motion needed to solve the task (0 reward). This undermines robustness claims. The Ant results highlight instability in switching, limiting applicability to harder agents.
- Methodological details:
  - The decision to freeze skills and hold T fixed simplifies training but may handicap performance; however, the paper does not provide end-to-end fine-tuning results or learned termination, nor a systematic study showing freezing/T are near-optimal.
  - The bilinear integration ablation is helpful, but a deeper analysis of why/when it helps, and alternatives (e.g., FiLM-like conditioning) is absent.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines and fairness:
  - Include option-critic, HiREPS, and a goal-conditioned pretraining baseline (e.g., sampling random goals in pretrain as in Heess et al., 2016), as well as intrinsic motivation methods on the long-horizon settings (not just short-horizon VIME).
  - Add a mixture-of-experts or mixture-of-Gaussians policy with shared backbone trained end-to-end as a direct comparator to the SNN latent conditioning.
  - Report all results under matched environment steps and compute budgets.
- Robustness and scaling:
  - Vary K and report training stability, sample cost, diversity metrics, and downstream performance vs K.
  - Provide quantitative diversity measures (e.g., MI estimates, state coverage, pairwise trajectory divergences) beyond visitation plots.
  - Extend to more challenging agents (e.g., Ant) and show mitigation strategies (e.g., learned transition policies, warm-starting new skills from current states, end-to-end fine-tuning, learned termination). Demonstrate robustness across many seeds.
- Clarify MI estimation:
  - Analyze sensitivity to discretization granularity and the choice of state subspace C. Show whether a learned density model for p(z|c) changes outcomes.
  - Demonstrate generality by using different C for different domains (e.g., orientation, end-effector pose in manipulation) and test transfer beyond locomotion.
- Hierarchy design:
  - Compare fixed T vs learned termination; include end-to-end fine-tuning vs frozen skills, and report impacts.
  - Provide manager behavior analysis: skill selection distributions, switching patterns, and their relation to task progress.
- Reporting:
  - Precisely report sample budgets for pretraining and downstream training per method.
  - Clarify how many seeds per setting and provide success rates and confidence intervals.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide matched-budget comparisons to option-critic, HiREPS, and goal-conditioned pretraining on the same long-horizon tasks? How does your method fare then?
- How does performance and training stability scale with K? Is “K×” sample efficiency still observed empirically when K increases beyond 6?
- How sensitive are results to the MI discretization grid and to the choice of C? Have you tried learned density models for p(z|c)? Do skills remain diverse if C is not CoM?
- Can you show long-horizon VIME (and other intrinsic motivation) baselines on the 5,000-step Gather and mazes?
- What is the compute/time overhead of SNN training vs a single policy? Please report wall-clock and gradient steps for all methods.
- Do end-to-end fine-tuning of the low-level skills and learning terminations materially improve performance or robustness (especially on Ant)?
- How reproducible are the results across seeds when using stronger regularization or diversity constraints? Can you reduce the variance without cherry-picking pretrained skills?
