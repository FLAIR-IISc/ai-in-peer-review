Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a simple and broadly applicable framework for learning reusable, diverse skills via a single stochastic policy, and then composing those skills with a high-level controller to solve sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks. The key ideas are:
- Use a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy with a categorical latent variable to induce multi-modality (i.e., multiple skills) with shared weights. A bilinear integration of observations and latent code is found to be critical.
- Encourage distinct, interpretable skills via a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of the agent state (e.g., CoM (x,y) position), estimated via simple discretization and counts.
- Freeze the learned low-level skills and train a high-level “Manager” that selects a latent z every T steps to drive exploration and solve downstream sparse tasks.

Empirically, the approach learns diverse locomotion “gaits” that are interpretable by latent code, greatly increases exploration, and enables faster learning on sparse Maze and Gather tasks compared to baselines (including adding the proxy reward at train time). The paper includes ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, MI strength), analysis of switch time, demonstrations on Swimmer and Snake, partial results on Ant, and provides code and videos.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Novelty and conceptual contribution: Learning a suite of distinct, reusable skills within a single policy via an SNN with a categorical latent, combined with an MI regularizer on behavior, is a clean and bold idea that directly targets exploration and reusability in sparse RL. The bilinear integration detail is insightful and practically impactful.
2) Empirical evidence that the learned skill span meaningfully improves exploration and enables solving sparse tasks that are hard for standard methods, with reasonable ablations and qualitative interpretability.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles a salient and difficult problem—solving families of sparse tasks by acquiring broadly useful skills in a task-agnostic pre-training phase. The framing (shared agent-state, task-specific rest-state) is sensible and practical. The approach is well situated with respect to HRL, options, intrinsic motivation, and skill discovery; the use of InfoGAN-style MI to induce interpretable, behaviorally distinct skills is apt.
- Method clarity: The SNN formulation is straightforward, requiring only replacing a unimodal policy with a policy conditioned on a discrete latent, and treating z as part of the observation for TRPO. The MI regularizer is simple to implement (count-based posterior over a discretized subset of state), and the hierarchical manager is standard. The paper is clear about the commitment time T and freezing low-level policies.
- Empirical support: 
  - Visitation maps convincingly show that the SNN (especially with bilinear integration) yields distinct, temporally consistent skills; MI further de-overlaps them and induces more varied motions.
  - One-million-step rollouts under random managers demonstrate dramatically improved coverage versus Gaussian noise or multi-policy baselines, supporting the exploration claim.
  - On multiple mazes and Gather, the hierarchical approach outperforms a strong proxy-reward baseline; ablations indicate bilinear integration generally helps, and MI helps more on tasks requiring precise navigation (Gather).
  - Analysis of switch times suggests robustness across a range of T; the variance analysis across pretrained seeds is honest and informative.
- Scope and limitations: The approach is mainly validated on locomotion; Ant reveals a failure mode for unstable agents when switching. The authors acknowledge and discuss future directions (learned termination, end-to-end fine-tuning, recurrent manager), aligning with my tolerance for minor imperfections given the core novelty.

Areas for improvement (not decisive for acceptance)
- Generality beyond locomotion: The proxy reward and MI target (CoM position) are tailored to navigation. While the framework is claimed to be general (e.g., manipulation), an additional non-locomotion domain would strengthen the case or, at minimum, justify the choice of state subset for MI more formally.
- Comparative baselines: Include stronger HRL/skill-learning baselines where feasible (e.g., option-critic, HiREPS-style mixtures) and report sample budgets carefully to ensure fairness with multi-policy baselines. Some statistical tests or confidence intervals would help.
- Quantitative diversity metrics: Beyond visitation plots, provide numerical diversity measures (e.g., pairwise trajectory divergence, MI estimates, skill confusion) to quantify “span of skills” and avoid cherry-picking.
- Skill collapse analysis: Formalize how often collapse occurs across seeds and K; sensitivity to K and MI coefficient αH.
- Stability and switching: Report a metric for failure-on-switch (especially for Ant), and explore simple mitigations like warm-start transitions or transition policies.
- Clarity and polish: Fix minor typos/formatting in references and text; clarify some implementation details (e.g., how bilinear integration is parameterized).

Questions for the authors
1) Sensitivity: How does performance vary with the number of skills K, the MI weight αH, and the CoM grid resolution? Is there a regime where MI harms control quality?
2) State subset for MI: How do you choose the subset of state for MI in a general setting? Have you tried alternatives (e.g., end-effector for manipulation, full agent state via a learned discriminator) or continuous MI estimators?
3) Sample efficiency: What are the wall-clock and environment-step budgets across pretrain and downstream phases? How does training a single SNN compare to training K separate policies when K grows?
4) Switching policies: Did you try simple transition smoothing (e.g., action blending for a few steps) or a learned transition policy to mitigate instability (especially on Ant)?
5) Baselines: Can you report comparisons to option-critic or a contemporaneous mixture-of-experts low-level controller with a manager? If not feasible, please discuss expected differences.
6) Transfer breadth: Have you attempted tasks where Srest changes more drastically (e.g., different terrain or sensors) to validate the “agent-state shared” assumption? How robust are the learned skills to such shifts?

Overall, this paper introduces a compelling, simple, and novel route to skill learning and reuse via SNNs + MI, with clear evidence of impact on exploration and sparse-task performance. I recommend acceptance.
