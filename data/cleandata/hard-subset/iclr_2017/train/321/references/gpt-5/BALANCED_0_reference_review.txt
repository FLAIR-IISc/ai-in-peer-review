Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles sparse-reward RL by learning a reusable repertoire of temporally extended skills in a proxy/pretraining environment, then training a high-level “manager” to select among these skills on downstream tasks.
- Skills are represented by a stochastic neural network (SNN) policy conditioned on a discrete latent code; this yields a compact, weight-shared, multi-modal low-level controller.
- To promote diversity and interpretability of skills, the authors add a mutual-information (MI)–based bonus between the latent code and a subset of state features (e.g., CoM (x, y) for locomotion), implemented via a simple count-based posterior estimate on a discretized grid.
- A hierarchical controller freezes the low-level SNN and trains a manager to pick a latent code every T steps, improving exploration in long-horizon sparse tasks.
- Empirical results on locomotion agents (Swimmer, Snake; partial analysis on Ant) in Maze and Gather tasks show: (a) SNN+MI learns more diverse skills than training multiple separate policies, (b) hierarchical use of the skills substantially expands state-space coverage, and (c) downstream learning improves versus intrinsic-reward baselines. Ablations analyze the effect of bilinear vs concatenation integration and the MI coefficient; additional analyses consider switch time T and failure cases on Ant. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Empirical evaluation is limited and comparisons to strong hierarchical/option-learning baselines are insufficient; performance gains are modest or high-variance on several tasks. (2) The MI regularizer and its implementation are ad hoc and task-specific (CoM grid), with limited evidence of generality or robustness.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and motivation: Addressing exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon control is important and well motivated, and the idea of amortizing pretraining across tasks is appealing. The decomposition into agent-specific vs task-specific state is plausible in robotics domains.
- Novelty and positioning: Using an SNN policy conditioned on discrete latents to represent multiple skills concurrently, paired with an MI-style diversity bonus, is a sensible combination of known ingredients (latent-conditioned policies, InfoGAN-style MI, skill discovery). Related work (HiREPS, options, option-critic, macro-actions, intrinsic motivation) is discussed, but the paper would benefit from deeper contrasts to option-learning baselines and macro-action learning driven by external reward.
- Methodological soundness: The SNN architecture and the “treat z as observation in TRPO” trick are reasonable. The bilinear integration choice is empirically motivated. However:
  - The MI objective optimizes I(Z;C) for C = CoM (x, y) only, with a coarse discretization and count-based posterior. This biases skill diversity to locomotion direction and may not transfer to other domains where relevant features are not obvious or low-dimensional.
  - No quantitative measure of MI or skill diversity is reported—results rely on visitation plots and qualitative interpretations. It is hard to assess how reliably the objective achieves its intended effect across seeds and tasks.
  - The manager commits to a fixed T and the low-level skills are frozen; while the paper acknowledges this and shows some T sensitivity analysis, the setup limits adaptability and may hinder performance on dynamic tasks.
- Empirical support: 
  - The main results focus on Swimmer/Snake in custom mazes and a Gather variant. Baselines include “proxy reward in downstream” and a “multi-policy” hierarchy that is sample-intensive to pretrain. Comparisons to option-critic or other hierarchical methods are not provided; VIME comparisons are relegated to the appendix and in a setting where the downstream proxy reward baseline is strong.
  - Reported gains vary: SNN with bilinear integration often helps; MI sometimes helps (e.g., Gather) but not consistently on mazes; variance across runs is substantial and some pretrained SNNs fail to solve tasks at all.
  - Ant results reveal instability during switching (falling), highlighting that the approach struggles for more challenging agents unless additional mechanisms are introduced.
- Claims vs evidence: The claim that SNNs deliver K-fold sample efficiency for learning K skills is plausible for pretraining but does not account for downstream amortization and variance in utility across tasks. The broader claim of uniformly reducing sample complexity across a “wide collection of tasks” is only partially supported on relatively simple locomotion tasks with shared morphology and state.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affected the decision)
- Report quantitative metrics of skill diversity: estimated MI, state-space coverage, pairwise skill distinguishability, or goal-conditioned success with fixed latents.
- Provide stronger and more direct baselines: option-critic with learned termination, HiREPS-style mixtures, macro-action RL, and hierarchical methods trained end-to-end. At minimum, include option-critic on the same tasks.
- Clarify sample accounting: show total environment steps including pretraining, and amortized cost across multiple downstream tasks to substantiate the “uniform reduction” claim.
- Generality of MI target: discuss/experiment with different C choices (e.g., end-effector pose for manipulation) and non-gridded density models; include sensitivity to grid resolution and αH.
- Analyze K (number of skills): effect on diversity, manager learning, and downstream performance; any mechanism for pruning or merging redundant skills?
- Consider simple termination mechanisms or state-dependent switching; show whether learned termination improves performance or stability (especially for unstable agents like Ant).
- Provide more stable training or regularization for switching on unstable morphologies (e.g., warm-starting skills near switching states, transition policies).
- Minor: clean up references (several entries look malformed), define all hyperparameters and network details in the main text or a consolidated appendix.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide quantitative MI estimates or diversity metrics during pretraining, and correlate them with downstream performance?
2. How sensitive are results to the choice of the state subset C and its discretization? Have you tried learned density models for p(Z|C) or continuous MI estimators?
3. What is the end-to-end sample budget, including pretraining, when solving multiple downstream tasks? Does amortization provide a net gain over training from scratch per task with strong exploration bonuses?
4. How does your approach compare experimentally to option-critic (with learned terminations) on the same mazes/Gather tasks?
5. What is the effect of varying K on both pretraining stability and downstream manager learning? Can the manager effectively ignore redundant skills?
6. Have you tried end-to-end fine-tuning (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) after initializing with frozen skills? Does this alleviate Ant instability or improve maze performance?
7. For Ant, did you evaluate state-dependent switching intervals or transition policies to reduce falls? Any empirical evidence these help?
8. Beyond locomotion, can you demonstrate the method on a manipulation pretrain (e.g., reach/grasp proxy) and transfer to sparse downstream tasks to support generality claims?
