1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes a simple, general framework for unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical reinforcement learning aimed at sparse-reward tasks. The key idea is to pretrain a single low-level policy as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with a discrete latent code, so that different latent values induce distinct skills while sharing parameters. To encourage diversity and interpretability of skills, the authors add a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent variable and a subset of state features (e.g., the center-of-mass (CoM) position), estimated via discretized visitation counts. A bilinear integration of observations and latent code is used to enrich expressivity. In downstream tasks, they freeze the low-level SNN and train a high-level “Manager” that selects a skill every T steps, which improves exploration and reduces sample complexity. The paper presents empirical results on Swimmer and Snake in maze navigation and gather tasks with sparse rewards, ablations on latent integration (concatenation vs bilinear), MI bonus strength, and switching period T, along with analyses of skill diversity via visitation maps. Code and videos are provided.

2) Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- A practical, well-motivated, and surprisingly effective approach to unsupervised skill discovery that cleanly combines SNNs and an MI bonus to produce diverse, temporally coherent skills.
- Empirical evidence shows clear exploration and performance gains on sparse tasks, with careful ablations (integration, MI, T), and reproducibility via open-source code.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper addresses exploration in long-horizon, sparse-reward RL by (i) learning a repertoire of reusable skills in a pretraining environment with minimal domain knowledge, and (ii) leveraging these skills hierarchically to shrink effective horizon and improve exploration.

Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature? Yes. The motivation to learn diverse, task-agnostic skills is compelling. The SNN with discrete latent code provides a simple mechanism for multi-modality with weight sharing. The MI regularizer is inspired by InfoGAN-style objectives and prior work on encouraging diverse options. Related work on options/skill discovery (HiREPS, Option-Critic), intrinsic motivation (e.g., VIME), and goal-conditioned pretraining (Heess et al.) are discussed. The paper’s focus on minimal instrumentation (a simple proxy reward) and explicit skill diversity control is a useful angle relative to goal-conditioned pretraining.

Do the results support the claims? Largely, yes. The paper:
- Demonstrates that bilinear integration and MI bonus reliably yield distinct, interpretable skills (visitation plots; forward/backward/sideways motions).
- Shows that a high-level manager over these skills substantially expands exploration versus Gaussian action noise or a “multi-policy” baseline trained separately per skill (more sample use).
- Provides learning curves on multiple sparse mazes and gather tasks where non-hierarchical baselines struggle; shows ablations on MI and switch time T; examines transfer variability across pretrained SNNs; and discusses failure modes on Ant.
- Uses multiple seeds and multiple pretrained SNNs, avoiding cherry-picking, with code released.

That said, claims around sample efficiency (e.g., “K times better”) are intuitive but not rigorously quantified; the scope is limited to locomotion domains; and some baselines (e.g., Option-Critic, other hierarchical methods) are missing. MI estimation is simple and task-specific (CoM discretization). Nonetheless, within the chosen scope, the empirical evidence is convincing.

4) Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper)
- Stronger baselines: Include comparisons to Option-Critic (and/or HIRO-style hierarchical methods if contemporaneous) and goal-conditioned policies for downstream tasks, to position the gains relative to other hierarchical approaches, not only intrinsic motivation.
- Quantify sample efficiency: Empirically report total environment steps for pretraining + downstream, and compare to training K independent policies and to a non-hierarchical baseline on downstream tasks. This can substantiate the “K-times” efficiency claim.
- Generality of MI design: The MI bonus relies on CoM (x,y) and discretization. Discuss alternative, task-agnostic state subsets and scalable estimators (e.g., learned posteriors or density models) to handle higher-dimensional agents and non-locomotion domains. Some empirical sensitivity analysis of grid resolution and α_H would help.
- Skill quality and coverage: Provide simple quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise trajectory divergence or coverage metrics) in addition to visitation plots. Analyze sensitivity to K and the learned skill occupancy under the manager to understand redundancy vs complementarity.
- Manager design: While fixed switching works here, discuss or try a learned termination mechanism, and report whether shorter T helps in more dynamic tasks. A small experiment with end-to-end fine-tuning (e.g., straight-through estimators) could strengthen the case for adaptability.
- Broader domains: Even a small manipulation proxy task (as suggested in the text) would help demonstrate generality beyond locomotion.
- Ant instability: Consider pretraining with a mixture-of-terminations or “warm start” transition policy, or adding regularization that aligns state distributions across skills near switching points. Reporting partial attempts would be informative.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the MI coefficient α_H and the grid resolution? Can you provide a small hyperparameter sweep and guidance for practitioners?
- How do you choose K? Does performance saturate or degrade when K is too large (e.g., due to redundancy or manager confusion)?
- Can you quantify pretraining time/steps for SNN vs K separately trained policies, and total end-to-end samples per downstream task?
- For MI estimation, did you try learned posteriors (e.g., an auxiliary classifier on continuous C)? How does this affect stability and diversity?
- How often does SNN collapse to a few effective modes without MI, and does bilinear integration alone suffice in some settings?
- In downstream training, what fraction of time does the manager select each skill? Do certain skills dominate, and does this correlate with pretraining visitation?
- Could the manager benefit from recurrence to leverage information within a commitment window? Any preliminary results?
- How robust are results to using PPO or off-policy algorithms (e.g., SAC) for either stage?

Overall, the paper offers a clear, practical approach to unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical control with solid empirical support and good ablations. With stronger comparisons and some quantification of sample efficiency and MI sensitivity, it would be even more compelling.
