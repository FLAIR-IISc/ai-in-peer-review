Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles exploration and sample-efficiency in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by learning a reusable skill set in a pre-training environment using only a simple proxy reward. It then composes these skills via a high-level “manager” for downstream tasks.
- Skills are represented by a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy conditioned on a categorical latent code. A mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position) encourages diverse, interpretable behaviors. A bilinear observation–latent integration improves expressivity over simple concatenation.
- The methodology: (i) pre-train the SNN policy with an MI bonus and proxy reward, sampling a latent per rollout; (ii) freeze skills; (iii) train a high-level policy that selects a latent for T steps to solve sparse-reward tasks.
- Experiments on MuJoCo locomotion (primarily Swimmer; with Snake and Ant in the appendix) show: (a) SNN+MI yields broader, more differentiated skills than either separate unimodal policies or SNN w/o MI; (b) hierarchical composition substantially broadens exploration; (c) on sparse mazes and gather tasks, the hierarchical manager over pre-trained SNN skills learns faster and more reliably than baselines that use intrinsic velocity rewards at training time. Ablations on integration type, MI strength, and switch time T are provided. Code and videos are released.

Decision: Accept (weak)
Key reasons: (1) A simple, well-motivated, and practical approach to unsupervised skill discovery that demonstrably enhances exploration and performance on sparse tasks, with careful ablations. (2) The combination of SNN conditioning, bilinear integration, and MI regularization is novel in this context and produces interpretable, reusable skills with minimal domain knowledge.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks remain challenging; reducing search via reusable skills is compelling. The assumption of shared agent dynamics across tasks is realistic in many robotics settings.
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well positioned relative to HRL (options/MAXQ), intrinsic motivation (VIME, count-based), and recent pretraining work. The MI objective is clearly motivated by InfoGAN-like disentanglement and interpretability. Compared to methods requiring goal labels or demonstrations, the proxy-reward-only pretraining is appealing.
- Method soundness: Using SNNs to induce multi-modality and parameter sharing is sensible; treating the discrete latent as input keeps TRPO applicable. The MI bonus via p(z|c) encourages separable behaviors; discretized counting is a simple, stable estimator. The bilinear integration’s effect is convincingly demonstrated.
- Empirical validation: 
  - Skill learning: visitation plots show diverse, interpretable skills; MI strength and bilinear integration matter.
  - Exploration: million-step rollouts with uniform manager selections indicate much broader state coverage than Gaussian noise or a multi-policy ensemble of independently trained controllers.
  - Downstream tasks: On multiple mazes and gather, the hierarchical SNN manager outperforms baselines, including adding the proxy reward to the downstream task. The variability across pre-trained SNNs is reported (10 runs per curve), and the high-level ablations (T, MI) provide insight.
- Limitations: 
  - Scope: Main gains are on relatively simple locomotion with Swimmer/Snake. Ant results show difficulties with instability when switching skills. This limits claims about robustness.
  - Baselines: While comparisons to intrinsic motivation baselines are provided, head-to-head against other HRL/option methods (e.g., option-critic) are not shown. The “multi-policy” baseline is strong but more expensive; still, broader comparisons would strengthen the case.
  - Assumptions: The factorization Sagent/Srest and access to CoM (or similar) for MI are reasonable for robotics but may not generalize to raw-pixel settings; discussion on how to implement MI with high-dimensional observations is brief.
  - Analysis: Claims about sample-efficiency improvements (∼K for pretraining) are plausible but not rigorously quantified; sensitivity to K and αH is partially explored but could be deeper.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify robustness of the MI estimator: provide sensitivity plots for grid resolution, αH, and batch size; report whether using learned density models (MLP) changes stability or performance.
- Elaborate on generalization: demonstrate transfer to tasks that differ more substantially from pretraining (e.g., with dynamic obstacles, goals requiring sustained turning) to show the value of sideways/turning skills, or add a task where the MI-induced diversity is critical.
- Strengthen baselines: include an HRL baseline (e.g., option-critic) and a macro-action approach learned with external rewards (e.g., Mnih et al. 2016) to contextualize gains on sparse tasks.
- Address instability: briefly explore two mitigations for unstable agents (Ant): (i) warm-start skill transitions (e.g., a transition policy or blending), (ii) train with a small probability of random “perturbation transitions” during pretraining to improve cross-skill compatibility.
- Discuss observation-only settings: outline how to compute MI with high-dimensional observations—e.g., contrastive estimators, mutual information neural estimators—and whether using an encoder bottleneck (learned c) works in practice.
- Learning termination: while fixed T works here, a learned termination policy could help on Ant and dynamic tasks; a small experiment or a pointer to feasibility would be valuable.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the number of skills K? Is there an empirical “sweet spot,” and what are the failure modes when K is too small/large?
2. How does the MI estimator’s discretization resolution affect skill diversity and downstream performance? Did you try learned density models for p(z|c)? Any instability?
3. Can the manager benefit from a short fine-tuning of the low-level SNN (e.g., via straight-through Gumbel-Softmax) without losing skill interpretability? Any preliminary results?
4. For Ant, did you attempt transition smoothing (e.g., short action interpolation or “handover” phases) or curriculum on T to mitigate falls? How much does this help?
5. How would you operationalize the proxy reward and MI variable c in non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation from pixels)? Any preliminary experiments or guidance?
6. What is the sample budget breakdown across pretraining and downstream training? Can you quantify the net improvement versus training from scratch across a suite of tasks?
7. Does the manager rely primarily on Srest inputs? If not, would restricting it to task-specific observations improve transferability across tasks with varying Srest?
