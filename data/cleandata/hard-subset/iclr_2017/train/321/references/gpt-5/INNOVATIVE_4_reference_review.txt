Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a simple and compelling framework for learning reusable, temporally extended skills for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings. The key idea is to pretrain a latent-conditioned, multimodal low-level policy using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with categorical latent variables, and to encourage diversity among skills via an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between the latent and a small subset of the state (e.g., CoM position). A bilinear integration of the latent with observations improves expressivity over naïve concatenation. Skills are learned with only a proxy reward (e.g., speed magnitude), independent of downstream tasks. Downstream, a high-level “Manager” selects a skill every T steps (skills are frozen), dramatically improving exploration and sample efficiency relative to naïve exploration. The method uses TRPO throughout, treating the latent as part of the observation to avoid mixture-of-Gaussians complications. Experiments on Swimmer (multiple mazes, Gather) and Snake demonstrate: (i) interpretable, diverse skills, (ii) substantially improved exploration coverage, and (iii) strong performance on sparse long-horizon tasks; with discussion of limitations on Ant. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Novelty and clarity of the core idea: MI-regularized SNNs with bilinear latent integration to learn a span of unsupervised skills via a single proxy reward, then re-used hierarchically. This is a clean, bold approach that avoids goal instrumentation and heavy domain knowledge.
2) Empirical evidence that the learned skills materially enhance exploration and enable solving sparse tasks that standard deep RL cannot, with sensible ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, with/without MI, hierarchical with SNN vs multiple independently trained policies).

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: The paper tackles the difficult setting of sparse rewards and long horizons by reducing search complexity via skill-based temporal abstraction. Unlike prior work relying on hand-crafted options or goal-specified pretraining, this approach learns skills with minimal supervision and shares parameters across skills through SNNs. Compared to HiREPS/option-critic, the MI objective directly targets diversity/interpretablility, and compared to Heess et al. (2016), the need for pre-specified goals is removed.
- Methodological strengths:
  • SNNs provide a natural vehicle for multimodal policies with weight sharing; bilinear integration gives the latent meaningful control over the policy.
  • The MI bonus (I(Z;C)) is simple, tractable via discretized counts, and empirically prevents mode collapse and yields interpretable, directionally distinct locomotion skills.
  • The hierarchical manager is minimalistic (discrete selection, fixed commitment), yet effective; freezing skills simplifies training and improves stability.
  • Treating z as observation preserves the use of standard TRPO without approximation of mixture policies.
- Empirical support:
  • Pretraining: visitation plots convincingly show multi-directional, distinct gaits; MI and bilinear integration clearly increase diversity.
  • Exploration: one-million-step rollouts with random managers show vastly expanded coverage relative to Gaussian action noise, with skill switches producing coherent direction changes.
  • Downstream tasks: consistent gains over a strong intrinsic-motivation baseline across multiple mazes and Gather; SNN hierarchy generally outperforms multi-policy baseline despite far less pretraining data. Reported runs include multiple random seeds and pretrains (no cherry-picking), with discussion of variance sources.
- Scope and honesty: The paper openly discusses limitations (variance, fixed T, frozen skills, instability on Ant) and outlines plausible extensions (learned termination, end-to-end adaptation with straight-through estimators, recurrent managers).

Points to improve (helpful suggestions, not decision-critical)
- Quantification of sample efficiency: Provide explicit pretraining and downstream sample budgets for all methods, and cumulative sample complexity vs performance, to substantiate the claimed K-fold efficiency.
- Additional baselines: Include hierarchical baselines such as option-critic or goal-conditioned policies (where feasible) and report the Appendix comparison to VIME in the main paper.
- Diversity metrics: Complement visitation plots with quantitative measures (e.g., MI estimates, state coverage, skill confusion rates H(Z|C), pairwise trajectory divergence).
- Sensitivity and scaling: Analyze effects of K, grid resolution for MI estimation, and latent commit time T on both skill diversity and downstream performance; report failure/degeneracy modes of the MI objective (e.g., skills specializing on spatial partitioning rather than behavior).
- Generality: Discuss how to define C beyond CoM for non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulators), and whether heading/velocity features or learned discriminators improve skill separation.
- Ant results: Offer preliminary evidence of mitigations (e.g., short warmup blending between skills, termination policies, or low-level adaptation) to bridge the instability gap.

Questions for the authors
1) MI target: How sensitive is performance to the choice of C and to the discretization granularity? Did you try including heading or velocity in C, or learning p(Z|C) with a classifier rather than counts?
2) Number of skills: How do results vary with K? Do more skills saturate benefits or increase manager difficulty/variance?
3) Sample complexity: What are the exact environment step counts for pretraining vs downstream across methods (SNN hierarchy vs multi-policy vs intrinsic baseline)?
4) Manager training: Did you try end-to-end fine-tuning (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) or learning a termination policy? Any empirical evidence on benefits vs stability?
5) Transfer: How robust are pretrained skills across changes in dynamics or morphology (e.g., different masses/friction)? Any evidence of cross-task generalization beyond mazes/Gather?
6) Stability: On Ant, did you attempt techniques such as gradual skill interpolation, reset-to-skill-state datasets, or skill-conditioned value initialization to reduce post-switch failures?

Overall, this paper introduces a clear, novel direction—unsupervised skill learning via MI-regularized SNNs with minimal instrumentation—and demonstrates meaningful gains on challenging sparse tasks. Despite some limitations and missing baselines, the conceptual contribution and empirical evidence warrant acceptance.
