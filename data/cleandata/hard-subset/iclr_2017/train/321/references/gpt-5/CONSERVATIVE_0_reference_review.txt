Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings. In pre-training, a stochastic neural network (SNN) policy is conditioned on a discrete latent code sampled once per episode to induce multi-modal behaviors (“skills”). A mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of state variables (e.g., the agent’s CoM position) encourages diversity. In downstream tasks, a high-level “Manager” selects among the learned skills at a fixed temporal granularity T, keeping the low-level SNN fixed. The approach is implemented with TRPO, includes a bilinear integration of latent code and observation, and is evaluated on locomotion tasks (Swimmer, Snake) with mazes and a Gather task. The authors claim improved exploration, interpretable skills, and faster learning than baselines, with code and videos provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Limited novelty relative to established literature and reliance on a heuristic MI regularizer targeted to specific state components; the contribution is largely a combination of known ingredients (stochastic latent policies, MI-based skill induction, hierarchical selection) with modest architectural tweaks (bilinear integration).
2) Empirical validation is not sufficiently rigorous: baselines are weak or incomplete for HRL; results are mostly on relatively simple locomotion domains; gains are inconsistent and high variance; key claims (e.g., “K× sample efficiency”) are not substantiated.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and motivation: The idea of inducing diverse skills via latent variables and MI is well-established (e.g., InfoGAN-style objectives; contemporaneous/nearby HRL works use similar diversity bonuses). Conditioning the low-level policy on a latent code fixed per episode is closely related to options/mixture-of-experts. The paper’s proxy-reward pretraining is intuitive, but the MI uses a discretized CoM state subset, which bakes in domain knowledge and may bias skills toward spatial separation rather than generally useful behaviors.

- Novelty: The main technical elements (SNN with categorical code, bilinear integration, per-episode latent, MI bonus to encourage diversity, hierarchical manager) are incremental. The bilinear integration is an engineering choice; the MI computation via counts over a grid is a heuristic. There is no theoretical analysis of the MI objective, the induced option policies, or transfer/sample complexity.

- Claims vs evidence:
  • “Effectively K× better sample efficiency” in pretraining is not convincingly demonstrated. An SNN that shares parameters can sometimes train as fast as a single policy, but this depends on optimization difficulty; no wall-clock/sample complexity curves for K policies vs SNN are provided.
  • Diversity and interpretability are shown via visitation plots, but no quantitative diversity metrics or mutual information estimates are reported, nor robustness to grid resolution, αH, K, or latent cardinality.
  • Hierarchical benefits: In mazes, performance improvements appear sensitive to the particular pretrained SNN and sometimes fail (zero reward runs). For Gather, improvements are more consistent, but the main comparison is against a CoM intrinsic reward baseline and VIME (in appendix). Missing are stronger HRL baselines like Option-Critic and goal-conditioned or feudal/manager-worker methods. This makes it hard to attribute gains to the proposed SNN/MI vs generic temporal abstraction.
  • Generality: Results emphasize Swimmer and Snake; Ant is a failure case. The approach’s brittleness when switching skills on unstable systems is acknowledged but leaves open whether the core idea scales to more realistic agents/tasks.

- Experimental rigor:
  • High variance across seeds; some pretrained SNNs do not contain the motions needed to solve certain mazes, undermining the central promise of reusable, generally helpful skills.
  • The MI objective uses handpicked state coordinates (CoM) and discretization; no sensitivity analysis of grid density or alternative state subsets is provided.
  • Fixed commitment time T=500 is large; the limited analysis of T is in the appendix for Snake only; joint training of Manager and low-level is left as future work, despite being critical to robustness.
  • Evaluation predominantly relies on learned navigation by bumping/reorientation against walls; the claimed “interpretable skills” are not tied to measurable transfer across a diverse set of downstream tasks beyond locomotion/navigation.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Broaden baselines: include Option-Critic, FeUdal/manager-worker, goal-conditioned policies with hindsight relabeling, and other diversity-forcing skill discovery methods. Compare against learning multiple heads/options with shared trunk without MI. Evaluate pretraining via goal-reaching rewards to match Heess et al. (2016).
- Quantify diversity: report MI estimates, entropy of Z|C, state coverage metrics, pairwise trajectory divergence, and success rates across seeds. Provide sensitivity to K, αH, grid resolution, and latent parameterization (categorical vs continuous).
- Substantiate sample efficiency: plot learning curves (samples vs return) for training K separate policies vs a single SNN; include wall-clock.
- Strengthen downstream evaluation: add more challenging tasks (long-horizon navigation without wall-assisted reorientation, manipulation tasks), and test transfer to tasks with different sensory modalities or dynamics changes to validate the Sagent/Srest assumption.
- Address instability: incorporate termination policies, learned switching, or warm-starts for new skills; evaluate joint fine-tuning with straight-through estimators and report stability improvements.
- Clarify the MI estimator: present a principled lower bound (e.g., variational MI estimators) rather than grid counts; analyze bias/variance and computational overhead.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the choice of state subset C and the grid resolution used to estimate p(Z|C)? Have you tried learned discriminators or variational MI estimators?
2) Can you provide quantitative evidence for the “K× sample efficiency” claim in pretraining (SNN vs K independent policies), including convergence speed and final performance?
3) Why not compare against Option-Critic and other HRL baselines with similar temporal abstraction? How do your skills differ functionally from learned options?
4) How robust are results to K and αH? Is there a regime where too many skills or too strong MI harms downstream performance?
5) For Ant, did you attempt joint fine-tuning of low-level with the Manager or shorter commit times to mitigate falls? Any empirical results?
6) How does the method perform with high-dimensional observations (e.g., pixels) where C is not easily defined? Can the approach scale without manual selection of CoM-like variables?
7) Do skills learned in one morphology or dynamics variant transfer to another (e.g., mass/friction changes), supporting the Sagent/Srest factorization claim?
