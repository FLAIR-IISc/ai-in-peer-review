1. Summary and claimed contributions
The paper tackles exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL across a family of related tasks by (i) pretraining a diverse set of reusable low-level skills in a proxy environment and (ii) learning a high-level manager that switches among these skills to solve downstream tasks. The key technical pieces are:
- Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a categorical latent z to represent multi-modal policies (multiple skills) in one network with shared weights.
- A simple but effective bilinear integration of z with observations to increase expressivity relative to concatenation.
- An information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information I(Z; C) between the latent and a subset of state features C (e.g., the CoM (x,y)), implemented via a count-based estimate of p(z|c), to encourage diverse, interpretable skills.
- A hierarchical policy where a high-level manager selects a skill every T steps; low-level skills are frozen (no end-to-end fine-tuning).
Empirically, the method yields broad, interpretable skill repertoires, improved exploration, and substantial performance gains on sparse-reward benchmarks (Swimmer/Snake in Mazes and Gather), beating strong intrinsic-motivation baselines (e.g., CoM speed reward; VIME under the benchmark setting). The paper includes ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear; MI coefficient), sensitivity to the switch period T, variance across pretraining seeds, and a discussion of failure modes (Ant). Code and videos are provided.

2. Decision
Weak accept. The approach is simple, well-motivated, and produces convincing empirical gains in a challenging setting, with thoughtful analyses and ablations.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses efficient exploration for sparse rewards across multiple related tasks by reusing pretrained skills. This is a meaningful, important problem in RL, where naive exploration is known to fail on long-horizon tasks.
- Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature? Yes. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is natural; the MI regularizer is inspired by InfoGAN-like objectives; the method sits alongside prior HRL/skill discovery work (Options, Option-Critic, HiREPS) and closely relates to goal-conditioned pretraining (e.g., Heess et al. 2016) but removes the need for goal labels by using a minimal proxy reward. The paper articulates the structural assumption (shared agent-space) and why pretraining can reduce overall sample complexity.
- Do results support the claims? Largely yes:
  - Diversity: Visitation plots demonstrate that SNNs + bilinear + MI produce distinct, interpretable locomotion skills (forward/backward/sideways/turning) far more consistently than concatenation or no MI.
  - Exploration: One-million-step trajectories under a random manager show vastly broader coverage than Gaussian action noise and broader than multi-policy baselines trained separately.
  - Downstream performance: In multiple mazes and Gather, hierarchical policies trained on SNN skills outperform strong baselines (including the same proxy reward used directly in the task; and VIME under benchmark conditions). The paper reports 10 runs per curve (2 seeds × 5 pretrains) and explicitly avoids cherry-picking, which increases confidence.
  - Ablations and analyses: Choice of integration, MI strength, switch time T, and variance across different pretrained SNNs are examined. The Ant failure analysis is candid and informative.
Limitations temper the strength of the claim:
  - Generality: The proxy reward and MI target (CoM position) are hand-designed for locomotion/navigation; applicability to other domains (e.g., manipulation) is argued but not demonstrated.
  - Comparisons: No direct comparisons to other HRL skill-learning baselines (e.g., Option-Critic) in the same environments.
  - Freezing skills and fixed commitment T simplify training but may leave performance on the table.
  - The MI estimator is coarse (grid counts on C), and the choice of C and αH is task-specific; no quantitative MI measurements or robustness analysis to these design choices.
  - The stronger Ant setting exposes instability when switching skills.

Overall, the empirical evidence is solid for the stated domain; the ablations are thoughtful; the method is simple and practical.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and quantify sample complexity: Report pretraining sample budgets and total environment interactions (pretrain + downstream) vs. baselines (including “multi-policy” that trains K policies independently).
- Strengthen comparisons: Add an HRL baseline that learns options end-to-end (e.g., Option-Critic) on the same tasks, even if imperfect, to contextualize gains beyond intrinsic motivation.
- MI estimation: Consider a learned discriminator p̂(z|c) (e.g., an auxiliary classifier) and report the achieved MI or a bound; analyze sensitivity to the choice of C and αH. This would generalize beyond CoM and reduce handcrafting.
- Skill number K: Provide sensitivity to K and qualitative behavior when K is too small/large.
- Manager design: Explore adaptive termination (learned option lengths) and/or a recurrent manager; show whether smaller T helps on more dynamic tasks.
- Beyond locomotion: Demonstrate at least one non-locomotion domain (even a simple manipulator with “grasp anything” proxy) to underscore generality.
- Ant: Report mitigations (e.g., safety filters, smoother transitions, overlap in skill state visitation) to reduce switching-induced instability.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to K and αH? Is there a principled way you chose them, and how robust are results across a reasonable range?
- How do the total samples (pretraining + downstream) compare to training from scratch with stronger exploration methods or to the “multi-policy” baseline normalized by total data?
- How critical is the choice of C for MI? Have you tried higher-dimensional C (e.g., velocities or headings) and a learned p̂(z|c) instead of grid counts?
- Does the MI encourage spatial partitioning of the plane more than genuinely distinct actuation patterns? Can you show diversity beyond directionality (e.g., distinct gaits at same heading)?
- Could you report results with end-to-end fine-tuning (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) or with a learned termination policy, even on one task, to assess the headroom over frozen skills with fixed T?
- What is the computational overhead of bilinear integration versus concatenation, and does it scale to high-dimensional observations (e.g., with learned embeddings)?
- For Ant, did you try regularizing skill overlap (e.g., KL constraints across z-conditioned policies at visited states) to stabilize switching?
