1) Summary of contributions
The paper tackles sparse-reward RL by first pretraining a multi-modal low-level policy that spans a diverse set of reusable skills and then training a high-level “manager” that composes these skills to solve downstream tasks. The key ideas are:
- Use Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a categorical latent variable z fed into the policy to induce multi-modality, enabling multiple distinct skills to be represented with shared parameters.
- Encourage skill diversity via an information-theoretic regularizer: a per-step bonus proportional to the mutual information between z and a subset of the state (e.g., center-of-mass (CoM) position), estimated via discretized visitation counts.
- Use a simple bilinear integration between z and observations to increase expressivity and separability between skills, outperforming naive concatenation.
- Freeze the low-level SNN and train a high-level policy that selects one skill every T steps (temporal commitment) using TRPO.
Empirically, on locomotion tasks with sparse rewards (mazes and a gather task), SNN pretraining produces interpretable and diverse skills (e.g., moving in different directions and gaits), greatly enlarges exploration coverage, and enables solving tasks that standard exploration fails on. Ablations show bilinear integration and the MI bonus both matter. Results extend to a harder “Snake” robot; challenges with an unstable “Ant” are documented.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated, and general approach to skill discovery and reuse that demonstrably improves exploration and sample efficiency in sparse-reward continuous control.
- Clear empirical validation with ablations (SNN vs multi-policy, concatenation vs bilinear integration, with/without MI), evidence of skill interpretability, and nontrivial tasks where standard methods fail.

3) Supporting arguments
Motivation and positioning
- The paper directly addresses a central pain point in deep RL—exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks—using skill abstraction rather than reward shaping or heavy domain engineering.
- The method is well-situated in HRL and intrinsic motivation literature, with thoughtful discussion of options/HiREPS/option-critic and contemporaneous pretraining ideas. The choice of an intrinsic proxy reward during pretraining is pragmatic and requires minimal domain knowledge.

Methodological soundness
- The SNN architecture with a categorical latent sampled per rollout is a natural way to induce temporally coherent skills; treating z as part of the observation sidesteps mixture complications in TRPO.
- The MI-style regularizer is sensible: maximizing I(Z; C) via minimizing H(Z|C) encourages skills to be distinguishable by the agent’s CoM position, which is directly relevant to locomotion behaviors. The discretized posterior estimate is simple and computationally cheap.
- Bilinear integration is a meaningful design choice; the paper provides intuitive justification and ablation evidence that it substantially improves skill separability relative to concatenation.

Empirical evidence
- Pretraining: Visitation plots convincingly show distinct, interpretable skills; bilinear integration + MI yields broader and less overlapping coverage. The claim that SNNs achieve K× data efficiency relative to training K independent policies is plausible (single SNN trained with similar samples as a single policy), and the empirical span of behaviors supports it.
- Exploration: One-million-step trajectories under a random manager clearly show markedly improved space coverage vs Gaussian noise; skill switches correspond to directional changes, indicating good temporal abstractions.
- Downstream tasks: On mazes and gather, the hierarchical agents learn where standard RL fails; performance generally improves further with SNN+bilinear integration, and MI often helps (especially in gather). The evaluation includes multiple seeds and multiple pretrained SNNs, with variance transparently reported and discussed.
- Scaling: Results with the Snake robot show the approach extends to higher-dimensional agents; failure modes with Ant are honest and instructive.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper
These points are to help strengthen the work; they are not all required for acceptance.
- Quantify skill diversity: Beyond visitation plots, report metrics such as empirical MI estimates, state coverage area, pairwise skill distinguishability (e.g., a simple classifier accuracy), and/or diversity in action distributions. This would make the MI bonus effect more rigorous.
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide curves for K (number of skills), MI coefficient αH, grid resolution for MI, and switch time T (you have some in the appendix; consider surfacing key findings in the main text). Report how these hyperparameters affect both skill diversity and downstream performance.
- Sample complexity: Report absolute environment steps to reach predetermined success thresholds (e.g., success rate ≥ X) and compare to baselines; include area-under-curve if appropriate.
- Baselines: In addition to the strong “CoM proxy reward in downstream task” baseline and VIME (provided in Appendix B), it would be useful to include option-critic or HiREPS on at least one domain (even if adapted), or provide further discussion about why such comparisons are impractical here.
- Pretraining-task generality: All downstream tasks are closely related to locomotion in the same simulator. Discuss or demonstrate transfer to a different class of tasks (e.g., manipulation proxy reward → downstream grasping/stacking), or clarify the scope and limitations of the “shared agent-space” assumption.
- Manager architecture details: Clarify manager inputs (Sagent vs Srest), whether manager observes proprioception plus task sensors, and exact training budgets. A brief description of reward normalization, advantage estimation, and entropy regularization would help reproducibility.
- MI implementation details: Specify bilinear integration concretely (outer product shape and how it feeds into the first layer), and discuss the bias of the discretized MI estimate; an alternative is to learn p(z|c) with a small classifier.
- Robust switching: For unstable agents (Ant), preliminary results on smoothing transitions (e.g., interpolation between skill policies over a short horizon, or training a transition policy) would be valuable.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to K? Do more skills always help, or do redundant/unused skills emerge?
- How do you choose the subset C of state for MI? Have you tried other choices (e.g., heading angle, velocity) and what was the effect?
- Could you report quantitative MI or decoding accuracy (predict z from c) to corroborate the qualitative visitation plots?
- What is the exact bilinear integration implementation (dimensionalities, parameterization)? Did you try multiplicative gating or FiLM-like modulations?
- How consistent is the SNN pretraining performance across seeds for larger robots, and how does that variability translate to downstream success rates?
- In the gather tasks, can you report success under fixed horizon and also longer horizons with identical training budgets to isolate the effect of temporal abstraction?
- Have you tried limited fine-tuning of low-level skills jointly with the manager (e.g., small learning rate, or only last layer) to mitigate mismatch without losing generality?
- For Ant, did you explore regularizing skills to be robust to off-manifold states (e.g., domain randomization on initial states or adversarial perturbations) to reduce falls at switches?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, practical, and effective HRL approach with meaningful empirical validation and insightful ablations. I recommend acceptance.
