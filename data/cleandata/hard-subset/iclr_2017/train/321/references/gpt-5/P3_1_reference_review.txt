1) Summary and contributions
The paper tackles exploration and sample-efficiency in sparse-reward RL by learning a reusable set of low-level skills in a pre-training environment and then composing them with a high-level policy for downstream tasks. The key ideas are:
- Pre-train with only a minimal proxy reward (e.g., forward speed), avoiding task-specific supervision.
- Use a stochastic neural network (SNN) policy with a discrete latent variable z to induce multi-modality, with bilinear integration between z and observations to modulate the first-layer weights (akin to conditional policies).
- Encourage skill diversity and interpretability with a mutual information (MI) bonus I(Z; C) between the latent code and a subset of state (the CoM position), implemented via a simple count-based estimate of p(z|c).
- For downstream tasks, freeze the SNN and learn a “Manager” that selects skills every T steps, substantially improving exploration.
Empirically, on locomotion-based sparse tasks (Swimmer, Snake; Maze and Gather), the approach yields: (i) diverse, interpretable skills; (ii) dramatically improved exploration; and (iii) better learning curves than strong baselines, including intrinsic CoM-shaped rewards and a multi-policy baseline. Ablations show bilinear integration and MI both matter.

2) Decision
Weak accept.
Key reasons:
- The framework is simple, well motivated, and practically useful. The SNN+MI pretraining reliably yields diverse skills that improve exploration and enable solving otherwise intractable sparse tasks.
- The empirical study, while focused on locomotion, includes ablations and clear evidence that the proposed components (bilinear integration and MI) are important.

3) Supporting arguments
Motivation and placement in literature:
- The paper is well situated relative to hierarchical RL (options, HRL with MAXQ), skill discovery (HiREPS), and exploration via intrinsic motivation (VIME). The use of an SNN with a discrete latent that modulates the policy is closely related to mixture-of-experts/options and conditional computation; the MI regularizer is inspired by InfoGAN. Compared to Heess et al. (2016), learning is driven by a single proxy reward and MI without goal labels. The contribution is primarily an effective synthesis tailored to RL skill discovery.

Methodology:
- SNN design choices are sensible: discrete z, kept fixed per rollout to induce temporal consistency; bilinear integration to increase capacity and enable conditional modulation; TRPO treating z as part of the observation to avoid intractable mixture policies.
- The MI bonus targeted at the CoM subset is pragmatic and provides a clear behavioral signal that disentangles skills by direction/locomotion mode. The count-based estimator is simple and computationally light.

Empirical evidence:
- Clear ablations demonstrate: (a) SNN vs single-policy; (b) concatenation vs bilinear; (c) MI coefficient αH; (d) hierarchical manager vs flat exploration; and (e) sensitivity to switch time T.
- Exploration visualizations and learning curves convincingly show that the learned skills expand the reachable state space and materially help solve sparse tasks that standard baselines fail on.
- Results are reproduced over multiple seeds with some reporting of variance; code and videos are provided.

4) Concerns and limitations
- Scope of evaluation: All tasks are locomotion-centric with proprioception and simple LIDAR-like inputs. While appropriate for the structural assumption (shared Sagent), the generality to other domains (manipulation, vision) is not demonstrated.
- Baselines: Comparisons to Option-Critic, mixture-of-experts/gated conditional policies trained end-to-end, and recent macro-action learners would strengthen the case. The “multi-policy” baseline trains K policies independently but remains weaker than a competitive conditional policy baseline.
- MI design: The MI is computed on CoM only and via coarse counts on the current batch. This shapes behavior towards directional diversity, which is well matched to mazes/gather, but may not discover more abstract skills. Sensitivity to the grid resolution and αH is only partially explored.
- Sample complexity claims: The paper asserts K-fold gains from training K skills in one SNN, but does not quantify how training difficulty scales with K or provide head-to-head sample curves vs training K separate policies. Evidence of actual wall-clock or environment-step savings as K grows would be valuable.
- Fixed commitment time and frozen skills: The manager uses a fixed T; no termination/persistence learning is used. Low-level skills are frozen for downstream tasks. While the authors discuss future work, this limits adaptability, and the instability on Ant illustrates failure modes when switching.
- Statistical rigor: Variances are high for some maze tasks and success can hinge on pretrain seed quality; a more systematic robustness analysis would help.

5) Suggestions to improve the paper
- Add stronger baselines: (i) option-critic; (ii) mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network (no pretraining), and (iii) a conditional policy without MI (to isolate the MI’s benefit). If feasible, include a comparison to learning end-to-end with Gumbel-Softmax.
- Analyze scaling with K: report diversity metrics (e.g., coverage, pairwise behavioral distance), performance on downstream tasks, and training stability as K varies.
- MI analysis: study sensitivity to αH and grid resolution; consider a learned density estimator for p(z|c) (the authors mention MLP as an alternative) and compare.
- Provide more quantitative diversity measures beyond visitation plots (e.g., mutual information estimates, state-occupancy overlap, dispersion scores).
- Explore partial finetuning of low-level policies in downstream tasks or learning a termination policy to mitigate switching failures, especially for unstable agents.
- Discuss computational cost (per-iteration time, memory) and provide environment-step budgets for fair comparisons.

6) Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to αH and the discretization granularity for CoM? Does MI ever induce degenerate behaviors (e.g., camping in unique cells) without the speed proxy?
- How does performance and learned skill diversity scale with K (e.g., 2, 4, 8, 16)? Are there diminishing returns or training instabilities?
- Could you report head-to-head environment-step/sample comparisons showing that one SNN achieves K skills for roughly the cost of one unimodal policy, across multiple K?
- How do results change if you replace bilinear integration with other conditioning mechanisms (e.g., FiLM/conditional affine transformations, hypernetworks)?
- Have you tried a learned p̂(Z|C) (e.g., a classifier) instead of counts? Does it improve MI estimation and downstream skills?
- Can you provide results on alternative proxy rewards (e.g., energy efficiency, rotation, contact diversity) and show transfer to tasks where direction alone is insufficient?
- In the Gather task with longer horizons, can you quantify improvements due to turning/sideway skills (e.g., success rate of reorientation maneuvers)?
- For Ant, did you try manager-aware switch smoothing (e.g., action blending over a short window) or training with random skill switches during pretraining to increase robustness?

Overall, the paper presents a practical and well-motivated approach with solid empirical gains and useful ablations. Strengthening baselines, scaling analyses, and MI sensitivity would elevate the contribution.
