Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes an unsupervised pretraining framework for hierarchical RL that learns a diverse, interpretable set of motor skills using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy with discrete latent codes. A mutual-information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., robot CoM position) is used to encourage behavioral diversity.
- A simple yet effective “manager” policy is trained on top of the frozen skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards by selecting a skill and committing to it for T steps. This yields temporally coherent exploration and strong performance on long-horizon, sparse-reward benchmarks (mazes, gather) with Swimmer and Snake.
- Key design insights include bilinear integration of latents and observations (vs concatenation) to increase expressivity and optimization quality; discretized MI estimation for tractability; and a clean separation between unsupervised skill discovery and task-specific high-level control. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novel and bold direction: leveraging SNNs with MI-guided unsupervised pretraining to learn a multi-modal skill set in one policy, then reusing the learned latent-controlled skills across tasks. This is a compelling and relatively simple recipe that increases exploration and reduces sample cost compared to training K separate policies.
- Empirical evidence and ablations show that the approach reliably produces diverse, interpretable skills and enables solving challenging sparse tasks that standard RL fails on, with clear benefits from bilinear integration and the MI bonus.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles exploration in long-horizon, sparse-reward domains and knowledge reuse across related tasks by learning task-agnostic skills. This is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature (options, HiREPS, VIME, GPS, goal-conditioned pretraining). The use of InfoGAN-style MI to induce diversity is appropriate and elegantly instantiated.
- Technical novelty: Using an SNN policy with discrete latent inputs to represent multiple skills with weight sharing, coupled with bilinear integration to modulate the first-layer weights based on the latent, is a neat architectural contribution. The MI regularizer over partial state features is simple, practical, and directly tied to interpretability and spatial diversity of behaviors.
- Empirical validation:
  - Skill learning: Visitation plots and qualitative rollouts demonstrate distinct, interpretable modes (forward/backward/sideways/turning), with bilinear+MI clearly outperforming concatenation and no-MI in coverage and distinctness.
  - Hierarchical composition: Randomized managers over frozen skills substantially increase exploration radius versus action noise. In downstream mazes and gather, the manager learns effectively where flat RL and intrinsic CoM-reward baselines struggle, with consistent improvements across seeds and robots (Swimmer, Snake). Ablations include switch time T studies, variance across seeds, and multi-policy vs SNN pretraining.
  - Reproducibility: Code and hyperparameters are provided; the training algorithm and estimators are straightforward (TRPO, count-based MI estimation).
- Limitations acknowledged: Fixed T and frozen skills, reliance on privileged state features for MI and manager inputs, occasional failures on unstable agents (Ant) due to switching-induced instability. The paper discusses straightforward extensions (learned termination, end-to-end finetuning with Gumbel-Softmax, recurrent managers).

Areas for improvement (not blockers)
- Evaluation breadth: Downstream tasks, while challenging and sparse, are still relatively constrained and use full-state features; adding more diverse tasks (e.g., with obstacles requiring precise turning, or visually rich inputs) would strengthen claims of broad transfer.
- Quantify sample efficiency: The claim of “effectively K times” better sample usage for learning K skills with SNNs is compelling; please report actual environment steps and wall-clock versus training K independent policies to make this concrete.
- MI estimator: The discretized posterior over CoM cells is simple but may induce bias; a learned classifier/regressor-based MI estimator (or variational bound) could improve robustness and generality beyond 2D CoM. Sensitivity to grid resolution is not reported.
- Comparisons: Option-Critic and goal-conditioned pretraining (Heess et al., 2016) are discussed; direct empirical comparisons (even limited) would help position the approach more clearly.
- Robustness to switching: For unstable agents, learning a stabilizing transition policy or skill-initialization adaptor might mitigate falls; worth exploring as the paper suggests.

Questions for the authors
1. MI estimation and sensitivity:
   - How sensitive are results to the grid resolution for CoM discretization? Have you tried a learned posterior (e.g., a classifier p(z|c)) and does it change outcomes?
   - For Snake/Ant, what state subset c did you use for MI? Does choosing different partial states (e.g., heading vs position) qualitatively change the learned skills?
2. Sample complexity:
   - Please report the number of environment steps used for pretraining (SNN vs multi-policy baseline) and for downstream tasks, and the total across tasks. How often can a single pretrained SNN be reused across different task layouts before retraining?
3. Manager details:
   - Can the manager select the same skill repeatedly (i.e., effectively extending commitment)? Did you observe any degenerate behavior where a few skills dominate?
   - Is the low-level policy stochastic during execution? Does increasing/decreasing its entropy affect manager learning or stability?
4. Hyperparameters and ablations:
   - How sensitive is performance to K (number of skills) and the MI weight αH? Is there a regime where too large K hurts manager learning?
   - For Gather, could you share success breakdowns by skill diversity (e.g., correlation between pretrain visitation diversity metrics and downstream returns)?
5. Scope and generalization:
   - Have you tried proxy rewards encouraging turning or agility (beyond CoM speed)? Do those skills transfer better to maze-like tasks?
   - How much does the approach rely on the Sagent/Srest factorization in practice? Could the manager operate on proprioception-only inputs while still benefiting from the skills?

Overall, this work introduces a simple, elegant, and effective path toward unsupervised skill discovery and reuse for HRL. Despite some limitations in evaluation breadth and estimators, the conceptual contribution and empirical promise warrant acceptance.
