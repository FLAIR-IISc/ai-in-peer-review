1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings that (i) pre-trains a diverse set of low-level skills in a proxy environment using a minimal intrinsic objective, and (ii) trains a high-level “manager” that selects among these skills to solve downstream tasks. The key technical elements are: (a) representing a multi-skill policy via a stochastic neural network (SNN) with a categorical latent variable, integrated with observations via bilinear interactions; (b) adding a mutual-information (MI) bonus between the latent and a subset of the state (e.g., CoM position) to encourage diversity and interpretability of skills; and (c) freezing the learned skills and training a manager that selects a skill every T steps. Optimization uses TRPO, treating the latent as part of the observation. The paper provides ablations (concatenation vs bilinear integration; with/without MI), qualitative visitation analyses, and downstream results on MuJoCo tasks (Swimmer/Snake: mazes and gather), showing improved exploration and sample efficiency relative to baselines and prior intrinsic motivation methods, with code and hyperparameters provided.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated and coherent approach that is clearly placed in the HRL and intrinsic motivation literature; the combination of SNNs with an MI bonus for skill discovery and a simple manager yields a practical, reusable skill set.
- Empirical evidence (including ablations) shows that the approach improves exploration and solves sparse tasks that standard RL fails on, with reasonable methodological transparency.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: The paper addresses exploration and sample complexity in sparse-reward, long-horizon continuous control by learning reusable, temporally extended skills and composing them hierarchically.
- Motivation and literature placement: The work positions itself against (1) hand-designed hierarchies and (2) intrinsic motivation methods that do not directly yield reusable skills. It relates to options, HiREPS, and recent pre-training for skill controllers, highlighting its unsupervised proxy-reward setup (no goal specification), explicit multi-modality via SNNs, and an MI regularizer to enforce skill diversity. This is a reasonable and distinctive combination for the time.
- Technical soundness:
  - Using a categorical latent as input to a policy and treating it as part of the observation is sound for TRPO, avoiding marginalization over a mixture.
  - The MI bonus is implemented as a log p(z|c) term where p is estimated from discretized CoM visitation counts. This is a practical, if approximate, approach akin to InfoGAN’s variational MI lower bounds; the induced non-stationary reward shaping is common and acceptable in policy gradient practice.
  - The bilinear integration argument (latent modulating first-layer weights) is plausible and supported by ablations.
  - Freezing low-level skills and training a high-level categorical manager is standard and conservative; discussion of extensions (e.g., end-to-end fine-tuning, learned termination) is appropriate.
- Empirical support:
  - Ablations demonstrate that bilinear integration and the MI bonus materially increase the diversity and interpretability of skills (visitation plots and qualitative behaviors), versus simple concatenation or no MI.
  - Hierarchical control with these skills substantially expands exploration coverage compared to Gaussian noise, and outperforms a proxy-reward baseline and prior intrinsic motivation (VIME) in the reported settings.
  - Tasks (multiple mazes and gather) are standard, and the paper controls for pre-training variance by averaging across seeds and reporting variability.
  - Limitations are candidly discussed: unstable agents (Ant) expose switching issues; fixed T and frozen low-level policies may be suboptimal; performance depends on the pre-trained skill set in some tasks.
- Novelty: Moderate but meaningful. The specific synthesis—SNN with categorical latent and bilinear integration, MI regularization over a chosen state subset for skill diversity, and subsequent hierarchical control—differs from prior skill discovery and HRL methods and is empirically validated.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Quantify diversity: Beyond visitation plots, report quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., MI estimates, conditional entropy H(Z|C), skill occupancy overlap, state coverage) to support the claims on diversity and interpretability.
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide sensitivity to αH (MI weight), grid resolution for estimating p(z|c), skill count K, and switch time T across tasks. Some of this appears in the appendix; consolidating key trends in the main text would strengthen the message.
- Sample efficiency: Substantiate the “K-times better” sample efficiency claim by reporting actual environment samples for SNN vs training K independent policies to reach comparable skill diversity/performance.
- Baselines: If feasible, include comparisons to option-critic or other hierarchical methods in continuous control, or explain constraints preventing fair comparisons.
- Generality of the MI target: The CoM-based MI is task- and morphology-specific. Discuss or demonstrate MI defined on more general state embeddings (e.g., learned features) and its effect on skill diversity and transfer.
- Manager learning: Explore or discuss learned termination (options-style) or variable T, and provide a small-scale experiment on joint fine-tuning to quantify potential gains vs instability.
- Transfer breadth: Show transfer across a wider set of downstream tasks or environment changes to better support the “span of skills” as reusable primitives beyond the studied benchmarks.

Questions for the authors
- How does the choice of K affect both pre-training diversity and downstream performance? Is there a principled way to select K or prune redundant skills?
- How sensitive are results to αH and the discretization used to estimate p(z|c)? Have you tried learned discriminators for p(z|c) instead of grids, and does that improve stability?
- Can you report quantitative MI estimates (or a proxy) achieved during pre-training and correlate them with downstream success rates?
- How large is the performance gap between frozen low-level and end-to-end fine-tuned hierarchies in your setting?
- For unstable agents (Ant), did you try warm-starting each skill from states sampled from other skills to reduce distribution shift upon switching? If so, what was the effect?
- To what extent does the factorization assumption (Sagent vs Srest) matter in practice? Does giving the manager only Srest degrade performance?
- Could you provide exact environment sample counts (pre-training + downstream) to reach a fixed success rate, compared to baselines?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and offers a practically useful contribution to HRL with evidence on challenging sparse tasks. The suggested additions would further solidify the empirical claims and broaden applicability.
