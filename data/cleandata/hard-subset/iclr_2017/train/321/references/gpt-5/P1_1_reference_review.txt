Summary and contributions
The paper tackles exploration and sample-efficiency in sparse-reward reinforcement learning via hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL). It proposes a two-stage framework: (1) pre-train a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy equipped with a discrete latent variable to induce multimodal behavior (skills) in a minimally instrumented environment using a proxy reward (e.g., speed), and an information-theoretic mutual information (MI) bonus that encourages each latent to produce distinct behaviors; (2) freeze the learned low-level skills and train a high-level “Manager” that selects a latent/skill every T steps to solve downstream sparse-reward tasks. The key technical elements are: using categorical latent codes injected through bilinear interaction with observations (yielding stronger specialization than concatenation), sampling one latent per episode in pre-training to obtain consistent skills, and an MI reward term I(Z;C) approximated via a discretized posterior p(z|CoM position) to encourage diversity. Training in both phases uses TRPO, treating z as part of the observation during pre-training.

Empirically, the paper shows:
- SNNs with bilinear integration and an MI bonus produce a diverse, interpretable set of locomotion skills for continuous-control agents (Swimmer, Snake; partial analysis on Ant), as visualized by visitation plots and qualitative rollouts.
- A Manager over frozen skills substantially improves exploration relative to Gaussian action noise and a strong “proxy reward in-task” baseline, enabling learning in long-horizon sparse tasks: multiple maze variants and a gather task. The SNN-based hierarchy often outperforms a “multi-policy” baseline that trains K separate low-level policies (at higher pre-training cost).

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) The paper addresses an important challenge (exploration in sparse rewards) with a practical, well-motivated HRL approach that is simple, modular, and comes with code. (2) The architectural choices (SNN with bilinear integration and MI regularizer) are novel in this context and ablated convincingly. (3) Results demonstrate clear benefits on nontrivial continuous-control tasks with sparse rewards.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and motivation: The need for better exploration and skill reuse in sparse-reward settings is well established. The design—learning reusable skills without task-specific rewards, then composing them via a high-level policy—is compelling and aligns with HRL goals, while minimizing domain knowledge (proxy reward + MI bonus over agent-centric state).
- Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself among HRL (options, MAXQ), skill discovery (HiREPS), intrinsic motivation (VIME, count-based), DRAW-like macro-actions, and pre-training for control (Heess et al. with goal supervision). Unlike goal-conditioned pre-training, the proposed method uses a single proxy reward and an MI regularizer to induce diversity without goal labels. The link to InfoGAN for MI is appropriate.
- Methodological clarity: The architecture, the role of the latent, bilinear vs concatenation integration, MI bonus construction and estimation via discretized CoM, and Manager training are clearly described. Treating z as part of the observation to keep TRPO unchanged is neat and practical. The commitment time T and freezing choices are explicit.
- Empirical support:
  - Skill diversity: Extensive visitation plots and ablations (concatenation vs bilinear; MI coefficient αH) show that bilinear integration and MI markedly increase the distinctness and coverage of skills. Temporal consistency by fixing z per rollout yields interpretable, stable skills.
  - Exploration and task performance: Single long-rollout exploration plots demonstrate that hierarchies over skills dramatically expand reachable space compared to Gaussian noise. Learning curves across multiple mazes and the gather task show consistent gains over a strong “proxy reward in-task” baseline; the SNN hierarchy often matches or exceeds the multi-policy baseline while using fewer pre-training samples. The paper reports multiple pre-trained SNN seeds and Manager seeds, with discussion of variance and failure cases.
  - Generality: Additional Snake experiments support scalability; Ant analysis reveals limitations (instability on switching), with reasonable discussion.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters are documented, code and videos are provided.

Limitations and concerns
- Evaluation scope and baselines: The downstream environments (Swimmer/Snake mazes and gather) are meaningful but still stylized. There is no direct comparison to other hierarchical baselines (e.g., option-critic or other option discovery methods) in the same sparse settings. The multi-policy baseline is helpful but requires more pre-training samples; comparisons under equal pre-training budget would strengthen claims about sample-efficiency.
- MI bonus design: The MI term uses CoM (x,y) only and a discretized estimate, which is simple but introduces design choices (grid density, subset of state). The extent to which performance depends on this choice (vs. using full state, learned q(z|s), or different proxies) is only partially explored.
- Sensitivity and hyperparameters: While ablations on integration and αH are provided, sensitivity to K (number of skills) and switch time T is not deeply analyzed for the main agent; some analysis appears in the appendix for Snake, but more systematic study would help.
- Frozen skills and fixed T: The approach freezes low-level skills and uses a fixed commitment time. The authors acknowledge that learned terminations or end-to-end fine-tuning could help (especially for unstable agents), but no experiments are shown.
- Claims about K-times sample efficiency in pre-training are plausible but not quantified beyond qualitative statements; a controlled measurement (returns vs pre-training environment samples) would be useful.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Add a comparison to a learned options baseline (e.g., option-critic) and/or macro-action baselines in exactly the same sparse environments, and/or equalize pre-training budgets between the multi-policy and SNN variants.
- Report quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., MI estimate, state coverage, angular spread of locomotion directions) alongside visitation plots to complement qualitative evidence.
- Provide a sensitivity study for K and αH on both the diversity metric and downstream performance; include guidance on choosing grid resolution and the subset of state for MI.
- Include an ablation where the MI posterior p(z|c) is learned with a small neural classifier on continuous c (or full s), to test robustness to the discretization.
- Consider a simple learned termination condition for the Manager (even a Bernoulli hazard) to show that variable-duration skills can further help, or provide more extensive results varying T in the main settings.
- Where possible, present sample-efficiency curves (performance vs environment steps) for pre-training and downstream phases to substantiate the claimed efficiency gains.
- Clarify whether the Manager can choose the same skill consecutively, and how often this happens in trained policies.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the number of skills K and the MI coefficient αH? Is there a regime where too many skills hurt Manager training?
- How was the CoM grid chosen, and how sensitive are results to grid density or to using different subsets of state (e.g., including orientation)?
- In the multi-policy baseline, how do results change when pre-training sample budget is matched to the SNN (i.e., each individual policy trained less)? Conversely, if the SNN is given K× more pre-training steps, how much better can it get?
- Can the Manager pick the same skill repeatedly, effectively increasing commitment beyond T? What is the empirical distribution of switch intervals in trained Managers?
- Did you try a learned q(z|s) network for the MI bonus instead of discretization? If so, how did it compare?
- For Ant, did you try end-to-end fine-tuning of the low-level SNN with the Manager (e.g., with straight-through estimators), or warm-starting skills per-region to mitigate instability?

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated paper with solid empirical evidence and useful code. Addressing the baseline and sensitivity concerns would further strengthen it.
