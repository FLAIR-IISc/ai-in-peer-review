Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a two-phase hierarchical RL framework for sparse-reward tasks: (1) pretrain a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy with a discrete latent code to induce a multimodal action distribution corresponding to a repertoire of “skills,” guided only by a proxy reward and a mutual-information (MI) regularizer that encourages skill-state discriminability; (2) freeze the low-level skills and train a high-level “Manager” to select skills every T steps to solve downstream tasks. The SNN uses bilinear integration of state and latent code to increase expressiveness. The MI bonus is approximated by encouraging low conditional entropy of Z given a subset of state (e.g., CoM position) via a discretized posterior.
- The method uses TRPO throughout (treating z as part of the observation in pretraining). Experiments on MuJoCo locomotion with sparse-reward mazes and gather tasks (Swimmer; Snake in appendix) show: (i) SNN+MI produces interpretable, diverse skills; (ii) hierarchical composition substantially improves exploration relative to naive Gaussian noise; (iii) downstream performance (learning curves) improves over non-hierarchical baselines and over a “proxy reward” baseline; (iv) ablations demonstrate the benefits of bilinear integration and the MI regularizer. Ant is discussed as a failure mode due to instability on skill switches.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
1) The paper presents a simple, well-motivated, and practically effective way to learn diverse skills without task rewards and to reuse them for improved exploration and sample efficiency on sparse downstream tasks. The combination of SNNs with a lightweight MI regularizer and hierarchical control is novel and well executed.
2) Empirical results support the core claims with informative ablations (concat vs bilinear, with/without MI, effect of T), and the qualitative analysis of exploration breadth and skill interpretability is convincing.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper targets a critical pain point—exploration in long-horizon sparse-reward settings—by learning reusable, temporally-extended primitives. It relates appropriately to options/HiREPS/option-critic, intrinsic motivation (VIME, count-based), and concurrent work (Heess et al. 2016), and argues for minimal domain knowledge via proxy rewards. The idea to induce multimodality via SNN latent codes and to encourage diversity via MI is clear and technically sound.
- Methodology: 
  - SNN design choices (categorical latent, bilinear integration) are well reasoned; treating z as an observation avoids marginalization headaches with TRPO. Sampling z per episode yields temporally coherent skills.
  - The MI regularizer is intuitive (encouraging z to be recoverable from state) and computationally cheap with the binned CoM posterior. While heuristic, it is effective and nicely complements the proxy reward.
  - The hierarchical manager that commits to a skill for T steps is standard and fits the aim of boosting exploration by shrinking effective horizon.
- Empirical support:
  - Skill diversity: visitation plots show interpretable modes (forward/backward/sideways/turning), and bilinear+MI clearly outperforms concatenation. This is the central enabler of the downstream gains.
  - Exploration analysis: one-million-step trajectories under random managers demonstrate dramatically wider coverage than Gaussian action noise.
  - Downstream tasks: On multiple mazes and gather, the hierarchical SNN (especially with bilinear integration) outperforms the proxy-reward baseline and, per appendix, outperforms VIME under benchmark settings. Reporting across multiple pretrain seeds (without cherry-picking) is commendable; the variance analysis is honest, and the Snake results show some scalability. The Ant discussion is a useful diagnostic.

Limitations and concerns (impacting confidence but not fatal)
- Baselines and fairness: The “multi-policy” baseline uses 6× more pretraining samples to train separate skills, which complicates fairness. More importantly, comparisons to learned option frameworks (e.g., option-critic) or other hierarchical baselines are missing; only VIME appears in the appendix. This weakens claims of superiority over alternative HRL approaches.
- Scope of environments: Most evidence is on relatively simple 2D locomotion (Swimmer; Snake), with privileged state inputs and a particularly favorable proxy reward (speed magnitude). The MI bonus uses only CoM (x,y), which is tailored to locomotion in a plane; generality to other domains (manipulation, pixel observations) is not demonstrated.
- MI estimation: The discretized posterior p̂(Z|C) is ad hoc and can be brittle; a learned classifier or variational MI lower bound would be more principled and scalable to higher-dimensional C.
- Transfer and sample efficiency claims: The paper argues that SNN pretraining is “K× cheaper” than training K separate policies, but does not quantify total pretrain cost vs downstream gains or compare against training the manager with end-to-end fine-tuning. Also, downstream learning still shows high variance tied to the pretraining seed.
- Fixed commitment T and frozen low-levels: While partially analyzed, fixed T and freezing skills can be limiting, especially for unstable agents and dynamic tasks.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not required for acceptance)
- Strengthen baselines: include option-critic and/or HiREPS-like mixtures under comparable sample budgets; include a learned macro-action baseline (e.g., Mnih et al. 2016).
- Report sample complexity: plot return vs total environment steps including pretraining; compare SNN vs K independent skills under equal pretraining budget.
- MI regularizer: replace count-based posterior with a learned discriminator qφ(z|c) and optimize a variational MI lower bound; report MI estimates over training and quantify skill diversity (e.g., entropy of inferred z, coverage metrics).
- Broaden evaluation: add at least one domain where C is not planar CoM (e.g., manipulation with contact events or orientations), and a pixel-based observation variant with an encoder.
- Robustness: analyze sensitivity to K and αH; show results with randomized initial orientations; provide significance tests across seeds.
- Hierarchical control: include a learned termination policy or variable T; report limited end-to-end fine-tuning results to show potential gains and mitigate instability (particularly for Ant).

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to the choice of C used in the MI bonus? Have you tried including orientation, velocities, or more general embeddings? How does performance change if the initial heading is randomized?
2) How did you choose K and αH? Is there evidence of diminishing returns or mode collapse for larger K?
3) Can you report total sample budgets (pretrain + downstream) and learning curves vs environment steps to substantiate the claimed sample efficiency?
4) How does the method perform if observations are images? Do you first learn an encoder and then apply bilinear integration with z?
5) Could you include comparisons to option-critic or other hierarchical baselines under matched pretraining/training budgets?
6) Did you try a learned q(z|s) classifier for MI estimation? Does it improve stability over the grid-based estimator?
7) For Ant, did you try overlap between skills in state distribution (e.g., behavior cloning warm-start on switch) or switching-aware pretraining to mitigate falls?

Overall, this is a solid, well-motivated paper with clear ideas and promising empirical evidence. With stronger baselines and broader evaluations, it would be even more compelling.
