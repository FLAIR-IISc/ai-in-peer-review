Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles exploration and sample efficiency in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by learning a reusable set of low-level skills via pretraining and composing them with a learned high-level “manager” policy. 
- Skills are implemented as a stochastic neural network (SNN): a feed-forward policy conditioned on a discrete latent code z sampled per episode. A “bilinear” integration of z with observations gives the policy multi-modal capacity with weight sharing across skills.
- To avoid mode collapse and promote behaviorally distinct skills, the authors add an information-theoretic regularizer: a mutual-information (MI) bonus between z and a subset of agent-centric state features (e.g., center-of-mass x,y), estimated via discretized visitation counts.
- At transfer time, the low-level SNN is frozen and a high-level categorical manager selects skills (latent codes) every T steps. TRPO is used throughout.
- Empirically, the approach yields clearly interpretable, diverse locomotion behaviors (directional gaits) and substantially improves exploration and performance on sparse tasks (Swimmer in several mazes and Gather). Ablations show bilinear integration and MI help skill diversity and downstream performance. Some results on a higher-DoF Snake support scalability; instability issues on Ant are discussed.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) A simple, well-motivated combination of multi-modal policies and MI-based diversity yields robust, interpretable skill sets that materially improve exploration and solve difficult sparse tasks where standard baselines fail. (2) The empirical analysis includes meaningful ablations (concat vs bilinear, MI coefficient, SNN vs multiple independently-trained policies) and demonstrates reuse and variance considerations. The idea is timely, practical, and likely to influence subsequent HRL work.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Sparse rewards and long horizons are central challenges. Pretraining reusable skills in an environment requiring minimal instrumentation (proxy reward only) provides a compelling way to amortize exploration across tasks sharing an agent-centric state space.
- Methodological soundness:
  - The SNN architecture is an appropriate way to realize multi-modal policies with parameter sharing; conditioning on a discrete latent sampled per rollout gives temporally coherent primitives.
  - Treating z as part of the observation in TRPO sidesteps mixture-policy complications while preserving Markovianity in the augmented state.
  - The MI bonus I(z; c) with c = CoM(x,y) is a clear and interpretable diversity signal; the discretization-based estimator is simple and aligns with visitation-coverage goals.
  - The manager’s fixed-commitment scheme is a straightforward and effective design for static tasks; authors correctly note extensions to learned termination.
- Empirical validation:
  - Qualitative visitation plots convincingly illustrate skill diversity and the impact of bilinear integration and MI.
  - Exploration rollouts show dramatic coverage gains over Gaussian action noise; random manager samples confirm that skills themselves afford macro-action exploration.
  - On multiple sparse tasks (four mazes, Gather) the hierarchical method outperforms baselines, including a reasonably strong “proxy reward in downstream” baseline. The Snake results suggest scalability, and Ant analysis candidly describes limits on unstable platforms.
- Positioning and novelty:
  - The work is well situated relative to options, HiREPS, macro-actions, and intrinsic motivation (VIME, InfoGAN). Using MI in pretraining to induce skill diversity and a simple bilinear integration for z-conditioning is a neat, pragmatic contribution that differs from goal-conditioned pretraining.

Caveats and limitations impacting the strength of the case
- The MI estimator uses hand-selected features (CoM) and a discretization grid; sensitivity analyses are minimal. This could bias skills toward spatial specialization rather than richer control repertoires.
- Quantitative diversity metrics are largely qualitative (visitation plots). No report of actual mutual information estimates, state coverage metrics, or skill distinguishability on held-out contexts.
- The claim of K-fold sample savings versus training K independent policies is plausible but not directly supported by controlled sample-efficiency plots versus K.
- Comparisons to other HRL/option discovery baselines are limited (e.g., option-critic), although some were contemporary. A stronger comparative study would bolster claims.
- Manager design is fixed-commitment and non-recurrent; no experiments on learned termination or joint fine-tuning, which might be important for more dynamic tasks.
- High variance across pretraining seeds affects downstream success in some mazes; while this is transparently reported, additional mechanisms to ensure coverage-completeness would strengthen robustness claims.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not all required for acceptance)
- Provide quantitative diversity metrics: estimated MI, marginal state coverage, pairwise distinguishability of skills (e.g., accuracy of a z-classifier from short state snippets), and option usage entropy during downstream training.
- Sensitivity of MI bonus: report performance/diversity as a function of αH and grid resolution; discuss alternative posteriors (e.g., learned discriminators) to avoid discretization bias and to handle higher-dimensional c.
- Analyze dependence on K: show how skill diversity and downstream returns vary with the number of latent codes; discuss over/under-parameterization.
- Stronger baselines: include option-critic or macro-action methods trained directly on sparse tasks; if infeasible, discuss expected behavior and limitations.
- Demonstrate joint fine-tuning or learned termination on at least one task to show the framework’s extensibility.
- Clarify bilinear integration implementation details and computational cost; consider comparing to multiplicative gating or FiLM-like conditioning.
- Provide sample-efficiency curves directly comparing SNN vs K independent policies at equal total environment steps.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of state subset c used in the MI term? Have you tried incorporating orientations or joint angles/velocities, or using a learned discriminator p(z|state) as in InfoGAN/DIAYN-style setups?
- How does performance and diversity scale with K? Is there evidence of mode collapse or redundancy when K is large?
- Can you quantify the purported K-fold sample-efficiency gain of SNN pretraining versus training K separate policies, holding total environment interactions constant?
- How sensitive are downstream results to the discretization grid for MI and to αH? Do you see consistent gains across a broad range?
- Did you attempt a recurrent manager or learned termination? Any preliminary results?
- In Ant, beyond instability, do skills trained with MI + proxy reward still cover directionally diverse gaits? Could warming up newly activated skills (e.g., short ramp-up) mitigate falls?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clearly written, and practically useful contribution to HRL with solid empirical evidence and transparency about limitations. Despite some missing comparisons and quantitative diversity analysis, the work has clear merit and potential impact.
