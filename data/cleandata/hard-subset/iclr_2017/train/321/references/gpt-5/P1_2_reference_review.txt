Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the challenge of exploration and sample efficiency in sparse-reward reinforcement learning by proposing a two-stage hierarchical framework. Stage 1 (pre-training) learns a diverse set of skills without task-specific supervision, by training a latent-conditioned Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy in a minimal environment with a simple proxy reward (e.g., speed). The SNN uses categorical latent variables (skills) and a bilinear integration of state and latent code to induce multi-modality while sharing parameters. To prevent mode collapse and encourage interpretable diversity, the authors add a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent variable and a subset of the state (the robot’s CoM position), estimated via simple discretization. Stage 2 (downstream) freezes the low-level skills and trains a high-level “manager” that selects a skill every T steps, enabling temporally consistent exploration.

Empirically, the method learns interpretable, distinct locomotion behaviors; improves state-space coverage; and significantly outperforms strong baselines on sparse-reward tasks (several mazes and Gather) with Swimmer, and scales to Snake; Ant is discussed as a challenging failure mode. Ablations show the impact of bilinear integration and MI on skill diversity. Code is released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Practical and conceptually clean approach to unsupervised skill discovery that yields clearly interpretable, reusable primitives and materially improves exploration on challenging sparse-reward tasks.
- Solid empirical evidence with ablations (integration type, MI) and multiple environments; the approach is simple to implement on top of standard policy gradient methods, and code is provided.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper addresses known weaknesses of naive exploration in sparse-reward settings and gives a credible, low-assumption alternative to hand-engineered options or goal-specified pre-training. Using SNNs for multi-modal policies with weight sharing is well-motivated; the MI regularizer is inspired by InfoGAN and tailored to skill disambiguation via CoM, which is a minimal, domain-agnostic signal for locomotion. Related work is covered, including options, HiREPS, GPS, and intrinsic motivation (e.g., VIME), and the contrast to goal-conditioned pre-training is clear.
- Methodological clarity: The architecture and training protocol are straightforward. Treating z as part of the observation avoids marginalizing mixtures in TRPO. The MI reward is simple, cheap to estimate, and targeted (H(Z|CoM)). The choice to fix z per rollout induces temporally extended behaviors by construction.
- Empirical support: 
  - Skill learning: Visitation plots convincingly demonstrate distinct, interpretable modes emerging with bilinear integration and MI. The comparison to multiple independently trained policies shows SNNs achieve comparable or richer spans at significantly reduced sample cost (K skills for the price of one).
  - Exploration: Long rollout visualizations show that hierarchical use of skills (even with a random manager) drastically increases coverage versus Gaussian noise, aligning with the claim that skills enhance exploration.
  - Downstream performance: On multiple mazes and Gather, the hierarchical method outperforms baselines (including adding the CoM proxy reward at training time). Ablations indicate bilinear+MI tends to help, especially for tasks demanding orientation changes (e.g., Gather). Results are averaged over multiple pre-trained SNNs and seeds, with variance reported. Scaling to Snake is shown; limitations on Ant are candidly discussed.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters are provided; code is released.

Limitations and areas to strengthen (do not block acceptance)
- Baselines: While VIME is included in the appendix for Gather, comparisons to other hierarchical/skill-discovery methods (e.g., HiREPS, option-critic) on at least one shared environment would strengthen claims. A comparison to training a mixture-of-experts with a learned gating network (without MI) would help isolate the MI and bilinear contributions.
- Quantitative diversity metrics: Beyond visitation plots, it would be useful to report aggregate measures (empirical MI, coverage/entropy over CoM, pairwise skill divergence) to quantify skill diversity and overlap.
- Sensitivity and hyperparameters: The method likely depends on K (number of skills), αH (MI weight), grid resolution for the posterior, and the chosen subset c. A more systematic sensitivity analysis would aid practitioners.
- Sample complexity accounting: Provide total environment steps (pre-training + manager training) and compare to baselines under equal budgets. The claim of K-fold efficiency in pre-training is compelling but should be contextualized with downstream costs.
- Generality beyond locomotion: The proxy reward and MI choice are specific to locomotion (CoM). Guidance or preliminary results on other domains (manipulation) would broaden impact.
- Fixed commitment and frozen skills: The manager uses a fixed switch time T and cannot adapt skill internals. While acknowledged as future work, an experiment with learned termination or light fine-tuning would be informative.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to K, αH, and the discretization resolution for estimating p(Z|c)? Can you report empirical MI and coverage as a function of αH?
2. How do results change if c is richer than CoM (e.g., includes heading) or learned via a discriminative auxiliary network instead of discretization?
3. What is the total environment interaction budget (pre-train + downstream) relative to baselines, and how does wall-clock compare?
4. Can the manager recover when a pre-trained SNN lacks a specific needed behavior (e.g., by composing multiple skills)? Did you try continuous latent variables or mixtures to allow interpolation between skills?
5. How robust is the method to mismatch between pre-train and downstream dynamics or sensor distributions?
6. For Ant, did you try smoothing skill switches (e.g., blend actions over a short horizon) or training with switch noise during pre-training to improve robustness?

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Add quantitative diversity metrics and a short sensitivity analysis (K, αH, T).
- Report total sample counts and success rates; include at least one additional hierarchical baseline.
- Clarify architectural details (exact bilinear integration implementation, manager inputs/outputs) and provide guidance on selecting c for non-locomotion domains.
- Consider a small experiment with learned termination or end-to-end fine-tuning to demonstrate extensibility.
- Improve discussion on failure modes (skill switching instability) and simple mitigations (action blending, regularizing skills to overlap in shared state regions).

Overall, the paper proposes a simple, effective, and well-validated approach to learning reusable skills for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings, with clear empirical benefits and good ablations. I recommend acceptance.
