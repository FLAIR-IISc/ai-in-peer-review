Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles exploration and sample efficiency in sparse-reward continuous-control tasks by learning a reusable “span of skills” in a pretraining environment and then training a high-level policy to select among these skills on downstream tasks. The key idea is to parameterize the low-level policy as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) conditioned on a discrete latent variable, enabling multi-modality and weight sharing across skills.
- To avoid mode collapse and to encourage interpretable, diverse behaviors, the authors add a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of the state (e.g., the agent’s CoM position), with an efficient posterior estimator via discretized visitation counts. They also show that bilinear integration of latent and observation (rather than concatenation) is important to elicit distinct skills.
- After pretraining, a Manager network selects a latent code every T steps (skills are frozen), which reduces the effective horizon and greatly improves exploration. Using TRPO throughout, they demonstrate improved exploration and performance on hard sparse tasks (various mazes and a gather task) for Swimmer and Snake; they analyze failure modes for Ant.
- The paper is well written, ablates key design choices (concat vs bilinear, MI strength), provides code and videos, and discusses limitations and future directions.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A simple, clearly motivated method that combines multi-modal policies and MI regularization to learn diverse skills with minimal supervision, and (2) convincing empirical evidence that these skills substantially improve exploration and solve sparse-reward tasks that standard approaches fail on, with careful ablations and analysis.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper directly addresses sparse rewards and long horizons, where naïve action noise performs poorly. The idea of amortizing pretraining across tasks that share an agent subspace is sensible and broadly applicable. Using SNNs for multi-modal policies offers a principled way to share data/parameters across skills, plausibly yielding ≈K-fold sample savings vs training K independent policies.
- Novelty and placement: While SNNs and MI bonuses (e.g., InfoGAN) are known, their integration for unsupervised skill discovery in continuous control, with a simple proxy reward and a hierarchical manager, is a valuable contribution. The paper situates itself with options/HiREPS/option-critic, and contrasts with work requiring goal supervision in pretraining. The bilinear integration insight is a useful architectural contribution.
- Empirical support:
  - Skill diversity: Visitation plots show that bilinear integration and MI bonus produce distinct, interpretable locomotion skills (forward/backward/sideways/turning) with a single SNN trained with the same samples as a single unimodal policy. This directly supports the multi-modality claim and the role of the MI term.
  - Exploration: One-million-step rollouts under random managers demonstrate that SNN-based hierarchies cover much larger state space than Gaussian action noise and even than multi-policy baselines trained independently with much more data.
  - Downstream performance: In multiple mazes and gather tasks, hierarchical policies with SNN skills learn significantly faster and to higher returns than strong baselines (e.g., adding CoM-speed intrinsic reward to the downstream tasks). They also report outperforming VIME in the benchmark gather setting. The Snake results show scalability; the Ant analysis is candid about instability when switching skills.
  - Rigor and transparency: No cherry-picking of pretrains; they report variance across seeds and discuss when some pretrains lack a needed motion to solve a maze. Hyperparameters and implementation details are in the appendix; code and videos are provided.
- Limitations: Evaluation domains are still MuJoCo locomotion with relatively simple downstream tasks; the MI estimator uses a discretization heuristic; no direct comparison to contemporary HRL baselines like option-critic in these settings; manager uses fixed switch time and low-level skills are frozen. The sample-efficiency claim is empirical rather than theoretically grounded.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Baselines: Include comparisons to option-critic or other hierarchical methods on the same tasks, and to independently trained multi-policy hierarchies with matched pretraining budgets to isolate the effect of weight sharing.
- Quantification of diversity: Report quantitative metrics for skill separability/diversity (e.g., MI value, average pairwise divergence over state visitation) in addition to plots.
- Sensitivity analyses: Systematically study sensitivity to K, MI coefficient αH, grid resolution for MI estimation, and switch time T across tasks; provide guidance for practitioners.
- Sample accounting: Provide amortized sample efficiency analysis (pretraining cost vs per-task gains) and how performance scales with the number of downstream tasks.
- Robustness: Explore manager fine-tuning of low-level skills (e.g., with straight-through estimators) and learned termination policies to handle unstable agents (Ant) and dynamic tasks; consider recurrent managers to exploit history.
- Generality: Discuss applicability beyond locomotion (e.g., manipulation) and to high-dimensional observations (images), and whether MI over other state subsets is effective.

Questions for the authors
1. Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the MI grid resolution and αH? Can you provide heuristics for choosing αH and K? Does the MI estimator bias learning in highly non-uniform visitation regimes?
2. Manager design: Did you try learned termination policies (variable T) or recurrent managers? How do these impact performance and stability, especially for Ant?
3. Fine-tuning: What happens if you jointly fine-tune low-level skills with the manager using straight-through estimators? Does this reduce failure cases or improve asymptotic performance?
4. Amortization: Considering the pretraining budget, after how many downstream tasks does the method become net sample-efficient relative to training from scratch with strong intrinsic motivation?
5. Robustness to changes: Do learned skills transfer when the agent’s initial orientation or morphology slightly changes, or when mild dynamics shifts are introduced?
6. Baselines: Can you include comparisons to option-critic/HiREPS on your tasks, or explain why such comparisons are not straightforward/fair in this setting?
7. Skill diversity metrics: Can you report MI values or other quantitative measures correlating with downstream performance to better motivate the diversity objective?

Overall, the paper is well motivated, clear, and empirically substantiated, with a practical and broadly useful approach to HRL in sparse-reward settings. Despite some limitations in scope and baselines, the contributions warrant acceptance.
