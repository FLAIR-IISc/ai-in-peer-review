Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a simple but compelling framework for unsupervised skill discovery and reuse in hierarchical RL. It trains a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy with a categorical latent z to represent K skills, using bilinear integration of z with observations and an InfoGAN-inspired mutual information (MI) bonus between z and a low-dimensional state subset (agent CoM). One z is sampled per rollout to produce temporally coherent, interpretable skills.
- These skills are frozen and reused by a high-level “Manager” that selects a skill every T steps to solve sparse-reward downstream tasks. Both phases use TRPO; the latent z is treated as part of the observation in pretraining.
- Empirically, the approach yields diverse locomotion primitives without task-specific supervision and substantially improves exploration and learning on sparse mazes and gather tasks with Swimmer and Snake. Ablations show bilinear integration and MI bonus matter. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novelty and simplicity: Using an SNN with categorical latents, bilinear integration, and an MI bonus targeted to a small, semantically meaningful agent subspace is a neat, general mechanism to induce diverse, reusable skills with minimal domain knowledge.
- Impactful direction with promising evidence: The framework demonstrably expands exploration and solves challenging sparse tasks that standard methods fail on, while training K skills for the cost of one policy (effective K× savings). Even with frozen skills and fixed T, the results are competitive.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem: Efficiently solving multiple sparse-reward tasks that share agent dynamics by first learning a repertoire of agent-centric skills in a proxy environment and reusing them hierarchically.
2) Motivation and placement: The paper is well motivated by the limitations of naïve exploration and hand-designed hierarchies. It situates itself relative to options/option-critic, HiREPS, and goal-conditioned pretraining (Heess et al.), emphasizing minimal supervision (single proxy reward vs sets of goals) and a scalable continuous-control setting. The MI-based diversity bonus draws a clear line to InfoGAN.
3) Evidence and correctness:
- Skill learning: Visitation plots show clear, interpretable multimodality with bilinear integration; the MI bonus reduces overlap and adds sideways/turning skills. Sampling one z per rollout induces temporal coherence.
- Exploration: Random managers over pretrained skills cover vastly larger areas than Gaussian action noise or collections of independently trained policies, supporting the claim that SNN skills broaden exploration.
- Downstream performance: In multiple mazes and gather settings (long horizons), the hierarchical agent learns faster and more consistently than baselines (including proxy CoM reward). The approach scales to Snake; variance analyses across seeds are honest and informative. Limitations on Ant are acknowledged with sensible diagnoses.
- Methodological rigor: Ablations on integration (concat vs bilinear) and MI strength are provided; training details are transparent; code is released. While some baselines could be stronger, the qualitative and quantitative evidence supports the main claims.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
- Stronger baselines and placement: Include direct comparisons to learned options/option-critic, HiREPS on continuous control, and modern goal-conditioned methods (where applicable). Bring the VIME comparison into the main text.
- Quantify sample complexity: Report total environment steps (pretrain + downstream) to reach fixed thresholds, and compare to learning from scratch and to training K independent policies. This would back the K× efficiency claim more concretely.
- Generality of MI target: The CoM-based MI bonus is elegant but specific to locomotion. Discuss/experiment with other agent-subspaces (e.g., end-effector pose for manipulation) and with learned posteriors (MLP regression) beyond discretized counts to demonstrate broader applicability.
- Sensitivity analyses: Include results on the effects of K, α_H, grid resolution, and T, beyond the partial analysis in the appendix; add metrics like MI itself, coverage, and diversity scores.
- Stability and switching: For unstable agents (Ant), consider blending between skills, smoothing latent transitions, or training skills with broader state-distributions (e.g., randomized initializations) to improve compatibility; learning a termination policy and/or end-to-end fine-tuning would likely help.
- Manager architecture: A recurrent manager that conditions on the previous skill and observation history could be valuable; discuss this more concretely and add a small experiment if feasible.

Questions for the authors
- Sample efficiency: What are the total env steps to reach fixed performance on each downstream task, including pretraining, versus learning from scratch and versus multi-policy training? Please provide a table.
- MI hyperparameters: How sensitive are results to α_H and the grid resolution for CoM discretization? Any automatic tuning strategies?
- Role of bilinear integration: If the network capacity is increased under concatenation, does bilinear still dominate? An ablation controlling parameter count would clarify whether the gain is from expressivity or inductive bias.
- Skill count K: How does performance scale with K? Is there evidence of diminishing returns or mode collapse as K grows?
- Manager inputs: Does the manager observe the previous z (and time since switch)? If not, would including it help stability/performance?
- Incremental skills: Can the repertoire be expanded (increase K) without retraining from scratch? Any thoughts on continual skill discovery?
- Diversity metrics: Beyond visitation plots, can you report MI estimates, coverage, or entropy of skill-conditioned occupancy measures to quantify diversity?

Overall, this work puts forward a clean, novel, and practically useful approach to unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical reuse. Despite some limitations in evaluation breadth, it opens a promising direction and is well suited for ICLR.
