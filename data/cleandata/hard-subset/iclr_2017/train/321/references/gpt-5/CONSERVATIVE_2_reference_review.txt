Summary
The paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings. In a pretraining phase, a stochastic neural network (SNN) with categorical latent variables is trained with a proxy reward (e.g., center-of-mass velocity) to induce a set of diverse skills. Diversity is encouraged via an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes mutual information between the latent code and a subset of the state (CoM position), estimated via a discretized grid. In downstream tasks, a high-level “Manager” policy selects among these skills at a fixed timescale T, while low-level skills are frozen. The approach is implemented with TRPO and evaluated on continuous-control locomotion domains (Swimmer and Snake) on Maze and Gather tasks, showing improved exploration and faster learning than simple baselines. The paper includes ablations (concatenation vs bilinear integration of z, MI coefficient), qualitative “visitation” analyses, some sensitivity to T, and notes failure cases on Ant. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical evaluation is narrow and the baselines are not sufficiently strong for the claims made; comparisons to established hierarchical/exploration methods are missing or limited to restricted settings.
2) The core mechanisms rely on task-specific, ad hoc design choices (MI on CoM via coarse discretization, fixed T, frozen low-level skills) that limit generality, and the paper does not provide rigorous evidence that the approach transfers beyond toy locomotion settings.

Supporting arguments
- Baselines and fairness: The primary baseline in downstream tasks adds the proxy reward directly. This is weak for hierarchical RL. There is no systematic comparison to established HRL methods (e.g., options/Option-Critic, HiREPS) or more advanced exploration methods beyond a brief Gather-only comparison to VIME under a short horizon. Claims of improved exploration and sample efficiency would be more convincing with those baselines and with matched pretraining budgets.
- Generality and design choices: The MI bonus is computed on CoM (x, y) via discretization. This is tightly coupled to locomotion and directional motion; it is unclear how to choose c for other domains (manipulation, vision-based tasks), or how sensitive performance is to grid resolution. The paper discusses manipulators but provides no experiments outside locomotion.
- Evidence and rigor: The skill diversity is mostly qualitative (visitation plots). There is no quantitative metric of diversity or disentanglement (e.g., MI estimates, coverage, state-conditional separability), nor statistical testing. Reported results show high variance, and the method sometimes fails depending on the pretrained SNN seed; the paper acknowledges this but does not mitigate it. The claim that SNN training gives K-fold gains in sample efficiency is asserted but not demonstrated with a controlled sample budget comparison against training K independent skills.
- Scope of evaluation: Results are primarily on Swimmer and Snake, with Ant presented as a failure case. There is limited analysis of sensitivity to key hyperparameters (K, α for MI, T beyond a small study), and no results with image observations or tasks where proxy rewards are less obvious. The manager uses a fixed switching period; learned terminations or joint fine-tuning are deferred to future work despite being central to modern HRL.
- Novelty and positioning: Using SNNs to induce multi-modal policies and maximizing MI between latent codes and states is a reasonable idea and related to InfoGAN-style regularization, but conceptually incremental relative to prior HRL work on skill discovery and diversity. The paper cites related work but does not empirically situate the method against option learning or RECENT hierarchical controllers; this undermines the case for publication.

Additional feedback (to help improve the work)
- Strengthen baselines: include Option-Critic, HiREPS, and state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods under the same horizons and environments; if possible, include hierarchical methods with learned terminations. Ensure equalized pretraining and downstream sample budgets.
- Quantify diversity: report MI estimates, conditional classification accuracy of z from states, state-space coverage metrics, and relation between diversity and downstream returns.
- Generalize the MI regularizer: avoid hand-picked c; consider learning a discriminator p(z|s) (classifier) or density model for broader domains. Analyze sensitivity to grid resolution and α.
- Robustness and sensitivity: provide systematic studies of K, T, α, bilinear vs concatenation, and manager architecture. Report confidence intervals over sufficient seeds; reduce dependence on “lucky” pretrained SNNs.
- Task diversity: include manipulation or navigation with distractors; consider image-based observations with an encoder. Demonstrate transfer across tasks differing more substantially than maze layouts.
- Learn terminations and/or allow limited low-level adaptation; show whether end-to-end fine-tuning materially improves stability (especially for Ant) and performance.
- Clarify the sample-efficiency claim with controlled comparisons versus training K independent policies under fixed total interaction budgets.

Questions for the authors
1) How does performance compare to Option-Critic or HiREPS on your tasks under matched pretraining and downstream budgets? If not feasible, why?
2) How is the CoM grid resolution chosen, and how sensitive are results to this choice and to α? Can you replace the discretization with a learned p(z|s) classifier and obtain similar gains?
3) How does K impact both pretraining (mode collapse) and downstream learning (manager action space size)? Is there an optimal range?
4) Can you provide quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., accuracy of inferring z from s, MI estimates, state coverage) and correlate them with downstream returns?
5) Under equal total samples, how do SNN-pretrained skills compare to training K independent skills (multi-policy) in terms of both diversity and downstream performance?
6) Have you tried learned terminations or limited low-level fine-tuning for unstable agents (e.g., Ant)? Does this mitigate failure modes?
7) Beyond locomotion, what is your choice of c for manipulation or visual tasks, and do the skills remain interpretable/useful?
