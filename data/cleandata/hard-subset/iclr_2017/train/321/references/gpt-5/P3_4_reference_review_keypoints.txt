- Paper proposes learning reusable low-level skills via a stochastic neural network (SNN) with discrete latent z and composing them with a high-level manager for sparse, long-horizon RL tasks.
- Skills use a feed-forward policy conditioned on an episode-sampled latent z and bilinear integration of z with observations for multi-modal, weight-shared behaviors.
- An information-theoretic mutual-information (MI) bonus between z and selected agent-centric features (e.g., CoM x,y) with discretized visitation counts prevents mode collapse and encourages distinct skills.
- During transfer the SNN is frozen and a categorical manager selects skills every T steps; TRPO is used for all training.
- Empirically produces interpretable, diverse locomotion behaviors (directional gaits) and substantially improves exploration and performance on sparse tasks (multiple mazes, Gather).
- Ablations show bilinear integration and the MI bonus materially improve skill diversity and downstream task performance.
- Some scalability evidence provided (Snake); Ant experiments reveal instability limitations discussed candidly.
- Decision: Weak accept, based on practical, timely idea with solid empirical ablations and demonstrated reuse.
- Strength: Addresses a significant problem (sparse rewards/long horizons) with minimal instrumentation and plausible amortized exploration across tasks.
- Methodologically sound choices: SNN for multimodality, treating z as observation to preserve Markovianity, and an interpretable MI signal on CoM.
- Manager fixed-commitment design is simple and effective for static tasks, with authors noting possible extensions.
- Qualitative validation: visitation plots and exploration rollouts convincingly show coverage gains over Gaussian noise and that skills act as macro-actions.
- Comparisons beat reasonable baselines including a proxy-reward downstream baseline; Snake results suggest scaling, Ant shows limits.
- Positioning: well-situated relative to options, macro-actions, intrinsic motivation; novelty lies in MI-pretraining for skills and bilinear conditioning.
- Key caveat: MI estimator relies on hand-chosen, discretized state features (CoM) with limited sensitivity analysis, possibly biasing spatial skills.
- Quantitative diversity metrics are lacking (no MI estimates, coverage stats, or held-out skill distinguishability measures).
- Claim of K-fold sample savings over training K independent policies is not directly supported by controlled experiments.
- Comparisons to some HRL/option-discovery baselines (e.g., option-critic) are limited, weakening comparative claims.
- Manager is non-recurrent and fixed-commitment; no experiments on learned termination or joint fine-tuning are provided.
- High variance across pretraining seeds affects downstream success in some mazes, indicating robustness concerns.
- Suggested improvements: report estimated MI, coverage, classifier-based distinguishability, option usage entropy, and sensitivity to MI weight and grid resolution.
- Suggested experiments: vary K to show scaling/overfit, compare to option-critic/macro-action baselines, demonstrate joint fine-tuning or learned termination, and compare sample-efficiency vs K independent policies.
- Implementation clarifications requested: bilinear integration details, computational cost, and comparisons to multiplicative gating or FiLM-like conditioning.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to choice of state subset c and discretization, scaling with K, quantified K-fold savings, sensitivity to αH and grid, experiments on recurrent manager/learned termination, and ways to mitigate Ant instability.
