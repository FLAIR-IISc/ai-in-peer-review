Summary and contributions
- Problem: How to efficiently solve multiple sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks by learning reusable, diverse low-level skills without task-specific shaping.
- Approach: Pretrain a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy with a categorical latent z on a proxy reward (e.g., CoM speed). Encourage skill diversity with an information-theoretic regularizer: a mutual information (MI) bonus between z and a subset of agent state (CoM position), estimated via simple discretized counts. Use a bilinear integration of z and observations to increase expressivity. For downstream tasks, freeze the low-level SNN and train a high-level “Manager” that selects a skill every T steps with TRPO.
- Claims: (1) SNNs plus MI reliably yield multiple interpretable, temporally consistent skills; (2) hierarchical selection of these skills substantially improves exploration and sample-efficiency on sparse tasks; (3) this yields strong performance on challenging locomotion mazes and a gather task; (4) pretraining is sample efficient (learning K skills with roughly the cost of one policy) and requires minimal domain knowledge (generic proxy reward).
- Evidence: Ablations on integration (concat vs bilinear) and MI strength; skill visitation plots showing distinct modes; exploration coverage under random managers; learning curves on multiple mazes and gather; analysis of switch time T; scaling to a higher-DoF Snake; discussion of failure modes on Ant. Code and hyperparameters provided.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: The paper presents a simple, well-motivated, and practically effective framework that learns diverse reusable skills and leverages them hierarchically to make real progress on sparse-reward continuous control benchmarks. The MI-regularized SNN plus bilinear integration is a neat design choice that clearly improves diversity, and the empirical study is careful (ablations, multiple seeds, code release).

Supporting arguments
- Significance and originality: Learning a family of skills via an SNN conditioned on discrete latents and incentivized by MI, then composing them via a high-level policy, is a pragmatic and novel combination at the time. The MI objective is well tailored to induce interpretable, directionally distinct locomotion skills without goal instrumentation. The bilinear integration insight is simple and impactful.
- Motivation and related work: The paper is well situated relative to options/HRL, intrinsic motivation, and prior skill discovery (HiREPS, option-critic). It explicitly contrasts with methods requiring goal sets or demonstrations and motivates pretraining on a proxy reward shared across tasks. Some contemporaneous works (e.g., end-to-end option learning) are discussed and future directions are identified.
- Empirical support: 
  - Skill diversity: Clear qualitative evidence that bilinear+MI produces multiple distinct locomotion modes; ablations show concat struggles; MI reduces skill overlap. 
  - Exploration: One-million step trajectories under random managers cover far more area than Gaussian action noise or multi-policy baselines; skill switches visibly change direction.
  - Downstream performance: On several mazes and gather tasks, hierarchical policies outperform strong baselines (including proxy reward shaping and, in the benchmark setting, VIME), with results averaged over multiple pretrain seeds and manager seeds. Analysis of T suggests robustness; scaling to Snake shows similar benefits.
- Rigor and clarity: The algorithm is clearly described, with TRPO adaptation, MI posterior estimation, and training protocol. Hyperparameters and code are provided. The paper candidly reports failure modes on Ant and variance across seeds.

Reservations and limitations
- Scope and generality: Most successes are on locomotion with relatively simple agents (Swimmer, Snake). Transfer to more complex/unstable robots (Ant) is challenging due to switching-induced instability; this limits immediate generality.
- Quantitative diversity metrics: Diversity is demonstrated mainly via visitation plots; no explicit quantitative MI estimates, state coverage metrics, or skill dissimilarity measures are reported.
- Baselines: While intrinsic motivation baselines are considered, there is no empirical comparison to other HRL/skill-discovery methods (e.g., option-critic, HiREPS) on the same tasks. Also, the “K-times” sample efficiency claim for pretraining is intuitive but not quantified.
- Sensitivity: Limited analysis of sensitivity to K, MI coefficient αH, grid resolution for MI estimation, and bilinear embedding dimensionality.
- Fixed skills and switch time: Freezing skills and fixing T simplifies training but may cap performance; the paper acknowledges this and suggests future end-to-end training.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Add quantitative diversity measures: e.g., mutual information estimates, pairwise disagreement in action distributions across skills, state coverage metrics, or skill confusion accuracy.
- Report sensitivity studies: Vary K, αH, grid density, and bilinear embedding size; include learning curves and diversity metrics.
- Strengthen baselines: Include option-critic or other hierarchical methods; add a monolithic policy with parameter noise or long-horizon action persistence; compare to training multiple independent skills with an MI bonus but without SNN weight sharing.
- Clarify sample efficiency: Report wall-clock time and environment steps for pretraining vs training K independent policies, including manager training cost, to substantiate the claimed efficiency.
- Stability strategies: For unstable agents, try skill warm-start blending, short-term action smoothing at switch time, or learning skill-specific initial stabilizers; report whether such simple fixes help Ant.
- Generality: Consider a non-locomotion pretrain proxy (e.g., manipulators) or tasks where sideways/turning skills are essential to underscore transfer breadth.

Questions for the authors
1) MI estimation: How sensitive is performance to the CoM grid resolution and αH? Did you try learning p(z|c) with a parametric classifier instead of counts, and does it change stability or results?
2) Non-stationarity: Since MI is computed per batch, does the moving bonus introduce instability for TRPO? Any variance reduction tricks used?
3) K selection: How did you choose K=6? What happens when K is larger than the number of distinguishable skills? Do some latents become unused?
4) Switch time T: Beyond the appendix, how does T interact with different task dynamics (e.g., moving goals)? Any evidence that learning a termination policy significantly helps?
5) Manager inputs: Does the manager need full Srest? How does performance change if the manager only sees Sagent vs full state?
6) Transfer robustness: Are skills invariant to initial orientation or modest changes in dynamics (e.g., mass/friction)? Did you try domain shifts between pretrain and downstream?
7) Ant instability: Did you observe that some specific skills are particularly brittle at switch time? Would a brief overlap period where both old and new skills blend reduce falls?
8) Computational cost: What is the training time overhead of bilinear integration vs concat and of SNN vs multiple independent policies?

Overall, the paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and empirically convincing within its scope. I recommend acceptance after addressing the above questions/clarifications.
