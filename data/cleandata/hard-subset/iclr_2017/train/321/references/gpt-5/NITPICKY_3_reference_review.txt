Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon RL by learning a reusable set of low-level skills in a pretraining environment, then training a high-level “manager” that selects among these skills to solve downstream tasks.
- Skills are implemented as Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a discrete latent code sampled per rollout and held fixed, enabling multimodal action distributions with weight sharing. Two integration schemes are considered: concatenation and a bilinear (outer-product) interaction.
- To avoid mode collapse and encourage distinct skills, the authors add a mutual information (MI) bonus I(Z; C) between the latent code Z and a subset of state coordinates C (CoM (x,y)), approximated via discretized visitation counts; the corresponding log posterior term is added to the reward.
- High-level training freezes the low-level SNN and uses TRPO to learn a categorical manager that switches skills every T steps.
- Empirical results on Swimmer in Maze and Gather environments (and some Snake/Ant appendices) show that bilinear SNNs + MI produce diverse, interpretable locomotion skills, broaden random exploration under a random manager, and speed up learning on sparse tasks compared to baselines (e.g., adding CoM-speed intrinsic reward; VIME in an appendix).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Substantial issues in empirical rigor and baselines, limited and fragile evaluation (mostly toy locomotion tasks; Ant fails), and several unsupported efficiency claims; (2) Numerous methodological and presentation problems (mutual information estimator lacks smoothing, fairness of bilinear vs concatenation unaddressed, many typos/formatting/reference errors) that undermine clarity and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Empirical scope and baselines
  - Main results are restricted to Swimmer and Snake in maze/gather-like tasks; the more challenging Ant setting reveals instability and switching failures. This makes it hard to conclude robustness or broad applicability of the framework.
  - Baselines are weak for hierarchical/skill learning: comparisons to contemporaneous HRL or skill-discovery methods (e.g., options/option-critic, other MI-based skill discovery approaches, feudal/latent HRL hierarchies) are missing. VIME is included only for Gather and under a specific configuration.
  - Claims that SNN training is “K times better” in sample efficiency than training K separate policies are not substantiated. Since each latent code is sampled uniformly, only ~1/K data per skill is gathered per batch, and increased capacity could confound the comparison. No quantitative pretrain cost vs. quality trade-offs are provided.
  - High variance is acknowledged; however, there are no confidence intervals/error bands in the main learning curves and no statistical tests, making it difficult to assess significance.
- Methodological details and correctness
  - MI bonus: p̂(Z|C) is estimated by discretized visitation counts. The paper does not describe smoothing or pseudocounts; log p̂ can be −∞ if mc(z)=0 for a visited cell. This is a critical omission.
  - The MI estimation is nonstationary and policy-dependent; while acceptable as a reward bonus, its stability and sensitivity (e.g., to grid resolution “10 divisions/unit”, unit calibration) are not analyzed. The chosen C=(x,y) is task-specific and may bias skills toward spatial diversity while ignoring orientation or other relevant factors; there is no general guidance.
  - Bilinear integration vs concatenation: bilinear dramatically increases representational capacity. The paper does not control for parameter count, making it unclear whether benefits arise from true multiplicative interactions or simply from more parameters. No ablation normalizing parameter budgets is provided.
  - Manager with fixed commit time T: most experiments use large T (e.g., 500) without principled selection; sensitivity analyses are pushed to an appendix and limited. For dynamic tasks, fixed T is a strong assumption; learning termination or variable-duration execution is not explored.
  - Treating z as part of the observation for TRPO avoids mixture-of-Gaussians issues, but pretraining is then a mixture of stationary subpolicies observed through a nonstationary bonus. This is plausible, but the reasoning about stability and variance could be deepened.
- Assumptions and transfer
  - The factorization Sagent vs Srest and “weak interaction” are not operationally defined nor tested. Transfer is evaluated only across closely related locomotion tasks in the same domain; claims of generality (e.g., manipulation with proxy grasp reward) are not empirically supported.
- Clarity, formatting, and references
  - The draft has many typos and grammatical errors (“signle”, “coeficient”, “sparcity”, “SILL LEARNING”, “Mulit-policy”, “preform”), repeated footnotes in-line (“Code available…”), and inconsistent sectioning.
  - References are severely malformed (stray tokens like “? \Q2016\E”, inconsistent author/year formatting, truncated titles, punctuation errors, unmatched parentheses). This makes it hard to verify placement in the literature.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide proper smoothing for p̂(Z|C) (e.g., Dirichlet prior or additive ε) and report sensitivity to grid resolution, scaling of (x,y), and αH. Consider learning p̂ with a small classifier regressor and stopping gradients to avoid instability.
- Normalize for parameter count when comparing concatenation vs bilinear; add an ablation with equal capacity, and report compute overheads.
- Expand baselines to include more HRL/skill-discovery approaches; if infeasible, at least include learned-options/termination and diverse-skill discovery methods with MI objectives.
- Strengthen tasks: demonstrate on more challenging continuous-control domains with stability (e.g., Walker2d/Humanoid variants) and show successful switching; if Ant remains fragile, incorporate mechanisms to stabilize switching (e.g., warm-start adapters, transition policies).
- Quantify pretraining cost vs downstream benefits: report sample counts, per-skill data, and wall-clock. Evaluate sensitivity to K and provide guidance on setting it.
- Improve statistical reporting: show mean±95% CI across seeds, number of seeds per plot, and significance tests where appropriate. Reduce reliance on single long-rollout visitation plots in favor of quantitative coverage metrics.
- Clean up writing, figures, and references; ensure URLs are placed as footnotes or in appendix, not in main text flow.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you prevent log p̂(Z|c) from diverging when a cell was not visited with a given z? Do you use pseudocounts or clipping? What is the impact on stability and performance?
2) How sensitive are results to the MI grid resolution and to the scaling of (x,y)? Did you try MI on other state subsets (e.g., orientation, end-effector pose) and how did that affect skill diversity and downstream performance?
3) Does bilinear integration outperform concatenation when parameter counts are matched? Please provide a capacity-controlled ablation.
4) What is the empirical pretraining sample cost relative to training K independent policies to comparable diversity/quality? Please report per-skill data utilization.
5) How is K chosen? What happens when K is larger than the number of distinct modes the proxy allows? Do redundant codes collapse or split existing modes?
6) Can the manager learn variable-duration execution (learned termination) or options-style termination? Any preliminary results?
7) For Ant, did you try gradual switching (interpolation/warm-up), state adapters, or curriculum over switch frequency to mitigate instability?
