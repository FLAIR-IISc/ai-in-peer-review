1) Summary of contributions
The paper tackles exploration and sample efficiency in sparse-reward RL by learning a reusable set of temporally extended skills in a pre-training phase, then training a high-level policy to select among these skills on downstream tasks. The key ideas are:
- Use stochastic neural networks (SNNs) with a discrete latent variable z as policy input to represent a multimodal, skill-conditioned policy. A bilinear integration between observations and z provides stronger conditioning than simple concatenation.
- Encourage diversity and interpretability of skills via a mutual information (MI) regularizer between z and a subset of states (e.g., robot CoM position), implemented as a per-step reward bonus proportional to log p(z|c), estimated with simple discretized counts.
- Freeze the learned skill policy and train a high-level “Manager” to select a skill every T steps, improving exploration and reducing effective horizon on sparse tasks.
- Empirical results show: (i) SNNs with bilinear integration + MI produce diverse, interpretable locomotion behaviors; (ii) hierarchical skill selection dramatically enlarges exploration coverage; (iii) improved performance on sparse downstream tasks (several mazes and a gather setting) versus baselines, with ablations on latent integration and MI strength. Code and videos are provided.

2) Decision
Accept.
Key reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated framework that effectively learns diverse skills with minimal supervision and demonstrates clear empirical gains on challenging sparse-reward tasks.
- Strong ablations and qualitative/quantitative evidence that the architectural choices (SNN multimodality, bilinear integration, MI regularization) materially improve both skill diversity and downstream exploration.

3) Supporting arguments
Significance/fit:
- The problem (exploration and transfer via skills/options) is central in RL. The proposed formulation—unsupervised/pre-trained skill learning with minimal task-specific information, then hierarchical reuse—is highly relevant for ICLR.

Novelty/positioning:
- Using SNNs as latent-conditioned policies to induce multimodality, and maximizing I(Z;C) in an RL setting to encourage skill diversity, is a compelling combination. The framing of MI on a small, semantically chosen subset of the state (CoM) is pragmatic and easy to implement. Related works (HiREPS, option-critic, goal-conditioned pretraining) are discussed; the contrast (no external goals during pretraining; simple proxy reward; MI for diversity) is clear. While individual ingredients (latent conditioning, MI regularization) are known, their integration for skill discovery + hierarchical reuse is a coherent and practically useful contribution.

Technical soundness:
- Treating z as part of the observation to keep TRPO tractable is sensible. The MI bonus via discretized p̂(z|c) is a straightforward, low-variance surrogate; while approximate, it aligns with the goal of making skills state-distinguishable. The hierarchical manager is standard and clearly defined. Claims are empirical, not theoretical, and are appropriately scoped.

Empirical evaluation:
- The paper provides multiple lines of evidence: visitation plots to illustrate diversity; exploration coverage comparisons; learning curves on multiple mazes and gather tasks; ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, MI coefficient, switch time T); some transfer to a more complex robot (Snake). The results show that the method solves tasks where vanilla exploration fails and outperforms a strong intrinsic-reward baseline. Reporting multiple seeds and analyzing variance is appreciated. Limitations on unstable agents (Ant) are candidly discussed.

Clarity and reproducibility:
- The method is clearly described, with algorithm pseudocode, hyperparameters, and code release. The rationale for proxy rewards and the MI regularizer is explained with intuitive interpretations.

Limitations:
- Evaluation domains are mostly locomotion-centric continuous control with a locomotion-oriented MI target (CoM), which limits generality claims (e.g., to manipulation). The MI estimator relies on discretization and tuning of α; sensitivity is not fully quantified. The high-level policy uses fixed commit time T and no learned termination; skills are frozen, and end-to-end fine-tuning is not explored. Comparisons to contemporaneous hierarchical baselines (e.g., option-critic) are limited. Variance across pre-trained SNNs is non-trivial in some tasks.

On balance, the strengths outweigh these limitations for an ICLR contribution.

4) Feedback to improve the paper (not gating the decision)
- Provide quantitative diversity metrics for the learned skills (e.g., mutual information achieved, state coverage entropy, trajectory diversity) alongside visitation plots.
- Expand sensitivity analyses: K (number of skills), MI coefficient α, discretization granularity, and the bilinear integration dimensionality. Report how these choices trade off diversity vs performance.
- Broaden comparisons to hierarchical baselines (e.g., option-critic, HiREPS) and curiosity/count-based methods on the same tasks with matched budgets.
- Discuss generalization beyond locomotion: how to choose the MI target C for manipulation or visual tasks; consider learning C (e.g., via unsupervised embeddings) rather than handpicking CoM.
- Report sample complexity more explicitly (environment steps to solve) and wall-clock considerations for SNN training vs multiple independent policies.
- Consider learned terminations or variable commitment to reduce brittleness and improve responsiveness, and provide preliminary results if possible.
- Improve clarity on bilinear integration details (shapes, parameter counts, initialization), to aid reproduction.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to K, α, and the cell grid resolution for p̂(z|c)? Are there failure modes (e.g., too many skills leading to redundancy or fragmentation)?
- Can you provide quantitative metrics of diversity (beyond visuals), and correlate them with downstream performance?
- How does the approach fare when C is high-dimensional (e.g., contact states, object pose) and discretization is impractical? Have you tried learned density models for p(z|c) or regressors as briefly mentioned?
- How robust is transfer if the pretraining environment dynamics differ moderately from downstream tasks (e.g., different friction or minor morphology changes)?
- Did you attempt light end-to-end fine-tuning of the low-level SNN during downstream training? Does it reduce variance across seeds or help on unstable robots?
- Can you compare to option-critic or other hierarchical methods on at least one shared benchmark to contextualize gains?
- For Ant, did longer commitment times or cautious switching policies materially help? Any evidence that learning a switching/termination policy in pretraining stabilizes downstream use?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clearly presented paper with a practical and effective method for skill discovery and reuse that improves exploration in sparse-reward settings. I recommend acceptance.
