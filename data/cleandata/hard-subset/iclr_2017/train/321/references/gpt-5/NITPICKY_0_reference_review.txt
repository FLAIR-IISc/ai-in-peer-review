Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a two-phase framework for hierarchical RL in sparse-reward settings: (i) pretrain a low-level skill policy as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) conditioned on a discrete latent z, using only a simple proxy reward (e.g., speed magnitude) and an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage diverse skills; (ii) freeze these skills and train a high-level “Manager” that selects a skill every T steps to solve downstream sparse tasks. 
- The SNN uses a bilinear integration between observation and latent code to increase expressivity and encourage distinct behaviors per z. Diversity is further promoted by adding a mutual information (MI) bonus between z and a subset of the state (CoM (x, y)), implemented with a discretized posterior p̂(z|c).
- Experiments on Swimmer (and in appendices Snake and Ant) show that the learned skills are interpretable (e.g., different locomotion directions) and that hierarchical policies improve exploration and performance on mazes and a gather task compared to baselines. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical evaluation and evidence are not sufficiently rigorous to support the central claims on sample efficiency and skill diversity; several comparisons are unfair or under-specified. (2) The methodological choices for the MI bonus and the SNN integration, while plausible, are insufficiently analyzed and reported (sensitivity, ablations, metrics), and many critical implementation details are missing.

Supporting arguments
- Evidence for “K-times” sample efficiency is asserted but not measured. Training a K-skill SNN is claimed to cost roughly that of a single policy, yet there is no wall-clock, sample, or stability comparison versus training K separate policies to equal performance. The multi-policy baseline uses 6× more pretraining samples, making comparisons potentially unfair.
- Skill diversity is presented largely via qualitative visitation plots. There is no quantitative metric (e.g., MI estimates, state-coverage, pairwise trajectory divergence, option usage entropy) to substantiate the diversity claim or to compare concatenation vs bilinear vs varying αH. The conclusion that bilinear+MI “consistently” yields diverse skills lacks statistical backing.
- MI bonus design is ad hoc and under-specified. The reward term log p̂(z|c) depends on batch-level visitation counts over a discretized CoM grid. This introduces non-stationarity and potential bias (reward depends on other trajectories), and its sensitivity to grid resolution, bounds, and batch size is not analyzed. The scale of αH and its tuning are not reported systematically; units (“10 divisions/unit”) are ambiguous.
- Evaluation scope is limited. Main results are on Swimmer mazes and a gather task with sparse rewards; these are relatively simple, and improvements are plausible but not conclusive. The manager’s fixed switch time T=500 is large and task-specific; only limited sensitivity analysis is provided in appendices for Snake. The negative results/failure modes on Ant highlight brittleness when switching skills, but no mitigation is demonstrated.
- Baselines are weak or mismatched. Aside from a “proxy reward in-task” baseline and a brief appendix reference to VIME in Gather, there is no comparison to prominent hierarchical/skill discovery methods referenced (e.g., Option-Critic, HiREPS). The manager’s random exploration coverage (1M-step trajectory plots) is anecdotal and not tied to success likelihood or sample complexity.
- Claims about generality are not supported beyond locomotion. The method presumes a state subset for the MI term (e.g., CoM (x, y)); it is unclear how to choose this in other domains (e.g., manipulation) without domain knowledge, and no experiments demonstrate transfer to such domains.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues: several hyperparameters are missing (αH per experiment, value function/GAE settings, exact discretization ranges, normalization, entropy regularization for the manager, termination when skills fail). K=6 is fixed with no sensitivity. The bilinear integration’s dimensionality and parameter count are not matched against concatenation to rule out capacity effects. References have multiple formatting errors and typos; some footnotes and figures are referenced ambiguously.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are part of the decision)
- Provide quantitative diversity metrics and report MI estimates during training. Include option usage entropy and state-coverage measures, plus success probability vs budget.
- Report full sample accounting: pretraining cost amortized over N tasks; plot performance vs number of downstream tasks to justify the “uniformly reduced sample complexity” claim.
- Add ablations: K (number of skills), αH, grid resolution and bounds, switch time T, freezing vs fine-tuning low-level, concatenation vs bilinear with matched capacity. Include manager entropy regularization and temperature studies to avoid option collapse.
- Fair baselines: (i) equal total sample budgets (pretrain + downstream) for multi-policy vs SNN; (ii) comparisons to Option-Critic/HiREPS on the same tasks; (iii) a manager over hand-crafted directional velocity primitives to isolate benefits from SNN skill learning; (iv) a latent-conditioned policy without MI bonus but with increased capacity.
- For MI estimation, consider density models or classifiers p(z|f(s)) beyond coarse grids; analyze bias/variance and stabilization (e.g., EMA of counts, separate target network).
- For unstable agents, explore conservative switching (e.g., terminate at safe states), transition policies, or learning skill initiation/termination conditions. Show quantitative improvements on Ant or a similar challenging domain.
- Clarify manager training details (discrete TRPO setup, baselines/advantages, value function architecture), reward scaling, and the role of observation normalization.
- Fix numerous typos and reference formatting inconsistencies; ensure figures/appendices are self-contained and clearly labeled.

Questions for the authors
1) Sample efficiency: What is the exact pretraining sample count and wall-clock for training the SNN vs K independent policies to comparable skill quality? Can you provide an equal-budget comparison across pretraining strategies?
2) MI bonus: What αH values were used per experiment and how sensitive are results to αH and grid resolution/range? How is the (x, y) domain bounded for discretization, and how do you prevent degenerate estimates in rarely visited cells?
3) Bilinear vs concatenation: Are parameter counts matched? Could improved diversity be a capacity effect? Please provide quantitative diversity metrics to support the superiority of bilinear integration.
4) K sensitivity: How do performance and diversity vary with K? Do skills collapse or become redundant as K increases?
5) Manager details: What entropy regularization/temperature do you use? Do learned managers exploit all skills or collapse to a subset? Please include option usage statistics.
6) Amortization across tasks: Can you show that, as the number of downstream tasks grows, the upfront pretraining improves total sample efficiency compared to training from scratch?
7) Robustness: For Ant, did you try constraining switches to stable phases or adding a transition/stabilization sub-policy? Any quantitative evidence that such mechanisms help?
8) Generality: How would you choose the MI state subset in non-locomotion domains (e.g., manipulation)? Do you have preliminary results beyond locomotion demonstrating this?
