1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a simple, well-motivated framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning in sparse-reward settings. The key idea is to pretrain a single multi-modal policy that spans a set of reusable “skills” using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with a categorical latent code, then learn a high-level “manager” that selects among these skills for downstream tasks. To encourage diversity and interpretability of skills, the authors add an information-theoretic regularizer: a mutual-information-inspired bonus that maximizes predictability of the latent code from a subset of the state (e.g., the robot’s center-of-mass (CoM) position). A bilinear integration of the latent code with observations yields more expressive skill conditioning than simple concatenation. Pretraining uses only a proxy reward (e.g., speed magnitude), requiring minimal domain knowledge, and skills are frozen when training the manager. The method improves exploration and sample efficiency in sparse downstream tasks (Swimmer/Snake Maze and Gather), outperforming baselines that use naive exploration and a strong intrinsic-motivation baseline. The paper includes ablations (latent integration and MI coefficient), analysis of switching horizon T, and qualitative/quantitative assessments of skill diversity (visitation plots), plus discussions of limitations (e.g., Ant instability).

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The approach is simple, novel in its combination (SNN with categorical latent code + MI-based bonus on a state subset + bilinear integration), and well-motivated for sparse-reward HRL. It produces clearly diverse, interpretable skills that can be composed by a manager to solve tasks that standard deep RL fails on.
- Empirical results consistently support the claims, with clear evidence that pretraining yields diverse skills and that the hierarchical controller substantially improves exploration and performance on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks, with code provided.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper directly tackles the exploration and long-horizon credit assignment challenges via temporal abstraction and skill reuse, with minimal task-specific reward design. It is well positioned relative to options/HRL (MAXQ, option-critic, HiREPS), intrinsic motivation (VIME, count-based), and skill discovery; the InfoGAN-inspired MI objective is appropriate and clearly explained.
- Technical soundness and clarity:
  - SNN with a categorical latent z represents multi-modal Gaussian policies while sharing weights; sampling z once per rollout gives temporally consistent skills.
  - Bilinear latent–observation integration usefully increases expressivity over concatenation; experiments show it matters.
  - The MI bonus is implemented as −H(Z|C), estimated via discretized CoM visitation counts. Treating z as part of the observation avoids complications in TRPO.
  - The manager selects z every T steps, reducing effective horizon and improving exploration; freezing the low-level skills keeps training simple and stable.
- Empirical support:
  - Pretraining yields diverse locomotion primitives (forward/backward/sideways), with MI increasing skill separability; visitation plots are clear and convincing.
  - Hierarchical rollouts cover much larger areas than Gaussian action noise; most skill switches produce behavior changes.
  - On sparse Maze and Gather tasks, the hierarchical approach significantly outperforms the proxy-reward baseline and intrinsic motivation baselines (VIME) under comparable settings. The SNN hierarchy generally outperforms a “multi-policy” hierarchy trained from scratch, while using fewer pretraining samples (K skills for the cost of one).
  - Snake results show scalability; Ant analysis and failure modes are honest and informative.
  - Multiple seeds are used and variance is reported; no cherry-picking of pretrained SNNs.

4. Suggestions to improve (not necessarily decision-critical)
- Evaluation breadth:
  - Current downstream tasks are locomotion-centric; demonstrating transfer beyond locomotion (e.g., manipulation proxy rewards) would better support the “wide collection of tasks” claim.
  - Include stronger hierarchical baselines if feasible (e.g., option-critic), or discuss why they are not directly applicable to sparse rewards considered here.
- Fairness and reporting:
  - Equalize pretraining sample budgets in comparisons with the “multi-policy” baseline (or clearly normalize results by total samples) to remove confounds.
  - Provide a quantitative diversity metric (e.g., MI estimate, state coverage, pairwise behavior distance) in addition to visitation plots.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Report sensitivity to αH, grid resolution for p(Z|C), number of skills K, and commit horizon T (the appendix partially addresses T; consolidating main insights in the body would help).
  - Compare bilinear integration to deeper/wider concatenation networks to isolate whether expressivity or inductive bias drives gains.
- Methodological details:
  - Clarify smoothing of p̂(Z|C) to avoid degenerate log probabilities, and how grid resolution was chosen.
  - Discuss replacing the discretized posterior with a learned classifier/density model or a variational MI lower bound.
  - Consider a learned termination policy or variable T; show a small experiment with end-to-end fine-tuning of low-level skills (even if unstable) to quantify potential gains.
- Discussion:
  - The “minimal domain knowledge” claim depends on choosing the proxy reward and the state subset C; discuss how to choose C in domains where CoM is not meaningful, and failure cases when C is misspecified.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of C used in the MI bonus? Have you tried using the full agent state or learned embeddings to infer Z?
- How did you choose the grid resolution for p̂(Z|C), and do you apply smoothing or priors to avoid zero counts? How robust are results to these choices?
- Can you quantify skill diversity (MI, coverage, or behavior distances) and correlate it with downstream performance?
- How do results change when equalizing the total number of pretraining samples between the SNN and “multi-policy” baseline?
- What is the sensitivity to K? Do redundant or unused skills harm the manager’s learning?
- Would a learned termination policy (options-style) or variable T improve Maze/Gather performance, especially in larger or dynamic environments?
- Have you tried concatenation with larger networks to match bilinear capacity? Is bilinear still superior?
- Beyond locomotion, can you demonstrate transfer to a different domain (e.g., simple manipulation) with a different proxy reward and C definition?

Overall, this paper proposes a clear, well-motivated, and practically useful approach to skill discovery and reuse for sparse-reward HRL, with solid empirical evidence and open-sourced code. The limitations are acknowledged and suggest promising future directions. I recommend acceptance.
