Summary
The paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning in sparse-reward settings. In pre-training, a policy is modeled as a stochastic neural network (SNN) that takes a latent categorical variable z and uses either concatenation or bilinear integration to modulate a Gaussian action policy. To encourage diversity of skills, the authors add an intrinsic reward term proportional to the mutual information between z and a subset of the state (center-of-mass coordinates), estimated via simple discretization and counts. In downstream tasks, the low-level SNN is frozen and a high-level “Manager” selects a skill (z) to execute for T steps. Using TRPO for both stages, the approach is evaluated on Swimmer and Snake in maze and gather tasks, showing improved exploration and better performance compared to a proxy-reward baseline and a “multi-policy” baseline that trains multiple unimodal policies separately. The authors release code and provide ablations on integration type and MI strength.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Limited novelty and insufficient positioning relative to established HRL and skill discovery literature, with core ideas (latent-conditioned multimodal policies; MI-based diversity; fixed-duration options) being incremental compositions of known ingredients.
2) Empirical evaluation lacks rigorous comparisons to strong, established baselines and does not convincingly substantiate key claims (e.g., sample efficiency “K×” gains, robust transfer across tasks), with high variance and acknowledged failure modes on more challenging agents.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: The use of latent-conditioned policies to represent multi-modality is well established; the MI objective to induce diverse behaviors closely echoes prior work (e.g., InfoGAN-inspired objectives; HiREPS-constrained formulations; options/termination frameworks). The main architectural twist—bilinear integration—appears as an engineering choice and is demonstrated via qualitative visitation plots; its conceptual contribution over standard conditioning is modest.
- Claims vs. evidence:
  • Sample efficiency: The paper states that training an SNN yields “effectively K× better” sample efficiency than training K policies. However, no quantitative sample complexity analysis is provided across equalized budgets (including MI estimation overhead), nor are compute/time costs reported. The multi-policy baseline also differs in parameter count and training regime, confounding fairness.
  • Transfer/generalization: The structural assumption (shared agent state Sagent across tasks) is reasonable, but experiments remain within a narrow domain (MuJoCo locomotion variants). The “transfer” is primarily between closely related navigation tasks; it is unclear how broadly the method applies when the relevant state subset for MI is not obvious or when Srest interacts more strongly with Sagent.
  • Robustness: The method exhibits high variance across seeds; some pre-trained SNNs lack critical motions to solve a maze, yielding zero returns. The Ant case reveals brittle switching and instability—precisely the scenarios where HRL should help—without a demonstrated remedy.
- Experimental baselines and rigor:
  • The baselines do not include standard HRL methods with learned options/terminations (e.g., option-critic), or strong intrinsic exploration baselines beyond a brief appendix mention of VIME. Given the paper’s framing around exploration in sparse-reward tasks and skill learning, these comparisons are essential.
  • Diversity is largely assessed via visitation plots (qualitative). A quantitative diversity metric (e.g., MI estimates held-out, trajectory clustering dispersion, state coverage) would make claims more convincing.
  • The MI bonus depends on estimating p(z|c) via discretized CoM visitation counts from the current batch, which makes the reward non-stationary and potentially biased. There is no analysis of sensitivity to grid resolution, batch size, or the stability of learning under this shaped reward.
- Design choices and ablations:
  • While concat vs. bilinear and MI coefficient αH are explored qualitatively, more systematic ablations (vary K, T, grid resolution, choice of C beyond CoM, effect of freezing vs. fine-tuning low-level) would clarify what truly drives gains.
  • The fixed commitment time T is only partially analyzed; results suggest sensitivity depends on task size/dynamics. No learned termination policy is attempted, despite this being standard in HRL.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is generally clear and code is provided, which is commendable. However, some claims (e.g., “comparable samples to a uni-modal policy”) would benefit from exact numbers and run-time statistics.

Additional feedback (to help improve the work; not all are part of my decision)
- Include rigorous baselines: option-critic with learned terminations; macro-action learning; recent intrinsic-exploration approaches under identical settings and budgets. Ensure parameter counts and pre-training budgets are matched.
- Quantify diversity and coverage: report an explicit MI estimate on a held-out set; state-space coverage metrics; diversity of trajectories (e.g., pairwise distances, clustering quality).
- Analyze MI estimator: sensitivity to discretization and batch size; impact on stability; consider density models for p(z|c) beyond counts.
- Provide a true sample-efficiency study: total environment steps for pre-training + downstream vs. baselines; wall-clock time.
- Ablate K and show how performance scales; discuss how to select K a priori.
- Consider end-to-end fine-tuning with straight-through estimators (as mentioned) and/or learned terminations to address Ant-like instability and reduce high variance across seeds.
- Broaden task suite or demonstrate settings where Srest interacts non-trivially (e.g., moving targets), and where directional/turning skills are decisively required.
- Report details about fairness of the multi-policy baseline (total samples, parameter counts, optimizer settings).

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the discretization of C for MI estimation? Did you try density models (e.g., KDE or learned regressors) and do they change stability or diversity?
2) Can you provide quantitative sample-efficiency comparisons (environment steps and wall-clock) against multi-policy and option-critic-like baselines, including pre-training costs?
3) How do results scale with K? At what point does mode collapse or overlap arise, and does MI still help?
4) How is p̂(z|c) computed without leakage from future timesteps within the same batch? Does the non-stationarity of the reward shaping affect TRPO’s guarantees or stability?
5) For Ant, did you try learned terminations or warm-starting the next skill to reduce switching-induced instability? Any results with short T or state-dependent T?
6) Beyond CoM, what choices of C have you tried? For manipulation-like settings, how do you define C without task-specific instrumentation while still obtaining useful skills?
7) Could you report quantitative diversity metrics and state coverage (not just visitation plots) and correlate them with downstream performance and variance across seeds?

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the idea is appealing, but the contribution feels incremental and the experimental evidence against strong, established baselines is not yet sufficient to justify acceptance.
