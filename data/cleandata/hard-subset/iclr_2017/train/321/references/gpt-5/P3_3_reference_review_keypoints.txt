- Proposes a two-phase hierarchical RL: pretrain a stochastic policy conditioned on discrete latent z with a proxy reward, then train a high-level manager to select frozen skills for sparse-reward tasks.
- Adds an MI bonus I(Z;C) between z and a subset of state (CoM) estimated via discretized posterior to prevent mode collapse and make skills distinct.
- Uses bilinear (FiLM-like) integration of z with observations, shown crucial for expressivity over simple concatenation.
- Both phases use TRPO; manager commits to a skill for T steps while low-level skills remain frozen.
- Provides ablations (concatenation vs bilinear, with/without MI), a multi-policy baseline, and experiments on Swimmer and Snake in Maze and Gather tasks; Ant discussed as a failure case.
- Decision: Weak accept.
- Strength: Simple, well-motivated approach that empirically improves exploration and solves sparse tasks standard methods fail on.
- Strength: Bilinear modulation and MI regularization are novel in this context and yield interpretable, temporally consistent skills.
- Methodological note: Sampling categorical z once per rollout yields temporally coherent skills and avoids TRPO mixture complications.
- MI implementation: per-step log p̂(z|c) reward is pragmatic, low-variance, and effective when applied to low-dimensional behaviorally salient subspace (CoM).
- Empirical: Pretraining produces diverse skills (forward, backward, sideways, turning) and MI reduces skill overlap.
- Empirical: Manager improves exploration coverage vs Gaussian noise and multi-policy baseline, even with less pretraining data.
- Empirical: Outperforms proxy-reward downstream baseline and VIME on sparse tasks, especially with long horizons; results reported across seeds.
- Scalability: Snake experiments show scaling to higher-dimensional agents and study impact of switch time T.
- Limitation: MI regularizer requires handpicking c (CoM) and grid resolution, weakening minimal-supervision claim and limiting other domains.
- Limitation: Missing comparisons to contemporary hierarchical methods (e.g., Option-Critic); multi-policy baseline uses more pretraining data complicating sample-efficiency claims.
- Limitation: Instability when switching skills (Ant failure) limits applicability to complex agents; end-to-end finetuning and learned termination left for future work.
- Limitation: No theoretical analysis of MI bias from batch posterior estimation or its interaction with TRPO guarantees.
- Feedback: Clarify sample efficiency by reporting total environment steps including pretraining and amortization across downstream tasks.
- Feedback: Add sensitivity analyses for K, MI weight α, grid resolution, and T; include alternative skill-diversity metrics beyond CoM visitation.
- Feedback: Consider learning p(z|s) with a classifier (InfoGAN/DIAYN-style) instead of count grids for higher-dimensional c and discuss stability/bias tradeoffs.
- Feedback: Add baselines like Option-Critic or feudal-style methods on at least one domain to contextualize gains.
- Feedback: Demonstrate generality with a non-locomotion pretrain proxy or varied c choices; discuss learned termination/variable T and safety for Ant.
- Questions: How sensitive is performance to K and does larger K yield diminishing returns or manager instability?
- Questions: How to select c in domains without obvious low-dimensional kinematics and have classifiers been tried instead of grid counts?
- Questions: Report aggregate sample complexity including pretraining and how many downstream tasks are needed to amortize pretraining.
- Questions: Did finetuning low-level SNNs with manager training (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) help Ant stability?
- Questions: How robust are results to random initial orientations during pretraining and do they affect skill diversity?
- Questions: Can authors provide success rates and per-seed outcomes to quantify pretrain variance effects on downstream performance?
