1. Summary of contributions
The paper addresses efficient exploration and transfer in sparse-reward reinforcement learning by (i) pretraining a single policy that embodies a span of diverse, temporally consistent skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with discrete latent codes, and (ii) learning, for each downstream task, a high-level policy that selects among the pretrained skills at a fixed interval. Key elements:
- Use of SNNs to model multi-modal action distributions with weight sharing across skills; a discrete latent z is sampled once per episode in pretraining and later controlled by a manager during downstream tasks.
- A simple but effective proxy reward in pretraining (e.g., center-of-mass speed for locomotion) that does not require task-specific goals.
- An information-theoretic mutual information (MI) regularizer between the latent code and a subset of state (e.g., CoM (x,y)) to encourage diversity and interpretability of skills; implemented via a discretized posterior p(Z|C) estimated from visitation counts.
- Bilinear integration of latent and observation features to improve expressivity over simple concatenation.
- Empirical evaluation on locomotion+maze and gather tasks (Swimmer, Snake) with ablations (concatenation vs bilinear; different MI weights; switching period T), multiple seeds, and comparisons against baselines, showing improved exploration and performance on long-horizon sparse tasks. Code and videos are provided.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The approach is technically sound, simple to implement on top of standard policy gradient methods, and the MI regularization is well-motivated to enforce skill diversity.
- Empirical results convincingly show improved exploration and downstream task performance over strong non-hierarchical baselines, with ablations that isolate the contributions of SNN multimodality, bilinear integration, and MI.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
- Problem tackled: Efficiently learning reusable skills that improve exploration and reduce sample complexity across a collection of sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks sharing agent dynamics. The paper explicitly targets settings where naive action noise fails and reward shaping is undesirable.
- Motivation and placement in literature: The work builds upon HRL (options) and intrinsic motivation, but avoids hand-crafted temporal abstractions and goal instrumentation by pretraining with a single proxy reward. Compared to prior skill discovery (e.g., HiREPS, option-critic), the SNN-based multi-modal policy with MI regularization provides an explicit mechanism to learn multiple diverse skills in one training run with weight sharing. Compared to Heess et al. (2016), pretraining does not require goal specification. The connection to InfoGAN is appropriate and the role of bilinear integration is justified by prior work on multiplicative interactions.
- Support for claims:
  - Skill diversity: Visitation plots and qualitative rollouts show distinct, interpretable behaviors linked to different latent codes; bilinear integration and MI increase separability and coverage. The episodic sampling of z yields temporal consistency.
  - Exploration: One-million-step rollouts with a random high-level policy demonstrate substantially broader spatial coverage versus Gaussian action noise and multi-policy baseline, indicating that the learned skill set materially changes exploration dynamics.
  - Downstream performance: On multiple mazes and gather tasks, hierarchical policies over SNN skills achieve higher returns and faster learning than the proxy-reward baseline and, in benchmark settings, outperform VIME on gather. Results are averaged over seeds and different pretrained SNNs, with ablations of switch time T (showing robustness).
  - Limitations and failure modes are candidly discussed (e.g., instability of Ant under skill switching), which supports the soundness of the empirical methodology.

Potential weaknesses (do not outweigh the strengths):
- Baselines do not include contemporary HRL methods (e.g., option-critic) on the same tasks; comparisons are primarily to non-hierarchical exploration and a multi-policy baseline.
- Diversity metrics are largely qualitative; MI values or coverage statistics beyond plots would strengthen the case.
- The claim of K-fold sample efficiency for learning K skills with an SNN is plausible but not quantified across compute/training dynamics; pretraining cost vs downstream savings could be reported more rigorously.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Provide quantitative diversity metrics: report MI estimates, inter-skill state-occupancy divergence, or coverage area to complement visitation plots.
- Clarify sample efficiency: report wall-clock time and environment steps for pretraining vs training K separate policies; quantify downstream savings across tasks to support claims of uniform sample complexity reduction.
- Expand baselines: include option-critic or other HRL methods where feasible; at minimum, discuss expected differences (e.g., need for extrinsic reward during skill learning).
- Sensitivity analysis: add results on the number of skills K, grid resolution for p(Z|C), and MI coefficient αH to show robustness.
- Broaden tasks: include more challenging, goal-based or dynamic environments where turning/sideways skills materially improve performance; quantify gains in those settings.
- Ant handling: consider a brief experiment with a learned termination/transition policy or short warm-up adaptation when switching skills to mitigate instability, even if preliminary.
- Clarity: tighten reference formatting, ensure figures are self-contained with captions specifying hyperparameters (e.g., αH, K), and summarize key training details (e.g., batch sizes, TRPO constraints) in the main text.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the number of skills K? Does increasing K beyond the number of “distinct” locomotion modes hurt downstream learning due to redundancy?
- How did you choose the MI grid resolution and αH? Can you report performance and diversity metrics across a grid of these hyperparameters?
- Can you quantify the training cost of the SNN pretraining relative to training K separate policies (environment steps and time), and the net effect on overall sample usage across the suite of downstream tasks?
- How does the method perform when the proxy reward differs from downstream needs (e.g., penalizing speed but rewarding precise manipulation)? Any evidence on negative transfer?
- Did you try learning p(Z|C) with a discriminator/MLP rather than counts? Does that enable using higher-dimensional C (e.g., orientation) to further disambiguate skills?
- What is the effect of allowing the manager to adaptively choose the switching time (termination policy) or to fine-tune the low-level SNN? Any preliminary results with straight-through estimators?
- For Ant, did short overlap windows (blended actions) or state-dependent switching improve stability? If not tried, what do you anticipate as the main obstacle?

Overall, the paper is clear, methodologically sound, and presents a novel, practical approach to skill discovery and hierarchical control with solid empirical support on sparse-reward tasks.
