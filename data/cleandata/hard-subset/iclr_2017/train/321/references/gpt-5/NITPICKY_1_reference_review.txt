Summary
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning in sparse-reward settings: (1) pretrain a low-level, multi-skill policy using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a categorical latent z and a proxy reward (e.g., CoM speed) to learn a “span of skills”; (2) at test time, freeze the low-level SNN, and train a high-level “Manager” to select a skill every T steps to solve downstream sparse tasks (mazes and gather). To prevent mode collapse and promote interpretable behaviors, the pretraining adds a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent z and a subset of the state (the agent’s CoM (x, y)), implemented via a discretized posterior estimate p̂(Z|c). The paper claims improved exploration and performance on sparse tasks, better sample efficiency vs training K independent policies, and interpretable skills; code and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Empirical support is limited and brittle: results are largely on Swimmer and a custom Snake; Ant fails in hierarchical use. High variance and frequent failure modes undermine robustness. Several key claims (e.g., sample-efficiency gains “K times”) are asserted but not convincingly quantified.
2) Insufficiently strong baselines and ablations: comparisons to modern intrinsic motivation/exploration and hierarchical baselines are weak or relegated to appendices with mismatched settings; key design choices (bilinear vs concatenation, MI strength, K, T) are not evaluated rigorously or consistently across tasks.
3) Technical/methodological clarity and rigor issues: MI estimator details and stability (e.g., zero-count smoothing), nonstationary shaping, discretization choices, and sensitivity are underspecified; formatting/citation quality is poor.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: Using an SNN policy π(a|s, z) to induce multimodality and a mutual-information regularizer to discover diverse skills is sensible and timely; the formulation is related to mixture-of-experts/options with shared parameters and to InfoGAN-inspired skill discovery. The paper is reasonably well-situated (HiREPS, option-critic, GPS, VIME), though direct comparison to concurrent works that discover skills via MI/unsupervised objectives is missing (understandable if contemporaneous, but then the empirical evaluation should be correspondingly stronger).
- MI regularizer: The intended objective I(Z; C) with C the CoM is coherent; maximizing E log p̂(Z|C) encourages skill-state discriminability. However:
  - The posterior p̂(Z|c) is obtained via counts on a discretized grid without any stated smoothing; log p̂ can be ill-defined for unseen z in a cell (log 0). Even if an epsilon is used, its value is not reported; the impact on gradients and stability is unaddressed.
  - The MI bonus is recomputed batch-wise using data collected under the current policy, creating a moving target/ nonstationary shaped reward. There is no analysis of stability, bias, or sensitivity to grid resolution (beyond a single value in Appendix A).
  - Restricting C to (x, y) may bias skills to direction-of-travel variants and ignore rotational symmetries; the paper acknowledges dependence on initial orientation and shows behaviors tied to a fixed heading. This raises concerns about generalization to tasks requiring orientation/pose-dependent manipulation, yet no remedy is explored (e.g., including heading or velocity as C).
- Architecture and optimization:
  - Treating z as part of the observation to keep TRPO tractable is standard. However, the claim that training an SNN “takes a comparable amount of samples to a single policy,” hence yielding K-fold sample savings, is not substantiated with learning curves or quantitative pretraining convergence metrics. Interference between modes (shared parameters) and capacity limitations could hurt per-skill quality; no such analysis is provided.
  - The bilinear integration vs concatenation observation is supported only by visitation plots and anecdotal percentages; no quantitative diversity metric (e.g., MI achieved, state coverage, skill overlap) or statistical test is reported.
  - The high-level Manager commits for T=500 in Swimmer tasks; such long commitments can mask credit assignment issues and are unlikely to transfer to reactive settings. Sensitivity to T is only studied in an appendix for Snake, not on the main tasks.
- Empirical evaluation:
  - Main environments are Swimmer mazes and a gather task; Ant is reported as failing to switch robustly (Appendix D). This substantially limits the generality of the approach to unstable/high-DoF agents.
  - Baselines: The primary non-hierarchical baseline is adding the proxy reward to the task, which is weak for mazes; prior exploration methods (VIME) appear only in Appendix B with different episode lengths (500 vs 5000), complicating interpretation. No comparison to option-critic or to training K independent options with a Manager under matched sample budgets is provided.
  - Variance/brittleness: Authors note that some pretrained SNNs simply lack a motion needed to solve certain mazes, leading to zero return even after training the Manager. This indicates that skill discovery is not reliably aligned with downstream needs; no mechanism is proposed to diagnose or refine skills post hoc.
- Reproducibility and presentation:
  - Code is provided, but key hyperparameters and procedures critical to MI (grid size, smoothing, bonus scaling, normalization over discounting) and to the manager (temperature/entropy regularization for categorical policy) are not clearly specified in the main text.
  - Numerous typos and formatting/citation issues (e.g., “signle,” “SILL,” dangling footnotes “3,” malformed references with missing years, duplicated author names) detract from professionalism.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide quantitative measures of skill diversity and coverage (e.g., estimated I(Z; C), pairwise occupancy divergence, controllability metrics), not only visitation plots.
- Report pretraining sample complexity vs K independent policies under matched budgets; show that SNN pretraining achieves comparable or better per-skill quality.
- Add smoothing for p̂(Z|c) and report its effect; analyze sensitivity to grid resolution and alpha scaling; consider density models for p̂(Z|C) when C is higher-dimensional.
- Evaluate across more challenging agents with successful hierarchical switching (e.g., Hopper/Walker2d) and provide strategies to mitigate instability in Ant (e.g., warm-starting internal states, transition policies, termination policies).
- Strengthen baselines: option-critic, mixture-of-policies, parameter noise, count-based/hash-based exploration, and matched VIME settings; include hierarchical baselines with independently trained skills.
- Study sensitivity to K and T on the main tasks; consider learning a termination policy and allowing end-to-end finetuning to adapt skills to downstream tasks.
- Reduce dependence on fixed initial orientation; consider rotational augmentation or including heading in C for MI.
- Clean up formatting and references; ensure all citations are complete and consistent.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you handle zero-count cells in p̂(Z|c)? Is any smoothing used? How sensitive are results to the smoothing constant and grid resolution?
2) Can you report the actual MI achieved during pretraining and relate it to downstream performance and skill interpretability?
3) What is the empirical pretraining cost vs training K independent policies? Do SNNs ever hurt per-skill quality due to interference?
4) How does performance vary with K and T on the main Swimmer mazes? Can a learned termination policy improve robustness?
5) Can you provide matched-setting comparisons to option-critic and VIME (same horizons, batch sizes) and to a hierarchical baseline with K independently trained skills and a Manager?
6) For Ant, what concrete strategies failed/succeeded to stabilize switching? Could you pretrain with switch events to improve robustness?
