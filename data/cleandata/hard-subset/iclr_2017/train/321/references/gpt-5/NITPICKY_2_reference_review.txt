Summary
The paper proposes a two-stage framework for solving sparse-reward tasks with hierarchical reinforcement learning. In a pre-training environment, a single stochastic neural network (SNN) policy conditioned on a discrete latent z (K categories) is trained with a proxy reward (e.g., center-of-mass speed) and an additional mutual information (MI) bonus between z and a subset of the state (e.g., (x, y) CoM position), estimated via a discretized count-based posterior p̂(z|c). This is intended to yield a diverse, interpretable span of “skills.” In downstream tasks, these low-level skills are frozen and a high-level manager policy selects a skill every T steps. The authors argue this yields: (1) multimodal skill policies with clear semantics, (2) significantly improved exploration, and (3) strong performance on long-horizon sparse tasks (Swimmer in mazes and gather), with some results for Snake and failure analysis on Ant. They also study an architectural choice (bilinear integration of z and observation vs concatenation) and report that bilinear + MI yields more diverse skills. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous for the strength of the claims (limited domains, mostly qualitative analyses of “diversity,” missing strong baselines for the key sparse-reward tasks, and unsubstantiated sample-complexity claims). (2) There are numerous methodological and presentation issues (ad hoc MI estimator tied to CoM and grid choice, unclear accounting of sample budgets, and many formatting/citation problems) that reduce confidence, despite promising ideas.

Supporting arguments
- Missing or weak baselines on the core tasks. For the mazes, there is no comparison to strong intrinsic exploration methods (e.g., VIME, count-based exploration) or hierarchical baselines (e.g., option-critic, HiREPS). The only baseline is adding the proxy reward to the downstream task, which is relatively weak. For gather, VIME is compared only in a specific setting. The claimed advantage of the hierarchical approach over non-hierarchical exploration is thus not convincingly established.

- Diversity claims are largely qualitative. The central contribution—learning a “span of skills”—is evaluated via visitation plots and anecdotes (e.g., forward/backward/sideways). There is no quantitative diversity metric (e.g., estimated MI, conditional entropy H(Z|C), coverage/uniformity over directions, cluster separability) or statistical analysis across seeds beyond cursory statements. The MI term’s effect is not characterized beyond a few plots.

- Proxy reward and MI bonus design are narrow and ad hoc. The MI bonus is computed via a discretized p̂(z|c) on CoM (x, y), with a fixed grid (10 divisions/unit) and batch-wise counts, adding a non-stationary reward shaping term. There is no sensitivity study to grid resolution, environment scale, or to the MI weight αH beyond a couple of values; no comparison to other conditioning mechanisms (e.g., classifier-based MI estimates, InfoNCE) or to different state subsets. The approach appears strongly tailored to locomotion/directional partitioning; no evidence is provided for manipulation or other domains.

- Sample efficiency claims are not supported. The paper asserts that training an SNN “takes a comparable amount of samples” to a single uni-modal policy and thus yields an effective K× reduction versus training K policies. There is no empirical evidence, no wall-clock/gradient-step comparison, no scaling study in K, and no reporting of the full sample budgets for pre-training and downstream phases versus the multi-policy baseline. The multi-policy baseline is also given substantially more pretraining samples, making conclusions murky.

- Limited scope and fragile generality. Experiments primarily use Swimmer; Snake results are relegated to appendix; Ant fails due to instability when switching skills. The promised generality (“minimal domain knowledge”) is not demonstrated beyond locomotion; the design hinges on CoM-based proxy/MI, which may not transfer to manipulation or rich sensory inputs. The structural assumption (shared Sagent, weakly interacting Srest) is reasonable but the paper does not test scenarios with stronger coupling.

- Methodological clarity gaps. TRPO is applied by treating z as part of the observation in pretraining; while practical, this choice precludes learning stochastic z decisions and sidesteps the mix-of-Gaussians issue. Manager training details are brief; choice of T is largely heuristic (appendix analysis suggests mild sensitivity but only on specific tasks). No ablation on bilinear vs simpler conditioning (e.g., FiLM/gated biases) with controlled parameter counts.

- Presentation and references. The manuscript contains many typos and formatting issues (“signle,” “coeficient,” “preform,” “sparcity,” “Mulit-policy,” “SILL”), broken/incomplete citations with placeholders (e.g., “? \Q2016\E”), repeated/garbled entries, stray footnotes in text (“2Videos”), and inconsistent figure references. These undermine readability and professionalism.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide quantitative diversity metrics: estimated MI (with confidence intervals), H(Z|C), coverage over directions, trajectory entropy, or mutual predictability across time. Report statistics across seeds and K values.

- Compare against stronger baselines on mazes: VIME, recent count-based methods, option-critic or HiREPS variants, mixture-of-experts/multi-head policies conditioned on one-hot codes, and non-hierarchical policies with better exploration noise.

- Clarify and standardize sample accounting: pretraining environment steps, downstream steps to reach target returns, and total samples including pretraining. Test scaling in K (quality vs K, training stability, compute).

- Analyze sensitivity to αH, grid resolution, and choice of c (state subset). Consider classifier-based MI estimators (as in InfoGAN) or contrastive MI estimates that avoid coarse gridding.

- Explore more general proxy rewards and state subsets, and at least one non-locomotion domain to support claims of generality.

- Ablate the effect of bilinear integration vs concatenation vs FiLM/gating while matching parameter counts; report parameter sizes.

- Strengthen Ant experiments by incorporating stabilization during skill switching (e.g., warm-up blending, skill transition policy, or end-to-end fine-tuning with Gumbel-Softmax) and report quantitative success rates.

- Substantially clean up writing and references.

Questions for the authors
1) How many environment steps are used in pretraining and downstream for each method? Do you count pretraining toward total sample complexity, and how does performance vs total samples compare to baselines?

2) How sensitive are results to αH and to the discretization of (x, y) (grid size, environment scale)? Please provide curves and statistics across seeds.

3) What is the parameter count and training cost overhead of bilinear integration vs concatenation? Would a FiLM-style conditioning perform similarly?

4) Can you report quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., estimated MI, entropy of p(z|c), directional coverage) and how they correlate with downstream performance?

5) For mazes, can you compare against VIME and count-based exploration under the same horizons? For hierarchical baselines, can you include option-critic or HiREPS?

6) How robust are learned skills to changes in initial orientation and to domain shifts (e.g., slightly different dynamics)?

7) Do you have results on at least one manipulation domain to support generality beyond locomotion?
