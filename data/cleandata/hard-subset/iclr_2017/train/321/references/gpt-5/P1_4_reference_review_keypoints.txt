- Proposes a two-phase framework: pretrain a multi-skill Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) with discrete latent z, then freeze skills and train a high-level Manager to select skills every T steps.  
- Induces diverse, interpretable skills via bilinear integration of z with observations and an MI bonus between z and a low-dimensional state subset (CoM), estimated by discretization and counts.  
- Pretraining uses a domain-agnostic proxy reward (e.g., speed magnitude) and requires no task-specific reward; TRPO is used in both phases.  
- Empirical results on MuJoCo swimmers and snakes show broad skill diversity, improved exploration in sparse mazes/gather tasks, and success where naïve exploration fails.  
- Ablations demonstrate the importance of bilinear integration and the MI bonus; hierarchical Manager with SNN skills outperforms a multi-policy baseline with fewer pretraining samples.  
- Paper provides code, videos, hyperparameters, and multiple seeds; discusses failure modes (Ant) and sensitivity to switch time T.  
- Decision: Accept — method is simple, practical, and well-motivated with strong empirical support for skill discovery and reuse in sparse-reward settings.  
- Addresses a core exploration/sample-efficiency challenge and leverages HRL to reduce effective horizon by reusing a skill basis across tasks.  
- Methodological novelty: SNN with categorical latent amortizes pretraining; bilinear integration improves skill separability; CoM-based MI regularizer is simple and effective.  
- Pretraining validation: visitation plots and ablations show distinct skills from different z values and benefits from bilinear + MI.  
- Exploration validation: random skill switching expands explored region far beyond Gaussian noise or multi-policy baselines.  
- Downstream validation: hierarchical policies learn faster and achieve higher returns across tasks and seeds; scales to harder agents (Snake); Ant exposes limitations.  
- Reproducibility: code and videos available; implementation details reasonably documented.  
- Baseline recommendations: include more HRL baselines (option-critic, macro-actions) and matched-pretraining-budget comparisons to strengthen claims.  
- Generality of MI bonus: current CoM choice favors locomotion; need discussion/demonstration for choosing C in other domains (e.g., manipulation) and robustness to misspecification.  
- Quantify skill diversity: add quantitative metrics (MI estimate, state coverage, direction entropy, policy divergence) beyond visitation plots.  
- Sensitivity analyses: report sensitivity to number of skills K, grid resolution for p̂(Z|C), and computational overhead of MI estimation.  
- Statistical rigor: include confidence intervals, statistical tests, and clarify any early stopping or model selection to address high variance.  
- Manager design: analyze performance gap for varying T, consider termination heuristics or reactive alternatives to fixed commitment.  
- Implementation detail request: provide exact bilinear integration parameterization, dimensionalities, and normalization of z and s.  
- Question — C selection: How to choose relevant state subset C in non-locomotion domains, and have you tried a learned discriminator qφ(z|c) instead of count-based MI?  
- Question — K sensitivity: How sensitive are results to number of skills K, and do too many/few skills harm performance?  
- Question — pretraining budget: If pretraining sample budgets are matched between SNN and multi-policy baselines, does SNN still outperform?  
- Question — transfer breadth: Have you tried downstream tasks requiring behaviors beyond locomotion direction (e.g., precise turning/stopping), and does MI encourage such skills?  
- Question — Ant instability: Did you try curriculum switching, warm-starting skills near seen states, or regularizing skill overlap to reduce falls?  
- Question — Manager stability: Do you see non-stationarity when manager explores rarely used skills, and were entropy bonuses or temperature tuning crucial?  
- Question — MI hyperparameters: How was α_H chosen, how robust are results to it, and what is the trade-off between grid granularity and stability of p̂(Z|C)?
