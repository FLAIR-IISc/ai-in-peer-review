Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a simple and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) in sparse-reward settings. The key idea is to pretrain a low-level, multi-modal policy (a Stochastic Neural Network, SNN) using only a domain-agnostic proxy reward (e.g., speed for locomotion), while injecting a discrete latent variable to induce skill diversity. To avoid mode collapse and encourage distinct behaviors, the authors add a mutual information (MI) bonus between the latent code and a subset of state variables (e.g., the center-of-mass position), estimated via a grid-based posterior. In downstream tasks with sparse rewards, a high-level “Manager” policy chooses among the K pretrained skills and commits for T steps, greatly improving exploration. The paper presents careful ablations (concatenation vs bilinear integration of z; effect of MI; multi-policy vs SNN) and shows that this approach yields interpretable, reusable skills and solves challenging sparse locomotion tasks (mazes and gather) where standard exploration fails. Code and videos are provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated method that combines SNNs with an MI diversity bonus to learn a diverse skill set from very weak supervision, and a simple hierarchical controller to exploit these skills.
- Empirical evidence is convincing for the intended use case: substantial exploration gains and improved learning on sparse, long-horizon tasks, with informative ablations and released code.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Exploration in sparse-reward continuous control is notoriously hard; learning reusable, temporally-extended behaviors that reduce effective horizon is valuable.
- Positioning and motivation: The approach sits at the intersection of hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation. It is well placed relative to options, HiREPS, GPS, and recent work on learning locomotion skills. The novelty lies in: (i) using SNNs to realize multi-modal policies with weight sharing; (ii) a lightweight MI regularizer to induce interpretable, distinguishable skills; (iii) showing that a simple manager that commits to skills suffices to crack sparse tasks.
- Method soundness: Treating z as input keeps optimization with TRPO straightforward. The bilinear integration of z increases expressivity versus concatenation, empirically crucial for skill separation. The MI bonus is intuitive and easy to compute; the discretized posterior is a pragmatic choice.
- Empirical support: 
  - Pretraining: Visitation plots clearly show diverse, interpretable behaviors as z varies; MI and bilinear integration matter.
  - Exploration: One-million-step rollouts demonstrate dramatically increased coverage under the hierarchical controller versus Gaussian noise.
  - Downstream performance: On multiple mazes and the long-horizon Gather variant, the method outperforms strong baselines (including VIME in the benchmark setting) and a proxy-reward baseline; ablations indicate that SNN+bilinear and MI often help. The authors also report variance across seeds and avoid cherry-picking.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is clearly written, includes architectural and training details, ablations, code, and videos.

Limitations and concerns
- Scope of evaluation: Most strong results are with Swimmer/Snake; Ant highlights a failure mode (instability when switching). It would strengthen the paper to show success on at least one more unstable agent or to mitigate the failure with a learned termination or transition policy.
- MI estimation and reward shaping: The MI uses hand-chosen state subsets (CoM) and coarse discretization. While effective, it is somewhat heuristic and task-specific; generality to other domains (e.g., manipulation with images) is not demonstrated.
- Fixed skill duration and frozen skills: The manager uses a fixed commitment time T and low-level policies are frozen. Although discussed, this may limit adaptability and sample efficiency in dynamic environments.
- Baselines: While intrinsic motivation and a “multi-policy” baseline are included, comparisons to learned options (e.g., option-critic) or skill-discovery methods with diversity regularization would strengthen claims about hierarchy quality and reuse.
- Quantification of diversity: The paper relies mainly on visitation plots; more quantitative metrics (e.g., MI estimates, state-conditional skill classification accuracy, coverage statistics) would substantiate claims about distinctness and breadth of skills.

Additional feedback and questions to help improve the paper (constructive; not all are decision-critical)
- Quantify diversity and identifiability:
  - Report the estimated I(Z;C) during pretraining and a skill classification accuracy from state (or low-dimensional features), to support the “interpretable, distinct skills” claim.
  - Provide coverage metrics (e.g., area reached, directional entropy) to complement visitation plots.
- Sensitivity analyses:
  - Number of skills K: How does performance and diversity scale with K? Is there diminishing return or mode collapse?
  - Switch time T: You include some analysis in the appendix; summarizing main trends in the main text would help. Could a learned termination improve Ant-like cases?
  - MI coefficient αH and grid resolution: Provide sensitivity curves and practical tuning guidance; discuss bias/variance trade-offs of the discretized posterior.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Include a comparison to an option-critic baseline (with and without intrinsic rewards) or HiREPS on at least one task, to position your approach among learned option frameworks.
  - For the proxy-reward baseline in downstream tasks, consider combining it with hierarchical control (manager over trivial sub-policies) to isolate the effect of skill learning vs management.
- Ant instability:
  - Try a simple stabilizing tweak (e.g., short warm-up when switching skills, or blending actions across a few steps) to mitigate falls; or a transition policy conditioned on current state and next z.
  - Alternatively, allow the manager to select “no-op” or a “stabilize” skill.
- Generality:
  - Discuss how to choose the proxy reward in other domains (e.g., manipulation) and what minimal domain knowledge is needed.
  - Clarify how the MI target would be defined when the “relevant state subset” is not obvious; consider learning a discriminator q(z|s) with a small network instead of grid counts.
- Implementation clarity:
  - Elaborate on the bilinear integration: dimensions, parameterization, and computational overhead relative to concatenation.
  - Clarify how often skill collapse happens without MI, and whether early stopping or entropy regularization on z helps.
  - Report wall-clock training costs for pretraining vs training K separate policies, to quantify the sample/compute benefits.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the choice of K? Do too many skills hurt manager training or induce redundant modes? Any automatic way to prune/merge?
2. How do you choose αH and the grid resolution in practice? Have you tried learning p̂(z|c) with a small classifier instead of counts, especially for higher-dimensional “relevant” features?
3. Could you report quantitative diversity metrics (e.g., MI estimates, directional coverage, skill-usage entropy under the trained manager) to complement the visitation plots?
4. How does the method perform with learned terminations (variable T) or soft switching (e.g., action blending for a short horizon) on unstable agents like Ant?
5. Can the approach handle high-dimensional observations (e.g., images)? Would you embed observations first and then apply the bilinear integration with z?
6. In downstream tasks, does the manager rely mostly on Srest (e.g., LIDAR/goal sensors), and how critical is including Sagent? Any ablation?
7. Could you provide results with option-critic or another learned options baseline to better position your contributions?

Overall, the paper presents a clear, well-motivated, and empirically effective approach to skill discovery and hierarchical control for sparse-reward RL. Despite some limitations in scope and baselines, the contributions are meaningful and likely to be useful to the community.
