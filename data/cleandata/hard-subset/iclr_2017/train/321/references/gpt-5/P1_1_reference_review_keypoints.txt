- Proposes a two-stage HRL: pre-train a stochastic neural network (SNN) with categorical latent codes and MI bonus to induce skills, then freeze skills and train a high-level Manager to select latents every T steps.  
- Key technical choices: bilinear latent–observation interaction, sampling one latent per episode for temporal consistency, MI reward I(Z;CoM) estimated via discretized posterior.  
- TRPO used for both phases by treating z as part of the observation for practical training.  
- Empirical: bilinear SNN + MI produces diverse, interpretable locomotion skills (Swimmer, Snake; partial Ant analysis) shown via visitation plots and rollouts.  
- Hierarchy with frozen skills dramatically improves exploration vs Gaussian action noise and a strong in-task proxy-reward baseline on long-horizon sparse tasks (mazes, gather).  
- SNN hierarchy often matches or outperforms a multi-policy baseline while using fewer pre-training samples.  
- Decision: Weak accept — addresses an important problem with a simple, modular, and well-ablated approach and provides code.  
- Strength: Clear motivation aligning with HRL goals and minimal domain knowledge (single proxy reward + MI over agent-centric state).  
- Related work: Properly situated among HRL, skill discovery, intrinsic motivation, and pre-training literature; links to InfoGAN for MI are appropriate.  
- Methodological clarity: Architecture, bilinear vs concatenation ablation, MI construction, and Manager training are clearly described; freezing and commitment time T are explicit.  
- Empirical rigor: Skill diversity ablations (αH, integration method), exploration plots, learning curves across tasks, multiple seeds, and discussion of variance/failures provided.  
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters, code, and videos are provided.  
- Limitation: Downstream environments are still stylized and lack direct comparisons to other hierarchical/option-discovery baselines (e.g., option-critic) in the same sparse settings.  
- Limitation: MI bonus uses discretized CoM grid, introducing design choices (grid density, state subset) with only partial exploration of their impact.  
- Limitation: Sensitivity to K (number of skills) and switch time T not systematically analyzed in main experiments; some appendix results only.  
- Limitation: Freezing skills and fixed commitment T may hurt unstable agents (Ant); no experiments on learned terminations or end-to-end fine-tuning shown.  
- Limitation: Claims about K× sample-efficiency in pre-training lack controlled quantitative measurement.  
- Suggested improvements: Add comparisons to learned options/macro-action baselines and equalize pre-training budgets between SNN and multi-policy.  
- Suggested improvements: Report quantitative diversity metrics (MI estimate, state coverage, angular spread) alongside visitation plots.  
- Suggested improvements: Provide sensitivity studies for K, αH, grid resolution, and MI state choices; try learned q(z|s) vs discretization.  
- Suggested improvements: Experiment with learned termination/hazard for Manager or vary T more extensively; present pre-training vs downstream sample-efficiency curves.  
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to K and αH; regime where too many skills hurt Manager training?  
- Questions for authors: How was CoM grid chosen and how sensitive are results to grid density or including orientation/full state?  
- Questions for authors: How do multi-policy results change when pre-training sample budgets are matched/mismatched with SNN?  
- Questions for authors: Can Manager pick the same skill consecutively and what is the empirical distribution of switch intervals?  
- Questions for authors: Did you try learned q(z|s) for the MI posterior; how did it compare?  
- Questions for authors: For Ant, did you try end-to-end fine-tuning or region-specific warm-starts to reduce instability?
