### Review Summary
The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework aimed at solving complex, sparse-reward tasks. The approach is divided into two phases: a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned, and a downstream training phase where a high-level policy learns to compose these skills. The core of the skill-learning phase is the use of a "Stochastic Neural Network" (SNN), which is a policy conditioned on a discrete latent variable. To encourage the emergence of distinct skills corresponding to each latent code, the authors introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent code and the agent's spatial location. The authors demonstrate that this approach can learn a wide range of interpretable locomotion skills for simulated robots and that using these skills in a hierarchical setup significantly improves exploration and performance on challenging maze and collection tasks compared to a non-hierarchical baseline.

### Decision and Key Reasons
**Decision: Reject**

My recommendation is to reject this paper in its current form. While the problem of skill discovery for HRL is important and the proposed information-theoretic approach is interesting, the paper suffers from two main weaknesses:

1.  **Limited Generality of the Proposed Method:** The core technical contribution—the mutual information (MI) regularizer—relies on a pre-specified, low-dimensional subset of the state space (the agent's 2D coordinates). This dependence on hand-picked state variables for measuring diversity raises significant questions about the method's applicability to domains where the relevant axes of behavioral variation are not as obvious or easily accessible.
2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation against Established Baselines:** The experiments convincingly show that the proposed hierarchical approach outperforms a naive baseline where the pre-training reward is used for reward shaping. However, the paper lacks a comparison to other well-established methods for automated skill discovery and hierarchical learning, such as the Option-Critic architecture. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess the true performance and contribution of the proposed framework relative to the state of the art.

### Supporting Arguments

**On the Limited Generality of the MI Regularizer:** The proposed MI bonus, `I(Z;C)`, is designed to maximize the predictability of the latent skill `Z` given the agent's location `C`. In the experiments, `C` is chosen to be the (x, y) coordinates of the agent's center of mass. This is a sensible choice for the tested 2D navigation tasks, as it directly encourages skills that result in spatially distinct trajectories. However, this formulation is not generally applicable. For many robotics tasks (e.g., in-hand manipulation, tool use, object assembly), diversity in behavior cannot be captured by 2D spatial coordinates alone. The paper does not provide a general principle for selecting these crucial state variables `C`, making the approach appear tailored to a specific class of problems. Furthermore, the posterior `p(Z|C)` is estimated via discretization of the coordinate space, a method that does not scale to higher-dimensional state representations. While the authors suggest using a function approximator as an alternative, this is not implemented or evaluated, leaving the scalability of the core mechanism unproven.

**On the Insufficiency of Experimental Baselines and Validation:** The primary baseline in the main paper involves augmenting the downstream task's reward with the proxy reward from the pre-training phase. While this is a reasonable sanity check, it is not a competitive HRL algorithm. To demonstrate a significant advance, the method should be compared against established approaches that also learn options or skills without explicit supervision of the skills themselves. For example, the Option-Critic architecture (Bacon & Precup, 2016) is a highly relevant and well-regarded baseline that is mentioned in the related work but conspicuously absent from the experimental comparisons.

Furthermore, the appendix reveals significant limitations that question the robustness of the method. Appendix D.2 reports "failure modes for unstable robots," noting that the abrupt switching between frozen skills causes the "Ant" agent to become unstable and fall. This is a critical issue for any practical HRL method, as it suggests the learned skills are not composable in a robust manner. While mentioned as a limitation, its implications for the method's viability on complex agents are severe and should be addressed more directly. Similarly, Appendix C.4 shows that the performance on downstream tasks is highly dependent on the quality of the pre-trained SNN, with some random seeds failing to learn the necessary skills to solve the task at all. This high variance undermines the claim that the method *consistently* yields a useful span of skills.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The paper is well-written, and the core idea of using an information-theoretic objective to encourage skill diversity is appealing. The qualitative visualizations of the learned skills and exploration patterns are effective and insightful. However, to be considered for publication, the work needs to be strengthened in the following areas:

*   **Addressing Generality:** The authors should discuss in detail how the state variables `C` for the MI bonus could be selected in other domains. Better still would be to propose and evaluate a method for learning or automatically identifying these variables.
*   **Strengthening Evaluation:** The experimental section must include comparisons to relevant and established HRL baselines. A direct comparison with the Option-Critic architecture on the same environments would be a necessary addition to contextualize the paper's contribution.
*   **Addressing Robustness:** The issue of instability when switching skills needs to be tackled more directly. Simply freezing skills is a strong simplification. The authors could explore fine-tuning the low-level policies on the downstream task or learning a specific transition policy between skills to ensure smoother and more stable compositions.

I would like the authors to answer the following questions to clarify my understanding:

1.  Beyond 2D navigation, how do you propose to select the variables `C` for the MI regularizer in a more general setting, such as a robotic manipulation task where skill diversity might relate to object interaction rather than agent position?
2.  Have you considered the potential negative interference of the MI bonus? For instance, could it discourage multiple distinct skills that happen to visit the same regions of the state space (e.g., two different ways of picking up an object at the same location)?
3.  The performance of the hierarchical policy seems sensitive to the quality of the pre-trained skills (Appendix C.4). Does this suggest that the pre-training phase is brittle, and what steps could be taken to make the skill discovery process more reliable across different random seeds?
