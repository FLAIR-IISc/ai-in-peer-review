### **Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"**

#### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a general and sample-efficient framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to solve a collection of challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of reusable, low-level skills in a simple environment with only a generic "proxy reward" (e.g., rewarding forward velocity), requiring minimal domain knowledge. To learn multiple skills simultaneously and share weights, the authors propose using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as the policy, where a discrete latent code conditions the network's output to produce different behaviors. To ensure the learned skills are distinct, they introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent code and the agent's state. The paper also provides an insightful architectural comparison, showing that a bilinear integration of the latent code and observation is superior to simple concatenation for generating varied skills. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as a set of macro-actions for a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on specific downstream tasks. The experimental results on challenging continuous control benchmarks show that this hierarchical approach drastically improves exploration and enables the agent to solve sparse-reward tasks where standard RL methods fail.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Effective and Novel Method for Skill Discovery:** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated method for unsupervised skill discovery. The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks to represent a multi-modal policy, a simple proxy reward for pre-training, and an information-theoretic regularizer to ensure skill diversity is a novel and significant contribution. This approach provides a practical solution to the long-standing challenge of automatically learning useful options in HRL.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The claims are strongly supported by a well-designed set of experiments. The paper provides clear qualitative evidence of skill diversity and enhanced exploration through "visitation plots," which effectively visualize the distinct behaviors learned and their collective coverage of the state space. Quantitatively, the method demonstrates superior performance on difficult sparse-reward benchmark tasks compared to relevant baselines, including a strong one that uses independently trained policies. The ablation studies effectively justify the main design choices, namely the bilinear integration method and the MI bonus.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant and practical approach to skill learning. By leveraging SNNs, it achieves significant sample efficiency in the pre-training phase compared to training separate policies for each skill (as demonstrated by the "Multi-policy" baseline). The introduction of the InfoGAN-inspired MI bonus is a key contribution that moves beyond simply hoping for multimodality and actively encourages the emergence of distinct, interpretable skills. This is clearly shown in the ablation study in Figure 4, where increasing the MI bonus leads to a cleaner separation of behaviors.

The experimental methodology is rigorous. The use of benchmark environments from Duan et al. (2016) grounds the work in established challenges. The choice of baselines is appropriate: the "proxy reward" baseline shows that pre-training is essential, and the "Multi-policy" baseline shows that the SNN approach is not only more sample-efficient but also competitive or superior in performance. The paper's transparency is commendable, with code and videos provided, and the appendices offer valuable further analysis, including scaling to a more complex "Snake" agent and an honest discussion of failure modes with the unstable "Ant" agent. This level of thoroughness increases confidence in the results.

Finally, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is explained logically, and the results are presented in an intuitive manner. The connection between the component parts—the SNN architecture, the MI regularizer, and the final hierarchical policy—is coherent and well-justified.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions for strengthening the work further and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Scalability of MI Estimation:** The current method for estimating the posterior `p(z|c)` for the MI bonus involves discretizing the state space into a grid. While effective for 2D coordinates, this approach may not scale to higher-dimensional state representations relevant for the MI calculation. The paper briefly mentions that an MLP regressor could be used instead. Expanding on this point, perhaps with a small-scale experiment or a more detailed discussion of the potential challenges and implementation details of such a function approximator, would make the method's applicability feel more general.
*   **Fixed Time Abstraction `T`:** The high-level manager commits to a chosen skill for a fixed number of timesteps `T`. The appendix provides a good analysis showing this is not a critical hyperparameter for the static tasks studied. However, for more dynamic environments where rapid reactions are needed, a fixed `T` could be a significant limitation. It would be beneficial to explicitly connect this to the options framework in the discussion/future work section and mention the possibility of learning a termination policy, as is done in works like the Option-Critic architecture.
*   **Relation to Empowerment:** The objective of making the latent skill `Z` predictable from the resulting state `C` (by maximizing `I(Z;C)`) has interesting conceptual parallels with the principle of empowerment, which seeks to maximize the mutual information between an agent's actions and future states. A brief mention of this connection could help contextualize the information-theoretic objective within the broader literature on intrinsic motivation.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points during the author response period:

1.  The MI bonus is calculated over a specific, hand-picked subset of the state (the CoM coordinates). How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to this choice? Have you experimented with using other state variables (e.g., orientation, joint velocities) or perhaps an automatically learned low-dimensional embedding of the state?
2.  The high variance in performance on the maze tasks (Sec 7.3) is attributed to some pre-trained SNNs failing to learn a "critical" skill (e.g., moving backward). This suggests the unsupervised pre-training does not guarantee a complete or sufficient basis of skills. Do you have thoughts on how to encourage the learning of a more "complete" set of skills, for instance, by modifying the proxy reward or the regularization term to explicitly promote coverage of the agent's capabilities?
3.  The SNN is shown to be much more sample-efficient than the Multi-policy baseline, which uses 6x the samples. To make the comparison even fairer, how does an SNN trained with the same total number of samples (i.e., 6x longer) perform? Does the diversity and quality of skills continue to improve with more training, or does it converge relatively quickly?
