### Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes a novel and effective framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of reusable skills in an unsupervised fashion. This is achieved by training a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as a multi-modal policy, conditioned on a discrete latent variable. Critically, the agent is guided only by a simple, generic proxy reward (e.g., forward speed), and skill diversity is explicitly encouraged through a novel information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes the mutual information (MI) between the latent skill code and the agent's state. The second stage involves freezing these learned low-level skills and training a high-level "manager" policy for each new downstream task. This manager learns to select the appropriate skill at a lower temporal frequency, effectively simplifying the exploration problem in sparse-reward environments. The authors demonstrate that this approach enables agents to solve challenging continuous control tasks, such as navigating complex mazes, where standard RL methods with naive exploration fail completely.

### Decision: Accept

This paper presents a bold, creative, and highly novel approach to unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical control. The central idea of using a mutual information objective as an intrinsic reward to train a multi-modal SNN policy is a significant conceptual leap that elegantly connects unsupervised representation learning with reinforcement learning. The empirical results are compelling, providing strong evidence that the method not only learns an interpretable and diverse span of skills but also that these skills dramatically improve exploration and enable the solution of otherwise intractable sparse-reward problems. While there are simplifications in the methodology, the novelty and potential impact of the core ideas are substantial, representing a valuable new direction for the field.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel formulation for skill discovery. The combination of three key ideas is particularly powerful: (i) using a latent-variable conditioned SNN to represent a family of policies with shared weights, (ii) a fully unsupervised pre-training phase driven by a simple, task-agnostic proxy reward, and (iii) the use of a mutual information bonus as an intrinsic reward to explicitly drive the latent codes to produce distinct, predictable behaviors. This last point, inspired by InfoGAN but cleverly adapted to the RL setting, is a standout contribution. It provides a principled way to learn disentangled and interpretable skills without needing to manually define them.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Analysis:** The paper provides excellent support for its claims through a series of well-designed experiments. The "visitation plots" in Figure 4 offer clear, intuitive, and convincing qualitative evidence that the SNN with bilinear integration and the MI bonus learns a much richer and more diverse set of motor primitives than alternative approaches. The exploration plots in Figure 5 starkly illustrate the practical benefit of this skill set, showing a massive improvement in state-space coverage compared to naive exploration. Finally, the performance on the downstream maze and gather tasks (Figure 6) demonstrates that this enhanced exploration translates directly into the ability to solve complex, long-horizon problems with sparse rewards, significantly outperforming a strong baseline. The inclusion of ablations (concatenation vs. bilinear, effect of MI bonus) and analysis on more complex agents in the appendix further strengthens the paper.

3.  **Broad Applicability and Potential Impact:** The proposed framework is general and requires minimal domain knowledge—only a simple proxy reward that encourages "interesting" behavior. This makes it broadly applicable to various domains, from locomotion to manipulation. By effectively decoupling the difficult problem of low-level motor control from high-level strategy, this work provides a scalable recipe for tackling a wide range of challenging RL problems. It opens up a promising new avenue for research in unsupervised skill learning and hierarchical RL.

### Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further. These are intended as suggestions and do not detract from my positive assessment.

*   **Scalability of MI Estimation:** The current method for estimating the posterior for the MI bonus involves discretizing the (x,y) coordinates into a grid. This is simple and effective here, but it may not scale well to higher-dimensional state spaces or tasks where the relevant state variables are not as obvious. A brief discussion of this limitation and the proposed alternative (fitting an MLP regressor) in the main text would be beneficial.
*   **Fixed Temporal Abstraction:** The manager policy commits to a chosen skill for a fixed duration `T`. This is a reasonable simplification, but more advanced HRL frameworks learn termination conditions. While the appendix shows robustness to the choice of `T`, acknowledging this simplification in the Future Work section and citing relevant work like the option-critic architecture could provide more context.
*   **Failure Modes:** The appendix honestly reports failure modes on the unstable "Ant" agent due to state distribution shift when switching skills. This is an important finding. Briefly alluding to this limitation in the main discussion would provide a more complete picture of the method's current capabilities and challenges.

### Questions for Authors
I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points:

1.  The choice of the state subset for the MI bonus (here, the CoM coordinates) seems crucial. How sensitive is the method to this choice? Have you experimented with using other state variables or even the full agent-centric state? One might worry that using the full state would introduce too much noise for the MI signal to be effective.
2.  The paper focuses on pre-training a single SNN and then using it for multiple downstream tasks. Did you consider or experiment with any form of joint optimization or fine-tuning of the SNN's weights during the downstream task training? While this could lead to over-specialization, it might also allow the skills to adapt to task-specific dynamics.
3.  The manager is trained from scratch for each task. Since the tasks in `M` share a common structure (`S_agent`), could the manager itself benefit from transfer learning or meta-learning across tasks?
