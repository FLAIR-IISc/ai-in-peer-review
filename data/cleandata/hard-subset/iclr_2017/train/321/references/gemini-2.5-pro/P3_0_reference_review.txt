This paper presents a compelling and well-executed framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to tackle challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned in an unsupervised manner using a generic proxy reward; second, a transfer phase where a high-level "manager" policy is trained on top of these frozen skills to solve specific downstream tasks. The main contributions are the use of Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with latent codes to represent a multimodal policy embodying a repertoire of skills, and a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information (MI) to explicitly encourage diversity and interpretability among these skills.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1.  **Principled and Effective Method for Skill Discovery:** The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant approach to unsupervised skill discovery. The combination of SNNs for multi-modality and an MI bonus to enforce skill separation is well-motivated and principled. It directly addresses a key challenge in HRL: how to automatically acquire a useful and diverse set of primitives without task-specific supervision.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and provide strong support for the paper's claims. The qualitative "visitation plots" are highly effective at visualizing the diversity of learned skills and clearly demonstrate the benefit of the bilinear integration and the MI bonus. The quantitative results on challenging benchmark tasks show significant performance gains over a relevant baseline, and the comparison against the "Multi-policy" architecture validates the sample-efficiency benefits of the SNN's weight sharing.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The methodology is a clever synthesis of several powerful ideas. Using a latent variable `z` to condition a policy is a natural way to represent a family of behaviors. However, the authors rightly identify that standard optimization might lead to mode collapse, where different values of `z` produce similar behaviors. The introduction of the mutual information regularizer, `I(Z;C)`, is the paper's most significant contribution. Inspired by work like InfoGAN, this objective provides a direct and principled learning signal to ensure that the skill `Z` has a statistically significant and predictable effect on the agent's state `C`. This not only encourages diversity but also leads to highly interpretable skills, as demonstrated in Figure 4. The practical implementation, which treats the latent code `z` as an augmented observation, allows the use of off-the-shelf policy gradient algorithms like TRPO, making the method widely applicable.

The experimental section is rigorous. The ablation studies in Section 7.1 are crucial, clearly showing that both the bilinear integration (for better interaction between state and skill code) and the MI bonus are important for consistently learning a rich skill set. The exploration analysis in Figure 5 provides a dramatic visualization of why HRL is effective in sparse-reward settings, showing that a random high-level policy explores vastly more of the state space than unstructured low-level action noise. The final results on the maze and gather tasks are strong. I particularly appreciate the authors' transparency in evaluating their method not on a single cherry-picked pre-trained model, but by averaging over multiple pre-training runs (5 SNNs x 2 seeds). This addresses the stochasticity of the skill discovery process and provides a more honest assessment of the method's reliability. The appendix further strengthens the paper with analyses on more complex agents (Snake, Ant) and the effect of the switching time `T`.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve its impact:

*   **Scalability of the MI Bonus Calculation:** The MI bonus is estimated by discretizing the 2D (x,y) coordinate space into a grid. This is practical for locomotion but may not scale to tasks where the relevant state information `C` is higher-dimensional (e.g., manipulator arm joint angles, or object configurations). The paper briefly mentions that an MLP regressor could be used to estimate the posterior `p(z|c)`. This is a critical point for the method's generality. A more detailed discussion of this alternative, perhaps with some analysis of its potential stability or sample complexity, would be a valuable addition.
*   **Proxy Reward Design:** The paper suggests the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," which is true for locomotion where rewarding forward speed is a simple and effective heuristic. However, the example for a manipulator arm ("successful grasping of any object") seems to imply a more complex, potentially engineered reward function. A deeper discussion on the principles for designing good proxy rewards and the sensitivity of the learned skills to this design choice would strengthen the paper's claims of generality.
*   **Fixed Time Horizon `T`:** The use of a fixed time horizon `T` for executing skills is a common simplification but can be suboptimal. An agent might need to switch skills more quickly in reactive situations. The paper acknowledges this limitation and suggests learning a termination policy as future work. This is the right direction. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss this in the main body, perhaps contextualizing it with respect to the option-critic framework, to give readers a clearer picture of the method's current limitations and future potential.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The posterior `p(z|c)` used for the MI bonus is estimated empirically from the rollouts in the current batch. Does this introduce any non-stationarity issues for the policy optimization, given that the reward function is changing from one TRPO iteration to the next as the visitation counts `m_c(z)` are updated? Did you observe any training instability related to this?
2.  In Appendix C.4, it is shown that performance on the maze task is highly dependent on the quality of the pre-trained SNN, as some seeds fail to learn a necessary skill (e.g., backward motion). Does this suggest a limitation of the pre-training objective? Could the framework be extended to encourage a more "complete" or "orthogonal" basis of skills, perhaps by modifying the MI objective or adding other diversity-promoting terms?
3.  The framework freezes the low-level skills during downstream task training. Have you experimented with fine-tuning the SNN policy while training the manager? While the paper mentions this as future work using Gumbel-Softmax, I'm curious if you have any preliminary insights into the trade-offs between sample efficiency (from freezing) and asymptotic performance (from fine-tuning).
