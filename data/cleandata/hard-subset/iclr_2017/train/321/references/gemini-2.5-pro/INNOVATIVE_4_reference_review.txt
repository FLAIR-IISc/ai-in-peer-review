### Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

**Summary of Contributions**
This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle challenging continuous control tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, an unsupervised or self-supervised "pre-training" phase learns a diverse set of low-level skills, and second, a high-level "manager" policy is trained to select from these skills to solve downstream tasks. The key contributions are the specific mechanisms for the pre-training phase: (1) using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) conditioned on a latent categorical variable to represent a multi-modal policy capable of encoding multiple skills in a single network; (2) training this SNN using only a simple, generic "proxy reward" (e.g., forward velocity) that requires minimal domain knowledge; and (3) introducing an information-theoretic regularizer, based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state, to explicitly encourage the learned skills to be distinct and interpretable. The authors demonstrate qualitatively that this procedure learns a rich span of behaviors and quantitatively that using these skills in a hierarchy dramatically improves exploration and performance on several challenging maze and object-gathering tasks.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper presents a bold, elegant, and highly novel approach to unsupervised skill discovery for hierarchical RL. The central idea of combining a multi-modal policy (parameterized by an SNN) with an information-theoretic objective for encouraging behavioral diversity is a significant conceptual contribution to the field. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the strength and creativity of the core idea, supported by compelling qualitative and quantitative results, make this a clear accept. This work has the potential to open up new and fruitful research directions in HRL.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. Instead of relying on hand-crafted options or complex intrinsic motivation schemes, the authors propose a simple and principled pre-training objective. The use of an SNN to learn multiple skills with shared weights is efficient, and the InfoGAN-inspired mutual information bonus is a particularly clever way to enforce that different latent codes correspond to meaningfully different behaviors. This moves beyond simply hoping that local optima of a proxy reward will produce useful skills, and instead provides a direct optimization pressure for diversity.

The experimental results strongly support the main claims. The visitation plots in Figures 4 and 5 are particularly compelling. They provide clear, intuitive evidence that the SNN with the MI bonus learns a diverse and interpretable set of skills (e.g., moving forward, backward, turning), and that a random high-level policy over these skills explores a vastly larger portion of the state space than naive action-level exploration. This is a powerful demonstration of the framework's primary benefit.

The quantitative results on the downstream sparse-reward tasks (Figure 6) confirm that this enhanced exploration translates directly into learning success. The proposed hierarchical methods significantly outperform the baseline of simply adding the proxy reward, which often fails to learn at all. The fact that the SNN-based hierarchy can outperform a "Multi-policy" baseline (which required 6x the pre-training samples) highlights the efficiency of the proposed approach.

Furthermore, the paper includes a thoughtful ablation study on the SNN architecture (concatenation vs. bilinear integration) and the impact of the MI bonus, which validates the specific design choices made. The overall framework is clean, well-motivated, and the pieces fit together logically.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for Improvement**

While I am enthusiastic about this paper, the following points could help strengthen it further. These are intended as suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

1.  **Scalability of the MI Bonus:** The mutual information bonus is calculated by discretizing the (x, y) coordinates of the agent's center of mass. This is a reasonable choice for the locomotion tasks presented, but it raises questions about scalability. How would this approach be adapted to tasks where the "relevant" subset of the state is not obvious or is high-dimensional (e.g., learning skills from pixel inputs)? The paper mentions the possibility of using a neural network to estimate the posterior `p(z|s)`, which would be a more general solution. Even a brief discussion or a small-scale experiment demonstrating this would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of generality.

2.  **Fixed Temporal Abstraction:** The high-level policy commits to a selected skill for a fixed number of steps `T`. This is a common simplification, but it is less flexible than the option-critic framework, where the model also learns a termination condition. The analysis in Appendix C.3 suggests performance is not overly sensitive to `T` for these static tasks, but it could be a critical limitation in more dynamic environments. A discussion of this limitation and the potential for learning termination policies would be welcome.

3.  **Skill Transitions and Stability:** The appendix rightly points out the failure mode on the unstable "Ant" agent, where sharp transitions between skills can cause the agent to fall. This is a key challenge for any hierarchical method that composes independently-learned skills. It would be valuable to bring some of this discussion from the appendix into the main paper's conclusion or future work section, perhaps suggesting potential solutions like learning a dedicated transition policy or smoothing the switch between skill policies.

4.  **Questions for Authors:**
    *   Regarding the bilinear integration, which proved crucial, have you considered other modern conditioning mechanisms, such as FiLM layers, for injecting the latent code `z` into the policy? Could this further improve the distinctiveness of the learned skills?
    *   The variance in performance depending on the pre-trained SNN (Appendix C.4) is noteworthy. Does this suggest that the pre-training objective, while effective, is still susceptible to local optima? Is there a way to make this process more robust, perhaps by modifying the MI objective or the proxy reward?
    *   You mention the possibility of end-to-end fine-tuning of the skill policies on downstream tasks. Did you perform any preliminary experiments in this direction? I wonder if there is a risk of "catastrophic forgetting," where fine-tuning on a specific task causes the specialized skills to collapse into a single mode, losing the diversity that was so beneficial for exploration.
