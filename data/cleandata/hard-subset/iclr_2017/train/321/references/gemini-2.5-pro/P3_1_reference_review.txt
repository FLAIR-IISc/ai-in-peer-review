### **Review Summary**

This paper proposes a compelling and practical framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, an unsupervised pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) guided by a simple, task-agnostic proxy reward and an information-theoretic regularizer. Second, these learned skills are used as primitives for a high-level policy that is trained on specific downstream tasks. The methodology is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results convincingly demonstrate that this approach enables solving challenging continuous control problems where standard RL methods fail.

### **Decision: Accept**

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Effective and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper addresses a critical challenge in RL—exploration and sample efficiency in sparse-reward settings. The proposed two-stage framework is an elegant and practical solution that effectively combines unsupervised skill discovery with hierarchical control. The use of a simple proxy reward instead of hand-crafting skills or requiring pre-defined goals is a significant strength.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by extensive experiments on challenging benchmark tasks. The qualitative results (visitation plots) provide clear intuition for why the method works, and the quantitative results (learning curves) demonstrate a substantial performance improvement over relevant baselines. The paper includes important ablations that validate the key design choices (SNN architecture, MI regularizer).

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its thoughtful composition of several powerful ideas into a cohesive and effective framework.

**Methodology:**
*   **Unsupervised Skill Discovery:** The pre-training phase is a highlight. By using a generic proxy reward (e.g., forward velocity), the agent is encouraged to learn useful behaviors without needing supervision for specific skills. This is a more scalable and general approach than methods that require pre-defined subgoals.
*   **Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for Skill Representation:** Using an SNN with a latent variable `z` to represent a family of skills is a clever architectural choice. It allows for weight sharing across skills, leading to a K-fold improvement in sample efficiency during pre-training compared to training K separate policies, as demonstrated by the comparison to the "Multi-policy" baseline.
*   **Information-Theoretic Regularization:** The direct application of SNNs does not guarantee skill diversity. The introduction of the mutual information (MI) bonus, inspired by InfoGAN, is a crucial contribution. It provides a principled way to encourage the learned skills (conditioned on the latent code `z`) to be distinguishable in their state visitations, leading to a broader and more useful set of primitives. The ablation studies in Figure 4 convincingly show that both the bilinear integration and the MI bonus are key to learning a rich span of skills.

**Experimental Rigor:**
*   **Clear Evidence of Improved Exploration:** Figure 5 provides a powerful visualization of the core benefit of the hierarchical approach. While naive exploration is confined to a small local area, the random switching between pre-trained skills explores a vastly larger portion of the state space, making it possible to discover sparse rewards.
*   **Strong Performance on Downstream Tasks:** The method shows excellent performance on the Maze and Gather tasks (Figure 6), which are known to be very difficult for standard RL algorithms. The comparison against the baseline where the proxy reward is directly added to the sparse task reward is particularly insightful; it shows that temporal abstraction, not just reward shaping, is the key to solving these long-horizon problems.
*   **Thoroughness and Honesty:** The appendices provide valuable additional results, including scaling to a more complex "Snake" agent and analyzing the effect of the switching time `T`. Crucially, the authors also include a frank discussion of failure modes on the unstable "Ant" agent, which points to an important challenge for future work (state-distribution mismatch when switching skills) and adds credibility to the research.

**Placement in Literature:** The paper is well-situated. It correctly identifies the limitations of classic HRL (manual specification) and standard intrinsic motivation (no explicit skill reuse). It builds upon recent ideas in representation learning (InfoGAN) and makes a clear connection to concurrent work (Heess et al., 2016), while highlighting a key advantage of its own approach (not requiring a set of pre-specified goals).

### **Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could further improve its clarity and impact:

1.  **Scalability of the MI Bonus Calculation:** The MI bonus is currently computed by discretizing the 2D Center-of-Mass (CoM) coordinates. This is effective for the locomotion tasks presented but may not scale gracefully to tasks where the relevant state space for distinguishing skills is higher-dimensional. The paper briefly mentions using an MLP to estimate the posterior `p(z|s)`, which would be a more general solution. Even without implementing it, a more detailed discussion of how this would work and its potential challenges would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Sensitivity to the Proxy Reward:** The concept of a generic proxy reward is very appealing. However, the choice of this reward (e.g., CoM velocity) still encodes a significant prior. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to this choice would be valuable. For instance, what happens if the reward encourages turning, or only moving backward? Would this still yield a useful, albeit different, set of skills?
3.  **Fixed Temporal Abstraction:** The use of a fixed skill execution time `T` is a simplification. While the appendix shows this is not a critical parameter for the tasks studied, more dynamic tasks might require the high-level policy to decide when to terminate a skill. The authors rightly point to the option-critic framework as a direction for future work; this connection could be highlighted more prominently.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Regarding the MI bonus calculation: How sensitive is the performance to the resolution of the grid used for discretization? Have you performed any preliminary experiments with the MLP-based posterior estimator for `p(z|c)`, and do you foresee any optimization challenges with that approach (e.g., stabilizing the joint training of the policy and the estimator)?
2.  The hierarchical policy freezes the low-level skills during downstream training. Did you experiment with fine-tuning the SNN weights end-to-end? While the paper notes this is left for future work, it would be interesting to know if you attempted it and what difficulties, if any, were encountered.
3.  In the Gather task (Figure 6d), the SNN with the MI bonus not only achieves a higher average return but also has noticeably lower variance. Do you have an intuition for why the MI bonus leads to more stable and consistent learning across different pre-training runs? Is it simply that it guarantees a more consistently diverse set of skills?
