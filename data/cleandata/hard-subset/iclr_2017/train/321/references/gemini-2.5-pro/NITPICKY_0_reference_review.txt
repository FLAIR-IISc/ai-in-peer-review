### **Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"**

#### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to solve a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution lies in the first stage, where a diverse set of skills is learned in a pre-training environment using minimal supervision. This is achieved by training a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy, which can represent multiple skills through a latent variable input. To ensure the learned skills are distinct, the authors introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on maximizing the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and a subset of the state (e.g., the agent's coordinates). The paper empirically demonstrates that specific architectural choices, like bilinear integration for the latent variable, combined with the MI bonus, lead to a wide and interpretable span of skills. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as primitives by a high-level "manager" policy, which is trained on the downstream sparse-reward tasks. The experiments, conducted on challenging continuous control benchmarks, show that this hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and enables solving tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of errors, ranging from critical typos in fundamental mathematical definitions to severe formatting issues in the bibliography. This pervasive lack of attention to detail undermines the paper's credibility and suggests a rushed preparation, making it difficult to confidently assess the technical contributions.
2.  Key methodological choices lack rigorous justification and analysis. The MI regularizer, a central component of the method, relies on a highly specific and potentially limiting implementation (i.e., using 2D Center of Mass coordinates and a coarse grid for density estimation) whose generality and sensitivity are not adequately addressed.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments**

My recommendation for rejection is primarily based on the accumulation of numerous flaws which, in aggregate, fall below the standard of rigor expected for an ICLR publication.

**a) Errors, Inconsistencies, and Formatting Issues:**

*   **Incorrect Objective Function:** The policy objective in Section 3 is defined as `η(πθ) = Eτ [ ∑T t=0 γ tr(st, at)]`. The term `γ tr(st, at)` is incorrect. The standard discounted return is `∑ γ^t r(st, at)`. This is a significant error in a foundational definition of the reinforcement learning problem.
*   **Grossly Malformed References:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. Many entries are duplicated and contain parsing errors from the BibTeX file (e.g., "Bacon and Precup.,? \Q2016\E, Bacon and Precup.,", "Duan, Duan,? \Q2016\E, Duan,"). This demonstrates a profound lack of proofreading and care. A properly formatted and curated reference list is a basic requirement for scholarly work.
*   **Sloppy Notation and Formatting:** There are numerous minor yet distracting issues throughout the text. The introduction uses "-greedy" instead of the standard "ε-greedy". Footnotes are misplaced (e.g., the "2" after the first URL) and duplicated (e.g., the code URL appears in the intro and again as footnote 4). The MI bonus term in Eq. (1) and Algorithm 1 uses the notation `αH`, which is ambiguous; it is unclear if `H` is part of the constant's name or a variable. Reward modification is denoted for a whole rollout (`Rnt`) when it is a per-step modification (`r_{n,t}`).

**b) Methodological Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Justification for MI State Space:** The MI bonus `I(Z;C)` is calculated with `C` being the 2D (x, y) coordinates of the agent's Center of Mass (CoM). This choice is convenient for locomotion but is not universally applicable. For manipulation tasks, for instance, gripper orientation or object proximity might be far more relevant for defining a skill. The paper briefly mentions using an MLP for higher-dimensional `C` but provides no experiments or analysis, leaving the reader to question how the method would generalize beyond simple navigation.
*   **Crude Posterior Estimation:** The posterior `p(Z|C)` is estimated via discretization of the 2D space into a grid. This method is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality and is only feasible for very low-dimensional spaces. The impact of approximation errors from this coarse gridding on the learning signal is not discussed. While this is an implementation detail, it is a critical one for a core contribution of the paper.
*   **Vague Assumptions:** The problem statement in Section 4 assumes the state space can be factored into `Sagent` and `SMrest` which "only weakly interact with each other." This crucial assumption is never formalized, rendering the problem statement less rigorous than it could be.

While the paper's empirical results are promising and the core idea is compelling, these numerous shortcomings prevent a confident endorsement of the work in its current form.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite my recommendation, I believe the work has significant potential. The empirical study is thorough, with strong baselines, thoughtful ablations, and an honest discussion of failure modes (e.g., for the Ant agent). The following suggestions are intended to help strengthen the manuscript for a future submission:

*   **Meticulous Proofreading:** The first and most critical step is to perform a thorough proofreading of the entire manuscript to correct all mathematical typos, notational inconsistencies, grammatical errors, and, most importantly, to completely rebuild the reference list.
*   **Strengthen the MI Regularizer Discussion:** Provide a more robust justification for the choice of `C`. A discussion of heuristics for selecting the appropriate state variables for different task domains would be valuable. Even a small-scale experiment showing the feasibility of the proposed MLP-based posterior estimator would significantly strengthen the claim of generality.
*   **Analyze Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The MI bonus relies on the grid resolution (`10 divisions/unit`) and the coefficient `αH`. An analysis of the method's sensitivity to these hyperparameters would be insightful.
*   **Clarify Experimental Reporting:** The reporting of "10 runs: 2 random seeds for 5 different SNNs" is slightly unconventional. While averaging over pre-training seeds is good practice, it would be clearer to report the mean and variance across N distinct pre-training runs, where for each run, the downstream task is evaluated M times.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Can you confirm that the policy objective in Section 3 is intended to be the standard discounted return, `∑_{t=0}^T γ^t r(st, at)`, and that the current formulation is a typo?
2.  The choice of the CoM coordinates for the MI bonus is central to the pre-training. How would you propose selecting the relevant state variables `C` for a more complex task, such as a robotic arm learning to manipulate a variety of objects, where CoM position is less descriptive of the skill?
3.  The performance on the Maze tasks shows high variance depending on the pre-trained SNN (Sec. C.4). Does this imply that the unsupervised pre-training objective is not a fully reliable proxy for generating a universally competent set of skills, and a successful outcome for certain downstream tasks depends on a fortunate pre-training run?
