### **Summary**

The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve complex tasks with sparse rewards. The method consists of two main stages. First, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a simple environment using only a generic "proxy" reward signal. To learn multiple skills efficiently, the authors employ a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy conditioned on a discrete latent variable. To ensure the learned skills are distinct, they introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and the agent's state. In the second stage, the learned low-level SNN policy is frozen, and a high-level "manager" policy is trained on top of it to solve specific downstream tasks. This manager selects a skill (i.e., a latent code for the SNN) and executes it for a fixed number of timesteps. The authors demonstrate their approach on several simulated robotics tasks, showing that the pre-trained skills enhance exploration and enable solving challenging maze and gathering tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's primary contribution is a novel method for hierarchical reinforcement learning. However, the empirical evaluation is primarily conducted against a weak baseline (a flat policy with the pre-training proxy reward). To validate the claims, the proposed method must be compared against more established and relevant HRL or intrinsic motivation baselines, such as the Option-Critic framework or other contemporary skill-discovery methods. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the true contribution and performance of the proposed approach relative to the state of the art.

2.  **Questionable Scalability of Key Methodological Components:** The proposed information-theoretic regularizer, which is crucial for learning diverse skills, relies on a discretization of the state space to estimate the posterior `p(z|s)`. This approach works for the 2D center-of-mass coordinates used in the experiments but is not a general solution and will not scale to higher-dimensional or more abstract state representations. The paper briefly mentions an alternative using a function approximator but provides no experiments, leaving a critical component of the method unvalidated for more complex scenarios.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of unsupervised pre-training of skills followed by hierarchical deployment is compelling. However, the current manuscript does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of effectiveness and novelty in the broader context of HRL.

Regarding the **choice of baselines**, the paper compares its hierarchical SNN approach against a "Multi-policy" architecture (training separate policies for each skill) and a flat policy augmented with the proxy reward. While the Multi-policy comparison is a useful ablation study that demonstrates the sample efficiency of the SNN, the main baseline is not representative of current HRL methods. The problem of skill discovery and temporal abstraction has been studied extensively. Methods like Option-Critic (Bacon et al., 2016) learn options end-to-end, while other works like Heess et al. (2016) also explore pre-training skills for downstream tasks. A comparison to such methods is necessary to properly situate the paper's contribution. The results against VIME in the appendix are encouraging, but this should be a central comparison in the main paper, and it should be analyzed more thoroughly.

The second major concern is the **methodological soundness and scalability**. The mutual information bonus is a key contribution for encouraging skill diversity. The proposed estimation technique, `p̂(Z = z|c) = mc(z) / ∑z′ mc(z′)`, hinges on discretizing the state space into a grid of cells (`c`). This is a strong limitation. For agents with high-dimensional states (e.g., from pixels or complex morphologies like the Ant), defining a relevant low-dimensional subspace and an appropriate discretization scheme is non-trivial and requires significant domain knowledge, which contradicts the goal of a general framework. The suggestion to use a separate neural network to estimate the posterior is a potential solution, but without empirical validation, it remains speculative. This leaves a critical part of the proposed algorithm on uncertain ground.

Furthermore, the hierarchical structure itself is quite rigid. The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. This is a significant simplification compared to frameworks like options, which can learn termination conditions, allowing for more flexible and adaptive behavior. The appendix shows experiments with varying `T`, but the fact that this is a fixed, manually-tuned hyperparameter limits the autonomy and potential performance of the agent. The failure case reported for the Ant robot, where switching skills leads to instability, highlights the brittleness of this fixed-timing and frozen-skill approach.

Finally, the experiments show very high variance in the performance of the hierarchical policies, which is attributed to the quality of the pre-trained SNNs. This indicates that the pre-training procedure is not robust and may require significant trial and error (i.e., multiple pre-training runs) to find a set of skills that is useful for downstream tasks. A reliable method should consistently produce useful and diverse skills.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

While I am recommending rejection, I find the central ideas of the paper to be promising. The use of SNNs for sharing weights across multiple skills is elegant, and the application of an MI bonus for diversification is intuitive. The following points are intended as constructive feedback.

*   **Clarity on Proxy Reward Design:** The paper claims the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge." However, designing a reward that encourages a *useful* set of skills still seems to require considerable foresight. For example, rewarding forward speed is a natural choice for locomotion, but what would be an equally general proxy reward for a manipulation task? More discussion on the principles of designing these rewards would strengthen the paper.
*   **Ablation of MI Bonus:** The visitation plots in Figure 4 are a nice qualitative demonstration of the MI bonus's effect. It would be beneficial to also include a quantitative measure of skill separation (e.g., the final MI value achieved, or a metric measuring the distance between state distributions conditioned on different skills).
*   **End-to-End Training:** The paper mentions the possibility of fine-tuning the low-level skills for downstream tasks. While this is left as future work, even a small experiment showing the potential benefits or challenges of this approach would make the framework seem more flexible and powerful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more justification for not comparing against established HRL frameworks like Option-Critic in the main paper? How do you see your method's core strengths and weaknesses in relation to theirs?
2.  The MI estimation via state-space discretization appears to be a major limitation. Have you performed any experiments with a function approximator-based estimator for `p(z|s)`, as suggested in the paper? If so, how does it affect the stability and performance of the pre-training phase?
3.  The performance on downstream tasks is highly dependent on the random seed of the pre-training phase. What are your thoughts on improving the robustness of the skill discovery process to ensure that a useful and comprehensive set of skills is learned more consistently?
