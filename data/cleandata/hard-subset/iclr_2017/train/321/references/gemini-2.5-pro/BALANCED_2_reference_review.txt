### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is a two-phase approach: first, an unsupervised pre-training phase where a diverse set of reusable skills is learned in a simple environment using only a generic "proxy reward" signal. Second, these learned skills are used as primitives by a high-level "manager" policy to solve a variety of complex downstream tasks.

The main contributions lie in the methodology for the skill discovery phase. The authors propose using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent the entire skill repertoire, where different skills are conditioned on a latent categorical code. This approach allows for efficient weight sharing and is significantly more sample-efficient than training separate policies for each skill. To encourage the emergence of distinct and diverse behaviors, the authors introduce two key innovations: (1) a bilinear integration mechanism to combine the state observation and the latent code, which proves more expressive than simple concatenation, and (2) an information-theoretic regularizer that maximizes the mutual information between the latent code and the agent's state. This regularizer explicitly pushes the network to learn skills that are distinguishable from one another.

The experiments, conducted on challenging continuous control locomotion tasks (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) and sparse-reward downstream tasks (Mazes, Gather), compellingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework. The results show that the SNN with the proposed regularizer learns a rich and interpretable set of skills, which in turn enables a hierarchical agent to significantly improve exploration and solve tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Principled Approach to Unsupervised Skill Discovery:** The paper introduces a well-motivated and elegant method for learning a repertoire of skills without direct supervision. The combination of a Stochastic Neural Network with an information-theoretic objective provides a principled way to generate diverse and reusable behaviors, which is a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors not only demonstrate strong performance on challenging benchmark tasks but also provide excellent qualitative analysis (e.g., visitation plots) and ablation studies that clearly validate the importance of each component of their proposed method (SNN vs. multiple policies, bilinear integration, MI bonus).

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and well-supported by the evidence provided.

*   **Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The work is very well-motivated, addressing the critical challenge of exploration in sparse-reward settings. It is well-positioned in the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from prior work on hand-engineered HRL and pure intrinsic motivation methods by proposing a hybrid approach that learns reusable skills in a task-agnostic manner.
*   **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed method is clear, technically sound, and elegant. Using a latent-variable-conditioned policy (SNN) is a natural fit for learning multiple skills, and the addition of the mutual information bonus `I(Z;C)` is a principled way to enforce diversity. This avoids mode collapse and results in interpretable and distinct skills, as convincingly shown in Figure 4. The justification for using bilinear integration over simple concatenation is well-supported by the ablation results.
*   **Empirical Rigor:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed and executed.
    *   The qualitative visualization of learned skills (Figure 4) and the exploration coverage of the hierarchical policy (Figure 5) provide powerful, intuitive evidence for the method's effectiveness.
    *   The quantitative results on the downstream Maze and Gather tasks (Figure 6) show that the proposed hierarchical architecture significantly outperforms a relevant baseline (using the proxy reward directly).
    *   The comparison against the "Multi-policy" architecture serves as a strong baseline, demonstrating that the SNN approach is not only ~6x more sample-efficient during pre-training but can also lead to better or more comprehensive exploration.
    *   The authors are transparent about the limitations of their approach, including the instability observed with the "Ant" robot, which adds to the credibility of the research. The detailed appendices provide valuable further analysis.

While the paper is strong, there are minor weaknesses that could be addressed to further improve it. The mutual information bonus calculation relies on discretizing the 2D coordinates of the agent's center of mass. While effective here, this may not be straightforwardly applicable to tasks where a relevant low-dimensional state subspace is not obvious. The paper mentions the possibility of using a learned function approximator (MLP) for the posterior, but this is not explored experimentally. Additionally, the hierarchical control scheme is relatively simple, using a fixed time `T` for skill execution, which may be suboptimal for more dynamic tasks. However, the authors acknowledge these limitations and appropriately frame them as avenues for future work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Comparison to Concurrent Work:** The information-theoretic objective is a powerful idea. It would be beneficial to expand the related work section to discuss other contemporary approaches that also use mutual information for unsupervised skill discovery, explaining the key differences in formulation and application.
*   **Designing Proxy Rewards:** The success of the pre-training phase hinges on the design of the proxy reward. For the locomotion tasks, rewarding forward speed is intuitive. The paper mentions "successful grasping of any object" for a manipulator. A more general discussion on the principles or heuristics for designing effective proxy rewards would be a valuable addition for practitioners seeking to apply this framework to new domains.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper provides a good analysis of the switch time `T` in the appendix. A similar analysis for the MI bonus coefficient `αH` in the main paper would be welcome. Showing how the diversity of skills and downstream performance vary with this parameter would provide a better understanding of the method's robustness.
*   **Fine-tuning Skills:** The paper suggests that end-to-end training (fine-tuning the low-level SNN policy) is a possible extension. While keeping skills frozen simplifies the problem, an experiment demonstrating the trade-offs of fine-tuning would be very insightful. Does it improve performance on a specific task at the cost of "forgetting" the general-purpose nature of the skills?

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their response:

1.  The MI bonus calculation relies on a grid-based discretization of the (x, y) coordinates. How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to the resolution of this grid? Have you experimented with the proposed MLP-based posterior estimator `p̂(Z|c)` for cases where `c` might be higher-dimensional or continuous?
2.  The instability when switching skills for the Ant robot is an important finding. Do you have a hypothesis for why this is more pronounced for the Ant compared to the Swimmer or Snake? Could this be mitigated by incorporating a cost for switching, or by encouraging state-space overlap between skills during pre-training?
3.  The SNN-based hierarchy not only matches but sometimes appears to provide better exploration coverage than the Multi-policy baseline (Figure 5), despite being trained with far fewer samples. Do you have an intuition for why the weight-sharing architecture of the SNN might lead to a qualitatively better or more diverse set of skills, beyond the sample efficiency gains?
4.  In the experiments, some pre-trained SNNs failed to solve certain mazes (Appendix C.4), highlighting the variability of the unsupervised pre-training. Does the MI bonus help in reducing this variability across different pre-training runs? Is it possible to quantify the "completeness" of a learned skill set before deploying it on downstream tasks?
