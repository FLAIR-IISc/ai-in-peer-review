### Review Summary

This paper presents a compelling and well-executed framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to tackle challenging tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process: first, an unsupervised pre-training phase learns a diverse set of skills using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) guided by a simple, task-agnostic proxy reward and an information-theoretic regularizer. Second, these frozen skills are used as primitives by a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on specific downstream tasks. The paper's main claims are that this method (1) efficiently learns a wide and interpretable span of skills, (2) significantly improves exploration compared to naive strategies, and (3) enables solving complex, sparse-reward tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Skill Discovery Method:** The paper introduces a powerful and principled approach for unsupervised skill discovery. The combination of a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent a multi-modal policy, a simple proxy reward for general guidance, and a mutual information (MI) bonus to explicitly encourage skill diversity is elegant and well-motivated. The experiments compellingly demonstrate that this combination, particularly with the proposed bilinear integration, consistently produces a rich set of interpretable, low-level skills (e.g., moving forward, backward, turning) from a single training run, which is significantly more sample-efficient than training separate policies.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation on Challenging Tasks:** The paper provides strong evidence that the proposed HRL framework successfully leverages the pre-trained skills to solve benchmark tasks known to be difficult for standard RL due to sparse rewards. The experiments clearly show that the hierarchical agent explores the environment far more effectively than agents with unstructured exploration noise. Furthermore, the learning curves demonstrate a substantial performance gain over a well-chosen baseline (direct optimization with the proxy reward), effectively isolating the benefit of the hierarchical structure itself. The inclusion of extensive ablations and analysis in the main paper and appendix (e.g., on different robot morphologies and the effect of hyperparameters) adds to the rigor of the evaluation.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's strength lies in its clear problem formulation and the methodical construction of a solution. The introduction correctly identifies the limitations of naive exploration, hand-engineered hierarchies, and standard intrinsic motivation methods, motivating the need for an approach that learns reusable skills for a *collection* of tasks.

The methodology is the paper's centerpiece. The use of an SNN with a latent code `z` to represent different skills within a single network is a key insight for sample efficiency and weight sharing. The authors correctly identify a potential failure mode—mode collapse, where all latent codes map to the same behavior. Their solution, an MI-based reward bonus `I(Z; C)`, is inspired by successful work in unsupervised representation learning (InfoGAN) and provides a principled way to enforce diversity. The visitation plots in Figure 4 provide clear, qualitative evidence that the MI bonus and the bilinear integration architecture are crucial for learning a wide span of distinct skills.

The empirical results convincingly support the paper's claims. Figure 5 offers a striking visualization of the exploration benefit: the hierarchical agent covers a vastly larger portion of the state space than an agent with random action noise. This enhanced exploration directly translates to success on the downstream tasks. In Figure 6, the proposed methods significantly outperform the baseline on all maze and gather tasks, demonstrating that decomposing the problem into selecting among meaningful primitives makes the credit assignment problem tractable. The authors are also transparent about the limitations, such as the high variance in performance on the maze tasks, and provide a plausible explanation: the performance depends on whether the randomly seeded pre-training phase happened to discover the specific skills (e.g., backward motion) necessary for that particular maze. This honesty strengthens the paper. The analysis extends to more complex agents (Snake, Ant) in the appendix, showing both the scalability and potential failure modes (instability during skill switching), which is characteristic of thorough research.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The paper is very well-written and the work is of high quality. The following points are intended to help further strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

1.  **Tension between Skill Diversity and Task Performance:** There appears to be a slight disconnect between the results in Fig. 4 and Fig. 6. The MI bonus clearly improves the diversity and separation of skills in pre-training (Fig. 4d vs 4c), but this does not consistently translate to better final performance on the maze tasks (Fig. 6a-c). It would be beneficial to add a short discussion on this. Is it possible that maximizing diversity for its own sake can lead to skills that are less relevant for a specific set of downstream tasks? Perhaps a "sharper" but less diverse set of skills is sometimes preferable.

2.  **Sensitivity to Proxy Reward Design:** The paper argues that the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge." While the reward for forward speed is indeed simple and effective for locomotion, the quality of the learned skills seems fundamentally tied to the quality of this reward. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the framework to this choice? For instance, what might happen if the proxy reward encouraged suboptimal behaviors (e.g., just rewarding any joint movement, which could lead to thrashing in place)? A brief discussion on principles for designing good proxy rewards would be valuable.

3.  **Scaling the MI Bonus Calculation:** The MI bonus is calculated by discretizing the (x, y) coordinates of the agent. The paper mentions that for higher-dimensional state spaces, one could train a neural network to estimate the posterior `p(z|c)`. This is a crucial point for the general applicability of the method. Could the authors clarify if they have experimented with this, and comment on the stability and sample complexity of training this auxiliary network simultaneously with the policy?

4.  **Fixed Time Abstraction (`T`):** The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The appendix shows this is not a critical parameter for the tasks studied, but for more dynamic environments, a fixed `T` could be limiting. The authors rightly point to learning a termination policy (as in the options framework) as future work. Acknowledging this limitation more explicitly in the main body of the paper would be helpful for positioning the work.
