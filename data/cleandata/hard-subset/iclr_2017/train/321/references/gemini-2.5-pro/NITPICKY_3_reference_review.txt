**Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned in an unsupervised manner using a simple, task-agnostic proxy reward. Second, a downstream training phase where a high-level "manager" policy is trained to select from these pre-trained, frozen skills to solve specific tasks. The authors leverage Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent a multi-modal policy capable of learning multiple skills within a single network, which improves sample efficiency compared to training separate policies. To ensure the learned skills are distinct, they introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on maximizing the mutual information (MI) between the latent skill variable and a subset of the agent's state. The experimental results on challenging continuous control benchmarks (e.g., Swimmer and Snake robots in mazes) demonstrate that this approach significantly enhances exploration and enables solving tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a conceptually appealing framework and strong empirical results, I recommend rejection due to two primary reasons:
1.  The manuscript is beset by numerous and severe presentation flaws, including a completely corrupted reference section and inconsistent formatting throughout. This lack of attention to detail makes it difficult to properly contextualize the work and undermines confidence in its scientific rigor.
2.  The methodological claims of generality and minimal supervision are overstated. The proposed MI-based regularizer, a key component of the method, relies on a discretization of the state space that does not scale and requires manually specifying relevant state dimensions, which constitutes significant domain knowledge.

**Supporting Arguments**

**1. Severe Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
A manuscript submitted for review should be polished and clear. This paper fails to meet that standard.
*   **Corrupted References:** The entire reference list is improperly formatted, with BibTeX compilation artifacts (`? \QYEAR\E`) appearing in every entry. This makes the bibliography nearly unreadable and prevents proper verification of citations. Furthermore, a stray sentence fragment appears to be appended to the end of the reference list.
*   **Inconsistent Footnotes:** The footnote numbering is erratic. An out-of-place footnote marker '2' appears mid-URL in the introduction. Later, the paper uses footnotes '4' and '5' for identical URLs that were already provided in the introduction. This suggests a rushed and careless preparation of the manuscript.
*   **Lack of Detail:** Critical hyperparameters, such as the coefficient for the MI bonus (`αH`), are discussed and varied in the experiments (Fig. 4) but are conspicuously absent from the hyperparameter table in Appendix A. This is a significant omission for reproducibility.

**2. Overstated Claims and Methodological Limitations:**
The paper's claims of being a "general framework" requiring "minimal domain knowledge" are not fully substantiated by the methodology presented.
*   **Non-Scalable MI Estimation:** The mutual information bonus is calculated by discretizing the 2D (x,y) coordinate space into a grid. This approach is fundamentally limited to low-dimensional state representations. The authors briefly mention in a single sentence that an MLP regressor could be used for higher-dimensional spaces, but this is presented without any implementation details, analysis, or empirical validation. As presented, this core component of the method is not applicable to tasks with high-dimensional states (e.g., from pixels), contradicting the claims of generality.
*   **Non-Minimal Domain Knowledge:** The framework requires more domain knowledge than advertised. In addition to designing a suitable proxy reward, the practitioner must also manually select a "relevant subset of the state" (e.g., the center of mass coordinates) for the MI calculation. The choice of this subset is critical for defining what constitutes a distinct "skill" and is a form of non-trivial feature engineering.
*   **Guaranteed Skill Usefulness:** The pre-training is entirely agnostic to the downstream tasks. The high variance reported in the maze experiments (and analyzed in Appendix C.4) confirms that this unsupervised approach offers no guarantee that the learned skills will be useful. For some pre-training seeds, the agent fails to learn entirely because it lacks a necessary primitive skill (e.g., moving backward), highlighting a potential brittleness in the framework.
*   **Structural Assumptions:** The entire framework rests on the assumption that the state space can be factored into `S_agent` and `S_rest`. While acknowledged, calling this a "minimal" assumption is debatable, as it may not hold for a wide range of problems.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The core ideas in this paper are promising. Should the authors choose to revise their work, I would suggest the following:
*   Thoroughly proofread and reformat the entire manuscript. Correcting the reference list and ensuring consistent formatting is a mandatory first step.
*   Provide a more detailed and honest discussion of the domain knowledge required by the framework (proxy reward design, state subset selection for MI).
*   To support the claim of generality, the non-scalable discretization method for the MI bonus must be addressed. An implementation and evaluation of the proposed MLP-based posterior estimator would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   The baseline using the proxy reward directly in the downstream task is good. However, the paper could be strengthened by comparing against other unsupervised skill discovery methods, such as DIAYN (Eysenbach et al., 2018), which also uses an MI-based objective.
*   The claim that "most previous work" resorts to cherry-picking pre-trained policies is a strong accusation and should be either substantiated with citations or removed.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  What specific values for the MI bonus coefficient `α_H` were used to generate the results in Figure 4 and for the final SNN policies evaluated in the downstream tasks in Figure 6?
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential challenges of using an MLP to estimate the posterior `p(z|c)` in a high-dimensional continuous space? For instance, how would you ensure the estimator is accurate enough to provide a useful learning signal, especially early in training when state coverage is sparse?
3.  Given that the utility of a pre-trained SNN for a downstream task can vary dramatically (as shown in Appendix C.4), have you considered any mechanisms to adapt the pre-training objective to be more "aware" of the distribution of downstream tasks, perhaps by incorporating a small amount of information about them?
