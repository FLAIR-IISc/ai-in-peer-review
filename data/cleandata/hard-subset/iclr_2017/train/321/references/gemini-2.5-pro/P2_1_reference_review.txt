### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to solve complex, sparse-reward tasks. The core contribution is a novel and sample-efficient method for unsupervised skill discovery in a pre-training phase. In this phase, the agent learns a diverse set of behaviors, or "skills," using only a simple, task-agnostic proxy reward. The key technical innovations are:
1.  The use of a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as the policy, where a latent categorical variable conditions the network's output. This allows a single network to represent a multi-modal policy, effectively learning multiple distinct skills simultaneously and sharing weights, leading to significant gains in sample efficiency compared to training separate policies.
2.  The introduction of an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent skill variable and the agent's state. This regularizer explicitly encourages the learned skills to be distinct and cover different parts of the state space, preventing mode collapse and leading to a richer, more interpretable set of behaviors.

In the second stage, these pre-trained, frozen skills are used as a set of primitive actions for a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on the specific downstream sparse-reward tasks. The paper demonstrates that this hierarchical approach drastically improves exploration and enables solving challenging locomotion and navigation tasks where standard RL methods fail.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a clear and well-motivated contribution to a significant problem in reinforcement learning. The proposed method is elegant, the experiments are thorough and convincing, and the results are impressive.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Skill Discovery Method:** The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks (specifically with the proposed bilinear integration) and a mutual information bonus provides a principled and highly effective method for learning a diverse set of reusable skills in an unsupervised manner. This is a significant step forward from hand-engineering options or training multiple policies independently, which is sample-inefficient.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence to support its claims. The qualitative visualizations (visitation plots) clearly demonstrate the diversity of the learned skills and the superiority of the proposed SNN architecture and MI bonus. The quantitative results on challenging benchmark tasks show that the framework significantly outperforms relevant baselines and can solve tasks with very sparse rewards that are intractable for standard policy gradient methods.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper successfully addresses the three key questions for a strong submission.

**1. Problem Tackled:** The problem of exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks is a central challenge in modern RL. The paper tackles this by learning a useful behavioral prior through unsupervised skill discovery, which is a well-recognized and promising direction.

**2. Motivation and Positioning:** The approach is well-motivated and situated within the existing literature. The authors correctly identify the limitations of prior work in HRL (requiring domain knowledge) and intrinsic motivation (not explicitly providing transferable skills). Their method provides a practical bridge between these two areas. The comparison to concurrent work (Heess et al., 2016) and prior methods (Daniel et al., 2012) is clear and effectively highlights the novelty of their approach (e.g., not requiring pre-specified goals for skill learning).

**3. Support for Claims:** The claims are very well-supported by a rigorous set of experiments.
*   **Skill Diversity (Sec 7.1):** The visitation plots in Figure 4 are excellent. They provide a clear ablation study, showing that: (a) bilinear integration is superior to simple concatenation for generating distinct behaviors from the latent codes, and (b) the MI bonus further encourages diversity and reduces skill overlap. This strongly supports the main technical contributions.
*   **Enhanced Exploration (Sec 7.2):** Figure 5 provides a powerful visualization of the exploration benefit. The hierarchical agent with pre-trained skills explores a vastly larger state space than an agent with random actions, directly demonstrating its potential to find sparse rewards.
*   **Downstream Task Performance (Sec 7.3):** The learning curves in Figure 6 show that the hierarchical agent learns much faster and achieves significantly higher returns than a strong baseline (a flat policy with the same proxy reward). Crucially, the SNN-based agent is competitive with the "Multi-policy" baseline while using 6x fewer samples in the pre-training phase, confirming the claimed sample efficiency. The authors' transparency about the variance in performance due to the quality of the pre-trained SNN is a sign of scientific maturity.

The appendices further strengthen the paper by providing additional results on a more complex agent ("Snake"), an analysis of the switch time hyperparameter `T`, and an honest discussion of failure modes on unstable agents like "Ant".

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could help further improve it:
*   **MI Estimation Scalability:** The MI bonus is calculated by discretizing the 2D Center of Mass (CoM) space. This is effective here, but it would not scale to higher-dimensional state representations. The paper briefly mentions fitting an MLP as an alternative. Expanding slightly on this and discussing the potential challenges (e.g., instability of training a discriminator/regressor simultaneously with the policy) would make the method feel more general.
*   **Sensitivity to Proxy Reward:** The paper argues the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge." For the locomotion tasks, rewarding speed is a natural choice. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on how one might design a proxy reward for different domains, such as manipulation, and how sensitive the framework might be to this choice.
*   **Fixed Time Abstraction:** The use of a fixed time commitment `T` is a simplification. The analysis in Appendix C.3 is very helpful in showing this is not a critical limitation for the tasks studied. However, mentioning the connection to the options framework where a termination policy is learned would be a valuable addition to the main body's discussion section, as it's a natural and important next step for more dynamic tasks.

### 5. Questions for the Authors
1.  Regarding the MI bonus estimation: How sensitive is the performance to the grid resolution chosen for the CoM space discretization? Have you experimented with the alternative of training a neural network classifier to estimate `p(z|c)`, and if so, how did it compare in terms of stability and final results?
2.  The manager policy only receives an observation every `T` timesteps. Did you consider a recurrent architecture for the manager that could process the sequence of states observed during a skill's execution to make a more informed decision at the next switching point?
3.  The failure mode on the "Ant" agent due to instability during skill switching is a very interesting and general problem for HRL. Beyond the ideas mentioned in the future work, do you have any intuition on whether this could be mitigated during the pre-training phase, for example by encouraging skills to have overlapping initial state distributions or by explicitly training for smooth transitions?
