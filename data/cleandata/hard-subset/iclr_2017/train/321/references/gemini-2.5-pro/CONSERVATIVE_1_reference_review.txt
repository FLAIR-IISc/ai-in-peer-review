### Summary

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework to tackle tasks with sparse rewards. The method consists of two stages. First, in a pre-training phase, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in an unsupervised manner using a generic, task-agnostic proxy reward. To learn multiple skills efficiently, the authors employ a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) where a latent categorical variable conditions the policy's behavior. To encourage diversity among the skills, they introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills are frozen and used as primitives for a high-level manager policy, which is trained on specific downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The manager selects a skill to execute for a fixed period, effectively simplifying the exploration problem. The authors demonstrate their approach on challenging continuous control tasks, showing that the learned skills are interpretable and that the hierarchical policy can solve sparse reward problems where standard RL algorithms fail.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty and Positioning:** The proposed framework, while effective on the chosen tasks, combines several existing ideas without offering a significant conceptual advance. The two-stage "pre-train skills, then train manager" paradigm is a well-established pattern in HRL. The use of a latent variable policy to represent skills and an information-theoretic objective to ensure diversity are direct adaptations of ideas from other domains (e.g., InfoGAN). The paper does not sufficiently engage with or differentiate itself from closely related, established HRL methods for skill discovery, such as the option-critic architecture or other intrinsic motivation approaches that also aim to learn diverse skills.

2.  **Insufficiently Rigorous Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating positive results, is not comprehensive enough to support the broad claims of the paper. The primary baseline for downstream tasks—adding the proxy reward directly to the sparse task reward—is not a state-of-the-art exploration method. A convincing case for the superiority of this approach would require comparison against more established and powerful exploration baselines (e.g., count-based models, VIME) and other contemporary skill-learning methods on the final tasks. Furthermore, the high variance in performance reported across different pre-trained policies suggests that the skill discovery process is not consistently reliable, which undermines its robustness as a general-purpose framework.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Regarding Methodological Soundness and Novelty:**

The core components of the proposed method are heavily inspired by prior work. The idea of using a latent code to generate diverse outputs is central to generative models like GANs, and the specific use of a mutual information bonus to make this code interpretable was popularized by InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016). The paper applies this idea to RL policies, which is a reasonable step, but not a fundamental innovation in itself. Similar MI-based objectives have also been explored for skill discovery in other concurrent or prior works.

The hierarchical structure itself is quite standard. However, the implementation is methodologically simplistic compared to established HRL frameworks like the options framework (Sutton et al., 1999). Specifically, the use of a fixed time `T` for skill execution is a rigid form of temporal abstraction. More sophisticated methods learn termination conditions, allowing the agent to switch skills more flexibly based on the current context. While the authors show in the appendix that performance is not overly sensitive to `T` on their static tasks, this is a significant limitation that restricts the general applicability of the method and represents a step back from more developed HRL theories.

The calculation of the MI bonus relies on discretizing the agent's (x, y) coordinates. This is a form of engineered feature that requires domain knowledge (i.e., knowing that the agent's 2D position is the most relevant aspect of its state for differentiating skills). This approach may not scale to tasks where the relevant state information is higher-dimensional or less obvious, undermining the claim of requiring only "minimal domain knowledge".

**Regarding Experimental Rigor:**

The choice of baselines is a major weakness. The main comparison on the sparse-reward tasks is against a flat policy that is augmented with the proxy reward. While this is a sensible sanity check, it is not a competitive baseline for exploration. The benchmark paper by Duan et al. (2016) already established that naive methods fail on these tasks, and the community has since developed more advanced exploration strategies. The comparison to VIME in the appendix is noted, but this should be a central part of the main results, alongside other strong baselines, to properly contextualize the paper's contribution. Without a thorough comparison to established methods like option-critic or other skill-discovery algorithms, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed SNN-based approach is truly superior.

The high variance in the learning curves for the downstream tasks (Figure 6) is a significant concern. The authors attribute this to the variability in the quality of skills learned during pre-training. This indicates that the pre-training procedure is not robust and that the success of the overall framework is highly dependent on a successful (and seemingly lucky) pre-training run. A method that only works with a "good" set of pre-trained skills is not reliable. This empirical finding seems to contradict the claim that the proposed method "consistently yields a large span of skills."

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

1.  **Strengthen Positioning within HRL Literature:** The paper would be much stronger if it included a more detailed comparison to the rich literature on HRL and skill discovery. Explicitly discussing the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method relative to the options framework (including option-critic), DIAYN, and other unsupervised skill discovery methods would provide crucial context.

2.  **Improve Experimental Baselines:** To make a compelling case, the method should be benchmarked against stronger, more relevant baselines on the downstream tasks. This should include state-of-the-art generic exploration methods and other well-known HRL algorithms designed for skill discovery.

3.  **Address Methodological Limitations:** The reliance on a fixed switching time `T` and the hand-crafted state discretization for the MI bonus are notable limitations. The paper could be improved by discussing these limitations more directly and proposing or testing more scalable solutions (e.g., learning a termination policy, using a neural network to estimate the posterior for the MI bonus without discretization).

4.  **Investigate Pre-training Robustness:** The high variance in downstream performance suggests a problem with the robustness of the pre-training phase. An analysis of what constitutes a "good" vs. "bad" set of skills and why the pre-training sometimes fails to produce a good set would be a valuable addition. If the method is highly sensitive to random seeds, its practical utility is limited.

The visualizations of the learned skills and exploration patterns are a clear strength of the paper and are very effective at building intuition. The writing is also clear and well-structured. However, at present, the work feels more like a successful application of existing ideas rather than a novel, rigorously-validated contribution that pushes the boundaries of the field.
