**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and compelling framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of reusable skills in an unsupervised manner, guided only by a simple, task-agnostic proxy reward (e.g., forward velocity). To learn multiple skills efficiently within a single model, the authors employ a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), where a latent categorical variable conditions the policy's behavior. A key innovation is the introduction of an information-theoretic regularizer, inspired by InfoGAN, which maximizes the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's state. This elegantly encourages the different latent codes to produce distinct and interpretable behaviors. In the second stage, these learned skills are treated as a set of primitives, and a high-level "manager" policy is trained on top of them to solve specific downstream tasks. The experiments on challenging continuous control environments (mazes and object gathering) demonstrate that this approach dramatically improves exploration and enables the agent to solve tasks where standard RL methods fail.

**Decision: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work introduces a bold and highly novel approach to automatic skill discovery that elegantly connects ideas from unsupervised representation learning with hierarchical reinforcement learning. The central idea of using a latent-conditioned policy regularized by mutual information is a significant conceptual contribution that opens up a promising new direction for HRL. This novel direction is supported by strong empirical results, including compelling qualitative visualizations and quantitative performance gains on difficult benchmark tasks.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. Framing unsupervised skill discovery as the problem of learning an interpretable, disentangled latent representation of behaviors is a powerful idea. Instead of relying on complex heuristics like identifying bottleneck states, the method uses a simple, generic proxy reward and an information-theoretic objective to encourage behavioral diversity. The adaptation of the mutual information maximization principle from generative models to RL is creative and exceptionally well-suited for this problem. This represents a significant and inspiring departure from more incremental work in the field.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by a thorough set of experiments. The qualitative "visitation plots" (Figure 4) provide clear and intuitive evidence that the SNN, particularly with bilinear integration and the MI bonus, learns a diverse and interpretable set of skills (e.g., moving forward, backward, turning). The exploration plots (Figure 5) are particularly striking, showing a night-and-day difference between the exploration coverage of the hierarchical policy and naive exploration. Most importantly, this enhanced exploration translates directly into solving complex tasks with very sparse rewards (Figure 6), where strong baselines fail completely. The authors' choice to evaluate performance averaged over multiple pre-trained SNNs, rather than cherry-picking the best one, adds to the credibility of the results.

3.  **Methodologically Sound and Well-Analyzed:** The paper does an excellent job of dissecting its own contributions. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the superiority of the bilinear integration over simple concatenation for the latent variable and clearly show the positive effect of the MI bonus on producing distinct skills. The honesty and transparency in discussing failure modes (e.g., the instability of the Ant robot when switching skills) in the appendix is commendable and strengthens the paper by clearly delineating the limitations and opportunities for future work.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While I am enthusiastic about this work, the following points could help strengthen the paper further:

*   **Scalability of MI Estimation:** The current method for estimating the posterior `p(z|c)` relies on discretizing the 2D coordinate space. This is a reasonable proof-of-concept, but it will not scale to tasks where the relevant state is higher-dimensional (e.g., full joint configurations, or visual inputs). While the paper briefly mentions fitting an MLP regressor as an alternative, expanding on this point—even just speculatively—would be valuable. Discussing the potential challenges and solutions for applying this MI bonus in more complex state spaces would be a great addition.
*   **Simplicity of the Hierarchy:** The manager policy's commitment to a skill for a fixed duration `T` is a major simplification compared to the classic options framework, which allows for state-dependent termination. The appendix shows this is not a critical limitation for the static tasks explored, but it is likely to be a bottleneck in more dynamic environments requiring reactive behavior. Acknowledging this more directly in the main text and framing it as a key simplification for the sake of studying the skill-learning component would be helpful.
*   **Analysis of Pre-training Efficiency:** The paper claims that the SNN approach is K times more sample-efficient for learning K skills. This is a very strong and intuitive claim. However, the paper would be even more convincing if it included a direct empirical comparison, for example, a plot showing the total samples/wall-clock time required to train 6 independent policies versus one 6-skill SNN to achieve a comparable level of performance on the pre-training task.

**Questions for Authors**

1.  How sensitive is the method to the MI bonus coefficient `αH`? Was this hyperparameter difficult to tune, and does its optimal value vary significantly across different robot morphologies (e.g., Swimmer vs. Snake)?
2.  The instability observed with the Ant robot when switching skills is a very interesting result. Have you considered alternative switching mechanisms to mitigate this? For example, could a learned "transition policy" be used to bridge two skills, or could the high-level policy's output be used to softly interpolate between skill policies rather than making a hard switch?
3.  Could the pre-training proxy reward be learned or adapted? In this work, it is simple and hand-designed (e.g., CoM velocity). Do you have thoughts on how this framework might be extended to scenarios where a good proxy reward is not as obvious?
