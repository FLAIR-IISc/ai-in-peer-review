### Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and well-structured framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) aimed at solving complex, sparse-reward tasks. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of low-level skills in an unsupervised manner within a simple environment. This is achieved using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as a multi-modal policy, where a latent code input determines the specific skill, or behavior, to be executed. To ensure that the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the authors introduce a key technical innovation: an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent code and the agent's state. This regularizer, inspired by InfoGAN, encourages the emergence of interpretable and diverse skills. Second, for downstream tasks, these pre-trained, frozen skills are used as a primitive action space for a high-level "manager" policy, which learns to sequence them to solve the sparse-reward problem. The paper provides strong empirical evidence on challenging continuous control benchmarks (e.g., Swimmer in various mazes), demonstrating that this approach significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency compared to standard RL and a strong baseline using the pre-training reward as intrinsic motivation.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Sound Methodology:** The paper's main technical contribution—the use of an SNN with an MI-based regularizer for unsupervised skill discovery—is both novel in the context of HRL and technically sound. It provides a principled and effective solution to the common problem of "mode collapse" in skill learning, forcing the policy to learn distinct and useful behaviors. The architectural choice of bilinear integration for the latent code is also well-motivated and shown to be effective.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough, well-designed, and provide clear support for the paper's claims. The use of visitation plots offers compelling qualitative evidence of improved skill diversity and exploration. The quantitative results on difficult sparse-reward tasks from a standard benchmark demonstrate a significant performance gain over relevant baselines. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of the key components (SNN vs. separate policies, bilinear integration, MI bonus).

#### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's approach is well-motivated by the persistent challenges of exploration in sparse-reward settings. It smartly connects ideas from HRL, unsupervised representation learning (specifically, InfoGAN), and multi-modal policy learning.

*   **Methodological Strengths:**
    *   **Unsupervised Pre-training:** The framework's ability to learn skills with only a simple, generic "proxy" reward (e.g., forward velocity) makes it widely applicable, as it avoids the need for hand-crafting task-specific rewards or collecting demonstrations. This is a significant advantage over many prior skill-learning methods.
    *   **Principled Diversity:** The MI regularizer `I(Z;C)` is the paper's standout feature. It provides a clear objective for learning diverse skills, moving beyond heuristic or less direct methods. The interpretation of maximizing this term as minimizing the uncertainty about the skill `Z` given the state `C` (`H(Z|C)`) is elegant and intuitive. The practical implementation via discretizing the state space and using batch statistics is a reasonable and effective approximation.
    *   **Efficient Skill Representation:** Using a single SNN to represent multiple skills enables weight sharing and is shown to be far more sample-efficient than training K separate policies. The ablation study in Figure 4 provides a powerful visualization of this, showing that the SNN can learn a richer set of skills with the same number of samples as a single standard policy.

*   **Empirical Rigor:**
    *   **Challenging Benchmarks:** The choice of tasks from the Duan et al. (2016) benchmark, which are known to be difficult for naive exploration strategies, effectively showcases the method's capabilities.
    *   **Strong Baselines:** The authors compare not only against standard RL but also against a strong baseline where the proxy reward is used directly as an intrinsic reward in the downstream task. The fact that their hierarchical method outperforms this baseline demonstrates that it is the structured, temporally-extended exploration provided by the skills, not just the reward signal itself, that is crucial for success.
    *   **Insightful Analysis:** The paper includes valuable analysis in the appendix regarding the choice of switch time `T` and the impact of different pre-trained SNNs on downstream performance. This transparency and honest evaluation of failure modes (e.g., for the unstable Ant agent) strengthens the paper's scientific contribution.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could further improve its clarity and impact. These are suggestions for refinement and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Scalability of MI Estimation:** The MI bonus is estimated by discretizing the 2D (x, y) coordinate space. This is effective for the locomotion tasks presented, but its scalability to higher-dimensional state representations is a key question. The paper briefly mentions using an MLP regressor for the posterior `p(z|s)`. It would be beneficial to elaborate on this in the main text: What is the architecture? How is it trained (e.g., jointly with the policy, or from batch data)? What are the potential stability challenges?
*   **The Role of the Proxy Reward:** The design of the proxy reward is critical for learning a useful set of skills. The paper argues it requires "minimal domain knowledge," which is true for the locomotion tasks (rewarding speed). However, a more detailed discussion on the principles for designing good proxy rewards for other domains (e.g., manipulation, where rewarding contact or object movement might be better) would strengthen the paper's claim of generality.
*   **Fixed vs. Learned Termination:** The high-level manager commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The appendix shows this is not overly sensitive, but for more dynamic tasks, this could be a limitation. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the connection to the options framework, where termination conditions can be learned, and frame this as a clear avenue for future work in the main discussion section.

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  Your MI bonus focuses on a low-dimensional subset of the state (the CoM coordinates). Have you experimented with defining the MI bonus over other state variables, such as joint velocities or the agent's orientation? How does the choice of state variables for the MI objective affect the nature of the skills that are learned?
2.  The low-level skills are frozen during downstream task training. Have you explored fine-tuning the SNN while training the manager? Could this allow for specialization of skills to the new task, or does it risk catastrophic forgetting and loss of exploratory capability?
3.  The experiments show high variance in downstream performance depending on the specific pre-trained SNN used (Appendix C.4). This suggests that the pre-training is not always guaranteed to find the "critical" skills needed for a specific downstream task. Do you have any thoughts on how to make the pre-training phase more robust or how one might select or combine different sets of pre-trained skills for a new task?
