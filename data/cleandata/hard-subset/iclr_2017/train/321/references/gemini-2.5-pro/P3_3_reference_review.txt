### **ICLR Review**

**Paper Title:** STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

---

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to solve a collection of challenging, sparse-reward tasks. The core idea is a two-stage process: first, an unsupervised or self-supervised pre-training phase to learn a diverse set of reusable skills, followed by a second phase where a high-level "manager" policy is trained to select among these skills to solve specific downstream tasks.

The key contributions are threefold:
1.  **A General Framework for Skill Discovery:** The paper proposes learning skills using a simple, task-agnostic proxy reward (e.g., rewarding movement speed) that requires minimal domain knowledge. This avoids the need for explicit goal-conditioning or reward shaping during pre-training.
2.  **Sample-Efficient Skill Learning with SNNs:** To learn multiple skills efficiently, the authors employ a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) where a latent categorical variable conditions the policy's behavior. This allows for significant weight sharing between skills, making the pre-training process K-times more sample-efficient than training K separate policies. The paper also astutely identifies that a bilinear integration of the latent variable and state representation is superior to simple concatenation.
3.  **Encouraging Skill Diversity with Mutual Information:** To prevent the SNN from collapsing to a single mode or learning redundant skills, an information-theoretic regularizer is introduced. By rewarding the mutual information (MI) between the latent skill variable and the agent's state, the framework explicitly encourages the emergence of distinct and interpretable behaviors.

The authors demonstrate the efficacy of this framework on a set of challenging continuous control benchmarks (locomotion in mazes and a gathering task), showing that the learned hierarchy significantly improves exploration and enables solving tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

---

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Elegant and Effective Solution to a Major Problem:** The paper tackles the critical and long-standing challenges of exploration and skill acquisition in sparse-reward RL. The proposed solution is elegant, combining principled ideas from HRL, generative models (latent-variable conditioning), and information theory (the InfoGAN-style regularizer) into a cohesive and practical framework. It strikes an excellent balance between generality and performance.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by thorough and well-designed experiments. The visualizations of learned skills (Fig. 4) and exploration patterns (Fig. 5) provide clear, intuitive evidence for the method's effectiveness. The quantitative results on the downstream tasks (Fig. 6) demonstrate a substantial performance improvement over a strong and relevant baseline, confirming that the learned skills are indeed useful for solving complex problems. The scientific rigor, including the use of multiple seeds and analysis of failure modes, is commendable.

---

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strength lies in its clear motivation, sound methodology, and rigorous evaluation.

**Methodology:** The two-stage training paradigm is well-motivated. Decoupling skill discovery from task-specific learning is a powerful abstraction that addresses the exploration problem head-on. The choice of an SNN is a clever way to achieve sample-efficient learning of a multi-modal policy. The insight that bilinear integration is necessary for the latent code to meaningfully modulate the policy's behavior is a valuable contribution. The most novel component, the MI bonus, provides a principled mechanism to enforce skill diversity, moving beyond heuristic or architectural priors. The implementation detail of treating the latent code `z` as part of the observation to apply standard TRPO is a simple and effective trick that makes the method easy to adopt.

**Experimental Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of dissecting their method and justifying each component.
*   **Skill Quality (Sec 7.1):** The visitation plots in Figure 4 are highly effective. They convincingly show that (a) training independent policies is inefficient, (b) the SNN with bilinear integration learns a much richer set of skills than one with simple concatenation, and (c) the MI bonus further refines and separates these skills. This provides strong support for the core methodological choices.
*   **Exploration (Sec 7.2):** Figure 5 provides a dramatic visualization of the exploration benefit. Contrasting the tiny area covered by naive noise with the broad coverage of the hierarchical policy clearly demonstrates that the learned skills act as powerful temporal abstractions for exploration.
*   **Downstream Performance (Sec 7.3):** The learning curves in Figure 6 show that the enhanced exploration translates directly into the ability to solve sparse-reward tasks. The SNN-based hierarchies consistently outperform or match the more sample-intensive "Multi-policy" baseline, validating the SNN approach. The comparison against adding the proxy reward directly to the downstream task is a strong baseline, and outperforming it highlights the value of the hierarchical structure itself.

The paper is also well-situated within the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from prior HRL, intrinsic motivation, and concurrent work. The honest discussion of limitations, such as the instability of switching skills for the Ant agent and the fixed sub-policy commitment time, adds to the paper's credibility.

---

#### **4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Scalability of the MI Estimator:** The MI bonus is currently calculated by discretizing the 2D (x, y) state space into a grid. This approach is simple and effective for the tasks presented, but it does not scale well to higher-dimensional state spaces where such discretization would be intractable. The paper briefly mentions that an MLP regressor could be used to estimate the posterior `p(z|s)`. It would strengthen the paper to elaborate on this point. Have the authors experimented with this, and do they foresee any optimization challenges (e.g., stability) in simultaneously training the policy and the posterior-estimating network?
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameter T:** The high-level policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. This is a significant hyperparameter. While Appendix C.3 provides a good analysis of `T` for the Snake robot, showing mild sensitivity, this is an important point. A brief mention of this analysis in the main body (perhaps in Section 7.3 or 8) would be beneficial, as it preempts a common question about fixed-length temporal abstraction.
*   **Direct Comparison to Intrinsic Motivation Methods:** The paper compares against a baseline that incorporates the pre-training proxy reward into the downstream task. This is a good sanity check. However, in Appendix B, the authors state their method outperforms VIME. A learning curve comparing directly against VIME (or another state-of-the-art exploration method) in the main paper's plots would make the empirical claims even stronger, especially on the Gather task where intrinsic motivation is known to perform well.
*   **End-to-End Fine-tuning:** The authors correctly identify that fine-tuning the low-level skills for the downstream task is a promising direction for future work. Given the success with frozen skills, it would be interesting to know if the authors have preliminary thoughts on the trade-offs. For example, could fine-tuning lead to catastrophic forgetting of general skills, making them less transferable to a *third* task?

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. It is well-written, methodologically sound, and empirically convincing.
