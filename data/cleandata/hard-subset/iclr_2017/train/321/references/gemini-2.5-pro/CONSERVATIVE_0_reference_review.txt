### Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The approach consists of two stages. First, a diverse set of low-level skills is learned in a "pre-training" environment using a generic proxy reward (e.g., rewarding movement speed). This skill acquisition is performed using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), where different skills correspond to different settings of a categorical latent input variable. To encourage the learned skills to be distinct, the authors introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent variable and the agent's position. Second, for each downstream task, a high-level "manager" policy is trained on top of the frozen, pre-trained skills to solve the sparse-reward problem. The manager selects which skill to execute for a fixed duration. The authors evaluate their method on challenging continuous control benchmarks (mazes and a collection task), demonstrating qualitatively that the SNN can learn an interpretable and diverse set of locomotion skills and quantitatively that the hierarchical architecture outperforms a non-hierarchical baseline.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **The core technical contribution for encouraging skill diversity—the mutual information (MI) regularizer—relies on strong, domain-specific assumptions that limit its general applicability.** The implementation requires hand-selecting a low-dimensional, relevant subset of the state space (the 2D center of mass) and applying a simple discretization, which is not a general-purpose solution.
2.  **The experimental results demonstrate a significant lack of robustness in the skill discovery phase.** The paper's own analysis shows that the success of the hierarchical agent is highly contingent on the quality of the pre-trained SNN, which can fail to learn skills that are critical for downstream tasks, resulting in high variance and complete failure on some runs. This brittleness undermines the reliability of the overall framework.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The ambition to automate skill discovery for hierarchical RL is commendable, and the use of SNNs for multi-modal policy learning is a sound and efficient approach. However, the proposed method suffers from critical limitations in its current form.

My primary concern lies with the methodological soundness and generality of the MI regularizer. The regularizer is defined as `I(Z; C)`, where `Z` is the latent skill and `C` is the agent's center of mass (CoM). This formulation is convenient for the locomotion tasks presented, but it constitutes a strong, hand-engineered prior. The method's success hinges on the assumption that distinct skills should lead to distinct 2D locations. This may not hold for other domains; for example, in manipulation, multiple distinct skills might be executed in the same location. The paper suggests that for higher-dimensional spaces, a separate neural network could be trained to estimate the posterior `p(Z|S)`, but this is presented without any empirical validation and would introduce significant additional complexity and potential instability. As presented, the MI bonus feels less like a general principle for diverse skill discovery and more like a task-specific heuristic for encouraging spatial coverage.

My second major concern is the demonstrated fragility of the unsupervised pre-training phase. While the visitation plots in Figure 4 are qualitatively appealing, the results in Appendix C.4 are worrying. It is shown that a pre-trained SNN for the Snake agent fails entirely on Maze 0 because it did not acquire a necessary "backward" motion skill. The authors state that the high variance in results for the hierarchical methods is due to this phenomenon. A framework for skill acquisition is only useful if it is reliable. If the pre-training requires multiple attempts with different random seeds in the hopes of generating a useful skill set, its practical utility is severely diminished. This suggests that the proposed objective (proxy reward + MI bonus) is not sufficient to guarantee the emergence of a comprehensive skill repertoire, which is the foundational promise of the approach.

Furthermore, while the baselines are sensible, the main comparison against a state-of-the-art exploration method (VIME) is relegated to the appendix. For a paper tackling sparse rewards and exploration, this comparison should be central. The baseline of simply adding the proxy reward to the downstream task is a useful ablation, but it is not a competitive alternative from the literature. The primary baseline presented is the "Multi-policy" approach, which is an ablation of the SNN architecture rather than a competing method. This makes it difficult to situate the performance of the proposed framework within the broader context of existing solutions for sparse-reward problems.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The idea of using SNNs with bilinear integration to learn a skill basis is strong and well-motivated. The qualitative results showing the improved diversity over simple concatenation are a valuable contribution.
*   The paper is well-written, and the hierarchical structure is clearly explained. The honest reporting of failure modes (e.g., for the Ant robot and the unlucky pre-training seed) is appreciated.
*   The fixed-time commitment `T` for skill execution is a standard but rigid choice. While acknowledged as a limitation, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough analysis of its sensitivity in the main text, as it is a critical hyperparameter in any hierarchical system based on time-extended actions.
*   The framework could be made more convincing by either (1) demonstrating the MI regularizer in a domain where the relevant state features are not as obvious as 2D position, or (2) proposing and validating a more robust pre-training objective that reduces the variance in downstream performance by more reliably producing a comprehensive set of skills.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on how the MI regularizer could be practically implemented for tasks where a simple, low-dimensional projection of the state is not sufficient? For example, in a dexterous manipulation task, what would be the analogue of the "CoM coordinates"? Have you performed any experiments with the proposed MLP-based posterior estimator?
2.  Given the observed unreliability of the pre-training phase, do you have concrete ideas for how to modify the unsupervised objective to more robustly generate a comprehensive set of skills? Is it possible that the proxy reward itself is insufficient, and that a more structured objective is needed to ensure all critical behaviors are discovered?
3.  Why was the comparison to VIME, a key method from the literature on intrinsic motivation for sparse-reward tasks, not featured in the main results section? How does your method compare in terms of both final performance and overall sample efficiency (including pre-training) on the more challenging versions of the tasks?
