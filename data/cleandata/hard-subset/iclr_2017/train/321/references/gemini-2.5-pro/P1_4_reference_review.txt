### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a general framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to solve a collection of challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-phase approach: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned in an unsupervised manner, guided only by a simple, generic proxy reward. Second, a downstream training phase where a high-level "manager" policy learns to compose these pre-trained skills to solve specific tasks.

The key contributions lie in the pre-training phase. To learn multiple skills efficiently within a single model, the authors employ a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) where a discrete latent variable conditions the policy's behavior. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the authors introduce two crucial components: (1) a bilinear integration module to combine the state observation and the latent variable, which they show is more expressive than simple concatenation, and (2) an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and the agent's state. This MI bonus, inspired by InfoGAN, explicitly encourages the behaviors corresponding to different latent codes to be distinguishable, preventing modal collapse and promoting skill diversity. The paper provides strong empirical evidence on continuous control benchmarks, demonstrating that this framework learns interpretable skills, significantly enhances exploration, and successfully solves complex maze navigation and object gathering tasks that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Effective and Well-Motivated Method for Skill Discovery:** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated solution to the crucial problem of unsupervised skill discovery in RL. The combination of SNNs for multi-modal policy representation and an information-theoretic objective for encouraging behavioral diversity is elegant and principled. This approach effectively translates ideas from unsupervised representation learning into the domain of behavior learning.

2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Validation:** The claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors not only demonstrate superior performance on challenging sparse-reward tasks but also provide insightful ablation studies and visualizations. The "visitation plots" clearly illustrate the quality and diversity of the learned skills and the direct impact of the proposed technical contributions (bilinear integration and MI bonus). The comparison against relevant baselines, including a multi-policy approach, effectively highlights the sample efficiency and performance benefits of the proposed method.

### Supporting Arguments

The problem of efficient exploration in sparse-reward environments remains a major obstacle in deep RL. This paper's approach of decoupling skill acquisition from task-specific learning is a powerful and scalable paradigm. By learning a reusable library of skills in a simple, unsupervised pre-training stage, the framework reduces the complexity of downstream tasks to a higher-level decision-making problem over temporally extended actions, thus mitigating the challenges of long horizons and sparse rewards.

The empirical results are convincing. Figure 4 provides a clear and powerful ablation study on the importance of the architectural choices. It shows that simple concatenation of the latent variable is insufficient, while the proposed bilinear integration consistently produces more distinct skills. Furthermore, the addition of the MI bonus demonstrably improves the diversity and separation of these skills. Figure 5 effectively visualizes the massive improvement in exploration coverage of the hierarchical policy over naive exploration, which is the foundational reason for its success on the downstream tasks.

Finally, the performance on the Maze and Gather tasks in Figure 6 shows that this enhanced exploration translates directly into solving tasks that are otherwise unsolvable. The outperformance over the strong baseline of directly using the proxy reward in the downstream task highlights the benefit of the hierarchical abstraction. The comparison with the "Multi-policy" architecture, which requires training K separate policies, underscores the sample efficiency gains of learning a shared representation through the SNN. The paper is well-written, the method is clearly explained, and the work is well-positioned within the existing literature on HRL and intrinsic motivation.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further:

*   **Scalability of the MI Bonus:** The MI bonus is calculated by discretizing the (x, y) coordinate space. This approach is simple and effective here, but its scalability to higher-dimensional state spaces is a concern. The paper briefly mentions that an MLP regressor could be used instead. A short discussion on the potential challenges and benefits of such an approach would strengthen the paper by addressing this potential limitation.
*   **Fixed Skill Duration `T`:** The high-level policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The appendix provides a good analysis showing this is not a critical parameter for the tasks studied. However, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss this limitation in the main body (e.g., in the Discussion section) and contrast it with methods that learn termination conditions, such as the option-critic framework, as a direction for future work.
*   **Interpreting Mixed Results of MI Bonus:** In Section 7.3, the authors note that the MI bonus, while improving skill diversity, does not always boost final performance on the maze tasks. This is an interesting result. A brief speculative discussion on why this might occur would be valuable. For example, perhaps the mazes used only require a few simple skills (e.g., forward, turn-by-bouncing-off-wall), and the additional, more nuanced skills learned via the MI bonus add unnecessary complexity for the manager to learn from.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The subset of the state used for the MI calculation (the CoM coordinates) seems crucial. How was this choice made, and how sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to this selection? For instance, for the Swimmer agent, would including its orientation in the state representation `C` lead to skills that are more oriented toward specific directions rather than just regions of space?
2.  The results show high variance in downstream performance depending on the specific pre-trained SNN used, particularly for the maze tasks. This suggests that while the pre-training process is effective, it doesn't guarantee the discovery of a "complete" set of skills necessary for all potential downstream tasks. Do you have thoughts on how the pre-training objective could be modified to encourage even greater "coverage" of the behavior space, perhaps to more robustly generate all necessary primitive movements?
3.  The paper leaves end-to-end fine-tuning of the low-level SNN policy as future work. Have you performed any preliminary experiments in this direction? Intuitively, it seems this could allow the generic skills to adapt to task-specific dynamics and potentially improve asymptotic performance, though it might complicate the training process. Any insights on this trade-off would be appreciated.
