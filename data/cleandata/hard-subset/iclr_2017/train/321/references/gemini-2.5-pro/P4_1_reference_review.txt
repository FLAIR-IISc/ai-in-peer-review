### **Summary**

This paper proposes a general, two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to solve a collection of challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to first learn a diverse set of reusable skills in an unsupervised pre-training phase and then use these skills as primitives for a high-level policy to solve downstream tasks.

In the first stage, a policy is trained in a simple environment with a generic proxy reward that requires minimal domain knowledge (e.g., rewarding the agent for its forward speed). To learn multiple skills efficiently within a single model, the authors employ a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) where the policy is conditioned on a discrete latent variable. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the paper introduces two key technical contributions: (1) a bilinear integration method to combine the state observation and the latent variable, which is shown to be more expressive than simple concatenation, and (2) an information-theoretic regularizer based on maximizing the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and the agent's state, encouraging each latent code to correspond to a distinct and predictable behavior.

In the second stage, the learned low-level SNN policy is frozen and used as a set of macro-actions. For each new downstream task, a high-level "manager" policy is trained on top of these skills. This manager observes the full state and selects a skill (by choosing a latent code) to execute for a fixed number of timesteps. The paper demonstrates empirically on challenging continuous control benchmarks, including maze navigation and object gathering, that this hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and leads to much faster learning on sparse-reward tasks compared to non-hierarchical baselines.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound framework for unsupervised skill discovery that effectively addresses the critical challenge of exploration in sparse-reward reinforcement learning. The proposed components, particularly the use of SNNs with bilinear integration and an information-theoretic regularizer, are well-justified and elegantly combined.
2.  The empirical evaluation is thorough, convincing, and scientifically rigorous. The experiments clearly demonstrate the benefits of each component of the proposed method through insightful visualizations and ablation studies. The framework achieves strong performance on established, difficult benchmark tasks, substantially outperforming relevant baselines.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement is clear, the methodology is described logically with helpful architectural diagrams, and the motivation for each design choice is well-articulated. The use of "visitation plots" (Figure 4 and 5) is an excellent way to provide qualitative, intuitive evidence for the diversity of learned skills and the improvement in exploration, which strongly supports the paper's central claims.

**Technical Soundness and Novelty:** While the paper builds on existing concepts in HRL, unsupervised learning, and information theory, its synthesis is novel and effective. The main technical contributions are the specific application and analysis of SNN architectures for skill learning in RL. The ablation study in Section 7.1 convincingly demonstrates that the combination of bilinear integration and the MI bonus is crucial for learning a diverse and useful set of skills, a significant finding for practitioners. The MI bonus, inspired by InfoGAN, is adapted thoughtfully to the RL context by framing it as a reward bonus based on an empirical estimate of the posterior over latent codes given the state. The overall two-stage training procedure is simple, general, and appears straightforward to implement.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental design is strong.
*   **Baselines:** The comparison is not against weak baselines. The paper compares its hierarchical methods against a flat policy that is given the same proxy reward as reward shaping, which is a very relevant comparison point. It also compares the sample-efficient SNN approach to a "Multi-policy" baseline where skills are learned independently, directly showing the benefits of weight-sharing.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper systematically evaluates the impact of the latent variable integration method (concatenation vs. bilinear) and the MI bonus, clearly isolating the contribution of each component.
*   **Reproducibility and Transparency:** The authors provide code, videos, and sufficient details on hyperparameters. The analysis in the appendix on more complex agents (Snake, Ant) and failure modes (for the Ant) demonstrates a high degree of scientific transparency and provides valuable insights into the limitations of the current approach. The reporting of results over multiple pre-training seeds and multiple evaluation seeds adds confidence in the conclusions.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Comparison to Option-Critic:** The paper briefly mentions the Option-Critic architecture. A more detailed comparison in the related work section could strengthen the paper. For instance, you could contrast your pre-training approach with the end-to-end nature of option-critic, discussing the potential trade-offs (e.g., sample efficiency in pre-training vs. task-specific option adaptation).
*   **MI Bonus Estimation:** The MI bonus relies on discretizing a portion of the state space (the CoM coordinates). While this works well for the 2D navigation tasks, it might be challenging to scale to higher-dimensional state representations. The paper mentions using an MLP regressor as an alternative. A brief discussion on the potential sensitivity to the discretization resolution or the challenges and variance associated with training a neural posterior estimator `p(z|c)` would be a valuable addition.
*   **Fixed Time Horizon for Skills:** The manager commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The appendix provides a helpful analysis showing this is not a critical parameter for the static tasks studied. However, mentioning how a learned termination policy could be integrated into the framework (perhaps by augmenting the manager's output) would provide a more complete picture for future work, especially for tasks requiring more reactive behavior.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Answering these questions could help clarify some aspects of the work and further strengthen the paper.

1.  The MI bonus is calculated using the (x, y) coordinates of the agent's Center of Mass. Could you elaborate on the process for selecting this subset of the state? Have you explored how sensitive the quality and diversity of the learned skills are to this choice? For example, would including velocity or orientation in the state representation `C` used for the MI calculation lead to learning different kinds of skills?
2.  The high-level manager policy receives the full state `(S_agent, S_rest)` as input. Did you experiment with providing only the task-specific state `S_rest` to the manager? One might hypothesize that the low-level policy is responsible for handling the agent-centric part of the state, so the manager's decision could primarily depend on the external task context.
3.  In Section 7.3, you note that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" on the maze tasks. Could you offer more intuition as to why this might be the case? Is it possible that the MI bonus encourages the discovery of skills (e.g., precise turning motions) that are less critical for the specific maze layouts tested compared to the simpler forward/backward skills that can be learned without the bonus and are sufficient to solve the task (e.g., by bumping against walls to reorient)?
