### **Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) aimed at solving tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution is a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned with minimal supervision, using only a generic proxy reward. This is accomplished by parameterizing the low-level policy as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), where a latent categorical variable conditions the policy's behavior. To encourage the emergence of distinct skills corresponding to each latent code, the authors introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent code and the agent's state. The paper also investigates architectural choices for integrating the latent variable, finding bilinear integration superior to simple concatenation. In the second stage, these pre-trained, frozen skills are used as a macro-action space for a high-level "manager" policy, which is then trained on specific downstream tasks. The method is evaluated on a set of challenging locomotion and navigation tasks, demonstrating improved sample efficiency and exploration compared to non-hierarchical baselines.

**2. Decision: Reject**

While the paper addresses the important problem of skill discovery for HRL and presents a promising direction, I am recommending rejection due to two primary reasons:
1.  Several key methodological components are heuristic in nature and lack rigorous justification or analysis, most notably the implementation of the mutual information regularizer.
2.  The paper suffers from a significant number of presentation flaws, including formatting errors, inconsistent terminology, and typographical mistakes, which collectively detract from the clarity and professionalism of the work and suggest a lack of meticulous preparation.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

**Methodological Weaknesses:**

*   **Mutual Information (MI) Calculation:** The proposed method for estimating the posterior `p(Z|C)` for the MI bonus is a major concern. The state space is discretized into a grid, and posteriors are estimated via visitation counts (Eq. 2). This approach is highly problematic. Firstly, it does not scale beyond two dimensions, which the authors acknowledge by briefly mentioning an MLP regressor could be used, but this alternative is neither implemented nor analyzed. Secondly, the performance is critically dependent on the granularity of the grid (a hyperparameter detailed as "10 divisions/unit" in Appendix A), but no sensitivity analysis is provided. This crude discretization feels like an ad-hoc solution rather than a principled one.
*   **Arbitrary Design Choices:** The framework relies on several crucial but poorly justified hyperparameters. The number of skills `K` is fixed to 6 without justification or ablation. The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`, a known limitation of many HRL approaches. While Appendix C.3 provides a brief analysis of `T`, the main text presents it as a fixed design choice (`T=500`), which is misleading. A more robust approach would involve learning a termination condition.
*   **Justification of SNN Architecture:** The paper claims bilinear integration is superior to concatenation based on empirical results. While this is a useful finding, the motivation is somewhat superficial. The claim that concatenation "corresponds to changing the bias term" while bilinear integration changes "all the first hidden layer weights" is an oversimplification. A deeper analysis or theoretical justification for why this specific form of multiplicative interaction is crucial for skill separation would strengthen the paper.

**Experimental Rigor and Claims:**

*   **Qualitative Evaluation of Skills:** The primary evidence for the diversity of learned skills comes from visual inspection of "visitation plots" (Fig. 4). While illustrative, this is a purely qualitative assessment. The claims that the MI bonus yields "less overlapped skills" and "new advancing/turning skills" are not supported by any quantitative metric of skill diversity or coverage. A more rigorous evaluation would involve metrics like pairwise trajectory divergence or state-space coverage.
*   **High Variance and Reliability:** The authors commendably report high variance in their results and correctly attribute it to the stochastic nature of the pre-training (Appendix C.4). However, this points to a significant reliability issue. If the pre-training phase sometimes fails to generate a skill set capable of solving the downstream task (as shown for seed 50 in Fig. 12(a)), the practical utility of the entire framework is questionable. The method should reliably produce a useful span of skills.
*   **Limited Scope of Success:** The failure modes reported for the "Ant" robot in Appendix D are concerning and should be discussed more prominently in the main paper. The instability upon switching skills suggests the framework may not be generally applicable to more complex or dynamically unstable agents, a significant limitation that is currently relegated to the appendix.

**Presentation and Formatting:**

The paper is marred by a distracting number of minor errors that collectively indicate a lack of careful preparation.
*   **Citations:** The reference list is improperly formatted, with BibTeX artifacts throughout (e.g., `Bacon and Precup.,? \Q2016\E, Bacon and Precup.,`). This is unacceptable for a formal submission.
*   **Footnotes:** Footnotes are used inconsistently. Footnote 3 is a complete sentence that should be in the main text. Footnotes 4 and 5 in Section 7 are redundant repetitions of footnotes 1 and 2.
*   **Terminology:** The terms "pre-training" and "pretrain" are used interchangeably, sometimes within the same paragraph. "uni-modal gaussian" should be "unimodal Gaussian".
*   **Typos and Grammar:** There are numerous small errors, such as "can results in" (Section 1), "expressiveness power" (Section 5.2), "coeficient" (Section 7.1), and a missing space after the footnote marker in the first paragraph.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   I strongly encourage the authors to develop a more principled and scalable approach for the MI bonus calculation. Using neural estimators for mutual information (e.g., MINE) would be a significant step up from the current discretization scheme.
*   The evaluation of skill diversity needs to be quantitative. Consider metrics based on the final state distribution for each skill, or the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the state visitation distributions of different skills.
*   The paper would benefit greatly from an ablation study on key hyperparameters, particularly the number of skills `K` and the MI bonus coefficient `α_H`.
*   The problem of catastrophic state changes when switching skills for unstable agents is a critical challenge. The authors should consider potential solutions, such as learning a "transition policy" between skills or incorporating skill-switching directly into the pre-training phase, and discuss these limitations more centrally.
*   The paper requires a thorough proofreading pass to correct the multitude of formatting, grammatical, and typographical errors. The current state of the manuscript is not up to the standards of a top-tier conference.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  How was the grid density for the MI bonus calculation (10 divisions/unit) selected? How sensitive are the resulting skills to this parameter?
2.  The high variance in maze performance is attributed to some pre-trained SNNs lacking a critical skill (e.g., backward motion). Does this not suggest a fundamental flaw in a pre-training method that is meant to discover a *diverse* and *useful* set of skills? Is there a way to make the skill discovery process more robust?
3.  Could you elaborate on how the MLP-based posterior estimation for the MI bonus would be implemented? Specifically, how would you collect the training data `(c, z)` pairs without introducing bias, and would this approach be sample-efficient enough for online policy updates?
