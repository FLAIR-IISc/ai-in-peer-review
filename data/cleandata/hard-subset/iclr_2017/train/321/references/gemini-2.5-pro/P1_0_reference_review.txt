### Summary

The paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework designed to solve a collection of challenging continuous control tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is a two-stage process. First, in a "pre-training" phase, a diverse set of skills is learned in a simple environment with a generic, dense "proxy reward" (e.g., rewarding forward speed). This skill learning is achieved using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), where different skills correspond to different settings of a latent categorical variable fed as input to the policy. To encourage the learned skills to be distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the authors introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on maximizing the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and the agent's state. Second, for downstream tasks, the pre-trained SNN is frozen and used as a set of low-level primitives. A new high-level "manager" policy is then trained on top, which selects a skill (a latent code) to execute for a fixed period. The paper demonstrates empirically that this approach enables agents to explore much more effectively than standard methods and solve difficult tasks like navigating mazes and collecting food, which are intractable for conventional RL algorithms.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Effective and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper tackles the critical and challenging problem of exploration in sparse-reward environments. The proposed two-stage framework is an elegant and practical solution. The use of SNNs for sample-efficient multi-skill learning and the novel application of an InfoGAN-style mutual information bonus to ensure skill diversity are significant contributions. The method requires minimal domain knowledge (only a simple proxy reward), making it widely applicable.

2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by extensive experiments on established, difficult benchmarks. The results clearly show that the hierarchical approach drastically improves exploration and enables solving tasks that are otherwise impossible. The paper includes excellent ablation studies and qualitative analyses that convincingly demonstrate the benefits of each component of the proposed method (e.g., bilinear integration over concatenation, the positive effect of the MI bonus). The comparison against a strong "Multi-policy" baseline, which uses 6x the pre-training samples, effectively highlights the sample efficiency of the SNN approach.

### Supporting Arguments

The motivation for the work is clear: standard RL fails on sparse-reward tasks due to inefficient exploration, while existing HRL methods often require manual skill design. This paper sits at the intersection of unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical control, providing a compelling, automated solution.

The technical contributions are sound. The idea to use a latent-variable-conditioned policy (SNN) to learn a family of skills within a single network is not entirely new, but its application in this pre-training/fine-tuning HRL framework is novel. The key innovation is the MI regularizer, `I(Z;C)`. It provides a principled way to force the skills (indexed by `Z`) to produce distinguishable outcomes (in terms of state `C`), solving the common problem of "mode collapse" where the network learns redundant skills. The practical implementation of the MI bonus via state-space discretization and visitation counts is a reasonable and effective approximation.

The experimental section is a major strength.
*   **Skill Visualization (Fig. 4):** The visitation plots provide clear, intuitive evidence that the SNN with bilinear integration and the MI bonus learns a richer and more diverse set of skills compared to training separate policies or using a simpler SNN architecture. This directly supports the core claims about the skill-learning phase.
*   **Exploration Analysis (Fig. 5):** The 1-million-step rollout plots are a powerful visualization of the exploration benefit. They show that the hierarchical policy with learned skills explores a vastly larger region of the state space than naive exploration, providing a clear mechanism for why the method succeeds on downstream tasks.
*   **Downstream Task Performance (Fig. 6):** The learning curves on the maze and gather tasks demonstrate significant performance gains over a relevant baseline (providing the proxy reward directly). The fact that the SNN-based methods perform competitively with or better than the much more sample-intensive Multi-policy baseline is a strong result. The honest reporting of high variance and its attribution to the quality of the pre-trained skills is good scientific practice.
*   **Appendices:** The additional results on more complex agents (Snake) show the method scales, while the analysis of failure modes on an unstable agent (Ant) provides valuable insight into the method's limitations, strengthening the paper's overall contribution.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could be considered for further improvement:

1.  **MI Bonus Estimation:** The current method of estimating the posterior `p(Z|C)` by discretizing the state space into a grid is simple and effective for the 2D case presented. However, this approach does not scale to higher-dimensional state spaces. The authors briefly mention using an MLP regressor as an alternative. It would strengthen the paper to elaborate on this or other scalable approaches (e.g., using a discriminator network as in InfoGAN or variational methods like MINE) in the main text, as this is a key practical limitation of the current implementation.

2.  **Fixed Time Horizon `T` for Skills:** The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation and suggest learning a termination policy as future work. While the analysis in Appendix C.3 shows performance is not overly sensitive to `T` for these static tasks, it remains a significant constraint for more dynamic environments. A brief discussion in the main text about the implications of this choice would be beneficial.

3.  **Choice of `K` and `αH`:** The number of skills `K` and the MI bonus coefficient `αH` are important hyperparameters. The paper does not discuss how these were chosen or the sensitivity of the method to them. A short discussion on this would add practical value.

4.  **Clarity on "Multi-policy" Baseline:** The "Multi-policy" baseline is very important for demonstrating sample efficiency. It is described as training six independent policies. For clarity, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the total number of environment samples used for pre-training this baseline is 6 times that of the SNN pre-training.

### Questions for Authors

1.  Regarding the MI bonus, how sensitive were the results to the discretization density of the (x, y) grid? And for the MI bonus coefficient `αH`, what was the process for selecting its value (e.g., 0.01)? Is there an intuitive explanation for what happens when `αH` is too large?

2.  The instability observed with the Ant robot when switching skills is a very interesting failure mode. You attribute this to the state-space distribution shift. Could this also be related to discontinuities in dynamics? For example, abruptly changing motor torques from a "forward" gait to a "backward" gait might be dynamically unstable regardless of the controller. Does your framework offer any mechanisms to encourage smoother transitions between skills?

3.  In Section 7.3, you mention that for the maze tasks, some pre-trained SNNs may lack a specific motion needed to solve the task, leading to a reward of 0 and high variance in the results. Does this suggest a need for a more robust pre-training objective that guarantees a certain minimal coverage of behaviors (e.g., ability to move in all cardinal directions)?
