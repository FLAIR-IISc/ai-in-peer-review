### **Review Summary**

This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is a two-phase approach: first, a pre-training phase where a diverse set of skills is learned in a simple environment using a generic, task-agnostic "proxy reward". Second, a downstream training phase where a high-level "manager" policy is trained to select from these pre-trained, frozen skills to solve a specific sparse-reward task. The key contributions are: (1) the use of Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a latent variable to efficiently learn a repertoire of skills within a single model; (2) a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information (MI) to encourage diversity among the learned skills and prevent mode collapse; and (3) a demonstration of this framework on challenging continuous control benchmarks, showing significant improvements in exploration and final performance over non-hierarchical baselines.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Framework:** The paper presents a clean, well-motivated, and practical framework for skill discovery and reuse in RL. The decoupling of skill learning (via a simple proxy reward) from task-specific learning is an elegant solution to the exploration problem in sparse-reward settings, promoting skill reusability across a collection of tasks.
2.  **Strong Technical Contributions and Empirical Support:** The use of SNNs with bilinear integration and an MI-based regularizer for learning diverse skills is a significant contribution. The claims are strongly supported by a series of well-designed experiments and clear visualizations. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of both the bilinear architecture and the MI bonus for generating a wide and interpretable span of skills.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, the elegance of its proposed solution, and the thoroughness of its empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**
*   **Motivation and Positioning:** The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself within the literature on HRL and intrinsic motivation. It correctly identifies the shortcomings of existing approaches (hand-engineering in classical HRL, lack of transfer in some intrinsic motivation methods) and proposes a solution that combines the strengths of both.
*   **Methodology:**
    *   The idea of using a simple, generic proxy reward (e.g., speed) for pre-training is powerful and practical, requiring minimal domain knowledge.
    *   Using an SNN to represent multiple skills is far more sample-efficient than training separate policies, as it allows for significant weight sharing. The paper's investigation into how the latent variable is integrated (concatenation vs. bilinear) is insightful, with the bilinear integration proving much more expressive.
    *   The information-theoretic regularizer, I(Z;C), is a principled and effective method to ensure that the latent codes `z` correspond to distinct, distinguishable behaviors. This is a clever adaptation of ideas from representation learning (like InfoGAN) to the RL context.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are convincing and well-structured.
    *   The "visitation plots" in Figure 4 provide compelling visual evidence for the quality and diversity of skills learned by the SNNs, clearly showing the positive effects of bilinear integration and the MI bonus.
    *   The exploration plots in Figure 5 demonstrate that the hierarchical structure dramatically improves exploration coverage compared to naive action-space noise, which is a prerequisite for solving the target sparse-reward tasks.
    *   The final learning curves in Figure 6 show that the proposed hierarchical methods significantly outperform the baseline on challenging maze and gather tasks, confirming that the enhanced exploration translates into successful task learning.
*   **Reproducibility and Honesty:** The authors provide code, hyperparameters, and detailed appendices. They also honestly report on failure modes (e.g., with the unstable Ant robot), which strengthens the paper by clearly defining the limitations of the current approach.

### **Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further. These are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from the decision to accept.

*   **Generalizing the MI Bonus Calculation:** The MI bonus is calculated by discretizing a hand-selected part of the state space (the 2D Center of Mass coordinates). While effective for locomotion tasks, this may not scale well to higher-dimensional state spaces or tasks where the relevant state features are not known a priori. It would be beneficial to discuss potential alternatives, such as using neural network-based mutual information estimators (e.g., MINE/InfoNCE) which could operate on learned state embeddings and would not require manual discretization or feature selection.
*   **Fixed Time Horizon `T` for Skills:** The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The appendix shows this is surprisingly robust, but for more dynamic tasks requiring reactive behavior, a fixed `T` is a limitation. The authors rightly point to learning a termination policy (as in the options framework) as future work, and perhaps this point could be emphasized more in the main discussion as a key next step towards a more general HRL system.
*   **Feedforward Manager Policy:** The manager is a feedforward network, making decisions based only on the state at the moment of switching. A recurrent architecture (e.g., an LSTM or GRU) could allow the manager to integrate information over the duration of the previously executed skill, potentially leading to more intelligent high-level decisions. This is another avenue for future work worth mentioning.

### **Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  How was the relevant state subset for the MI bonus (the CoM coordinates) chosen? How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to this choice? For instance, what would happen if the bonus was calculated based on joint velocities instead of, or in addition to, the CoM position?
2.  The failure mode for the Ant robot is attributed to the state distribution shift upon switching skills. Have you considered any mechanisms to mitigate this, such as learning a "transition policy" to bridge between skills, or regularizing the skill policies to be valid over a wider state distribution during pre-training?
3.  Appendix B shows a comparison to VIME on the Gather task. Could you provide more context on the sample complexity of your full two-stage method versus training a state-of-the-art exploration method like VIME from scratch on the downstream task? While your method clearly excels at transfer, understanding the total sample budget (pre-training + downstream) would be useful.
