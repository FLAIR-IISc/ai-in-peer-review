### 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a general framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to solve a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution is a two-stage approach. In the first "pre-training" stage, the agent learns a diverse set of skills in a simple environment using only a generic proxy reward, which requires minimal domain knowledge (e.g., rewarding movement speed for a locomotion agent). To learn multiple skills in a sample-efficient manner, the authors propose parameterizing the low-level policy as a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), conditioned on a discrete latent variable. Two key technical innovations are introduced to ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors: (1) a bilinear integration mechanism for combining the latent variable with the state observations, which grants the latent code more control over the policy's weights, and (2) an information-theoretic regularizer that adds a reward bonus proportional to the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and the agent's state, explicitly encouraging different latent codes to lead to distinguishable trajectories.

In the second stage, the pre-trained SNN policy is frozen and used as a set of primitive actions. For each new downstream task, a high-level "manager" policy is trained on top of these skills. This manager observes the full task state and selects which skill to execute for a fixed duration. The authors demonstrate empirically on challenging continuous control benchmarks (locomotion in mazes and a gathering task) that this hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and enables the agent to solve sparse-reward problems that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound method for unsupervised skill discovery. The proposed use of a Stochastic Neural Network with bilinear integration and a mutual information bonus provides a novel and effective combination for learning a diverse and useful repertoire of skills from a simple, task-agnostic reward signal.
2.  The experimental results are thorough and provide convincing evidence supporting the paper's claims. The framework is shown to drastically improve exploration and successfully solve a variety of challenging, sparse-reward continuous control tasks, significantly outperforming a relevant baseline. The ablation-style analysis clearly demonstrates the positive impact of each of the core technical contributions.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in its clarity, technical soundness, and the strength of its empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The problem of sample-efficient learning on sparse-reward tasks is clearly motivated, and the proposed two-stage solution is explained logically. The methodology section systematically builds up the final algorithm, justifying each component (SNN for weight sharing, bilinear integration for expressiveness, MI bonus for diversity). The use of visitation plots (Fig. 4 and 5) is highly effective for visualizing the diversity of learned skills and the resulting improvement in exploration, making the core ideas intuitive.

**Technical Soundness:** The overall approach is methodologically robust. Using a single SNN conditioned on a latent code is a more sample-efficient approach to learning a skill set than training multiple policies from scratch. The information-theoretic regularizer, inspired by InfoGAN, is a principled method for encouraging the latent codes to have a distinct, observable effect on the agent's behavior, preventing modal collapse. The choice of TRPO as the underlying policy gradient algorithm is standard and reliable. The entire framework provides a practical recipe for skill discovery that is broadly applicable.

**Novelty and Significance:** While HRL and unsupervised skill discovery are established fields, this work makes a significant contribution by presenting a concrete and highly effective implementation. The primary novelty lies in the specific combination of an SNN policy with bilinear integration and an MI-based objective for the purpose of skill discovery in RL. This approach moves beyond prior work that often required hand-designed options, demonstrations, or task-specific goal distributions for pre-training. By requiring only a simple proxy reward, the framework represents a step towards more autonomous and general-purpose skill acquisition.

**Empirical Support:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling.
*   **Skill Diversity (Sec 7.1):** The results in Figure 4 clearly demonstrate that the combination of bilinear integration and the MI bonus leads to a richer and more diverse set of locomotion skills (e.g., forward, backward, turning) compared to both independently trained policies and SNNs with simple concatenation.
*   **Exploration (Sec 7.2):** Figure 5 provides a stark visual contrast between the limited exploration of a standard policy and the broad state-space coverage enabled by the hierarchical policy, confirming that the learned skills serve as effective exploration primitives.
*   **Downstream Performance (Sec 7.3):** The learning curves in Figure 6 show that the proposed hierarchical methods consistently and substantially outperform the baseline on all tasks. This empirically validates the central claim that the pre-trained skills can be leveraged to solve complex, sparse-reward problems efficiently. The rigorous evaluation, involving multiple pre-trained models and multiple downstream seeds, adds confidence to these conclusions. The inclusion of further experiments on more complex agents (Snake, Ant) in the appendix strengthens the paper's claims of generality.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   The term "Stochastic Neural Network" is used quite broadly. The specific architecture is a deterministic network conditioned on an observation and a sampled latent code, which outputs the parameters of a unimodal distribution. It might be clearer to refer to it as a "latent-conditioned policy" early on to avoid potential confusion with other forms of SNNs, such as Bayesian Neural Networks.
*   The mutual information bonus is calculated based on a discretization of the 2D Center of Mass (CoM) coordinates. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss the scalability of this approach. While the paper mentions that an MLP regressor could be used for higher-dimensional spaces, a short discussion on potential challenges or preliminary findings would strengthen the paper's claim of generality.
*   The analysis of the switching time T in Appendix C.3 is valuable. A brief summary of these findings could be integrated into the main paper, as `T` is a key hyperparameter of the hierarchical model, and its impact on performance is of general interest.
*   The open reporting of failure modes for the "Ant" agent is commendable. This issue of state distribution mismatch upon switching skills is a fundamental challenge in HRL. Highlighting this more prominently in the main discussion section could better frame it as a key avenue for future research.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The MI bonus is estimated from empirical visitation counts within the current batch of trajectories. How is the stability of this estimation and the overall learning process affected by the batch size (`N`)?
*   In the downstream tasks, the high-level manager policy takes the full state `s = (s_agent, s_rest)` as input. Is the low-level SNN policy also fed the full state `s`, or only the `s_agent` part on which it was pre-trained?
*   The experiments use a fixed number of skills `K=6`. How was this value chosen? Have you experimented with varying `K`, and does the framework gracefully handle cases where `K` is set higher than the number of "natural" skills discoverable from the proxy reward?
*   The design of the proxy reward is crucial. For the locomotion agents, rewarding speed is an intuitive choice. Could you provide some examples or intuition on how one might design a similarly generic and minimal proxy reward for other domains, such as robotic manipulation, where the goal isn't just movement?
