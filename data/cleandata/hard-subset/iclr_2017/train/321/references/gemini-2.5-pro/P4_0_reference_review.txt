### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a two-stage hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve a collection of challenging continuous control tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to first learn a diverse set of reusable skills in an unsupervised pre-training phase, and then use these skills as primitives for a high-level policy to solve specific downstream tasks.

The first stage involves training a single policy, parameterized by a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), in a simple environment with a generic proxy reward (e.g., rewarding the agent for moving). The SNN takes a discrete latent code as input, allowing it to represent a multi-modal policy where each latent code corresponds to a different behavior or "skill". To ensure that the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the authors introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer. This regularizer adds a reward bonus proportional to the mutual information (MI) between the latent code and the agent's state, encouraging the agent's behavior to be predictable from its latent code.

In the second stage, the learned SNN policy is frozen and used as a set of low-level skills. For each new sparse-reward task, a high-level "manager" policy is trained. This manager observes the task-specific state and selects which skill (i.e., which latent code) to execute for a fixed period. The paper empirically demonstrates that this hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and enables solving complex locomotion and navigation tasks (e.g., maze navigation, object gathering) that are intractable for standard RL algorithms with naive exploration. The authors also provide valuable ablation studies showing the importance of the SNN architecture (specifically, bilinear integration of the latent code) and the MI bonus for learning a rich skill repertoire.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound framework for unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical control. The proposed method elegantly combines SNNs for multi-modal policy learning with an information-theoretic objective to produce a diverse and interpretable set of skills from minimal supervision.
2.  The claims are supported by strong and thorough empirical evidence. The experiments clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method, provide insightful visualizations of the learned skills and their impact on exploration, and show substantial performance gains over relevant baselines on challenging benchmark tasks.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement is clear, the methodology is described in sufficient detail, and each design choice (e.g., SNNs, bilinear integration, MI bonus) is well-motivated. The use of figures, particularly the visitation plots (Fig. 4 and 5), is highly effective in illustrating the quality of the learned skills and the resulting improvement in exploration.

**Technical Soundness:** The proposed approach is technically solid. Using a latent-conditioned SNN to represent a skill repertoire is a sensible way to achieve parameter sharing and sample efficiency compared to training separate policies. The introduction of the MI bonus, inspired by work in unsupervised representation learning, is a novel and effective way to enforce skill diversity in an RL context. The method for estimating the MI bonus via state-space discretization and empirical counts is practical and shown to work well. The overall hierarchical architecture is a standard and proven approach, but its application with the pre-trained SNN is what makes it powerful. The choice of TRPO as the optimization algorithm is robust and appropriate for the continuous control tasks considered.

**Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution is the specific formulation for the unsupervised pre-training phase. While the idea of learning skills is not new, this paper presents a complete and practical framework that does not require demonstrations, hand-crafted sub-goals, or task-specific rewards for skill discovery. The combination of a simple proxy reward, a latent-conditioned SNN, and an MI-based regularizer for diversity is a novel contribution that significantly advances the state of the art in automated skill discovery for continuous control. The demonstrated ability to solve sparse-reward tasks where previous methods fail highlights the significance of the work.

**Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
*   **Skill Learning Analysis (Sec 7.1):** The ablation study effectively isolates the contributions of the SNN architecture (concatenation vs. bilinear integration) and the MI bonus. The results compellingly show that both are crucial for learning a wide and non-overlapping set of skills.
*   **Exploration Analysis (Sec 7.2):** The comparison of exploration patterns clearly visualizes the benefit of the hierarchical structure over naive exploration, directly linking the diversity of pre-trained skills to broader state-space coverage.
*   **Downstream Task Performance (Sec 7.3):** The learning curves on the maze and gather tasks demonstrate that the framework is not just an academic exercise in skill discovery but a practical tool for solving difficult sparse-reward problems. The performance significantly surpasses a strong baseline (a flat policy trained with the proxy reward), validating the hierarchical approach. The experiments are conducted with multiple seeds, and the appendix provides further results and analyses, adding to the credibility of the findings.

### **4. Additional Feedback**

These points are intended for improvement and are not critical to the decision.
*   The MI bonus calculation relies on discretizing the 2D (x, y) coordinates of the agent. This works well for the environments tested, but its scalability to higher-dimensional state representations is a potential concern. While the paper briefly mentions using an MLP regressor as an alternative, expanding on this or including a small experiment would be beneficial for generality.
*   The choice of which state variables to use for the MI calculation (here, the center of mass) seems to require some domain knowledge. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to this choice or potential avenues for automating it would strengthen the paper's claim of using "minimal domain knowledge."
*   The high variance in performance on the maze tasks depending on the specific pre-trained SNN (discussed in Appendix C.4) is an important finding. It suggests that the pre-training process can sometimes fail to produce the specific skills necessary for a given downstream task. Highlighting this limitation more prominently in the main text could provide a more balanced perspective and motivate future work on ensuring the completeness of the learned skill set.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

*   The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed time `T`. The appendix shows that performance is not overly sensitive to `T` for the static tasks presented. Have you considered learning a termination condition for each skill, similar to the options framework? What challenges do you foresee in integrating learnable terminations into your framework, especially concerning the stability of the high-level policy's training?
*   The MI bonus is estimated using empirical visitation counts from the current training batch. How sensitive is this estimation and the overall training stability to the batch size? One might expect that a smaller batch size could lead to a noisy and biased posterior estimate `p(z|c)`, potentially harming the quality of the learned skills.
*   For the unstable "Ant" agent, the paper mentions that switching between skills can cause the agent to fall. Have you explored methods to smooth the transitions between skills? For instance, one could interpolate between the action distributions of the old and new skills for a short period, or train a dedicated transition policy. Do you think such an approach could mitigate the observed instability?
