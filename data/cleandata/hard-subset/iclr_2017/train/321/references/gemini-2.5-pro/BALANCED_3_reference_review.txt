### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a two-stage framework to tackle reinforcement learning problems in environments with sparse rewards. The central idea is to first learn a diverse set of reusable skills in an unsupervised "pre-training" phase, and then use these skills as primitives for a high-level policy to solve downstream tasks. For the skill-learning phase, the authors propose using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), where a latent code input conditions the policy to produce different behaviors. This approach allows for weight sharing and is more sample-efficient than training multiple policies independently. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the authors introduce two key innovations: (1) using a bilinear integration layer to combine the state and latent code, which is more expressive than simple concatenation, and (2) adding an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent code and the agent's state. The experiments, conducted on challenging continuous control locomotion tasks, demonstrate that this framework can learn an interpretable and diverse set of skills that significantly improve exploration and enable the agent to solve complex, sparse-reward tasks like navigating mazes.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Effective Method for Skill Discovery:** The paper presents a simple, elegant, and effective method for unsupervised skill discovery. The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks with a mutual information bonus is a principled way to learn a diverse set of skills within a single policy network, representing a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The approach is well-motivated and cleanly addresses the sample inefficiency of training skills independently.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The qualitative visualizations of learned skills and exploration patterns provide clear intuition for why the method works. The quantitative results on challenging sparse-reward benchmarks demonstrate a substantial performance improvement over a sensible baseline. The inclusion of detailed ablations (SNN architecture, MI bonus) and analysis in the appendix (different robots, hyperparameter sensitivity) further strengthens the paper's conclusions.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

**Strengths:**
*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed method is described logically, and the motivation for each component (SNNs, bilinear integration, MI bonus) is well-articulated. The connection to prior work is established effectively, positioning the contribution clearly within the field.
*   **Technical Contribution:** The core idea of using a single SNN regularized by mutual information is a powerful one. It elegantly transforms the problem of learning K distinct skills into training a single network, which is much more sample efficient. The finding that bilinear integration is critical for generating diverse behaviors compared to simple concatenation is an important practical insight.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental design is sound. The use of visitation plots (Figs. 4 and 5) is highly effective for qualitatively demonstrating the diversity of learned skills and the resulting improvement in exploration. The learning curves on downstream tasks (Fig. 6) provide strong quantitative evidence of the framework's effectiveness. The authors' practice of reporting results over multiple pre-training seeds and then averaging over downstream task seeds is commendable and provides a realistic picture of the method's robustness.
*   **Reproducibility and Honesty:** The authors provide code and supplementary videos, which is excellent for reproducibility. Furthermore, the appendix includes a frank discussion of failure modes (e.g., with the unstable Ant robot), which increases the credibility of the work and provides valuable insights for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Baselines:** The primary baseline is adding the pre-training proxy reward directly to the downstream task. While this is a reasonable baseline, a comparison against another contemporary unsupervised skill discovery method (e.g., DIAYN) or a state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation method (e.g., VIME) in the main paper would have provided a more complete picture of the state of the art. A comparison with VIME is present in the appendix but would be more impactful in the main results section.
*   **MI Estimation Details:** The estimation of the posterior for the MI bonus relies on discretizing the state space. This approach is simple and effective for the 2D case presented, but its scalability to higher-dimensional state subsets `C` is a concern. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to the grid resolution or a small experiment using the mentioned MLP-based estimator would have been beneficial.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive and are not critical to my decision.
*   The claim of requiring "minimal domain knowledge" is largely fair, but it is worth noting that both the design of the proxy reward (e.g., rewarding velocity) and the choice of the state variables for the MI bonus (e.g., CoM position) are important design choices that require some intuition about the task domain. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to these choices could further improve the paper.
*   The variance in performance on downstream tasks, attributed to the quality of the pre-trained SNN (as analyzed in Appendix C.4), is a very interesting result. It suggests that not all "diverse" skill sets are equally useful. It might be worthwhile to highlight this finding more prominently in the main text, as it points towards an important open question: what makes a set of pre-trained skills "good" for transfer?
*   The fixed skill execution time `T` is a simplification. While the analysis in Appendix C.3 shows it's not critical for the tasks studied, it's a clear limitation. Explicitly framing the current work as a strong foundation for future investigations into learning termination conditions (as mentioned in the conclusion) would be a good way to position the contribution.

### **5. Questions for Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
1.  How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to the choice of the state subset `C` used for the MI bonus and the granularity of the discretization grid? Have you experimented with other choices for `C`, for example, including the agent's orientation or velocities?
2.  In Figure 6, the hierarchical architectures are compared against the baseline in terms of sample complexity on the downstream task. Could you clarify if the sample complexity of the pre-training phase is included in these plots? If not, a plot showing performance against *total* environment steps (pre-training + downstream) would powerfully illustrate the sample efficiency benefits of the SNN approach over the "Multi-policy" baseline.
3.  Regarding the failure case with the Ant robot, you hypothesize that instability arises from switching to a skill in an unfamiliar part of the state space. Do you believe this could be mitigated by learning a dedicated "transition policy" between skills, or perhaps by incorporating skill-switching into the pre-training phase itself?
