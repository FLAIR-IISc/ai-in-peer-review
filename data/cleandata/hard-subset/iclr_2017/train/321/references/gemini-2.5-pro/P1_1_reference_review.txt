This paper presents a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to solve complex, sparse-reward continuous control tasks. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of skills using a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as a multi-modal policy. This learning is guided by a simple, task-agnostic "proxy reward" (e.g., rewarding forward velocity) and an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the skill's latent code and the agent's state. This MI bonus, inspired by InfoGAN, encourages the learned skills to be distinct and interpretable. Second, for downstream tasks, the learned skills are frozen and a high-level "manager" policy is trained to select among these skills to solve the specific sparse-reward problem. The paper demonstrates empirically that this approach learns a wide span of interpretable skills, significantly improves exploration, and achieves strong performance on challenging benchmark tasks like navigating mazes and gathering objects.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method for unsupervised skill discovery is novel, elegant, and well-motivated. It effectively combines the representational power of SNNs with an information-theoretic objective to generate a diverse set of skills from a very simple supervisory signal, addressing a key challenge in HRL.
2.  The experimental evaluation is thorough and compelling. The paper includes strong qualitative visualizations that build intuition (skill visitation plots, exploration coverage) and robust quantitative results on challenging sparse-reward tasks, clearly outperforming a relevant baseline. The ablation studies on the SNN architecture (bilinear vs. concatenation) and the impact of the MI bonus strongly support the specific design choices.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The paper tackles the critical problem of exploration in sparse-reward environments. The proposed solution is well-situated within the HRL literature, acknowledging classic work on options while addressing the key difficulty of automatic skill discovery.

The method itself has several strong points. Using a single SNN parameterized by a latent code to represent multiple skills is more parameter-efficient and scalable than training numerous independent policies. The introduction of the mutual information bonus is the paper's most significant technical contribution. It provides a principled way to enforce diversity among skills, preventing the policy from collapsing to a single mode and ensuring the latent codes correspond to meaningfully different behaviors. This is clearly demonstrated in the visitation plots in Figure 4, where increasing the MI bonus leads to more distinct and varied skills.

The empirical results provide strong support for the paper's claims. The exploration plots in Figure 5 are particularly convincing, showing a dramatic increase in the agent's ability to cover the state space when using the hierarchical policy compared to naive exploration. This directly illustrates *why* the method is effective. Subsequently, the learning curves in Figure 6 confirm that this enhanced exploration translates into successful policies for tasks where standard RL methods and even a shaped-reward baseline fail. The authors' diligence in running experiments on multiple robot morphologies (Swimmer, Snake) and their honest reporting of failure modes on more complex, unstable agents (Ant) adds to the paper's credibility.

**Additional Feedback and Questions:**
These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   **MI Bonus Scalability:** The mutual information bonus is estimated by discretizing the 2D (x,y) state space into a grid. While effective here, this approach may not scale well to tasks where the relevant state-space for distinguishing skills is higher-dimensional (e.g., a 3D environment or tasks where orientation is key). The paper briefly mentions using an MLP regressor as an alternative; a bit more discussion on the challenges and potential of this alternative would strengthen the paper.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The manager policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The appendix includes an excellent analysis of `T` for the Snake agent, but this is a crucial hyperparameter for the overall framework. A brief discussion in the main text about the choice of `T` for the Swimmer experiments and its general sensitivity would be beneficial. Similarly, how sensitive is the pre-training to the MI bonus coefficient `αH`?
*   **Proxy Reward Design:** The paper states the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," which is a key selling point. However, the choice of this reward (e.g., CoM speed) seems fundamental to the types of skills that will be learned. A short discussion on the philosophy of designing this reward or the potential impact of different proxy rewards would be insightful. For instance, what kind of skills would emerge if the reward was for angular velocity instead of linear velocity?

**Questions for Authors:**
1.  Regarding the mutual information bonus in Sec 5.3, could you provide more detail on the discretization of the state space? How sensitive is the method to the grid resolution, and how do you envision this approach scaling if the relevant state dimensions for skill discrimination were higher than two (e.g., for a flying agent)?
2.  The failure mode for the Ant robot is attributed to instability when switching skills. Do you believe this is a fundamental limitation of hard-switching HRL frameworks for unstable agents, or could a "soft" transition mechanism (e.g., a learned transition policy between skills) potentially alleviate this issue?
3.  For the downstream tasks, the low-level skills are frozen. You mention end-to-end fine-tuning as future work. Do you have any preliminary insights into the trade-offs between keeping skills frozen (ensuring consistent primitives) versus fine-tuning them (allowing for task-specific adaptation)?
