### **Summary**

This paper proposes a two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a method for learning a diverse set of reusable skills in an unsupervised pre-training phase, which are then utilized by a high-level policy to solve a collection of downstream tasks.

The key components of the proposed method are:
1.  **Unsupervised Skill Discovery:** The agent is pre-trained in a simple environment with a generic "proxy reward" (e.g., rewarding forward velocity) that encourages useful behaviors without task-specific knowledge.
2.  **Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs):** Instead of training multiple separate policies, the authors use a single SNN where a latent categorical variable is fed as input alongside the state. Different values of this latent code condition the policy to produce different behaviors, allowing for efficient learning and weight sharing across skills.
3.  **Information-Theoretic Regularization:** To prevent the SNN from collapsing to a single mode and to actively encourage skill diversity, the authors introduce a reward bonus based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent code and the agent's state (specifically, its coordinates). This is inspired by InfoGAN and encourages the latent codes to correspond to discoverable, distinct behaviors.
4.  **Hierarchical Control:** For downstream tasks, the pre-trained SNN's weights are frozen. A high-level "manager" policy is then trained on top, which observes the full task state and selects a latent code (i.e., a skill) for the low-level SNN to execute for a fixed period.

The authors evaluate their framework on challenging continuous control benchmarks (Swimmer, Snake) in sparse-reward maze navigation and gathering tasks, demonstrating that their approach significantly improves exploration and enables learning where standard RL methods fail.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a clean, well-motivated, and effective framework for unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical control. My recommendation is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation for Skill Discovery:** The paper's main strength lies in its novel combination of SNNs with an information-theoretic objective for learning a multi-modal skill policy. This approach is more sample-efficient than training independent policies and provides a principled way (via the MI bonus) to ensure the learned skills are diverse and distinguishable. The connection to information-theoretic unsupervised learning (like InfoGAN) is insightful and well-applied to the RL context.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The qualitative results (visitation plots in Figs. 4 and 5) provide clear, intuitive evidence for the diversity of learned skills and the resulting improvement in exploration. The quantitative results (learning curves in Fig. 6) demonstrate superior performance on challenging sparse-reward tasks. The ablation studies, particularly on the choice of latent variable integration (bilinear vs. concatenation) and the impact of the MI bonus, are crucial for justifying the specific design choices.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation in the introduction is clear, and the related work section effectively positions the contribution with respect to classic HRL, intrinsic motivation, and other skill discovery methods.

The methodology is explained in detail. The idea of using a simple proxy reward is practical and lowers the barrier for applying this method, as it requires minimal domain knowledge. The use of a bilinear integration for the latent code and observation is a well-justified architectural choice that the experiments show is critical for success. The MI regularizer is the most innovative part of the pre-training phase; discretizing the state space to estimate the posterior `p(z|s)` is a simple and effective implementation choice.

The experimental setup is rigorous. The authors use established, difficult benchmarks. The visualization of the skills learned by the SNNs is highly effective and makes the contribution easy to grasp. Figure 4 convincingly shows that the combination of bilinear integration and the MI bonus leads to a much richer and more distinct set of skills than simpler alternatives. Figure 5 provides a stark visualization of how this diverse skill set translates into vastly superior random exploration compared to action-space noise. The final results on the downstream tasks show that this enhanced exploration directly leads to successful policies where a strong baseline (TRPO + proxy reward) fails, confirming the utility of the hierarchical approach. The inclusion of results for multiple robot morphologies (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) in the appendix further demonstrates the generality of the approach. The authors are also transparent about failure modes (e.g., instability for the Ant robot), which adds to the credibility of the work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could further improve it:

*   **Baselines:** The main baseline is TRPO with the proxy reward added to the sparse task reward. This is a reasonable choice, as it controls for the information provided by the proxy reward itself. However, a comparison against other contemporary skill-discovery methods (e.g., DIAYN, Option-Critic) on the same tasks would have further strengthened the paper's claims of superiority. The comparison to VIME in the appendix is excellent, but integrating a summary of this into the main paper would be beneficial.
*   **Sensitivity of the MI Bonus:** The MI bonus is calculated with respect to the agent's (x, y) coordinates. This seems reasonable for navigation skills but might be less obvious for other types of tasks (e.g., manipulation). A brief discussion on the sensitivity to this choice or how one might choose the relevant state variables (`C`) more generally would be welcome.
*   **Fixed Time Horizon `T` for Skills:** The high-level policy commits to a skill for a fixed duration `T`. The authors rightly point this out as a limitation and show an analysis in the appendix. While learning a termination condition is beyond the scope of this work, it would be interesting to see a brief discussion in the main text about how the choice of `T` might interact with the task's temporal structure. For instance, a `T` that is too long might be inefficient for tasks requiring reactive behavior.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the pre-training phase to the coefficient `αH` of the MI bonus? Is there a clear trend (more diversity with higher `αH`) up to a point where it might distort the proxy reward objective?
2.  In Section 5.4, you mention the possibility of end-to-end fine-tuning of the skill policies. Did you attempt this? My intuition is that it could be beneficial but might also risk "catastrophic forgetting" of the general-purpose skills. What are your thoughts on balancing skill adaptation with skill reusability across tasks?
3.  The performance on the maze tasks shows high variance depending on the pre-trained SNN (Appendix C.4). This suggests that the pre-training is not guaranteed to find the *specific* skills (e.g., a strong backward motion) needed for a particular downstream task. Could this be addressed by increasing the number of latent codes `K` to encourage an even wider variety of skills, or do you see other potential solutions?
