### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) designed to tackle challenging continuous control tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of reusable skills in a simple environment with only a generic proxy reward (e.g., rewarding forward velocity). To learn multiple skills efficiently, the authors propose using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) as a multi-modal policy, where different skills are conditioned on a discrete latent code. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, they introduce a clever information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent code and the agent's state. In the second stage, these pre-trained skills (with frozen weights) are used as a primitive action space for a high-level manager policy, which is then trained on specific downstream tasks. This hierarchical structure is shown to dramatically improve exploration and enable solving complex tasks like maze navigation and object gathering, which are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning and unsupervised skill discovery. The decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Principled Approach:** The paper presents an elegant and effective framework that combines several powerful ideas—SNNs for multi-modal policy representation, a simple unsupervised pre-training objective, and an information-theoretic bonus for skill diversification. This combination provides a practical and general solution to the long-standing problem of automatic skill discovery in continuous domains.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by extensive experiments on challenging benchmark tasks. The results clearly demonstrate the benefits of each component of the proposed method. The visualizations are particularly compelling, providing clear qualitative evidence of the diverse skills learned and the superior exploration of the hierarchical policy. The ablation studies effectively justify the design choices, such as the use of bilinear integration and the mutual information bonus.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

*   **Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper tackles the critical and difficult problem of exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks. The motivation for a hierarchical approach that first learns skills without task-specific rewards is very clear and well-argued.
*   **Methodology:** The proposed method is a highlight. Using an SNN to learn a shared representation for multiple skills is an efficient and scalable alternative to training separate policies, as convincingly shown by the comparison to the "Multi-policy" baseline. The introduction of the mutual information bonus, inspired by InfoGAN, is a key contribution that provides a principled way to encourage the emergence of distinct, interpretable skills, moving beyond simple heuristics.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental design is thorough and robust. The choice of environments from the Duan et al. (2016) benchmark is appropriate, as these are known to be difficult for non-hierarchical methods. The paper provides extensive quantitative results (learning curves) and qualitative analysis (visitation plots) that strongly support its claims. The inclusion of results on multiple agents (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) and a detailed appendix demonstrates a high degree of diligence.
*   **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors are also transparent about the limitations of their work, such as the failure modes for the unstable "Ant" robot, which strengthens the credibility of the research.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Generalizing the MI Bonus:** The mutual information bonus is calculated with respect to a manually selected, low-dimensional subset of the state space (the CoM coordinates). While this is effective for the locomotion tasks studied, it might be challenging in domains where the relevant state variables are not as obvious. The paper could be strengthened by discussing the sensitivity to this choice or briefly outlining potential approaches for automatically identifying these salient state dimensions.
*   **Fixed Temporal Abstraction:** The high-level manager commits to a chosen skill for a fixed number of timesteps, `T`. This is a common simplification in HRL, but it lacks the flexibility of approaches where skills can terminate based on the state. While the appendix provides a good analysis of the sensitivity to `T`, a brief discussion in the main paper comparing this design choice to learned termination conditions (e.g., as in the option-critic architecture) would be beneficial for positioning the work.
*   **Fine-tuning vs. Freezing Skills:** The paper focuses on freezing the low-level skills during downstream training. This is a reasonable choice that demonstrates the quality of the pre-trained skills. However, a brief discussion or a small experiment on the effects of fine-tuning the SNN policy along with the manager could provide valuable insights into the trade-off between skill stability and task-specific adaptation.
*   **Comparison to Other Methods:** The baselines used are strong internal comparisons. However, positioning the results against other contemporary unsupervised skill discovery methods (e.g., DIAYN) or placing the comparison with VIME (from the appendix) into the main text would further highlight the method's standing within the field.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  The performance on the Maze tasks shows significant variance depending on the pre-trained SNN used (as shown in Appendix C.4). Does the mutual information bonus, which encourages a more consistently diverse set of skills, also help reduce this run-to-run variance in downstream performance compared to SNNs trained without it?
2.  Regarding the failure case with the "Ant" robot, the instability seems to arise from state distribution mismatch when switching skills. Could you elaborate on whether this is primarily an issue of the kinematic state (e.g., joint positions/velocities being unsuitable for the new gait) or a more fundamental dynamics problem? Have you considered simple heuristics to mitigate this, such as resetting joint velocities or interpolating between skill policies for a short duration?
3.  The bilinear integration is shown to be crucial for learning distinct skills compared to simple concatenation. Could you provide some intuition as to why this architectural choice is so effective? Does it allow the latent code to modulate the policy's response to observations in a more expressive way than just changing a bias term?
