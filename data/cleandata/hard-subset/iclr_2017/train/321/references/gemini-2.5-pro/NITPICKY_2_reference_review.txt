### **Review**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework designed to solve a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, a diverse set of skills is learned using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy. This pre-training occurs in a simple environment with a generic "proxy reward" that requires minimal domain knowledge (e.g., rewarding forward velocity). To encourage the learning of distinct skills corresponding to different latent codes, the authors introduce two key components: a bilinear integration of the latent variable with the observation, and an information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent code and the agent's state. In the second stage, for each downstream task, a high-level "Manager" policy is trained on top of the frozen, pre-trained skills to solve complex, sparse-reward problems. The paper provides empirical evidence on continuous control benchmarks (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) in maze and collection tasks, demonstrating that the learned skills enhance exploration and lead to successful policies where standard RL methods fail.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper is marred by a significant number of formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography, along with redundancies and stylistic inconsistencies. This lack of attention to detail raises concerns about the overall care taken in the research and reporting process.
2.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Clarity:** Several key aspects of the methodology are either ambiguously defined, poorly justified, or rely on potentially fragile assumptions. This includes the formulation and notation of the MI bonus, the non-scalable state discretization, and unsubstantiated claims about sample efficiency.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

While the paper presents a compelling and intuitive framework, the execution and presentation suffer from a multitude of minor flaws that, in aggregate, undermine the work's credibility.

**3.1. Presentation and Formatting:**

*   **Bibliography:** The reference section is critically flawed. Many entries are incorrectly formatted, containing raw BibTeX-like fields (e.g., `Bacon and Precup.,? \Q2016\E, Bacon and Precup.,`). This is not a minor typographical error but a systemic issue across the entire list, making it difficult to parse and suggesting a lack of diligence in the final preparation of the manuscript.
*   **Redundancy:** The links to the code and videos are provided via footnotes in both the Introduction (footnotes 1 and 2) and Section 7 (footnotes 4 and 5). This redundancy is unnecessary.
*   **Clarity of Figures:** In Figure 4(b), the caption states it superposes rollouts "for each of the 6 policies, each with a different color". However, the legend is not provided, making it impossible to map a color to a specific policy. While the overall point is visual, the presentation is imprecise.
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** In Eq. (1), the coefficient for the MI bonus is denoted `αH`. This is a confusing choice, as `H` is the standard symbol for entropy, which is used in the same section (`I(Z;C) = H(Z) −H(Z|C)`). This could easily be misinterpreted as the coefficient `α` being multiplied by the entropy `H`. A less ambiguous notation, such as `α_I` or `β_MI`, would be far clearer.

**3.2. Methodological Ambiguities and Lack of Rigor:**

*   **MI Bonus Formulation:** The paper claims to maximize `I(Z;C)`, which is equivalent to minimizing `H(Z|C)`. The reward bonus is given as `αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt )`. This is a sample-based reward for maximizing the log-posterior, which *in expectation* minimizes the conditional entropy. The connection is not explicitly stated, and the wording "penalize H(Z|C)" is imprecise. The implementation relies on discretizing the 2D coordinate space into cells. This approach is crude, does not scale to higher-dimensional state representations `C`, and introduces a sensitive hyperparameter (cell size). While the paper mentions an MLP regressor as an alternative, this is not implemented or evaluated, leaving the scalability of the core regularizer in question.
*   **"Minimal Domain Knowledge":** The paper repeatedly claims that the framework requires "minimal domain knowledge". However, designing the proxy reward (e.g., rewarding CoM speed) and, more critically, selecting the "relevant subset of the state" `C` for the MI calculation (e.g., the CoM coordinates) both require significant, task-relevant prior knowledge. This claim seems overstated.
*   **SNN Architecture and Efficiency:** The paper motivates the use of SNNs by citing their ability to "approximate any well-behaved probability distributions," a property of general SNNs. However, it then immediately specializes to a simple latent-variable-input model. The choice of bilinear integration over simple concatenation is justified empirically but lacks deeper theoretical grounding in this context. Furthermore, the claim that using an SNN "effectively reduces the sample complexity by a factor equal to the numbers of skills" is an informal assertion. While plausible, it assumes the SNN trains in the same number of samples as a single uni-modal policy, which is an empirical claim that is not substantiated with data (e.g., training curves for one vs. the other).
*   **Experimental Variance:** The learning curves in Figure 6 exhibit extremely high variance. While the authors are commendable for not cherry-picking pre-trained policies and averaging over multiple runs, this high variance makes it difficult to draw statistically significant conclusions. In Figure 6(a), the final performance of the SNN (no MI) method is not clearly superior to the baseline, as the confidence intervals heavily overlap. The critical analysis of this variance is relegated to Appendix C.4, but it is a central factor in interpreting the main results and should be addressed more prominently.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The entire reference list must be corrected and properly formatted. This is non-negotiable for a publication.
*   The pragmatic trick of augmenting the observation with the latent code `z` to apply vanilla TRPO is clever. This should be explained more formally, clarifying that the policy being optimized is `π(a|s,z)`, and how this relates to the overall hierarchical objective.
*   The discussion in Appendix C.4 regarding the impact of the specific pre-trained SNN on downstream performance is very insightful and helps explain the high variance in the main results. I would strongly recommend integrating this analysis into the main body of the paper (Section 7.3) to provide better context for the learning curves.
*   Consider running more pre-training seeds to potentially find a set of SNNs that more consistently produces the necessary skills, which might reduce the variance in the downstream tasks and strengthen the paper's conclusions.

#### **5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the motivation for the notation `αH` in Equation (1)? Was there a reason to not use a more standard notation like `α_I` to avoid confusion with the entropy `H`?
2.  The state discretization for the MI bonus seems limiting. Have you performed any experiments with the proposed MLP-based posterior estimator? If so, how did it perform, and how sensitive was it to the architecture and training of this auxiliary network?
3.  Regarding the claim that the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," how would one go about designing a suitable proxy reward and selecting the state-subset `C` for a more complex agent like a humanoid, where simple CoM velocity might not be sufficient to encourage a diverse set of useful skills (e.g., balancing, crouching, turning in place)?
