### **ICLR Review**

**Paper:** STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

---

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to tackle tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The core contribution is a two-stage process. In the first stage, the agent undergoes an unsupervised "pre-training" phase in a simple environment with a generic proxy reward (e.g., rewarding movement speed). This phase aims to learn a diverse set of reusable, low-level skills. To achieve this, the authors model the policy as a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN), where a latent categorical variable conditions the policy's behavior. To ensure the learned skills are distinct and diverse, they introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and the agent's state. The paper also finds that a bilinear integration of the latent code and state observation is more effective than simple concatenation.

In the second stage, the pre-trained SNN policy (with its weights frozen) serves as a set of low-level primitives. A new, high-level "manager" policy is then trained on top of these skills to solve specific downstream tasks. The manager's action is to select a latent code, which in turn activates the corresponding skill for a fixed duration. The authors demonstrate empirically that this hierarchical approach drastically improves exploration and enables the agent to solve challenging sparse-reward tasks (mazes and object gathering) that are intractable for standard RL algorithms.

---

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper presents an elegant and principled approach to unsupervised skill discovery. The combination of Stochastic Neural Networks for multi-modal policy representation, a simple proxy reward for pre-training, and an information-theoretic bonus for encouraging diversity is a compelling solution to the long-standing problem of exploration in RL. It effectively bridges the gap between classic HRL (which often requires hand-designed skills) and intrinsic motivation methods (which don't explicitly build reusable primitives).

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Support:** The claims are backed by a thorough set of experiments. The qualitative results (visitation plots in Fig. 4 and exploration plots in Fig. 5) provide clear, intuitive evidence for the effectiveness of the skill-learning and hierarchical exploration strategy. The quantitative results on sparse-reward benchmark tasks (Fig. 6) demonstrate a significant performance gain over a relevant baseline. The paper includes important ablation studies (integration method, effect of MI bonus) that validate the specific design choices.

---

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's main strength lies in its well-conceived methodology. The problem of acquiring useful skills without external supervision is central to scaling RL. The proposed solution is both simple and powerful. Using a single SNN to learn multiple skills simultaneously is highly sample-efficient compared to training separate policies, a point the authors clearly demonstrate (Fig. 4a vs. 4c/d). The introduction of the MI bonus, inspired by InfoGAN, is a key contribution that provides a principled mechanism to prevent mode collapse and promote the learning of distinct, interpretable skills. This is a significant improvement over hoping that multimodality emerges naturally from the optimization process.

The experimental evaluation is rigorous and provides strong support for the paper's claims.
*   **Skill Diversity (Sec 7.1):** The visitation plots are an excellent visualization tool. They clearly show that the bilinear SNN with an MI bonus consistently learns a wider and more diverse set of locomotion skills (e.g., forward, backward, turning) compared to both independently trained policies and the simpler concatenation-based SNN.
*   **Improved Exploration (Sec 7.2):** The comparison of exploration patterns in Figure 5 is striking. It visually confirms the core hypothesis: a hierarchical policy that switches between meaningful, temporally-extended skills explores the state space far more effectively than unstructured, step-by-step random noise.
*   **Downstream Task Performance (Sec 7.3):** The learning curves in the Maze and Gather tasks show that the improved exploration translates directly into the ability to solve complex tasks with very sparse rewards. The proposed method significantly outperforms the baseline, which itself is non-trivial (a flat policy augmented with the proxy reward). The fact that the SNN-based hierarchy often outperforms the "Multi-policy" baseline further strengthens the case for the proposed weight-sharing and diversification approach.
*   **Honesty and Thoroughness:** The inclusion of experiments on more complex agents (Snake, Ant) and a frank discussion of failure modes (instability during skill switching for the Ant) in the appendix adds to the paper's scientific credibility.

---

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The paper is very well-written and the work is solid. The following points are intended as constructive suggestions for further improvement and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

*   **Fixed Time Horizon `T` for Skills:** The manager commits to a skill for a fixed number of steps `T`. While the appendix shows this is not overly sensitive for the tasks studied, this is a limitation. It would be interesting to discuss the possibility of learning a termination policy for each skill, in the spirit of the Options framework (Sutton et al., 1999). This would allow for more flexible and adaptive behavior.
*   **Manager Policy Input:** The manager is a feedforward network that only observes the state at the moment of decision. Information gathered during the execution of the previous skill is lost. A recurrent architecture (e.g., an LSTM) for the manager policy could potentially lead to more intelligent high-level decision-making by maintaining a memory of the recent trajectory.
*   **Scalability of MI Estimation:** The MI bonus is calculated by discretizing the 2D coordinate space. This is effective here but may not scale well to tasks where the relevant state information for distinguishing skills is higher-dimensional. The paper briefly mentions fitting an MLP as an alternative, and it would strengthen the paper to elaborate on this or show even a preliminary result, as it is a more general solution.
*   **Clarification of Assumptions:** The problem statement in Section 4 assumes the state space factors into `S_agent` and `S_rest` that "weakly interact." This is an intuitive and reasonable assumption, but the term "weakly interact" is not formally defined. A brief discussion on what this means in practice (e.g., agent dynamics are largely independent of task-specific variables) could add clarity.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

To further clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following:

1.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to the MI bonus coefficient `α_H`? The paper shows a clear benefit for `α_H=0.01`, but is there a "sweet spot," and does performance degrade gracefully if it's set too high or too low?
2.  **Choice of Proxy Reward:** The paper argues the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge." Could you comment on the robustness of the framework to the choice of this reward? For instance, if the proxy reward for the swimmer was simply "positive velocity along the x-axis," how would that limit the diversity of learned skills and the ability to solve downstream tasks that require, e.g., backward or sideways motion?
3.  **End-to-end Fine-tuning:** The paper freezes the low-level skills for downstream tasks. Have you experimented with fine-tuning the SNN weights end-to-end with the manager? While this risks catastrophic forgetting of general skills, it could also allow for valuable task-specific adaptation. What are your thoughts on the trade-offs involved?
