### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a general and effective two-stage framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning, aimed at solving a collection of challenging tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to first pre-train a diverse set of low-level skills in an unsupervised manner, and then learn a high-level policy that composes these skills to solve downstream tasks.

The main contributions are threefold. First, the paper proposes using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) conditioned on a latent variable to learn multiple skills simultaneously. This is more sample-efficient than training separate policies for each skill and allows for weight sharing. Second, to ensure that the learned skills are distinct and cover a wide range of behaviors, the authors introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information between the latent skill variable and the resulting agent state. This elegantly encourages the emergence of diverse and interpretable skills. Third, the authors conduct a thorough empirical investigation into architectural choices, demonstrating that bilinear integration of the latent variable and the state is more effective than simple concatenation. The proposed framework is evaluated on a set of challenging locomotion and navigation tasks (Maze and Gather), where it significantly outperforms baselines and demonstrates superior exploration capabilities.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of exploration in sparse-reward RL tasks with a well-motivated and principled framework for unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical control.
2.  The technical contributions—specifically the combination of SNNs for multi-modal policy learning and an information-theoretic regularizer for encouraging skill diversity—are novel, elegant, and shown to be highly effective through comprehensive empirical evaluation and ablation studies.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

**Strengths:**
*   **Problem Significance:** The work tackles one of the most significant hurdles in modern RL: efficient exploration in environments with sparse rewards and long time horizons. The proposed solution of pre-training reusable skills is a very promising and scalable direction for addressing this challenge.
*   **Methodological Soundness and Novelty:** The proposed framework is clean, intuitive, and well-executed. The use of an SNN to represent a skill repertoire within a single network is a sample-efficient approach to multi-modal policy learning. The key novelty lies in the mutual information (MI) regularizer, inspired by InfoGAN. This provides a principled objective for encouraging skill diversity without requiring hand-crafted rewards for each desired skill, moving beyond simple heuristics. The paper also provides valuable insights into network architecture, showing that bilinear integration significantly improves skill separation.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by extensive experiments on challenging continuous control benchmarks. The visualizations are particularly effective. The visitation plots in Figure 4 provide clear, qualitative evidence that both the bilinear integration and the MI bonus contribute to learning a richer and more distinct set of skills. The exploration plots in Figure 5 convincingly demonstrate that the hierarchical policy vastly improves exploration over naive strategies. Finally, the learning curves in Figure 6 show that this improved exploration translates directly into superior performance and faster learning on the difficult downstream tasks.
*   **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of their method, including ablations on the MI bonus and network architecture. The appendices extend this analysis to more complex agents (Snake, Ant), investigate the sensitivity to the skill switching time `T`, and honestly discuss failure modes (e.g., instability for the Ant agent), which strengthens the credibility of the research.

**Weaknesses:**
While the paper is strong, there are a few areas that could be improved to further solidify the contributions.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The primary baseline is a flat policy trained with the same proxy reward used during pre-training. While this is a fair comparison, the paper would be stronger if it included empirical comparisons against other state-of-the-art unsupervised skill discovery methods, such as DIAYN, VALOR, or the Option-Critic architecture. A more direct comparison to other intrinsic motivation methods beyond the one in the appendix (VIME) would also help to better position the work in the current landscape.
*   **Frozen Skills and Fixed Duration:** The low-level skills are frozen after pre-training, and the high-level policy commits to each skill for a fixed time `T`. This limits the adaptability of the agent, as skills optimal for the pre-training task might be suboptimal for a specific downstream task, and a fixed duration lacks the flexibility of a learned termination condition. The authors acknowledge these as limitations and potential future work, but they are noteworthy constraints of the current framework.
*   **Scalability of the MI Bonus Calculation:** The MI bonus is computed by discretizing the 2D coordinates of the agent's center of mass. It is not clear how well this would scale to tasks where the relevant state information for distinguishing skills is higher-dimensional or less easily identifiable. The paper mentions the possibility of using a neural network to estimate the posterior, but this is not explored empirically.

Despite these points, the core contributions of the paper are significant and well-supported, and the work represents a clear step forward for hierarchical reinforcement learning.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following suggestions are intended to be constructive and are not critical for the decision.
*   The comparison to VIME in Appendix B is very valuable. It might be worth summarizing this result in the main text to give readers a clearer sense of how the method performs relative to other state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation techniques.
*   The discussion on the impact of different pre-trained SNNs on downstream performance (Appendix C.4) is very insightful. This analysis of variance is important and could be briefly mentioned in the main results section to provide a more complete picture of the method's robustness.
*   A brief discussion on the sensitivity to the MI bonus coefficient `α_H` would be beneficial. How was this value selected, and how much tuning did it require?
*   The framework seems general. It would be interesting to see a brief discussion on how the concept of a "proxy reward" might be instantiated in other domains beyond locomotion, for example, in manipulation tasks.

### **5. Questions for Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points:
1.  The MI bonus is calculated based on the agent's (x, y) coordinates. Have you experimented with including other state variables, such as orientation or velocity, in the state subset `C`? How sensitive is the quality of the learned skills to this choice?
2.  In Section 7.3, you note that the high variance in maze-solving performance is due to some pre-trained SNNs lacking a specific, critical skill. This suggests a potential brittleness. Do you have thoughts on how to mitigate this? Could the pre-training objective be modified to encourage a more uniform "coverage" of behaviors, ensuring that critical skills for a wider range of potential downstream tasks are learned?
3.  How does your hierarchical approach compare to a flat policy trained on the sparse reward task but equipped with a more advanced, non-skill-based exploration bonus, such as one based on pseudo-counts or random network distillation? This would help clarify whether the benefit comes purely from better exploration or from the temporal abstraction and structure provided by the hierarchy.
