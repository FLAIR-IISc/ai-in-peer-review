### 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes a general framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning designed to solve a collection of challenging continuous control tasks with sparse rewards. The core contribution is a two-stage process. First, in a pre-training phase, the agent learns a diverse set of low-level skills in a simple environment with only a generic "proxy" reward signal (e.g., rewarding forward velocity), which requires minimal domain knowledge. To learn multiple skills efficiently, the authors propose using a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to represent a multi-modal policy, where a latent categorical variable conditions the policy's behavior. The paper demonstrates that using a bilinear integration for the latent variable and observation is more effective than simple concatenation. To explicitly encourage the diversity of the learned skills, the authors introduce a novel information-theoretic regularizer based on the mutual information (MI) between the latent variable and the agent's state.

In the second stage, the pre-trained SNN, with its weights frozen, is used as a set of primitive actions or "skills". For each new downstream task, a high-level "manager" policy is trained on top of these skills. This manager observes the task-specific state and selects which skill to execute for a fixed duration, effectively reducing the temporal horizon and enabling structured exploration. The paper empirically validates this framework on difficult locomotion and navigation tasks (mazes and object gathering), showing that the proposed hierarchical structure significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency, leading to strong performance where standard RL methods fail.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound method for unsupervised skill discovery and hierarchical control. The use of an SNN with an information-theoretic regularizer is a novel and effective combination for learning a diverse set of skills with high sample efficiency.
2.  The claims are strongly supported by a thorough empirical evaluation. The experiments include clear qualitative visualizations of the learned skills and their exploratory behaviors, as well as quantitative results on challenging benchmark tasks that demonstrate significant performance gains over relevant baselines. The ablation studies effectively isolate and justify the core components of the proposed method.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully tackles the critical problem of exploration in sparse-reward RL tasks. The proposed two-stage framework is conceptually simple, practical, and well-positioned within the existing literature on HRL and intrinsic motivation.

**Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The main technical contribution lies in the pre-training stage. While using latent variables to condition policies is not new, the specific combination of an SNN, a bilinear integration module, and an MI-based regularizer for the purpose of discovering a diverse skill repertoire is a novel and valuable contribution. The MI bonus, inspired by InfoGAN, is adapted effectively to the RL setting to prevent modal collapse and ensure that different latent codes correspond to distinct, interpretable behaviors. This is a more direct and principled approach to encouraging diversity than simply training multiple policies from different random seeds. The empirical results in Section 7.1 clearly show that both the bilinear integration and the MI bonus are crucial for consistently learning a rich span of skills, supporting the value of these specific design choices.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing.
*   **Skill Quality:** The visitation plots in Figure 4 provide compelling qualitative evidence that the proposed method learns a more diverse and useful set of skills (e.g., forward, backward, and turning motions) compared to the baseline of training independent policies.
*   **Exploration:** The exploration plots in Figure 5 demonstrate that the hierarchical structure enables the agent to cover a significantly larger portion of the state space than naive exploration, which is a prerequisite for solving the sparse-reward tasks.
*   **Downstream Performance:** The learning curves in Figure 6 show that the hierarchical methods substantially outperform the baseline of applying the proxy reward directly to the downstream task. Importantly, the proposed SNN-based hierarchy performs competitively with or better than a "Multi-policy" baseline that uses six times as many samples during pre-training, validating the claim of improved sample efficiency. The experiments are conducted on established benchmark environments, and the results are averaged over multiple seeds for both pre-training and downstream training, lending credibility to the conclusions. The inclusion of results with more complex agents and further analysis in the appendix strengthens the paper's empirical contributions.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for further strengthening the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   The implementation of the MI bonus relies on discretizing a low-dimensional subset of the state space (the agent's 2D coordinates). This raises questions about its applicability to tasks where the relevant state variables are higher-dimensional or less obvious. While the paper mentions that an MLP could be used to estimate the posterior, demonstrating this or discussing the sensitivity to the grid resolution for the current method would make the approach's generality clearer.
*   The choice of which state variables to use for the MI objective (here, Center of Mass) requires some domain insight. It would be helpful to discuss how this choice might be automated or how sensitive the method is to a suboptimal choice (e.g., using joint angles instead).
*   The paper correctly identifies end-to-end finetuning of the skill policies and learning a termination condition (instead of a fixed switch time `T`) as promising avenues for future work. A brief discussion of the potential challenges associated with these extensions, such as optimization stability for end-to-end training with discrete variables, would be insightful for readers.

### 5. Questions for the Authors
*   The MI bonus is calculated using visitation counts from the current batch of rollouts. How sensitive is this estimate to the batch size? Have you considered using a running average of visitation counts over several batches to obtain a more stable estimate for the reward bonus?
*   The failure mode reported for the unstable "Ant" agent is an important finding. The paper suggests that learning a "transition policy" could alleviate this. Could you elaborate on what this transition policy would entail? For instance, would it be a short-horizon policy trained to steer the agent from the termination state of one skill to a suitable initiation state for the next?
*   In Section 7.3, you observe that the MI bonus, while creating more diverse skills, does not always improve final performance on the maze tasks. This suggests a potential trade-off between the diversity of the pre-trained skill set and its specialization for a specific family of downstream tasks. Do you have any thoughts on how one might balance this trade-off, perhaps by shaping the pre-training objective with some prior knowledge about the downstream task distribution?
