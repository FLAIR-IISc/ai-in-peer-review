

# Review: "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward environments. The authors introduce a pre-training phase where a single Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) policy learns a diverse span of skills using a minimal proxy reward signal (requiring only basic domain knowledge), followed by a hierarchical architecture where a high-level policy selects among these skills for downstream tasks. Key innovations include using SNNs with bilinear integration to represent multi-modal policies and introducing an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information (MI) to encourage skill diversity. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baselines on benchmark sparse-reward tasks (Duan et al., 2016), showing that their approach enables more efficient exploration and faster learning.

## Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper tackles a well-motivated problem in RL: efficient exploration in sparse reward settings, where naive exploration strategies fail and domain-specific hand-engineering of hierarchies is impractical. The authors provide a strong literature review that correctly situates their work within the context of hierarchical RL, intrinsic motivation, and skill learning, distinguishing their approach from related work (e.g., HiREPS, Option-critic, Heess et al. 2016).

The core contribution—using SNNs with MI regularization to learn multiple skills simultaneously—is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The authors correctly identify that training separate policies for each skill is sample-inefficient, while SNNs with weight-sharing provide a more efficient alternative. The MI regularizer is a particularly clever addition to address the common problem of SNNs collapsing to a single mode. The ablation studies (Fig. 4) convincingly demonstrate that bilinear integration combined with MI regularization yields the most diverse and interpretable skills.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. The authors compare against relevant baselines, including a strong intrinsic motivation baseline (VIME), and provide detailed analysis of the skill diversity (visitation plots in Fig. 4). The hierarchical use of skills significantly improves exploration (Fig. 5), and the downstream tasks (Maze, Gather) demonstrate clear performance gains (Fig. 6). The paper also includes thorough analysis of the method's scalability to more complex robots (Snake) and discusses limitations with unstable agents (Ant).

## Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify computational efficiency**: The paper states that SNNs reduce sample complexity by a factor equal to the number of skills, but doesn't quantify the exact computational savings. A brief discussion of the trade-off between sample efficiency and computational cost would strengthen the argument.

2. **Address randomness in pre-training**: The paper notes significant performance variation based on random seeds in pre-training (Section C.4). The authors should discuss this sensitivity more thoroughly and suggest potential solutions (e.g., ensemble methods or improved pre-training strategies) rather than simply noting it as a limitation.

3. **Expand discussion of MI regularizer**: The paper states that the MI regularizer "does not always boost performance" (Section 7.3). A more detailed analysis of when and why the MI bonus is beneficial would help readers understand the conditions under which this component is most valuable.

4. **Strengthen comparison with Heess et al. (2016)**: While the paper mentions Heess et al. (2016) as similar work, it doesn't provide a thorough comparison. A brief discussion of how this work differs in terms of pre-training requirements (single proxy reward vs. specified goals) would better position the contribution.

## Clarifying Questions for Authors

1. The paper uses a proxy reward that is "proportional to the magnitude of the speed of the robot" for locomotion tasks. How sensitive is the approach to the exact form of the proxy reward? Could the framework work with a different proxy reward (e.g., based on different aspects of movement), or is the specific choice of proxy reward critical to the success of the approach?

2. In cases where a pre-trained SNN lacks a specific skill needed for a downstream task (e.g., backward motion for Maze 0), what strategies could be employed without retraining the entire SNN? Could the high-level policy adaptively select skills from a subset of available skills, or would this require additional mechanisms?

3. The MI regularizer (αH) has a significant impact on skill diversity (Fig. 4). Could you provide more insight into how to choose the optimal αH for different environments, and whether there are theoretical bounds on its value?

## Conclusion

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The authors present a well-motivated, novel approach to learning diverse skills with minimal supervision, and provide compelling empirical evidence for its effectiveness. While the paper has minor weaknesses in addressing the sensitivity to random seeds and the exact computational trade-offs, these are easily addressable in revision. The framework has clear potential for broader application in robotics and other domains requiring efficient exploration in sparse reward settings. I believe this work would be of significant interest to the ICLR community.
