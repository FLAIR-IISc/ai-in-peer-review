

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a span of skills in a pre-training environment using a proxy reward signal, then leverages these skills for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors employ Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies, introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage skill diversity, and demonstrate that this approach can learn interpretable skills that improve exploration in challenging sparse reward tasks. The paper claims this method reduces sample complexity by learning a diverse set of skills that can be reused across multiple tasks.

## 2. Decision: Reject
The paper makes a compelling claim but fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to support its novelty and advantages over established methods. The core contribution appears to be the application of known techniques (SNNs and MI regularization) to a specific problem, rather than a fundamentally new method with rigorous validation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The primary reason for rejection is the paper's insufficient empirical validation. While the authors present promising results on a few simple environments (Swimmer in Maze and Gather tasks), they fail to provide adequate comparison against established baselines that would validate their claims. Specifically:

- The paper compares primarily to a baseline that simply adds the same proxy reward to the downstream task, rather than to established hierarchical RL methods like Option-Critic, HiREPS, or Heess et al. (2016) which also learn skills for hierarchical reinforcement learning. This comparison does not properly situate their work in the literature.

- The paper claims their approach "reduces sample complexity uniformly" but provides no rigorous comparison of sample efficiency against the standard approach of training multiple independent policies (which they acknowledge is less sample efficient). The paper states that "the number of samples used to train a SNN is the same as a single feed-forward policy," but doesn't compare the total samples required to learn multiple skills between their approach and the standard approach.

- The paper lacks statistical significance testing for their results. While they mention "10 runs (2 random seeds for 5 different SNNs)," they don't provide statistical tests to demonstrate that the observed improvements are significant.

- The paper's claim that their MI regularizer "consistently yields a large span of skills" is supported primarily by visualizations (Fig. 4) rather than quantitative metrics of skill diversity. The paper doesn't provide any metrics (e.g., entropy of skill distribution, skill distinctiveness scores) to objectively measure the quality of the span of skills learned.

- The paper's experiments are limited to a single robot (Swimmer) and a small number of environments. The claim that this approach "can be applied to a wide collection of different tasks" isn't properly validated, as the paper only demonstrates results on a few simple maze environments and a single Gather task.

The paper's main contribution appears to be the application of known techniques (SNNs and MI regularization) to hierarchical RL, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this combination provides significant advantages over established methods for the task. The authors cite Neal (1990, 1992) and Tang & Salakhutdinov (2013) as prior work on SNNs, yet the paper doesn't provide evidence that these techniques are novel for skill learning in RL.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a more thorough comparison against established hierarchical RL methods (Option-Critic, HiREPS, Heess et al. 2016) rather than just comparing to a simple proxy reward baseline.

2. Provide quantitative metrics of skill diversity and span (beyond visualizations) to objectively demonstrate that their approach learns more diverse skills.

3. Include statistical significance testing for all results to demonstrate that observed improvements are not due to random chance.

4. Expand the experimental evaluation to include more complex environments and robots (e.g., Ant, Humanoid) to better demonstrate scalability and generalizability.

5. Clarify the relationship between their approach and the Heess et al. (2016) work, which also learns skills for downstream tasks. The paper states that their pre-training setup "requires a single proxy reward as the only signal," while Heess et al. "requires a set of goals to be specified." However, the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how their approach is fundamentally different or better than Heess et al.'s.

6. Address the limitation that their approach requires a pre-training environment, which might not be feasible in all settings. How does the paper handle cases where such a pre-training environment is unavailable?

7. Provide a clearer explanation of why SNNs are necessary for this task rather than simpler approaches like training multiple independent policies (with the same total sample budget) and using a simple ensemble.

I would be happy to reconsider this paper if the authors could provide more rigorous validation of their claims through thorough comparisons with established baselines, statistical significance testing, and expanded experimental evaluation. As it stands, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and effectiveness.
