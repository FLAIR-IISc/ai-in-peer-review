

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of efficient exploration in sparse-reward environments. The authors introduce a two-phase approach: first, learning a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and second, leveraging these skills in a hierarchical architecture to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key technical contributions are the use of Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously, and an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage diversity of behaviors during pre-training. The paper demonstrates that this approach yields interpretable skills and significantly improves exploration and learning efficiency in challenging sparse-reward tasks.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with a well-motivated approach that effectively addresses the problem of efficient exploration in sparse-reward environments. The key reasons for acceptance are the technical soundness of the approach and the compelling empirical results demonstrating clear advantages over baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses a meaningful problem in reinforcement learning. The motivation for the work is clear: standard exploration strategies perform poorly in sparse-reward settings, and existing hierarchical approaches either require domain-specific knowledge or lack transferability across tasks. The authors properly situate their work within the literature, correctly identifying the limitations of prior approaches and differentiating their method (particularly the use of a single proxy reward signal without goal specifications).

The technical approach is sound and well-justified. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is appropriate, and the architectural choices (bilinear integration over concatenation, MI regularizer) are carefully selected and validated through ablation studies. The MI regularizer is well-motivated by prior work on InfoGAN and effectively encourages diversity of skills without complicating the optimization process. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including multiple robot models (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) and diverse sparse-reward tasks (Mazes, Gather), with appropriate baselines for comparison.

The empirical results strongly support the paper's claims. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the importance of the bilinear integration and MI regularizer for learning diverse, interpretable skills. The hierarchical use of these skills significantly improves exploration (as shown in visitation plots) and leads to faster learning in downstream tasks (as shown in learning curves). The results are statistically robust, with multiple random seeds reported and careful analysis of variance across different pretrained SNNs. The comparison to strong baselines, including intrinsic motivation approaches like VIME, demonstrates clear advantages of the proposed framework.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proxy reward design. While the paper mentions the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," it would be helpful to provide specific guidelines for designing the proxy reward for different task domains.
- A brief theoretical analysis of why the MI regularizer improves skill diversity (beyond empirical evidence) would strengthen the paper. While the paper focuses on empirical results, a short theoretical justification could enhance the contribution.
- The connection to InfoGAN could be elaborated upon, particularly regarding the similarities and differences in how mutual information is used for representation learning versus skill learning.
- The paper mentions "theoretical" results but doesn't provide any formal analysis. Clarifying whether the focus is purely empirical or whether there are theoretical guarantees would help readers understand the scope of the contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How does the choice of proxy reward affect the diversity and quality of skills learned? Are there specific guidelines for designing the proxy reward for different types of downstream tasks?
- The paper shows that the MI regularizer improves skill diversity, but does it have any negative effects on the quality of individual skills? For example, does it make some skills less effective at their primary task?
- In the Ant robot experiments, the authors mention instability during skill switching. How does this relate to the choice of switch time T, and are there principled ways to choose T for different environments?
