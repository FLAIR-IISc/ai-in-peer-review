

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, then leverages these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors claim that their approach significantly reduces sample complexity compared to naive exploration strategies, and that the SNN-based method with mutual information (MI) regularization consistently yields a wider span of interpretable skills than training separate policies. The framework is evaluated on several sparse-reward tasks including mazes and a food collection task, with results showing improved performance over baselines that use naive exploration or simple intrinsic motivation.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed SNN-based approach offers meaningful advantages over established methods, and the core technical innovation lacks theoretical grounding and thorough empirical validation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary issue with this paper is its lack of rigorous validation against established baselines and failure to convincingly demonstrate the necessity of the proposed unconventional approach. While the paper identifies a genuine challenge in sparse-reward reinforcement learning, it proposes a method that is both technically unorthodox and insufficiently validated.

First, the paper's core innovation—using SNNs for skill learning—lacks a compelling justification. The authors cite historical work on SNNs (Neal, 1990; 1992; Tang & Salakhutdinov, 2013) but fail to explain why SNNs are uniquely suited for this task compared to more established approaches like Option-Critic (Bacon & Precup, 2016) or the recently proposed HiREPS (Daniel et al., 2012). The paper acknowledges that "direct application of SNNs does not always guarantee that a wide range of skills will be learned," which suggests that the approach is inherently unstable without the proposed MI regularizer. This is a red flag for a methodological innovation—it shouldn't require such an ad-hoc fix to work at all.

Second, the empirical validation is insufficiently thorough. The paper evaluates on only a few tasks (Swimmer, Snake, and Ant) and a limited number of maze configurations. The results in Figure 6 show modest improvements over baselines, but the paper fails to provide statistical significance tests for these results. More critically, the paper doesn't demonstrate that the SNN approach is significantly better than simpler alternatives that could achieve similar skill diversity without the complexity of SNNs. For instance, the paper doesn't compare against a straightforward multi-policy approach (where each policy is trained independently) with the same number of skills, which would be a natural baseline for the claim that "the sample complexity of training K skills is effectively K times better."

The paper also makes a misleading claim about sample efficiency: "the sample efficiency of training K skills is effectively K times better." This is not supported by the data. The paper states that the SNN is trained with the same number of samples as a single policy, but it doesn't show that the SNN learns a comparable or better span of skills than training K separate policies with the same total sample count. In fact, the paper's own Figure 4(b) suggests that training six separate policies (which would require six times more samples) yields comparable skill diversity to the SNN approach, undermining the claimed sample efficiency advantage.

Finally, the MI regularization component lacks theoretical grounding. The paper introduces an MI bonus to encourage skill diversity but provides no principled justification for this specific form of regularization or why it's better than other methods for achieving diversity (e.g., using a diversity loss as in some recent work). The paper also fails to demonstrate that the MI regularizer is necessary—would the same skill diversity be achieved with a simpler approach like adding entropy regularization to the latent code?

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve this paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough comparison against established hierarchical RL methods, particularly Option-Critic and HiREPS. The paper claims to be "similar" to Heess et al. (2016), but this comparison is superficial and doesn't address the key differences.

2. Conduct a proper ablation study showing that each component of their method (SNNs, MI regularization) is necessary. For example, show that a standard multi-policy approach with the same number of skills (trained with the same total samples) performs comparably to the SNN approach.

3. Provide statistical significance tests for all performance claims. The paper's results show variability across runs (as noted in Section 7.3), but doesn't address whether the observed differences are statistically meaningful.

4. Clarify the sample complexity claim. If the SNN approach truly reduces sample complexity by a factor of K, the paper should demonstrate this by training K separate policies with the same total sample count as the SNN and showing comparable or better skill diversity.

5. Address the instability issue with skill switching (Section D.2) more thoroughly. The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but doesn't provide any solutions or discussion of how this impacts practical deployment.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that the SNN approach "effectively reduces the sample complexity by a factor equal to the numbers of skills being learned." Can you provide evidence that the SNN achieves comparable skill diversity to training K separate policies with the same total number of samples, rather than the same number of samples per policy?

2. The paper shows in Figure 7.1 that training six separate policies (with the same total samples as the SNN) yields comparable skill diversity. If this is true, why is the SNN approach necessary? Why not simply train multiple policies directly?

3. The MI regularization appears to be crucial for obtaining diverse skills, but the paper doesn't provide a theoretical justification for why this specific form of regularization works. How does the MI bonus differ from other approaches to encouraging diversity (e.g., entropy regularization on the latent code)?

4. The paper states that "the span of skills generated is also richer" with the SNN approach. What metrics were used to quantify "richer" skill diversity, and how do these metrics compare to those used in other skill discovery papers?
