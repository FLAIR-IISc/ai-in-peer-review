- Addresses learning reusable skills for multiple sparse-reward tasks via pre-training diverse skills with minimal supervision and hierarchical reuse (SNN + MI regularizer).
- Introduces Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) as a unified, weight-sharing policy architecture that can represent multi-modal skills.
- Proposes a Mutual Information-based regularizer to encourage diversity of learned skills during pre-training.
- Shows that the framework improves exploration and sample efficiency on challenging sparse-reward tasks.
- Decision: Accept — paper makes a strong, novel contribution to hierarchical RL.
- Key acceptance reasons: identifies a clear gap (learning diverse, reusable skills without task-specific supervision) and provides a sound, well-justified methodology.
- Empirical support includes comprehensive ablation studies demonstrating effects of architectural choices (bilinear vs. concatenation) and the MI bonus.
- Exploration analysis visually demonstrates better coverage from the hierarchical approach versus naive exploration.
- Learning curves show consistent, significant improvements on downstream tasks and outperform baselines while requiring fewer pre-training samples.
- Rigorous experimental design: careful proxy reward setup, multiple ablations, evaluations across maze configurations and Gather task, and analysis of failure modes (e.g., Ant).
- Suggestion: clarify the relationship between the MI regularizer and prior work like InfoGAN to highlight novelty.
- Suggestion: discuss scalability and computational complexity as the number of skills K increases.
- Suggestion: more precisely define what is meant by “minimal domain knowledge” with concrete examples.
- Question: how does the pre-training approach generalize to non-locomotion tasks (e.g., manipulation involving grasping or object placement)?
- Question: when and why does the MI regularizer help or not help across different tasks/mazes?
- Question: what specific advantages would end-to-end training provide over frozen low-level policies, and how might it affect skill diversity?
