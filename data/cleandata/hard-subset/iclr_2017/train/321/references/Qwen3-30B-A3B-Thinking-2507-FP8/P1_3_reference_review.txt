

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards in complex tasks. The authors introduce a two-stage approach: first, a pre-training phase where a span of diverse skills is learned using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a proxy reward signal requiring minimal domain knowledge; second, a downstream phase where high-level policies are trained to select these pre-learned skills for specific tasks. The key innovation lies in using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing, and introducing an information-theoretic regularizer (based on mutual information) to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate that their approach learns interpretable skills that significantly improve exploration in downstream tasks with sparse rewards, outperforming baselines that use naive exploration or intrinsic rewards without skill transfer.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing an effective, sample-efficient method for learning reusable skills with minimal supervision. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the approach is well-motivated and addresses a clear limitation in prior work (lack of skill transfer across tasks without domain knowledge), and (2) the empirical results are compelling, showing clear improvements in exploration and sample efficiency across multiple tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's contribution is well-placed in the literature. The authors thoroughly review related work on hierarchical RL, skill learning, and intrinsic motivation, clearly positioning their approach as a solution to the limitations of both hand-engineered hierarchies and task-specific intrinsic rewards. The paper effectively argues that their framework enables skill transfer across tasks without requiring the same domain knowledge as previous approaches.

The empirical validation is strong and scientifically rigorous. The authors conduct comprehensive experiments on two benchmark tasks (Maze and Gather) with multiple variations, including ablation studies on key design choices (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus strength). The visitation plots in Section 7.1 provide compelling visual evidence of the diversity and interpretability of skills learned. The learning curves in Section 7.3 clearly demonstrate that the hierarchical approach with SNNs significantly outperforms baselines, particularly in the Gather task where the MI bonus leads to more consistent learning. The authors also acknowledge limitations (e.g., with unstable robots like Ant) and provide analysis on the impact of hyperparameters like the switch time T.

The paper's methodology is well-justified. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing is a natural fit for skill learning, and the information-theoretic regularizer effectively encourages diversity without requiring additional supervision. The choice of TRPO as the optimization algorithm is reasonable given its robustness in continuous control tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but could be strengthened in several areas:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the MI regularizer, a brief theoretical discussion of why maximizing I(Z;C) encourages diverse skills would strengthen the contribution. How does the MI term specifically relate to the diversity of the skill space?

2. **Comparison to Related Work**: The paper references Heess et al. (2016) as having a similar framework, but doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs. A more detailed comparison would help readers understand the novelty.

3. **Broader Impact**: The paper could briefly discuss potential applications beyond robotics, such as in natural language processing or multi-agent systems, where hierarchical skill learning might be beneficial.

4. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions using "simple categorical distributions with uniform weights for the latent variables" but doesn't specify how the number of skills K was chosen. A discussion of how to select K for different tasks would be helpful.

5. **Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges limitations with unstable robots, it doesn't discuss how the framework might be adapted for continuous action spaces (beyond the discrete skill selection) or for tasks where the state factorization isn't known.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify some aspects of the paper and strengthen the submission:

1. The paper states that "the construction of which only requires minimal domain knowledge or instrumentation of the environment" for the proxy reward. Could you provide specific examples of how the proxy reward was designed for the Swimmer and Snake robots in your experiments, and how much domain knowledge was actually required?

2. In Section 7.3, the paper reports that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" in the Maze tasks. Could you elaborate on when the MI bonus was beneficial versus when it wasn't, and whether there's a pattern related to the specific maze structure?

3. The paper mentions that "the span of skills learned by the SNN" is a key advantage. Could you quantify the span of skills (e.g., number of distinct behaviors learned) and compare it to the multi-policy baseline? This would help readers understand the sample efficiency gains.

4. For the Ant robot (Section D), the paper mentions "switching between skills is a hard task because of the instability." Could you elaborate on how the failure modes could be mitigated without significantly increasing the complexity of the framework?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning with a well-motivated approach that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The authors have provided a compelling solution to a persistent challenge in RL, and the work is well-positioned to influence future research in the area. With the minor revisions suggested above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
