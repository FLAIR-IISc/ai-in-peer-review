

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer, then leverages these skills for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors claim their method reduces sample complexity by learning multiple skills simultaneously (via SNNs) and encourages skill diversity through a mutual information (MI) bonus. The approach is evaluated on maze navigation and food collection tasks, with results suggesting improved exploration and faster learning compared to baselines.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**. The paper contains a critical technical error that undermines the core contribution, and the experimental evidence fails to sufficiently support the claims made.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The most serious issue is a fundamental error in the MI bonus implementation. In Section 5.3, the authors define the reward modification as:

Rnt ← Rnt + αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt)

However, log p̂(Z = zn|cnt) is negative (since p̂ is a probability between 0 and 1), meaning this modification would *decrease* the reward rather than increase it. The authors should have used Rnt ← Rnt - αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt) to obtain a positive bonus. This is a critical error that would prevent the method from working as intended. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) and all experimental results are based on this incorrect implementation, making the entire empirical evaluation questionable.

Beyond this critical error, I identified several other issues that further undermine the paper's claims:

1. **Lack of clear definition of "span of skills"**: The paper repeatedly uses the term "span" without defining it, making it impossible to quantify or verify the claimed improvements in skill diversity.

2. **Misrepresentation of the baseline comparison**: The paper claims their method "effectively reduces the sample complexity by a factor equal to the numbers of skills being learned," but they only trained a single SNN (using the same samples as a single policy) rather than comparing to training K separate policies (which would require K times more samples).

3. **Inconsistent experimental reporting**: The paper states "the MI bonus yields less overlapped skills" but provides no quantitative metrics to support this claim—only qualitative visual comparisons in Figures 4(c)-4(d).

4. **Inadequate explanation of key design choices**: The paper doesn't justify why they chose K=6 for the number of skills, or why they used bilinear integration instead of other methods. The statement "our bilinear integration already yields a large span of skills, hence no other type of SNNs is studied in this work" is not sufficient justification.

5. **Formatting and reference errors**: The reference list contains numerous LaTeX compilation errors (e.g., "Duan et al.,? \Q2016\E"), inconsistent citation styles, and typos like "Mulit-policy" instead of "Multi-policy." These are unprofessional and undermine the paper's credibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. **Correct the critical technical error**: Recast the MI bonus as Rnt ← Rnt - αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt) and re-run all experiments to verify the results.

2. **Define and quantify "span of skills"**: Provide a clear metric for skill diversity (e.g., pairwise distance between skill trajectories) and report quantitative results rather than relying solely on visual comparisons.

3. **Clarify the sample complexity claim**: Compare training a single SNN (with K skills) against training K separate policies (which would require K times the sample size), rather than comparing to a single policy.

4. **Address the high variance in results**: The paper notes high variance across random seeds (Section 7.3) but doesn't analyze why. Provide statistical significance tests for the main claims.

5. **Fix formatting and reference issues**: Correct all LaTeX errors in the reference list and ensure consistent terminology (e.g., "pre-training" vs. "pre-train").

6. **Explain the proxy reward more thoroughly**: The paper claims the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," but the speed-based reward for the swimmer robot clearly requires knowing that locomotion speed is a useful proxy for skills.

7. **Provide more details about the MI calculation**: Explain why CoM coordinates were chosen as the relevant state subset, and how the discretization into cells affects the results.

**Questions for Authors**:
1. How would the corrected MI bonus implementation (with a negative sign) affect the experimental results shown in Figures 4-6?
2. Can you provide quantitative metrics for "skill diversity" to support the claim that the MI bonus yields "less overlapped skills"?
3. What is the actual sample complexity comparison between training a single SNN with K skills versus training K separate policies?
4. Why was K=6 chosen? How does this choice affect the results?
5. Can you provide the statistical significance of the performance improvements claimed in Section 7.3?
