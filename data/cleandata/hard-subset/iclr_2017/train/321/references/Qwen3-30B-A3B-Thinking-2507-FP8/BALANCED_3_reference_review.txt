

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer. The authors argue that this approach can solve tasks with sparse rewards by learning skills in a pre-training phase using a proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), then reusing these skills in downstream tasks. The key contributions include: (1) using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing; (2) introducing a mutual information regularizer to encourage skill diversity; and (3) demonstrating that these skills can be effectively reused across downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach is evaluated on locomotion and food collection tasks, showing improved exploration and faster learning compared to baselines.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The primary reasons are: (1) the technical novelty is limited as the core approach builds on existing methods with insufficient differentiation from prior work; (2) the experimental evaluation is insufficiently thorough to support the claims of broad applicability; and (3) the paper fails to clearly articulate how it improves upon the most similar prior work (Heess et al., 2016).

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution—using SNNs for skill learning with MI regularization—lacks sufficient novelty. The application of SNNs to RL for multi-modal policies is not new (e.g., InfoGAN uses similar concepts for representation learning, and Option-critic architecture also learns interpretable skills). The MI regularization is inspired by InfoGAN, but the paper does not convincingly demonstrate why this particular approach is better than alternatives for skill diversity. The authors claim "minimal domain knowledge" for the proxy reward, but the reward design (e.g., "speed of the robot" or "successful grasping") actually requires domain knowledge about what constitutes useful behavior, which is not substantially less than what other methods require.

The experimental evaluation is inadequate for supporting the paper's claims. The authors evaluate only on a single robot type (Swimmer) with limited maze variations, despite claiming to work for "a wide collection of tasks." The Snake and Ant experiments are mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed, with the Ant results showing clear failure modes that aren't addressed. The paper lacks a proper ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component (SNN structure, MI regularization, hierarchical architecture) to final performance. The comparison to Heess et al. (2016) is superficial—they acknowledge similarity but don't clearly explain how their approach improves upon it.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Strengthen novelty claims**: Provide a more detailed comparison with Heess et al. (2016), explicitly stating how your approach differs and why it's better. Quantify the improvements in terms of sample efficiency, skill diversity, or task coverage.
- **Expand experimental evaluation**: Include more diverse environments (e.g., different robot morphologies, more complex navigation tasks) to better demonstrate the generality of the approach. The current evaluation is too narrow.
- **Improve ablation studies**: Conduct a thorough ablation to isolate the contribution of each component (SNN architecture, MI regularizer, hierarchical structure). Show how much performance improvement comes from each element.
- **Address limitations**: Discuss the failure modes with unstable robots (Ant) more thoroughly. Explain how the approach might be modified to handle such cases, rather than simply acknowledging them in an appendix.
- **Clarify "minimal domain knowledge"**: Explicitly state what specific domain knowledge is required for the proxy reward and compare it quantitatively to other methods.

## 5. Questions for the Authors
1. How does the proxy reward signal used in the pre-training phase differ from the intrinsic reward used in previous work (e.g., Houthooft et al., 2016)? What specific domain knowledge is required to design the proxy reward, and how does this compare to the domain knowledge required for other methods?
2. The paper claims the MI bonus consistently yields a larger span of skills, but Figure 4(d) shows some latent codes still produce similar behaviors (e.g., codes 1 and 2). How does the authors quantify skill diversity, and what is the threshold for considering skills to be "distinct"?
3. The paper mentions "high variance" in performance due to different pre-trained SNNs. How does the authors plan to address this issue in practice, and what is the expected performance impact if a randomly selected pre-trained SNN lacks the required skills for a task?
4. How does the proposed approach scale to tasks requiring more than 6 skills (K > 6)? The paper doesn't discuss how performance might degrade as the number of skills increases.
