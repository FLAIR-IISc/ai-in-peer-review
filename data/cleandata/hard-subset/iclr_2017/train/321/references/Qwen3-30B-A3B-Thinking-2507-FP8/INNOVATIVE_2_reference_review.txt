

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards by learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment with minimal supervision. The key innovation is using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously, rather than training separate policies for each skill. The authors introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage diversity in the learned skills. The framework is evaluated on several benchmark tasks with sparse rewards, demonstrating significant improvements over baselines that use naive exploration strategies or directly apply the proxy reward to downstream tasks. The paper provides clear ablation studies showing the importance of key components like bilinear integration and the MI bonus.

## Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a genuinely novel and bold approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses a fundamental challenge in the field. The core idea of using SNNs to learn a span of skills with minimal supervision is sufficiently different from existing approaches and represents a significant conceptual advancement.

## Supporting Arguments
This paper stands out for its conceptual novelty and boldness, which I prioritize highly as a reviewer. The authors propose a fundamentally new way to learn skills for hierarchical RL that doesn't require domain-specific knowledge for skill specification or external rewards for skill learning. Instead, they use a simple proxy reward (e.g., robot speed) and an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity. This is a significant departure from existing approaches that either require hand-engineered hierarchies (Parr & Russell, 1998; Sutton et al., 1999) or rely on external rewards for skill learning (Mnih et al., 2016).

The paper's approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, with a thorough review of related work that clearly identifies the gaps the paper addresses. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is theoretically justified and supported by strong empirical results. The ablation studies (Figs. 4-5) convincingly demonstrate the importance of the bilinear integration and MI bonus for learning diverse, interpretable skills.

The empirical results are rigorous, with multiple runs, different seeds, and careful analysis of the impact of architectural choices. The paper shows clear improvements over baselines on challenging sparse reward tasks, with the SNN-based approach significantly outperforming methods that use naive exploration or apply the proxy reward directly to downstream tasks. The videos and visualizations (Fig. 4) effectively demonstrate the interpretability of the learned skills.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. A more detailed discussion of the relationship between this work and Heess et al. (2016), which also proposes learning skills in a pre-training environment. The paper mentions this work but doesn't fully clarify how the current approach differs in terms of requirements (Heess requires goal specification, while the current method uses a single proxy reward).
2. A brief discussion of how the approach might scale to more complex environments beyond the Swimmer and Snake robots, particularly regarding the computational complexity of the MI estimation as the state space grows.
3. More explicit discussion of the theoretical guarantees (if any) for the diversity of skills achieved with the MI regularizer.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the framework handle cases where the proxy reward is less intuitive or harder to design? For example, how would you adapt this approach for a manipulation task where the proxy reward isn't as straightforward as "robot speed"?
2. The paper shows that the performance on Maze 0 depends strongly on the specific pretrained SNN (Fig. 12). What are the implications of this variability for real-world applications, and how might this be mitigated?
3. The paper mentions that the MI regularizer sometimes doesn't boost performance (Fig. 6). Why does this happen, and are there conditions under which the MI regularizer is particularly beneficial?
