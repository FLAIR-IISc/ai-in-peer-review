

# Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a framework for learning policies across multiple tasks with sparse rewards by first learning a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using minimal domain knowledge. The authors introduce Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies, where latent variables serve as skill identifiers. To encourage diverse skill learning, they propose an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information between the latent variable and state. The learned skills are then used as building blocks for high-level policies that solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The framework demonstrates improved exploration and faster learning in challenging environments compared to baselines, with experiments on Swimmer, Snake, and Ant robots across maze navigation and object gathering tasks.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a compelling contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with clear technical soundness and empirical validation. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the approach addresses a meaningful problem in RL with sparse rewards that has limited effective solutions, and (2) the experimental results provide strong evidence that the proposed method improves exploration and learning efficiency compared to baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper tackles a significant challenge in reinforcement learning: efficiently learning policies for tasks with sparse rewards. The authors clearly motivate their approach by highlighting limitations of existing methods (hand-engineered hierarchies requiring domain knowledge, or intrinsic reward approaches that don't provide transferable knowledge). Their solution is well-placed in the literature, building on prior work on skill learning while addressing its limitations.

The technical approach is sound. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is well-justified, and the authors provide a clear architectural design with bilinear integration of latent variables. The information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information is a reasonable approach to encourage skill diversity, and the authors provide empirical evidence that it improves the span of skills learned. The methodology for both pre-training and downstream task learning is clearly described with appropriate algorithmic details.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies on different design choices (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus magnitude) and demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple robots (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) and tasks (mazes, gather). The visitation plots and learning curves provide clear visual evidence of the benefits of their approach. The paper also acknowledges limitations and provides valuable analysis of failure modes (particularly for unstable robots like Ant), which strengthens the scientific rigor.

The paper's contributions are clearly demonstrated through multiple experiments with appropriate baselines, including a Multi-policy architecture that uses six times more samples for pretraining. The results show that the SNN approach with MI bonus consistently learns a wider span of skills than baselines, and that this leads to significantly improved exploration and learning in downstream tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from more quantitative measures of skill diversity beyond visual inspection of visitation plots. Metrics like skill overlap or entropy of skill distribution would strengthen the claims about diversity.
- The relationship between the proxy reward design and skill transferability could be more thoroughly explored. While the paper mentions "minimal domain knowledge" is required, a deeper analysis of how different proxy rewards affect skill quality would be valuable.
- The discussion of the Ant robot's instability during skill switching is important but could be expanded with more specific recommendations for addressing this issue, particularly regarding the feasibility of learning a transition policy as suggested in the future work.
- The paper mentions "the span of skills generated is also richer" but doesn't provide specific metrics to quantify this improvement compared to baselines.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How sensitive is the performance of the framework to the choice of proxy reward? Could a poorly designed proxy reward lead to skills that are not useful for downstream tasks, and how might this be mitigated?
2. The paper shows that MI bonus improves skill diversity, but is there a theoretical basis for why the MI term specifically encourages diverse skills, or is this purely empirical?
3. In the experiments with the Snake robot, the authors increased the task size but kept the switch time T constant. How does the framework perform when T needs to be adjusted for different task complexities, and what factors should guide this adjustment?
4. For the Ant robot, the authors mention instability during skill switching. What specific changes to the framework (e.g., learning a transition policy) would most effectively address this issue, and how would these changes impact the sample complexity of the pre-training phase?
