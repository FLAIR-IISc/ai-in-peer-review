- Proposes a hierarchical RL framework that pre-trains diverse skills in a proxy-reward environment and reuses them for downstream sparse-reward tasks.  
- Uses Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously.  
- Introduces a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage and maintain skill diversity.  
- Designs a simple hierarchical architecture where a Manager policy selects pre-trained skills for downstream tasks.  
- Demonstrates improved exploration and sample efficiency on sparse-reward tasks (mazes, object gathering) with minimal pre-training domain knowledge.  
- Decision: Accept — the paper makes a compelling contribution to hierarchical RL for sparse rewards.  
- Acceptance reason: well-motivated problem addressing limitations of naive exploration in sparse reward settings.  
- Acceptance reason: method builds on prior work while addressing key limitations and is well-placed in the literature.  
- Acceptance reason: strong empirical results with thorough ablations and failure-mode analysis.  
- Methodology strength: bilinear integration with categorical latent codes yields more expressive skill representations than simple concatenation.  
- Methodology strength: MI regularizer is well-motivated (InfoGAN-style) and effectively encourages distinct skills.  
- Hierarchical design is simple, effective, and shown to improve exploration vs naive baselines; ablations validate components.  
- Results: comprehensive experiments showing skill diversity, improved exploration, downstream performance gains, robustness to seeds, and failure-mode analysis.  
- Baseline comparisons are fair, including the proxy-reward downstream baseline which performs poorly due to long horizons.  
- Limitation: switching skills is challenging for unstable robots (Ant); authors analyze this failure mode.  
- Limitation: proxy reward still requires some minimal domain knowledge (e.g., speed for locomotion).  
- Area for improvement: broader evaluation across more robot morphologies and environments to establish generality.  
- Area for improvement: quantify skill diversity with formal metrics (e.g., normalized mutual information between latent codes and behaviors).  
- Area for improvement: more detailed comparison with related work such as Heess et al. (2016).  
- Area for improvement: provide analysis of computational complexity of training SNNs vs training multiple separate policies.  
- Question for authors: clarify how "minimal" the domain knowledge for proxy rewards is and give examples where it may be hard to obtain.  
- Question for authors: have you considered integrating skill switching into pre-training to help unstable robots?  
- Question for authors: would adaptive/learned switching times T (instead of fixed T) benefit tasks with different dynamics?  
- Question for authors: why does the MI regularizer sometimes not boost maze-task performance despite increasing diversity?  
- Conclusion: strong, well-written paper presenting an effective, reusable skill-learning framework that merits acceptance.
