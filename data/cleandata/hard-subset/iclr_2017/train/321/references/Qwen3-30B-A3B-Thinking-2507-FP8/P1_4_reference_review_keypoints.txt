- Proposed method: learn diverse, reusable skills via Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a mutual-information (MI) regularizer during pre-training using only a simple proxy reward.  
- Outcome: enables faster learning on multiple downstream sparse-reward tasks and yields interpretable skills (e.g., forward/backward/sideways locomotion).  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Key merit 1: Tackles efficient exploration in sparse-reward RL with a well-motivated bridge between hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation.  
- Key merit 2: Technical novelty of SNNs + MI regularization for skill diversity is well-justified and positioned in the literature.  
- Writing and context: Clear problem motivation, comprehensive related work, and good exposition.  
- Architecture insight: SNNs provide multi-modal policies with weight sharing; bilinear integration + MI bonus outperforms concatenation/no-MI alternatives.  
- Empirical strength: Thorough experiments (maze navigation, food collection) with appropriate baselines and ablations showing improved exploration and faster learning.  
- Interpretability & reproducibility: Visitation plots demonstrate distinct skills; code and videos available.  
- Ask for more: Detailed computational cost analysis (training/inference memory and time) compared to baselines.  
- Ask for more: Systematic hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for the MI bonus coefficient αH.  
- Ask for more: Discussion of scalability to more complex agents/tasks (e.g., Ant) and challenges/solutions.  
- Ask for more: Analysis of when and why the MI bonus does not help (e.g., Maze 0).  
- Q1 for authors: Why does the MI bonus sometimes fail to boost performance—task-specific or skill-related causes?  
- Q2 for authors: How might the method be extended to dynamic tasks and unstable robots like Ant?  
- Q3 for authors: How can high variance across random seeds in pre-trained SNNs be reduced, and what are implications for deployment?  
- Q4 for authors: Would end-to-end training with straight-through estimators affect the interpretability of learned skills?
