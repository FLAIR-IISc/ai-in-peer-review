

# Peer Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that addresses the challenge of sparse reward environments with long horizons. The authors' key contribution is a two-phase approach: (1) a pre-training phase that learns a diverse span of skills using a minimal proxy reward signal, and (2) a hierarchical structure that reuses these skills for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors leverage Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies, with an information-theoretic regularizer (based on Mutual Information) to encourage skill diversity. Their method demonstrates improved exploration and faster learning on challenging sparse-reward tasks (Maze and Gather) compared to baselines, including approaches that use intrinsic rewards. The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple robot environments, with a focus on interpretability of the learned skills through visitation plots.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the HRL literature with a well-motivated, empirically validated approach that addresses a key challenge in reinforcement learning (sparse rewards with long horizons). The core idea of using SNNs with an MI regularizer for skill diversity is both novel and effective, and the results provide strong evidence for the approach's superiority over relevant baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper addresses a well-defined problem (learning in sparse reward environments) with a compelling solution that is well-positioned in the literature. The authors clearly situate their work relative to prior approaches: (a) hand-engineered hierarchies that require domain knowledge, and (b) intrinsic reward approaches that don't enable transfer across tasks. Their framework effectively bridges these gaps by learning transferable skills with minimal domain knowledge.

The paper's claims are strongly supported by rigorous experiments. The authors provide:
- Comprehensive ablation studies showing the impact of key design choices (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus)
- Visual evidence of skill diversity through visitation plots
- Clear comparison against relevant baselines (including the standard intrinsic reward baseline)
- Analysis of the effect of different random seeds to demonstrate robustness

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous: they use appropriate benchmark tasks (Duan et al., 2016), report multiple runs to account for variance, and provide detailed analysis of performance across different environments. The paper demonstrates that their approach consistently outperforms baselines on Maze and Gather tasks, with the SNN approach (particularly with bilinear integration and MI bonus) showing clear advantages in exploration and learning efficiency.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Clarify the theoretical basis**: While the MI regularizer is well-motivated by InfoGAN, the paper could better explain why this specific form of regularization leads to diverse skills rather than just better representation. A brief theoretical discussion of how the MI term prevents mode collapse would strengthen the methodological contribution.

2. **Expand on limitations**: The paper acknowledges limitations with unstable robots (Ant) but could provide more concrete guidance on when this approach might fail and how to mitigate those failures. A brief discussion of the conditions under which the MI bonus is most beneficial would help readers understand the method's applicability.

3. **Improve clarity on sample complexity**: The paper claims to reduce sample complexity but only provides empirical evidence. A more precise discussion of the theoretical sample complexity benefits (beyond the empirical results) would strengthen the contribution.

4. **Include more quantitative diversity metrics**: While visitation plots are visually informative, adding quantitative metrics for skill diversity (e.g., entropy of skill distribution, distance between learned behaviors) would make the claims about "diverse span of skills" more concrete.

5. **Clarify the relationship to Option-Critic**: The paper references Option-Critic but doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs in terms of skill transferability. A more direct comparison would help position the work within the HRL literature.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "training a single SNN allows for flexible weight-sharing schemes among different policies" but doesn't elaborate on how this weight-sharing is implemented or what specific benefits it provides beyond sample efficiency. Could you clarify this aspect?

2. In Section 7.3, you report that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" on the Maze tasks. Could you elaborate on why the MI bonus sometimes appears to have no effect on Maze performance (despite improving skill diversity in the pre-training phase)?

3. The paper uses a fixed switch time T in the hierarchical structure. While you analyze the effect of T in Appendix C.3, could you comment on how this choice might impact the applicability of your method to tasks requiring more dynamic switching between skills?

4. The paper states that "the span of skills learned by the SNN policy" can be used "in a wide collection of different tasks." What are the theoretical or empirical limits to the number of tasks that can be effectively solved with a single pre-trained skill set?
