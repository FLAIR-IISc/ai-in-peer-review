

# Peer Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sample efficiency in sparse reward tasks. The authors introduce a method to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information (MI). The key contribution is a unified approach that: (1) learns multiple skills simultaneously via SNNs with bilinear integration of latent variables, (2) encourages skill diversity through MI maximization between latent codes and state coordinates, and (3) leverages these pre-trained skills in a hierarchical architecture to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The framework requires minimal domain knowledge for the pre-training reward (e.g., speed for locomotion) and demonstrates significant improvements over baselines on maze navigation and object gathering tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning, with strong empirical validation and a well-motivated approach that bridges the gap between hand-engineered hierarchies and intrinsic motivation methods. The core idea of using SNNs with MI regularization to learn diverse skills is novel and well-executed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, thoughtful methodological choices, and thorough empirical evaluation:

- **Problem formulation**: The paper correctly identifies the limitations of existing approaches (hand-engineered hierarchies requiring domain knowledge, intrinsic reward methods not enabling transfer) and articulates a compelling solution that requires minimal domain knowledge for pre-training.

- **Methodological soundness**: The use of SNNs with bilinear integration is well-justified by prior work on representation power, and the MI regularizer is a clever application of InfoGAN principles to skill diversity. The ablation studies (Figs. 4-6) clearly demonstrate the impact of each component.

- **Empirical validation**: The experiments on Swimmer, Snake, and Ant robots across Maze and Gather tasks provide strong evidence that the approach improves exploration and sample efficiency. The comparison to baselines (including the CoM proxy reward baseline) is fair and well-designed.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that the skills learned are interpretable (Fig. 4), which is often lacking in RL work. The analysis of skill diversity (Fig. 4) and the discussion of how different skills help in different maze configurations (Fig. 6) provide important insights into why the approach works.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the "minimal domain knowledge" claim**: The paper states the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge," but for the swimmer robot, the reward is proportional to speed magnitude. This still requires knowledge that speed is a useful metric. The authors should more precisely define what constitutes "minimal domain knowledge" and provide examples where it's truly minimal (e.g., for a robot with no prior knowledge).

2. **Add theoretical justification for MI regularizer**: While the empirical results support the MI regularizer, a brief theoretical argument about why maximizing MI between latent codes and state coordinates encourages skill diversity would strengthen the methodological contribution.

3. **Address scalability to larger skill sets**: The paper uses K=6 skills consistently. For more complex tasks, larger K might be needed. The authors should discuss how their approach scales with K and whether the MI regularizer remains effective with larger numbers of skills.

4. **Include comparison to modern RL algorithms**: Given the focus on sample efficiency, it would be valuable to compare against more recent off-policy algorithms like SAC or TD3, which are known to be more sample efficient than TRPO.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims the pre-training environment requires "only a proxy reward signal, whose design only requires very minimal domain knowledge." For the Swimmer robot, the proxy reward is "proportional to the magnitude of the speed of the robot." How would this translate to a completely unknown robot with no prior knowledge of its dynamics? What specific domain knowledge is required for this proxy reward?

2. The MI regularizer is designed to maximize I(Z;C), where C is the CoM coordinates. Why was this particular state subset chosen rather than other state components (e.g., joint angles or velocities)? How would the approach generalize to tasks where such a clear state subset isn't available?

3. The paper shows that SNNs with MI regularization learn more diverse skills (Fig. 4), but doesn't quantify how this diversity translates to downstream task performance. Could the authors provide a metric that directly correlates skill diversity with downstream learning speed?

4. For the Ant robot (D.2), the paper notes instability when switching skills. The authors mention possible solutions in a technical report but don't include them in the paper. Could the authors summarize the most promising solution approach and discuss its potential limitations?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to hierarchical RL. With the suggested clarifications, it would be an excellent addition to ICLR.
