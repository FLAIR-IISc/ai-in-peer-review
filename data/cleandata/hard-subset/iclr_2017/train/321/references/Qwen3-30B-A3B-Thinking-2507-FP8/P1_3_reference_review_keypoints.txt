- Proposes a two-stage hierarchical RL framework: pretrain diverse skills with Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) using a proxy reward, then train high-level policies to select these skills for downstream tasks.  
- Key innovation: use SNNs with weight sharing to represent multi-modal policies and an information-theoretic (mutual information) regularizer to encourage skill diversity.  
- Claims that learned skills are interpretable and significantly improve exploration and sample efficiency on sparse-reward tasks, outperforming naive exploration and intrinsic-reward baselines.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions due to significant, sample-efficient contribution to hierarchical RL and compelling empirical results.  
- Well-positioned in literature; addresses limitations of hand-engineered hierarchies and task-specific intrinsic rewards and enables skill transfer with minimal domain knowledge.  
- Empirical validation is strong: experiments on Maze and Gather tasks, ablations (integration methods, MI strength), visitation plots, and learning curves showing clear advantages.  
- Methodology is justified: SNNs naturally fit skill learning, MI regularizer promotes diversity, and TRPO is an appropriate optimizer for continuous control.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., unstable robots like Ant) and analyze hyperparameter effects such as skill switch time T.  
- Suggest adding theoretical discussion on why maximizing I(Z;C) yields diverse skills to strengthen the contribution.  
- Request clearer comparison to Heess et al. (2016) to delineate novelty relative to similar frameworks.  
- Recommend a brief broader-impact discussion on applications beyond robotics (e.g., NLP, multi-agent systems).  
- Ask for implementation details: explain how the number of skills K was chosen and guidance for selecting K across tasks.  
- Note missing discussion on adapting the framework to continuous skill selection or tasks without known state factorization.  
- Question: provide concrete examples of proxy rewards used for Swimmer and Snake and quantify required domain knowledge.  
- Question: clarify when MI pretraining helps versus when it does not (e.g., specific Maze structures).  
- Question: quantify the span of skills learned (number of distinct behaviors) and compare to multi-policy baselines to illustrate sample efficiency gains.  
- Question: elaborate on failure modes for Ant switching and propose ways to mitigate instability without greatly increasing framework complexity.
