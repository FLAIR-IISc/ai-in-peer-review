

# Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward learning through skill transfer. The authors introduce a method that first pre-trains a diverse span of skills in a simple proxy-reward environment, then uses these skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovations include:

1. Using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously
2. Introducing an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage skill diversity
3. Creating a hierarchical architecture where a "Manager" policy selects skills for downstream tasks

The paper demonstrates that their approach significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency compared to standard RL methods on sparse reward tasks like mazes and object gathering, while requiring minimal domain knowledge for the pre-training phase.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses a meaningful problem with sparse rewards. The key reasons for acceptance are:

1. The problem is well-motivated and addresses a significant limitation in current RL approaches (naive exploration strategies failing in sparse reward settings)
2. The method is well-placed in the literature, building on prior work while addressing its limitations
3. The empirical results are strong and scientifically rigorous, with thorough ablation studies and analysis of failure modes

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Problem and Motivation (Strengths)
The paper clearly identifies the limitations of existing approaches: hierarchical methods requiring domain knowledge (Parr & Russell, 1998; Sutton et al., 1999) and intrinsic reward methods that don't provide transferable knowledge (Houthooft et al., 2016). The authors position their work as filling a critical gap by learning diverse skills with minimal supervision that can be reused across tasks. This is a well-motivated problem with significant practical relevance.

### Methodology (Strengths)
The SNN approach with bilinear integration and mutual information regularization is a clever solution to the problem of learning diverse skills. The key insight that using a categorical latent variable with bilinear integration allows for more expressive skill representations than simple concatenation is well-supported by the experiments. The information-theoretic regularizer is well-motivated by prior work (InfoGAN) and effectively encourages skill diversity.

The hierarchical architecture (Manager policy selecting from pre-trained skills) is simple yet effective, and the paper provides a clear explanation of how it improves exploration compared to naive methods. The ablation studies (Figs. 4-5) convincingly demonstrate the value of both the SNN architecture and the MI regularizer.

### Results (Strengths)
The experimental results are comprehensive and well-designed. The paper demonstrates:
- The diversity of skills learned (Fig. 4)
- The improved exploration enabled by the hierarchical structure (Fig. 5)
- The performance gains on downstream tasks (Fig. 6)
- The robustness to different random seeds (Fig. 12)
- Analysis of failure modes (Fig. 15)

The comparison to baselines is fair, including the important baseline of using the same proxy reward in the downstream task (which performs poorly due to the long time-horizon issue). The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms baselines, particularly on the maze tasks where the diverse skills (especially with MI regularization) provide tangible benefits.

### Limitations and Addressing Them
The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the difficulty of switching skills for unstable robots (Ant), and provides a thorough analysis of this failure mode. The authors also acknowledge that their proxy reward still requires some minimal domain knowledge (e.g., speed for locomotion), which is a reasonable trade-off for the method's simplicity.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

### Strengths to Highlight
- The paper's clear explanation of the skill diversity problem and how their method addresses it
- The thoughtful design of the MI regularizer to encourage distinct skills
- The thorough experimental analysis across multiple environments and robot types

### Areas for Improvement

1. **Broader Evaluation**: While the paper demonstrates results on Swimmer and Snake robots, it would be valuable to include a more diverse set of robot morphologies and environments to better establish the generality of the approach. The Ant robot analysis is good but limited to failure modes.

2. **Quantifying Skill Diversity**: The paper uses visitation plots to show skill diversity, but a more quantitative measure of diversity (e.g., normalized mutual information between latent codes and behaviors) would strengthen the claims about skill distinctiveness.

3. **Comparison to Related Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, a more detailed comparison with Heess et al. (2016) would help clarify the distinction between their approach and this independently proposed similar framework.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions sample efficiency benefits but doesn't quantify the computational complexity of training the SNN versus training multiple separate policies. A brief analysis of this would be helpful for practitioners.

### Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims to require "minimal domain knowledge" for the proxy reward. How minimal is "minimal" in practice? Could you provide examples of tasks where this minimal knowledge would be difficult to obtain?

2. In the Ant experiments (Section D), you note that switching skills is challenging for unstable robots. Have you considered integrating skill switching into the pre-training phase (e.g., by including transitions between skills in the pre-training environment)?

3. The paper uses a fixed switch time T for all tasks. For tasks with different dynamics, would adaptive switching (where T is learned rather than fixed) provide additional benefits?

4. Could you provide more insight into why the MI regularizer sometimes doesn't boost performance on the maze tasks (Fig. 6a-c), even though it improves skill diversity (Fig. 4)?

## Conclusion

This paper makes a strong contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by presenting a simple yet effective framework for learning diverse skills with minimal supervision that can be reused across tasks. The method is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough and scientifically rigorous, and the results clearly demonstrate the advantages of the approach over baselines. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a compelling case for its methodological contributions. I recommend acceptance.
