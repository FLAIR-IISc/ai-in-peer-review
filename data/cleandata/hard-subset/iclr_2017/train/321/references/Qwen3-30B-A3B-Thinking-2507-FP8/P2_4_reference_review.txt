

# Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning: efficiently solving a collection of tasks with sparse rewards. The authors propose a novel framework that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using minimal domain knowledge, then leverages these skills in a hierarchical architecture to solve downstream tasks. Their key contributions include:
- Introducing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) as a unified policy architecture that can represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing
- Proposing an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage skill diversity during pre-training
- Demonstrating that this framework significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency on challenging sparse reward tasks

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing a general approach that requires minimal supervision for skill learning and enables knowledge transfer across tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

This paper makes a strong contribution that addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning with a well-motivated and novel approach. The key reasons for acceptance are:

1. The paper identifies a clear and important gap in the literature: how to learn a diverse set of skills that can be reused across multiple sparse reward tasks without requiring task-specific supervision.
2. The methodology is sound, well-justified, and thoroughly evaluated with appropriate ablation studies and comparisons to relevant baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing the three key questions for evaluation:

**1. Specific problem tackled:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of learning reusable skills for multiple sparse reward tasks with minimal supervision. It correctly identifies that existing approaches either require domain knowledge (hierarchical action composition) or don't provide knowledge transfer (intrinsic rewards). The problem statement is well-justified with examples from realistic scenarios.

**2. Motivation and literature placement:** The related work section is comprehensive and well-structured, properly situating the paper within the broader context of hierarchical RL, skill learning, and intrinsic motivation. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon prior work, particularly noting that their pre-training setup requires only a single proxy reward signal rather than specified goals as in related work by Heess et al. (2016).

**3. Support for claims:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence for its claims. The ablation studies (Figs. 4-5) clearly demonstrate the impact of different architectural choices (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus). The exploration analysis (Fig. 5) provides visual evidence of how the hierarchical structure improves coverage compared to naive exploration. Most importantly, the learning curves on downstream tasks (Fig. 6) show consistent and significant improvements over baselines, with the SNN approach outperforming the Multi-policy baseline despite requiring fewer samples for pre-training.

The paper also demonstrates scientific rigor through:
- Careful design of the pre-training environment with a proxy reward requiring minimal domain knowledge
- Multiple ablation studies showing the necessity of key components (MI regularizer, bilinear integration)
- Evaluation on multiple maze configurations and the Gather task
- Analysis of failure modes and limitations (e.g., with the Ant robot)

The results are both statistically and practically significant, with the framework showing clear improvements in sample efficiency on challenging sparse reward tasks where naive exploration strategies fail.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, the following points could further strengthen it:

1. **Clarify the relationship between SNNs and previous work:** The paper mentions InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016) as inspiration for the MI regularizer, but doesn't explicitly connect how the authors' use of MI differs from InfoGAN's application to representation learning. A brief discussion would help readers understand the novelty of their application.

2. **Expand the discussion on scalability:** The paper shows promising results on Swimmer, Snake, and Ant, but doesn't thoroughly discuss the computational complexity of the SNN approach as the number of skills increases. A brief analysis of how the training time scales with K (the number of skills) would be valuable for practitioners.

3. **Clarify the "minimal domain knowledge" claim:** The paper states that the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," but doesn't precisely define what constitutes "minimal" in this context. Providing specific examples of how much domain knowledge would be required (e.g., "only needs to know that movement is beneficial, not the specific directions") would strengthen this claim.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the pre-training phase, the authors use a proxy reward that encourages locomotion (e.g., speed for a mobile robot). How would this approach generalize to tasks where the desired skills aren't directly related to movement (e.g., manipulation tasks where skills might involve grasping or object placement)?

2. The paper shows that the MI regularizer improves skill diversity, but the ablation study in Fig. 7.1 shows that its effect is somewhat task-dependent (beneficial for Maze 0 but not consistently for all mazes). Could the authors provide more insight into when the MI regularizer is most beneficial and why it might not always improve performance?

3. The paper mentions that end-to-end training could be beneficial but leaves it as future work. Given that the authors have already shown that frozen low-level policies are sufficient for good performance, what specific advantages would end-to-end training provide, and how might it impact the diversity of skills learned?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The methodology is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed approach. I recommend acceptance.
