

# Peer Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of efficient exploration in sparse reward environments. The key contribution is a method to learn a diverse span of reusable skills in a pre-training phase using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a mutual information-based regularizer. The approach requires only a simple proxy reward during pre-training (which needs minimal domain knowledge) and enables faster learning across multiple downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn interpretable skills (e.g., forward, backward, sideways locomotion) that significantly improve exploration and learning efficiency compared to baselines. The framework effectively combines unsupervised skill learning with hierarchical policy reuse, making it applicable to a collection of related tasks without requiring task-specific rewards during pre-training.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for acceptance: 
1) The paper addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning (efficient exploration in sparse reward settings) with a well-motivated and novel approach that bridges the gap between hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation.
2) The technical contribution of using SNNs with MI regularization for skill diversity is well-justified and well-placed in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of the problem. The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of naive exploration strategies and existing hierarchical approaches (hand-engineered hierarchies or intrinsic rewards that don't facilitate knowledge transfer across tasks). The related work section is comprehensive and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the field.

The technical approach is well-motivated: SNNs provide a natural way to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing, and the MI regularizer addresses the critical issue of skill diversity. The authors provide a clear explanation of why their approach (bilinear integration, MI bonus) outperforms alternatives (concatenation, no MI bonus), with compelling evidence from visitation plots (Fig. 4) showing distinct, interpretable skills.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors test their approach on multiple maze navigation and food collection tasks, with appropriate baselines including both standard RL approaches and intrinsic motivation methods. The ablation studies (Figs. 4-6) effectively demonstrate the importance of key components (bilinear integration, MI bonus). The results consistently show that the proposed method achieves better exploration (Fig. 5) and faster learning (Fig. 6) compared to baselines, particularly on challenging sparse reward tasks.

The paper is well-written with clear figures and explanations. The code and videos are available, enhancing reproducibility. The authors also provide insightful analysis of the learned skills' interpretability, which is a significant strength of their approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to further improve it:

1. **Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper mentions that SNNs reduce sample complexity by a factor equal to the number of skills, but it would be valuable to provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (both training and inference) compared to baselines. Does the SNN approach require significantly more memory or computation time per sample?

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper shows that increasing αH (MI bonus coefficient) improves diversity, but it would be helpful to see a more systematic analysis of how performance varies with αH. A sensitivity analysis would help readers understand how to choose this hyperparameter in practice.

3. **Scalability to More Complex Tasks**: The paper demonstrates success on Swimmer and Snake robots, but the appendix mentions challenges with the Ant robot. It would be valuable to include a more detailed discussion of these challenges and potential solutions, as this speaks to the scalability of the approach.

4. **Why MI Bonus Doesn't Always Help**: The paper notes in Section 7.3 that the MI bonus doesn't always boost performance (e.g., on Maze 0). A more detailed analysis of when and why the MI bonus is beneficial would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Section 7.3, you mention that the MI bonus doesn't always boost performance (e.g., on Maze 0). Could you provide more insight into why this happens in some cases but not others? Is it related to the specific nature of the task or the learned skills?

2. The paper states that the approach works well for static tasks but might face challenges with dynamic tasks (Section 8). Could you elaborate on how the approach might be extended to handle dynamic tasks more effectively, particularly for unstable robots like Ant?

3. You mention in the appendix that the pre-trained SNNs show high variance in performance across different random seeds (Section C.4). How might this variance be reduced, and what are the implications for real-world deployment where consistent performance is critical?

4. The paper mentions that end-to-end training with straight-through estimators could be explored in future work. Have you considered how this might affect the interpretability of the skills, which is one of the key strengths of your approach?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The proposed framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. With minor revisions to address the points above, this paper would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
