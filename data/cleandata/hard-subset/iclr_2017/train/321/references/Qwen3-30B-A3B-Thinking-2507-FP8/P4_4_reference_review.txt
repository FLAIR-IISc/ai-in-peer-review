

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a framework for efficiently learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a proxy reward signal that requires minimal domain knowledge. These skills can then be reused across multiple downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key contributions include: (1) using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously; (2) introducing an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage diversity in the learned skills; and (3) demonstrating that this hierarchical approach significantly improves exploration and learning efficiency on sparse reward tasks. The method is evaluated on locomotion and object collection tasks, showing clear advantages over standard RL baselines and intrinsic motivation approaches.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a technically sound contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with strong empirical validation. The approach is well-motivated, the methodology is sound, and the results provide convincing evidence for the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper clearly identifies a well-defined problem: efficiently learning skills for multiple sparse reward tasks without requiring domain-specific knowledge for each task. The authors effectively position their work within the literature, noting that prior approaches either require hand-engineered hierarchies or fail to provide knowledge transfer across tasks.

The methodology is technically sound. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is a reasonable choice, and the information-theoretic regularizer is well-motivated by recent work on interpretable representation learning (InfoGAN). The experimental design is strong, using the standard Duan et al. (2016) benchmark and including ablation studies that demonstrate the impact of key components (bilinear integration, MI bonus). The results consistently show that the proposed approach outperforms baselines, with the hierarchical structure significantly improving exploration (as shown in the visitation plots) and learning efficiency on sparse reward tasks.

The paper provides sufficient evidence to support its claims. The ablation studies in Section 7.1 demonstrate that the SNN approach with the MI bonus consistently yields a larger span of interpretable skills compared to training separate policies. The results in Section 7.3 show that the hierarchical approach with SNNs achieves faster learning on maze and gather tasks compared to baselines, with the MI bonus providing additional benefits in some cases. The experiments are conducted on multiple robot types (Swimmer, Snake) and include detailed analysis of the impact of architectural choices.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could provide more theoretical justification for why the MI regularizer works well. A brief analysis of how the MI term relates to skill diversity would strengthen the contribution.
- The experiments are limited to locomotion and object collection tasks. Including more diverse tasks would further validate the generality of the approach.
- The failure modes with unstable robots (Ant) are mentioned but not deeply analyzed. A more detailed discussion of these limitations would be valuable.
- The paper mentions that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" but doesn't elaborate on when it's most beneficial. A more thorough analysis would help practitioners understand the conditions under which this component is most useful.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper states that "the MI bonus yields less overlapped skills and new advancing/turning skills, independently of the integration of the latent variables." Could the authors provide more quantitative evidence of how the MI bonus specifically affects skill diversity beyond the qualitative observations in the visitation plots?
- The paper mentions that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" (Section 7.3). Could the authors elaborate on when the MI bonus is most beneficial and why it sometimes doesn't provide a clear advantage?
- In Appendix C.4, the paper notes that "the performance depends strongly on the pretrained SNN" for maze tasks. Could the authors provide more insight into what characteristics of the pretrained SNN lead to better performance on specific tasks, and whether there's a way to select or train SNNs that are more likely to generalize well across tasks?
