- Problem & approach: tackles exploration in sparse-reward RL by pre-training diverse skills with Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) and using them hierarchically for downstream tasks.  
- Main innovation: SNNs for multimodal policies plus an information-theoretic (mutual information) regularizer to encourage diverse skills.  
- Pipeline: learn skills with a simple proxy reward in a pre-training phase, then use a high-level controller to compose skills on target tasks.  
- Empirical gains: shows significant improvements in exploration and sample efficiency on Maze and Gather benchmarks (Swimmer, Snake, Ant).  
- Key components validated: ablations demonstrate bilinear integration and the MI regularizer are crucial for learning diverse, interpretable skills.  
- Experimental rigor: experiments use multiple random seeds (10 runs per curve) and include failure-mode analysis (e.g., Ant).  
- Practical benefit: requires only a simple proxy reward (e.g., speed) and yields transferable, interpretable skills, reducing need for hand-engineered hierarchies.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant, timely contribution with strong empirical and methodological support.  
- Clarify "minimal domain knowledge": proxy reward design may not be as minimal as claimed; authors should define and compare this requirement more precisely.  
- Expand comparisons: add more thorough comparisons to relevant prior hierarchical methods (e.g., HiREPS, Option-Critic).  
- Justify MI choice: explain why MI between latent code and CoM coordinates was chosen versus alternatives (e.g., MI with actions or other state components).  
- Address scalability: discuss how the method scales to higher-dimensional states/actions and more complex dynamics.  
- End-to-end training: clarify integration with straight-through estimators and applicability to discrete latent-variable end-to-end learning.  
- Sample complexity claims: provide quantification or theoretical analysis for the asserted uniform reduction in sample complexity.  
- Questions for authors: robustness to mis-specified proxy rewards, whether MI also improves per-skill quality during pre-training, causes and fixes for Ant instability when switching skills, and theoretical guarantees for sample-complexity improvements.
