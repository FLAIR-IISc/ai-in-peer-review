

# Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards in complex tasks. The authors introduce a two-phase approach: first, pre-training in a minimal environment with a proxy reward to learn a diverse span of skills using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer; second, using these pre-learned skills to efficiently solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key contributions are: (1) using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing across skills; (2) introducing a mutual information-based regularizer to encourage skill diversity; and (3) demonstrating that this approach significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency in sparse reward tasks. The authors validate their approach on benchmark tasks (Maze and Gather) with sparse rewards, showing that their method outperforms both standard RL approaches and previous hierarchical methods.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning. I recommend acceptance primarily because: (1) the paper addresses a significant challenge in RL (sparse rewards) with a well-motivated, novel approach that builds on existing literature while introducing meaningful innovations; and (2) the empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough ablation studies and clear demonstration of the method's advantages over baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is well-motivated and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. Prior work on skill learning either requires domain knowledge for hand-engineering hierarchies (Parr & Russell, 1998; Sutton et al., 1999) or relies on intrinsic rewards that don't enable knowledge transfer across tasks (Houthooft et al., 2016; Bellemare et al., 2016). The authors' approach of using a minimal proxy reward for pre-training that requires only basic domain knowledge (e.g., rewarding speed for a mobile robot) to learn a diverse span of skills is both elegant and practical. This is a significant step toward truly generalizable skill learning that doesn't require task-specific reward engineering.

The methodology is clearly presented and well-justified. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is appropriate, and the authors provide a thorough justification for their architectural choices (bilinear integration vs. concatenation). The information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information is well-motivated by InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016) and effectively encourages skill diversity without requiring additional supervision. The ablation studies (Section 7.1) convincingly demonstrate that both the bilinear integration and the MI bonus are necessary for achieving a diverse span of skills.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and compelling. The authors conduct thorough experiments on multiple maze configurations and the Gather task, comparing against appropriate baselines. They demonstrate that their method significantly outperforms standard RL approaches and even outperforms the baseline of adding the same proxy reward to the downstream tasks. Crucially, they report results across multiple random seeds, showing that the improvements are consistent rather than dependent on a lucky initialization. The visitation plots (Fig. 4) provide clear visual evidence of the diverse skills learned, and the hierarchical exploration analysis (Fig. 5) convincingly shows how the pre-trained skills enhance exploration.

The paper also thoughtfully addresses limitations and future work, including the challenges with unstable robots (Ant) and the impact of the switch time T on performance. This demonstrates a mature understanding of the approach's boundaries.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would further enhance its quality:

1. **Clarify the relationship with Heess et al. (2016)**: The authors mention that Heess et al. independently proposed a similar framework but required specified goals for pre-training. A more detailed comparison would help readers understand the key differences in their approaches, particularly why their proxy reward approach is more generally applicable.

2. **Provide more concrete evidence of transfer learning**: While the paper shows that skills learned in pre-training help solve downstream tasks, it would be valuable to quantify the sample efficiency gains more precisely. For example, how many times fewer samples does the proposed method require compared to training from scratch?

3. **Expand the discussion of the MI bonus**: The paper shows the MI bonus improves skill diversity, but it would be helpful to include a more detailed analysis of how different values of αH affect the learned skills, especially for more complex robots like Ant.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the MI bonus helps prevent skill collapse, but it's not clear why the MI between the latent code and CoM coordinates is the right measure for skill diversity. Could you provide more theoretical justification for this choice, or discuss alternative measures that were considered?

2. The paper mentions that the pre-training environment requires "minimal domain knowledge" about the downstream tasks. For the Maze task, the proxy reward is speed, which is reasonable for locomotion. However, for the Gather task, the proxy reward is grasping any object. Could you elaborate on how this proxy reward would generalize to other tasks where grasping might not be relevant (e.g., navigation tasks without objects)?

3. In Appendix D.2, the authors describe failure modes with unstable robots like Ant. Have you explored alternative approaches to handle the instability during skill switching, such as using a termination policy at the Manager level as mentioned in the discussion?

This paper makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing a practical, sample-efficient approach to skill learning that requires minimal supervision. The methodology is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. I believe this work will be of interest to the broader RL community and makes a strong case for acceptance at ICLR.
