

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward tasks with long horizons. The authors introduce a method to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a bilinear integration mechanism and an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information (MI). This approach allows the agent to learn multiple interpretable skills without task-specific supervision, using only a generic proxy reward signal. The learned skills are then reused across multiple downstream tasks through a hierarchical architecture that significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach through comprehensive experiments on Maze navigation and object gathering tasks, showing clear improvements over strong baselines.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing a method to learn diverse skills without domain-specific supervision and effectively reuse them across tasks. The experimental results are rigorous and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses three key aspects of the problem:

First, the problem statement is clearly articulated and well-motivated. The authors identify the limitations of existing approaches (hand-engineered hierarchies requiring domain knowledge, and intrinsic reward methods that don't enable knowledge transfer between tasks), and clearly position their work as a solution that requires minimal domain knowledge for pre-training and enables skill transfer across tasks.

Second, the approach is well-placed in the literature. The related work section thoroughly reviews hierarchical RL, skill learning, and intrinsic motivation, correctly identifying the distinctions between their method and prior work (particularly in how they learn skills without requiring goal specification during pre-training). The authors provide a compelling justification for their architectural choices (bilinear integration, MI regularizer) through ablation studies.

Third, the paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. The experiments include thorough analysis of skill diversity (visitation plots in Figure 4), exploration capabilities (Figure 5), and downstream task performance (Figure 6). The authors report results across multiple random seeds and provide ablation studies that demonstrate the contribution of each component (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI regularizer). The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms baselines, including strong intrinsic motivation methods like VIME.

The methodology is technically sound, with clear explanations of the SNN architecture, the MI regularizer, and the hierarchical framework. The paper effectively demonstrates that their approach addresses the stated problem of learning diverse skills without domain-specific supervision and reusing them across tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:

- Provide more quantitative metrics for skill diversity (beyond visual inspection of visitation plots) to better support their claims about the quality of the learned skills.
- Discuss the computational cost of the MI regularizer and how it scales with the dimensionality of the state space.
- Include a more detailed analysis of the relationship between the MI regularizer's hyperparameter (αH) and skill diversity/quality.
- Expand the discussion of limitations to include potential failure cases beyond the Ant robot example.

## 5. Questions for Clarification
1. How sensitive is the MI regularizer to the choice of state subset (CoM coordinates) used for computing mutual information? Would the method work equally well with other state subsets, and how would the authors recommend selecting these subsets for new environments?

2. The paper demonstrates that the MI regularizer helps learn more diverse skills, but it would be valuable to understand the theoretical relationship between the MI regularizer and the diversity of the skills learned. Are there theoretical guarantees that the MI regularizer will produce diverse skills, or is this primarily empirical?

3. In the experiments, the authors report that the SNN approach with MI regularizer outperforms the baseline on Gather but not consistently on Maze. Could the authors provide more insight into why the MI regularizer appears to be more beneficial for certain types of tasks?
