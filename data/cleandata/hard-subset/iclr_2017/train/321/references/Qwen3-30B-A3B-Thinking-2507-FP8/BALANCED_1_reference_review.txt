

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards through a two-stage process. First, it learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a minimal proxy reward signal (requiring only basic domain knowledge), then leverages these skills to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key technical contributions are: (1) using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing, (2) introducing a bilinear integration mechanism for latent variables, and (3) employing an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate their approach on benchmark tasks (Duan et al., 2016) with multiple robot environments, showing improved exploration and faster learning compared to baselines.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with a well-motivated approach that addresses a significant challenge in the field. The technical novelty of combining SNNs with MI regularization for skill learning is compelling, and the experimental results provide strong empirical support for the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's strengths are substantial. The problem statement is clearly articulated and well-motivated, addressing a genuine limitation in current RL approaches for sparse reward environments. The literature review effectively positions the work in relation to hierarchical RL, skill learning, and intrinsic motivation approaches. The technical approach is novel and well-justified: SNNs provide a natural way to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing, and the MI regularizer effectively addresses the problem of skill collapse during training.

The experimental validation is thorough, with ablation studies showing the impact of different design choices (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus strength). The results on benchmark tasks (Maze and Gather) clearly demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms baselines, including the proxy reward baseline and standard RL algorithms. The use of multiple robot environments (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) strengthens the claims about generalizability. The paper also thoughtfully addresses limitations and failure modes (e.g., with unstable robots like Ant), providing valuable insights for future work.

The main weaknesses are minor: the claim about "minimal domain knowledge" is slightly overstated (the proxy reward requires some domain understanding), and the theoretical justification for the MI regularizer is somewhat heuristic. However, these do not undermine the overall contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Clarify the "minimal domain knowledge" claim by explicitly stating what specific domain knowledge is required for the proxy reward (e.g., for a mobile robot, the paper mentions "speed" as a reward, which requires knowing what constitutes movement in the environment).

2. Provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of why the MI regularizer works, perhaps by connecting it to the underlying RL theory or providing a simple theoretical justification.

3. Include a more explicit comparison of sample efficiency (total samples required) rather than just learning speed, to better demonstrate the claimed "reduced sample complexity."

4. In the results section, consider adding a comparison to the most recent hierarchical RL methods (e.g., Heess et al., 2016) to better position the work in the current literature.

5. Discuss the computational cost of the MI regularizer (e.g., the grid discretization) and how it scales with problem complexity.

## 5. Clarifying Questions
1. The paper states the proxy reward "only requires very minimal domain knowledge." Could you provide specific examples of how the proxy reward would be designed for different environments (e.g., a humanoid robot vs. a drone), and what exactly constitutes "minimal domain knowledge" in each case?

2. The MI regularizer uses a discretization of the state space to compute the posterior. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of grid density (e.g., 10 divisions/unit)? Would a more sophisticated approach (e.g., using a neural network to estimate the posterior) provide better results with less discretization bias?

3. The paper mentions that "the switch time T does not critically affect the performance for these static tasks." Could you elaborate on how you would determine the optimal T for dynamic tasks where the environment changes rapidly, and whether your framework could adapt T during training?
