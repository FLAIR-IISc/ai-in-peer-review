

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward tasks. The authors introduce a two-phase approach: first, pre-training a span of skills in a minimal setup using a proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and second, leveraging these skills through a hierarchical architecture to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key technical contributions include using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing, and introducing an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage skill diversity. The paper demonstrates that their approach can learn interpretable skills and significantly improve exploration in challenging sparse-reward environments.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. The paper presents a reasonable approach with promising empirical results, but the claims are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The experimental evaluation is limited in scope, lacks sufficient ablation studies, and the theoretical justification for key design choices is weak. The contribution does not represent a significant advance over related work in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Key weakness 1: Insufficient experimental evaluation.** The paper only evaluates on two robot types (Swimmer and Snake) and three tasks (Mazes and Gather), which are relatively simple continuous control problems. The benchmark tasks from Duan et al. (2016) have been extensively studied, and the paper does not demonstrate a clear advantage over state-of-the-art methods on more challenging benchmarks. The claim that "the framework can learn a wide range of skills, which are clearly interpretable" is supported only by qualitative visualizations without quantitative measures of skill diversity or interpretability.

**Key weakness 2: Weak theoretical justification for key components.** The paper introduces an information-theoretic regularizer but provides no theoretical justification for why this particular form of regularization is beneficial for skill diversity. The authors claim that "the MI bonus yields less overlapped skills," but the experimental evidence for this is limited to visualizations without statistical significance testing. The paper lacks any analysis of how the MI regularizer affects the underlying policy distribution or the optimization landscape.

**Key weakness 3: Overstated claims about sample efficiency.** The paper claims that "the sample efficiency of training K skills is effectively K times better" than training separate policies, but this is misleading. The paper uses the same number of samples to train a single SNN as to train K separate policies, but the SNN is learning a mixture distribution rather than K distinct policies. The paper does not demonstrate that the SNN achieves the same performance as K separate policies with the same number of samples, which would be required to claim better sample efficiency.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Expand the experimental evaluation to include more challenging environments and benchmarks, particularly those with very sparse rewards or long horizons. Compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods for sparse-reward tasks (e.g., from the 2019-2022 ICLR/ICML proceedings).

2. Provide quantitative metrics for skill diversity and interpretability, not just qualitative visualizations. For example, compute the pairwise distance between skills in state space or use clustering metrics to demonstrate distinctness.

3. Include more thorough ablation studies on the key components: the bilinear integration vs. concatenation, the MI regularizer, and the pre-training setup. The current ablation (Section 7.1) is insufficient to draw strong conclusions.

4. Clarify the sample efficiency claim. Demonstrate that the SNN approach achieves comparable or better performance on downstream tasks than training K separate policies, using the same total number of samples.

5. Address the limitations of the approach, particularly the instability observed with more complex robots (Section D.2) and the lack of analysis on how the switch time T affects performance in more complex environments.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that the pre-training environment requires "only minimal domain knowledge" for the proxy reward. Could you elaborate on what specific domain knowledge is required for the proxy reward in the Swimmer, Snake, and Ant robots? How does this compare to the knowledge required for other approaches like Heess et al. (2016)?

2. In Figure 5(b)-(d), the exploration coverage appears to be significantly better with the hierarchical approach. However, the paper states that the SNN was trained with the same number of samples as a single policy (Section 7.1), while the Multi-policy baseline trained six separate policies. How does the sample efficiency claim hold when the Multi-policy baseline uses six times more samples?

3. The paper mentions that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" (Section 7.3). Can you provide more detailed analysis on when the MI bonus is beneficial versus when it is not? Is there a clear pattern related to the task structure or the specific skills needed?

4. In Section 8, the authors mention that "the decision of what skill to use next only depends on the observation at the moment of switching." How would the authors expect this limitation to affect performance in more complex environments where temporal dependencies might be important? What specific modifications would be needed to address this limitation?

The paper presents an interesting idea that could contribute to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning, but the current evidence is insufficient to support the claims made. With more thorough evaluation and clearer justification of key design choices, this work could be strengthened for future publication.
