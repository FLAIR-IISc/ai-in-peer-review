

# Peer Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of learning in sparse reward environments. The authors introduce a method to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information (MI). The key contributions are:

1. A framework for learning a diverse set of skills through a single pre-training phase with minimal domain knowledge (using only a proxy reward signal)
2. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously with weight sharing
3. An MI-based regularizer that encourages diversity of behaviors in the SNN policy
4. A hierarchical architecture that uses the learned skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards

The authors demonstrate their approach on the Maze and Gather tasks from Duan et al. (2016), showing significant improvements over baselines, including a strong baseline of using the same proxy reward in the downstream task. The paper is well-organized, clearly motivated, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and addresses a significant challenge in RL (sparse reward learning)
2. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear advantages over baselines across multiple tasks
3. The method is practically useful and generalizable to different robotic platforms

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical RL by providing a practical framework that doesn't require domain-specific knowledge for skill learning. The use of SNNs with MI regularization is a clever approach to learn diverse skills without requiring multiple separate policies or domain-specific reward shaping.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare against a strong baseline (using the same proxy reward in the downstream task), which is important because previous work often used weaker baselines. The ablation studies (Fig. 4) clearly demonstrate the impact of the MI regularizer and the bilinear integration. The results on both the Maze and Gather tasks show consistent improvements across multiple random seeds, and the analysis of the learned skills (visitation plots) provides valuable insights into the method's behavior.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. The related work section carefully contextualizes the contribution relative to prior work on hierarchical RL, skill learning, and intrinsic motivation. The authors clearly identify the limitations of previous approaches (requiring domain-specific knowledge or solving downstream tasks first) and position their method as addressing these gaps.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper relies primarily on empirical results to support the MI regularizer. A more detailed theoretical justification for why maximizing I(Z;C) leads to better skill diversity would strengthen the paper. The authors could explore the connection between the MI regularizer and the underlying MDP structure.

2. **Scalability Analysis**: The paper demonstrates the approach on Swimmer and Snake robots, but the analysis of scalability to more complex tasks is limited. The authors could provide more insight into how the method scales with the number of skills (K) and the complexity of the robot.

3. **Comparison with Recent HRL Methods**: The related work section could be expanded to include more recent hierarchical RL methods like HRL with Option-Critic or other modern approaches to skill learning. This would better position the work in the current landscape.

4. **Failure Analysis**: The paper mentions failure modes with the Ant robot (Appendix D), but this is only briefly mentioned. A more detailed analysis of when the method fails and why would be valuable for understanding the limitations.

5. **Computational Cost**: The paper mentions the sample efficiency benefit of using SNNs instead of multiple separate policies, but doesn't quantify the computational cost of training the SNN versus multiple separate policies. This would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that the MI regularizer is equivalent to minimizing H(Z|C). However, the authors note that H(Z) is constant because the latent variable distribution is fixed to uniform during pre-training. Could the authors elaborate on why the uniform distribution for the latent variable is optimal for this approach? Is there a theoretical justification for this choice?

2. In the experiments, the authors report that the MI regularizer doesn't always boost performance (Fig. 6). Could the authors provide more insight into when the MI regularizer is most beneficial? Is it more effective for certain types of tasks or robot morphologies?

3. The paper mentions that the approach could be improved by end-to-end training using straight-through estimators (Sec. 5.4). Would the authors be willing to share preliminary results on this direction, as it could be a significant extension of their work?

4. The paper demonstrates the approach on Maze and Gather tasks, but the authors mention that the "extra skills" (like turning or sideways motions) would "shine more in other tasks requiring more precise navigation." Could the authors provide a specific example of such a task where the MI regularizer would be particularly beneficial, and why?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses a significant practical challenge in the field. The proposed method is well-motivated, well-executed, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over baselines. With the minor revisions suggested above, this paper would be a strong contribution to ICLR.
