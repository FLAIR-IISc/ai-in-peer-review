

# Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of learning policies for tasks with sparse rewards. The authors introduce a method to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a minimal proxy reward signal (requiring only basic domain knowledge), which can then be reused across multiple downstream tasks. Their key technical contributions are: (1) using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing, (2) introducing an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage skill diversity, and (3) demonstrating that this approach significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency in sparse reward environments. The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple robotic tasks (Swimmer, Snake, and Ant) with strong ablation studies.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper addresses a significant problem in reinforcement learning (sparse reward learning) with a novel and well-motivated approach, and (2) the empirical results are robust, comprehensive, and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed method.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several critical areas:

**Problem Significance and Novelty:** The authors correctly identify that sparse reward problems are common in real-world applications and that current approaches either require domain knowledge (hierarchical methods) or don't facilitate knowledge transfer across tasks (intrinsic reward methods). Their framework bridges this gap by learning a span of reusable skills with minimal supervision. The use of SNNs with an MI regularizer to learn diverse skills is particularly novel and well-motivated.

**Technical Soundness:** The methodology is well-justified. The authors provide clear reasoning for their architectural choices (bilinear integration over concatenation) and the MI regularizer. The theoretical grounding in information theory for the regularizer is appropriate and aligns with recent successful applications in representation learning (e.g., InfoGAN). The implementation details are sufficiently clear, and the use of TRPO for policy optimization is well-justified.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental design is strong, with multiple ablation studies (integration method, MI bonus, skill diversity), multiple environments (Mazes, Gather), and multiple robot models (Swimmer, Snake, Ant). The paper addresses the critical issue of reproducibility through code and video availability. The results consistently show that the SNN approach with MI regularizer outperforms baselines, particularly in the Maze tasks where the additional skills learned through the MI bonus prove valuable. The authors also thoughtfully discuss limitations and failure modes (e.g., with the unstable Ant robot).

**Literature Context:** The related work section is comprehensive and properly situates the paper within the broader context of hierarchical RL, skill discovery, and intrinsic motivation. The comparison with Heess et al. (2016) is particularly relevant, highlighting how their approach requires less supervision than prior work.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of theoretical analysis regarding the convergence properties of the MI regularized SNN training. While the empirical results are strong, a more formal analysis of how the MI regularizer affects the learning dynamics would further strengthen the paper. However, this is not a deal-breaker for acceptance, as the empirical evidence is already compelling.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements to enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the "proxy reward" design**: The paper states that the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," but doesn't specify what that minimal knowledge entails. Providing concrete examples of what constitutes "minimal domain knowledge" would help readers understand the practical applicability of the approach.

2. **Expand the theoretical discussion**: While the empirical results are strong, a brief theoretical analysis of how the MI regularizer affects the learning dynamics would complement the empirical findings. For instance, discussing how the MI bonus prevents mode collapse in the SNN could add theoretical depth.

3. **Address generalization to more complex tasks**: The paper demonstrates success on relatively controlled Maze and Gather tasks. Discussing how the approach might scale to more complex, real-world tasks (e.g., with more diverse object interactions or multiple agents) would strengthen the paper's broader impact.

4. **Clarify the relationship to prior skill learning work**: While the related work is comprehensive, the paper could more explicitly distinguish its approach from other recent skill discovery methods (e.g., those based on variational inference or other information-theoretic objectives).

## Questions for Authors

1. In the pre-training environment, how would you handle cases where the proxy reward signal is not well-defined for a particular domain (e.g., for tasks where speed is not a meaningful metric)?

2. The paper mentions that "the construction of which only requires minimal domain knowledge" for the proxy reward. Could you provide concrete examples of what this minimal domain knowledge might look like for different application domains (beyond the robotic examples in the paper)?

3. In Figure 7.3, the SNN with MI bonus shows better performance on the Gather task but not consistently on all Maze tasks. Could you elaborate on why the MI bonus appears to be more beneficial for some task structures than others?

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with a practical, well-motivated approach that demonstrates significant empirical advantages over existing methods. With the minor clarifications suggested, it would be an excellent addition to the ICLR proceedings.
