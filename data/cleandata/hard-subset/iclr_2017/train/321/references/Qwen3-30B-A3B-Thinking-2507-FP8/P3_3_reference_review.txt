

# Peer Review: "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper addresses the critical challenge of efficient exploration in reinforcement learning (RL) for tasks with sparse rewards. The authors propose a novel framework that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a minimal proxy reward signal (requiring only basic understanding of the agent's capabilities), then leverages these skills hierarchically to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovation is using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies that can learn diverse skills simultaneously, enhanced by an information-theoretic regularizer (based on mutual information between latent codes and state) to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly improves exploration and sample efficiency on challenging benchmark tasks (Maze and Gather) compared to baselines, including naive exploration and intrinsic motivation methods.

The paper is well-motivated, with a clear problem statement, thorough related work analysis, and strong empirical validation across multiple robotic environments (Swimmer, Snake, and Ant). The framework elegantly combines unsupervised skill learning with hierarchical task decomposition, offering a promising path toward transferable skills in RL.

## 2. Decision: Accept

**Key reasons for acceptance:** 
1) The paper makes a significant contribution to the challenging problem of sparse reward RL through a well-motivated and novel approach that demonstrates strong empirical results.
2) The method is theoretically sound, with careful design choices (bilinear integration, MI regularizer) that address key challenges in multi-modal policy learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contribution is timely and addresses a fundamental limitation in current RL approaches. The authors clearly articulate the problem with sparse rewards and the limitations of prior work (hand-engineered hierarchies requiring domain knowledge, intrinsic reward methods lacking transferability). Their framework effectively bridges this gap by learning skills in a domain-agnostic pre-training phase.

The methodology is well-executed and thoroughly validated. The ablation studies (Figs. 4-5) convincingly demonstrate that the bilinear integration and MI regularizer are essential for learning diverse, interpretable skills. The hierarchical application (Figs. 5-6) shows a clear improvement in exploration and learning speed across multiple downstream tasks. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with proper statistical reporting (multiple random seeds, 10 runs per curve) and thorough analysis of failure modes (Ant experiments).

The paper also makes a strong case for the practical benefits of the approach. By requiring only a simple proxy reward for pre-training (e.g., "speed" for locomotion), the method avoids the need for complex reward engineering. The interpretability of the learned skills (via latent code variation) is a significant advantage for understanding and debugging the learned policies.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, the following points would enhance its contribution and clarity:

1. **Clarify the "minimal domain knowledge" claim:** The paper states the proxy reward requires "very minimal domain knowledge," but in practice, designing the proxy reward (e.g., "speed" for locomotion) does require understanding the agent's capabilities. The authors should more explicitly characterize what constitutes "minimal" domain knowledge and how it compares to other methods (e.g., intrinsic reward methods that also require some domain understanding).

2. **Expand comparison to relevant recent work:** The paper mentions HiREPS (Daniel et al., 2012) and Option-Critic (Bacon & Precup, 2016) but doesn't provide a detailed comparison to these methods. Since these are direct predecessors to the authors' approach, a more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning.

3. **Theoretical justification for MI metric:** While the MI regularizer is inspired by InfoGAN, the paper doesn't fully justify why MI between latent code and CoM coordinates is the appropriate metric. The authors should discuss why this specific MI metric was chosen over alternatives (e.g., MI with actions or with state components more relevant to the downstream task).

4. **Address scalability concerns:** The paper shows promising results on benchmark tasks but doesn't sufficiently address scalability to more complex environments. The authors should discuss how their method would scale to more challenging tasks (e.g., with higher-dimensional state/action spaces or more complex dynamics).

5. **Clarify the relationship to end-to-end training:** The paper mentions that end-to-end training could be done using straight-through estimators but doesn't explore this direction. The authors should discuss how their approach would integrate with these emerging techniques for discrete latent variable models.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How would the method perform if the proxy reward was designed incorrectly (e.g., for a locomotion agent, using "distance from goal" instead of "speed")? Would the learned skills still be useful for downstream tasks?

2. The paper demonstrates that the MI regularizer improves skill diversity, but does it also improve the quality of individual skills (e.g., in terms of reward achieved during pre-training)? If so, how much?

3. For the Ant robot experiments, the paper mentions that switching between skills causes instability. Could the authors elaborate on why this happens (is it primarily due to the discontinuity in state space when switching skills, or due to the specific dynamics of the Ant agent)? What would be the most promising direction for addressing this instability?

4. The paper claims the framework "reduces sample complexity uniformly," but doesn't provide theoretical analysis of the sample complexity reduction. How would the authors quantify this reduction, and what theoretical guarantees can be made about the sample complexity improvement?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning, with a novel method that effectively addresses the sparse reward problem through skill learning. With minor clarifications and expansions to address the points above, this work would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
