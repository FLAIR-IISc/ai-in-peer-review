

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information. The authors claim that this approach enables efficient exploration in downstream tasks with sparse rewards by learning a span of interpretable skills that can be reused across different tasks. The paper demonstrates results on Maze and Gather tasks from the Duan et al. benchmark, showing that their method outperforms a baseline using a Center of Mass proxy reward.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant methodological advancement, and the novelty of the approach is limited compared to established methods in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary issue with this paper is that it presents an incremental application of existing techniques without providing rigorous evidence that the proposed approach offers meaningful advantages over established baselines. Specifically:

First, the paper's core contribution—using SNNs for skill learning—is not convincingly justified. The authors claim that SNNs "can easily represent multi-modal policies, while achieving weight sharing among the different modes," but they fail to compare against alternative multi-modal policy representations (e.g., mixture density networks, hierarchical policies with discrete latent variables, or other approaches to multi-modal policy learning). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether SNNs are truly the optimal choice for this problem, or whether the observed improvements are due to the specific architecture rather than the fundamental approach.

Second, the empirical results show only modest improvements over baselines, which doesn't justify the paper's claims of "reducing sample complexity uniformly" or providing a "general framework" for hierarchical RL. The paper compares against a Center of Mass proxy reward baseline (which is weakly motivated and, as the authors acknowledge, performs better than expected in some settings), but doesn't compare against established hierarchical RL methods like Option-Critic, HiREPS, or other skill learning approaches. The performance gains shown in Figure 6 are marginal (e.g., 10-20% improvement in return), and the paper fails to establish that these gains are statistically significant or consistently reproducible across different environments.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach. The appendix reveals significant instability issues with more complex robots (e.g., Ant), where switching between skills causes the robot to fall over. This is a critical limitation for real-world applications, yet the paper treats it as a minor footnote rather than a major challenge that needs to be addressed. The authors claim that "the span of skills learning can be greatly improved by using SNNs," but the results in Appendix D suggest this approach may not be robust to more challenging environments.

Finally, the paper's claim about sample efficiency is unsubstantiated. While the authors note that training a single SNN "takes a comparable amount of samples to train a regular uni-modal gaussian policy," they don't demonstrate that this translates to meaningful sample efficiency gains in practice. The paper shows that their method can learn skills without training multiple policies, but doesn't provide evidence that this actually reduces the total sample complexity required to solve downstream tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Comparative analysis with established methods**: The authors should compare their approach against multiple existing hierarchical RL methods (e.g., Option-Critic, HiREPS, GPS) to establish whether the improvements are due to the specific SNN architecture or the hierarchical framework. This would help contextualize the contribution more clearly.

2. **Rigorous sample complexity analysis**: The paper claims to reduce sample complexity, but provides no formal analysis or empirical evidence to support this claim. The authors should either provide a formal analysis or demonstrate clear sample efficiency gains across multiple environments.

3. **Addressing instability issues**: The paper should dedicate more attention to the instability issues observed with complex robots (e.g., Ant) in Appendix D. This is a critical limitation that needs to be addressed, not just mentioned as a future direction.

4. **More thorough ablation studies**: The paper should provide more detailed ablation studies to demonstrate the specific contribution of each component (bilinear integration, MI regularizer, etc.) rather than just showing overall performance improvements.

5. **Clarify the novelty**: The authors should more clearly articulate what makes their approach novel compared to existing work. The use of SNNs for skill learning has been explored in other contexts (e.g., generative models), and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this is the right approach for RL skill learning.

## Questions for Authors

1. Why did you choose SNNs specifically for this problem instead of alternative approaches to multi-modal policy learning (e.g., mixture density networks, discrete latent variable models, or other hierarchical representations)? What evidence do you have that SNNs are superior for this specific application?

2. The paper claims to reduce sample complexity, but the empirical results don't provide clear evidence of this. Can you provide a more rigorous analysis of the sample efficiency of your approach compared to training multiple independent policies?

3. The appendix reveals significant instability issues with the Ant robot. How would you address these issues in a practical application? What modifications to the framework would be necessary to handle unstable agents?

4. In Figure 6(d), the SNN with MI bonus shows lower variance than the baseline, but the paper states "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance." Can you clarify this apparent contradiction and explain when the MI bonus is beneficial versus when it's not?
