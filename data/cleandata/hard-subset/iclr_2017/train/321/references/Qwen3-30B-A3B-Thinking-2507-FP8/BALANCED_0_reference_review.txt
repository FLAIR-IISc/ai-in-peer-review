

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) that learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), then leverages these skills for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovations include using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing, and introducing an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate their approach on maze navigation and food collection tasks, showing improved exploration and sample efficiency compared to baselines.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution to HRL, but has significant weaknesses in theoretical grounding, experimental design, and clarity that prevent it from meeting ICLR's standards for acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main strengths lie in its practical framework for skill learning and the empirical results showing improved exploration. However, three key weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution:

First, the theoretical foundation is underdeveloped. The paper claims to learn "a span of skills" but provides little formal justification for why SNNs with the proposed architecture would produce a diverse set of skills. The mutual information regularizer is described as being inspired by InfoGAN, but the paper doesn't establish a theoretical connection between the regularizer and the diversity of skills learned. The authors claim the approach "consistently yields a large span of skills" but provide no formal guarantees or analysis of the conditions under which this occurs.

Second, the experimental evaluation has significant limitations. The authors compare their method against a baseline that uses the same proxy reward in the downstream task, but this baseline is not a fair comparison since it's designed to be effective for the specific tasks (as noted in Appendix B). The paper also doesn't compare against other recent HRL methods (e.g., Option-Critic, HIRO, or other skill discovery approaches) that might be more directly relevant to their framework. The evaluation on the "Ant" robot (Appendix D) shows significant failure modes that undermine the robustness claims.

Third, the paper's presentation is unclear in several critical aspects. The relationship between the pre-training environment and the downstream tasks is not well-defined. The paper states "the construction of which only requires minimal domain knowledge," but doesn't specify what constitutes "minimal" or provide examples of how this minimal knowledge is obtained. The description of the SNN architecture and training procedure (Section 5.2-5.5) lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the experiments, particularly regarding how the latent variable integration is implemented and how the MI regularizer is tuned.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of why the proposed SNN architecture with the MI regularizer produces diverse skills. This could include a formal statement of the conditions under which the skills become diverse, or an analysis of the optimization landscape.

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include more relevant baselines (e.g., Option-Critic, HIRO, or other recent skill discovery methods) and conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (SNN architecture, MI regularizer, hierarchical structure).

3. Clarify the relationship between the pre-training environment and downstream tasks. Provide concrete examples of what "minimal domain knowledge" entails and how it's obtained for different task domains.

4. Improve the reproducibility by providing more details about the implementation (e.g., specific network architectures, hyperparameters, and training procedures) and making the code publicly available in a more accessible format.

5. Address the failure modes observed with unstable robots (like Ant) and discuss how the framework might be extended to handle these cases.

## 5. Questions for the Authors
1. The paper claims the pre-training environment requires "minimal domain knowledge," but doesn't specify what that knowledge is or how it's obtained. Could you provide concrete examples of the minimal domain knowledge required for different task domains (e.g., mobile robots vs. manipulators)?

2. In Section 7.3, the authors note that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" on the mazes. What are the specific conditions under which the MI bonus is beneficial, and what is the theoretical justification for this observation?

3. The paper states that "the span of skills learned is also richer" with bilinear integration and MI bonus, but doesn't provide a quantitative measure of skill diversity. How would you formally measure the diversity of skills, and what metrics would you use to compare different architectures?

4. In Appendix D.2, the authors identify failure modes with unstable robots like Ant. What are the most promising directions for addressing these failure modes, and how would they change the proposed framework?

The paper addresses an important problem in reinforcement learning with a potentially useful framework, but requires substantial improvements in theoretical grounding, experimental evaluation, and clarity before it can be considered for publication at ICLR.
