

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards by learning a diverse span of skills in a pre-training phase with minimal domain knowledge. The key innovation is using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a mutual information-based regularizer to learn multiple interpretable skills simultaneously, rather than training separate policies for each skill. The authors demonstrate that these learned skills can be effectively reused across different downstream tasks, significantly improving exploration and sample efficiency in sparse reward environments. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of current hierarchical RL methods (requiring domain-specific knowledge) and intrinsic reward methods (lacking transferability across tasks).

## Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new concept that represents a bold step forward in hierarchical reinforcement learning, with strong empirical support for the claims.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's novelty and boldness are its strongest assets. The core contribution—using SNNs with mutual information regularization to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training phase with a single proxy reward—is highly novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in current hierarchical RL approaches. This is not just an incremental improvement but a fundamentally new direction that could influence how we think about skill learning in RL.

The paper effectively positions itself within the literature, clearly distinguishing its approach from related work (e.g., Heess et al., 2016; HiREPS; Option-critic). The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the problem space and the limitations of existing methods. The use of SNNs for this purpose is particularly clever, as it leverages the inherent multimodality of these architectures to represent diverse skills while maintaining weight sharing.

The empirical results are compelling. The visitation plots in Figure 4 clearly demonstrate the diversity of skills learned, and the hierarchical use of these skills (Figure 5) shows significantly improved exploration compared to standard approaches. The results on the maze and gather tasks (Figure 6) provide strong evidence that the approach outperforms baselines, including those using intrinsic motivation. The authors are transparent about the limitations (e.g., the impact of random seeds on performance), which adds credibility to their claims.

The paper's willingness to embrace a simple but effective solution (using a single proxy reward for pre-training) is particularly appealing. This approach requires minimal domain knowledge, which is a significant advantage over methods that need carefully designed rewards for each skill or task. The bilinear integration of latent variables and the MI regularizer are well-justified choices that consistently improve skill diversity.

While the paper has some minor methodological limitations (e.g., the MI regularizer doesn't always boost performance across all tasks), these are overshadowed by the significance of the core idea. The reviewer is willing to overlook these minor issues because the paper introduces a genuinely new direction that could have broad impact on hierarchical RL.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of why the MI regularizer sometimes doesn't improve performance (as seen in Figure 6). A deeper analysis of the conditions under which it's most beneficial would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

2. The connection between the learned skills and the specific requirements of downstream tasks could be better articulated. While the paper shows that certain skills (like sideways motion) help in specific mazes, a more systematic analysis of skill-task alignment would be valuable.

3. The discussion of limitations (Section 8) is good but could be expanded to include potential failure modes in more complex environments beyond the Ant robot. A brief analysis of how the approach might scale to more challenging real-world robotics tasks would be helpful.

4. The paper could clarify the relationship between the pre-training environment and the actual downstream tasks. How much domain knowledge is actually required to design the proxy reward? A more concrete example would help readers understand the minimal domain knowledge requirement.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper notes that the MI regularizer doesn't always boost performance. Can you provide more insight into why this happens, and what specific task characteristics make the MI regularizer more or less effective?

2. In Figure 6, the SNN with MI bonus shows higher variance in performance across different random seeds. How does this compare to the variance in the Multi-policy baseline, and what does this imply about the robustness of the approach?

3. The paper mentions that the framework could be extended to use end-to-end training with straight-through estimators. How would you expect this to impact the diversity of skills learned, and what challenges might arise in implementing this?

4. The paper focuses on tasks with sparse rewards, but many real-world robotics tasks have intermediate rewards (e.g., for reaching intermediate goals). How would your framework handle such tasks, and would it still provide a significant advantage over standard methods?
