

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward settings across multiple related tasks. The authors introduce a unified approach using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment with minimal supervision (using a single proxy reward signal), which can then be reused across downstream tasks. The key innovation lies in using SNNs with a mutual information-based regularizer to encourage skill diversity, enabling a single policy to represent multiple distinct behaviors through its latent variables. This approach significantly reduces sample complexity compared to training separate policies for each skill or task, while requiring minimal domain knowledge for the pre-training phase. The paper demonstrates that the learned skills are interpretable, diverse, and effective for solving challenging sparse-reward tasks.

## 2. Decision
Accept

Key reasons: The paper introduces a genuinely novel conceptual approach to skill learning in hierarchical RL, moving beyond incremental improvements to a fundamentally different paradigm. The core idea of using SNNs with MI regularization to learn a diverse span of skills in a unified framework represents a significant conceptual advance that could reshape how we approach hierarchical RL with sparse rewards.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
This paper's novelty and boldness make it stand out from the crowded field of hierarchical RL methods. The authors identify a critical gap in existing approaches: most hierarchical RL methods either require domain-specific knowledge or don't provide clear mechanisms for knowledge transfer across tasks. Their solution—using SNNs as a unified architecture for learning multiple skills simultaneously with a simple proxy reward signal—represents a genuine conceptual leap rather than an incremental improvement.

The paper's core contribution is particularly compelling because:
- It challenges the conventional wisdom of training separate policies for each skill (which requires linearly more samples as the number of skills increases)
- The MI regularization technique effectively encourages skill diversity without requiring complex task-specific reward engineering
- The pre-training setup with a single proxy reward (e.g., robot speed for locomotion tasks) requires minimal domain knowledge, making the approach widely applicable

The empirical results support the claims effectively, with clear ablation studies showing the impact of different design choices (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus strength). The visitation plots in Figure 4 provide compelling visual evidence of the diverse skills learned, and the learning curves in Figure 6 demonstrate significant performance gains over baselines on challenging sparse-reward tasks.

While the paper has some methodological imperfections (e.g., the ad-hoc discretization for MI calculation, limited testing on more complex robots like Ant), these are secondary to the novelty of the core idea. As a reviewer prioritizing novel concepts over methodological perfection, I find these minor flaws acceptable given the paper's significant conceptual contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the framework scales to larger numbers of skills (e.g., K > 6) and whether the MI regularization remains effective at scale.
- It would be valuable to include a comparison with more recent hierarchical RL methods (e.g., recent advances in option-critics or intrinsic motivation approaches) to better position the work in the current landscape.
- The failure modes with the Ant robot (Section D.2) are intriguing but could be more thoroughly analyzed. A brief discussion of how to mitigate these issues would strengthen the paper.
- The paper could clarify the relationship between their approach and the concurrent work by Heess et al. (2016), which also proposes learning skills in a pre-training environment but requires goal specification.

## Questions for Authors
1. How would you expect the performance to scale as the number of skills (K) increases beyond 6? Would the MI regularization need to be adjusted, or would the approach remain effective without modification?
2. The paper mentions that the pre-training environment requires minimal domain knowledge, but how minimal is "minimal"? Could you provide examples of tasks where this approach would fail due to insufficient proxy reward design?
3. In Section 5.5, the authors note that end-to-end training with straight-through estimators is left for future work. Given the promising results with frozen low-level policies, what evidence do you have that end-to-end training would provide significant additional benefits?
