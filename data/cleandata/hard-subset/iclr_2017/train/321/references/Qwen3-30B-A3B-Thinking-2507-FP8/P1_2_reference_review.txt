

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of exploration in sparse reward settings. The authors introduce a two-phase approach: first, learning a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal (e.g., robot speed), and second, using these skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks. The key innovation lies in using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously, combined with an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage skill diversity. The paper demonstrates that their approach can learn interpretable skills (e.g., forward/backward/sideways motion) that significantly improve exploration and sample efficiency in sparse reward environments like mazes and object gathering tasks. The framework is well-motivated, builds upon relevant literature, and shows strong empirical results across multiple robot platforms.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning with a well-motivated, novel approach that effectively addresses a persistent challenge (exploration in sparse reward settings). The empirical results are strong, well-designed, and clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method over relevant baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in three key areas:

First, it identifies a critical gap in the literature: most hierarchical RL approaches either require domain-specific knowledge for skill definition or fail to provide a mechanism for reusing learned skills across multiple tasks. The authors' use of SNNs with a simple proxy reward for pre-training elegantly solves this problem by learning a diverse span of skills without task-specific rewards.

Second, the methodological contributions are substantial. The use of bilinear integration for combining latent variables with observations (rather than simple concatenation) and the MI-based regularizer are well-justified and significantly improve skill diversity. The ablation studies (Fig. 4-5) provide strong evidence for the importance of these design choices.

Third, the empirical validation is comprehensive. The paper demonstrates the approach on both the benchmark Maze and Gather tasks from Duan et al. (2016) and extends it to more complex robots (Snake and Ant). The results consistently show that the hierarchical approach with SNNs outperforms baselines, with the MI regularizer improving skill diversity and performance in many cases. The use of "visitation plots" to visualize skill diversity is particularly effective.

The paper is well-written, clear, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including code availability. The technical depth is appropriate for ICLR, and the results are scientifically rigorous.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following minor improvements to strengthen it further:

1. **Address variance in results**: The paper reports high variance across random seeds (especially in Maze tasks), with some pretrained SNNs failing to learn critical skills. A more detailed analysis of this variance would strengthen the paper. The authors could include a metric quantifying skill diversity (e.g., entropy of skill distribution) or discuss how to select the most useful pretrained SNNs for downstream tasks.

2. **Theoretical grounding**: While the empirical results are compelling, a brief theoretical discussion of why the MI regularizer encourages diversity would strengthen the paper. The authors could briefly explain how the MI term relates to the underlying optimization landscape.

3. **Comparison with more recent work**: The paper cites literature up to 2016, but there have been significant advances in hierarchical RL (e.g., PRL, SAVI) since then. A brief comparison with one or two more recent methods would provide better context for the contribution.

4. **Clarify the MI bonus implementation**: The paper states that the MI bonus "does not always boost performance" (Fig. 6). The authors could provide more insight into when this regularizer is most beneficial and why it sometimes doesn't help.

## Questions for Authors

1. In the Ant robot experiments (D.2), you mention that switching between skills is challenging due to instability. Could you elaborate on potential solutions to this problem, such as modifying the switching mechanism or adding a transition policy?

2. The paper mentions that the MI bonus improves skill diversity but doesn't always improve downstream performance (Fig. 6). Could you provide more insight into the conditions under which the MI bonus is most beneficial?

3. You state that "the span of skills learned is also richer" with SNNs compared to training separate policies. Could you provide a quantitative measure of this "richer" span (e.g., entropy of skill distribution, coverage of state space)?

4. The paper claims that the approach reduces sample complexity by a factor equal to the number of skills. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the actual sample efficiency gains compared to training separate policies?

5. In the Gather task, you note that the hierarchy "shines more" with longer time horizons. Could you provide results for Maze tasks with longer time horizons to see if the same pattern holds?

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning with clear empirical validation of a novel and practical approach. The paper is well-positioned for acceptance at ICLR with the minor revisions noted above.
