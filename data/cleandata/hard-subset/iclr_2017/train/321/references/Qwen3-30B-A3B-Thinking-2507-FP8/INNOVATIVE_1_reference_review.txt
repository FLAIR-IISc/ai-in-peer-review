

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward tasks. The core contribution is a method to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training phase using a minimal proxy reward signal, without requiring domain-specific knowledge about downstream tasks. The authors introduce Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) as a flexible policy architecture that can represent multi-modal policies with weight sharing across skills, and propose an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage skill diversity. The framework then uses these learned skills as building blocks for high-level policies in downstream tasks, significantly improving exploration and sample efficiency. The approach is evaluated on challenging sparse-reward environments including maze navigation and food collection tasks, demonstrating clear advantages over baselines.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a bold, novel approach to skill learning that fundamentally changes how we think about hierarchical reinforcement learning. The core idea of using SNNs with MI regularization to learn a diverse span of skills in a single pre-training phase is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance, even with some limitations in the evaluation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's novelty lies in its conceptual shift from learning skills per task to learning a universal span of skills applicable across tasks. While hierarchical RL and skill learning have been studied extensively, the authors' approach of using SNNs as a unified policy architecture for multi-modal skill representation is a significant conceptual leap. The MI regularizer is a clever adaptation of recent representation learning techniques to the RL domain, and the authors convincingly demonstrate its necessity through ablation studies.

The framework is well-motivated by the limitations of existing approaches: hand-engineered hierarchies require domain knowledge, while intrinsic reward methods don't provide transferable skills. The authors position their work precisely in this gap and demonstrate through careful experiments that their approach overcomes these limitations.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing, with clear ablation studies showing the impact of different architectural choices (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus strength). The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms baselines on challenging sparse-reward tasks, with the SNN approach achieving better exploration coverage than the multi-policy baseline despite using fewer samples for skill learning.

While the paper acknowledges limitations with unstable robots (like the Ant), these are presented as future work rather than fundamental flaws in the core approach. The sample efficiency gains demonstrated in the main experiments are substantial and directly address the paper's central problem.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The claim that the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge" could be strengthened with more concrete examples of how this knowledge is minimal compared to previous approaches (e.g., how much knowledge is required for the proxy reward versus specifying skills for each task).
- The paper mentions that the MI regularizer sometimes doesn't boost performance (Section 7.3), but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of when it's most beneficial. A deeper exploration of this would help practitioners understand when to use the MI bonus.
- The relationship between the MI regularizer and the concept of "diversity" in skill learning could be more explicitly connected to existing literature on representation learning and exploration.
- The discussion of the Ant robot's failure modes (Section D.2) is valuable, but the paper could better position these as opportunities for future work rather than limitations of the core approach.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the minimal domain knowledge requirement for the proxy reward scale to more complex robots or environments beyond those studied in the paper?
2. The paper states that "the set of skills can be used later in a wide collection of different tasks" - what are the theoretical limits to this transferability, and how might the authors quantify the scope of transferable skills?
3. In the experiments, the authors use the same pre-training environment for all downstream tasks. How would the approach perform if the pre-training environment was not perfectly aligned with the downstream tasks' dynamics?
