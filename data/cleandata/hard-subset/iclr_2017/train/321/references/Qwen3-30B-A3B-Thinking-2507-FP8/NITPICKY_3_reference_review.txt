

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning in sparse reward settings, where a span of skills is learned in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer. The authors claim that their approach enables efficient exploration in downstream tasks by learning a diverse set of skills that can be reused across multiple tasks, reducing sample complexity. The paper demonstrates experiments on locomotion and navigation tasks using a Swimmer robot, showing that their method outperforms baselines that rely on intrinsic motivation based on Center of Mass speed.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's fundamental mischaracterization of its methodology as "Stochastic Neural Networks" and the lack of quantitative support for key claims undermine the paper's credibility.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The most critical flaw is the paper's misrepresentation of its core methodology. The authors repeatedly refer to their approach as using "Stochastic Neural Networks" (SNNs), but the described architecture is not an SNN. The paper states: "We use a simple class of SNNs, where latent variables with fixed distributions are integrated with the inputs to the neural network." This is a standard conditional policy approach with categorical latent variables, not a stochastic neural network. Standard SNNs have stochastic units within the computation graph (e.g., RBMs, DBNs), but the authors are simply using a categorical latent variable as a condition to the policy network. This mischaracterization is pervasive throughout the paper, including in the title, abstract, and methodology sections, creating significant confusion about the paper's technical contribution.

Furthermore, the paper makes several unsupported claims without providing quantitative evidence. The authors claim that "the span of skills generated is also richer" (Section 7.1) and "the MI bonus yields less overlapped skills and new advancing/turning skills" (Section 7.1) but provide only qualitative visualizations without any metrics to support these assertions. They state that "the sample efficiency of training K skills is effectively K times better" (Section 5.2), but offer no evidence for this claim - they merely state that "it takes a comparable amount of samples to train an SNN than a regular uni-modal gaussian policy." This is a critical omission, as the paper's entire premise relies on this efficiency gain.

The paper also contains multiple formatting inconsistencies and minor technical errors that suggest poor attention to detail:
- The reference list contains inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., "Bacon and Precup.,? \Q2016\E, Bacon and Precup.,")
- In equation (1), the reward modification is written as "Rnt ← Rnt + αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt )" which would be negative since p̂ < 1, suggesting a potential sign error
- The code availability statement contains a typo: "Code available at: https://github.com/florensacc/snn4hrl 2Videos available at: http://bit.ly/snn4hrl-videos" (the "2" should not be there)
- The paper claims to use "Stochastic Neural Networks" but then describes the architecture as "concatenation" or "bilinear integration" - neither of which is a standard SNN technique

These issues collectively indicate a lack of rigor in the paper's technical presentation, which is particularly concerning for a venue like ICLR where methodological precision is paramount.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the methodology**: If the authors are not actually using SNNs, they should rename their approach to accurately reflect their method (e.g., "Conditional Policy Networks with Categorical Latent Variables" or "Multi-modal Policy Learning via Latent Variables"). The paper's title and core claim must be revised to reflect this.

2. **Provide quantitative metrics**: The paper needs to provide quantitative measures for key claims about skill diversity (e.g., entropy of skill distributions, skill overlap metrics) and sample efficiency (e.g., actual sample counts for different numbers of skills).

3. **Conduct ablation studies**: The paper should include ablation studies to justify the choice of bilinear integration over concatenation and to demonstrate the necessity of the MI bonus, rather than relying on qualitative observations.

4. **Fix technical errors**: Correct the equation (1) typo, standardize citation formatting, and remove the "2" from the code availability statement.

5. **Clarify the relationship to prior work**: The paper should better distinguish its approach from existing work on hierarchical RL (e.g., HiREPS, Option-critic) and explain why SNNs (or their actual method) are necessary for this problem.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide quantitative evidence supporting the claim that "the span of skills generated is also richer" with your method compared to baselines?

2. What specific aspect of your method qualifies as a "Stochastic Neural Network" rather than a standard conditional policy with categorical latent variables?

3. Can you provide the actual sample counts for training the SNN versus training multiple independent policies to support the claim of "sample efficiency of training K skills is effectively K times better"?

4. Why does the paper claim to use "Stochastic Neural Networks" throughout when the method does not match the standard definition of SNNs in the literature?
