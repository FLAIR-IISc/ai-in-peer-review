

# Peer Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward tasks. The authors introduce a pre-training phase where a span of skills is learned using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a minimal proxy reward signal requiring only minimal domain knowledge. The key contributions include: (1) using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously; (2) introducing an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage skill diversity; and (3) demonstrating that these pre-trained skills can significantly enhance exploration and learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach is evaluated on locomotion and food collection tasks, showing superior performance compared to standard exploration methods and baselines.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper makes a strong contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing a method that learns diverse, interpretable skills in a pre-training phase without requiring task-specific knowledge, then effectively reuses these skills across multiple downstream tasks. The methodology is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results convincingly demonstrate the approach's effectiveness.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

**Strong motivation and literature positioning:** The paper clearly identifies the gap between existing approaches to sparse reward problems: hierarchical methods requiring domain knowledge and intrinsic reward methods lacking transferability. The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the paper against prior work, particularly distinguishing it from Heess et al. (2016) and HiREPS.

**Sound methodology:** The authors' use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is well-justified and effectively implemented. The bilinear integration of latent variables and the MI regularizer are well-chosen architectural and training choices that address the core challenges of skill diversity and representation. The paper provides a clear rationale for each design choice and demonstrates their impact through ablation studies.

**Rigorous empirical validation:** The experiments are well-designed with appropriate baselines, including the multi-policy baseline (which requires 6x more samples) and the proxy reward baseline. The authors provide detailed visitation plots, learning curves, and analysis across multiple tasks (Maze 0-3 and Gather) and robots (Swimmer, Snake, Ant). The thorough ablation studies on integration methods and MI bonus strength demonstrate a deep understanding of the method's components.

**Clear demonstration of key claims:** The paper convincingly shows that: (1) SNNs with MI bonus learn a wider span of skills than training separate policies; (2) this span of skills significantly improves exploration; and (3) the hierarchical approach solves sparse reward tasks that standard methods cannot. The results on the Gather task, where the MI bonus consistently improves performance, are particularly compelling.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

**Clarify the sample complexity claim:** The paper states that "the sample efficiency of training K skills is effectively K times better" compared to training separate policies. This is slightly misleading - the SNN is trained with the same number of samples as a single policy, but the pre-training environment may require different sampling strategies. Clarify that the SNN learns K skills with the same sample complexity as a single policy, not K times less.

**Expand on the proxy reward design:** While the paper mentions the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," it doesn't provide guidance on how to design it for different domains. Adding a brief discussion of how to select appropriate proxy rewards for different task types would strengthen the practical applicability of the approach.

**Deepen the analysis of MI bonus effectiveness:** The paper notes that the MI bonus doesn't always improve downstream performance. A deeper analysis of why it helps in some tasks (Gather) but not others (Mazes) would provide valuable insights. Is it related to task geometry, reward structure, or skill requirements?

**Provide more diverse examples of skills:** The paper shows forward/backward/sideways motion as examples of learned skills, but these are somewhat limited. Including more diverse examples of skills (e.g., turning, complex trajectories) would better demonstrate the method's capability to learn useful, non-obvious skills.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the MI regularizer, you use CoM coordinates to compute mutual information. Why not use more detailed state information (e.g., joint angles) to potentially learn more complex skills? How might this affect skill diversity?

2. In the Ant experiments, you note instability when switching skills. Have you considered incorporating a transition policy that learns when to switch skills, or integrating skill switching into the pre-training phase? How might this affect the sample complexity?

3. The paper claims the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge." Could you provide examples of how the proxy reward would be designed for a completely different domain (e.g., robotic manipulation of novel objects)?

4. In the Maze tasks, the MI bonus doesn't always improve performance. What specific characteristics of Maze 0-3 make the MI bonus more or less beneficial, and how might this inform future work?

5. How does the quality of skills learned by the SNN compare to skills learned by training separate policies with the same total number of samples (i.e., K times more samples)? This would clarify the true efficiency gains.

This is a strong contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning with clear practical implications. The paper is well-written, methodologically sound, and provides convincing empirical evidence for its claims. With minor clarifications to the sample complexity claim and additional analysis of the MI bonus, this paper would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
