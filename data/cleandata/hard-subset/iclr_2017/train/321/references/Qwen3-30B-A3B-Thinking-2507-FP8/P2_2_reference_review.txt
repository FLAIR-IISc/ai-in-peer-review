

# Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward environments. The authors introduce a method that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and then leverages these skills in a hierarchical structure for downstream tasks. The key innovation lies in using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies, combined with an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information (MI) to encourage skill diversity. The paper demonstrates that this approach can learn interpretable skills (e.g., forward, backward, sideways locomotion), significantly improve exploration, and outperform baselines on challenging sparse-reward tasks like mazes and food collection. The work is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and provides valuable insights for the RL community.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

Key reasons for this decision:
1. The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in RL (sparse rewards) with a well-motivated, novel solution.
2. The empirical evidence is strong, thorough, and clearly demonstrates the advantages of the proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing the core problem of sample inefficiency in sparse reward environments. The authors effectively position their work within the literature, identifying the limitations of existing approaches (hand-engineered hierarchies requiring domain knowledge, and intrinsic reward methods lacking transferability) and demonstrating how their framework overcomes these limitations. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is particularly clever, as it enables weight sharing across different skills while maintaining diversity.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The visitation plots (Fig. 4) clearly demonstrate that SNNs with the MI regularizer learn a diverse span of skills, outperforming both independently trained policies and SNNs without the regularizer. The exploration comparison (Fig. 5) convincingly shows how the hierarchical structure with pretrained skills significantly expands the agent's exploration capabilities compared to standard Gaussian exploration. Most importantly, the learning curves on sparse reward tasks (Fig. 6) demonstrate that the proposed framework consistently outperforms baselines, achieving faster learning and better final performance.

The paper also demonstrates scientific rigor through:
- Multiple random seeds for all experiments (10 runs per curve)
- Clear ablation studies (e.g., impact of integration method, MI bonus coefficient)
- Transparent hyperparameter choices (Appendix A)
- Reproducibility through code and video availability

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the minimal domain knowledge requirement**: The paper claims the proxy reward requires "minimal domain knowledge," but this is not sufficiently explained. For the Swimmer robot, the proxy reward is "proportional to the magnitude of the speed of the robot." It would be valuable to explicitly state what domain knowledge is required (e.g., "knowing that speed is a useful metric for locomotion") and discuss whether this can be automated or generalized to other domains.

2. **Deepen the theoretical justification for the MI regularizer**: While the paper mentions the connection to InfoGAN, it would strengthen the paper to provide a brief theoretical explanation of why the MI regularizer encourages skill diversity. How does it relate to the underlying RL problem, and what guarantees (if any) does it provide?

3. **Address the inconsistent performance of the MI regularizer**: The paper notes that the MI regularizer "does not always boost performance" (e.g., in some mazes). A deeper analysis of when the MI regularizer is most beneficial and why it sometimes fails would provide valuable insights for practitioners.

4. **Expand the discussion of limitations**: The paper acknowledges limitations with unstable robots like Ant (Appendix D), but it would be helpful to discuss potential solutions to these issues more thoroughly rather than just referencing a technical report.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. Could you elaborate on the minimal domain knowledge required for the proxy reward? For instance, is there a way to automatically determine an appropriate proxy reward without any human intervention?

2. The paper states that "the MI bonus yields less overlapped skills and new advancing/turning skills." Could you provide more specific insights into why the MI regularizer sometimes fails to boost performance (e.g., in Maze 1), and how the optimal value of αH might be determined automatically for different task types?

3. The Snake robot experiments (Appendix C) show promising results, but the paper doesn't discuss how the approach scales to even more complex robots (e.g., with higher-dimensional action spaces or more complex dynamics). Could you provide more insights on the scalability of your approach?

4. The Ant robot experiments (Appendix D) reveal a significant challenge with skill switching. What is the fundamental reason for this instability, and what specific architectural or training modifications would you recommend to address it?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The proposed framework is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and provides valuable insights for the RL community. With the minor revisions suggested above, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
