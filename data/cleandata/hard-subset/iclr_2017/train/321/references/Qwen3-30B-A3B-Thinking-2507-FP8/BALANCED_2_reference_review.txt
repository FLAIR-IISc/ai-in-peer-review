

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse reward learning across multiple related tasks. The authors introduce a method to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a mutual information-based regularizer, which can then be reused across downstream tasks. The key contributions include: (1) leveraging SNNs to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously, (2) introducing an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity, and (3) demonstrating that this pre-trained skill span enables more efficient exploration and learning in sparse reward downstream tasks. The framework is evaluated on a variety of tasks including maze navigation and object gathering with different robotic agents.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper presents an interesting combination of ideas that could be valuable for hierarchical RL, but the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient rigor and the approach has significant limitations that undermine the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Key Strengths:** The problem statement is well-motivated, identifying a genuine gap between hand-engineered hierarchies and non-transferable intrinsic reward methods. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is a natural fit for skill learning, and the information-theoretic regularizer is well-motivated by related work in representation learning. The paper provides clear visualization of learned skills and demonstrates the hierarchical framework's potential for improved exploration.

**Key Weaknesses:**
1. **Inadequate Baselines:** The comparison against the baseline of adding a Center of Mass proxy reward to the downstream task is insufficient. This baseline essentially provides the same reward signal that was used in the pre-training phase, making it an unfair comparison. A proper baseline would be a standard RL algorithm (e.g., SAC, PPO) trained directly on the downstream task without any skill-based hierarchy.

2. **High Variance in Results:** The paper acknowledges high variance in performance across different random seeds for the pre-trained SNNs (Section 7.3). This is a fundamental limitation of the approach, as the performance of the downstream tasks critically depends on the specific skills learned in pre-training. The paper fails to address how to mitigate this variance, which is particularly problematic for real-world applications.

3. **Misleading Performance Claims:** The paper claims that their method "can learn a wide range of skills, which are clearly interpretable as the latent code is varied" (Section 1). While the visitation plots in Figure 4 show some interpretability, the paper doesn't demonstrate that these skills are actually useful for solving the downstream tasks. The performance improvement over the baseline (Section 7.3) is marginal in most cases, and the paper doesn't show that the specific skills learned are necessary for task success.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks any theoretical analysis to support the use of the mutual information regularizer or the hierarchical framework. The claim that "the MI bonus yields less overlapped skills" is purely empirical, without any theoretical grounding.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. **Improve Baseline Comparisons:** Compare against standard RL algorithms (SAC, PPO, TRPO) trained directly on the downstream tasks without any skill-based hierarchy. This would provide a more meaningful evaluation of the proposed framework's contribution.

2. **Address Variance Issue:** Analyze why certain pre-trained SNNs work better than others for specific tasks, and propose methods to reduce this variance (e.g., better pre-training objectives, ensemble methods). The paper's current approach of simply reporting average performance across multiple seeds is insufficient.

3. **Provide More Rigorous Evaluation:** Include ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (SNNs, MI regularizer, hierarchical structure) to the final performance. For example, show how performance changes when removing the MI regularizer or using a different integration method.

4. **Clarify the Scope of the Framework:** The paper claims "our framework can learn a wide range of skills" but doesn't specify what constitutes "wide" or how this compares to other skill learning methods. Provide quantitative metrics for skill diversity and coverage.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper reports that "SNNs with bilinear integration and αH = 0.01" perform best in the Gather task (Fig. 6d). However, the paper also states that "pretraining with MI bonus does not always boost performance" (Section 7.3). Can you clarify when the MI bonus is beneficial, and when it might actually hurt performance?

2. The paper mentions that "the performance of the different pretrained SNNs" varies significantly (Section 7.3). For the Maze 0 task, the authors state that "the one obtained with the random seed 50 has a visitation with a weak backward span (Fig. 9e), which happens to be critical to solve the Maze 0." How can the authors guarantee that the pre-trained SNN will learn the necessary skills for a given downstream task? What mechanisms could be added to the framework to address this issue?

3. The paper states that "the span of skills learned by the SNN" is "clearly interpretable as the latent code is varied." What quantitative metrics are used to measure the interpretability of the skills, and how do they compare to other skill learning methods?
