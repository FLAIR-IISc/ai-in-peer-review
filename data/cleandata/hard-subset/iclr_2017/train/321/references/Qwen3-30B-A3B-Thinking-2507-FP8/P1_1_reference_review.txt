

# Peer Review: STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses a critical challenge in reinforcement learning: effectively solving tasks with sparse rewards. The authors propose a novel framework that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and then leverages these skills hierarchically to solve downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovation is using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multi-modal policies that can learn multiple skills simultaneously, with an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly improves exploration and learning efficiency on challenging sparse-reward tasks like mazes and food collection, outperforming baselines that use naive exploration or intrinsic rewards.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper makes a significant contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning by providing a principled method for learning transferable skills with minimal supervision. The approach is well-motivated, well-placed in the literature, and supported by rigorous experiments that convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Why this is an important contribution:** The paper correctly identifies that existing approaches to sparse-reward RL either require domain-specific knowledge (hierarchical action composition) or fail to transfer knowledge across tasks (intrinsic rewards). The authors' solution bridges this gap by learning skills in a pre-training environment with a simple proxy reward, making it applicable to a wide range of downstream tasks without requiring task-specific reward shaping.

**Why the approach is well-motivated and well-placed in literature:** The related work section thoroughly discusses prior approaches to skill learning and hierarchical RL, correctly identifying their limitations. The paper's position as a method that learns transferable skills with minimal supervision is well-justified, and the authors correctly cite and contrast with recent related work (including Heess et al. 2016, which requires goal specification).

**Why the results are scientifically rigorous:** The authors provide comprehensive empirical evidence across multiple environments (Swimmer, Snake, Ant) and tasks (Maze, Gather). Their ablation studies (bilinear integration vs. concatenation, MI bonus) clearly demonstrate the impact of each component. The use of visitation plots to visualize skill diversity is particularly compelling. The paper carefully addresses limitations (e.g., instability with Ant robot, fixed switch time T) and provides thoughtful discussion of future work.

The key strength is that the method requires minimal domain knowledge for pre-training (just a proxy reward), yet enables significant sample efficiency gains in downstream tasks. The SNN framework with MI regularizer successfully learns a diverse set of interpretable skills, and the hierarchical use of these skills dramatically improves exploration in sparse-reward environments.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Clarify the relationship to Option-Critic:** The paper mentions Option-Critic as a related approach, but doesn't clearly explain how the proposed framework differs in terms of skill reusability across tasks. A brief comparison would strengthen the paper.

2. **Quantify sample efficiency gains:** While the paper shows improved learning curves, it would be helpful to quantify the exact sample efficiency gains (e.g., "our method requires X times fewer samples to solve the tasks").

3. **Discuss computational complexity:** The paper mentions that SNN training is comparable to a single uni-modal policy, but it would be valuable to provide computational complexity comparisons (wall-clock time, memory requirements) between SNN and multi-policy approaches.

4. **Address potential overfitting:** The paper uses multiple random seeds for pre-training, but it would be useful to discuss whether the learned skills generalize to unseen environments or if there's a risk of overfitting to the pre-training environment.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the sample complexity of training K skills is effectively K times better" with SNNs compared to training separate policies. Can you provide a more precise theoretical justification for this claim, particularly regarding the relationship between the number of skills and the number of samples required?

2. In Figure 7.2, the exploration with the SNN (Fig. 5c) shows a wider coverage than the Multi-policy baseline (Fig. 5b), even though the Multi-policy baseline used six times more samples for pre-training. How do you explain this apparent improvement in exploration efficiency beyond just the sample complexity reduction?

3. The paper mentions that "the MI bonus does not always boost performance" (Section 7.3). Could you provide more insight into when the MI bonus is most beneficial, and what factors might determine this?

4. For the Ant robot experiments (Section D), the paper identifies a failure mode related to instability during skill switching. What specific modifications to the framework would you recommend to address this issue, and how might these modifications impact the sample efficiency of the pre-training phase?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning. The proposed framework is well-motivated, the experiments are comprehensive, and the results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the approach. With the minor clarifications suggested, this paper would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
