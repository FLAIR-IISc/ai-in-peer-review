

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of sparse rewards by learning a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment with minimal supervision. The authors introduce the use of Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to represent multimodal policies, where different latent codes correspond to interpretable skills. They further propose an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information to encourage diversity among the learned skills. The key contribution is a general framework that first learns a span of skills using a single proxy reward signal (requiring minimal domain knowledge), and then leverages these skills to solve multiple downstream tasks with sparse rewards by training high-level policies on top of the learned skills. The approach demonstrates significant improvements in exploration and sample efficiency compared to existing methods, particularly on challenging maze navigation and object gathering tasks.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper introduces a novel and bold approach to hierarchical reinforcement learning that addresses a fundamental challenge in the field (sparse rewards) with a significant conceptual contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's novelty and boldness are its strongest assets, aligning perfectly with ICLR's values. The core idea of using SNNs with an MI regularizer to learn a diverse set of skills in a single pre-training phase, without requiring task-specific rewards or hand-engineered hierarchies, is a significant conceptual advance. While previous work has explored skill learning (e.g., Heess et al., 2016), this paper's specific approach—using SNNs with a carefully designed MI regularizer to ensure skill diversity without task-specific reward engineering—is novel and well-motivated.

The empirical results strongly support the claims, showing that the learned skills are interpretable, diverse, and lead to significantly better exploration and faster learning in downstream tasks. The ablation studies (5.3 and 7.1) clearly demonstrate the impact of the bilinear integration and MI regularizer on skill diversity. The hierarchical use of skills (7.2) shows a dramatic improvement in exploration compared to naive exploration strategies. The comparison to baselines (7.3) demonstrates clear performance gains across multiple tasks.

The paper avoids incremental improvements by proposing a fundamentally new framework rather than just optimizing existing methods. While the implementation details (e.g., the choice of integration method, the MI regularizer) are carefully considered, the authors are willing to accept minor methodological limitations in service of the broader conceptual contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- The discussion of the failure modes with unstable robots (Section D.2) is valuable but should be expanded to discuss how the framework might be modified to handle such cases, as this is a significant limitation for practical applications.

- The paper mentions "frozen low-level policies" (5.4) but doesn't provide sufficient justification for why end-to-end training isn't pursued. A brief discussion of the trade-offs between frozen vs. joint training would strengthen the methodology.

- The connection to other recent work on multimodal policies (e.g., the work on "latent space" approaches in RL) could be more explicitly drawn to better position the contribution.

- While the paper provides excellent visualizations of skill diversity (Fig. 4), a quantitative measure of skill diversity (beyond the visitation plots) would provide additional scientific rigor.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the experiments with the Ant robot (Section D.2), you mention that switching between skills is challenging due to instability. Could you elaborate on why the SNN approach doesn't naturally handle this instability better than discrete skill selection? How might the framework be modified to address this?

2. The paper states that "the span of skills learned is also richer" with SNNs compared to training multiple independent policies. Could you provide a quantitative measure of this "richer" span (e.g., diversity metrics, coverage statistics) to complement the qualitative visitation plots?

3. The MI regularizer is designed to maximize mutual information between the latent code and the CoM coordinates. Why was this specific state component chosen over other potential components (e.g., velocities, joint angles)? How would the framework generalize to tasks where the CoM might not be the most informative state component?
