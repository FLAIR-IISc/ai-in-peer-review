

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer. The key contributions include: (1) using SNNs to learn multiple skills simultaneously with weight sharing, (2) introducing a mutual information regularizer to encourage skill diversity, and (3) demonstrating that the learned skills can be reused across different downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The approach requires minimal domain knowledge for the proxy reward in the pre-training phase, and the resulting skills are interpretable and directly applicable to new tasks through a hierarchical architecture.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a technically sound contribution to hierarchical reinforcement learning with clear empirical validation of its claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a well-motivated problem in reinforcement learning: efficiently learning policies for multiple tasks with sparse rewards. The approach is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough comparison to related work on skill learning, hierarchical RL, and intrinsic motivation.

The technical soundness of the paper is strong. The SNN architecture with bilinear integration and MI regularizer is well-justified, with ablation studies demonstrating the impact of each component. The experimental evaluation is rigorous, with multiple random seeds, careful baseline comparisons, and clear visualization of skill diversity through visitation plots. The results on benchmark tasks (Maze and Gather) consistently show that the proposed method outperforms baselines, particularly in sparse reward settings where naive exploration strategies fail.

The paper provides compelling evidence that the SNN approach with MI regularizer learns a wider span of diverse skills compared to training multiple independent policies or using simpler SNN architectures. The hierarchical use of these skills significantly improves exploration in downstream tasks, as demonstrated by the broader coverage in visitation plots and faster learning curves. The authors also provide valuable insights into the limitations of their approach (e.g., with unstable robots like Ant) and potential directions for future work.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity, particularly regarding the additional cost of the MI regularizer and how it scales with the number of skills.
- A more quantitative measure of skill diversity (beyond visual inspection of visitation plots) would strengthen the claim that the MI regularizer effectively increases diversity.
- The discussion of the "pre-trained SNN" variability (Section C.4) could be expanded to include a more systematic analysis of how different random seeds affect performance across tasks.
- The connection between the MI regularizer and the specific task requirements could be more explicitly addressed, particularly for tasks where certain skills are more critical than others.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How does the choice of latent variable dimension K affect the performance, and what are the guidelines for selecting K for new domains?
2. In the pre-training phase, how sensitive is the performance to the specific choice of proxy reward, and what are the practical guidelines for designing it for new domains?
3. The paper mentions that end-to-end training could be done using straight-through estimators. What are the main challenges in implementing this, and how significant would the performance improvement be compared to the current frozen low-level policy approach?
