

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that learns a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer. The authors claim their approach requires minimal domain knowledge for pre-training (using a proxy reward), learns interpretable skills that can be reused across downstream tasks with sparse rewards, and significantly improves exploration efficiency compared to standard methods. The framework is evaluated on maze navigation and object gathering tasks with promising results.

## Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and methodological flaws that undermine the credibility of the claims, particularly regarding the reward modification and pre-training policy selection.

## Supporting Arguments
1. **Critical Technical Error in Reward Modification**: In Equation (1), the authors modify the reward as Rnt ← Rnt + αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt). However, log p̂(Z = zn|cnt) is typically negative (since probabilities are ≤1), making this a negative term. The authors refer to this as a "bonus," but it's actually a penalty that would discourage the desired behavior. This is a fundamental error that affects the core mechanism of their approach. The authors should have written Rnt ← Rnt - αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt) to implement the intended mutual information bonus.

2. **Contradictory Claim About Policy Selection**: The paper claims "there is no cherry picking of the pre-trained policy to use in the sparse environment" (Section 7.3), but Appendix C.4 explicitly states that "the one obtained with the random seed 50 has a visitation with a weak backward span (Fig. 9(e)), which happens to be critical to solve the Maze 0" and that "the lack of learning in Fig. 12(a) for this particular pretrained SNN" occurs because it lacks the necessary skill. This directly contradicts their claim, suggesting they are cherry-picking pre-trained policies for specific tasks.

3. **Formatting Inconsistencies**: The references section contains numerous formatting errors (e.g., "Bacon and Precup.,? \Q2016\E, Bacon and Precup.,"), suggesting a lack of attention to detail. The GitHub URL provided (https://github.com/florensacc/snn4hrl) appears to be a placeholder rather than a valid repository.

4. **Implementation Ambiguities**: Section 5.4 states "If the skills are encapsulated in a SNN, z is used in place of the latent variable," but fails to clarify how this is implemented. The SNN samples a latent code at the beginning of each rollout, but the hierarchical architecture uses the Manager Network's output as the latent variable. This creates confusion about how the SNN is actually used in the downstream tasks.

## Additional Feedback
- **Equation Correction**: The reward modification must be corrected to properly implement the mutual information bonus. The authors should clarify whether they intended a positive or negative term in Equation (1).

- **Clarify Policy Selection**: The authors should either admit they cherry-pick pre-trained policies (and explain how this affects generalizability) or provide evidence that their method consistently learns all necessary skills without selection.

- **Fix References**: The references section requires careful formatting to match ICLR standards.

- **Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims to "minimize the total sample complexity" but provides no theoretical analysis of the sample complexity improvement. A brief theoretical argument would strengthen the paper.

- **GitHub URL**: Provide a valid GitHub URL for the code repository.

## Questions for Authors
1. In Equation (1), why do you add a negative term as a "bonus" instead of subtracting it? Shouldn't the mutual information bonus be implemented as a positive addition to encourage higher mutual information?

2. In Appendix C.4, you state that some pretrained SNNs lack skills necessary to solve Maze 0. How do you address this issue in practice, and does this contradict your claim that there's "no cherry picking of the pre-trained policy"?

3. The GitHub URL provided appears to be a placeholder. Please provide the correct URL for the code repository.

4. Can you provide a theoretical analysis of the sample complexity improvement of your approach compared to training from scratch for each task?

The technical errors identified are substantial and affect the core contribution of the paper. While the paper has interesting ideas and promising experimental results, the methodological inconsistencies and formatting errors indicate a lack of rigor that would prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper would need significant revisions to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
