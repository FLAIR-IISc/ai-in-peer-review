

# Review for "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that first learns a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with an information-theoretic regularizer. The authors claim this approach enables efficient exploration in downstream tasks with sparse rewards by providing a set of reusable skills. The method uses a proxy reward signal during pre-training that requires minimal domain knowledge, and then leverages these skills through a high-level policy that selects among them. The authors demonstrate their approach on locomotion and maze navigation tasks, showing improved exploration and task performance compared to baselines.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims about the methodological advantages of the proposed approach. The evidence provided is limited to a small number of simple environments, and the paper does not rigorously demonstrate why the specific SNN architecture and MI regularization are necessary or superior to simpler, established methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution hinges on two novel components: the use of SNNs with specific architectural choices (bilinear integration) and the information-theoretic regularizer. However, the paper fails to provide convincing evidence that these components are necessary or significantly superior to existing methods.

First, the paper claims that using SNNs reduces sample complexity by a factor equal to the number of skills, but this claim is not properly validated. The paper compares against a "Multi-policy" baseline that trains six separate policies, which itself requires six times more samples than the SNN approach. This comparison is inherently biased and does not demonstrate that the SNN approach is truly more sample-efficient than established methods. The paper provides no evidence that the SNN approach would outperform standard RL methods that do not use hierarchical structures.

Second, the paper's empirical results are limited to the Swimmer robot in relatively simple maze environments. The paper acknowledges limitations with more complex robots like Ant, where skill switching leads to instability and failure. This undermines the claim of a general framework applicable to a wide range of tasks. The paper also fails to demonstrate that the MI regularization is consistently beneficial - the results in Section 7.3 show that the bonus does not always boost performance.

Third, the paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to prove the necessity of the proposed components. For instance, the paper shows that simple concatenation of latent variables rarely yields distinctive behaviors, but does not explore whether standard RL methods could achieve similar skill diversity through better exploration techniques or reward shaping without the need for SNNs.

Finally, the paper's theoretical justification for using SNNs is weak. The authors claim SNNs "can easily represent multi-modal policies, while achieving weight sharing among the different modes," but provide no theoretical analysis to support this claim or to compare the representational capacity of SNNs to other methods for skill representation.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct more comprehensive experiments on a wider range of environments and robots, particularly addressing the instability issues with complex agents like Ant.

2. Provide a more rigorous comparison against established hierarchical RL methods beyond the simple baselines presented. The comparison to "standard reinforcement algorithms" that "could not solve them" (per Duan et al., 2016) is not sufficiently detailed or fair.

3. Include ablation studies that isolate the contributions of the SNN architecture, bilinear integration, and MI regularization. This would help determine whether the observed improvements are due to the specific components proposed or simply the hierarchical structure.

4. Provide a clearer theoretical justification for why SNNs are particularly suited for this problem compared to other methods for representing multi-modal policies.

5. Address the limitations of the approach more thoroughly, particularly regarding the sensitivity to the choice of pre-training environment and the instability issues with complex robots.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could the authors provide evidence that their SNN approach is genuinely more sample-efficient than standard RL methods that do not use hierarchical structures, rather than just comparing to a baseline that requires six times more samples for the same number of skills?

2. The paper claims that their approach "reduces sample complexity uniformly" across tasks, but the experiments are limited to a few simple environments. Could the authors provide evidence that this approach scales to more complex, real-world tasks where sample efficiency is truly critical?

3. The MI regularization is presented as a key component, but the paper shows that it does not always improve performance (Section 7.3). Under what conditions is this regularization most beneficial, and how should practitioners determine whether to use it?

4. The paper acknowledges that the choice of pre-trained skills significantly impacts performance on maze tasks (Section C.4). How can practitioners ensure they obtain the appropriate skills for a given downstream task, and what metrics should be used to evaluate skill diversity beyond the CoM visitation plots?
