

# Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that learns a diverse set of skills during pre-training using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) with a proxy reward signal, without requiring task-specific knowledge. The key innovation is the use of an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage diversity in the learned skills. The authors claim that their approach enables efficient exploration in downstream tasks with sparse rewards by leveraging these pre-trained skills through a hierarchical architecture.

## 2. Decision
Reject

## 3. Key Reasons for Rejection
1. A critical sign error in Equation (1) would likely prevent the proposed MI regularizer from working as intended, undermining the paper's core contribution.
2. The paper lacks quantitative metrics to support claims about "large span of skills" and "diversity," relying solely on visual inspection of visitation plots.

## 4. Supporting Arguments

The most severe issue is a critical sign error in Equation (1), which states: "Rnt ← Rnt + αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt)". Since p̂(Z = zn|cnt) is a probability (between 0 and 1), log p̂(Z = zn|cnt) is always negative. Therefore, adding αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt) to the reward would actually decrease the reward, working against the stated goal of encouraging diversity. This suggests the authors likely meant to write "Rnt ← Rnt - αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt)" (which would maximize MI), but the sign error as written would prevent the MI bonus from functioning correctly. Given the centrality of this regularizer to their contribution, this error is severe.

The paper also makes qualitative claims about "large span of skills" without providing quantitative metrics. For instance, in Section 7.1, they state "we show that the span of skills generated is also richer" and "80% of the trained SNNs with bilinear integration acquire at least forward and backward motion," but they don't provide a quantitative measure of "span" or "richness" beyond visual inspection. The paper claims "the MI bonus yields less overlapped skills," but doesn't provide metrics like entropy, mutual information values, or diversity indices to support this.

Additionally, the paper contains multiple minor but significant formatting inconsistencies:
- Inconsistent reference formatting ("Chen et al.,? \Q2016\E" in references)
- Inconsistent notation (using "zn" in the algorithm but "z" elsewhere)
- Repetitive and poorly formatted references to videos
- In Section 5.5, the statement "π(a|s, z) is still a Gaussian" is misleading - if z is part of the observation, the policy should be π(a|s, z), not just a Gaussian distribution

These minor issues, while individually small, collectively suggest a lack of attention to detail that is particularly concerning for a paper making significant technical claims.

## 5. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Correct the sign error in Equation (1)**: The current formulation would diminish the reward, not encourage diversity. If this was intentional, please provide a clear explanation of why a negative term would promote diversity. Otherwise, the equation needs correction.
  
- **Provide quantitative metrics**: Supplement visual inspection with metrics like entropy, mutual information values, or diversity indices to objectively measure the span of skills. For example, compute the entropy of the latent code distribution across different states or the mutual information between latent codes and state features.

- **Improve consistency**: Ensure consistent notation throughout the paper (e.g., use "z" consistently instead of "zn" in the algorithm). Proofread references for consistent formatting.

- **Clarify the pre-training environment**: The paper claims "the swimmer locomotion task described therein corresponds exactly to our pretrain task," but doesn't specify whether the environments are truly identical. Please clarify if the pre-training environment is identical to the benchmark's swimmer task.

- **Address the MI bonus implementation**: Provide specific values of αH used in experiments and explain how they were chosen.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify the sign error in Equation (1)? Is it intentional, or should it be Rnt ← Rnt - αH log p̂(Z = zn|cnt)?
2. What specific quantitative metrics did you use to claim "large span of skills" and "diversity" beyond the visitation plots? 
3. How did you determine the value of αH used in the experiments, and how sensitive are your results to this hyperparameter?
4. Could you confirm whether the pre-training environment is truly identical to the benchmark's swimmer task as claimed in Section 6? If not, what differences exist and how might they affect the results?
