

# Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

## Summary
The paper proposes a framework for hierarchical reinforcement learning that uses Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse span of skills in a pre-training environment with minimal domain knowledge. The authors introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information to encourage diversity in the learned skills, which can then be reused across different downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core claim is that this approach reduces sample complexity by enabling the reuse of skills across multiple tasks, while avoiding the need for hand-engineered hierarchies or task-specific intrinsic rewards.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes a promising claim but fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to support its novelty and effectiveness. The experimental validation is limited, and the comparison against strong baselines is inadequate.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim is that their SNN-based approach with the MI regularizer enables learning a diverse span of skills that can be reused across tasks, reducing sample complexity. However, the evidence provided falls short of establishing this claim with scientific rigor. 

First, the experimental evaluation is limited to a small number of environments (only two main tasks: Locomotion + Maze and Locomotion + Food Collection) with a maximum of four different mazes. This is insufficient to demonstrate the generality of the approach across diverse reinforcement learning domains. The paper claims their method "outperforms state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation results like VIME," but Appendix B provides only a single comparison on the Gather task with limited context. The comparison against established HRL methods (e.g., Option-Critic, HiREPS) is notably absent, which is a critical omission for a paper claiming to advance hierarchical reinforcement learning.

Second, the paper fails to provide adequate ablation studies to isolate the contributions of its key components. The authors claim that the bilinear integration and MI regularizer are "key to consistently yield a wide, interpretable span of skills," but the evidence for this claim is limited. The results in Figure 7.1 show that the MI regularizer sometimes improves performance (e.g., in the Gather task) but not always (e.g., in Maze 0), yet the paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this happens or under what conditions the MI regularizer is most beneficial.

Third, the paper acknowledges limitations with unstable robots like Ant (Section D.2), noting that "switching between skills is a hard task because of the instability," but fails to provide a rigorous analysis of this failure mode or propose concrete solutions. The appendix mentions "failure modes" but doesn't integrate these insights into the main evaluation, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the approach.

## Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse environments and tasks, particularly those with different reward structures and action spaces. This would better demonstrate the generality of their approach.

2. Conduct a more comprehensive comparison against established HRL methods, including Option-Critic, HiREPS, and other recent hierarchical RL algorithms. The paper should clearly position its method relative to these baselines.

3. Provide a thorough ablation study that isolates the contribution of each component (SNN architecture, bilinear integration, MI regularizer) to the overall performance. This would help determine whether the observed improvements are due to the SNN approach itself or specific design choices.

4. Address the failure modes with unstable robots more rigorously. The paper should provide a systematic analysis of why switching between skills fails for certain agents and propose specific modifications to handle these challenges.

5. Clarify the theoretical basis for the claim about sample complexity reduction. The statement that "the sample complexity of training K skills is effectively K times better" is presented as an empirical observation but lacks theoretical justification or detailed empirical evidence.

## Questions for Authors
1. How do you explain the inconsistent performance of the MI regularizer across different tasks (e.g., beneficial in Gather but not in Maze 0)? What specific characteristics of the tasks determine its effectiveness?

2. The paper claims that "the sample complexity of training K skills is effectively K times better" compared to training separate policies. Can you provide more precise empirical evidence for this claim, including the actual sample counts for different numbers of skills?

3. Why does the paper not compare against established hierarchical RL methods like Option-Critic or HiREPS, which are directly relevant to the problem of skill learning and reuse?

4. The failure modes with unstable robots like Ant are acknowledged but not thoroughly analyzed. Could you provide more details about how the switching between skills leads to instability and what specific modifications to the approach might address this issue?

The paper presents an interesting idea that could contribute to hierarchical reinforcement learning, but it currently lacks the rigorous empirical validation and thorough comparison against established methods required for acceptance at ICLR. The authors should address these concerns before the paper can be considered for publication.
