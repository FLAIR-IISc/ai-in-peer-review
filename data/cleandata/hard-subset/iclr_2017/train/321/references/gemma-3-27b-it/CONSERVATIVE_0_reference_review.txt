This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery and subsequent task learning. The core idea is to pre-train an SNN with a proxy reward to learn a diverse set of skills, then utilize these skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors argue that SNNs, combined with a mutual information-based regularization, allow for learning a richer and more interpretable skill space than traditional methods.

**Reject.** While the motivation for hierarchical RL and skill discovery is sound, the paper presents an overly complex solution without sufficient empirical evidence to justify its novelty. The improvements over established baselines are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance, and the reliance on specific hyperparameter tuning (particularly the MI bonus) feels brittle and lacks robustness.

The primary concern is the lack of convincing comparison to simpler, well-established techniques. While the authors compare against VIME (Houthooft et al., 2016) in the Gather task, the comparison is limited. A more thorough comparison against other intrinsic motivation methods (e.g., ICM) and hierarchical RL approaches (e.g., options frameworks) is crucial. The claim of significant improvements necessitates demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods, which is currently lacking.

Furthermore, the results presented, while positive, are highly sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the mutual information bonus. The ablation studies focusing on the impact of the bilinear integration and MI bonus are helpful, but more analysis is needed to understand *why* these choices are critical. The success hinges heavily on these architectural and regularization decisions, and the paper does not adequately explain their importance or provide guidelines for their selection in other environments.

The extensive supplemental material, while detailed, further highlights the complexity of the approach. The dependence on numerous random seeds and the varying performance across these seeds raise concerns about the reproducibility and reliability of the results. The acknowledgment of failure modes with the Ant robot also casts doubt on the generalizability of the proposed method. The discussion of needing “end-to-end training” with estimators hints at further complexity and potentially undermines the claim of a relatively straightforward framework. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the advantages of SNNs:** The paper argues that SNNs are suitable for representing multi-modal policies. However, other methods exist for achieving this (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians). The authors need to articulate a clearer advantage of using SNNs specifically.
*   **Simplify the framework:** The combination of SNNs, bilinear integration, MI regularization, and hierarchical structure creates a complex system. Exploring simpler variations of this framework could provide valuable insights into the contributions of each component.
*   **Robustness analysis:** Perform a more extensive robustness analysis, varying hyperparameters and evaluating performance across a wider range of environments.
*   **Statistical significance:** Provide statistical significance tests to support the claims of improvement.
*   **Downstream Task Choice:** The choice of Maze tasks feels somewhat contrived. While useful for demonstration, exploring more realistic and complex downstream tasks would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why are SNNs specifically advantageous over alternative methods for representing multi-modal policies (e.g., mixture of Gaussians)?
2.  What is the theoretical justification for the bilinear integration? Can you provide insights into why it outperforms simple concatenation?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the MI bonus weight (αH)? Is there a principled way to determine an appropriate value for this hyperparameter?
4.  The paper mentions failure modes with the Ant robot. What specific modifications or additions to the framework are needed to address these issues?
5.  The extensive supplemental material suggests the results are highly dependent on the random seed. What steps were taken to ensure the reproducibility of the results, and how representative are the reported results of the overall performance distribution?
