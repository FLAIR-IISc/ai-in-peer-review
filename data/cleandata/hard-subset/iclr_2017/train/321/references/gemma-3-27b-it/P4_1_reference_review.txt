This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, subsequently used to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key idea is to use the stochasticity of SNNs to represent multiple skills simultaneously, guided by a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage diversity. The learned skills are then utilized by a high-level policy for solving specific tasks.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the presented evidence is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach, particularly the SNN component and the MI regularization. The empirical results, while showing improvements, lack sufficient rigor and comparison to strong baselines, and the justification for design choices is often weak.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The comparisons to existing methods, especially intrinsic motivation techniques like VIME, are not comprehensive. The paper mentions outperforming VIME, but primarily in an appendix and using settings previously shown to favor intrinsic motivation.  A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art HRL algorithms (e.g., option discovery methods, FeUdal networks) and modern exploration strategies is necessary. Furthermore, the benefits of the SNN specifically are not well isolated. It's unclear if the performance gain comes from the SNN architecture itself, the MI regularization, or simply the hierarchical structure. Ablation studies directly comparing a deterministic neural network with and without the MI regularization would be crucial.

2. **Insufficient Justification & Analysis of Design Choices:** The motivation for bilinear integration in the SNN is presented, but its superiority isn’t firmly established. The impact of hyperparameters, such as the MI coefficient (alpha_H), is not thoroughly analyzed. The choice of the CoM coordinates for the MI calculation feels arbitrary; a justification for why this specific state component is relevant for skill diversity would strengthen the argument. The switch time T is mentioned, but analyzed only superficially.  Why is a fixed switch time chosen, and how does it impact performance?

3. **Concerns About Scalability & Robustness (Ant Robot):** The discussion of the Ant robot reveals a significant limitation – instability when switching between skills. This severely limits the applicability of the proposed framework to more complex robots. While acknowledged, the paper doesn’t offer a convincing solution to this problem. The technical report link provides some details, but addressing this issue within the main paper would be essential.  The variability in performance across different pre-trained SNNs further suggests a lack of robustness.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written but can be more concise. The introduction could be streamlined to focus more sharply on the contributions.
* **Reproducibility:**  While code is available, the extensive hyperparameter tuning and the number of components involved (SNN architecture, MI regularization, TRPO settings) makes full reproducibility challenging without more detailed information.
* **Figures:**  Figures 4 & 5 are key to understanding the skill diversity and exploration, but could benefit from more quantitative metrics alongside the visualizations (e.g., coverage area, entropy of trajectories).
* **Future Work:** The listed future directions are sensible, but should be prioritized based on addressing the current limitations of the framework (e.g., instability, robustness).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more rigorous comparison of your framework against state-of-the-art HRL algorithms and modern exploration techniques, detailing the setup and results?
2.  What is the quantitative impact of the bilinear integration in the SNN versus a simpler concatenation of latent variables?
3.  What strategies have you explored or considered to address the instability issue observed with the Ant robot when switching between skills?
4.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the MI coefficient (alpha_H), and how did you determine the optimal value used in your experiments?
5.  Can you provide more insight into why certain pre-trained SNNs perform significantly better than others, and how can this variability be reduced?
6.  Why was a fixed switch time (T) selected, and have you investigated adaptive switch time strategies?
