This paper tackles the challenging problem of sparse reward reinforcement learning, specifically in scenarios requiring hierarchical control. It proposes a novel framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for learning a diverse set of skills during a pre-training phase, subsequently utilized by a high-level policy for task-specific learning. The core idea – using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies and employing an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity – is a compelling and potentially impactful contribution.

**Reject.** While the idea is novel and exciting, the paper feels underdeveloped and the experimental validation, despite being extensive, doesn’t fully convince. The impact of the information-theoretic regularizer feels marginal based on the presented results, and the overall performance gains over strong baselines (even with the limitations acknowledged by the authors) aren't substantial enough to warrant acceptance at ICLR.

The paper’s strengths lie in its conceptual novelty. The use of SNNs to learn a skill manifold is a clever approach to address the exploration problem in hierarchical RL. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional exploration strategies and the need for more sophisticated methods. The pre-training/fine-tuning paradigm is also well-motivated. The related work section is comprehensive. However, several weaknesses hamper the paper's overall impact.

First, the ablation studies, while present, are insufficient to truly isolate the contribution of each component. Specifically, the impact of the mutual information regularization feels weakly demonstrated. While the authors *show* different visitation plots, the quantitative benefits in the downstream tasks are not compellingly linked to the degree of skill diversity achieved through the MI bonus.  The comparison to the baseline using the CoM reward alone is not sufficient, as it's a relatively weak baseline. A comparison to more recent state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods (beyond VIME) would strengthen the results.

Second, the experimental setup feels complex, involving many variations of hyperparameters and architectures (different integration methods, alpha values). While exploration is good, it's not entirely clear *why* certain configurations perform better. The paper lacks a deeper analysis of the learned skill representations. Visualizing the learned latent space and understanding what features each latent code corresponds to would add significant value.

Third, the variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs (especially highlighted in the Ant robot experiments) raises concerns about the robustness of the approach. While acknowledged, this issue isn’t adequately addressed.  The authors mention the potential for instability in the Ant robot, but the explanation feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more thorough investigation into the causes of instability and potential mitigation strategies is needed.

Finally, the writing could be more concise. The paper repeats some concepts and the detailed descriptions of the methods can be trimmed without losing clarity.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Stronger Ablation Studies:** Perform a more systematic ablation of the MI regularization, varying the coefficient α across a wider range and explicitly quantifying the skill diversity (e.g., using a metric to measure the distance between learned behaviors).
*   **Skill Representation Analysis:** Include visualizations of the latent space and provide interpretations of what each latent code represents in terms of robot behavior.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the factors that contribute to the variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs. Explore techniques to improve the robustness of the learning process.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Compare against more recent and sophisticated intrinsic motivation techniques, providing a more thorough benchmark.
*   **Conciseness:** Streamline the writing and remove redundant information.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a quantitative metric to measure the diversity of the learned skills and correlate it with the performance in downstream tasks?
*   What is the computational cost of adding the MI regularization during training? Does it significantly increase the training time?
*   Why was bilinear integration chosen over other integration methods? Were alternative methods explored, and if so, what were the results?
*   Can you elaborate on the challenges encountered when applying this approach to the Ant robot and what steps could be taken to address the instability issues?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the pre-training environment? Would different proxy rewards lead to a different set of skills and impact downstream performance?
