This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, which are then used to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key idea is to use the stochasticity of SNNs to represent multiple policies simultaneously, and further encourage diversity through a mutual information (MI) regularizer. The framework then utilizes these pre-trained skills within a hierarchical structure, training a high-level policy on top of the learned skills for each task.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning – exploration in sparse reward environments – with a novel and well-motivated approach. The use of SNNs combined with an information-theoretic regularizer offers a compelling way to learn diverse skills, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in improving sample efficiency across several challenging tasks.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of SNNs, MI regularization, and a hierarchical structure is a novel contribution. While individual components (HRL, SNNs, intrinsic motivation) have been explored, their integration in this specific way appears unique and potentially impactful. The use of MI to directly encourage skill diversity within the SNN framework is a particularly interesting aspect.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a clear and technically sound approach. The mathematical formulation of the MI regularizer is well-defined, and the connection to existing work on information-theoretic exploration is clearly established. The experimental setup appears rigorous, with comparisons to relevant baselines. The architecture of the SNN and the hierarchical policy learning are clearly explained.
* **Results:** The experimental results, particularly those demonstrating improved performance compared to established methods (including the baseline of adding intrinsic rewards directly to the downstream task) are convincing. The ablation studies demonstrating the impact of the MI bonus and bilinear integration are informative. The visualization of learned skills (visitation plots) provides qualitative evidence supporting the claim of skill diversity. The fact that the approach works across different robots (Swimmer, Snake) strengthens the conclusions.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Discussion of limitations:** While the paper acknowledges some limitations (e.g., instability with the Ant robot), a more thorough discussion of potential drawbacks and failure cases would be beneficial. For example, the sensitivity of the MI regularization coefficient (αH) could be discussed, and the potential for negative transfer between skills could be explored.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable. The impact of different SNN architectures, MI weighting, and policy optimization parameters on performance is not fully explored.
* **Computational Cost:** The computational cost of training SNNs compared to traditional policies should be discussed. While the paper mentions sample efficiency, the training time and memory requirements are not explicitly addressed.
* **Clarification on implementation details:** The implementation details concerning the estimation of the posterior probability p̂(Z = z|c) in the MI regularization could be more detailed. The implications of the discretization of the state space for higher dimensional states are also worth elaborating.
* **Theoretical justification:** The paper relies heavily on empirical evidence. A more theoretical justification for why SNNs are particularly well-suited for learning diverse skills could strengthen the contribution.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* The ablation studies focus on the MI bonus and bilinear integration. Were other architectural choices for the SNNs (e.g., different types of stochastic units) explored and, if so, why were the chosen ones selected?
* How does the performance of the framework scale with the number of tasks in the collection M? Are there any diminishing returns or practical limitations on the size of M?
* The paper mentions potential for negative transfer between skills. Were any mechanisms explored to mitigate this issue, such as skill pruning or regularization?
* Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources (e.g., GPU hours) required to train the SNNs and high-level policies?
* In the Ant experiments, what specific modifications or techniques would be necessary to address the instability issues encountered when switching between skills?
