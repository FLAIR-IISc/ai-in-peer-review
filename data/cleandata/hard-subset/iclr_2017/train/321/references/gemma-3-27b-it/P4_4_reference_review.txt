This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework utilizing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, subsequently leveraged for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to use SNNs, combined with a mutual information (MI) regularizer, to encourage exploration and learn a broad range of skills with minimal domain knowledge. The learned skills are then used as building blocks within a high-level policy for task-specific learning.

**Reject.** While the paper addresses an important problem in RL – sample efficiency in sparse reward settings – and presents a novel approach incorporating SNNs and an MI regularizer, the experimental results, particularly the substantial variance reported and the limited comparative analysis, do not sufficiently support the claims of significant performance improvements. The contributions, while interesting, are not strong enough to warrant acceptance at ICLR given the current presentation.

The approach is well-motivated. The challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons in RL are clearly articulated, and the rationale for exploring hierarchical approaches and intrinsic motivation is well-established. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is also a reasonable approach. The connection to existing work is comprehensive, citing relevant papers in HRL, intrinsic motivation, and stochastic neural networks. The formulation is logical, breaking down the problem into a pre-training phase for skill acquisition and a subsequent phase for task-specific learning.

However, the support for the claims is weak. The core issue lies in the significant variance reported across different runs, especially in the Maze tasks (see Section 7.3 and Appendix C.4). This suggests the method is sensitive to initialization or other stochastic factors and lacks robustness. The authors acknowledge this, attributing it to the possibility of some pretrained SNNs lacking crucial skills (e.g., backward motion). However, this is a substantial weakness that undermines the claim of reliably learning a broad set of useful skills. While the authors compare against a baseline with a CoM reward, this comparison seems insufficient, and lacks comparison against stronger recent baselines in HRL. The impact of the MI bonus is also not conclusively demonstrated; its benefit is described as mixed. Furthermore, the ablation studies, while present, are not entirely convincing. The paper relies heavily on visualization (visitation plots) which, while helpful, are not a substitute for rigorous quantitative evaluation. The fact that the performance is quite variable highlights that the learned skills are not consistently transferring.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Variance Reduction:** The authors should investigate methods to reduce the variance in performance. This could involve ensembling multiple SNNs, using more robust optimization techniques, or carefully analyzing the factors contributing to the performance variation.
*   **Baseline Comparison:**  A more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art HRL algorithms (e.g., Option-Critic, HIREPS) is crucial. A fair comparison should use the same evaluation settings and metrics.
*   **Ablation Studies:** More thorough ablation studies are needed to isolate the contributions of the SNN architecture, the MI regularizer, and the bilinear integration. The current ablation studies are limited.
*   **Analysis of Learned Skills:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned skills. Beyond visualization, quantify the diversity and usefulness of the skills. Can the skills be clustered or categorized? How do different skills contribute to solving different tasks?
*   **Stability and Generalization:**  Address the instability issues observed with the Ant robot (Appendix D.2). Explore techniques to improve the robustness of skill transfer to more challenging environments.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters, particularly the MI coefficient (αH).
*   **Clarify the Relationship between Latent Code and Skill:** While the authors state a correlation, the actual relationship and impact of each latent code on the behavior needs to be further clarified. Is it a one-to-one mapping, or can a single skill be represented by multiple codes?
*   **Why bilinear integration?** Explain in greater detail *why* bilinear integration outperforms simple concatenation. A more theoretical justification would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific steps are being taken to address the high variance in performance observed across different runs?
2.  How do the learned skills generalize to tasks that are significantly different from the pre-training environment?
3.  What are the computational costs associated with using SNNs compared to traditional neural networks?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned skills, quantifying their diversity and usefulness beyond the visitation plots?
5.  How does the choice of the switch time T (in the high-level policy) affect performance, and are there any strategies for adaptively adjusting T during learning?
