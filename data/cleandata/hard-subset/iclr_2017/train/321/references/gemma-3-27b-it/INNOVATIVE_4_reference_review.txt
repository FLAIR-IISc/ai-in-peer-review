This paper tackles the problem of sparse reward reinforcement learning by proposing a hierarchical framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery during a pre-training phase. The core idea is to learn a diverse set of low-level skills using a proxy reward, then transfer these skills to downstream tasks with sparse rewards by training a high-level policy on top of them. The use of SNNs, combined with a mutual information regularization term, aims to encourage the learning of a broad and interpretable skill space.

**Reject.** While the approach is intriguing and presents a novel combination of techniques – SNNs, mutual information regularization, and hierarchical RL – the empirical validation feels incomplete and the benefits aren't convincingly demonstrated across all tasks. The results, while promising, don't overwhelmingly outperform established baselines, and the variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs raises concerns about the robustness of the method. The paper prioritizes novelty, which is appreciated, but falls short on thoroughly demonstrating practical advantage.

The key strength of the paper lies in its novelty. The use of SNNs for skill learning *is* a fresh perspective, and the MI regularization is a reasonable approach to encouraging skill diversity. The idea of pre-training a skill space with a simple proxy reward and then transferring that to multiple tasks is a compelling strategy for addressing sparse reward challenges. The ablation studies (figures showing the impact of bilinear integration and MI bonus) are helpful in understanding the contribution of different components.

However, the empirical evaluation has limitations. The comparison to baselines is somewhat uneven. While the paper acknowledges comparison to previous work (Duan et al., 2016; Houthooft et al., 2016), the presentation of these comparisons feels cursory, and it's not immediately clear how much improvement is gained. The relatively small number of tasks tested (a few variations of mazes and a gathering task) limits the generalizability of the findings. The variance in performance across different SNNs, as highlighted in section C.4, is particularly concerning. This suggests that the skill learning process is sensitive to initialization and may not consistently yield a useful skill set. The failure modes discussed for the "Ant" robot also cast doubt on the robustness of the approach for more complex, unstable systems. The improvement shown on the Gather task with increased complexity to size 10, feels marginal and doesn't clearly demonstrate the framework's effectiveness.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Baseline Comparisons:** A more rigorous comparison to stronger baselines (e.g., more recent state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods, hierarchical RL algorithms) is needed. Include error bars on the performance plots to better visualize the variance.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the factors that contribute to the variability in performance across different pre-trained SNNs. Can this be mitigated through better initialization strategies or regularization techniques?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters (e.g., the MI weighting factor αH, the number of latent variables K) would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Study on Manager Network:** An ablation study on the Manager Network architecture might be useful. How does the performance change with different network sizes or activation functions?
*   **Skill Visualization:** Beyond visitation plots, consider visualizing the learned skills in a more interpretable way. For example, can you extract key features or behaviors from each skill?
*   **Extend to More Tasks:** Test the framework on a wider range of tasks with different characteristics (e.g., varying levels of sparsity, different state and action spaces) to assess its generalizability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why was TRPO chosen as the policy optimization algorithm? Were other algorithms considered and, if so, what were the reasons for opting for TRPO?
2.  The paper mentions the possibility of end-to-end training using straight-through estimators. Were any preliminary experiments conducted in this direction? If so, what were the results?
3.  What is the computational cost associated with training the SNNs compared to training traditional neural network policies?
4.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the proxy reward during pre-training? Are there guidelines for designing an effective proxy reward for a given set of downstream tasks?
5.  Can you elaborate on the reasons for the instability observed with the "Ant" robot? Are there any specific modifications to the approach that could address this issue?
