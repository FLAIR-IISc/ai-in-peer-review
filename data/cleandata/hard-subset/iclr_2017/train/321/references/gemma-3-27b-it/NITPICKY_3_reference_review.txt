This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework utilizing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment. These skills are then leveraged to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to use the inherent multi-modality of SNNs, encouraged by a mutual information (MI) regularizer, to represent a spectrum of behaviors, and subsequently utilize these behaviors as building blocks for more complex tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of utilizing SNNs for skill discovery within an HRL framework is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws in presentation, methodology, and experimental rigor that, cumulatively, undermine confidence in the presented results and conclusions. The extensive length of the paper doesn’t compensate for a lack of clarity and precision.

The primary concerns stem from the following:

1. **Lack of Ablation Studies and Justification of Design Choices:** The paper introduces several components – bilinear integration within the SNN, the MI regularizer, the specific proxy reward – without providing sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the individual contribution of each. For instance, the rationale for choosing bilinear integration over simpler concatenation isn't convincingly presented; the performance gain, if any, isn't clearly quantified. Similarly, the effect of the MI coefficient (αH) feels empirically determined rather than theoretically motivated. The choice of the proxy reward is also largely unexplained.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Detail and Rigor:** The experiments, while attempting to replicate established benchmarks, lack the meticulous detail required for reproducibility. Hyperparameters (beyond a brief appendix) are not sufficiently discussed, and the impact of random seeds is acknowledged as significant but not thoroughly analyzed. The claim that "each of the latent codes in the categorical distribution will correspond to a different, interpretable skill" feels overstated given the acknowledged variance across runs. The multiple, lengthy appendices suggest the core paper struggled to contain the necessary detail, indicative of a complex and potentially over-engineered methodology.

3. **Overly Assertive Claims and Lack of Nuance:** The authors frequently make strong claims about the advantages of their approach without sufficient supporting evidence. For example, stating the framework "can learn a wide range of skills, which are clearly interpretable" requires significantly more rigorous demonstration than visual inspection of visitation plots. The paper dismisses previous work too readily, often highlighting minor differences while overlooking similarities in core ideas. The multiple caveats and future work sections throughout the paper reveal a lack of confidence in the current results.

4. **Formatting and Writing Issues:** The paper contains numerous instances of awkward phrasing, grammatical errors, and inconsistencies in notation. The writing style is often verbose and convoluted, making it difficult to follow the central arguments. The extensive use of citations, while demonstrating familiarity with the literature, sometimes feels excessive and disrupts the flow of the text.



**Additional Feedback (For Potential Revision):**

*   **Clarify the motivation for each design choice.** Why bilinear integration? Why this specific proxy reward? What is the theoretical basis for the MI regularizer?
*   **Provide a comprehensive ablation study.** Systematically evaluate the impact of each component of the framework.
*   **Improve the experimental setup.** Report all hyperparameters, conduct multiple runs with different random seeds, and provide statistical significance tests.
*   **Refine the writing and formatting.** Proofread the paper carefully to eliminate grammatical errors and improve clarity.
*   **Moderate the claims.** Avoid overly assertive statements and acknowledge the limitations of the work.
*   **Streamline the presentation.** Consider consolidating information from the appendices into the main body of the paper.
*  **Clarify what is gained by using SNNs.** It is not clear what advantage the use of SNNs provide over using a Mixture of Gaussians implemented with separate neural networks.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of bilinear integration over simpler alternatives?
*   What is the theoretical justification for the specific form of the MI regularizer used in this work?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the proxy reward?
*   Can you provide a quantitative measure of the diversity of skills learned by the SNNs, beyond visual inspection of visitation plots?
*   What are the computational costs associated with training and using SNNs compared to traditional neural networks?
*   What is the variance in performance observed across different random seeds, and how is this accounted for in the analysis?
