This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase, and then uses these skills to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea of using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies and then regularizing their diversity with a mutual information (MI) objective is a compelling approach to address the exploration challenges in sparse reward environments. The experimental validation on locomotion and maze tasks demonstrates the potential of the proposed method.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a novel and interesting idea, the experimental results, while positive, don’t fully convince me of its significant advantage over existing methods, and the presentation lacks sufficient detail to assess the robustness and generalizability of the approach. The comparisons often rely on fairly weak baselines or lack statistical significance, and key design choices are not always sufficiently justified.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The comparisons are primarily against a basic intrinsic reward baseline and a multi-policy approach trained independently. While the authors acknowledge and cite related work in HRL and intrinsic motivation, they lack a direct comparison to more sophisticated state-of-the-art algorithms in these areas (e.g., HER, Hindsight Experience Replay). Without this comparison, it’s difficult to assess whether the gains observed are due to the SNN and MI regularization, or simply due to the benefits of any hierarchical approach. The reported performance on the Gather task appears promising, but the claim of outperforming VIME requires more concrete evidence and statistical analysis.

2. **Lack of Statistical Significance & Robustness Analysis:** The experiments report average performance, but provide little information regarding the variance across different random seeds. Section C.4 explicitly mentions large variability in performance depending on the pre-trained SNN, which suggests the method is sensitive to initialization and training conditions. A proper statistical analysis (e.g., confidence intervals, statistical tests) is needed to demonstrate the reliability of the results. The Appendix mentions unstable behavior with the Ant robot. This lack of robustness needs to be addressed.

3. **Insufficient Justification of Design Choices:** Several key design choices, such as the specific architecture of the SNN (bilinear integration), the choice of the proxy reward, and the MI regularization coefficient, are not thoroughly justified. The rationale behind using bilinear integration over concatenation is given, but further investigation into other potential integration methods would be beneficial. The impact of different proxy rewards on the learned skills isn’t explored. A more systematic ablation study would help understand the contribution of each component.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Expand Comparative Evaluation:** Include comparisons to stronger baselines, such as HER, and other recent HRL algorithms.
* **Provide Statistical Analysis:** Report confidence intervals and statistical significance tests for all results.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (SNNs, MI regularization, bilinear integration, proxy reward).
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings (e.g., MI regularization coefficient, learning rate).
* **Clarify Implementation Details:** Provide more details regarding the implementation of the SNN and the MI regularization. What optimizer is used, and for how many steps?
* **Address Robustness Concerns:** Investigate the reasons for the instability observed with the Ant robot and explore potential solutions.
* **Explain Failure Cases:** Provide a more detailed analysis of failure cases, particularly those related to the pre-trained SNNs. Why do some pre-trained SNNs perform poorly?
* **Video Quality and Clarity:** The cited videos are important, but should have better explanations or captions to clarify the behaviors being demonstrated.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide a statistical comparison to HER or other strong baseline algorithms?
* What is the variance in performance across different random seeds, and is this variance statistically significant?
* What is the computational cost of training the SNN compared to a standard neural network policy?
* Why was bilinear integration chosen over other potential integration methods, and have you experimented with alternatives?
* What are the limitations of the proposed framework, and what future research directions do you envision to address these limitations?
* Could you clarify the specific implementation details of the MI estimation, including the grid resolution and the method used to estimate the posterior probabilities?
