This paper tackles the challenge of sparse reward reinforcement learning, particularly in hierarchical settings, by proposing a novel framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning. The core idea is to pre-train an SNN with a simple proxy reward to develop a diverse set of skills, which are then utilized as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards via a high-level policy. The authors motivate this approach by highlighting the limitations of existing exploration strategies and hierarchical RL methods that require domain expertise or struggle with transfer. They introduce an information-theoretic regularizer to further encourage skill diversity during pre-training.

**Reject.** While the idea of using SNNs to learn a diverse skill set and then composing them hierarchically is intriguing and novel, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating its effectiveness and robustness. The experimental results, while promising, lack the rigor needed to confidently support the claims, especially concerning the advantage over existing baselines and the impact of the MI regularizer. The significant variability in performance depending on the random seed of the pre-trained SNN is concerning.

Here's a breakdown of my reasoning:

1. **Novelty and Potential:** The approach *is* novel. Utilizing SNNs in this manner – to learn a *distribution* of skills and then transfer them – is a worthwhile direction. The theoretical motivation for the MI regularizer is sound, and the idea of pre-training a skill set is a sensible approach to address sparse rewards.

2. **Limited Empirical Support:** The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation.
   * **Baseline Comparisons:** The comparisons to baselines, while present, aren't always sufficiently convincing. The improvement over the intrinsic reward baseline in the Gather task appears modest, and the advantage isn’t clearly demonstrated across all environments. The claim of outperforming VIME (Houthooft et al., 2016) is made, but without a direct comparison in the same experimental setup.
   * **Variance and Robustness:** The significant performance variation across different pre-trained SNNs (reported in Appendix C.4) is a major concern. This suggests the approach is sensitive to initialization and the pre-training process, raising questions about its reliability and generalizability.  The authors acknowledge this, but don’t sufficiently address it.
   * **Ablation Studies:** The impact of the bilinear integration *and* the MI bonus are not clearly disentangled. While the authors show results with different configurations, a dedicated ablation study systematically evaluating each component's contribution would be crucial.
   * **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters (like the MI coefficient alpha) isn’t adequately explored.

3. **Insufficient Detail & Justification:**
    * **SNN Architecture:** While the authors mention bilinear integration, a more detailed explanation of the SNN architecture and the choice of hyperparameters would be helpful.
    * **Manager Network:** The Manager Network appears somewhat simplistic (two-layer FNN). Justification for this design choice is lacking. Is a more complex manager necessary for more intricate tasks?
    * **Switch Time (T):** While the authors briefly analyze the switch time, more in-depth investigation, particularly linking it to the complexity of the skill set and the downstream task, would be beneficial.

**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Stronger Baselines:** Include direct comparisons with state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods (like VIME) in the exact same experimental setup.
* **Robustness Analysis:** Conduct a more thorough investigation into the sources of variability in performance across different SNNs. Consider techniques to stabilize the pre-training process.
* **Ablation Studies:** Perform systematic ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component (bilinear integration, MI regularizer, SNN vs. deterministic networks).
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Provide a more comprehensive hyperparameter search and sensitivity analysis.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the empirical results are important, a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the benefits of using SNNs for skill learning could strengthen the paper.
* **Code Release:** The code is available, which is excellent. Encourage authors to release trained models to promote reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* The paper states the SNNs "can represent complex action distributions," but doesn't delve into *why* this is beneficial for skill learning compared to standard deterministic policies. Can you elaborate on this?
*  Given the high variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs, what strategies could be employed to mitigate this issue and ensure more consistent results?
* Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the bilinear integration method over other integration techniques?
* The Manager Network architecture seems relatively simple. Have you explored more complex architectures, and if so, what were the results?
* Could you comment on the computational cost of using SNNs versus standard deterministic networks, especially during pre-training and downstream task learning?
