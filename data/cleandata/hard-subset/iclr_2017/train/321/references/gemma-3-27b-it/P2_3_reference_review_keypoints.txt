- Paper proposes an HRL framework using Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn diverse skills via a mutual information (MI) regularizer for better exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks.

- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

- Clear problem definition and motivation: well-motivated limitations of existing exploration approaches in sparse rewards and connection to prior HRL/intrinsic motivation work.

- Novelty: using SNNs for skill learning and introducing an MI regularizer to prevent mode collapse and encourage distinct behaviors.

- Strong empirical results: improved sample efficiency and performance on locomotion+maze and locomotion+food tasks compared to baselines, including intrinsic-reward methods.

- Ablations: MI bonus and other ablation studies provide insight into component contributions.

- Thorough experiments and analysis: varied maze configurations, multiple robots, visitation plots to visualize learned skills, and acknowledgment of limitations (e.g., Ant robot).

- Code availability: public code enhances reproducibility.

- Request for computational cost discussion: need more detail on training cost of SNNs versus standard networks.

- Hyperparameter sensitivity: recommend analysis of sensitivity to MI coefficient, architecture, and other key hyperparameters.

- Clarify relation to option learning: explicitly connect learned skills to options and discuss pros/cons.

- Expand discussion on failure modes: elaborate on instability with Ant and potential remedies or adaptations.

- Figure clarity: improve labeling and visual presentation of some figures (e.g., Fig. 1).

- Question on MI coefficient sensitivity: how performance varies with αH and whether there is saturation.

- Question on end-to-end training: whether fine-tuning low-level SNNs during downstream tasks (e.g., via straight-through estimators) was tried and results.

- Question on integration methods: whether other integration types (e.g., multiplicative) were tested versus bilinear integration.

- Question on scalability: discuss applicability and challenges for more complex, high-dimensional environments with subtle rewards.
