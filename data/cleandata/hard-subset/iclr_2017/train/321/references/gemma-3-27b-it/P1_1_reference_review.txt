## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenge of sparse reward reinforcement learning by proposing a hierarchical framework. The core idea is to first pre-train a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to learn a diverse set of skills using a simple proxy reward, and then leverage these skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors argue that SNNs are well-suited for learning multi-modal policies, representing different skills, and further enhance skill diversity through a mutual information-based regularization term.  They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on locomotion and maze navigation tasks, showing improved performance compared to existing methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the paper presents a potentially promising approach, the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant and consistent advantage over existing methods, and the presentation lacks sufficient detail to fully evaluate the contributions. The experimental setup seems sensitive to hyperparameter choices, and the benefits of the MI regularization and SNN architecture are not clearly established.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The reported results, while positive, are often marginal or comparable to existing baselines (especially when considering the complexity added by the SNN framework). The comparison to VIME is mentioned but lacks details and the authors admit the proxy reward baseline performs surprisingly well. The advantage is most apparent in the Gather task, but even there, the improvement isn't overwhelmingly significant.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper explores the impact of different components (bilinear integration, MI bonus) but lacks comprehensive ablation studies to isolate their individual contributions. For example, the effect of the bilinear integration *without* the MI bonus isn’t explicitly shown, making it difficult to assess its true value.
* **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters & Random Seeds:** The authors acknowledge significant performance variation across different random seeds and the importance of pre-training quality, which suggests the approach is sensitive to hyperparameter tuning. This sensitivity isn’t adequately addressed, raising concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results. The paper mentions the large variance in performance depending on the pre-trained SNN, which calls into question the consistency of the method.
* **Insufficient Detail in Experiments:** While code is provided, the experimental setup and hyperparameter details are somewhat sparse, hindering reproducibility. Specifically, the precise configurations for the neural network architectures (number of layers, hidden units) and the TRPO algorithm are not always clearly stated. More details regarding the training process would be valuable.
* **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of SNNs and hierarchical RL is interesting, the individual components – SNNs, MI regularization, hierarchical RL – are not entirely novel. The paper needs to better articulate what the truly unique contribution is and how it advances the field beyond existing work.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify the Role of the MI Bonus:** The theoretical justification for the MI bonus is well-presented, but its practical impact seems somewhat limited.  A more detailed analysis of how the MI bonus affects the learned skills (e.g., visualizing the skill distribution) would be helpful.
* **Explore Alternative Skill Representation:**  Instead of relying solely on SNNs, consider comparing the performance with other methods for learning diverse skill sets (e.g., variational autoencoders).
* **Address the Scalability Concerns:** While the paper demonstrates success on relatively simple tasks, it's unclear how well the approach would scale to more complex environments with higher dimensionality.
* **More Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** A more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation and hierarchical RL algorithms would strengthen the evaluation. Specifically, provide a direct comparison on the exact same tasks, with the same hyperparameters, when possible.
* **Address instability in Ant robot:** The authors briefly mention the instability issues with the Ant robot. Providing more detail on these challenges and potential solutions would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the bilinear integration appears to be crucial for learning diverse skills? What specific advantages does it offer over simpler integration methods?
* How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the MI coefficient (αH)? Is there a principled way to tune this hyperparameter?
* Can you provide a visualization of the learned skill distribution (e.g., using t-SNE or PCA) to demonstrate the diversity of the skills learned by the SNN?
*  Why did you choose to fix the switch time T, and how might the performance be affected by adapting this value during training?
* Could you clarify the impact of the different random seeds on the pretraining performance? What strategies can be used to improve the consistency of the learned skills?
* The paper mentions a technical report on the Ant robot, could you provide a link?
