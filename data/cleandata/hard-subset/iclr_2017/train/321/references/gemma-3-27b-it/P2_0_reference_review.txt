## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of exploration in hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) for tasks with sparse rewards. The authors propose a framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a simple proxy reward. These pre-trained skills are then used as building blocks for learning high-level policies for downstream tasks, reducing sample complexity.  A key contribution is the use of a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage diversity in the learned skills. The results demonstrate improved performance on benchmark sparse reward tasks compared to existing methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.  The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to skill discovery and hierarchical RL. The experimental results are promising, particularly the demonstration of improved performance in sparse reward environments. The use of SNNs with a MI regularizer is a compelling approach for learning diverse skills, and the pre-training framework offers a practical solution for improving sample efficiency.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing exploration strategies in sparse reward environments and positions HRL as a potential solution. The motivation for learning skills *before* task-specific training, and the challenges of doing so, are well explained.
* **Novel Approach:** Combining SNNs with an MI regularizer for skill learning within a hierarchical framework is a novel contribution. The theoretical grounding for using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is sound. The MI regularizer addresses a critical issue of skill diversity that isn't adequately addressed in prior work.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the locomotion + maze/gather tasks demonstrate a clear advantage over baselines, including those using intrinsic motivation. The ablation studies (e.g., effect of bilinear integration and MI bonus) provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component.  The extension to the Snake robot provides further evidence of the method’s generalizability.
* **Well-Written and Structured:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and proposed solution. The methodology section is detailed and provides enough information to reproduce the experiments.



**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameters, a more detailed discussion of their sensitivity and the rationale for the chosen values would be beneficial.  How robust is the performance to changes in the MI weight (alpha)?
* **Computational Cost:**  The computational overhead of training SNNs compared to standard neural networks is not explicitly discussed.  A brief analysis of the computational cost would be helpful.
* **Further Analysis of Skill Diversity:**  While visitation plots are useful, a more quantitative metric of skill diversity could be valuable.  For example, calculating the average pairwise distance between learned policies (e.g., using a KL divergence or similar measure).
* **Clarify the Role of Random Seeds:** The numerous mentions of experiments run with multiple random seeds are somewhat confusing.  It would be clearer to explicitly state whether reported results are averages across seeds, and include standard deviations where appropriate.
* **Expand Discussion of Failure Cases:** The mention of the Ant robot struggling with skill switching is a valuable observation. Expanding on the reasons for this failure (e.g., instability, imperfect skill learning) and potential solutions would strengthen the paper.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Tuning:** What methods were used to tune the hyperparameters, particularly the MI weight (alpha)? Did you observe diminishing returns or instability with very high values of alpha?
2. **Skill Transferability:** How sensitive is the pre-trained skill set to the similarity between the pre-training environment and the downstream tasks?  Would the performance degrade significantly if the pre-training environment were substantially different from the mazes and gathering tasks?
3.  **Computational Cost:** Could you provide a rough estimate of the training time for the SNN-based approach compared to training separate policies for each skill?
4. **Skill Evaluation:** Besides visualization, did you explore any other metrics to quantify the diversity of learned skills?  If so, what were the results?
5.  **Ant Robot Failure:**  What specific modifications or extensions to the framework would be most promising to address the instability issues observed with the Ant robot? (e.g., learning a transition policy, using a more robust policy optimization algorithm, modifying the skill switching mechanism).



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning. Addressing the suggestions above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
