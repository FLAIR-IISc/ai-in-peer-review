This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework utilizing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, which are then leveraged for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea – using the multi-modality of SNNs, encouraged by a mutual information regularizer, to pre-train a useful skill set – is promising and addresses a key challenge in HRL: automatic skill discovery. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology and experimental results.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a novel approach and demonstrates some promising results, the experiments lack sufficient statistical rigor and the benefits over strong baselines aren't convincingly established, especially considering the complexity introduced. The claim of significant improvement, particularly in the Gather task, is not robustly supported.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Statistical Significance:** The results presented in Fig. 6, particularly for the Gather task, show considerable overlap in the learning curves. Without error bars or statistical tests, it's difficult to determine if the observed differences are statistically significant, and not simply due to random variation. The statement that "our SNN hierarchical approach outperforms state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation results" feels overstated without a quantitative comparison showing statistical significance. The comparison with the “CoM reward” baseline is helpful, but a comparison with more established intrinsic motivation methods, beyond a mention in the text, is crucial.

2. **Impact of Pre-training Variance:** The authors acknowledge a large variance in performance depending on the pre-trained SNN (Section C.4). This is a significant weakness. If the performance heavily depends on the specific initialization and training of the SNN, the method lacks robustness and the practical utility is questionable. The paper doesn’t adequately address this issue – simply observing the variance isn’t sufficient; an investigation into *why* this variance exists and potential mitigation strategies are needed.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper introduces several components – SNNs, bilinear integration, MI regularization. While each is justified, the paper lacks a thorough ablation study to demonstrate the individual contribution of each component. For example, what is the performance with SNNs *without* the MI regularization? How much does the bilinear integration improve over a simple concatenation? This would help isolate the key innovations and understand their impact.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Clarify the Role of MI Regularization:** The rationale behind using mutual information with respect to the CoM coordinates specifically could be better explained. Why is this a good proxy for skill diversity, and are there alternative measures that could be considered?
* **Investigate Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance might be sensitive to hyperparameters like the MI coefficient (αH). A sensitivity analysis would reveal the robustness of the approach.
* **Alternative Baselines:** Consider comparing against more recent and sophisticated HRL methods beyond VIME, potentially including option discovery algorithms or meta-RL approaches.
* **Visualize Learned Skills:** Provide more qualitative analysis of the learned skills. Beyond visitation plots, can you demonstrate the behaviors of different latent codes in a more intuitive way (e.g., short videos)?
* **Address the Switching Issue (Ant Robot):** The appendix mentions instability issues with the Ant robot.  This is a crucial observation and warrants more in-depth discussion. What are the potential solutions? Could smoother transitions between skills be beneficial?
* **Code Quality and Reproducibility:** Ensure the code is well-documented and readily reproducible, as this is vital for the community to validate and build upon the work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide statistical tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate the statistical significance of the performance differences reported in Fig. 6?
* What is the source of the observed variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs? Are there any strategies to mitigate this variance, such as ensemble methods or more robust training procedures?
* What were the results of ablation studies, specifically comparing the performance with and without MI regularization, and with and without bilinear integration?
* Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the CoM coordinates were chosen as the target for mutual information maximization?
