## Peer Review for ICLR 2024

**Paper:** STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of exploration in sparse reward reinforcement learning environments, particularly those requiring long horizons. The authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase. These skills are then utilized as building blocks for high-level policies trained on specific downstream tasks. A key contribution is the use of a mutual information (MI) regularizer during pre-training to encourage skill diversity. The authors demonstrate that this framework improves sample efficiency and performance on several challenging robotic manipulation and navigation tasks compared to standard RL and intrinsic motivation baselines.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject** with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper presents an interesting idea and shows promising results, significant concerns regarding clarity, rigor, and reproducibility prevent a clear recommendation for acceptance at this time.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Lack of Clarity & Motivation:** While the introduction outlines the problem well, the connection between the proposed solution (SNNs and the MI regularizer) and the problem isn’t always clear.  The motivation for *why* SNNs are particularly well-suited for learning a diverse skill set beyond their ability to represent multi-modal policies isn’t fully fleshed out. Furthermore, the authors frequently reference prior work without sufficiently explaining how their approach differs or builds upon these advancements.
* **Empirical Evaluation Concerns:** The experiments, while extensive, lack sufficient detail and comparative analysis. While the authors compare against a baseline with an intrinsic reward, comparisons to more sophisticated HRL algorithms or recent exploration techniques are missing.  The impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., the MI weight αH) isn’t consistently analyzed.  The claim of consistently learning a diverse set of skills is not unequivocally supported; several plots show overlap between skills, especially without the MI bonus. The variance in results across different SNN initializations (mentioned in Section 7.3 and Appendix C.4) is a significant issue and suggests instability.
* **Reproducibility:** The code is available, which is commendable. However, the numerous hyperparameters and the reliance on TRPO (which is sensitive to implementation details) make full reproducibility difficult. The paper lacks a detailed description of the experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning procedures.
* **Overly Ambitious Scope:** The paper attempts to cover a significant amount of ground, from theoretical motivations to architectural details and extensive experimentation. This breadth comes at the cost of depth in several areas.

**4. Additional Feedback (for revision):**

* **Strengthen the Motivation:** Provide a more compelling explanation of *why* SNNs and the MI regularizer are expected to be effective for hierarchical skill learning. Connect the theoretical properties of SNNs more directly to the problem of exploration.
* **Comparative Analysis:** Include comparisons to stronger baselines, such as Option-Critic, HIRO, or other state-of-the-art HRL algorithms.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters, including αH, network architecture, and TRPO parameters.
* **Address Variance:** Investigate the source of the variance in performance across different SNN initializations. Could pre-training be improved to yield more consistent results?
* **Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning procedures, training time, and computational resources used.
* **Clarify Technical Details:** Provide clearer explanations of the MI calculation and the bilinear integration used in the SNN architecture.
* **Streamline Presentation:** Focus the paper on the most compelling contributions and results. Consider removing or simplifying some of the less essential details.
* **Address reviewer comments from other reviewers.**

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* The paper mentions the instability of the Ant robot and suggests future work to address it. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered with the Ant robot and the potential solutions you are considering?
* The MI bonus appears crucial for skill diversity. However, the optimal value of αH seems problem-dependent. What strategies can be used to automatically tune this parameter?
* The paper compares against a baseline with a CoM speed reward. This reward seems quite specific. What would happen if you used a different intrinsic reward signal?
*  How does the complexity of the bilinear integration scale with the dimensionality of the state and latent spaces? Is it computationally feasible for very high-dimensional observations like images?
* The paper claims the approach reduces sample complexity. Could you provide a more quantitative analysis of the sample efficiency gains compared to the baselines (e.g., learning curves with different sample budgets)?




In conclusion, the paper presents an intriguing approach to HRL with potential benefits. However, the current presentation lacks the clarity, rigor, and reproducibility required for publication at a top-tier conference like ICLR. Addressing the feedback above, particularly focusing on improving the clarity of the motivation, strengthening the empirical validation, and addressing the reproducibility concerns, will significantly improve the paper's chances of acceptance upon resubmission.
