## Peer Review for "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of efficient exploration and learning in reinforcement learning tasks with sparse rewards and long horizons. The authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment using a proxy reward. These learned skills are then used as building blocks for solving downstream tasks, significantly reducing sample complexity. The key contributions include a novel application of SNNs for skill learning, an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage skill diversity, and a demonstration of the framework's effectiveness in challenging navigation tasks.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject** with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper presents an interesting idea and shows promising results, the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor and clarity, and the presentation could be significantly improved. The core idea is valuable, but the evidence presented doesn’t yet convince me of its substantial advantage over existing methods.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** The paper compares against a weak baseline (intrinsic reward only) and mentions outperforming VIME without providing quantitative results *within the paper*. A proper comparison against state-of-the-art HRL algorithms (e.g., option-critic, HiREPS, or more recent hierarchical approaches) is crucial to establish the contribution of the proposed method.  Simply stating that existing methods *cannot* be applied directly is insufficient; attempts to adapt or implement them for comparison are necessary.
* **Overly Complex Presentation & Lack of Clarity:** The paper is densely written and suffers from a lack of clarity in several sections. The notation is sometimes inconsistent, and the explanations of the information-theoretic regularizer and bilinear integration could be more accessible.  The flow of ideas is difficult to follow at times, hindering understanding of the contribution.
* **Variability in Results and Insufficient Analysis:** The significant variability observed in the results (particularly highlighted in Appendix C.4) raises concerns about the robustness of the approach. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't offer a thorough analysis of the causes or potential mitigation strategies.  The claim of consistently learning a diverse set of skills feels unsupported given the variability.
* **Experimental Setup and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While Appendix A details hyperparameters, the sensitivity of the approach to these parameters (particularly the MI bonus weight and network architecture) isn’t sufficiently explored.  A more systematic ablation study is needed.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Strengthen Baselines:**  Implement and compare against strong HRL baselines. If adaptation is needed, clearly explain the adaptations made.
* **Clarify Presentation:**  Reword the paper for improved clarity and conciseness. Define all notation clearly and consistently. Break down complex concepts (like bilinear integration) with more intuitive explanations.
* **Address Variability:** Investigate the sources of variability in the results.  Is it due to random seeds, hyperparameter sensitivity, or other factors? Discuss potential solutions (e.g., ensemble methods, robust optimization).
* **Ablation Study:**  Conduct a more thorough ablation study to assess the impact of different components of the framework (e.g., the SNN architecture, the MI bonus, the bilinear integration).
* **Visualizations:**  Include more informative visualizations.  Beyond visitation plots, consider visualizations of the latent space to understand the relationships between learned skills.
* **Code Quality and Reproducibility:** Ensure the code is well-documented and easily reproducible.  Provide clear instructions for running the experiments.
* **Discuss Limitations:**  More thoroughly discuss the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly regarding its applicability to different types of environments and robots.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Baseline Comparison:** Could you provide quantitative results comparing your approach to VIME and at least one other strong HRL baseline, ideally implemented within the same experimental framework?
2. **Variability Analysis:** What specific factors contribute to the observed variability in the results, and what steps could be taken to mitigate this variability?
3. **MI Bonus Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of the MI bonus weight (αH)? Did you perform a grid search to optimize this parameter, and if so, what were the results?
4. **Latent Space Visualization:** Can you provide a visualization of the latent space learned by the SNN, and explain how the different latent codes relate to the learned skills?  (e.g., using t-SNE or similar dimensionality reduction techniques)
5. **Scaling to More Complex Robots:** You mention issues with instability when using the Ant robot. What specific modifications or extensions would be necessary to address this issue and make the framework more robust to unstable robots?
6. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of training the SNN compared to training a traditional deterministic policy?



I believe this work has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of HRL. However, the current presentation and validation are insufficient to support a positive recommendation. I encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above and resubmit a revised version of the paper.
