This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, subsequently utilizing these skills to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to use the stochasticity of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies, enhanced by a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage skill diversity, and then employ these learned skills within a high-level policy for task-specific problem solving.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting idea with potential, the sheer volume of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and lack of rigorous validation ultimately undermines confidence in the presented results and conclusions. The excessive detail devoted to minutiae distracts from the core contributions and leaves the reader questioning the overall scientific rigor.

The primary concerns stem from a lack of clarity regarding experimental setup and a concerning lack of robustness demonstrated by the proposed method. The experiments, while comprehensive in their variety of tasks, are presented with insufficient detail to allow for replication. The authors state they are following settings from a benchmark paper (Duan et al., 2016), but crucial hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, network sizes) are only reported in an appendix, obscuring their importance. Furthermore, the performance is highly sensitive to random seeds, particularly for the Ant robot, highlighting a lack of robustness and raising concerns about the generalizability of the approach. The extensive appendices, while attempting to provide more details, only exacerbate the impression of a project struggling with consistency.

Specifically:

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Reporting:** The reliance on numerous hyperparameters, many of which are only mentioned in the appendix, and the sensitivity to random seeds create doubt about the reliability of the findings. The authors acknowledge variance but do not sufficiently address this issue.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the authors explore different components (bilinear integration, MI bonus), the ablation studies are not rigorous enough. It’s unclear *why* specific choices are superior. The claims about bilinear integration being essential seem unsubstantiated.
*   **Inconsistent Notation & Clarity:** The paper suffers from numerous instances of imprecise language and inconsistent notation. For example, the definition of ‘C’ changes without clear explanation. The level of detail devoted to describing minor implementation choices feels excessive while overlooking critical validation steps.
*   **Overly Detailed & Redundant Explanations:** The paper is unnecessarily verbose, dwelling on implementation details that do not significantly contribute to the core message. Sections are repetitive and lack concise summarization.
*   **Presentation of Results:** Figures and tables are often poorly labeled or lack sufficient context. The discussion of the results focuses too much on specific examples and not enough on overall trends or statistical significance.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision, not influencing the rejection decision):**

*   **Streamline the Presentation:** Eliminate redundant explanations and focus on the key innovations of the work.
*   **Improve Experimental Rigor:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to clearly demonstrate the benefit of each component of the framework. Provide error bars and statistical significance tests.
*   **Address Robustness:** Investigate the sensitivity to random seeds and explore techniques to improve the robustness of the approach.
*   **Clarify Notation & Definitions:** Ensure consistent notation throughout the paper and provide clear definitions for all variables and concepts.
*   **Simplify the Appendices:** Condense the appendices, focusing on essential details that are not crucial for understanding the main results.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of hyperparameters? Were any hyperparameter optimization techniques employed?
*   How does the performance of the SNN-based approach compare to alternative methods for learning diverse skills, such as variational autoencoders or generative adversarial networks?
*   Why is the Ant robot so sensitive to random seeds? What steps could be taken to improve the robustness of the approach for this robot?
*   The paper mentions that the bilinear integration yields better results. Could you provide a theoretical explanation for why this is the case?
*   How does the performance scale with the dimensionality of the state space? Would the approach remain effective for even higher-dimensional observations, such as raw images?
