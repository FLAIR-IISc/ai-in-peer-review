## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper tackles the challenge of sparse reward environments in reinforcement learning, particularly those requiring long horizons. The authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase. These skills are then reused as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards, significantly reducing sample complexity. The key contribution lies in using SNNs, combined with a mutual information (MI) based regularizer, to learn a diverse and interpretable set of skills without requiring domain-specific knowledge or hand-engineering.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on standard locomotion and maze navigation tasks.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**.  The paper presents a compelling approach to addressing sparse reward exploration in HRL. The combination of SNNs with the MI regularizer is a novel and well-motivated idea, and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in tasks with long horizons. The code release is a significant positive.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The problem of sparse reward exploration is well-established and critical for applying RL to real-world scenarios. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing approaches (hand-engineered hierarchies, intrinsic rewards lacking transferability). The proposed solution of learning a diverse skill set *before* task-specific training is a logical and promising approach.  The use of SNNs to enable multi-modal policies and the MI regularizer to encourage skill diversity are both novel ideas.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically sound.  The use of SNNs is well-explained, and the mathematical formulation of the MI regularizer is clear. The choice of TRPO for policy optimization is appropriate.  The bilinear integration method for combining latent variables and observations appears well-justified, and the ablation studies in the experiments demonstrate its importance.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing.  The comparisons with baseline methods (including existing intrinsic motivation approaches) show a clear advantage for the proposed framework.  The visitation plots and qualitative observations of learned skills demonstrate the diversity and interpretability of the SNN-learned policies. The results across multiple maze configurations and the Gather task provide evidence of generalizability.  The thorough experimentation, including the analysis with different robots, strengthens the claims.
* **Reproducibility:** The availability of code is a huge plus and significantly boosts confidence in the results.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify MI implementation details:** The explanation of how the MI is estimated (discretizing the CoM space) is a bit brief. Providing more details about the grid size and its impact on the results would be helpful.
* **Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the hyperparameters are listed in the appendix, a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters (e.g., the MI coefficient αH, the latent dimension K) would be valuable.
* **Limitations Section:** A dedicated limitations section would strengthen the paper. For instance, acknowledging the potential for instability with highly complex robots (as hinted at in the Ant experiments) and discussing potential future mitigation strategies would be beneficial.
* **More Detailed Analysis of Skill Transfer:** Quantifying the degree of skill transfer (e.g., by measuring the overlap in state visitation patterns between pre-training and downstream tasks) could provide further insights.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the MI regularization:** How was the value of the αH coefficient chosen? Was a hyperparameter search performed, and if so, what were the results?  Did different values of αH qualitatively change the learned skills (e.g., did higher values lead to more specialized skills)?
* **Regarding the switch time (T):**  The authors mention that the switch time doesn’t critically affect the performance. However, the experiments only varied T within a limited range. Could a significantly larger or smaller T have a noticeable impact, especially for more dynamic environments?
* **Regarding the SNN architecture:** Did you explore other types of SNN architectures beyond the bilinear integration method? If so, what were the results, and why did you choose the bilinear integration?
* **Regarding potential instability with more complex robots:** You mention issues with instability when applying the framework to the Ant robot.  What specific modifications could be made to the framework (e.g., adding a stabilization term to the reward function, modifying the skill switching mechanism) to address this issue?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of HRL. The authors have presented a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated approach.  The minor revisions suggested above should further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
