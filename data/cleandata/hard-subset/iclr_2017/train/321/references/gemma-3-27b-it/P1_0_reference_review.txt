## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenge of sparse reward reinforcement learning, particularly in tasks requiring long horizons. The authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase. These skills are then used as building blocks for learning high-level policies for downstream tasks, reducing sample complexity. The key contributions include: utilizing SNNs for multi-modal policy representation; employing a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage skill diversity during pre-training; and demonstrating the effectiveness of the framework in challenging robotic control tasks. The paper is well-written and addresses a relevant problem in RL.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The approach is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate a clear benefit of using SNNs and the proposed MI regularization for learning reusable skills in HRL. While there are areas for improvement (detailed below), the overall quality and potential impact of the work are strong.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation:** The problem of sparse rewards and long horizons is a critical bottleneck in RL. The proposed HRL approach directly addresses this by learning reusable skills, reducing the exploration burden in complex tasks.
* **Novelty:** The use of SNNs to represent a diverse skill set is a novel idea and provides a principled way to learn multiple behaviors within a single network. The MI regularizer is also a valuable addition, encouraging the SNN to explore a wider range of skills.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on locomotion + maze and food collection tasks demonstrate that the proposed framework outperforms existing methods, including intrinsic motivation baselines. The ablation studies presented in the appendices further strengthen these findings. The exploration visualizations (visitation plots) effectively illustrate the benefits of the SNN and MI regularization.
* **Technical Soundness:** The authors provide a clear and detailed description of the methodology, including the SNN architecture, MI regularization, and policy optimization procedure. The implementation details and hyperparameter settings are also clearly documented, increasing the reproducibility of the work.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify the Role of Bilinear Integration:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of bilinear integration in the SNN, a more intuitive explanation of *why* it performs better than simple concatenation would be beneficial.  What specific properties of bilinear integration enable it to learn more diverse skills?
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:**  The appendix briefly mentions failure modes with the Ant robot. Expanding this discussion, including potential reasons for the instability and possible future directions to address it (beyond those mentioned) would strengthen the paper.  Perhaps a more detailed analysis of the learned skills for Ant could reveal limitations.
* **Ablation Study on MI Coefficient:** A more systematic ablation study exploring the impact of the MI coefficient (αH) on performance across different tasks could provide further insights into the optimal balance between skill diversity and task-specific learning.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost associated with training SNNs compared to traditional policy networks would be useful, particularly as the SNN architecture appears more complex.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the bilinear integration:** Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* bilinear integration leads to improved skill diversity compared to simpler integration methods like concatenation?  Are there any theoretical insights into this difference?
* **Regarding the Ant robot:**  The appendix mentions instability issues. Were there any specific characteristics of the Ant robot's environment or action space that contributed to these difficulties? Did you explore alternative stabilization techniques, such as reward shaping or action clipping, beyond those mentioned?
* **Regarding scaling to more complex tasks:**  Do you anticipate any significant challenges in scaling this approach to even more complex tasks with higher-dimensional state and action spaces? If so, what modifications or extensions to the framework might be necessary?
* **Regarding reproducibility:** While the code is available, are there any specific considerations or dependencies that reviewers should be aware of to ensure successful reproduction of the results?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning. The use of SNNs and MI regularization provides a promising approach for learning reusable skills and tackling challenging sparse reward tasks. Addressing the feedback above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
