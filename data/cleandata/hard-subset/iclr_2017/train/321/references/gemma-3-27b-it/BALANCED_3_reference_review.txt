This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework utilizing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning, aiming to improve sample efficiency in tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to pre-train SNNs with a proxy reward, effectively learning a diverse set of skills, and then leveraging these skills in downstream tasks via a high-level policy. The authors address the challenge of skill diversity through a mutual information (MI) regularizer during pre-training.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a novel combination of SNNs and HRL with a reasonable motivation, the experimental results, despite claiming improvements, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods, and the explanations for the observed behaviors are often speculative. The reliance on ablation studies and comparisons to a somewhat weak baseline limits the strength of the conclusions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Justification:** The primary strength of the paper relies on demonstrating superior performance in sparse reward environments. However, the presented results (Fig. 6) show only marginal improvements over a baseline using a simple intrinsic reward, and the variance is considerable. The claim of outperforming VIME in the Gather task is mentioned but without direct comparative data within the paper itself, relying instead on reference to the original VIME paper.  Furthermore, the benefits of the MI regularizer appear inconsistent across tasks (Maze vs. Gather).  The improvement on the Gather task is only apparent at a larger horizon (5000 steps) which is not consistently shown throughout.  The performance on the more complex "Ant" robot suffers from instability issues that are not sufficiently addressed.

2. **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The comparison to related work is mostly descriptive rather than quantitative. While the authors acknowledge relevant papers, a more direct and thorough comparison with established HRL algorithms (e.g., option-critic, HiREPS, guided policy search) is needed to establish the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed framework.  The claim of avoiding "cherry-picking" pre-trained policies is appreciated, but the high variance suggests there's still considerable dependence on the specific pre-trained network.

3. **Insufficient Explanation of Mechanisms:** The paper describes the use of bilinear integration and the MI regularizer but lacks a deep dive into *why* these choices are effective. While the authors hypothesize about the roles of these components, more analysis or ablation studies are needed to substantiate these claims. The impact of the switch time *T* is acknowledged, but the provided discussion seems incomplete. Why does *T* have relatively little impact on the performance?



**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Compare against more competitive HRL algorithms. Consider implementing and comparing to option-critic or HiREPS.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more systematic ablation studies. Specifically, quantify the contribution of the bilinear integration *vs.* concatenation and thoroughly analyze the sensitivity of performance to different MI regularization coefficients.
*   **Detailed Analysis of SNN Learned Skills:** Beyond visualization (visitation plots), provide a quantitative analysis of the learned skills. Can you measure the diversity of the learned behaviors? Perhaps using metrics like the Frechet Inception Distance between trajectories generated by different latent codes?
*   **Ant Robot Stability:** Address the instability issues with the Ant robot more thoroughly.  Perhaps consider techniques to improve policy robustness or modify the reward function to encourage more stable behavior.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide error bars or statistical significance tests for all presented results to demonstrate the reliability of the findings.
* **Clarify MI calculation**: The description of MI estimation is somewhat vague. Provide more details about how the state space is discretized and how the posterior probabilities are estimated. Is there a potential sensitivity to the discretization granularity?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The MI regularizer appears to have varying effects across different tasks. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* it helps in some cases but not others?
2.  What is the computational overhead associated with using SNNs compared to standard deterministic policies?  How does this scale with the number of latent variables (skills)?
3.  Why were specific network architectures (2 layers of 32 hidden units) chosen? Was a hyperparameter search conducted, and if so, what were the results?
4.  You mention that the high variance in Maze performance is due to the dependence on the pre-trained SNN. Could you quantify this dependence with a correlation between pre-training performance and downstream task performance?
5.  The gathered results show a clear increase in performance only when the horizon is set to 5000. Is this due to a change in the difficulty of the task, or a hyperparameter that needs to be tuned with the horizon?
