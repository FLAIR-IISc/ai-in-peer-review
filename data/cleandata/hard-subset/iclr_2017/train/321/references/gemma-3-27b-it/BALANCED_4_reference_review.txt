This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, which are then utilized for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to use the stochasticity of SNNs, coupled with a mutual information (MI) regularizer, to encourage the learning of a rich and interpretable skill space. This skill space then forms the basis for high-level policies that can solve challenging tasks more efficiently.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting approach to HRL and explores the potential of SNNs in this context, the experimental validation isn't sufficiently convincing to warrant acceptance. The performance gains over strong baselines (like intrinsic reward baselines) are not consistently demonstrated across all tasks, and the improvements when observed, are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity of the proposed method. Furthermore, the significance of the MI regularizer is not unequivocally established – its removal doesn’t consistently lead to drastic performance drops.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Performance Gains:** The results presented in Figure 6 show only marginal improvements over the baseline, especially in the maze tasks. While the Gather task does show a more noticeable advantage, it is not clear if this is consistent, given the variations observed across different seeds (discussed in section C.4). The claim of outperforming VIME, a strong intrinsic motivation method, is made without a direct comparison in the same experimental setting as reported in the VIME paper, which weakens the claim.

2. **MI Regularizer Impact is Unclear:** The ablation study of the MI regularizer in Section 7.1 is not entirely conclusive. While the MI bonus *can* improve skill diversity (as shown in the visitation plots), the impact on downstream task performance is less pronounced. The authors acknowledge that the MI bonus doesn’t always lead to a performance boost, which raises questions about its necessity.  The benefit doesn't seem consistently tied to the learned diversity.

3. **Complexity vs. Benefit:** The proposed framework introduces significant complexity: SNNs with bilinear integration, MI regularization, and a hierarchical policy structure. This complexity isn't fully justified by the observed performance gains.  Simpler methods, like well-tuned intrinsic reward schemes, appear to achieve comparable results.

4. **Lack of Robustness & Generalization:** The experiments primarily focus on a limited set of environments and robots (Swimmer and Snake). The instability issues with the Ant robot (Section D) raise concerns about the robustness of the approach to more complex systems. The significant variability in performance across different pre-trained SNNs (Section C.4) further suggests limited generalization capability.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **More Rigorous Baselines:** Conduct a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods (VIME, RND) and HRL algorithms, ensuring a fair and direct comparison in the same experimental setup.
* **Ablation Studies:** Perform a more systematic ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component (SNNs, bilinear integration, MI regularizer) to the overall performance.  Quantify the impact of each component (e.g., by reporting statistical significance).
* **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the benefits of using SNNs for skill learning and the role of the MI regularizer.
* **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the robustness of the framework to different environments, robot morphologies, and hyperparameter settings. Address the instability issues observed with the Ant robot.
* **Computational Cost:** Analyze and report the computational cost of training SNNs compared to standard neural networks.
* **Visualize Latent Space:** Offer a more intuitive visualization of the latent space learned by the SNN. For instance, show how varying the latent code leads to continuous changes in the robot's behavior.
* **Switching Cost:** Analyze the effect of the switching cost on performance. Is there a benefit to having a longer or shorter time horizon for each skill?

**Questions for the Authors:**

* The paper mentions that “training each policy from scratch is not sample efficient.” Could you quantify this inefficiency with a numerical comparison between training K separate policies versus training a single SNN?
* The MI bonus relies on discretizing the state space. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of discretization granularity?
* The ablation studies primarily focus on the MI regularizer.  Have you explored the impact of using different types of stochasticity in the SNN (e.g., different distributions for the latent variables)?
* In the Gather task, you mention that the SNN with MI bonus exhibits lower variance.  Could you provide statistical evidence to support this claim?
* Why is the performance improvement for the Maze tasks so limited? Are there specific challenges inherent in these environments that hinder the effectiveness of the proposed approach?
