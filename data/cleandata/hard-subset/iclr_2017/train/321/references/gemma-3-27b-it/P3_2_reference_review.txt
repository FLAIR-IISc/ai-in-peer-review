## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of sparse reward environments in reinforcement learning by proposing a hierarchical learning framework. The core idea is to first pre-train a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to learn a diverse set of skills using a proxy reward, and then leverage these skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks. The authors argue that SNNs are well-suited for this purpose due to their ability to represent multi-modal policies and share weights. To further encourage skill diversity, they introduce an information-theoretic regularizer based on mutual information. The experimental evaluation on locomotion and maze tasks demonstrates that their approach can outperform existing methods, especially in sparse reward settings.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** The paper presents a compelling approach to hierarchical RL, addressing a significant challenge in the field. The combination of SNNs, the MI regularizer, and the overall framework is well-motivated and shows promising results. The experimental validation is relatively thorough and the authors provide code for reproducibility.  However, some areas require further clarification and more rigorous analysis.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation:** The problem of sparse rewards is a well-recognized bottleneck in RL, and the proposed hierarchical approach is a logical way to address it. The emphasis on learning a general skill set *before* tackling specific tasks is a valuable contribution.
* **Novelty:** The application of SNNs within a hierarchical RL framework, combined with the MI regularization, is a novel and interesting approach. While SNNs have been used previously, their application to skill learning and exploration in RL is relatively unexplored. 
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, particularly in the more challenging sparse reward environments.  The visualization of learned skills (visitation plots) provides a good qualitative understanding of the SNN’s capabilities.
* **Reproducibility:** Providing code is a significant strength, fostering transparency and allowing the community to build upon this work.

However, several points need strengthening:

* **MI Regularization Justification:** While the MI regularization demonstrably improves skill diversity, the authors could benefit from including a more in-depth discussion on *why* this specific regularizer is effective.  Is it simply encouraging exploration, or is there a more nuanced relationship between MI and skill quality?
* **Ablation Studies:** The paper could benefit from more thorough ablation studies.  For example, how much does each component (SNN, MI regularization, hierarchical structure) contribute to the overall performance?  What happens if the SNN is replaced with a standard neural network?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of hyperparameters, particularly the MI weight (alpha_H), is not fully explored.  A sensitivity analysis would help to understand the robustness of the approach.
* **Generalization to More Complex Tasks:** While the provided results are promising, the tasks are relatively simple.  It would be valuable to evaluate the framework on more complex, high-dimensional environments to assess its scalability and generalization capabilities.  The mention of results on the Ant robot is encouraging, but more details are needed.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarity of the Bilinear Integration:** The explanation of the bilinear integration could be more accessible. Perhaps a visual representation of the computation would be helpful.
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:** The authors briefly mention instability issues with the Ant robot. A more detailed discussion of failure modes and potential solutions would strengthen the paper.
* **Comparison with Related Work:** While the related work section is good, it could be expanded to include more recent advances in skill discovery and hierarchical RL. Specifically, mentioning work on option discovery with transformers or other advanced architectures could be beneficial.
* **State Space Factorization:** The assumption of a factorable state space (Sagent and SMrest) is crucial.  The authors should discuss the limitations of this assumption and how the framework might be adapted to non-factorable environments.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the MI regularization:**  Can you provide more intuition on *why* maximizing mutual information between the latent variable and the robot's CoM leads to the discovery of diverse and useful skills?  Are there any theoretical guarantees or connections to information-theoretic principles?
* **Regarding the ablation studies:**  Could you provide results comparing the performance of the framework with and without the SNN (i.e., using a standard neural network)?  This would help to quantify the benefit of using SNNs.
* **Regarding the Ant robot:**  Can you provide more details on the instability issues encountered with the Ant robot and any potential solutions you are considering?  (The link to the technical report is helpful, but a summary within the paper would be beneficial.)
* **Regarding scalability:** How does the computational cost of training the SNN scale with the dimensionality of the state and action spaces? Are there any strategies for mitigating this cost?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to hierarchical RL. Addressing the minor revisions and clarifying the points raised above will further strengthen the contribution and make it an even more valuable addition to the field.
