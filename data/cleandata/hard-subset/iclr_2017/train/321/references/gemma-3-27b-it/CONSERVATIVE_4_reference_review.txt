This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning and subsequent transfer to downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to pre-train an SNN with a proxy reward to learn a diverse set of skills, and then utilize these skills within a hierarchical policy for solving specific tasks. The authors address the challenge of exploration in sparse reward environments by learning reusable skills, and introduce a mutual information regularizer to encourage diversity in the learned skills.

**Reject.** While the idea of utilizing SNNs for skill discovery and hierarchical RL is interesting, the paper suffers from insufficient validation and a lack of convincing evidence to support its claims. The experiments, while extensive, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing approaches, and the reliance on ablation studies within a relatively narrow experimental setup feels incomplete. The methodology also feels unnecessarily complex, introducing several components (bilinear integration, MI regularizer) without fully justifying their necessity.

The primary weakness lies in the comparison to baselines. The authors compare against a baseline using the same proxy reward during task training, which feels weak. More compelling comparisons to established hierarchical RL methods (e.g., options frameworks, hierarchical actor-critic approaches) and state-of-the-art exploration techniques (e.g., intrinsically motivated exploration methods) are crucial. The claim of outperforming “state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation results” (specifically VIME) in the Gather task is mentioned briefly but lacks a clear quantitative presentation of these results. The extensive ablation studies, while detailed, largely focus on components *within* the proposed framework, rather than against alternative approaches. 

Furthermore, the impact of the pre-trained skills isn’t consistently demonstrated. The results on the Maze tasks show only marginal improvements, and the performance is heavily dependent on the specific pre-trained SNN used, raising concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the approach. The claim that the MI bonus consistently improves skill diversity needs stronger justification; the visitation plots, while illustrative, are somewhat subjective. The detailed discussion of the Ant robot experiments highlighting failure modes further underscores the lack of robustness. While acknowledging these limitations is commendable, it doesn't mitigate the core issue of insufficient evidence supporting the method’s overall effectiveness. The extensive appendixes suggest a lot of experimentation, but the main paper doesn’t effectively synthesize these results to present a strong, compelling argument.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include comparisons against well-established HRL and exploration methods. This is critical for establishing the value of the proposed approach.
*   **Quantitative Results:** Provide clear and quantitative results for all comparisons, including error bars and statistical significance tests.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the approach to different hyperparameters and random seeds. The dependency on specific pre-trained SNNs is a significant concern.
*   **Ablation Study Focus:** Shift the focus of the ablation studies to compare against alternative components, not just variations within the proposed framework. For instance, compare with a standard hierarchical RL agent *without* SNNs.
*   **Justification for Complexity:** Provide a more compelling justification for the architectural choices (bilinear integration, MI regularizer). Explain *why* these choices are necessary and how they contribute to the overall performance.
*   **Clarify MI Implementation:** Detail the practical implementation of the MI regularizer, including the discretization scheme and its impact on performance.
*   **Address Failure Modes:** While acknowledging the failure modes with the Ant robot is good, propose potential solutions and discuss their implications.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why were specific baselines (e.g., options frameworks, hierarchical actor-critic) not included in the experimental evaluation?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed quantitative comparison of your method against VIME on the Gather task, including the specific hyperparameters used for both methods?
3.  What is the computational overhead associated with using SNNs compared to standard neural networks in both the pre-training and task learning phases?
4.  The paper mentions the dependency on the specific pre-trained SNN for maze tasks. What strategies could be employed to mitigate this dependency and ensure more consistent performance?
5.  What is the sensitivity of the MI bonus coefficient (alpha_H) and how does it affect the quality of the learned skills? Provide a more systematic analysis of this parameter.
