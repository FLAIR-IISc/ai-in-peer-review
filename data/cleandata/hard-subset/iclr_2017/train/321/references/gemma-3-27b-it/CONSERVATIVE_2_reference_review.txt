This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning and transfer. The core idea is to pre-train an SNN with a proxy reward to learn a diverse set of skills, and then utilize these skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards. An information-theoretic regularizer based on Mutual Information is introduced to encourage skill diversity during pre-training. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on locomotion and maze tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of utilizing SNNs for hierarchical RL is intriguing, the paper falls short in providing sufficient evidence and rigorous validation to support its claims. The reliance on a relatively simple pre-training setup and the lack of comprehensive comparisons against well-established baselines raise serious concerns about the true benefit of the proposed approach.

The primary weakness lies in the limited and often unclear empirical validation. The experiments, while conducted on standard benchmarks, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods, particularly given the increased complexity introduced by the SNNs and the MI regularizer. The comparison to a baseline with a simple CoM reward highlights a potential weakness in the task setup more than the method's strength. The authors acknowledge the sensitivity to pre-trained SNN selection, indicating instability and a lack of robustness. Furthermore, the observed performance gains are not consistently reported across all tasks. The discussion of the Ant robot reveals significant challenges with the approach’s stability, hinting at underlying issues not adequately addressed in the paper. The claim of superior performance needs stronger, more conclusive evidence.

Several aspects require further clarification and stronger justification. The choice of bilinear integration for the SNN is presented as crucial, but the rationale for this choice beyond references to prior work is limited. A more thorough ablation study investigating different integration methods would be valuable. The impact of the MI bonus is also not fully clear, and the optimal tuning of the αH parameter seems sensitive.  The authors mention the possibility of end-to-end training, but dismiss it without sufficient exploration. The benefits of freezing the low-level policies are not convincingly demonstrated; perhaps end-to-end training could yield even better results. The impact of the switch time, *T*, is analyzed, but the findings are not particularly insightful, and the reasoning for selecting specific values of *T* remains unclear.

Here are some additional suggestions for improvement:

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Compare against more recent and competitive hierarchical RL algorithms, including those utilizing intrinsic motivation or option discovery.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (SNN, MI regularizer, bilinear integration).
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the robustness of the approach to different random seeds and pre-training conditions. Provide a more detailed analysis of the variation in performance across different pre-trained SNNs.
*   **End-to-End Training:** Explore the potential benefits of end-to-end training with straight-through estimators.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost associated with training and deploying SNNs compared to traditional neural networks.

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  Why was the bilinear integration chosen over other methods for integrating the latent variable? What specific advantages did it offer in your experiments?
2.  Given the instability observed with the Ant robot, what specific modifications would be required to make the approach more robust for more challenging environments?
3.  Could you elaborate on the reasons for dismissing end-to-end training without a more thorough investigation?
4.  The results show considerable variation depending on the initial SNN. How can this be mitigated to ensure more consistent performance?
5.  What is the computational overhead of using SNNs compared to deterministic networks, and how does this scale with the number of latent variables?
