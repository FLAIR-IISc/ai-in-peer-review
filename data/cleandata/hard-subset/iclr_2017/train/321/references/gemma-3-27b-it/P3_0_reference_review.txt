## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper addresses the challenge of sparse reward reinforcement learning, particularly in tasks with long horizons. The authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of low-level skills in a pre-training environment with a proxy reward. These skills are then used as building blocks for high-level policies trained to solve downstream tasks.  A key contribution is the introduction of a mutual information (MI) regularizer during pre-training to encourage the SNN to learn a richer and more diverse set of skills. The authors demonstrate that their approach improves sample efficiency and performance on challenging locomotion and manipulation tasks.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting idea and shows promising preliminary results, I find the experimental validation insufficient to support the strong claims made. Specifically, the comparisons to baselines are not comprehensive enough, and the variance in performance across different SNN initializations raises concerns about the robustness of the approach. The paper feels exploratory and lacks the rigor needed for ICLR acceptance.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Baseline Comparisons:** The paper primarily compares against a simple intrinsic reward baseline. While this demonstrates improvement, it doesn’t demonstrate superiority over more sophisticated HRL or exploration techniques.  There’s no comparison to methods like Option-Critic, HIREPS, or other contemporary skill discovery approaches. The claim of “strong performance” needs to be substantiated by benchmarks against established methods.
* **High Variance & Sensitivity to Initialization:** The results show significant variance in performance across different SNN initializations (demonstrated in section C.4). This is particularly concerning as the authors acknowledge the lack of clear explanation for this variability.  A robust method should ideally be less sensitive to the random seed. The authors do not offer sufficient insight into why certain SNNs perform better than others, hindering reproducibility and understanding.
* **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** While the authors demonstrate the impact of the MI regularizer, further ablation studies are needed. For instance, it would be valuable to analyze the effect of different bilinear integration methods or explore alternative regularizers. A more granular analysis of the contributions of each component would strengthen the argument.
* **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper primarily relies on empirical validation.  A more formal analysis of why SNNs are well-suited for skill discovery, or why the MI regularizer is effective, would be beneficial. While not strictly required, theoretical insights can strengthen the claims.
* **Overstated Claims:** Claims like "Our hierarchical policy-learning framework can learn a wide range of skills, which are clearly interpretable..." feel somewhat exaggerated. While visualization of the learned skills is provided, “clearly interpretable” is subjective and relies heavily on the chosen visualization.  Furthermore, the reliance on the proxy reward for pre-training raises questions about whether the learned skills are truly transferable or simply optimized for that specific reward structure.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Expand Baselines:** Include comparisons to more recent and competitive HRL and exploration methods.
* **Reduce Variance:** Investigate the sources of variance across different SNN initializations.  Perhaps averaging the performance of multiple SNNs could provide a more stable estimate.  Consider techniques to improve the stability of the pre-training process.
* **More In-Depth Ablation:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component (SNN architecture, MI regularizer, bilinear integration).
* **Theoretical Analysis:** Provide a theoretical justification for the proposed method, even if it’s preliminary.
* **Clarify Interpretability:**  Provide a more rigorous assessment of the interpretability of the learned skills. Are the skills truly distinct and useful, or are they subtly different variations of the same behavior?
* **Robustness Analysis:** Analyze the performance of the method under different noise levels or perturbations in the environment.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* What are the key characteristics of the SNNs that lead to the observed performance gains compared to deterministic networks?
* Why does the performance vary so significantly across different SNN initializations? What steps can be taken to mitigate this variability?
* What is the computational cost associated with training the SNNs compared to training separate policies?
* How sensitive is the MI hyperparameter (αH) to the specific task or environment?
* Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the skills learned by the SNNs in terms of their corresponding actions and their relevance to the downstream tasks?
* Are there any potential downsides to using a proxy reward for pre-training, and how might these be addressed?



In conclusion, while the idea presented in this paper is promising, the current level of experimental validation and analysis is insufficient for acceptance at a competitive conference like ICLR. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
