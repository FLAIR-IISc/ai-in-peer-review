This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase, which are then utilized by a high-level policy for downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to use the inherent multi-modality of SNNs, guided by a mutual information (MI) regularizer, to discover a broad range of useful skills without requiring task-specific knowledge or hand-engineering. The learned skills are then frozen and used as building blocks for solving complex tasks.

**Reject.** While the proposed approach addresses a relevant problem in reinforcement learning – sample efficiency in sparse reward environments – the paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of improvement over existing methods. The novelty of combining SNNs with an MI regularizer for skill learning is interesting, but the presented experimental results are not convincing enough, particularly given the extensive body of prior work in HRL and exploration strategies. The performance gains demonstrated are often marginal or within the range of variance observed across different initializations, and the comparisons to strong baselines are not consistently favorable.

The primary concern lies with the methodological rigor and the strength of the empirical validation. The ablation studies, while present, don't fully disentangle the contributions of each component (SNNs, MI regularization, bilinear integration). The authors claim benefits from the bilinear integration and MI bonus, but the improvements are not always clear and seem sensitive to the task and random seed. The experiments primarily focus on locomotion tasks, and it’s unclear whether the approach generalizes well to other domains.  Furthermore, the analysis of the learned skills, while providing qualitative insights, lacks quantitative metrics to assess their diversity and usefulness objectively. Relying heavily on visitation plots as the primary form of analysis is insufficient.

The paper also suffers from a lack of thorough comparison to established HRL methods. While the authors mention some related work, the comparisons are often limited to simpler intrinsic motivation techniques rather than state-of-the-art hierarchical methods that might already address the problem of sparse rewards effectively.  The claim that the proposed framework requires "minimal domain knowledge" is questionable, as designing the proxy reward still necessitates some understanding of potentially useful behaviors for the downstream tasks. The justification for choosing specific hyperparameters, such as the MI coefficient, is also lacking.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include comparisons to more competitive HRL algorithms (e.g., Option-Critic, Hierarchical Actor-Critic) and state-of-the-art exploration methods (e.g., prioritized experience replay with intrinsic rewards).
*   **Quantitative Skill Evaluation:** Develop metrics to quantify the diversity and usefulness of the learned skills beyond visual inspection of visitation plots. Consider measures of skill coverage, transferability, or contribution to solving downstream tasks.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of each component (SNNs, MI regularization, bilinear integration) on performance. Systematically vary hyperparameters and analyze the results.
*   **Generalization to Other Domains:** Evaluate the framework on a wider range of tasks and environments to assess its generalizability beyond locomotion problems.
*   **Hyperparameter Justification:** Provide a rationale for the selection of hyperparameters and conduct sensitivity analyses to demonstrate robustness.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Provide some theoretical justification for the choice of SNNs and the MI regularizer. Is there a connection to information-theoretic principles that explains why this approach should be effective?
*   **Code Quality:** While code is provided, ensure it is well-documented and easily reproducible.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the proxy reward in the pre-training phase? Can you provide a systematic analysis of different proxy rewards and their impact on the learned skills and downstream task performance?
2.  The MI bonus seems crucial for learning diverse skills. Can you explain why a simple L2 regularization on the latent variable distribution isn’t sufficient to achieve the same result? What specific properties of the MI bonus contribute to its effectiveness?
3.  You mention the instability issues with the Ant robot. Have you explored techniques to mitigate this instability, such as using robust control strategies or curriculum learning?
4.  Could you clarify how the learning curves are averaged across different random seeds? How many independent runs were performed for each experiment, and what is the standard deviation of the results?
5.  Why was TRPO chosen as the policy optimization algorithm? Were other algorithms considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
