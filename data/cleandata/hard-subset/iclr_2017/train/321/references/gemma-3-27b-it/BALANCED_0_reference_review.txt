This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework utilizing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for learning a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, which are then leveraged for faster learning in downstream sparse reward tasks. The core idea of employing SNNs to represent multi-modal policies and using a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage skill diversity is compelling, and the experimental results demonstrate promising performance on challenging locomotion and manipulation tasks.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting approach and solid experimental results, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and rigorous justification for several key design choices, hindering a confident acceptance. Specifically, the benefit of the SNN architecture *over* other methods for representing multi-modal policies isn't thoroughly demonstrated, and the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters (like the MI weighting factor) is not adequately discussed. Furthermore, the advantages of the bilinear integration compared to simpler alternatives aren't convincingly established.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Insufficient Justification for SNNs:** The paper argues that SNNs are well-suited for representing multi-modal policies. However, it lacks a comparative analysis with simpler methods, such as mixture density networks (MDNs), to demonstrate the clear advantages of SNNs in this context. While the experiments *show* good results with SNNs, they don't prove that SNNs are *necessary* or superior for achieving those results. A clear ablation study comparing SNNs to MDNs or other multi-modal policy representations is crucial.

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Lack of Robustness Analysis:** The performance of the proposed framework appears sensitive to the MI weighting factor (αH). While the authors demonstrate its impact on skill diversity, they don't adequately discuss the process of tuning this hyperparameter or its robustness across different tasks and robot morphologies. The observed performance variations among different pre-trained SNNs in the Ant experiments (Appendix D) highlight a potential lack of robustness that needs to be addressed. How consistently does this method perform with different random seeds and environments?

3. **Limited Analysis of Bilinear Integration:** The paper claims that bilinear integration is superior for skill learning, but the justification is weak. While the results show good performance with bilinear integration, there's no comparative study against simpler integration methods (e.g., concatenation) that would clearly establish its benefits. A detailed ablation study, demonstrating the impact of different integration methods on skill diversity and downstream task performance, is required.

4. **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper suffers from some lack of clarity in certain sections. For instance, the explanation of how the mutual information bonus is calculated (Eq. 1 & 2) is somewhat convoluted and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.  The connection between the discrete state representation used for MI calculation and its impact on the effectiveness of the regularizer is not fully explored.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of each component: the SNN architecture, the MI regularizer, the bilinear integration, and the pre-training phase.
* **Comparative Analysis:** Compare the performance of SNNs with MDNs or other suitable multi-modal policy representations.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning & Robustness:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.  Analyze the robustness of the framework across different random seeds and environments.
* **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more theoretical justification for the choice of SNNs and the MI regularizer.  Why are these particularly well-suited for hierarchical reinforcement learning?
* **Visualize Skills:** Provide a more comprehensive visualization of the learned skills, beyond just the visitation plots. For instance, show example trajectories for each latent code and discuss the corresponding behaviors.
* **Clarify MI Calculation:**  Rewrite the explanation of the mutual information calculation for improved clarity.
* **Discuss Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed framework, particularly in unstable environments like the Ant robot, would be beneficial.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific advantages do SNNs offer over other methods for representing multi-modal policies (e.g., MDNs) in the context of hierarchical reinforcement learning? Can you provide a more direct comparison, perhaps in an ablation study?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the MI weighting factor (αH)? What strategies were used to tune this hyperparameter, and how robust are the results to different values of αH?
3.  Why was bilinear integration chosen over simpler integration methods (e.g., concatenation)? Can you provide empirical evidence to support this choice?
4.  In the Ant experiments (Appendix D), why is there such significant performance variation among different pre-trained SNNs? What factors contribute to this variability?
5.  The paper mentions end-to-end training using straight-through estimators as future work. What are the anticipated benefits and challenges of this approach in the context of the proposed framework?
6.  Can you explain the potential trade-offs between the switch time (T) and the performance of the hierarchical policy? Is there a systematic way to determine an optimal value for T based on the characteristics of the environment?
