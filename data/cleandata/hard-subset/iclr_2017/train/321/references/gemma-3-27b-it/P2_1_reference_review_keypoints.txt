- Problem & approach: Use SNNs with an MI regularizer to learn diverse, pre-trained skills for hierarchical RL to tackle sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks.  
- Decision: Accept (with minor revisions).  
- Novelty: SNNs for multi-modal policies plus MI regularization to produce transferable, diverse skills is a novel and well-motivated idea.  
- Technical soundness: Mathematical formulation, use of TRPO, and bilinear latent-observation integration are well-justified; ablations support design choices.  
- Empirical validation: Strong improvements over baselines on locomotion and maze tasks; visitation plots and qualitative skill behaviors demonstrate diversity and interpretability.  
- Generalizability: Results across multiple maze configurations, Gather task, and different robots support broader applicability.  
- Reproducibility: Code release is a significant positive for reproducibility.  
- Clarify MI details: Need more information on how MI is estimated (CoM space discretization), grid size, and its impact on results.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Request discussion of sensitivity to key hyperparameters (αH MI coefficient, latent dimension K) and their effects.  
- Limitations section: Recommend an explicit limitations section, noting potential instability on complex robots (Ant) and possible mitigation strategies.  
- Skill transfer quantification: Suggest measuring overlap in state visitation between pretraining and downstream tasks to quantify transfer.  
- Question on αH choice: How was αH selected; was a hyperparameter search performed and how did different values affect learned skills?  
- Question on switch time T: Does a much larger or smaller T materially affect performance, especially in dynamic environments?  
- Question on SNN architectures: Were architectures other than bilinear integration explored, and why was bilinear chosen?  
- Question on Ant instability fixes: What specific modifications (e.g., stabilization terms, skill-switching changes) could address instability with complex robots?
