## Review of "STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework to address the challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is to pre-train a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to learn a diverse set of skills using a simple proxy reward. These skills are then leveraged as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards by training high-level policies on top of them. The authors introduce an information-theoretic regularizer (Mutual Information) to encourage skill diversity during the pre-training phase and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several challenging locomotion and manipulation tasks. The presented results show significant performance improvements compared to baseline methods, particularly in sparse reward environments.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem in RL, presents a novel and well-motivated approach, and provides compelling experimental evidence supporting its claims. The combination of SNNs, information-theoretic regularization, and hierarchical learning is a significant contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper tackles the well-known challenges of exploration in sparse reward RL, a critical bottleneck for real-world applications. The use of SNNs for skill learning is a novel approach that allows for multi-modal policies and weight sharing, potentially leading to more efficient exploration. The motivation for using a proxy reward and learning skills in a pre-training environment is well-articulated and addresses the limitations of existing HRL methods that rely on hand-engineered hierarchies or task-specific knowledge.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed methodology appears sound and logically consistent. The choice of using Mutual Information as a regularizer is justified, and the implementation details regarding the bilinear integration and policy optimization (TRPO) are clearly explained.  The theoretical motivation behind the MI regularization makes intuitive sense – encouraging distinctiveness in the learned skills.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach across several challenging tasks. The comparison with existing methods, including intrinsic motivation baselines, highlights the advantages of the proposed framework. The ablation studies (demonstrated with different architectural choices and hyperparameters) effectively illustrate the importance of each component of the approach. The fact that the method can learn skills transferable to multiple maze variations strengthens the claims of generalizability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The figures and tables are informative and help to understand the experimental results.  The provided code and videos further enhance the reproducibility and accessibility of the work.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper acknowledges some limitations in the discussion, a more thorough discussion of potential drawbacks would be valuable. For example, the computational cost of training SNNs compared to standard neural networks, the sensitivity of the MI regularization to hyperparameter tuning, and the challenges of applying the framework to very high-dimensional state spaces.
* **Further Analysis of Skill Diversity:** While the visitation plots provide a qualitative assessment of skill diversity, a quantitative measure of diversity (e.g., using feature statistics or trajectory similarity metrics) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Ablation Study on MI Coefficient:** A more detailed ablation study varying the coefficient 'alpha' for the MI bonus would be helpful to understand its impact on performance and skill diversity.
* **Scalability to more complex robots:** The results with the Ant robot show instability, and a more detailed discussion of how to mitigate this issue would be beneficial.
* **Runtime/Computational cost:** Briefly discuss the runtime and computational resources required to pre-train and fine-tune the framework.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the choice of bilinear integration:** Why was bilinear integration chosen over other possible forms of integration (e.g., concatenation)? Were other integration methods explored, and if so, what were the results?
* **Regarding the MI regularization:** Is there a principled way to choose the value of the MI coefficient (alpha) or is it largely determined through trial and error?
* **Regarding the Ant robot:** What specific modifications to the framework (e.g., different network architectures, reward shaping, or exploration strategies) would be necessary to stabilize training with more complex robots like the Ant?
* **Regarding the switch time T:** While mentioned briefly, a more comprehensive exploration of the influence of T on downstream task performance would be valuable. Does the optimal T vary significantly across different tasks?
* **Regarding computational cost:**  What is the approximate training time for the pre-training phase and the fine-tuning phase for the presented tasks?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning.  Addressing the suggestions above will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
