This paper tackles the challenging problem of sparse reward reinforcement learning by proposing a hierarchical framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery. The core idea is to pre-train an SNN with a simple proxy reward to learn a diverse set of skills, and then utilize these skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The authors motivate this approach by arguing that naive exploration strategies fail in sparse reward scenarios and that hand-engineering hierarchies is impractical. They introduce an information-theoretic regularizer to encourage diversity in the learned skills.

**Reject.** While the approach is intriguing and represents a bold attempt to address a significant challenge in RL, the paper feels somewhat preliminary and lacks sufficient rigor to warrant acceptance. The novelty lies in combining SNNs with hierarchical RL and MI regularization, but the experimental validation, while extensive, doesn't convincingly demonstrate a clear and substantial advantage over existing methods, and the results are highly sensitive to hyperparameters and random seeds.

The main arguments supporting this decision are as follows:

1. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The paper mostly compares against a baseline using the same proxy reward in the downstream task, which isn’t a strong comparison. The comparison to VIME is briefly mentioned, but lacks detail. A more thorough comparison against established hierarchical RL algorithms (e.g., options frameworks, HiREPS) and intrinsic motivation methods would be necessary to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach. The claim of outperforming VIME requires a direct, detailed comparison on the same settings.
2. **Sensitivity and Variance:** The results show significant variance across different random seeds, particularly for the Ant robot and Maze tasks. The success seems heavily reliant on the specific SNN learned during pre-training. The authors acknowledge this, but don’t provide a robust explanation or mitigation strategy. High variance undermines confidence in the effectiveness of the approach. The performance dependence on the initial SNN is a critical weakness.
3. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The impact of the different components – SNNs, bilinear integration, MI regularization – isn’t fully disentangled. While ablation studies are mentioned in the text, they aren't presented clearly, and it’s difficult to assess the individual contribution of each component. For example, the performance gains attributed to the MI bonus aren't consistently observed across all tasks. 

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

* **Clarify the Role of the Proxy Reward:** The choice of proxy reward (speed for locomotion) seems somewhat arbitrary. A discussion of how the choice of proxy reward impacts the learned skills and the downstream performance would be valuable.
* **Investigate Alternative Regularization Techniques:** While the MI regularization is a good starting point, exploring other regularization techniques to promote skill diversity could be beneficial.
* **Address the Instability Issue:** The problems encountered with the Ant robot highlight the instability of the approach. Explore methods to address this, such as incorporating robustness into the pre-training process or using more stable policy optimization algorithms.
* **Provide More Detailed Hyperparameter Tuning Information:** The hyperparameters are listed in the appendix, but a more detailed discussion of the tuning process and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings would be helpful.
* **Clarify the Connection to Existing Hierarchical RL Frameworks:** Position the work more explicitly within the broader context of hierarchical RL. Explain how it differs from and builds upon existing approaches.
* **Address scalability:** The approach relies on pre-training a single SNN. What happens when the number of tasks grows very large, requiring a vast repertoire of skills? Does the pre-training become prohibitively expensive?

Questions for the authors:

* Could you provide a more detailed comparison with other hierarchical RL algorithms (e.g., options framework) on the same tasks and with the same evaluation metrics?
* What strategies could be employed to reduce the variance in performance observed across different random seeds?
* What is the computational cost of pre-training the SNN, and how does it scale with the complexity of the environment and the number of desired skills?
* How sensitive is the performance to the choice of proxy reward during pre-training? Could you provide examples of different proxy rewards and their impact on the learned skills and downstream performance?
*  Given the instability observed with the Ant robot, what modifications to the pre-training process or the policy optimization algorithm would you recommend to improve robustness?
