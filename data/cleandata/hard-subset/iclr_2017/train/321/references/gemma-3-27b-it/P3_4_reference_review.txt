## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of exploration in sparse-reward, long-horizon reinforcement learning tasks. The authors propose a hierarchical RL framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase, followed by training high-level policies on top of these learned skills for downstream tasks. The core idea is to utilize the multi-modal nature of SNNs, guided by a mutual information (MI) regularizer, to discover a broad range of behaviors with minimal domain knowledge. The empirical results demonstrate that this approach outperforms existing methods in several benchmark tasks, highlighting the benefits of pre-training with SNNs and the resulting improved sample efficiency.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. 

The paper addresses a critical problem in RL, proposes a novel and well-motivated approach, and provides compelling empirical evidence to support its claims. The use of SNNs with an MI regularizer for skill discovery is a particularly strong contribution. While there’s a lot packed into the paper, the authors demonstrate a good understanding of related work and provide clear explanations of their methods.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation:** The challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons are clearly articulated, and the paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature on hierarchical RL and intrinsic motivation. The framework offers a compelling alternative to both hand-engineered hierarchies and purely intrinsic reward-based methods.
* **Novelty:** Combining SNNs with an MI regularizer for pre-training skill sets is a novel approach. The authors intelligently leverage the SNNs’ capacity to represent multiple policies within a single network and address the potential for mode collapse with the MI objective. 
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the locomotion + maze and gathering tasks provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.  The comparisons to previous work (including intrinsically motivated baselines) and the ablation studies (especially regarding the MI bonus) strengthen the validity of the results. The included visualizations (visitation plots) are extremely helpful in understanding the learned skills. The addition of results with the "Snake" robot shows generalization capability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow for reproduction. The code release is a significant positive.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors mention sample efficiency gains, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with training SNNs compared to standard policies would be valuable.  How does the increased complexity of the network impact training time?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameters in the appendix but could benefit from a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings, especially for the MI regularization strength (alpha).
* **Theoretical Justification for MI:** While the empirical results support the use of the MI regularizer, a slightly more in-depth discussion of the theoretical rationale for using MI in this context might be helpful. Is there a connection to information bottleneck principles or other relevant theoretical frameworks?
* **Addressing Instability (Ant Robot):** The authors acknowledge the challenges with the Ant robot due to instability. Further exploration of potential solutions (e.g., modifying the pre-training environment to specifically address robustness, or integrating a recovery mechanism into the high-level policy) would strengthen the paper.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Scalability:** How well does this framework scale to even higher-dimensional state and action spaces?  Are there any architectural modifications that would be necessary to maintain performance in more complex environments?
* **Choice of Proxy Reward:** The paper mentions the proxy reward should require minimal domain knowledge. Could you elaborate on guidelines for designing an effective proxy reward for new tasks? What happens if the chosen proxy reward is poorly designed?
* **Generalization to Different Task Distributions:** The experiments focus on relatively similar tasks. How robust is the pre-trained skill set to significant distributional shifts between the pre-training environment and the downstream tasks?  Would a curriculum learning approach for the pre-training phase be beneficial?
* **Regarding the MI Regularization:**  Did you experiment with other information-theoretic regularizers (e.g., total correlation) and, if so, why did you choose MI specifically?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling empirical evidence. Addressing the suggested feedback would further enhance the paper’s impact.
