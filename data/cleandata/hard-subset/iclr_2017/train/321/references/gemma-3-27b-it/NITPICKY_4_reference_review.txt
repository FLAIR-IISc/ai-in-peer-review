This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, subsequently used to accelerate learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to use the stochasticity of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies, capturing a range of skills, and then to guide this exploration with a mutual information (MI) regularizer. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on locomotion and maze navigation tasks.

**Reject.** While the core ideas are interesting and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws – particularly in experimental details, clarity of presentation, and a lack of rigorous comparison – that, cumulatively, raise significant concerns about the reliability and reproducibility of the results. The attention to detail necessary for a strong ICLR paper is simply lacking here.

The primary issue is the inconsistent and often vague reporting of experimental setup and results. The introduction of multiple architectural choices (concatenation vs. bilinear integration in SNNs) without a clear justification or ablation study makes it difficult to assess the contribution of each component. The impact of the MI regularizer, while claimed to be important, isn’t convincingly demonstrated; a simple increase in αH seems to always yield better results, suggesting a sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning that isn't adequately addressed. Furthermore, the multiple references to “videos” and an external GitHub repository as a substitute for detailed experimental descriptions within the paper itself are unacceptable. 

Specific concerns include:

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results appear highly sensitive to hyperparameters (αH, integration method). A more systematic ablation study is needed to understand the interplay between these parameters and the robustness of the approach.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The performance comparisons often lack statistical significance testing. Simply presenting learning curves without error bars makes it difficult to ascertain whether observed differences are meaningful.
*   **Incomplete Experimental Details:** Crucial details like network sizes (beyond the mention of 32 hidden units), optimization parameters, and the precise implementation of the MI regularizer are missing, hindering reproducibility. The grid size used for MI estimation is also only mentioned briefly.
*   **Overreliance on External Resources:** The paper heavily relies on supplementary material (videos, GitHub) for essential information. This is not a substitute for a thorough description within the main text.
*   **Unclear Motivation for Design Choices:** The rationale behind using bilinear integration isn’t fully explained, and its benefits aren’t clearly demonstrated through a comparative analysis with simpler alternatives.
*   **Inconsistent Terminology:** Phrases like "visitation plots" are used without proper definition initially.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Clarify the MI Regularizer:** Provide a more detailed explanation of how the MI is estimated and how it influences the learning process. Include a theoretical justification for its effectiveness.
*   **Conduct Ablation Studies:** Systematically evaluate the impact of each component of the proposed framework (SNNs, bilinear integration, MI regularizer) through ablation studies.
*   **Provide Statistical Analysis:** Include error bars or statistical significance tests to support performance comparisons.
*   **Enhance Experimental Details:** Include all relevant experimental details within the main text, including hyperparameter settings, network architectures, and optimization procedures.
*   **Improve Clarity and Organization:** Restructure the paper for better readability, with clearer section headings and a more logical flow of ideas.
*   **Address the Limitations:** Explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the approach, such as the sensitivity to hyperparameters and the potential instability issues.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions “straight-through estimators” but doesn’t utilize them. Was this intentionally omitted? If so, why?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the MI estimation grid resolution? Did you experiment with different resolutions?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with training the SNN compared to training separate policies?
4.  The GitHub repository is essential to understanding the implementation. Can you commit the *exact* code used to generate the results in the paper, including all dependencies and random seeds?
5.  What specific strategies were employed to tune the hyperparameters, particularly αH, and how were the reported values selected?
