This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework utilizing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning, aiming to address the challenges of sparse rewards and long horizons. The core idea is to pre-train SNNs with a proxy reward to learn a diverse set of skills, then leverage these skills with a high-level policy for downstream tasks. An information-theoretic regularizer (Mutual Information) is introduced to encourage skill diversity during pre-training. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on locomotion and maze navigation tasks, showing improved performance compared to baseline methods.

**Reject.** While the idea of using SNNs for hierarchical skill learning is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies, particularly regarding experimental detail and clarity, that collectively undermine confidence in the results and claims. The extensive reliance on ablation studies with unclear justification, combined with a lack of rigorous statistical analysis, makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of each component.

The primary concerns are:

1. **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** Results are presented as single runs or small numbers of runs without any statistical significance testing. Claims of improvement are made without establishing whether those improvements are statistically meaningful. This is particularly problematic given the acknowledged variance in performance based on pre-trained SNNs (Sec 7.3, C.4). The variations in results across different random seeds need to be properly quantified and analyzed.

2. **Inconsistent Experimental Detail:** The hyperparameters (Table 1 in Appendix A) are provided, but the specific details of the neural network architectures (number of layers, activation functions, etc.) are often vaguely described. Furthermore, the justification for architectural choices (e.g., bilinear integration vs. concatenation) feels superficial. The paper states, "We use simple categorical distributions with uniform weights for the latent variables…" yet doesn’t explicitly state *how* this uniform distribution is enforced or what its impact is. 

3. **Overly Complex Ablation and Justification:** The authors perform numerous ablation studies (varying MI bonus, integration type, switch time). However, the rationale for these specific ablations feels ad-hoc. For example, the claim that a larger switch time *should* improve performance in some environments (Sec. C.3) lacks sufficient theoretical backing and is presented as an observation rather than a hypothesis. The discussion surrounding the results of these ablations is often qualitative and lacks quantitative support.

4. **Ambiguous Claim of “Interpretability”:** The paper repeatedly mentions the "interpretability" of the learned skills as the latent code is varied. However, the provided visualizations (visitation plots) show general directional tendencies (e.g., forward, backward) and do not demonstrate a clear, high-level understanding of *what* each skill represents. The connection between the latent code and specific, meaningful actions needs to be better elucidated.  Simply showing different trajectories isn’t sufficient proof of meaningful skill separation.

5. **Presentation and Redundancy:** The paper is unnecessarily verbose.  There's a significant amount of repetition, particularly in the experimental sections. The extensive appendix, while providing some detail, also highlights the lack of conciseness in the main body of the paper.



Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):

*   **Statistical Analysis:** Include error bars or confidence intervals on all reported results. Perform statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to determine whether observed differences are statistically significant.
*   **Detailed Architecture Specifications:** Provide precise details of all neural network architectures used, including layer sizes, activation functions, and initialization schemes.
*   **Justification for Ablations:** Clearly articulate the motivation behind each ablation study and provide a strong theoretical or empirical rationale for the expected results.
*   **Skill Interpretability:** Provide a more rigorous analysis of the learned skills. Can the latent codes be associated with specific actions or behaviors? Quantify the similarity between skills and show how they differ.
*   **Code Quality and Documentation:** While code is provided, ensuring it is well-documented and readily reproducible is crucial.
*   **Conciseness:** Trim unnecessary repetition and streamline the presentation.



Questions for the authors:

1.  What specific statistical tests were used (if any) to assess the significance of the reported performance improvements?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the MI bonus influences the skill diversity, beyond the intuitive explanation provided in the paper?  Is there a relationship between the magnitude of the MI bonus and the dissimilarity of learned skills?
3.  What metrics were used to evaluate the "interpretability" of the skills, beyond visual inspection of the visitation plots?
4.  Why was bilinear integration chosen over other potential integration methods for the latent variables? Was a systematic comparison performed, and if so, what were the results?
5.  Given the observed variance in performance based on pre-trained SNNs, how can the pre-training process be made more robust and consistent? Are there techniques to select or refine the pre-trained policies to improve downstream performance?
