This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill learning, aiming to improve exploration in sparse reward environments. The core idea is to pre-train an SNN to learn a diverse set of skills using a proxy reward, and then utilize these skills as building blocks for downstream tasks via a high-level policy. The authors introduce a mutual information regularizer to encourage skill diversity during pre-training.

**Reject.** While the idea of utilizing SNNs for hierarchical RL and encouraging skill diversity with a mutual information regularizer is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws – primarily in presentation, experimental details, and justification of design choices – that collectively detract from the overall confidence in the work and its contribution. The excessive number of these flaws overshadows the potential novelty of the approach.

The paper’s presentation is often unclear and lacks sufficient detail. For instance, the bilinear integration method for combining latent variables and observations is introduced without a clear explanation of *why* this method was chosen over other potential integration strategies. The justification relies on references to prior work but lacks a specific rationale for its suitability to this problem. Similarly, the choice of the mutual information regularizer and its coefficient (αH) is not adequately motivated. The impact of varying αH isn't clearly articulated beyond “improving” the span of skills – quantitative analysis is missing. 

The experimental setup also raises concerns. The results, while showing some improvement, are presented with insufficient statistical rigor. There's a mention of multiple runs, but the variance is high, particularly in the Maze tasks, and the reasons for this variance – connection to specific SNNs – are noted but not thoroughly investigated.  The selection of hyperparameters (e.g., network size, learning rate) lacks justification. The ablation studies are minimal; it's unclear how crucial each component (SNNs, bilinear integration, MI bonus) is to the overall performance. The comparison to baselines, while included, feels somewhat superficial. The authors acknowledge the limitations of comparison settings (batch size, path length) and suggest that standard RL algorithms are unable to solve the tasks, but do not adequately explain why the intrinsic reward baseline struggles, or what precisely makes their approach different enough to succeed where others fail.

The numerous small details missing throughout the paper contribute to a feeling of incompleteness and a lack of thoroughness. For example, the implementation of TRPO, which is critical, is merely stated as being used "as is," without any specifics about tuning or adaptation for the SNN architecture. The link to the code is provided, but the code’s readability and documentation are not assessed in the review. There are several instances of self-referencing (“as shown in Figure X”) without adequate context or explanation within the surrounding text.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Clarity:** Rewrite sections 2, 3, and 5 to improve clarity. Provide more intuitive explanations of the mathematical concepts and design choices.
*   **Justification:** Provide stronger justifications for all architectural and algorithmic choices, citing relevant literature and explaining why these choices are expected to be effective.
*   **Experimental Rigor:**  Perform a more thorough statistical analysis of the results, including error bars, statistical significance tests, and comparisons with a wider range of baselines.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct detailed ablation studies to isolate the contributions of each component of the framework (SNNs, bilinear integration, MI bonus).
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Discuss the process of hyperparameter tuning and provide a justification for the chosen values.
*   **Code Quality:** Ensure the code is well-documented and easy to understand.
*   **Address the instability issues:** Elaborate on the issues encountered with the Ant robot, and explain the planned future work towards addressing this.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The bilinear integration appears to be a key component. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why this was chosen, and how it differs in its impact compared to simpler integration methods like concatenation?
2.  The choice of the MI bonus coefficient (αH) seems critical. What systematic exploration of this hyperparameter was performed, and what metrics were used to determine the optimal value?
3.  Given the high variance in the Maze experiments, could you provide more insight into the characteristics of the SNNs that perform well versus those that perform poorly? Are there any specific patterns in the learned skills that correlate with performance?
4.  Can you elaborate on the challenges encountered when applying this framework to more unstable robots like Ant, and what specific steps are being taken to address these challenges?
5.  The paper mentions several potential future directions. Could you elaborate on the expected computational cost of integrating the aforementioned ideas?
