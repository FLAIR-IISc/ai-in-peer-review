## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of exploration in sparse reward, long-horizon reinforcement learning tasks. The authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of low-level skills during a pre-training phase. These skills are then utilized by a high-level policy to solve downstream tasks, reducing sample complexity. The key innovation lies in using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies and employing a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage skill diversity during pre-training. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several challenging robotic control tasks, showcasing improved performance compared to existing methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**.  The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to hierarchical RL, addressing a critical challenge in the field. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear benefit to the proposed method. The use of SNNs and the MI regularization are both well-justified and contribute meaningfully to the framework.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of naive exploration strategies in sparse reward environments and positions the work within the broader context of HRL and intrinsic motivation. The problem is well-defined and relevant to current research challenges.
* **Novelty & Technical Soundness:**  The combination of SNNs with MI regularization for skill learning is a novel contribution. The justification for using SNNs (multi-modality, weight sharing) is sound, and the MI regularizer provides a principled way to encourage skill diversity. The bilinear integration method is a reasonable choice and appears to perform well.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the locomotion + maze and gather tasks are convincing. The paper convincingly demonstrates that the proposed method outperforms relevant baselines, including those employing intrinsic motivation. The ablation studies regarding the MI bonus and the SNN architecture provide valuable insights into the contributions of each component. The inclusion of results on a more complex robot (Snake) strengthens the findings.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methodology. The figures and tables are informative and aid in understanding the experimental results.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Deeper Dive into MI Regularization:** While the motivation for the MI regularizer is clear, a more detailed explanation of its impact on the learned skill space would be valuable. Perhaps visualizing the state distributions associated with each latent code could provide further insight.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper briefly mentions sample efficiency gains but doesn’t explicitly discuss the computational cost of training SNNs compared to standard policies. A more detailed analysis of the computational overhead would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the MI regularization coefficient (αH), could strengthen the results. How does performance change with different values of αH?
* **Clarify Role of Pre-training:**  It would be helpful to elaborate on the connection between the proxy reward used in pre-training and the types of skills that are learned. Are there specific characteristics of the proxy reward that are crucial for successful skill discovery?
* **Addressing Limitations in the Ant Robot Experiments:** The authors acknowledge the instability issues with the Ant robot and the associated failure modes.  Discussing potential solutions (e.g., adaptive switching times, more robust SNN architectures) would be valuable, even if they are beyond the scope of the current work.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the MI regularizer:**  You mention discretizing the state space to estimate the posterior probabilities.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of discretization resolution? Did you explore alternative methods for estimating the mutual information?
* **Regarding the bilinear integration:** Why was bilinear integration chosen over other forms of integration (e.g., concatenation)?  Were other options considered and compared?
* **Regarding task transfer:**  In the experiments, you train a new Manager Network for each downstream task.  Have you explored the possibility of *fine-tuning* the Manager Network across tasks to further improve sample efficiency?
* **Regarding scalability:**  How well does the approach scale to even more complex tasks with higher-dimensional state and action spaces?  Are there any limitations to the scalability of the SNN-based approach?
* **Regarding the instability of Ant:** You acknowledge the instability issues with Ant. Do you believe that incorporating domain knowledge or shaping rewards within the pre-training environment could alleviate these issues, even if it goes against the principle of minimal domain knowledge?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning. The proposed approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. Addressing the minor feedback points and answering the questions raised above will further strengthen the paper and solidify its impact.
