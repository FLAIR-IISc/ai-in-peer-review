## Peer Review for ICLR 2024

**Paper:** STOCHASTIC NEURAL NETWORKS FOR HIERARCHICAL REINFORCEMENT LEARNING

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment with a proxy reward. These learned skills are then utilized as building blocks for solving downstream tasks with sparse rewards, with a high-level policy learning to select and execute the appropriate skill. The authors argue that SNNs are particularly well-suited for skill learning due to their ability to represent multi-modal policies, and further improve diversity through a mutual information (MI) regularizer. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several challenging robotic manipulation and navigation tasks.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject** with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper presents an interesting idea with promising preliminary results, the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor and the presentation requires substantial clarification. The novelty, while present, isn't fully realized due to the current presentation and lack of comparison with established baselines.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Insufficient Baselines & Comparison:** The paper compares to a limited set of baselines. While acknowledging previous work, it doesn't provide a direct comparison to strong, recent HRL approaches (e.g., option discovery with modern exploration strategies, or other skill-learning methods) beyond a simplified intrinsic motivation baseline. The claim of outperforming previous work feels weakly supported without a more thorough competitive analysis.  Specifically, comparing to methods that also leverage pre-training for skill discovery (like Heess et al., 2016) is crucial.
* **Experimental Rigor & Statistical Significance:**  The experimental setup relies heavily on varying random seeds. While reporting results across seeds is good practice, the presentation lacks a clear analysis of statistical significance.  The variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs seems substantial, particularly for the more complex robot (Ant), raising concerns about the robustness of the approach. Presenting error bars or statistical tests would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper introduces several key components (SNNs, bilinear integration, MI regularization). However, it lacks a thorough ablation study to assess the individual contribution of each component. How much does each contribute to the final performance?  Is the bilinear integration crucial, or is a simple concatenation sufficient?  What is the sensitivity of the MI regularization coefficient?
* **Overly Complex Presentation:** The paper is dense and sometimes difficult to follow. The introduction of several concepts (SNNs, MI regularization, hierarchical structure) in quick succession can be overwhelming.  A more gradual introduction and clearer explanations would improve readability. The notation is also quite heavy and could be simplified.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarify the Novelty:** Clearly articulate *what* is novel about this approach compared to existing HRL methods. Is it the combination of SNNs, the MI regularization, the specific pre-training setup, or the entire framework?
* **Expand the Experimentation:**
    * Include more challenging downstream tasks.
    *  Compare to a wider range of HRL baselines.
    * Perform a comprehensive ablation study.
    * Investigate the impact of the number of latent variables (K).
    * Explore different pre-training environments.
* **Improve the Presentation:**
    * Simplify the notation and use more descriptive variable names.
    * Provide clearer explanations of the key concepts.
    *  Include illustrative diagrams to explain the architecture.
    * Streamline the text and remove redundant information.
* **Address the Stability Issue with Ant:** The failure cases observed with the Ant robot are concerning. Provide a more detailed analysis of why the SNN-based policy struggles with stability during skill switching. Explore potential solutions, such as adding a damping mechanism or adapting the skills during online learning.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding baseline selection:** Why were specific baselines chosen over others? What was the rationale for not including [mention specific relevant HRL baselines]?
* **Regarding the MI regularization:** How sensitive is the performance to the MI regularization coefficient (αH)? Did you perform a grid search to find the optimal value?
* **Regarding the SNN architecture:**  Did you experiment with other types of SNNs beyond the one described? If so, what were the results? If not, why?
* **Regarding the Ant robot instability:** What steps have you taken to address the instability issues observed with the Ant robot?  Could you provide more details on the failure modes and potential causes?
* **Regarding statistical significance:**  Can you provide statistical analysis (e.g., p-values) to support the performance claims?



In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea, it needs significant revisions to address the concerns regarding experimental rigor, baseline comparisons, and clarity of presentation.  I believe the authors have the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of HRL with a more thoroughly investigated and presented approach.
