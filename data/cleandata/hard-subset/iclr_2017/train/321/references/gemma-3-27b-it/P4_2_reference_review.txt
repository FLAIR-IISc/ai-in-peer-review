This paper proposes a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework utilizing Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training environment, which are then leveraged for faster learning in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The core idea is to use the stochasticity of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies, combined with a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage skill diversity. The framework then utilizes these pre-trained skills as options in a hierarchical policy, learning a higher-level policy to select and execute these skills.

**Reject.** While the idea of using SNNs for skill discovery within an HRL framework is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and rigor in its presentation and evaluation to warrant acceptance. The experimental results, while showing some improvement, are not convincingly demonstrated as significantly outperforming existing methods, and the ablation studies are not comprehensive enough to justify the design choices.

The paper’s primary weaknesses lie in the following areas:

1. **Lack of Quantitative Comparison & Statistical Significance:** The paper frequently mentions “outperforming” baselines (e.g., VIME) but provides limited quantitative comparison, particularly with appropriate statistical significance tests. Figures often show curves that intersect, making it difficult to ascertain clear superiority. Simply stating superiority isn't sufficient.
2. **Overly Complex Methodology & Limited Justification:** The use of bilinear integration in the SNN, while potentially impactful, is not sufficiently motivated beyond citing related work. A clearer explanation of *why* this specific integration is superior to simpler alternatives (e.g., concatenation) is needed. Similarly, the MI bonus parameter tuning lacks justification – why 0.05? How sensitive are the results to this parameter?
3. **Inconsistent Evaluation & Scope:** The experiments present results for a range of environments (Mazes, Gather). However, the rationale for choosing these environments and the specific characteristics that make them suitable for evaluating the proposed framework aren't clearly articulated. The mention of the “Ant” robot and its challenges in the discussion section suggests a failed experiment that deserves elaboration. What specific issues were encountered with the Ant, and what steps were taken to address them? Furthermore, the statement that “the baseline of having the CoM reward in the task happens to be stronger than expected” needs much more justification. The paper does not sufficiently clarify *why* this baseline performed well, and how that relates to the contribution of this work.

Despite the rejection, the following feedback is provided to potentially improve the paper:

* **Strengthen Quantitative Analysis:** Provide clear, statistically significant comparisons to relevant baselines. Include error bars or confidence intervals on the learning curves.
* **Clarify Design Choices:**  Offer more intuitive justifications for the chosen architectural components (bilinear integration) and hyperparameters (MI bonus weighting). A systematic ablation study examining the impact of each component is crucial.
* **Improve Experiment Documentation:**  Provide complete details of the experimental setup, including hyperparameter settings, network architectures, and training procedures.
* **Expand Discussion of Failure Cases:** Acknowledge and analyze the limitations of the approach, including failed experiments. Discuss potential solutions to address these limitations. The issues with the Ant robot deserve more attention.
* **Address Questions:**
    *  What is the computational cost associated with using SNNs compared to standard neural networks, and how does this affect scalability?
    *  How sensitive is the performance to the initial conditions and random seeds?
    *  How does the framework handle tasks where the optimal skill sequence is not fixed but depends on the current state?
    *  What is the effect of the pre-training environment on the skills learned? Would training in a more diverse pre-training environment lead to better transfer performance?
    * The statement about the CoM intrinsic reward being surprisingly strong needs to be explained in more detail. Why does the baseline perform so well?
