## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of exploration in sparse-reward reinforcement learning environments, particularly those requiring long horizons. The authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that leverages Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) to learn a diverse set of skills in a pre-training phase. These skills are then used as building blocks for faster learning in downstream tasks. A key contribution is the introduction of a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage diversity in the skills learned by the SNNs. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several challenging robotic manipulation and navigation tasks, showing improved sample efficiency and performance compared to existing methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to HRL with a solid experimental validation. The combination of SNNs and the MI regularizer appears to be a significant contribution, effectively addressing the exploration challenge in sparse reward settings.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly identifies the limitations of existing exploration strategies in sparse reward scenarios and provides a strong motivation for the proposed HRL framework. The connection to prior work in HRL and intrinsic motivation is well-established.
* **Novelty of the Approach:** The use of SNNs for learning a diverse skill set is a novel idea. The MI regularizer is a clever addition, directly addressing the potential for mode collapse in SNNs and encouraging the learning of genuinely distinct behaviors.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the locomotion + maze and locomotion + food collection tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison to baselines, including those utilizing intrinsic rewards, suggests a clear benefit from the SNN-based HRL framework. The ablation studies (e.g., impact of the MI bonus) provide valuable insights into the contribution of different components.
* **Thorough Experiments and Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation, including a variety of maze configurations and robots. The analysis of learned skills through visitation plots is particularly insightful, showcasing the diversity of behaviors achieved with the SNNs. The authors also acknowledge limitations and potential failure modes (e.g., with the Ant robot) which is commendable.
* **Code Availability:** Publicly available code is a significant plus, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors mention sample efficiency improvements, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with training SNNs (compared to standard neural networks) would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief analysis of the sensitivity of the approach to key hyperparameters (e.g., the MI coefficient, network architecture) would strengthen the results.
* **Clarify the Relationship to Option Learning:** While the paper touches upon option learning, explicitly connecting the learned skills to the concept of options and discussing potential benefits/drawbacks in this context could be insightful.
* **Expansion of the Discussion on Failure Modes:** The discussion on the instability issues with the Ant robot is a good start. Elaborating on potential solutions or adaptations of the approach to address these challenges would be valuable.
* **Figure Clarity:** Some of the figures (e.g., Fig. 1) could benefit from clearer labeling and visual presentation.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* The MI regularizer seems crucial for skill diversity. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the MI coefficient (αH)? Did you observe a saturation point beyond which increasing αH did not lead to further improvement?
* In the experiments, you freeze the low-level policies during downstream task training. Have you explored end-to-end training with backpropagation through the SNN, potentially using straight-through estimators? If so, what were the results?
* You mention that the bilinear integration in the SNN architecture yields better results. Did you experiment with other forms of integration (e.g., multiplicative integration) and compare their performance?
* The paper focuses on relatively simple environments. How well do you believe this approach would scale to more complex, high-dimensional environments with more subtle reward structures? What are the main challenges you foresee?



Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of hierarchical reinforcement learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results. I am confident that addressing the minor suggestions above will further strengthen the paper and its impact.
