## Review of "Stochastic Neural Networks for Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning"

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper tackles the challenge of sparse reward reinforcement learning, particularly in tasks requiring long horizons. The authors propose a hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) framework that first pre-trains a Stochastic Neural Network (SNN) to learn a diverse set of skills using a simple proxy reward. These skills are then leveraged as building blocks for learning high-level policies in downstream tasks with sparse rewards. The key innovation lies in using SNNs to represent multi-modal policies and employing a mutual information (MI) regularizer to encourage skill diversity during pre-training. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several challenging robotic manipulation and locomotion tasks.

**2. Decision**

**Accept (Weak)**. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to HRL with a strong theoretical foundation and promising empirical results. However, the claims are somewhat overstated given the experimental setup and the presentation could be significantly improved with clearer explanations and more robust statistical analysis.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The combination of SNNs with an MI regularizer for skill discovery is a novel contribution. Addressing sparse reward challenges in HRL is a significant problem, and the authors provide a clear and compelling motivation for their approach. The idea of learning a general set of skills through a pre-training phase is also well-justified.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies is theoretically sound and aligns with existing work on stochastic neural networks. The MI regularizer is a sensible way to promote diversity in the learned skills. The proposed framework is clearly described and appears logically consistent.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework can outperform baselines, including those employing intrinsic motivation. The visualisations of learned skills (visitation plots) are convincing and show that the SNNs learn diverse behaviors.  The results on the Maze and Gather tasks are promising, although the improvement over strong baselines isn't always dramatic.
* **Strengths:**
    * **Clear problem definition:** The paper clearly outlines the challenges of sparse reward RL and hierarchical learning.
    * **Well-defined framework:** The proposed framework is well-structured and integrates SNNs, MI regularization, and hierarchical policy learning effectively.
    * **Reproducibility:** Code availability is a significant strength.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

* **Statistical Significance:** The paper lacks robust statistical analysis of the results.  Reporting means and standard deviations for multiple runs is essential. Significance tests (e.g., t-tests) are needed to demonstrate that the observed improvements are statistically significant and not due to chance.  The authors mention 10 runs in the appendix, but this detail is missing from the main body and the results aren’t presented in a way that allows for analysis.
* **Baseline Comparisons:** While the authors compare against relevant baselines, the comparison is not always comprehensive. A more detailed comparison to other state-of-the-art HRL algorithms would strengthen the paper. Justification for the selected baselines would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper could benefit from a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly regarding the MI bonus coefficient (αH) and the switch time (T). How do these parameters affect the performance of the framework?
* **Ablation Studies:** More thorough ablation studies are needed to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the framework. For example, what is the performance without the MI regularizer? What if the SNN is replaced with a standard neural network?
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is dense and can be difficult to follow.  Simplifying the language, adding more intuitive explanations, and improving the figures would enhance readability. Specifically, the discussion of the bilinear integration could be made more accessible.
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:** The authors briefly acknowledge limitations with the Ant robot. Expanding this discussion with a more detailed analysis of failure modes and potential solutions would be valuable.
* **Impact of Pretraining:** It would be valuable to analyze the quality of the pre-trained skills in more detail. How does the diversity of the learned skills correlate with performance on downstream tasks?

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*  Could you provide a table summarizing the statistical significance of your results, including p-values for comparisons with baselines?
*  How sensitive is the performance of your framework to the choice of the MI bonus coefficient (αH)? Did you perform a hyperparameter sweep to determine the optimal value?
*  What is the computational cost of training the SNN compared to training a standard neural network with the same number of parameters?
*  You mention that the switch time (T) does not critically affect performance. Can you provide more details on your experiments with different values of T, and explain why you chose the specific value used in your main experiments?
*  The discussion of the bilinear integration is somewhat unclear. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why this integration method is beneficial for learning diverse skills?



In conclusion, while this paper has some weaknesses in terms of presentation and statistical rigor, it presents a promising approach to HRL with a solid theoretical foundation and encouraging empirical results. With revisions addressing the points above, it has the potential to become a significant contribution to the field.
