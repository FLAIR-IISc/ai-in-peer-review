This paper tackles the challenging problem of sparse reward reinforcement learning by proposing a hierarchical framework leveraging Stochastic Neural Networks (SNNs) for skill discovery. The core idea is to pre-train a diverse set of skills using a proxy reward, and then utilize these skills as building blocks for solving downstream tasks, significantly reducing sample complexity. The approach is well-motivated, addressing a known limitation of traditional RL methods in complex environments. The use of SNNs to represent multi-modal policies and the incorporation of a mutual information regularizer to encourage skill diversity are particularly promising contributions.

**Reject.** While the idea is compelling and novel, the presentation and experimental validation lack the rigor needed for acceptance at ICLR. The results, while positive, are presented in a somewhat fragmented way and the comparisons to existing methods are not always directly comparable or sufficiently detailed. The impact of individual components (bilinear integration, MI bonus) is not always clearly demonstrated, and the variance in performance across different pre-trained SNNs raises concerns about robustness.

The novelty of the approach is its strength. Combining SNNs with a hierarchical RL framework and utilizing a mutual information regularizer for skill diversity is a genuinely interesting direction. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing exploration strategies and propose a theoretically sound solution. The claim of learning interpretable skills through varying the latent code is also appealing. 

However, several issues weaken the paper’s overall impact. First, the experimental setup is complex, involving multiple hyperparameters and architectural choices. While the appendix details these, the justification for these choices is often lacking.  The significant performance variation based on the pre-trained SNN (Section 7.3, Appendix C.4) is concerning.  The authors acknowledge this but don’t provide a sufficient analysis of *why* some SNNs perform better than others – is it initialization, the specific random seed, the effect of the MI bonus?  This lack of understanding undermines the confidence in the approach's reliability. Second, the comparison to baselines, particularly in the Gather task, is not entirely convincing. The authors mention that the baseline struggles due to the long horizon, but don’t fully demonstrate how their hierarchical approach overcomes this challenge *specifically*. The improvements need to be more clearly attributed to the skill hierarchy and not just to a more efficient exploration strategy. Third, while the ablation studies are present, they are not always presented clearly. The impact of the bilinear integration *versus* concatenation is not fully explored and the benefit of the MI bonus seems task-dependent. Finally, the Ant robot experiments highlight a significant limitation – the instability issues when switching skills. While acknowledged, this is a critical issue that needs to be addressed or at least thoroughly discussed.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the benefit of bilinear integration:** Provide a more detailed explanation of why bilinear integration is superior to simple concatenation, potentially with visualization of the learned representations.
*   **Analyze SNN variance:** Investigate the factors contributing to the performance variance across different pre-trained SNNs. Can the selection of a "good" SNN be predicted or improved?
*   **Strengthen baseline comparisons:** Perform a more direct comparison with state-of-the-art intrinsic motivation methods, ensuring comparable settings and evaluation metrics.
*   **Address Ant instability:** Provide a detailed discussion of the instability issues with the Ant robot and potential solutions (e.g., smooth skill transitions, learning a termination policy).
*   **Provide more illustrative examples:** Include more visualizations of the learned skills, perhaps demonstrating how they are used to solve specific tasks.
*   **Refine the presentation:** Streamline the presentation and clarify the connections between different sections. Reduce redundancy and improve the flow of the narrative.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific characteristics of a pre-trained SNN correlate with good performance in downstream tasks? Are there any metrics you can use to identify promising SNNs *before* deploying them in a hierarchical framework?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of training SNNs compared to training separate policies?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the mutual information coefficient (αH)? Is there a systematic way to tune this parameter?
4.  Regarding the Ant robot experiments, have you explored any techniques to mitigate the instability issues during skill switching, such as blending between skills or learning a termination condition?
