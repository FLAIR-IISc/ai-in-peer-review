This paper proposes a new approach to image super-resolution (SR) using amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that traditional empirical risk minimisation methods, which often use pixel-wise mean squared error (MSE) loss, can produce blurry and over-smoothed outputs. Instead, they propose using a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that projects the model output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring that the high-resolution output is always consistent with the low-resolution input.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of traditional SR methods and the benefits of using MAP inference. They also provide a thorough review of related work, including recent attempts to improve perceptual quality using deep representations of natural images.

The approach proposed in the paper is novel and interesting. The authors introduce a new CNN architecture that uses a projection to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, which ensures that the high-resolution output is always consistent with the low-resolution input. They also propose three methods to solve the optimisation problem: Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN), denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior.

The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors show that the GAN-based approach performs best on real image data, and that the affine projection layer does not hurt the performance of CNNs for image SR.

Overall, I would accept this paper.
