**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel approach for image super-resolution (SR) using amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that traditional empirical risk minimisation methods using mean squared error (MSE) or mean absolute error (MAE) loss functions can produce blurry and over-smoothed outputs. Instead, they propose using a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that projects the model output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring that the high-resolution output is consistent with the low-resolution input. They then present three methods to approximate the MAP estimate: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior. Experimental results show that the GAN-based approach performs best on real image data.

* The proposed CNN architecture is well-motivated and provides a novel solution to the problem of ensuring consistency between the high-resolution output and the low-resolution input.
* The three methods proposed for approximating the MAP estimate are well-explained and provide a comprehensive approach to the problem.
* The experimental results are thorough and provide a clear comparison between the proposed methods and traditional approaches.

**Additional feedback**

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly with regards to the assumption of a Gaussian noise distribution.
* The authors could provide more insight into the choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the results to these choices.
* The connection between GANs and amortised variational inference is interesting and warrants further exploration.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for image super-resolution.
