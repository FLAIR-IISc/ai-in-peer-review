**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach for image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that projects the model output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between the input and output. They then propose three methods to approximate the MAP inference problem: (1) Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), (2) denoiser-guided SR, and (3) a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of these methods on both toy data and real image datasets.

**Decision: Accept**

I decide to accept this paper due to its novel approach to image super-resolution and its thorough evaluation on various datasets. The paper provides a clear and well-motivated explanation of the proposed methods and their connections to existing work in the field.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Novelty and Impact**: The paper proposes a new approach to image super-resolution that deviates from traditional empirical risk minimization methods. The use of amortized MAP inference and the introduction of the affine projection layer are novel contributions that have the potential to impact the field of image processing.
2. **Thorough Evaluation**: The authors conduct extensive experiments on various datasets, including toy data, grass textures, CelebA faces, and natural images from ImageNet. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the GAN-based approach, in producing perceptually superior SR results.
3. **Clear Explanation and Connections to Existing Work**: The paper provides a clear and well-motivated explanation of the proposed methods and their connections to existing work in the field. The authors discuss the limitations of traditional methods and how their approach addresses these limitations.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Instance Noise**: The authors propose a new technique called instance noise to stabilize GAN training. While this technique is promising, it would be beneficial to provide more analysis on its effectiveness and potential limitations.
2. **Comparison to Other Methods**: While the paper compares the proposed methods to traditional MSE-based methods, it would be beneficial to compare them to other state-of-the-art SR methods, such as those using deep learning-based approaches.
3. **Limitations and Future Work**: The authors discuss some limitations of their approach, including the assumption that the mode of the distribution is representative of the data. It would be beneficial to provide more discussion on potential limitations and future work directions.
