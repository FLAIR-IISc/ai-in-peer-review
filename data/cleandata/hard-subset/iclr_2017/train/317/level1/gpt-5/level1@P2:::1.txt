Summary
The paper proposes “amortised MAP” inference for single-image super-resolution (SR). The key idea is to avoid MSE-trained regressors (which average over multimodal posteriors and produce blurry outputs) and instead train a function that directly maps LR inputs to the MAP estimate under a natural image prior. The main technical contribution is an affine projection layer that enforces exact consistency with the imaging model x = Ay by projecting any network output onto the affine subspace {y: Ay = x}. With this, the amortised MAP objective reduces to minimising the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY], where qθ is the marginal distribution of reconstructions under LR inputs and pY is the HR data distribution. The paper develops three training schemes to approximate this objective: (1) AffGAN, an adversarial approach with a modified generator update and “instance noise” to stabilise training; (2) AffDG, which backpropagates gradients of log pY via a separately trained denoiser (DAE); and (3) AffLL, which uses a maximum-likelihood image model (PixelCNN+MCGSM) as a differentiable prior. Empirically, the affine projection improves training and guarantees LR-HR consistency; AffGAN produces sharper, more plausible images on textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet). The paper also provides theoretical connections between their GAN update and KL minimisation, and between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) The affine projection framework is a simple, elegant architectural contribution that exactly enforces the forward model and reframes SR training as matching qθ to pY, avoiding paired supervision. (2) The AffGAN formulation and “instance noise” provide useful theoretical and practical advances; the paper offers a clear MAP motivation, connects to literature, and shows convincing qualitative gains where MSE is known to fail (textures). The work is timely and relevant given interest in perceptual SR.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the mismatch between MSE training and perceptual plausibility in ill-posed SR, arguing for MAP estimates that lie in high-probability regions of the image prior. This is well-motivated and illustrated with a toy Swiss-roll example where MSE/MAE yield low-probability solutions while MAP selects the mode.

- Placement in literature: The approach builds on GANs, denoising-score connections, and PixelCNN-like density models, and distinguishes itself from prior SRGAN-type work by (i) enforcing exact observation consistency via affine projection rather than soft penalties; (ii) grounding adversarial training as (approximate) KL minimisation in the amortised MAP objective; and (iii) proposing instance noise to address discriminator over-separation. Related work is broad and mostly appropriate.

- Technical correctness and rigor:
  • Likelihood handling via exact projection is sound. The decomposition from maximising Ex log pY|X(fθ(x)|x) to minimising H[qθ, pY] under the affine projection is clear and insightful.
  • The AffGAN update derivation that relates to KL[q‖p] (generator loss as −E log D/(1−D)) is consistent with known f-GAN views and tailored to the SR setting by substituting the generator with ΠAx fθ(x). The instance noise rationale addresses support-mismatch pathologies and aligns with later stabilisation ideas.
  • The denoiser-gradient estimator of ∇y log pY(y) is standard; using it to backprop through the SR network is reasonable, though the observed underperformance is discussed.
  • The amortised VI connection (Appendix F) is a neat result: under the affine projection, KL(qY||pY) equals Ex KL(qY|X||pY|X). Although not empirically validated, it’s conceptually valuable.

- Empirical support:
  • Toy 2D: Cross-entropy H is reported and shows AffGAN/AffDG close to brute-force MAP; affine projection yields zero LR inconsistency and lower H than soft constraints.
  • MSE baseline with/without projection: Affine projection improves optimisation (faster training, better SSIM/MSE, exact consistency).
  • Real images: On grass textures, AffGAN clearly improves perceptual sharpness; AffDG/LL are blurry/unstable. On faces and ImageNet, AffGAN gives sharp, plausible results but sometimes adds high-frequency noise or hallucinates plausible but incorrect content (e.g., snake→water), consistent with the objective. Quantitative PSNR/SSIM favor MSE as expected; the paper appropriately argues those metrics don’t reflect perceptual quality.

Limitations (tempering the score but not fatal):
- Quantitative evaluation is limited for perceptual quality (no user study, no alternative perceptual metrics beyond SSIM). Comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., Ledig et al. SRGAN) are mostly qualitative and incomplete.
- The AffDG and AffLL variants underperform and their failure modes, while discussed, reduce the breadth of validated contributions.
- The method assumes a known downsampling operator A; real cameras have unknown or spatially varying degradations. The practicality of learning or adapting A/A+ is not explored.
- The “no paired data” claim hinges on access to LR pX and HR pY consistent with the same A; domain mismatch concerns are not evaluated.
- Theoretical claims about stabilisation via instance noise are persuasive, but ablations across datasets and discriminator update schedules are limited.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen empirical evaluation with perceptual studies (2AFC/MOS) and add comparisons to SRGAN and feature/perceptual-loss methods on standard SR benchmarks. Report diversity measures and LR-consistency errors for all models.
- Provide ablations: with/without instance noise; noise schedules; discriminator steps per generator step; and with/without fixed A+ initialisation.
- Explore robustness to misspecified A and to realistic noise in x; discuss or demonstrate how to estimate A (or jointly learn A+) from data.
- For AffDG/LL, try modern score-matching/denoising (e.g., annealed noise schedules, deeper DAEs) and stronger density models; clarify whether their underperformance is due to optimisation or model misspecification.
- Consider adding weak content losses or feature-matching terms to reduce high-frequency noise while preserving perceptual sharpness.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is AffGAN to misspecification of A (kernel shape/stride) and to observation noise? Can the affine projection be adapted to unknown or spatially varying A?
2. Can you quantify the impact of instance noise: convergence stability, mode coverage, and final image quality, compared to label smoothing or WGAN-like objectives?
3. How does AffGAN compare against SRGAN (Ledig et al.) and feature/perceptual-loss methods on the same datasets with human studies?
4. Does the entropy term implicit in minimising KL[q‖p] (vs pure cross-entropy) measurably increase output diversity? Could a stochastic generator (Appendix F) help navigate the MAP vs sampling trade-off?
5. How robust is training without paired data when pX is collected from a different domain or pipeline than pY? Any domain adaptation strategies?
6. What is the computational overhead of computing/fixing A+? Does learning A+ harm LR consistency or aid performance if A is only approximate?
