This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR), arguing that existing methods based on empirical risk minimization (like MSE) lead to blurry results due to averaging over plausible high-resolution solutions. The authors propose an approach based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, aiming to find the most probable HR image given a low-resolution input. They introduce a novel CNN architecture with an affine projection to ensure consistency between LR and HR images, and explore three methods for MAP inference: GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper. While the idea of framing SR as amortized MAP inference is interesting, and the affine projection is a clever concept, the empirical results presented don't convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods, particularly the GAN-based approach which is highlighted as the best performer. The presentation is also quite dense, and critical details regarding implementation and experimental setup are relegated to the appendix, hindering thorough evaluation.

My primary reason for rejection is the lack of robust and conclusive evidence supporting the claims of improved perceptual quality. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment convincingly demonstrates the method's ability to find MAP estimates in a simple setting (Table 1), but this doesn’t translate to compelling results on natural images. While the authors report qualitative improvements in some cases (Figures 3-5), these are often subtle and hard to objectively assess. The quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM in Table 2) often *favor* the MSE-trained baseline, contradicting the claim that MAP inference yields better results. The authors acknowledge high-frequency noise in the GAN results, which detracts from perceptual quality, and the example of the snake being “super-resolved” into water (Section 5.5) raises concerns about the plausibility of the generated images. The connection to variational inference established in the appendix is interesting but feels like an afterthought and isn’t sufficiently explored to add significant value.

Secondly, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity regarding implementation details and ablation studies. The choice of specific network architectures, hyperparameters, and training procedures isn’t adequately justified. The reliance on the appendix for crucial details (e.g., precise training parameters, details about the noise annealing schedule) makes it difficult to reproduce the results or understand the sensitivity of the performance to different choices. A clear ablation study demonstrating the benefits of the affine projection, different loss functions, and instance noise would be crucial. The fact that the authors needed instance noise to stabilize GAN training suggests a fundamental issue with the approach, and this isn't adequately addressed. The discussion of the DAE and density-guided models quickly dismisses them due to training issues, which seems premature without further investigation.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

* **More Robust Evaluation:** Conduct a more thorough perceptual evaluation using metrics like LPIPS or conducting a user study to assess the perceptual quality of the generated images.
* **Ablation Studies:** Include ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (affine projection, different loss functions, instance noise).
* **Detailed Implementation Details:** Provide a more complete and self-contained description of the experimental setup, including network architectures, hyperparameters, and training procedures. Move key details from the appendix into the main text.
* **Analysis of Failure Cases:** Analyze failure cases and discuss the limitations of the proposed approach.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** Compare the performance of the proposed methods to more recent and established SR techniques (e.g., EDSR, RCAN).
* **Clarify the Role of Instance Noise:** Provide a more thorough analysis of why instance noise is necessary to stabilize GAN training and its impact on the generated images.

Questions for the authors:

* How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the Gaussian kernel width used in the affine projection?
* Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the high-frequency noise observed in the GAN-generated images, and what steps are being taken to mitigate this issue?
* What is the computational cost of the affine projection compared to standard convolution operations?
* Why did the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods fail to produce satisfactory results? Were any attempts made to optimize their performance further?
* What specific user study protocol would you employ to validate the perceived quality of the generated images compared to the baseline methods?
