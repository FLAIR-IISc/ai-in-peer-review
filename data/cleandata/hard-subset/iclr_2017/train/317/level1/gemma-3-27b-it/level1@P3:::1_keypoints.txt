- Frames image super-resolution as MAP inference to avoid MSE-induced blurring.
- Proposes a novel CNN with an affine projection enforcing LR–HR consistency.
- Explores three amortized MAP methods (AffGAN, denoiser-guided AffDG, ML-prior AffLL); AffGAN performs best on real images.
- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient empirical evidence and limited exploration.
- Motivation for MAP is well-articulated; MSE averaging/blurriness issue is correctly identified.
- Affine projection is a clever idea that reduces the problem to minimizing cross-entropy.
- Appendix connection between GANs and variational inference adds theoretical depth.
- Paper is well-written and methodology is clearly explained.
- Experiments lack direct comparisons to state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, SRGAN).
- Quantitative results show the MSE model achieves better PSNR/SSIM, contradicting the paper’s argument against MSE.
- Results on the grass texture dataset are interesting but too narrow in scope.
- Claim that GANs yield best perceptual quality is insufficiently supported; visual examples are subjective.
- AffDG and AffLL methods exhibited instability/divergence and were not thoroughly investigated or stabilized.
- Instance noise discussion is valuable but its impact is not quantitatively assessed.
- The 2D Swiss-roll experiment is a toy example and doesn’t fully transfer to natural-image complexity.
- Requested improvements: benchmark on standard datasets/metrics (Set5, Set14, BSD100; PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS).
- Requested improvements: conduct detailed ablation studies of the AffGAN architecture and hyperparameters.
- Requested improvements: run a user study for objective perceptual evaluation.
- Requested improvements: analyze the affine projection more rigorously, including imperfect projection effects.
- Questions for authors: why omit comparisons to SOTA SR methods, details on AffGAN hyperparameter tuning, why MSE outperforms GAN in PSNR/SSIM, what caused AffDG/AffLL failures and stabilization attempts, and how instance noise relates to other regularizers (e.g., label smoothing).
