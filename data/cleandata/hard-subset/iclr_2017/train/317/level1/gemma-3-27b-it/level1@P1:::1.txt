## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by moving away from empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses (like MSE) and towards a Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference framework. The key idea is to build a convolutional neural network that directly estimates the MAP estimate, effectively learning an image prior. A novel architectural component, a projection to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, is introduced to ensure consistency between low- and high-resolution images. The authors explore three methods to train this network: GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a maximum-likelihood trained image prior.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting approach to SR, motivated by the limitations of MSE-based methods and the desire to incorporate a stronger image prior. However, the empirical results, while demonstrating some gains, are not convincing enough to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The improvements are often marginal and, critically, are not consistently demonstrated across datasets or metrics. The theoretical motivation is well-developed, but the practical realization appears to be a significant challenge.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Gains:** The most substantial improvements are shown in the Swiss-roll toy experiment, which is a good validation of the core idea but doesn't strongly translate to real image SR. On CelebA and ImageNet, the AffGAN model shows some qualitative improvements but often isn't clearly superior to existing methods, particularly when considering standard metrics (PSNR, SSIM – Table 2). The reported gains are often small and can be attributed to factors other than the proposed method (e.g., architectural differences). The comparison to existing SR methods, including Ledig et al. (2016), lacks sufficient detail and a rigorous benchmark.
2. **Inconsistent Results & Ablation Study Weakness:** The experiments with grass textures show promising results for AffGAN, but the denoiser-guided and density-guided models are dismissed due to poor performance. The authors indicate these models faced training divergence issues, but the discussion of this instability is too brief. It’s unclear whether the failure of these methods is intrinsic to the approach or due to implementation details or hyperparameter tuning. The ablation study (Figure 2) is helpful in demonstrating the benefit of the affine projection, but more comprehensive ablation studies, isolating the impact of different components of AffGAN (e.g., instance noise), are needed.
3. **Lack of Quantitative Robustness:** The paper demonstrates that the AffGAN and AffDG approaches minimize cross-entropy in the 2D Swiss roll example (Table 1). However, the correlation between minimizing cross-entropy and achieving high perceptual quality or PSNR/SSIM in the image SR task is not convincingly established. The low cross-entropy doesn’t automatically translate to superior performance on real images.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **More Rigorous Comparison:** A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, SRGAN, RDN) is crucial. This should include a fair comparison with identical network architectures and training procedures, varying only the loss function or training strategy.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the AffGAN performance to hyperparameters such as the learning rate, batch size, and the noise level in instance noise.
* **Visual Qualitative Assessment:** Provide more carefully selected and labeled visual examples, including comparisons to representative outputs from existing SR methods. Consider using a user study to assess the perceptual quality of the generated images.
* **Divergence Analysis:** Provide more detail on the challenges encountered during the training of the denoiser-guided and density-guided models. Explore techniques to stabilize their training, such as different optimizers, learning rate schedules, or regularization methods.
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of the proposed methods compared to existing SR techniques.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* The AffGAN model sometimes generates plausible but *incorrect* content, such as turning a snake into water (Section 5.5). How do you interpret this behavior, and what steps could be taken to mitigate this issue?
* The noise annealing schedule for instance noise is briefly mentioned. Can you provide more details on the specific schedule used and its impact on the training process?
* Could you elaborate on the reasons why the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods failed to produce competitive results? Were these methods equally sensitive to hyperparameter tuning, or were there specific challenges unique to each approach?
* How does the performance of the AffGAN model compare to that of SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016) with comparable network architectures and training parameters?
* What is the computational overhead introduced by the affine projection layer compared to a standard convolutional neural network?
