This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that existing methods relying on empirical risk minimization (e.g., MSE loss) lead to blurry results, and propose a novel CNN architecture with an affine projection to ensure consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs. They explore three methods for solving the resulting MAP inference problem: GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood trained image prior.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and explores several avenues for implementing amortized MAP inference for SR, the empirical results are not compelling enough to justify acceptance. The strongest results are obtained with the GAN-based approach, but even those results do not consistently and significantly outperform existing state-of-the-art methods, and the improvements, where observed, are often marginal. Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear and concise comparison against strong baselines, particularly recent advancements in GAN-based SR beyond Ledig et al. (2016).

The central argument of the paper—that MAP inference will yield better results than MSE minimization—is intuitively appealing and theoretically sound. The introduction effectively motivates this approach with a simple 2D example. The proposed affine projection is a clever way to enforce consistency between the input and output, and the connection drawn between GANs and variational inference is valuable. However, the experimental validation falls short. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment convincingly demonstrates the ability of the AffGAN and AffDG models to find the MAP solution in a simplified setting. However, this doesn't directly translate to improved performance on more complex natural image datasets. The grass texture experiment shows promise, but the lack of strong quantitative results and comparison with other SR algorithms limits its impact. The CelebA and ImageNet experiments reveal only modest gains with the AffGAN model. The reliance on PSNR and SSIM as evaluation metrics is acknowledged as imperfect, but more sophisticated perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) should have been included to provide a more robust assessment of visual quality.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Baselines:** A more comprehensive comparison with strong SR baselines, including SRGAN, ESRGAN, and other recent approaches, is crucial. The current comparison lacks sufficient depth.
*   **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies are needed to isolate the impact of the affine projection layer. Specifically, the effect of initializing A+ correctly versus learning it should be investigated more thoroughly.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost associated with the proposed methods, particularly the denoiser-guided approach, which involves training an additional denoising autoencoder.
*   **Instance Noise:** The explanation of instance noise is somewhat convoluted. A clearer explanation of its benefits and a more detailed analysis of its impact on training stability would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity of Notation:** While the theoretical derivations are generally well-presented, some of the notation could be clarified. For example, the distinction between different versions of q (qθ, qY;θ, etc.) could be made more explicit.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While a brief discussion of the limitations of MAP inference is provided, a more thorough analysis of the potential drawbacks of the proposed methods would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of AffGAN compare to ESRGAN, a current state-of-the-art GAN-based SR method, on the CelebA and ImageNet datasets?
2.  What is the computational overhead associated with the denoiser-guided SR approach compared to training a standard GAN-based SR model?
3.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of the benefits of instance noise and how it addresses the instability of GAN training?
4.  The paper mentions that the AffGAN model can sometimes "dream up" plausible but incorrect details (e.g., turning a snake into water). How can this behavior be mitigated or controlled?
5.  The theoretical framework highlights the connection to variational inference. Have you explored stochastic extensions of AffGAN more fully, and if so, what were the results?
