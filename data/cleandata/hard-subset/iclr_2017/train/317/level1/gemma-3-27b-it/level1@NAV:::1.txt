## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors motivate this by arguing that standard empirical risk minimization with losses like MSE leads to blurry results, lacking plausibility according to the image prior. They introduce a CNN architecture incorporating an affine projection to ensure consistency between LR and HR outputs, and then explore three methods – GANs, denoiser guidance, and a baseline with a learned prior – to solve the resulting inference problem. The core idea of explicitly modelling the image prior and enforcing consistency is well-motivated and represents a promising direction for SR.

**Reject** with a request for major revisions. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and some compelling initial results, the experimental validation feels incomplete and the connection between the different methods is not fully explored. Specifically, the jump from demonstrating cross-entropy minimization in a toy problem to convincingly showing perceptual gains in real images is not sufficiently strong.

The approach is well-motivated, building upon the limitations of MSE-based SR and the benefits of MAP inference. The authors correctly identify the challenges of directly applying MAP to SR and offer a clever solution with the affine projection. The theoretical justification for leveraging GANs within this framework is also well-presented, drawing connections to variational inference. However, the experiments primarily focus on demonstrating the theoretical results (cross-entropy minimization) rather than rigorously evaluating perceptual quality. The quantitative results, while providing some insights, are insufficient to demonstrate superiority over existing state-of-the-art SR methods, and the qualitative examples lack sufficient comparison. The ablation study comparing AffGAN and SoftGAN is promising, but more extensive evaluation is needed. The dismissal of the denoiser and density guided approaches after initial experiments feels premature, without a more detailed investigation into their limitations.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** Please include a more comprehensive quantitative evaluation using standard SR metrics (PSNR, SSIM) *alongside* perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS). Comparisons to state-of-the-art SR methods are crucial.
*   **Qualitative Evaluation:** Expand the visual comparisons to include more examples and a wider range of image content. Include comparisons to established SR algorithms (e.g., EDSR, RCAN).
*   **Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study of the AffGAN architecture and training parameters would be valuable.
*   **Connection Between Methods:**  The paper presents three methods (GAN, denoiser, density model) but doesn't fully analyze their relative strengths and weaknesses. Which scenarios is each method best suited for? What are the trade-offs?
*   **Instance Noise:** The instance noise technique appears promising; a more detailed justification for its effectiveness and a sensitivity analysis of the noise level would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   In Section 5.6, you mention the potential limitations of MAP inference regarding representation dependence. How does the affine projection help mitigate this issue in the context of your approach?
*   The results in Section 5.3 show visually compelling results for grass textures with AffGAN, but these are not quantified. Could you provide quantitative metrics for this specific experiment?
*   Why were the denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches largely abandoned? What specific challenges did you encounter that prevented them from achieving competitive results?
*   Could you elaborate on the specific choices made for the network architectures (number of layers, filter sizes, etc.) and their impact on performance?
