This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as a Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, motivated by the limitations of empirical risk minimization with losses like MSE which tend to produce blurry results. The authors propose a novel CNN architecture enforcing consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs via an affine projection. They then explore three methods for amortized MAP inference – GANs, a denoiser-guided approach, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood trained image prior – and demonstrate through experiments that the GAN-based approach performs best on real image data.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a thoughtful approach to SR and demonstrates some promising results, particularly with the GAN-based method, the empirical evidence isn’t sufficiently convincing to warrant acceptance. The core problem lies in a lack of comprehensive and convincing demonstration of improvement over existing state-of-the-art SR methods, and a somewhat limited exploration of the proposed techniques.

The motivation for MAP inference in SR is well-established and clearly articulated. The observation that MSE-trained models average over plausible solutions, resulting in blurry images, is a well-known issue. The introduction of the affine projection is a clever idea, ensuring consistency between LR and HR images and reducing the problem to minimizing cross-entropy.  The connection drawn between GANs and variational inference (in the appendix) is interesting and adds theoretical depth.  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and methodology.

However, the experimental validation falls short.  The comparison to existing SR methods (like EDSR, SRGAN, or other recent approaches) is insufficient. While the paper compares against MSE and MAE trained networks *within their proposed framework*, a direct comparison to established benchmarks is crucial. The quantitative results in Table 2, for instance, show the MSE model achieving better PSNR and SSIM, which directly contradicts the paper's motivation against MSE. The results on the grass texture dataset are interesting, but limited in scope.  The claim of GANs providing the best perceptual quality isn't rigorously supported; visual examples are provided, but subjective assessment isn't enough. Furthermore, the instability observed with the denoiser and density guided methods and the eventual focus solely on AffGAN suggest these methods weren’t thoroughly investigated or optimized.  The discussion of instance noise is valuable, but its impact isn’t thoroughly quantified. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment, while illustrative, is a toy problem and doesn't fully translate to the complexities of natural images.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Benchmarking:** The most crucial improvement would be a thorough comparison against state-of-the-art SR methods using standard datasets (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100) and evaluation metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS).
*   **Ablation Studies:**  A more detailed ablation study of the AffGAN architecture (e.g., impact of different network depths, filter sizes, loss weighting) would provide further insights.
*   **Qualitative Evaluation:** Conduct a user study to assess the perceptual quality of the generated images in a more objective manner.
*   **Detailed Analysis of Affine Projection:**  Explore the benefits of the affine projection more rigorously. For example, what happens if the projection is imperfect? Does it always improve performance?
*   **Clarify Limitations:** The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is good, but could be expanded. Discuss potential scenarios where MAP may not be the ideal approach.
*   **Further Investigation of Unstable Methods:** Provide more details regarding why the AffDG and AffLL models diverged, and what attempts were made to stabilize training. A discussion of potential causes would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why were state-of-the-art SR methods (EDSR, SRGAN, etc.) not included in the experimental comparison?
2.  Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, particularly for the AffGAN model?
3.  In Table 2, why does the MSE model achieve better PSNR and SSIM scores than the GAN-based models? How do you reconcile this with the paper’s motivation against MSE?
4.  What specific challenges were encountered when attempting to train the AffDG and AffLL models, and what steps were taken to address them?
5.  Could you elaborate on the connection between the instance noise technique and existing regularization methods (e.g., label smoothing)?
