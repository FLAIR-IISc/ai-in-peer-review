- Addresses MSE-induced blurriness in SR by proposing amortized MAP inference that targets the posterior mode via an affine projection layer ensuring LR consistency.
- Introduces three solution methods: AffGAN (GAN-based), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided), with AffGAN performing best on real images.
- Theoretical result: MAP inference reduces to cross-entropy minimization; strong theoretical grounding and novel link between GANs and amortized variational inference.
- Affine projection layer (Equation 7) is a novel architectural solution enforcing Aŷ = x and reducing the MAP problem.
- Empirical evaluation on a 2D toy problem and real datasets (grass, CelebA, ImageNet) shows AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible images than MSE baselines.
- Ablation studies show affine projection outperforms soft constraint alternatives.
- Correct diagnosis that MSE minimizes the posterior mean and thus yields blurry averaged outputs, convincingly illustrated by a toy example.
- Overall decision: Accept — significant, well-motivated contribution with solid empirical validation.
- Weakness: theoretical justification for why the posterior mode corresponds to perceptual quality could be strengthened.
- Weakness: lacks direct empirical comparison to recent perceptual-loss SR methods (e.g., Ledig et al., Li & Wand).
- Weakness: missing implementation details for instance-noise annealing and noise-level choices critical for GAN training.
- Question: request concrete examples of how the affine projection layer mitigates coordinate-transformation limitations of MAP inference.
- Question: ask for elaboration on the sharpness vs. high-frequency noise trade-off and its handling relative to human perception.
- Question: request more details on optimization difficulties with AffDG and AffLL and whether architectural changes could rescue them.
- Question: ask for expansion on the AffGAN–variational inference connection and implications for stochastic SR methods (diversity vs. quality).
