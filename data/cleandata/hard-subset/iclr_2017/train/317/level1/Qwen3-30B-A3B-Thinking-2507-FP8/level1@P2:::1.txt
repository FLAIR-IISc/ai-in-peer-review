

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the image super-resolution (SR) literature by addressing a fundamental limitation of current methods: the tendency of MSE-based approaches to produce blurry, over-smoothed outputs. The authors propose a novel framework for amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference in SR, which directly targets the mode of the posterior distribution rather than the mean. Their key innovation is a neural network architecture with an affine projection layer that ensures outputs are always consistent with the LR input, reducing the MAP inference problem to minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The paper introduces three methods to solve this optimization problem: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided), with AffGAN demonstrating the best performance on real image data. The authors provide strong theoretical grounding for their approach and establish an interesting connection between GANs and amortized variational inference.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a significant contribution to the SR literature by addressing a fundamental theoretical limitation of current methods. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper identifies and correctly diagnoses a critical problem in current SR methods (MSE leads to blurry outputs due to averaging), and (2) the proposed solution is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated across multiple datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation of current SR approaches. The authors correctly identify that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they minimize the expected loss, which corresponds to the mean of the posterior distribution, rather than the mode. Their toy example in Figure 1a effectively illustrates this problem and clearly motivates why MAP inference would be preferable.

The theoretical contribution is strong and well-executed. The derivation showing that MAP inference reduces to cross-entropy minimization (Equation 9) is clear and correct. The introduction of the affine projection layer (Equation 7) is a novel and elegant solution to the consistency constraint (Aŷ = x), which is a fundamental requirement in SR. This architectural innovation is well-justified and properly explained.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors conduct experiments on both a 2D toy problem (where exact MAP inference is tractable) and multiple real image datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet). The results consistently show that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible images than MSE-based baselines, with quantitative results (Table 1, Figures 3-5) supporting this claim. The ablation study comparing affine projections to soft constraints (Table 1, Figure 2) provides convincing evidence that the proposed architectural innovation is beneficial.

The paper also makes an important theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference (Appendix F), which is a novel insight that connects two major areas in deep learning.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1. **Clarity in the connection between MAP inference and perceptual quality**: The paper argues that MAP inference produces more plausible outputs, but the theoretical justification for why the mode corresponds to "plausible" images could be strengthened. The discussion in Section 5.6 acknowledges this limitation but could be expanded to provide more concrete examples of why the mode might be perceptually superior.

2. **Comparison to recent perceptual loss approaches**: The paper discusses perceptual loss functions in the introduction but doesn't directly compare their approach to recent work that uses perceptual losses (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016; Li & Wand, 2016). A brief comparison would strengthen the positioning of the work.

3. **Implementation details**: The paper mentions using instance noise for GAN training but could provide more details about the annealing schedule and noise level selection, as these are crucial for practical implementation.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Section 5.6, you discuss the limitations of MAP inference regarding coordinate transformations. Could you provide more concrete examples of how the affine projection layer helps mitigate this issue in practice?

2. The paper states that AffGAN produces slightly sharper images but with more high-frequency noise (Figure 4). Could you elaborate on how this trade-off between sharpness and noise is addressed in the context of human perception? Are there ways to further optimize this balance?

3. The paper mentions that the AffDG and AffLL models produced blurry results that couldn't be improved with various optimization methods. Could you provide more details on the specific optimization challenges encountered with these approaches, and whether you believe these methods could be salvaged with different architectural choices?

4. In Appendix F, you establish a connection between AffGAN and variational inference. Could you provide more details on how this connection might inform future work on stochastic SR methods, particularly regarding the trade-off between diversity and quality?
