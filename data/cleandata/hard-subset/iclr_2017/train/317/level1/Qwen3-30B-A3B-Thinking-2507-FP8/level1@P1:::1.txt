

# Peer Review: AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper tackles the fundamental problem of image super-resolution (SR), an underdetermined inverse problem where multiple high-resolution (HR) images can explain the same low-resolution (LR) input. The authors identify a critical limitation of current SR methods: the use of empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise MSE loss produces blurry, over-smoothed outputs because it outputs the mean of plausible solutions rather than the mode. The paper proposes a principled alternative using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, which prefers solutions with high probability under the natural image prior. The key innovation is a novel neural network architecture that projects the output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs. This architectural constraint transforms the problem into minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The authors propose three methods to solve this optimization problem: AffGAN (a GAN-based approach), AffDG (denoiser-guided SR), and AffLL (density-guided SR). The empirical results demonstrate that AffGAN produces visually superior SR results compared to MSE-based approaches, with the added benefit of being able to train without paired LR-HR datasets. The paper also establishes a theoretical connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by providing a theoretically sound approach to generating more perceptually plausible results. The key reasons for acceptance are the strong theoretical motivation for MAP inference, the novel affine projection architecture that ensures solution consistency, and the compelling empirical results showing superior visual quality.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in all three key areas of evaluation. First, the problem statement is clear and well-motivated: the authors effectively demonstrate through a 2D toy example why MSE-based approaches produce blurry outputs while MAP inference produces more plausible solutions. Second, the approach is well-placed in the literature, with thorough discussion of related work on GAN-based SR, perceptual metrics, and connections to variational inference. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of current approaches and positions its method as a principled alternative. Third, the paper provides strong theoretical and empirical support for its claims. The theoretical proofs establish how the affine projection enables MAP inference to reduce to cross-entropy minimization, and the empirical results on both toy and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The AffGAN model achieves the lowest cross-entropy values (Table 1) and produces the sharpest, most plausible SR results (Figures 3-5), with the added advantage of being able to train without paired LR-HR datasets. The introduction of "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training is a valuable technical contribution that addresses a known limitation of GANs.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, several improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The paper could provide a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between perceptual quality and traditional metrics like PSNR/SSIM. While the authors acknowledge PSNR is not the best proxy for perceptual quality, the paper could include more detailed analysis of why AffGAN performs better perceptually despite lower PSNR scores.

2. The paper notes that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results but doesn't thoroughly analyze why these approaches underperformed. A deeper exploration of this failure mode would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights for future work.

3. The computational complexity and training time comparisons between the proposed methods and standard SR approaches would be valuable for practitioners. The paper could include a brief discussion of these practical considerations.

4. The theoretical connection to variational inference (Appendix F) is intriguing but could be expanded to discuss potential practical benefits of this connection, such as how it might inform the design of future SR methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Figure 3, AffGAN produces sharp grass texture reconstructions but with some artifacts. Could the authors provide more insight into what these artifacts represent and whether they're inherent to the MAP approach or due to specific implementation details?

2. The paper notes that AffGAN produces slightly more high-frequency noise compared to SoftGAN (Section 5.4). Is this a consequence of the affine projection or inherent to the GAN approach? How might this be mitigated in future work?

3. In Appendix F, the authors establish a connection between AffGAN and variational inference. How does this theoretical connection inform the practical implementation of their method, and what are the potential benefits of using a stochastic version of AffGAN for SR?

4. The paper acknowledges that MAP estimates can be representation-dependent (Section 5.6). How might the authors' approach be adapted to be more robust to coordinate transformations, and what trade-offs would be involved?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by providing a principled approach to generating more perceptually plausible results. The theoretical foundation is solid, the proposed methods are well-motivated, and the empirical results are compelling. I strongly recommend acceptance.
