

# Peer Review: AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, addressing the well-known limitation of current MSE-based methods that produce blurry, over-smoothed outputs. The authors introduce an architectural innovation—a projection layer ensuring high-resolution outputs are always consistent with low-resolution inputs—and three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The paper makes a strong theoretical case for why MAP inference is superior to MSE for SR, demonstrating that it produces outputs with higher probability under natural image priors. The authors also establish a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference, a novel theoretical contribution. Empirical results on both synthetic (Swiss-roll) and real-world datasets (grass textures, faces, natural images) show that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE-based methods.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a compelling theoretical argument and demonstrates significant empirical improvements in perceptual quality over existing methods. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the elegant solution to the consistency problem via affine projection, which is theoretically well-founded and practically effective; and (2) the clear empirical demonstration that MAP inference via AffGAN produces perceptually superior results without sacrificing input-output consistency.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical foundation is strong. The authors correctly identify that MSE-based SR methods produce blurry outputs because they minimize the mean of the posterior distribution, whereas MAP inference finds the mode, which by definition lies in a high-probability region. Their derivation showing that MAP inference reduces to minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior is rigorous and well-motivated.

The affine projection layer (Equation 7) is a particularly clever architectural innovation. By using the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse to implement a projection to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, the authors ensure perfect consistency between low and high-resolution images—a problem that plagues soft-constrained approaches like those in Ledig et al. (2016). The ablation study in Section 5.2 convincingly demonstrates that this projection does not hurt performance and may even improve convergence and final quality.

Empirically, the AffGAN approach shows clear perceptual advantages over MSE and other baselines. The Swiss-roll experiment (Figure 1) provides a clear visualization of how MAP inference avoids low-probability regions. The real-world results (Figures 3-5) demonstrate that AffGAN produces sharper textures and more plausible reconstructions, even if it introduces some high-frequency noise. The quantitative results in Table 1 and Table 2 are consistent with the qualitative findings.

The theoretical connection between AffGAN and variational inference (Appendix F) is particularly valuable, as it provides a new perspective on the relationship between GANs and variational methods—a contribution that would be interesting even if the SR results were weaker.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, these improvements would further enhance it:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the affine projection layer relates to prior work on constrained SR methods (e.g., those using explicit consistency constraints). This would better position the work within the literature.

2. The explanation of why AffDG and AffLL underperform compared to AffGAN (Section 5.3) is somewhat superficial. The authors should provide more insight into the specific limitations of denoising and density models for this task, rather than simply stating that "the density model is not accurate enough."

3. The connection between AffGAN and variational inference (Appendix F) is promising but lacks sufficient technical detail. A more rigorous derivation would strengthen this contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Section 5.2, the authors show that affine projected networks train faster and achieve better MSE/SSIM scores. Could you provide more insight into why this architectural constraint improves performance beyond the initial consistency advantage? Does it regularize the optimization landscape?

2. The paper states that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results that "we were unable to improve upon using various optimization methods." Given the theoretical connection between denoising and log-probability gradient estimation (Equation 12), why does this approach fail to produce good SR results in practice? Is it due to the quality of the denoiser or the optimization process?

3. The authors introduce "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training (Section 3.2.1). How sensitive is AffGAN to the choice of noise level and annealing schedule? What evidence do you have that this is a general technique for stabilizing GAN training beyond SR applications?
