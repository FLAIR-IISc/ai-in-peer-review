

# Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce a convolutional neural network architecture with an affine projection layer that ensures the high-resolution output is always consistent with the low-resolution input. This transforms the MAP inference problem into minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior distribution. The authors propose three methods to solve this optimization problem: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The paper demonstrates theoretically and empirically that these methods produce sharper, more plausible SR results compared to traditional MSE-based approaches.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a clear connection between MAP inference and SR, with strong empirical validation showing superior perceptual quality.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
**Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for their approach. They correctly identify that SR is an underdetermined inverse problem where MSE optimization produces blurry outputs due to averaging solutions rather than finding the mode. The key insight of using an affine projection to ensure consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs is well-motivated and correctly derived. The transformation of the MAP inference problem into cross-entropy minimization is technically sound, with appropriate theoretical justification. The three proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) are well-grounded in existing literature on GANs, denoising, and density estimation.

**Novelty:** The paper makes a novel contribution by introducing the affine projection layer specifically for SR, which enables the use of MAP inference without requiring matched HR-LR pairs. While GANs have been previously applied to SR (Ledig et al., 2016), this paper provides the first theoretical justification for why GANs work well for SR by connecting them to MAP inference. The connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference (Appendix F) is a particularly novel theoretical contribution that establishes a previously unrecognized relationship between GANs and variational inference.

**Empirical Validation:** The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets (2D toy, grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet). The 2D Swiss-roll experiment clearly demonstrates that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the cross-entropy (H[qθ, pY]) as theoretically predicted, while MSE/MAE models produce outputs in low-probability regions. The real-world experiments consistently show that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results compared to MSE, MAE, and other baselines. The ablation study (Section 5.2) demonstrates that the affine projection does not hurt performance and can even improve training speed and quality when using MSE loss.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, consider:
- Providing more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and training time of AffGAN compared to standard SR methods.
- Including quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) for the AffGAN results alongside the qualitative visual comparisons.
- Clarifying the relationship between the instance noise technique and previous GAN stabilization methods, particularly how it differs from label smoothing.
- Expanding the discussion on the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6) with specific examples from the experiments where MAP inference might fail.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In Figure 3, AffGAN produces sharp grass texture results while AffDG and AffLL are blurry. Could you provide more insight into why the denoiser-guided approach (AffDG) and density-guided approach (AffLL) fail to produce similar quality results despite the theoretical equivalence in their optimization objectives?

2. The paper states that AffGAN is "more resilient to coordinate transformations" than other MAP inference approaches (Section 5.6). Could you provide empirical evidence or theoretical justification for this claim?

3. In the ImageNet experiments (Figure 5), the snake is super-resolved into water. While the authors note this is plausible under the image prior, could you clarify how the model determines that water has higher probability than snakes for this specific LR input?
