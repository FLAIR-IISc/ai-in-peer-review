

The paper introduces a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by leveraging amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference instead of standard empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors propose a neural network architecture that projects outputs to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs. They then formulate MAP inference as minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior, and develop three methods to solve this optimization problem: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The paper provides strong theoretical grounding, demonstrates the approach on both a 2D toy problem and multiple real-world image datasets, and establishes a connection between GANs and variational inference.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing the fundamental limitation of current SR methods (producing blurry outputs due to averaging over plausible solutions) and provides a theoretically sound alternative. The 2D experiment convincingly shows that AffGAN and AffDG correctly minimize the MAP objective, while real-world results demonstrate perceptually superior outputs compared to MSE-based baselines.

The theoretical derivation is rigorous, the experiments are well-designed and comprehensive across multiple datasets, and the connection to variational inference is novel and valuable. The paper effectively situates itself within the literature, acknowledging limitations of current approaches and positioning its contribution as a meaningful step forward.

For improvement, the authors should clarify the computational cost of their method compared to standard SR approaches, as this is important for practical adoption. Additionally, a more detailed discussion of why AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results despite theoretical soundness would strengthen the paper.

I have two questions: 1) How does the computational complexity of AffGAN compare to state-of-the-art SR methods like EDSR or RCAN? 2) Could the authors provide more insight into why AffDG and AffLL performed worse than AffGAN in practice, given their theoretical equivalence to the MAP objective?
