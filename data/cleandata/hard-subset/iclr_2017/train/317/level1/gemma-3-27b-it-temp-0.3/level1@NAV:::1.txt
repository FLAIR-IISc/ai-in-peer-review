## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR), arguing that current methods relying on empirical risk minimization (like MSE loss) produce blurry results due to averaging over plausible solutions. The authors propose a novel approach based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, aiming to find the most probable high-resolution image given a low-resolution input. They introduce a CNN architecture with an affine projection to ensure consistency between LR and HR images, and explore three methods for solving the resulting optimization problem: GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood trained image prior.

I **reject** this paper. While the motivation is strong and the idea of framing SR as MAP inference is appealing, the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of significant improvement, and the results are not clearly differentiated from existing GAN-based SR methods.

The paper is well-motivated, correctly identifying the limitations of MSE-based SR and positioning the work within the broader context of Bayesian inference and generative modeling. The affine projection idea is a clever way to enforce consistency and simplify the problem. However, the experimental results, while showing some promise, are not convincing. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment effectively demonstrates the theoretical advantage of the MAP approach, but the results on real images are less clear. The GAN-based approach (AffGAN) performs best, but the visual comparisons in Figures 3-5 are often subtle, and the quantitative results (Table 2) show only marginal improvements over existing methods, with MSE actually performing best in terms of PSNR/SSIM. The ablation studies are limited, and it's difficult to determine the true contribution of the affine projection. The claims of perceptual superiority are not strongly supported by the provided evidence.

Additional feedback:

*   **Clarify the novelty:** The paper often refers to existing GAN-based SR methods (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016). It needs to more clearly articulate *how* AffGAN differs and improves upon these existing approaches beyond the affine projection.
*   **More rigorous evaluation:** Conduct a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art SR methods, including more recent GAN-based approaches. Include more quantitative metrics beyond PSNR/SSIM, such as LPIPS, to better assess perceptual quality.
*   **Ablation studies:** Provide more detailed ablation studies to isolate the impact of the affine projection, instance noise, and different loss functions.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** The paper briefly mentions limitations in the conclusion. Expand on these, particularly regarding the potential issues with MAP inference and the sensitivity to the image prior.
*   **Explain the choice of noise distribution:** Why was Gaussian noise chosen for instance noise? Were other distributions considered?

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of AffGAN compared to other SR methods, particularly those based on GANs?
*   How sensitive is the performance of AffGAN to the choice of the affine projection kernel (A)?
*   The paper mentions instability during training. What specific techniques were used to stabilize training, beyond instance noise?
*   Could you elaborate on the connection between AffGAN and variational inference, as mentioned in Appendix F? What are the practical implications of this connection?
