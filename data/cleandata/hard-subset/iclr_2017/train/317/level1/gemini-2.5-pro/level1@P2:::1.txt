### Summary of Contributions

This paper reframes the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR) from the standard empirical risk minimization framework to one of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors persuasively argue that traditional pixel-wise losses like MSE lead to perceptually poor, blurry outputs because they average over the multi-modal distribution of plausible high-resolution (HR) solutions. In contrast, MAP inference seeks the most probable HR image given a low-resolution (LR) input, which is more likely to be sharp and realistic.

The paper's primary contributions are threefold. First, it introduces a novel neural network architecture featuring an "affine projection layer." This layer elegantly enforces the physical constraint that the super-resolved output, when downsampled, must exactly match the LR input. This transforms the constrained MAP optimization problem into an unconstrained one of minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the network's outputs and the distribution of true HR images. Second, the paper proposes and investigates three distinct methods to solve this cross-entropy minimization problem: a Generative Adversarial Network (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided baseline (AffLL). Third, through a series of well-designed experiments on toy data and real images, the paper demonstrates that the GAN-based approach is most effective, producing visually compelling and sharp results that are superior to those from traditional MSE-based methods. The work also provides a novel theoretical link between a stochastic version of their GAN approach and amortised variational inference.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes significant conceptual and technical contributions to the field of image super-resolution. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **A Novel and Principled Framework:** The paper successfully recasts SR as an amortised MAP inference problem. This is a crucial conceptual shift away from the limitations of pixel-wise losses, providing a solid theoretical foundation for using generative models to improve perceptual quality. The formulation is clear, logical, and insightful.
2.  **The Affine Projection Layer:** This is an elegant and powerful architectural innovation. By directly encoding the data consistency constraint into the model, it offers a more principled solution than the commonly used soft penalties. The experiments convincingly show that this hard constraint is effective, does not hinder performance on standard metrics, and leads to superior results compared to soft-constrained alternatives.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper is well-motivated and clearly positions itself within the literature. The introduction uses a simple 2D Swiss-roll example to perfectly illustrate the core weakness of MSE/MAE losses and the conceptual strength of MAP inference. This framing is a key strength.

The theoretical development in Section 3 is sound. The derivation that transforms the MAP objective into a cross-entropy minimization problem by using the affine projection layer is clever and powerful. It connects the supervised problem of SR to the unsupervised task of generative modeling, opening the door to powerful density-matching techniques like GANs.

The empirical validation is thorough and supports the paper's claims.
*   The 2D toy experiment (Section 5.1) serves as an excellent proof of concept, demonstrating that the AffGAN and AffDG methods do indeed approximate the true MAP solution and minimize cross-entropy more effectively than MSE/MAE baselines. It also provides clear evidence for the superiority of the hard affine projection over soft constraints.
*   The experiment in Section 5.2 is a crucial sanity check, showing that the affine projection layer does not harm, and may even improve, the performance of a standard MSE-trained network. This validates the proposed architecture as a general tool for SR.
*   The qualitative results on grass textures, faces, and natural images (Sections 5.3-5.5) are compelling. The images produced by AffGAN are visibly sharper and more perceptually realistic than the blurry outputs from MSE-trained models. The paper is also commendably transparent about the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods on complex image data, which, while disappointing, adds to the scientific rigor of the investigation.

Finally, the introduction of "instance noise" as a technique for stabilizing GANs and the theoretical connection to variational inference in the appendix are valuable additional contributions that broaden the paper's impact.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to my decision to accept the paper.

*   **Analysis of DG/LL Failure:** The paper states that the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods produced blurry results and were not pursued further on real images. The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion in the main text about the potential reasons for this failure. Is it due to the difficulty of training sufficiently accurate denoisers/density models in high dimensions, or are there fundamental optimization challenges in backpropagating gradients from such models? Deeper insight here would be very valuable to the community.
*   **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:** While the paper correctly criticizes PSNR and SSIM, the evaluation of the main AffGAN model relies solely on visual inspection. The claims of improved perceptual quality could be quantitatively supported by including no-reference image quality scores (e.g., NIQE, BRISQUE) or, ideally, a small-scale human perceptual study.
*   **Instance Noise Ablation:** "Instance noise" is presented as a helpful trick for stabilizing GAN training. Its contribution would be more impactful if supported by a small ablation study. For example, showing a comparison of training stability or final image quality with and without instance noise on the CelebA dataset would make a more convincing case for its general utility.
*   **Comparison to Concurrent Work:** The paper cites Ledig et al. (SRGAN), which explored a similar adversarial approach. A brief discussion clarifying the key differences—such as this paper's principled MAP formulation and affine projection versus SRGAN's use of a heuristic perceptual loss (VGG loss)—would help to better situate the unique contributions of this work.

### Questions for the Authors

*   The affine projection layer relies on computing the pseudo-inverse `A+` of the downsampling operator `A`. How sensitive is the model's performance to the precision of this numerically-estimated `A+`? Furthermore, how would your method adapt to scenarios common in practice where the exact downsampling kernel `A` is unknown?
*   The connection to amortised variational inference established in Appendix F is fascinating. It suggests that a stochastic version of AffGAN could be used to sample from the posterior distribution `p(y|x)`, potentially generating diverse yet plausible HR outputs for a single LR input. Have you conducted any preliminary experiments with this approach? How do the sampled results compare in quality and diversity to the single MAP estimate?
