### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single image super-resolution (SR) by formulating the task as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue compellingly that standard Mean Squared Error (MSE) minimisation leads to perceptually poor, blurry results by averaging over a multimodal posterior of plausible high-resolution (HR) images. In contrast, a MAP estimate seeks a mode of this posterior, which corresponds to a plausible HR image that is consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input.

The core technical contribution is a novel neural network architecture incorporating an "affine projection" layer. This layer elegantly and exactly enforces the physical constraint that the downsampled output must match the LR input (`Ay=x`). This is achieved by projecting the output of an arbitrary convolutional network onto the affine subspace of valid solutions. This architectural innovation transforms the MAP inference problem into an unconstrained task of minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real HR images, effectively turning supervised SR into a generative modelling problem.

To solve this cross-entropy minimisation, the paper proposes and evaluates three distinct methods:
1.  **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network where the generator incorporates the affine projection.
2.  **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided method that leverages a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the score (gradient of the log-prior) and backpropagates this gradient to train the SR network.
3.  **AffLL:** A method guided by an explicit, pre-trained log-likelihood model (a PixelCNN variant) to provide gradients.

The paper demonstrates through experiments on a toy dataset and real images that the AffGAN approach is highly effective, producing sharp and perceptually convincing results that significantly outperform MSE-based methods. The work also contributes a practical technique called "instance noise" to stabilise GAN training and provides a valuable theoretical connection between a stochastic version of their framework and amortised variational inference.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper provides a strong theoretical reframing of perceptual super-resolution as amortised MAP inference. The affine projection layer is a significant and elegant architectural contribution that enforces a hard physical constraint, moving beyond the ad-hoc soft penalties common in related work. This provides a solid foundation for producing realistic images.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation of the Core Idea:** The proposed AffGAN method produces compelling qualitative results that are sharp, detailed, and perceptually superior to standard baselines. This successfully validates the central hypothesis that optimising for a mode of the posterior via a generative loss leads to better perceptual quality, and provides a clear theoretical justification for the success of GAN-based SR.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

**Strengths:**
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and motivated. The use of the 2D Swiss-roll example is a highly effective way to build intuition for why MAP inference is preferable to MSE and to demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed methods in a controlled setting.
*   **The Affine Projection Layer:** This is the standout technical contribution. It is a general-purpose architectural component that perfectly enforces data consistency. The experiment in Section 5.2, showing that this layer can even improve convergence and final performance for standard MSE training, is a strong piece of evidence for its utility beyond the MAP framework. It is a more principled approach than the soft L1/L2 consistency losses used in concurrent works like SRGAN.
*   **Comprehensive Approach:** The exploration of three different methods (GAN, denoiser-guided, density-guided) to approximate the MAP objective is thorough. While only one method proved successful on complex image data, the systematic investigation is valuable.
*   **Insightful Discussion:** The "Criticism and Future Directions" section (5.6) is excellent. It demonstrates a deep understanding of the potential limitations of MAP inference (e.g., dependence on representation, atypicality of the mode) and thoughtfully points towards posterior sampling as a superior goal. The connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F solidifies this direction and adds significant theoretical depth.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Failure of Denoiser and Density-Guided Methods:** A significant weakness is that two of the three proposed methods, AffDG and AffLL, fail to produce good results on real image data, yielding blurry outputs despite the theoretical motivation. The paper is transparent about this failure, but the investigation into the cause is brief. This outcome somewhat limits the paper's claim of having developed multiple "methods" for amortised MAP inference, as only the GAN-based approach appears viable in practice.
*   **Comparison to Concurrent Work:** The paper is contemporaneous with SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016), another highly influential work that uses GANs for perceptual SR. While this paper's affine projection is a key differentiator from SRGAN's soft penalty, a more direct discussion or comparison of the two approaches would have been beneficial to better situate the contributions.

Despite these weaknesses, the strength and novelty of the core framework and the success of the AffGAN method are more than sufficient for acceptance.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The failure of AffDG and AffLL is an interesting negative result. The discussion in Appendix E could be expanded. It would be valuable for the community to understand more deeply why these theoretically-grounded approaches fail in high dimensions. Is the problem that state-of-the-art denoisers and density models are still not accurate enough to provide useful gradients, or is there a more fundamental optimisation challenge related to the sharpness of the log-probability landscape?
*   The instance noise technique is a nice practical contribution. It would be helpful to further elaborate on its relationship to other GAN stabilisation techniques, such as noise added to discriminator inputs or one-sided label smoothing, beyond the good discussion already present in Appendix C.
*   The connection to amortised variational inference is a key insight relegated to the appendix. Given its importance for motivating future work on posterior sampling (as mentioned in Section 5.6), it might be worth briefly summarising this finding in the main body of the paper to highlight it more prominently.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   Regarding the failure of AffDG and AffLL: You suggest the density model might not be accurate enough. However, the reported bits-per-dimension for the texture model seems quite strong. Could the issue be less about the model's overall accuracy and more about the local properties of the learned log-likelihood surface (e.g., excessive "peakiness" or noisy gradients) that make it unsuitable for guiding a generator?
*   The affine projection relies on the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse `A⁺`, which you compute numerically. How sensitive are the results to the accuracy of this `A⁺` approximation? Many deconvolutional networks effectively learn an `A⁺` from data (or simply use a transposed convolution). Did you compare your fixed, numerically-optimised `A⁺` against a learned one within the `(I - A⁺A)` projection framework, as you did for the MSE case in Figure 2?
*   Did you observe any benefits of the hard constraint from the affine projection on the stability of GAN training itself, when comparing AffGAN to the SoftGAN baseline? It seems plausible that enforcing this constraint would simplify the generator's task and could lead to a more stable training dynamic.
