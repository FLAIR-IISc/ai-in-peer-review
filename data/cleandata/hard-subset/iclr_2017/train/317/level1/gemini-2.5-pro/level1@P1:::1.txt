### **Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**
This paper addresses the common problem of blurry and overly smooth outputs from single image super-resolution (SR) models trained with pixel-wise MSE loss. The authors argue for a more principled approach based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, which aims to find a high-resolution (HR) output that is not only consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input but also has a high probability under the prior distribution of natural images, thus appearing more plausible.

The paper's key contributions are:
1.  A novel and elegant neural network architecture featuring an **affine projection layer**. This layer analytically enforces the consistency constraint that the downsampled version of the network's output must exactly match the LR input. This is achieved by projecting the output of a standard convolutional network onto the affine subspace of valid solutions.
2.  A reformulation of the amortised MAP objective. By using the affine projection layer, the problem is transformed from a constrained optimization into an unconstrained one of minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the distribution of real HR images. This insightfully recasts supervised SR as an unsupervised generative modeling task.
3.  The exploration of three different methods to solve this generative modeling problem:
    *   **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network where the SR network acts as the generator. A modification to the GAN objective is used to minimize the KL-divergence, and a technique called "instance noise" is introduced to stabilize training.
    *   **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided method that uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-prior of natural images, which is then backpropagated to train the SR network.
    *   **AffLL:** A baseline method that uses an explicit, pre-trained density model (a PixelCNN variant) to guide the training of the SR network.

Through experiments on a toy problem and real image datasets (textures, faces, and natural images), the authors demonstrate that their proposed methods successfully approximate the MAP objective and that the AffGAN approach, in particular, produces perceptually superior results that are significantly sharper and more detailed than those from MSE-trained models.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The introduction of the **affine projection layer** is a significant and broadly applicable contribution. It provides a principled and analytical way to enforce hard data-consistency constraints in inverse problems, moving beyond the common practice of using soft penalty terms. This architectural innovation alone is a strong reason for acceptance.
2.  The paper provides a **strong theoretical framework** for improving the perceptual quality of SR. By framing the problem as amortised MAP inference and connecting it to cross-entropy minimization and generative modeling, the work provides clear justification for using GAN-based approaches and moves the field towards more probabilistically grounded methods for perceptual enhancement.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, methodologically sound, and empirically supported.

*   **Motivation and Placement in Literature:** The paper clearly articulates the shortcomings of MSE loss for perceptual tasks like SR. The choice to pursue a MAP inference framework is well-justified and provides a robust alternative to empirical risk minimization. The work is well-situated with respect to existing literature on GANs, perceptual losses, and the connection between denoising and score estimation.

*   **Methodology:** The derivation of the cross-entropy objective from the MAP formulation is clear, and the affine projection layer is the key insight that makes this possible. The three proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) represent a thorough exploration of different ways to optimize this objective. The introduction of "instance noise" as a GAN stabilization technique is a valuable practical contribution, and its justification in the appendix is insightful.

*   **Empirical Support:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed and convincingly support the paper's claims.
    *   The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides an excellent, low-dimensional visualization that confirms AffGAN and AffDG are indeed approximating the desired MAP solution (the mode), in contrast to MSE/MAE which find the mean/median in low-density regions.
    *   The "proof of concept" experiment, where an affine projected network is trained with MSE, is a crucial sanity check. It demonstrates that the proposed architecture does not hinder performance on standard metrics and can even be beneficial, validating it as a general-purpose tool.
    *   The qualitative results on grass textures, CelebA, and ImageNet are compelling. The AffGAN model produces images that are visually much sharper and more plausible than the blurry MSE results. The honest reporting of the poorer performance of the AffDG and AffLL models is also a strength, providing valuable insights for the community.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to my decision.

*   The failure of the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods in the image experiments is an interesting result. The main text could benefit from a more detailed discussion of why these methods might have failed. Was the failure due to the difficulty of accurately estimating the score function or density in such high-dimensional spaces, leading to noisy or biased gradients? A deeper analysis would strengthen the paper.
*   The connection to amortised variational inference presented in Appendix F is highly significant, as it formally links GANs and VAEs within this framework. This is a powerful theoretical result that could be highlighted more prominently, perhaps with a brief mention in the conclusion, to underscore the work's theoretical depth.
*   The "instance noise" trick is very promising. While the appendix provides a good theoretical motivation, its practical impact could be made clearer with a small ablation study showing, for instance, a comparison of training stability or final performance with and without it.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding and strengthen the final paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  There appears to be a typo in Section 3.2, where two different expressions are shown for the generator loss `L(G;D)`. Could you please clarify the exact objective function used for the generator that corresponds to the KL-divergence minimization proven in Appendix A?
2.  Could you elaborate further on the practical challenges with the AffDG and AffLL methods on real images? Do you suspect the issues are fundamental to applying score-matching and explicit density modeling in this context, or could they potentially be overcome with, for example, more powerful denoisers/density models or different optimization schedules?
3.  The numerical estimation of the pseudo-inverse `A+` is a practical necessity. How sensitive was this optimization process? And did you verify how closely the `Ax = A(g_theta(x))` constraint was met in practice with the learned `A+`?
4.  The "snake-to-water" example in Figure 5 is a fascinating demonstration of plausible "hallucination." Does this suggest that the model is biased towards more common textures/objects in the training set (like water) when the LR input is ambiguous? Have you observed other examples of this phenomenon?
