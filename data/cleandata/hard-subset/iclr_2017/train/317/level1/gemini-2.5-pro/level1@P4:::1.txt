### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of single image super-resolution (SR), focusing on generating perceptually plausible high-resolution (HR) images rather than optimizing for pixel-wise metrics like MSE. The authors argue that standard MSE-based methods produce overly smooth and blurry results because they effectively average over the many possible HR images that could correspond to a single low-resolution (LR) input.

The core contribution is to frame the SR problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The goal is to train a network that directly outputs the mode of the posterior distribution `p(y|x)`, which corresponds to an HR image `y` that is both consistent with the LR input `x` and has a high probability under the natural image prior `p(y)`.

The authors make several key technical contributions to achieve this:
1.  They derive that the amortised MAP objective is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the true data distribution.
2.  They introduce a novel neural network architecture featuring an "affine projection" layer. This layer analytically enforces the consistency constraint that the downsampled output must exactly match the LR input, elegantly converting a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one.
3.  They propose and investigate three distinct methods for minimizing the cross-entropy objective:
    a. **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) approach where the generator is the SR network with an affine projection layer.
    b. **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided method that uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-prior and backpropagates this signal to train the SR network.
    c. **AffLL:** A baseline method guided by the log-likelihood of a pre-trained, explicit generative density model (a variant of PixelCNN).
4.  Through experiments on a toy 2D dataset, grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet), the paper demonstrates that the AffGAN approach is most effective, producing sharp and visually convincing results that are perceptually superior to those from MSE-trained models.
5.  The paper also provides a theoretical connection between a stochastic version of their AffGAN model and amortised variational inference, linking GANs to variational autoencoders.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Principled Framework and Novelty:** The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound framework for super-resolution based on amortised MAP inference. This provides a strong theoretical justification for using generative models for this task and moves the field beyond simple empirical risk minimization with flawed perceptual metrics like MSE.
2.  **Technical Soundness and Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed affine projection layer is an elegant and technically robust solution for enforcing data consistency. The primary proposed method, AffGAN, is shown to be highly effective, producing state-of-the-art perceptual results on several challenging datasets, which strongly supports the paper's central claims.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's motivation is exceptionally clear. It correctly identifies the fundamental limitation of MSE loss in SR tasks—its tendency to average multimodal posteriors, leading to blurry outputs. The shift to a MAP inference framework is a well-justified and powerful alternative. The formulation of this goal as minimizing the cross-entropy `H[q_θ, p_Y]` is a key insight that connects SR to unsupervised generative modeling.

The primary technical innovation, the affine projection layer, is a significant contribution. Previous works often used a soft penalty (e.g., an L1 or L2 loss on the downsampled output) to encourage consistency. By contrast, this paper's method enforces the consistency `A * f(x) = x` exactly and by construction. This is a more principled approach that simplifies the learning objective by removing the need to balance a reconstruction term against a prior/adversarial term. The experiments in Section 5.2 convincingly show that this layer does not harm, and may even help, performance even when training with a standard MSE loss.

The exploration of three different methods to optimize the cross-entropy objective is thorough. While the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods are ultimately shown to be ineffective for producing sharp images, the inclusion and honest reporting of these negative results are a mark of scientific rigor. The success of the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) is compelling. The visual results presented in Figures 3, 4, and 5 are qualitatively excellent, showing a clear improvement in sharpness and realism over traditional methods.

Finally, the theoretical contributions, including the alternative GAN update rule that minimizes KL-divergence and the connection to amortised variational inference in the appendix, add significant depth and value to the paper.

### 4. Additional Feedback

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   The empirical failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods is an interesting result in itself. The paper could be strengthened by a deeper investigation into the reasons for this failure. For instance, is the gradient signal from the denoiser simply too noisy or biased? For the density model, is the issue that the model's log-likelihood surface is too sharp or complex for effective gradient-based optimization of the generator, even if the model itself is accurate? A more thorough analysis could provide valuable lessons for the community.
*   The "instance noise" technique for stabilizing GANs is a useful practical contribution. An ablation study that empirically demonstrates its effect on training stability and final model performance compared to a baseline without it, or to other techniques like one-sided label smoothing, would make its value more concrete.
*   The connection to amortised variational inference presented in Appendix F is very insightful. Given its importance in linking two major families of generative models, this point could be given more prominence, perhaps by summarizing the result in the main body of the paper (e.g., in the conclusion or future work section).

### 5. Questions for Authors
*   In the denoiser-guided (AffDG) experiments, you use a pre-trained denoiser and anneal the noise level during the training of the SR network. Did you experiment with jointly training or fine-tuning the denoiser alongside the SR network? Could this potentially yield a more suitable gradient signal?
*   The paper argues that the AffLL method fails because the density model is not "accurate enough." However, the reported bits-per-dimension for the model seem strong. Could the failure instead be related to the optimization dynamics? For example, a very accurate density model might create an extremely non-convex loss surface for the generator, making optimization difficult. Have you explored the properties of the gradient field provided by your density model?
*   The results on ImageNet show that the model can sometimes "hallucinate" a plausible but incorrect texture (e.g., water instead of a snake). This is a fascinating outcome of MAP inference. Do you have thoughts on how one might control or balance this trade-off between plausibility under the prior and faithfulness to subtle features in the LR input that are not captured by the downsampling operator `A`?
