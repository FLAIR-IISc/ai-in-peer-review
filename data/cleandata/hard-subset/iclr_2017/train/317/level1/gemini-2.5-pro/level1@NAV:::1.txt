This paper presents a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors compellingly argue that traditional MSE-based methods produce blurry results by averaging over plausible solutions, whereas a MAP approach seeks the most probable high-resolution image, leading to more perceptually realistic outputs. The core contributions are threefold: 1) a novel affine projection layer that architecturally enforces consistency between the low-resolution input and the downsampled high-resolution output; 2) three distinct methods for approximating the MAP objective, based on Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), denoiser guidance (AffDG), and explicit density models; and 3) strong empirical evidence demonstrating that the AffGAN approach produces sharp, high-quality results on various image datasets. The paper also provides a valuable theoretical connection between GANs and amortised variational inference.

**Decision: Accept**

My decision is based on two key strengths. First, the introduction of the affine projection layer is an elegant and principled architectural innovation that directly addresses a core constraint of the SR problem, moving beyond the less effective soft penalties used in prior work. Second, the paper provides a robust theoretical framing and backs it up with convincing empirical results, particularly showing that the GAN-based approach successfully generates perceptually superior images compared to baseline methods.

The supporting arguments are well-founded. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a clear and intuitive validation that the proposed methods indeed optimize for the MAP objective, unlike MSE-based approaches. The ablation study in Section 5.2 effectively demonstrates that the affine projection layer improves, rather than hinders, performance even within a standard MSE framework. The qualitative results on challenging textures and faces clearly show the advantages of the AffGAN model in producing sharp and detailed reconstructions, which is the primary goal stated in the introduction. The paper’s honest assessment of the shortcomings of the denoiser- and density-guided methods on complex image data further strengthens its conclusions.

For improvement, I would suggest the following:
*   The paper quickly dismisses the denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches after one experiment. A brief discussion on *why* these methods might have failed (e.g., inaccurate gradient estimates in high dimensions, optimization challenges) would add valuable insight.
*   The "instance noise" technique for stabilizing GANs is interesting. Could you comment on its sensitivity to the annealing schedule and how it compares to other stabilization methods?

I would also appreciate clarification on these points:
*   The results show AffGAN can produce some high-frequency artifacts. How do you view the trade-off between achieving sharpness and introducing these artifacts, and is this controllable?
*   Appendix F draws an exciting connection to amortised variational inference. Could you speculate on the practical benefits of sampling from the posterior (via a stochastic generator) versus finding the mode for the SR task?
