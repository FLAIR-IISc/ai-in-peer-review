This paper presents a new framework for solving SR problems – an amortized MAP estimate and an a priori learned affinity projection layer to ensure that the output corresponds to the LR. It also proposes three different methods to solve the problem of reducing cross-entropy. Overall, it's an excellent paper. However, I still have some comments:

1) The proposed amortized MAP estimate is new and different from previous SR methods. Combined with GAN, this framework can achieve commendable and good results. Compared to photo-realistic single image super-resolution using another GAN-based SR method – the Generative Adversarial Network – the question may arise as to what this new formulation adds to the latest technology.
2) Using an affinity projection architecture as an obstacle, the model does not require any corresponding {HR, LR} image pairs for training. However, when training the affinity projection layer, we still need {HR, LR} image pairs. Does this mean we simply transfer this training process to affinity projection training?
3) The paper presents several results of the framework, including results from ImageNet to natural images. Could the authors also provide results from Set5, Set14, or BSD100, which are traditional test datasets for SR, so we can make a fair comparison with previous work?
4) I notice that the size of the nature image results presented in this paper is limited to 128*128. Can this framework perform well on larger image sizes? Because SR will have to face arbitrary-sized inputs.
5) In a typical GAN, a noise term will be a hidden space, so that it can be better represented as a distribution learning. Do the authors attempt a noise vector?

Overall, this paper provides a new framework for SR with solid theoretical analysis. The idea is new, and the authors explore several methods. However, questions like the need for more experimentation still exist. I think this work will provide good inspiration to the community.
