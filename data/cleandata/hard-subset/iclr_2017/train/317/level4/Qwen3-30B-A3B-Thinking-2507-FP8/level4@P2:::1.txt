

**Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"**

**Summary of Contributions**  
This paper presents a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) that shifts from traditional empirical risk minimization (ERM) approaches to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce a convolutional neural network architecture with an affine projection layer ensuring that all outputs are consistent with the low-resolution input. This enables the SR problem to be rephrased as minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior, effectively transforming training into a generative modeling task. The paper proposes three distinct methods to solve this optimization problem: an Affine Projected GAN (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The AffGAN method demonstrates superior perceptual quality on real-world images compared to conventional MSE-based approaches, while the theoretical grounding connecting GANs to amortized variational inference represents a significant contribution.

**Decision**  
I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions. The paper makes a compelling theoretical contribution to SR by establishing a principled connection between MAP inference and generative modeling, with strong empirical validation of the proposed methods.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper successfully addresses three key questions:  
1. **Problem Tackled**: The authors clearly identify the limitation of MSE-based SR methods (producing blurry outputs due to averaging over multimodal solution spaces) and propose MAP inference as a more appropriate approach.  
2. **Literature Motivation**: The paper is well-placed in the literature, building on recent advances in GANs (Ledig et al., 2016) while providing theoretical justification for why GANs are suitable for SR. The connection to variational inference (Appendix F) further strengthens the theoretical foundation.  
3. **Claim Support**: The claims are well-supported through both theoretical analysis (Section 3) and comprehensive empirical evaluation (Section 5). The cross-entropy metrics in Table 1 and visual comparisons in Figures 1-5 consistently demonstrate that AffGAN outperforms MSE, MAE, and other baselines in producing perceptually plausible results.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
1. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison with the state-of-the-art GAN-based SR method (Ledig et al., 2016). While AffGAN achieves better perceptual quality, it would be valuable to quantify how much the theoretical framework improves upon existing GAN approaches.

2. The training of the affine projection layer requires HR-LR pairs, which contradicts the claim that the method "does not need corresponding HR-LR image pairs." The authors should clarify whether the affine projection is trained once on a small set of data, or whether it requires HR-LR pairs for every new dataset.

3. The experiments should include standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) to enable direct comparison with prior work. While ImageNet results are valuable, conventional SR datasets provide more meaningful benchmarking for the community.

4. The paper should address whether the framework scales to larger image resolutions. The current results (128×128) are limited, but SR applications typically require handling arbitrary input sizes.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper claims training avoids HR-LR pairs after the affine projection is learned. However, the affine projection layer itself requires HR-LR pairs for training. Please clarify whether this is a one-time requirement or if it remains necessary for each new dataset.

2. The AffGAN architecture does not incorporate a noise vector in the latent space (unlike standard GANs). How does this affect the diversity of generated super-resolved images compared to standard GAN approaches?

3. The paper demonstrates excellent perceptual results but lacks quantitative metrics beyond PSNR/SSIM. Could the authors provide a human evaluation study comparing the visual quality of AffGAN outputs to other methods?

4. In Section 5.6, the authors discuss limitations of MAP inference. How does the AffGAN framework mitigate these limitations compared to conventional MAP approaches?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the SR literature by providing a theoretically grounded framework that produces perceptually superior results. The proposed AffGAN method offers a promising direction for future research, and the connection to variational inference (Appendix F) opens new avenues for exploration. With minor clarifications regarding the training requirements and standard benchmarking, this work would be an excellent addition to ICLR 2017.
