

This paper introduces an amortized maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation approach for single-image super-resolution (SR), leveraging a neural network to project observations onto an affine subspace of SR solutions consistent with the low-resolution (LR) observation model. The method enables efficient solution finding through integration with GAN-based, noise-assisted, and density-assisted optimization techniques. Results demonstrate effectiveness across multiple benchmark datasets, showing competitive performance.

While the core idea is promising and the experimental validation is strong, the manuscript requires significant refinement to enhance clarity and accessibility. The technical exposition is occasionally convoluted, particularly in describing the affine subspace projection mechanism and the integration of auxiliary optimization strategies. For instance, the relationship between the learned network, the affine subspace, and the specific optimization methods (GANs, noise assistance, density assistance) is not sufficiently elucidated. This ambiguity impedes understanding of how the approach differs from or improves upon existing amortized inference frameworks for SR.

The presentation would benefit from a more structured narrative. Key contributions—such as the theoretical justification for the affine subspace assumption, the network architecture design, and the interplay between the learned projector and the optimization modules—should be articulated more precisely. Figures and algorithm descriptions should explicitly clarify the flow of information and the role of each component. Currently, the reader must infer relationships between sections, which is challenging given the complexity of the method.

Furthermore, a deeper analysis of the learned network features would substantially strengthen the work. The authors should investigate: (1) What types of spatial or frequency-domain features the projector network encodes; (2) How these features correlate with reconstruction quality or robustness to noise; (3) Whether the network learns task-specific priors distinct from standard SR priors (e.g., those in EDSR or SRGAN). Such analysis would provide critical insight into *why* the method works and its generalizability beyond the reported results.

The writing also contains minor grammatical inconsistencies and redundant phrasing (e.g., "finds proper solutions with by using" should be "finds proper solutions using"). These should be corrected to ensure professionalism. The abstract and introduction could better contextualize the work within the broader landscape of amortized inference for inverse problems, highlighting specific gaps this method addresses.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and technically sound approach with compelling empirical results. With clearer exposition, better-defined contributions, and deeper analysis of the learned model, this work has strong potential for impact. The current version, however, risks obscuring its contributions due to presentation issues. Addressing these points would elevate the paper significantly for publication.
