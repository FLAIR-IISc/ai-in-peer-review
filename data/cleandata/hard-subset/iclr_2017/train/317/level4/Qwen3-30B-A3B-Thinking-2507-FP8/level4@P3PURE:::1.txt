

The paper introduces a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference, incorporating a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency between low-resolution (LR) and high-resolution (HR) outputs. It also proposes three distinct approaches to minimize cross-entropy loss. Overall, the work presents a compelling theoretical foundation and innovative methodology. However, the following points require clarification:  

1) While the amortized MAP inference framework is novel compared to prior SR methods and yields plausible results when combined with GANs, its specific contribution relative to the state-of-the-art—particularly in comparison to *Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network*—remains unclear. The paper should explicitly articulate how this formulation advances beyond existing GAN-based SR approaches.  

2) The affine projection layer is presented as a constraint that eliminates the need for paired {HR, LR} training data. However, training this layer itself requires {HR, LR} pairs. This implies that the training procedure for the affine projection layer is transferred to the overall model. The authors should clarify whether this constitutes a fundamental shift in training requirements or merely a reparameterization of existing supervised learning.  

3) The framework’s results on natural images from ImageNet are presented, but conventional SR benchmark datasets (Set5, Set14, and BSD100) are not included. Providing results on these standard datasets is essential for fair comparison with prior work.  

4) The resolution of test images in the results is limited to 128×128. Since SR applications must handle arbitrary input sizes, the authors should demonstrate the framework’s performance on larger images to validate its scalability.  

5) Standard GANs incorporate latent noise to model output distributions. The authors should address whether they explored adding a noise vector to the latent space and its impact on the model’s performance.  

Despite these concerns, the paper offers a novel SR framework with strong theoretical grounding. The methodology is well-motivated, and the exploration of multiple solution approaches for cross-entropy minimization demonstrates thoroughness. Addressing the above points—particularly regarding novelty justification, training dependencies, benchmark comparisons, scalability, and GAN latent space design—would significantly strengthen the contribution. The work provides valuable inspiration for the community and merits consideration for publication upon revision.
