

This paper introduces a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference, incorporating a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency between low-resolution (LR) and high-resolution (HR) outputs. The authors also propose three distinct approaches to minimize cross-entropy loss. Overall, the work presents a promising theoretical foundation with solid methodological contributions. However, several clarifications and additional experiments are required to fully establish its significance and reproducibility.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Novelty and Comparative Context:** The amortized MAP framework, particularly when integrated with GANs, yields visually plausible results. However, the paper does not sufficiently articulate how this formulation advances beyond the state-of-the-art, notably the 2016 *Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network* (SRGAN). It is unclear whether the proposed approach offers theoretical or empirical advantages over SRGAN in terms of reconstruction quality, convergence, or generalizability. A detailed ablation comparing the framework’s core innovations (e.g., amortized inference vs. iterative optimization) against SRGAN or similar GAN-based methods is essential to justify the contribution.  

2. **Training Data Requirements:** The paper claims the model requires no HR/LR pairs for SR training due to the affine projection layer. Yet, the affine layer itself is pre-trained using HR/LR pairs. This implies the training procedure merely shifts the need for paired data to the affine layer’s initialization phase. The authors should clarify whether the affine layer is trained once (e.g., using a fixed dataset) and then fixed during SR training, or if it is jointly optimized with the SR network. If the latter, the model still relies on paired data, undermining the stated advantage.  

3. **Benchmark Coverage:** While results on ImageNet are provided, the absence of standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) prevents fair comparison with prior work. These datasets are conventional for SR evaluation, and omitting them weakens the empirical validation. The authors must include quantitative metrics (e.g., PSNR, SSIM) on these datasets to contextualize their performance against established baselines.  

4. **Scalability to Larger Images:** The presented results for natural images are restricted to 128×128 resolution. SR applications typically handle arbitrary input sizes. The authors should demonstrate whether the framework maintains performance on larger images (e.g., 256×256 or higher) or if resolution constraints limit its practical utility. A brief analysis of computational complexity or memory requirements for larger inputs would also be valuable.  

5. **Latent Space Exploration:** Standard GANs employ noise vectors to model output distributions. The paper does not address whether a noise term was tested or why it was omitted. Given the framework’s GAN integration, investigating the impact of latent space noise (e.g., on diversity or reconstruction quality) is critical to validate the design choices.  

**Minor Concerns:**  
- The phrase "plausible and good results" is vague; replace with specific metrics (e.g., "improved PSNR by 0.3 dB over SRGAN on Set14") where possible.  
- Clarify the "three different methods" for cross-entropy minimization—briefly summarize their key distinctions (e.g., loss function variants, optimization strategies).  
- Ensure consistent terminology: Use "HR/LR pairs" instead of "{HR, LR} image pairs."  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper proposes a theoretically grounded framework with a novel amortized inference approach for SR, and the affine projection layer offers a promising path to reduce paired data dependency. The methodology is well-motivated, and the exploration of multiple cross-entropy strategies demonstrates thoroughness. However, the work’s contribution is currently under-justified due to insufficient comparison with SOTA, missing benchmark results, and unresolved questions about data requirements. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject** (with minor revisions). The core idea is promising but requires critical enhancements to meet ICLR’s standards for novelty and empirical rigor. Specifically:  
- Add comparative analysis against SRGAN and recent SOTA (e.g., EDSR, RCAN).  
- Include results on Set5/Set14/BSD100.  
- Clarify the affine layer’s training dependency on HR/LR pairs.  
- Demonstrate scalability to larger image sizes.  
- Investigate latent noise in the GAN component.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s claims about advancing the SR field remain unsubstantiated. With the requested clarifications and experiments, this could become a strong contribution to the community.
