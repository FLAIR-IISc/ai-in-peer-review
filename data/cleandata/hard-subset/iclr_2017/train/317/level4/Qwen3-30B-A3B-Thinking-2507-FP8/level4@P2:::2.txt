

**Review for ICLR 2017: "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"**

**Summary of Contributions**  
This paper presents a novel approach to single-image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors identify a key limitation of current SR methods: MSE-based approaches produce blurry outputs because they average over plausible high-resolution (HR) solutions. The paper makes three significant contributions: (1) a novel neural network architecture that projects outputs to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and HR outputs; (2) three methods for amortized MAP inference—AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided)—that minimize cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the HR image prior; and (3) thorough experimental validation demonstrating that AffGAN produces visually superior results on both toy datasets and real-world images. The theoretical grounding for using GANs in SR is particularly compelling, and the connection to variational inference in Appendix F provides an important conceptual link.

**Decision: Accept**  
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of image super-resolution, with a novel theoretical approach and compelling empirical results. I recommend acceptance for the following key reasons: (1) the paper successfully demonstrates that MAP inference produces more perceptually plausible results than MSE-based approaches, addressing a fundamental limitation of current SR methods; (2) the affine projection layer is a well-motivated architectural innovation that enables consistent HR-LR relationships without requiring paired data, and the experimental validation demonstrates its effectiveness.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper effectively addresses the core problem of SR as an underdetermined inverse problem. The theoretical motivation in Section 3.1 is particularly strong, showing that MSE-based methods produce outputs in low-probability regions of the image prior, while MAP inference naturally selects high-probability solutions. The affine projection layer (Equation 7) elegantly handles the constraint that HR outputs must be consistent with LR inputs, transforming the constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one. This architectural innovation is both simple and powerful.

The experimental results (Section 5) provide convincing evidence that the proposed methods outperform standard MSE approaches in perceptual quality. The Swiss-roll toy example (Figure 1b, Table 1) clearly demonstrates that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the cross-entropy objective, while MSE and MAE models fail to produce plausible outputs. On real-world datasets (Figures 3-5), AffGAN consistently produces sharper, more plausible images with better visual quality, even if PSNR scores are slightly lower than MSE models. The ablation study in Section 5.2 shows that the affine projection does not harm performance and actually improves training dynamics and final results.

The theoretical connection between AffGAN and variational inference (Appendix F) is a significant contribution that bridges two important areas of machine learning. The proposed instance noise technique (Section 3.2.1) also provides a practical solution to GAN training instability, which is a valuable contribution to the broader GAN literature.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
While the paper is strong overall, a few refinements would enhance its clarity and impact:  
1. The discussion of MAP inference limitations in Section 5.6 is important but could be expanded to address how the affine projection might interact with these concerns.  
2. The connection between the proposed methods and perceptual metrics (SSIM, PSNR) could be more explicitly discussed, particularly why visual quality improves despite potential PSNR trade-offs.  
3. The implementation details in Appendix D are comprehensive but could be summarized more clearly in the main text to help readers quickly grasp the experimental setup.  
4. A brief comparison to the recent work by Ledig et al. (2016) would help position this contribution more precisely within the current SR literature.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper states that AffGAN produces more diverse solutions than other methods due to the entropy term in the cross-entropy objective. Could the authors provide more specific examples of how this diversity manifests in the results, particularly for challenging textures like grass (Figure 3)?  
2. In Section 5.6, the authors note that MAP estimates are representation-dependent. How does the affine projection layer specifically mitigate this concern compared to other SR approaches?  
3. The instance noise technique appears to be a general method for stabilizing GAN training. Could the authors elaborate on how this might be applied to other image generation tasks beyond SR?

This paper represents an important advance in image super-resolution, with a theoretically sound approach that produces visually compelling results. The method is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and makes a clear contribution to both the SR community and the broader field of generative modeling. I believe it will be influential for future work in image super-resolution and related domains.
