

Review for ICLR 2017: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

1. Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as amortized maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference rather than empirical risk minimization. The authors introduce a key architectural innovation: a projection layer that ensures high-resolution outputs remain consistent with low-resolution inputs by projecting to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions. This enables training to minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior, rather than using pixel-wise losses like MSE. The authors present three distinct methods to implement this approach: (1) an affine-projected GAN (AffGAN), (2) denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and (3) density-guided SR (AffLL). The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces the most visually plausible results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, with compelling theoretical justification for their approach.

2. Decision
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation of current SR methods (the blurriness of MSE-based approaches), and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed MAP inference approach.

3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's key contribution is its theoretical framework for amortized MAP inference in SR, which elegantly addresses the problem of inconsistent HR-LR relationships. The affine projection layer (Section 3.1) is particularly well-motivated and implemented with mathematical precision, ensuring that all outputs satisfy the fundamental SR constraint (Aŷ = x). This architectural innovation transforms the SR problem into one more akin to generative modeling, where the objective becomes matching the marginal distribution of reconstructed images to natural image statistics.

The three proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) are well-justified and experimentally validated. The AffGAN approach is particularly compelling, with the authors providing both theoretical grounding and empirical evidence that it successfully minimizes the desired cross-entropy objective. The experiments (Section 5) are thorough and well-designed, including both the insightful 2D Swiss-roll toy problem and multiple real-world datasets. The results clearly demonstrate that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible images than MSE-based methods, with the AffGAN model achieving cross-entropy values close to the theoretical MAP solution (Table 1).

The paper also makes an important theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference (Appendix F), which is novel and potentially significant for the broader machine learning community.

4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions for improvement:

- The discussion of limitations in Section 5.6 is valuable but could be expanded. The authors note that MAP inference is representation-dependent, but it would be helpful to see more concrete examples of how different representations might affect the results in practical SR scenarios.

- The comparison with related work (Section 2) is good, but I would recommend explicitly addressing how the affine projection layer differs from previous work that used soft constraints (e.g., Garcia, 2016; Ledig et al., 2016). Specifically, the paper could clarify why the exact consistency enforced by the affine projection is critical for achieving the claimed improvements.

- The manuscript contains a few minor grammatical issues and typos that should be corrected in the final version (e.g., "amortised" should be consistently spelled as "amortized" in the final version, as this is the standard spelling in machine learning literature).

- For the AffDG and AffLL methods, the paper notes that these produced blurry results (Section 5.3). It would be helpful to understand why these approaches failed to match the performance of AffGAN, especially given the theoretical connection between denoising and gradient estimation (Section 3.3).

Questions for Authors:
1. In the AffGAN approach, you note that the objective minimizes KL[qθ‖pY], which differs from cross-entropy by the entropy of qθ. How does this affect the trade-off between visual quality and diversity of outputs? Could this explain why AffGAN produces sharper images than SoftGAN (Figure 4)?

2. In Section 5.6, you discuss the representation dependence of MAP estimation. How would you expect the affine projection layer to interact with this issue, particularly when considering different color spaces or feature representations?

3. The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces plausible results even when the reconstructed image appears to contain "wrong" content (e.g., the snake becoming water in Figure 5). How do you reconcile this with the claim that MAP inference produces high-probability outputs? Does this suggest that the image prior might be biased toward certain visual concepts?

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution. The theoretical framework is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the results are compelling. I believe this work will have a substantial impact on future research in image super-resolution and generative modeling.
