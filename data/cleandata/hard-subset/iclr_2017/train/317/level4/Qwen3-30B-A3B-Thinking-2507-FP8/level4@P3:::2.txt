

# Review for "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of image super-resolution by proposing a principled approach based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors recognize that conventional methods using MSE loss produce blurry, over-smoothed outputs because they correspond to the mean of the posterior distribution rather than the mode. They introduce a novel neural network architecture that enforces consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs through an affine projection layer. This architectural innovation allows the MAP inference problem to be reformulated as minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior, effectively transforming the problem into a generative modeling task. The paper then proposes three distinct methods to solve this optimization problem: an affine-projected GAN (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided baseline (AffLL). The authors provide thorough theoretical grounding for their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness through comprehensive experiments on both toy datasets and real-world images, showing that AffGAN produces visually superior results compared to competing methods.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper presents a fundamentally novel and well-motivated approach to image super-resolution that addresses a significant limitation of current methods, and (2) the experimental results provide strong empirical evidence supporting the proposed approach, particularly the AffGAN method.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—reformulating super-resolution as amortized MAP inference with the affine projection constraint—is conceptually elegant and addresses a genuine limitation of current approaches. The authors convincingly demonstrate that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they correspond to the mean of the posterior distribution, while MAP inference naturally favors high-probability (and thus more plausible) solutions. The theoretical development in Section 3 is particularly strong, clearly establishing the connection between the MAP inference problem and cross-entropy minimization, and demonstrating how the affine projection layer enables this reformulation.

The experimental validation is thorough and well-structured. The toy Swiss-roll example (Figure 1) effectively illustrates the conceptual advantage of MAP inference over MSE minimization. The ablation studies in Section 5.2 demonstrate that the affine projection layer not only enforces consistency but also improves training dynamics and final performance. Most significantly, the real-world results (Figures 3-5) show that AffGAN produces visually superior outputs compared to MSE, MAE, and other baseline methods, with the images appearing more natural and plausible despite the lack of pixel-wise accuracy. The quantitative metrics (Table 2) support the qualitative observations, with AffGAN producing the most perceptually pleasing results even when MSE and PSNR scores are lower.

The paper also makes an important theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference (Appendix F), which is a novel insight with potential implications beyond super-resolution.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, a few improvements would enhance its clarity and impact:

- The introduction could more explicitly highlight the specific limitations of current perceptual metrics (SSIM, etc.) compared to the MAP approach. A brief discussion of how the MAP approach overcomes these limitations would strengthen the motivation.
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent perceptual loss approaches (e.g., Larsen et al., 2015; Ledig et al., 2016) that use GANs for super-resolution. While the paper notes these approaches, it doesn't clearly articulate how the proposed affine projection method differs from and improves upon them.
- The discussion of the "criticism" of MAP inference (Section 5.6) is valuable but could be strengthened by providing more concrete examples of how the affine projection might mitigate these concerns in practice.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. In Section 5.3, the authors note that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results that could not be improved with various optimization methods. Could the authors elaborate on why these methods failed to produce visually appealing results, and whether they believe these methods could be salvaged with additional architectural modifications?

2. The paper establishes a connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference in Appendix F. Could the authors provide more intuition about why AffGAN (which uses a deterministic generator) can be connected to variational inference, which typically involves stochastic sampling? How might this connection inform future work on stochastic extensions of the approach?

3. In the theoretical analysis, the authors note that the cross-entropy minimization objective is equivalent to minimizing KL[qθ||pY] plus the entropy of qθ (Section 3.2). Given that AffGAN aims to minimize cross-entropy, while standard GANs minimize KL divergence, could the authors elaborate on how the affine projection contributes to this difference, and whether this explains AffGAN's superior performance in practice?
