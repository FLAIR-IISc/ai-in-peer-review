

The paper introduces a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference, incorporating a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and high-resolution (HR) outputs. It also proposes three distinct approaches for minimizing cross-entropy loss. Overall, this is a strong contribution with solid theoretical grounding and promising results. However, several points warrant clarification:

1.  **Novelty and Contribution:** The amortized MAP inference framework is indeed novel compared to prior SR methods, and its integration with GANs yields plausible, high-quality reconstructions. However, the paper does not clearly articulate how this formulation advances beyond the state-of-the-art, particularly when contrasted with the recent GAN-based SR work "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network" (Johnson et al.). It is unclear what specific novel contribution this formulation provides over existing GAN-based approaches.

2.  **Affine Projection Training Requirement:** The affine projection layer is presented as a constraint enabling training without paired {HR, LR} data. However, the layer itself requires {HR, LR} pairs for its initial training. Does this imply the training procedure for the affine projection layer is simply adapted from the supervised SR task, effectively shifting the need for paired data to this specific component rather than eliminating it entirely?

3.  **Benchmark Dataset Comparison:** While the framework demonstrates results on natural images from ImageNet, the authors should include comparisons on standard SR benchmark datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100) to enable fair and direct performance evaluation against established baselines.

4.  **Input Resolution Scalability:** The presented results for natural images use a fixed input resolution of 128×128. Given that SR applications must handle images of arbitrary sizes, it is crucial to demonstrate whether the framework maintains performance on larger inputs. The paper should address scalability to higher resolutions.

5.  **Latent Space Exploration:** Standard GANs incorporate a noise vector in the latent space to model the distribution of HR images. The authors should clarify whether they experimented with adding such a noise vector to the generator and, if not, why this component was omitted.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for SR with significant potential. The core idea is compelling, and the authors explore multiple solution paths effectively. However, the necessity and broader impact of the proposed formulation require stronger justification, and additional experiments on standard benchmarks and larger inputs are needed to fully validate the approach. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution. The work offers valuable inspiration for the community, and I believe it merits acceptance with revisions.
