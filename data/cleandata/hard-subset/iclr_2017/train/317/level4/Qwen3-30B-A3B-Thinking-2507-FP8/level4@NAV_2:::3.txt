

1. The paper introduces a novel approach for image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors propose a neural network architecture that projects outputs to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs. This enables them to reformulate the MAP inference problem as minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior, leading to three distinct methods for implementation: GAN-based, denoiser-guided, and density-guided approaches. The paper demonstrates that the GAN-based method (AffGAN) produces perceptually superior results compared to traditional MSE-based approaches.

2. Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing the fundamental limitation of MSE-based SR methods (producing blurry outputs) through a theoretically grounded approach that leverages natural image statistics. The AffGAN method demonstrates clear empirical advantages on real image data, and the theoretical connection between GANs and amortized variational inference is particularly valuable.

3. The theoretical foundation for using the affine projection to ensure consistency between input and output is well-motivated and properly placed within the literature on SR and generative models. The paper successfully demonstrates that the proposed methods minimize the cross-entropy objective (Table 1), with AffGAN achieving cross-entropy values closest to the MAP solution. The empirical results across multiple datasets (Swiss-roll toy problem, grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet natural images) consistently show that the proposed methods produce sharper and more perceptually plausible results compared to MSE-based approaches. The authors also provide a compelling theoretical connection between GANs and amortized variational inference in Appendix F.

4. The paper would benefit from improved clarity in explaining the technical details of the affine projection and the three proposed methods, particularly for readers less familiar with the mathematical foundations. The authors should consider adding more visualizations to illustrate how the networks learn features that contribute to the perceptual quality of the super-resolved images. The current presentation occasionally makes it difficult to follow the technical arguments, and a more systematic explanation would enhance the paper's accessibility.

5. Could you provide more detailed analysis of the features learned by the AffGAN model that contribute to producing perceptually superior SR results? Specifically, how do these features differ from those learned by MSE-based approaches, and what aspects of the image prior are most effectively captured by the affine projection architecture?
