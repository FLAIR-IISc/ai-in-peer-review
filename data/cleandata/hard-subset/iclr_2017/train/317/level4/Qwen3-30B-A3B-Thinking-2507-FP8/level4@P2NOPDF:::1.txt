

This paper introduces a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference, incorporating a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and high-resolution (HR) outputs. It further proposes three distinct approaches to minimize cross-entropy loss. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate promising performance, particularly when integrated with GANs. However, several clarifications and additional experiments are required to fully validate the claims and establish the method's advantages over existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) approaches.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Novelty and Contribution vs. SOTA:** The amortized MAP inference framework is indeed novel compared to prior SR methods. The integration with GANs yields plausible and high-quality results. However, a critical question arises when comparing to recent SOTA work like "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network" (Johnson et al., 2016). The paper does not sufficiently articulate *what specific theoretical or practical advantage* this new formulation provides over the Johnson et al. approach or other leading GAN-based SR methods. Simply stating "it works well" is insufficient; the authors must clearly delineate the *unique contribution* this framework offers to the field.

2.  **Affine Projection Layer Training Requirement:** The paper claims the model does not require paired {HR, LR} image pairs for *inference* or *training the main SR network*. However, the affine projection layer itself must be pre-learned using {HR, LR} pairs. This is a significant point that needs explicit clarification. Does the *entire training pipeline* still fundamentally rely on paired data (for the affine layer), thereby negating the claimed benefit of eliminating paired data requirements? If so, how is this pre-training procedure justified within the paper's stated premise? The manuscript must explicitly address this dependency and its implications for the method's practicality.

3.  **Standard Benchmark Validation:** The results presented include natural images from ImageNet. To enable a fair and rigorous comparison with the vast majority of prior SR work, the authors *must* report results on standard, widely accepted benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100. The current evaluation on ImageNet alone is insufficient for claiming competitive performance against the SOTA, which is typically evaluated on these specific datasets. Providing results on these benchmarks is essential for meaningful validation.

4.  **Scalability to Larger Image Sizes:** The results shown for natural images are limited to 128×128 resolution. Super-resolution models must handle arbitrary input sizes common in real-world applications. The paper lacks any discussion or experiments demonstrating the framework's performance on larger images (e.g., 256×256, 512×512, or full-resolution images). It is unclear whether the affine projection layer or the overall architecture scales effectively, or if significant modifications would be needed. This is a critical gap for practical deployment.

5.  **Latent Space and GAN Formulation:** The paper employs a GAN-based framework. A standard GAN utilizes a noise vector as the latent space input, enabling the generator to learn a distribution over plausible HR outputs. The manuscript does not mention whether the authors explored incorporating such a noise vector. Did they attempt this standard GAN practice? If not, why? If so, what were the results compared to the approach presented? This omission weakens the GAN integration and the claimed generative capabilities.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically grounded and conceptually novel framework for SR with a clear motivation for the amortized MAP approach and the affine projection constraint. The exploration of multiple cross-entropy minimization strategies is commendable. The core idea holds significant promise for the community. However, the current submission falls short in several key areas: the novelty claim relative to the most recent SOTA needs strengthening, the reliance on paired data for the affine layer requires explicit justification, essential standard benchmark results are missing, scalability to larger images is unaddressed, and the GAN implementation details regarding latent space are ambiguous.

The authors are encouraged to address these points thoroughly. Specifically, they should:
*   Clearly articulate the *unique contribution* of their framework over Johnson et al. and other leading GAN-based SR methods.
*   Explicitly state whether and how the affine projection layer training affects the claimed paired-data-free advantage.
*   Report results on Set5, Set14, and BSD100.
*   Demonstrate performance on larger image resolutions.
*   Clarify the use of latent noise in the GAN component.

With these revisions, the paper would provide a valuable and well-validated contribution to the SR literature. As it stands, the work offers interesting insights but requires stronger empirical validation and clearer articulation of its advancement over the current state of the art. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the exploration of multiple methods is thorough, but the practical significance and novelty claims need bolstering through the requested clarifications and experiments. This work has the potential to offer significant inspiration to the community upon addressing these concerns.
