

This paper proposes a novel perspective on super-resolution by framing it as amortized maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. The key innovation is a projection step designed to maintain consistent high-resolution (HR) and low-resolution (LR) relationships, which the authors empirically validate through extensive experiments. Additionally, the paper introduces three distinct methods for solving the cross-entropy minimization problem arising from Equation 9, each rigorously evaluated against baseline approaches.

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and structured, with clear technical exposition and logical flow. The experimental section is comprehensive, featuring well-chosen synthetic examples that effectively illustrate the core methodology alongside compelling real-world super-resolution results. The contributions in Sections 3.2, 3.3, and 3.4—particularly the amortized MAP framework, the HR-LR consistency projection, and the novel solution approaches for Equation 9—represent significant and original advances for the super-resolution field. While certain GAN-specific training techniques referenced in the paper (e.g., Section 3.4) have appeared in prior work with minor variations, the core integration and validation within this unified framework constitute a meaningful contribution. The finding that GAN-based training yields superior visual quality provides a strong foundation for future research directions in perceptual super-resolution, making this work highly relevant to the community.

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical clarity, methodological rigor, and thorough empirical validation. The ablation studies and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach across diverse datasets. The visual results are particularly compelling, with clear evidence of improved structural fidelity and reduced artifacts compared to alternatives.

Minor improvements would further enhance the manuscript. A few typographical errors (e.g., inconsistent hyphenation in "high-resolution" and minor grammatical phrasing in Section 4) should be corrected during final proofreading. Additionally, a brief discussion of computational complexity for the three proposed methods (Section 3.4) would strengthen the practical evaluation, though this is not essential for acceptance.

Overall, this is a strong submission with clear novelty, sound methodology, and significant potential impact. The work advances the theoretical understanding of super-resolution beyond standard optimization approaches and provides a practical, effective framework for future development. The experimental results are robust, the writing is precise, and the contributions are well-justified. The paper merits acceptance at ICLR 2017.
