

Summary: This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by proposing a principled approach that frames the problem as amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce a novel neural network architecture that projects outputs to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring high-resolution outputs are always consistent with low-resolution inputs. They then present three distinct methods to solve the resulting cross-entropy minimization problem (GAN-based, denoiser-guided, and density-guided approaches), with thorough experimental validation across both toy examples and real-world image datasets.

Decision: Accept. The paper presents a novel theoretical framework that addresses a fundamental limitation of current super-resolution methods, and the experimental results demonstrate clear visual improvements over existing approaches.

Supporting arguments: The paper's key contribution lies in its theoretical grounding for using MAP inference in super-resolution, which directly addresses the well-known issue of blurry outputs from MSE-based approaches. The affine projection layer is a technically sound innovation that enables the MAP inference framework while maintaining computational efficiency. The experimental results, particularly the visual quality improvements demonstrated by the AffGAN approach, provide strong empirical support for the proposed methodology. The connection established between GANs and amortized variational inference in Appendix F further strengthens the theoretical foundation of the work.

Additional feedback: The manuscript is well-written and technically sound overall. A few minor typos were identified that should be corrected in the final version. The authors might consider briefly discussing the computational overhead of the affine projection layer compared to standard SR networks.

Questions for authors: 
1. Could you provide more details about the computational cost of the affine projection layer compared to standard SR networks, particularly in terms of inference time and memory requirements?
2. The denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches produced blurry results in your experiments despite theoretically minimizing the correct objective. Could you elaborate on why these methods performed worse than AffGAN, and whether there are potential improvements to these approaches that could yield better results?
