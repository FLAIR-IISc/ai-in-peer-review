

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**Summary**  
This paper introduces a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors recognize that standard SR methods using MSE loss produce blurry, over-smoothed outputs because they minimize the mean of plausible solutions rather than preferring high-probability solutions under the natural image prior. The key innovation is an affine projection layer that ensures the network output is always consistent with the low-resolution input, transforming the problem into minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The authors propose three methods to approximate this optimization: AffGAN (a GAN-based approach), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). Their experiments show that AffGAN produces the most visually appealing results on real image datasets, while the affine projection framework enables training without paired HR-LR data.

**Decision**  
I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework with solid empirical results, but the main contribution lacks sufficient novelty compared to prior work, and the empirical evaluation is incomplete.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The primary issue is that the paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from existing GAN-based SR methods. The authors cite Ledig et al. (2016) as the most relevant prior work, but their AffGAN method is nearly identical to this prior work—both use GANs to generate high-resolution images from low-resolution inputs. The key theoretical contribution (the affine projection layer) is interesting, but the paper does not clearly demonstrate how this specific technical modification leads to meaningful improvements over the existing state-of-the-art. The authors claim their approach "establishes a connection between GANs and amortised variational inference," but this appears to be a minor theoretical extension rather than a substantial contribution.

The empirical evaluation is also incomplete. The authors report results on ImageNet and CelebA, but omit standard benchmark datasets like Set5, Set14, and BSD100 that are conventionally used in SR literature. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether their method outperforms existing approaches on standard metrics. Additionally, the paper presents results only for 128×128 images, while SR applications typically require handling images of arbitrary sizes. The authors should demonstrate that their framework scales to larger image dimensions.

**Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen this work, I recommend the following improvements:

1. Clearly articulate the specific technical contribution that distinguishes this work from Ledig et al. (2016). Does the affine projection layer provide measurable improvements over their method, or is it merely an architectural modification with no significant benefit?

2. Include results on standard SR benchmark datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100) with quantitative comparisons to recent state-of-the-art methods. This is essential for evaluating the practical impact of the proposed approach.

3. Test the framework on larger image sizes (e.g., 256×256 or higher) to demonstrate scalability. The current results on 128×128 images are insufficient for real-world applications.

4. Address whether adding noise vectors (as in standard GANs) would improve the model's performance. The paper mentions this question but doesn't provide any results or analysis.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. How does the affine projection layer specifically improve upon the GAN-based SR approach in Ledig et al. (2016)? Can you provide quantitative evidence of these improvements on standard benchmark datasets?

2. Why does the paper omit results on conventional SR datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100)? Would you be able to provide these results to allow for a fair comparison with existing methods?

3. The authors mention that AffGAN produces "sharper images" than the soft-constrained GAN approach, but the paper doesn't provide any perceptual metrics or human evaluation to support this claim. Could you elaborate on how you measured "sharpness" and whether these results hold under human evaluation?

4. The paper states that the affine projection layer "eliminates the need for paired HR-LR training data," but the training of the projection layer itself appears to require these pairs (as shown in Appendix B). Could you clarify whether the affine projection is trained using paired data, and if so, how this affects the claim of "no need for paired data"?
