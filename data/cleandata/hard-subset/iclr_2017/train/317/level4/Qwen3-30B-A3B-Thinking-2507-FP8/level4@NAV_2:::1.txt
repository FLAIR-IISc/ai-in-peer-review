

The paper introduces a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, which addresses a fundamental limitation of current SR methods that rely on empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise loss functions. The authors propose a neural network architecture with an affine projection layer ensuring consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, transforming the SR problem into cross-entropy minimization between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The paper presents three distinct approaches to approximate this minimization: an affine-projected GAN (AffGAN), denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and density-guided SR (AffLL). The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments that AffGAN achieves superior perceptual quality on real-world image datasets, while providing theoretical connections to variational inference.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a principled connection between MAP inference and SR, and the proposed AffGAN method demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing approaches in terms of perceptual quality. The paper's theoretical grounding and novel architectural constraint provide a stronger foundation for SR than previous GAN-based methods.

The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper successfully addresses a fundamental limitation of current SR methods by moving beyond empirical risk minimization to MAP inference, which produces more plausible high-resolution outputs; (2) the affine projection architecture provides a mathematically sound solution to the SR consistency problem, and the AffGAN implementation demonstrates superior perceptual quality on real image data compared to other methods.

The paper's theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results clearly demonstrate that the AffGAN approach achieves lower cross-entropy values than MSE-based methods, confirming that the model successfully minimizes the desired quantity. The results on the 2D Swiss-roll toy problem provide compelling evidence that the proposed methods indeed find the mode of the posterior distribution, while the natural image experiments show visually superior results. The connection to variational inference established in Appendix F further strengthens the theoretical contribution.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors: (1) explicitly compare their method with the most recent state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN) on standard benchmark datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100) to provide a more comprehensive evaluation; (2) clarify the training procedure for the affine projection layer, particularly whether it requires HR/LR pairs and how this compares to the requirement for the main SR network; (3) investigate the performance of the method on larger image sizes to demonstrate scalability beyond the 128×128 results presented.

I would appreciate clarification on the following points: (1) How does the proposed AffGAN framework specifically advance beyond the Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network (Ledig et al., 2016) in terms of theoretical foundation and practical performance? (2) Does the use of instance noise in AffGAN introduce any bias in the learned distribution, and how does this compare to other GAN stabilization techniques? (3) Could the authors provide results on the standard SR benchmark datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100) to facilitate more direct comparison with prior work?
