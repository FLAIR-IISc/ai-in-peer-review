

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## Summary
This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by formulating the problem as amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than empirical risk minimization. The authors recognize that standard MSE-based SR methods produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution of high-resolution images consistent with the low-resolution input. Instead, they propose to find the mode of this posterior distribution, which corresponds to the most probable high-resolution image under natural image statistics. The key technical contribution is a neural network architecture that projects outputs to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions (ensuring consistency between low- and high-resolution images), transforming MAP inference into a cross-entropy minimization problem. The authors then propose three methods to solve this optimization problem: AffGAN (a GAN-based approach), AffDG (denoiser-guided SR), and AffLL (density-guided SR). The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces visually superior results compared to existing methods on multiple datasets, including grass textures, faces, and natural images.

## Decision
I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretically grounded approach to image super-resolution that addresses a fundamental limitation of current methods. The key reasons for my recommendation are: (1) the paper successfully demonstrates that MAP inference produces perceptually superior results compared to MSE-based methods, and (2) the theoretical foundation for transforming MAP inference into cross-entropy minimization is sound and well-motivated.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution—using affine projection to enforce consistency between low- and high-resolution images—is well-justified and implemented with mathematical precision. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is particularly strong, clearly demonstrating how the affine projection layer transforms the constrained MAP inference problem into an unconstrained optimization problem. The authors effectively show that this approach is theoretically equivalent to minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior.

The empirical results in Section 5 provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) directly demonstrates that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the desired cross-entropy objective, while MSE and MAE methods fail to do so. On real-world datasets (Section 5.3-5.5), AffGAN produces sharper, more plausible images than MSE-based methods and other proposed approaches (AffDG, AffLL), as evidenced by both visual inspection and quantitative metrics (SSIM, PSNR). The ablation study in Section 5.2 further validates the importance of the affine projection layer by showing that it enables faster convergence and better performance.

The paper also makes an important theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference (Appendix F), which is a novel insight that could influence future work in generative modeling.

## Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong overall, I offer the following suggestions to further improve it:

1. The presentation of the technical contributions could be clearer. The explanation of how the affine projection layer works (Section 3.1) is mathematically precise but somewhat dense. A more intuitive explanation or a simple diagram would help readers grasp the key insight more easily.

2. The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of the features learned by the proposed networks. The authors note that AffGAN produces more perceptually plausible results but don't analyze what specific features or patterns the network has learned to prioritize. A comparison of feature activations or a qualitative analysis of the learned representations would strengthen the paper.

3. The discussion of limitations (Section 5.6) is valuable but could be expanded. The authors rightly note that MAP inference can produce results that are statistically plausible but perceptually incorrect (e.g., the snake-to-water example in Figure 5). A more thorough discussion of when MAP inference might fail and how it compares to sampling-based approaches would be helpful.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper establishes that AffGAN minimizes KL[qθ‖pY] rather than the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY]. How does this difference affect the qualitative properties of the generated images, particularly in terms of diversity versus sharpness?

2. In Figure 3, the AffGAN model produces sharp grass textures while AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results. Given that all models use the same affine projection architecture, what specific aspects of the training objective (GAN vs. denoiser vs. density) lead to this qualitative difference?

3. The paper states that the affine projection layer is "a standard operation used in deep learning" for upsampling. However, the specific implementation details (particularly the numerical optimization of A+ in Appendix B) are somewhat opaque. Could the authors provide more intuition about why the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse A+ is the optimal choice for this projection, rather than other upsampling methods?
