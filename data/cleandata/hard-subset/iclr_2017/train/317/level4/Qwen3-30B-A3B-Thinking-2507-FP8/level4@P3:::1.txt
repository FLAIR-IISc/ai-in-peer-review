

# Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, addressing a key limitation of current SR methods that rely on empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise MSE loss. The authors propose a convolutional neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and high-resolution (HR) outputs. By leveraging this structure, they transform the SR problem into minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior, eliminating the need for paired HR-LR training data. The paper proposes three distinct approaches to solve this optimization problem: an affine-projected GAN (AffGAN), denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and density-guided SR (AffLL). The authors demonstrate that AffGAN produces visually superior results compared to MSE-based methods and other approaches, with the strongest performance on real image datasets.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework with solid technical contributions, but the novelty compared to recent state-of-the-art methods is insufficiently justified, and the empirical evaluation lacks comprehensive comparison with standard SR benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary reason for rejection is the insufficient differentiation from existing GAN-based SR approaches, particularly Ledig et al.'s "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network" (2016), which introduced a similar GAN framework for SR. The paper claims that their affine projection layer provides a theoretical advantage, but fails to demonstrate how this translates to meaningful performance gains on standard SR benchmarks. While the theoretical justification for the affine projection is sound, the empirical evidence does not convincingly establish that the proposed framework outperforms the state-of-the-art in a measurable way.

Furthermore, the paper's experimental evaluation is limited in scope. The authors test on ImageNet natural images but do not provide results on standard SR benchmarks like Set5, Set14, or BSD100, which are essential for fair comparison with previous work. The visual comparisons in Figure 5 are promising but lack quantitative metrics on these conventional datasets. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide comprehensive quantitative comparisons on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) with both PSNR and SSIM metrics, alongside visual comparisons. This would allow readers to assess the method's performance relative to the current state of the art.

2. Clarify the practical necessity of the affine projection layer. The paper states that "training becomes more akin to training generative models," but does not demonstrate how this approach outperforms existing GAN-based SR methods that do not use affine projections. A thorough ablation study comparing AffGAN with standard GAN-based SR would be valuable.

3. Expand the experimental section to include results on larger image resolutions. The paper's results are limited to 128×128 images (Figure 5), but SR applications typically require processing images of arbitrary size. The authors should demonstrate the framework's scalability to larger images.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To better understand the paper and its contributions, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. How does the proposed AffGAN framework specifically improve upon Ledig et al.'s GAN-based SR method (2016), given that both use GANs for SR? What is the precise theoretical advantage of the affine projection layer that translates to measurable performance gains?

2. The paper states that "training becomes more akin to training generative models" and does not require paired HR-LR data. However, the affine projection layer itself requires paired HR-LR data for training (as shown in Appendix B). Could the authors clarify the extent to which the training procedure has been shifted from paired data requirements to unsupervised learning?

3. The paper does not incorporate noise in the GAN framework, unlike standard GAN implementations. Did the authors consider adding a noise vector as latent space input to better model the distribution? If not, why is this not necessary for the SR task?

4. The AffDG and AffLL models produced blurry results (Figure 3), while AffGAN was superior. Could the authors provide insight into why these methods underperformed and whether there are theoretical reasons to expect their failure?

The paper presents an interesting theoretical perspective on SR as MAP inference with a well-motivated architectural innovation. However, without demonstrating clear advantages over existing state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks, it does not provide sufficient evidence of a meaningful contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, this work could make a valuable contribution to the literature on image super-resolution.
