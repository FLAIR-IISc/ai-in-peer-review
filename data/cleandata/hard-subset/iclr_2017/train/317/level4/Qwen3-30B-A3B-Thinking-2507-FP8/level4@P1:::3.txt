

**Review for "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**  
This paper makes a compelling contribution to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization (ERM) with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce a novel neural network architecture that projects solutions to the affine subspace of valid SR outputs, ensuring consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and high-resolution (HR) outputs. This enables MAP estimation without requiring paired LR-HR training data. The paper then proposes three practical methods for amortized MAP inference: (1) AffGAN, a GAN-based approach that minimizes cross-entropy between generated and natural image distributions; (2) AffDG, a denoiser-guided method that backpropagates gradient estimates from optimal denoisers; and (3) AffLL, a density-guided approach using maximum-likelihood image priors. The authors demonstrate that AffGAN produces perceptually superior results on real-world datasets while theoretically connecting GANs to variational inference.

**2. Decision**  
**Accept**. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation of current SR methods (blurry outputs from MSE minimization), and the AffGAN approach achieves state-of-the-art perceptual quality on real data.

**3. Supporting Arguments**  
The paper successfully answers the three key questions for review:  

1. **Problem Tackled**: The paper clearly identifies the core issue in SR—MSE minimization produces blurry outputs because it averages over plausible HR solutions (Figure 1a), whereas MAP inference prefers high-probability solutions under the natural image prior. The authors correctly frame this as a fundamental limitation of ERM in underdetermined inverse problems.

2. **Motivation and Literature Placement**: The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, with a clear theoretical foundation for why MAP inference is preferable (Section 1). The connection to generative modeling and variational inference (Appendix F) places it firmly within current deep learning literature, and the comparison to related work (Section 2) is thorough and fair.

3. **Claim Support**: The claims are well-supported:  
   - The affine projection layer (Section 3.1) is rigorously derived and validated (Table 1, Figure 2).  
   - AffGAN's superiority is demonstrated empirically across multiple datasets (Section 5.3–5.5), with quantitative (PSNR/SSIM) and qualitative (Figure 3–5) evidence.  
   - The theoretical connection to variational inference (Appendix F) is sound and novel.  
   The only minor weakness is that the AffDG and AffLL methods underperform (Section 5.3), but this is acknowledged as a limitation rather than a flaw.

**4. Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper further:  
- **Clarity**: The paper is technically strong but occasionally difficult to follow. For example, Section 3.1's derivation of the affine projection could be simplified with a brief intuitive explanation before the formalism.  
- **Visualization**: Include a diagram showing how the affine projection layer integrates into a standard SR network (e.g., matching Figure 2's architecture).  
- **Analysis**: The authors note that AffGAN produces "high-frequency noise" (Section 5.5) but don't analyze *why*. A brief discussion of this trade-off (e.g., between sharpness and artifact generation) would add depth.  
- **Conciseness**: Some sections (e.g., Appendix C) could be trimmed to focus on the most critical insights for the main paper.

**5. Questions for Authors**  
To clarify my assessment:  
1. In Figure 1b, why does the AffGAN output (red) appear to slightly deviate from the true mode? Is this due to the finite sample size or an inherent limitation of the GAN approximation?  
2. The paper states that "MAP estimates appear plausible" (Section 5.6), but acknowledges potential pitfalls (e.g., mode collapse in high dimensions). Could the authors elaborate on how the affine projection mitigates these issues compared to standard MAP inference?  
3. For AffDG, the authors mention "diverging training" (Appendix E) but don't explore why. Could this be related to the noise annealing schedule, or is it a fundamental limitation of gradient-based MAP approximation?  

The paper makes a significant contribution to SR by bridging theoretical inference with practical deep learning. With minor revisions to improve clarity and add a few key analyses, it will be a valuable addition to the literature. The AffGAN approach is particularly compelling, as it not only improves perceptual quality but also provides a theoretically grounded connection between GANs and variational inference—making this work both practically useful and conceptually important.
