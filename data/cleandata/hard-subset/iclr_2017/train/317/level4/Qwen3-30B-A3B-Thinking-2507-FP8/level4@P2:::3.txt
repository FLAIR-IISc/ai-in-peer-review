

The paper introduces an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference approach for image super-resolution (SR), addressing a key limitation of current methods that rely on empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses like MSE. The authors argue that MSE-based approaches produce blurry, over-smoothed outputs because they average across the posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution images, whereas MAP inference selects the mode of this distribution, which better aligns with natural image priors. To make MAP inference computationally feasible, the authors propose a novel CNN architecture that projects the network output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions (ensuring consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs). This architectural innovation transforms the MAP inference problem into minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior distribution, enabling training without paired HR-LR datasets.

The authors present three methods to optimize this cross-entropy: (1) AffGAN, which uses a GAN framework with an affine projection layer; (2) AffDG, which leverages gradient estimates from a denoising network; and (3) AffLL, which uses a density model trained via maximum likelihood. Experiments demonstrate that AffGAN achieves the best perceptual quality on real image datasets, while also establishing a theoretical connection between GANs and amortized variational inference.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the SR literature by addressing a fundamental limitation of current methods and providing a theoretically grounded approach to generating more perceptually plausible super-resolved images. The affine projection layer is a particularly elegant solution to the consistency constraint in SR problems, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate that AffGAN produces superior perceptual quality compared to MSE-based methods and other approaches.

The supporting arguments for acceptance are as follows: First, the paper clearly identifies and addresses a fundamental problem in SR—current methods produce blurry outputs because they minimize MSE rather than seeking high-probability solutions under natural image priors. This problem is well-motivated through both theoretical discussion and the illustrative toy example in Figure 1a. Second, the paper provides strong empirical evidence that the proposed methods, particularly AffGAN, produce visually superior results on real image data, with perceptual quality that aligns better with human visual perception than traditional approaches. Third, the paper makes a theoretically significant contribution by establishing a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference, which is a novel insight for the field.

For improvement, I suggest the following:
1. The writing could be significantly polished to enhance clarity. Some sections, particularly the theoretical derivations, are dense and challenging to follow. A more structured presentation of the key insights would help readers grasp the contributions more easily.
2. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of the features learned by their networks. Understanding what the model has learned about natural image statistics would strengthen the theoretical justification for their approach.
3. The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of the limitations of MAP inference, particularly in relation to the criticism raised in Section 5.6 regarding the dependence of the mode on the representation space. How does the affine projection help address this issue?
4. While the paper demonstrates that AffGAN outperforms other methods on perceptual quality, it would be valuable to see a more detailed analysis of why AffGAN produces sharper results compared to the other methods.

To help clarify my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
1. The paper states that "the optimal deconvolution A+ is not simply the transpose of A" (Section 3.1). Could the authors provide a more intuitive explanation for why this is the case, and how this affects the network architecture?
2. In Figure 5, the snake example is super-resolved into water, which the authors describe as "plausible" under the image prior. Could the authors elaborate on how the model determines that water is a more probable solution than a snake, given the LR input?
3. The authors note that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results that they couldn't improve upon. Could they provide more insight into why these methods failed to produce perceptually superior results compared to AffGAN, and whether this is a fundamental limitation of the approach or a matter of implementation details?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important contribution to the SR literature. The theoretical insights are well-motivated, the methods are well-justified, and the empirical results are compelling. With some improvements to clarity and additional analysis, this work would make an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
