

The paper introduces an amortized MAP estimation approach for super-resolution (SR), where a neural network learns to project observations into an affine subspace of SR solutions consistent with the low-resolution input. This framework enables efficient solution finding by integrating diverse optimization strategies—including GAN-based, noise-assisted, and density-assisted techniques—without requiring per-instance optimization. The method demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple benchmark datasets, with compelling visual and quantitative improvements over baselines.

The core contribution is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful challenge in SR: reducing the computational burden of iterative MAP estimation. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental validation is comprehensive, supporting the claim that the learned projection effectively captures solution manifolds. The ablation studies further validate the utility of the proposed components.

However, the paper’s clarity and exposition require significant improvement. Key sections, particularly the methodological description and the rationale for the affine subspace assumption, are difficult to follow due to imprecise language and insufficient mathematical grounding. For instance, the phrase "project to an affine subspace of SR solutions which are consistent with the LR method" is ambiguous—consistency should be explicitly defined with respect to the observation model (e.g., "consistent with the low-resolution observation under the degradation operator"). The relationship between the neural network’s output and the optimization techniques (GANs, noise assistance) also lacks clear explanation, leaving readers uncertain about how these components interact within the amortized framework. A more structured presentation of the algorithmic pipeline, possibly with a concise pseudocode or diagram, would greatly enhance readability.

Additionally, the paper misses an opportunity to deepen its technical contribution through analysis of the learned network. While the results are strong, the reviewers would benefit from insights into *what* the network encodes: Do the projections emphasize high-frequency details? Are features related to common SR artifacts (e.g., blurring, ringing)? A brief analysis—such as visualizing intermediate activations, measuring feature diversity across layers, or comparing learned subspaces to ground-truth solution manifolds—would strengthen the argument for the method’s effectiveness and provide valuable intuition for the community. Without this, the work risks appearing as a "black-box" optimization shortcut rather than a principled advance in SR modeling.

Minor grammatical and phrasing issues further impede clarity. For example, "enables finding propoer solutions with by using a variety of methods" contains a redundant preposition and typo ("propoer" → "proper"). The phrase "the resulting the networks" should be "the resulting networks." These should be corrected in the final version to ensure professionalism.

In summary, the paper presents a technically plausible and empirically effective method for amortized SR estimation. Its core idea has potential, but the current exposition obscures its contributions. With improved clarity in the methodology, explicit justification for key design choices, and deeper analysis of the learned representations, this work could make a stronger impact. The reviewers recommend acceptance *only* if the authors address these concerns in the camera-ready version. The current draft would benefit from a thorough rewrite focusing on precision and accessibility for a broad ML audience.
