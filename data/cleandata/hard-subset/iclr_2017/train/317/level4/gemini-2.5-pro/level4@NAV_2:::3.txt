This paper introduces a framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, presenting it as a principled alternative to standard empirical risk minimisation methods that often produce blurry results. The core contribution is a novel neural network architecture featuring an affine projection layer, which cleverly ensures that any high-resolution output is always mathematically consistent with the low-resolution input. This formulation reframes the SR problem as one of minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the true data distribution. The authors thoughtfully explore three different methods for solving this optimisation problem: one leveraging Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), another guided by a pre-trained denoiser (AffDG), and a baseline using an explicit density model (AffLL). The work demonstrates through extensive experiments that the GAN-based approach yields perceptually superior results on challenging natural image datasets.

I recommend to **Accept** this paper. My decision is based on two key strengths:
1.  The work proposes a novel and theoretically elegant formulation of super-resolution as an amortised MAP inference problem. The introduction of the affine projection layer is a particularly strong contribution, as it enforces the downsampling consistency constraint by design rather than with a soft penalty.
2.  The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that clearly validate the proposed framework on both synthetic and complex real-world data, convincingly demonstrating the visual quality improvements of the proposed GAN-based method.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its principled theoretical approach. By shifting from pixel-wise loss functions like MSE to a MAP inference framework, the authors directly address the fundamental problem of generating plausible, rather than just average, high-resolution images. The affine projection layer, `gθ(x) = (I − A+A)fθ(x) + A+x`, is an elegant architectural solution that turns a constrained optimisation problem into an unconstrained one, guaranteeing that the model's output `gθ(x)` will always downsample back to the input `x`. This is a more rigorous approach than that taken by related works which use soft L1/L2 penalties on the downsampled output. The subsequent derivation showing this reduces to minimising cross-entropy between the output and data distributions is clear and compelling.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and effectively designed. The 2D Swiss-roll toy experiment provides an excellent sanity check, visually and quantitatively confirming that the AffGAN and AffDG methods successfully approximate the true MAP solution, unlike MSE or MAE. The experiments on challenging grass textures, faces (CelebA), and general natural images (ImageNet) convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the framework. The results, particularly from the AffGAN model, are visually sharp and detailed, standing in stark contrast to the blurry outputs from the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods. This systematic comparison of the three optimisation strategies is itself a valuable contribution, highlighting the practical advantages of the adversarial approach for this task.

For the purpose of improving the paper, I offer the following suggestions. These points are intended as constructive feedback and do not detract from my overall positive assessment.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** While the technical content is strong, the paper is at times dense and challenging to follow. The exposition in the theory section, particularly around the derivations, could benefit from additional intuitive explanations or simplified notation to make the work more accessible to a wider audience.
*   **Analysis of Method Failures:** The paper notes that the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods produced blurry results on textures and were subsequently dropped. A deeper analysis or hypothesis as to why these theoretically sound methods failed in practice would significantly strengthen the paper. For example, did they get stuck in poor local minima, or are the gradient estimates from current denoisers/density models not precise enough for this task?
*   **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The paper could be enhanced with a qualitative analysis of what the network learns. Specifically, it would be insightful to visualize the "residual" component `(I - A+A)fθ(x)`. This term represents the high-frequency details added by the network, and an analysis of its structure could provide a better understanding of the model's behaviour.

I also have a few questions for the authors that would help clarify my understanding:
*   Given the poor performance of the AffDG and AffLL models on complex images, could you elaborate on the practical difficulties encountered during their training? How sensitive were the results to the quality and training schedule of the pre-trained denoiser and density models?
*   The paper proposes "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training, which proved crucial for the ImageNet experiments. Could you provide more intuition on the selection and annealing schedule of the noise variance? Was there a principled way to set this, or was it found through extensive tuning?
*   Section 5.6 thoughtfully discusses the limitation that MAP estimation is dependent on the data representation. Did you perform any experiments using alternative color spaces (e.g., YCbCr instead of RGB) to investigate how this choice impacts the visual quality of the final AffGAN results?
