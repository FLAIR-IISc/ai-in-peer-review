This paper frames the problem of image super-resolution as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation, presenting a principled alternative to the more common empirical risk minimisation framework. A key contribution is a novel neural network architecture that incorporates an affine projection layer. This layer ensures that the high-resolution (HR) output is always mathematically consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input, effectively restricting solutions to the valid affine subspace. The authors show that with this constraint, the MAP inference problem reduces to minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real HR images. To solve this, the paper proposes and evaluates three distinct methods: one based on Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), one guided by a pre-trained denoiser (AffDG), and a baseline guided by an explicit maximum-likelihood density model (AffLL).

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for my decision are:
1.  The novel and well-motivated formulation of super-resolution as amortised MAP inference, enabled by the elegant affine projection layer, represents a strong conceptual contribution.
2.  The paper thoroughly validates this framework through a comprehensive empirical study, comparing three different solution methods on well-chosen toy problems and challenging real-world image datasets.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of approaching super-resolution as an amortised MAP estimation problem provides a principled alternative to the standard empirical risk minimization framework, which often leads to perceptually poor, blurry results when using MSE loss. The paper clearly motivates why MAP inference is desirable for producing plausible, sharp images.

The proposed affine projection layer is a simple yet powerful architectural innovation. It directly enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled output must match the input, which is a fundamental property of the SR problem. The experiments in Section 5.2 convincingly demonstrate that this constraint not only ensures theoretical correctness but also improves performance and training stability, even for standard MSE-based models.

The paper’s claims are well-supported by a solid set of experiments. The use of a 2D toy example (the Swiss-roll) is particularly effective for illustrating the core concepts and verifying that the proposed methods indeed optimize the intended objective (Table 1). The subsequent experiments on grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet) effectively showcase the practical benefits of the proposed framework. The finding that the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) produces the most visually appealing and sharpest results is significant and aligns with concurrent work, but this paper provides a stronger theoretical grounding for its success in the SR context.

While some of the techniques for stabilizing GAN training (like instance noise) may have parallels in other contemporary work, their application and analysis within this specific super-resolution framework are novel and well-executed. The comparison of three distinct approaches (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) represents a comprehensive and valuable exploration of the challenges of optimising the cross-entropy objective for this task.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is very well-written and the arguments are presented with great clarity. I believe this work will be highly relevant to future research in super-resolution and generative models for inverse problems. The following are minor suggestions for improvement.

*   The manuscript is of high quality, but a final proofread could catch a few minor typos.
*   The paper concludes that the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and likelihood-guided (AffLL) methods produced blurry results on image data and does not pursue them further. While this is an understandable practical decision, a slightly deeper discussion on *why* these principled approaches might have failed (e.g., difficulty in accurately estimating gradients from the denoiser, or inaccuracies in the density model in high-dimensional space) could be valuable for the community and for guiding future work in this direction.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the practical difficulties encountered with the AffDG and AffLL methods on real images? Was the blurriness primarily a result of optimization challenges, or do you believe there is a more fundamental limitation to using denoising score-matching or explicit density models for this task in high dimensions?
*   The affine projection requires calculating the pseudo-inverse `A+`. The paper mentions this is done numerically. How sensitive are the final results to the accuracy of this numerical approximation of `A+`?
*   The introduction of instance noise appears crucial for stabilizing GAN training on complex datasets. How sensitive is the model's performance to the specific annealing schedule of the noise variance?
