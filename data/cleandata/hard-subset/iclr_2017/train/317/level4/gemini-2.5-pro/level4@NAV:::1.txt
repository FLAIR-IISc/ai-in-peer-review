This paper introduces a novel framework for the Super-Resolution (SR) problem based on amortized MAP inference. The methodology employs a pre-trained affine projection layer to maintain consistency with the low-resolution (LR) input and explores three distinct approaches for optimizing the cross-entropy objective. Overall, this is a strong submission, though I have the following points for consideration:

1.  The proposed amortized MAP inference represents a significant departure from prior SR techniques. When integrated with a GAN, the framework produces perceptually convincing and high-quality results. However, when compared to other state-of-the-art GAN-based methods, such as the one described in "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network," it is not immediately clear what advantages this new formulation offers or how it advances the current state-of-the-art.

2.  The use of an affine projection architecture as a data consistency constraint reportedly allows the primary model to be trained without paired {HR, LR} images. Nevertheless, the affine projection layer itself requires such paired data for its pre-training. This raises the question of whether the requirement for paired training data is truly eliminated or simply offloaded to this preliminary training stage.

3.  The paper showcases extensive results, particularly on natural images from the ImageNet dataset. To facilitate a more direct and fair comparison with established SR literature, it would be beneficial for the authors to also provide performance metrics on standard benchmark datasets like Set5, Set14, and BSD100.

4.  The results presented on natural images appear to be limited to a resolution of 128x128. Given that real-world SR applications must handle inputs of arbitrary and often much larger dimensions, could the authors comment on the framework's scalability and performance on higher-resolution images?

5.  Conventional GAN architectures typically incorporate a random noise vector as a latent variable to effectively model a distribution. Did the authors experiment with including a noise vector as an input to their generator?

In summary, this paper puts forward a new and well-supported framework for SR, featuring a novel concept and solid theoretical analysis. While some questions remain regarding its comparative advantages and further experimentation is warranted, I believe this work offers valuable insights and will be an inspiration to the research community.
