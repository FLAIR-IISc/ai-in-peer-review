This paper introduces a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference. A key component of this framework is a pre-learned affine projection layer, designed to ensure that the super-resolved output is consistent with the low-resolution input. The authors also explore and compare three different methods for minimizing the cross-entropy loss within their proposed model. The overall approach is theoretically sound and presents an interesting new direction for SR research. However, I have several comments and questions for the authors:

1) The proposed framework, which combines amortized MAP inference with a GAN, is indeed novel and produces compelling, perceptually plausible results. However, its contribution relative to contemporary GAN-based SR methods needs further clarification. Specifically, could the authors elaborate on the advantages of this new formulation compared to recent state-of-the-art work, such as Ledig et al.'s SRGAN ("Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network")? A more direct comparison would help to position the paper's contribution more clearly.

2) A significant claim is that the model does not require paired low-resolution (LR) and high-resolution (HR) images for training, due to the use of the affine projection layer as a constraint. However, this projection layer itself must be pre-trained on corresponding {LR, HR} pairs. This raises the question of whether the requirement for paired training data has been eliminated, or simply shifted to a preliminary training stage. Please clarify if this understanding is correct and discuss the practical implications of this design choice.

3) The paper demonstrates strong qualitative results on natural images from the ImageNet dataset. To facilitate a more direct and quantitative comparison with existing literature, it would be highly beneficial to also evaluate the proposed method on standard SR benchmark datasets, such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100. Providing these results would substantially strengthen the paper's experimental validation.

4) The experiments presented are on images with a maximum size of 128x128. A practical SR algorithm must be able to handle inputs of arbitrary and often much larger dimensions. Could the authors comment on the scalability of the proposed framework? Are there any architectural or computational limitations that would prevent it from performing effectively on higher-resolution images?

5) Conventional GAN architectures typically incorporate a noise vector as input to the generator, which allows the model to learn a distribution over possible outputs. The current framework does not appear to use such a latent variable. Did the authors experiment with incorporating a noise vector, and if so, how did it affect the diversity and quality of the generated high-resolution images?

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded framework for super-resolution that offers a new perspective on the problem. The core ideas are interesting, and the exploration of different optimization methods is commendable. While the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the questions above—particularly regarding the comparison to state-of-the-art methods and evaluation on standard benchmarks—the work is promising and likely to inspire future research in the SR community.
