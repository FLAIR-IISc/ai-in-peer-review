This paper introduces a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP estimation. The central contribution is a method to learn a neural network that projects an input onto an affine subspace of high-resolution solutions. Crucially, all solutions within this subspace are guaranteed to be consistent with the given low-resolution input (i.e., they downsample back to the original LR image). This formulation elegantly decouples the consistency constraint from the prior, allowing for flexible optimization using various techniques to find a plausible solution within this subspace, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), noise-assisted, and density-assisted methods. The effectiveness of the approach is demonstrated with strong results on several standard datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Powerful Formulation:** The proposed method of learning a projection to an affine subspace of consistent solutions is a novel and powerful idea. It cleanly separates the problem of satisfying the physical constraints of the SR problem from the problem of finding a perceptually plausible image, which is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Framework Flexibility:** A key strength of this framework is its modularity. By ensuring the consistency constraint is satisfied by the projection network, the authors are free to explore different and more powerful priors (e.g., learned through a GAN) to select a final high-resolution image from the solution space. This makes the framework adaptable and promising for future extensions.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results presented are compelling and effectively demonstrate the capability of the approach in generating high-quality, realistic super-resolved images.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The primary weakness of the paper in its current form is its presentation. The subject matter is inherently complex, involving concepts from optimization, generative modeling, and signal processing. Unfortunately, the manuscript does not do enough to guide the reader through these complexities. The writing is often dense, and key concepts could be explained more intuitively before diving into the mathematical formalism. At several points, the flow of the argument was difficult to follow, which detracts from the impact of the strong technical ideas. A significant revision focused on improving clarity, perhaps with more illustrative figures or a more structured walkthrough of the methodology, would greatly enhance the paper's impact and accessibility.

2.  **Analysis of Learned Representations:** While the results demonstrate that the method works well, the paper would be significantly enhanced by a more in-depth analysis of the learned projection network. What kinds of features or internal representations does this network learn in order to satisfy the consistency constraint? An investigation into the learned filters or an analysis of the network's behavior on specific inputs could provide valuable insights into *why* the method is so effective and could inspire future work in this direction.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of super-resolution. The core idea is elegant, and the results are impressive. However, its potential is currently hindered by issues with clarity and presentation. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to undertake a significant revision to improve the manuscript's readability. Addressing the presentation issues and adding some analysis of the learned components would turn a good paper into an excellent one.
