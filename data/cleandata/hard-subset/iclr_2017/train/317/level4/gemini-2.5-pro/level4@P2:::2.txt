### Summary of Contributions

This paper recasts the single-image super-resolution (SR) problem from the conventional empirical risk minimization framework to one of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that standard pixel-wise loss functions like MSE lead to perceptually unsatisfying, blurry results because they average over the many plausible high-resolution (HR) images consistent with a given low-resolution (LR) input. Instead, they propose a framework to find a single, plausible HR image that has a high probability under the natural image prior.

The core contributions are threefold:
1.  An elegant neural network architecture incorporating an "affine projection" layer. This layer ensures that the network's HR output is always mathematically consistent with the LR input, effectively projecting any candidate solution onto the affine subspace of valid solutions.
2.  The formulation of the amortised MAP inference problem as the minimization of the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated HR images and the distribution of real HR images. This shifts the training paradigm from supervised learning on paired data to a generative modeling task.
3.  The proposal and empirical evaluation of three different methods to solve this cross-entropy minimization problem: a Generative Adversarial Network (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided method (AffDG), and a method guided by an explicit density model (AffLL).

Experiments on both a toy dataset and real-world images demonstrate that the proposed methods, particularly AffGAN, can produce perceptually sharp and plausible results that are superior to those from MSE-trained models.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and well-motivated contribution to the field of super-resolution. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  **Principled Approach and Novel Formulation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for moving beyond simple MSE loss in super-resolution. Framing the problem as amortised MAP inference and connecting it to cross-entropy minimization is a novel and insightful perspective that helps explain why generative approaches like GANs are a good fit for this task.
2.  **Effective Architectural Contribution:** The affine projection layer is a simple yet powerful architectural innovation. It directly enforces a fundamental physical constraint of the SR problem, which is more elegant and, as shown empirically, more effective than using a soft penalty term in the loss function.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are well-supported by its theoretical development and experimental results. The introduction clearly motivates the need for an alternative to MSE by highlighting its tendency to produce blurry, averaged outputs. The theoretical development in Section 3 logically progresses from the goal of MAP inference to the final cross-entropy objective, with the affine projection layer serving as the key component that makes the problem tractable.

The experimental design is rigorous and convincing. The 2D Swiss-roll toy problem is an excellent choice for illustrating the core concepts, as it allows for direct visualization and quantitative verification (Table 1) that the proposed GAN and denoiser-guided methods are indeed approximating the MAP solution, unlike MSE or MAE.

The "proof of concept" experiment in Section 5.2, which shows that adding the affine projection layer actually *improves* the performance of a network trained with a standard MSE loss, provides strong evidence for the utility of this architectural component on its own. The subsequent experiments on grass textures and faces effectively demonstrate the primary claim: the AffGAN model produces visually sharper and more plausible results than its competitors. The authors are also commendably forthright about the limitations and failure modes of the other proposed methods (AffDG and AffLL) and provide a thoughtful critique of the MAP inference approach itself in Section 5.6.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to my decision to accept.
*   The paper is very well-written, but a final proofread could catch a few minor typos and grammatical errors.
*   The discussion in Appendix E regarding the challenges with the denoiser-guided and density-guided models is very useful. It might be beneficial to briefly summarize the key insights from this appendix (e.g., issues with gradient precision, difficulty of learning from a peaked density model) in the main body of the paper to give the reader a more complete picture of why AffGAN was the most successful approach.
*   The introduction of "instance noise" is an interesting practical contribution to stabilizing GAN training. While the appendix provides a good motivation, the connection and comparison to parallel work could be slightly expanded upon in the main text if space permits, as this could be a generally useful technique.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points:
1.  In Appendix B, you describe a numerical method for finding the pseudo-inverse `A+`. How sensitive is the final performance of the SR network to the accuracy of this approximation? Have you considered if, for common downsampling kernels like bilinear or bicubic, a simpler analytical form for `A+` might exist?
2.  The "instance noise" technique seems crucial for stabilizing the training on ImageNet. Could you provide more detail or intuition about the annealing schedule used? How was this schedule determined (e.g., was it manually tuned, or adapted based on discriminator performance as suggested in the appendix)?
3.  The connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference established in Appendix F is fascinating and provides a strong link to another major family of generative models. While I understand it is noted as future work, have you run any preliminary experiments with this stochastic version? Even anecdotal results would be very interesting, as they could further solidify the paper's theoretical contributions.
