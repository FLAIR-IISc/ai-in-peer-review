This paper introduces a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference. A key component of this framework is a pre-learned affine projection layer, which enforces data consistency by ensuring that downsampling the high-resolution (HR) output reconstructs the original low-resolution (LR) input. The authors also explore three different methods for minimizing the cross-entropy objective within their framework. Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach that produces impressive results. The following points outline the strengths and areas for potential improvement.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The proposed amortized MAP inference framework is a significant and novel contribution to the super-resolution literature. It recasts the SR problem in a principled probabilistic manner, moving beyond the standard feed-forward regression approaches.

2.  **High-Quality Results:** The combination of the proposed framework with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) yields visually plausible and high-quality results, effectively capturing realistic textures and fine details that are often missed by traditional methods.

3.  **Solid Theoretical Grounding:** The proposed method is well-grounded in theory, and the authors provide a clear derivation of their approach. The exploration of multiple optimization strategies further strengthens the paper's contribution.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Author:**

1.  **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The proposed framework is compelling, but its advantages over other recent GAN-based SR methods are not fully elucidated. Specifically, a comparison to "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network" (Ledig et al., 2017) would be highly valuable. While the formulation is different, a discussion of the practical and theoretical benefits of amortized MAP inference over this state-of-the-art alternative would better position the paper's contribution.

2.  **Clarification on Training Data Requirements:** The paper suggests that a key advantage of the affine projection architecture is that the model does not require corresponding {HR, LR} image pairs for training. However, the affine projection layer itself must be pre-trained using such pairs. This seems to transfer the requirement for supervised data to a preliminary stage rather than eliminating it entirely. Could the authors clarify the extent to which their method can be considered unsupervised and comment on this data dependency?

3.  **Benchmarking on Standard Datasets:** The experiments primarily showcase results on natural images from ImageNet. For a fair and quantitative comparison with the established literature, it is crucial to report performance on standard SR benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100. Including these results would allow for a direct comparison of metrics like PSNR and SSIM against prior work.

4.  **Scalability to Larger Image Sizes:** The experimental results are presented on images of size 128x128. As real-world super-resolution tasks often involve inputs of arbitrary and much larger dimensions, it would be beneficial to demonstrate the method's performance and scalability on higher-resolution images. This would provide valuable insight into the practical applicability of the framework.

5.  **Role of Noise in the Generator:** Traditional GANs typically incorporate a noise vector as input to the generator to model the output distribution and enable diverse sampling. The proposed generator appears to be deterministic, conditioned only on the low-resolution input. Was a stochastic generator (e.g., by concatenating a noise vector to the input) explored? If so, what was its effect on the diversity and quality of the generated high-resolution images?

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for super-resolution that advances the field. The core idea of amortized MAP inference is insightful, and the authors have thoroughly explored its implementation. While several questions remain regarding experimental comparisons, data requirements, and specific architectural choices, these do not detract from the significance of the core contribution. This work provides a fresh perspective on the super-resolution problem and is likely to inspire future research in the community. I recommend acceptance.
