This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single image super-resolution (SR) by formulating the problem as amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core contributions are twofold: first, the introduction of a neural network architecture featuring an affine projection layer, which elegantly enforces the constraint that the super-resolved output is perfectly consistent with the low-resolution input. Second, by using this architecture, the authors reframe the SR task as an unsupervised learning problem of minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real high-resolution images. The paper thoroughly explores three distinct methods for solving this objective: one based on Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), another guided by a denoiser (AffDG), and a third guided by an explicit density model (AffLL). Through comprehensive experiments, the paper demonstrates that the GAN-based approach yields perceptually convincing results that are both sharp and consistent.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are the novel formulation of SR as an amortized MAP inference problem and the elegant introduction of the affine projection layer. This approach provides a strong theoretical foundation for generating plausible and sharp images, moving beyond the limitations of traditional pixel-wise loss functions, and is supported by solid theoretical analysis and compelling empirical results.

The proposed framework is well-motivated and addresses a fundamental shortcoming of common SR methods that rely on empirical risk minimization with MSE loss, which often leads to overly smooth and blurry outputs.
1.  **Principled Approach:** Framing SR as MAP inference is a principled way to seek solutions that are plausible under a natural image prior. The authors provide a clear theoretical derivation showing how this objective, under the consistency constraint, reduces to matching the distribution of generated images to the real data distribution. This offers a solid justification for using generative models in this context.
2.  **Novel Architecture:** The affine projection layer is a significant contribution. It enforces the physical consistency constraint `A*f(x)=x` by design, rather than through a soft penalty term as in other works. The experiments convincingly show that this architectural choice not only ensures consistency but can also speed up training and lead to better solutions compared to unconstrained or soft-constrained models.
3.  **Thorough Investigation:** The paper explores three different techniques to approximate the cross-entropy minimization objective. This comprehensive study strengthens the paper's conclusions, and the empirical finding that the GAN-based method performs best is well-supported by the qualitative results, particularly on challenging texture and face datasets.
4.  **Theoretical Insight:** The connection established in the appendix between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortized variational inference is an insightful and valuable contribution, linking two major families of generative models and enriching the theoretical understanding of GANs.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. I also include some questions to clarify my understanding.

*   **Comparison to Prior Art:** The paper positions itself well in the literature. However, a more direct comparison with other GAN-based SR methods, such as SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016), would be beneficial. Could the authors elaborate on the key differences in formulation and performance between their amortized MAP framework with a hard affine projection constraint and the perceptual loss formulation used in SRGAN, which combines adversarial and content losses?
*   **Standard Benchmarks:** The experiments on textures, faces, and ImageNet are compelling for demonstrating perceptual quality. However, including qualitative and quantitative results on standard SR benchmark datasets (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100) would allow for a more direct comparison with the broader SR literature and help contextualize the performance of the proposed methods.
*   **Scalability:** The results presented are on images up to 128x128 pixels. Does the proposed framework, particularly the GAN training and the affine projection, scale effectively to higher resolutions? A brief discussion on the computational and stability challenges for larger images would be valuable, as real-world applications often involve inputs of arbitrary and larger sizes.
*   **Data for Affine Projection:** The paper notes that the main training process does not require paired {HR, LR} data. However, the pseudo-inverse `A+` for the projection is learned numerically. Could the authors clarify the data requirements for this pre-training step? Is it learned from unpaired data and knowledge of the forward operator `A`, or does it necessitate paired examples, potentially transferring the data pairing requirement to this initial stage?
*   **Stochasticity in Generation:** The generator `f_theta(x)` is deterministic. In Section 5.6 and Appendix F, the authors insightfully discuss the limitations of a single MAP estimate and propose a stochastic generator `f_theta(x, z)` that connects to variational inference. Have the authors performed any experiments with this stochastic version? Such a model could sample from the posterior `p(y|x)`, potentially yielding a diverse set of plausible reconstructions for a single LR input, which would be a very interesting direction to explore.
