### Summary

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to single-image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The central argument is that standard empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses like MSE leads to blurry, perceptually implausible results because it averages over the many possible high-resolution (HR) solutions. To overcome this, the authors propose methods that seek a single, high-probability solution consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input.

The paper's primary contribution is a neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection layer. This elegant architectural constraint ensures, by construction, that any generated HR image is perfectly consistent with the LR input (i.e., downsampling the output yields the original input). This transforms the SR problem into an unconstrained task of maximizing the log-probability of the output under the true data distribution. This is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real HR images.

To solve this new objective, the paper explores three distinct training strategies:
1.  **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) variant where the generator is the affine-projected SR network.
2.  **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided method that leverages the connection between optimal denoising and the gradient of the data log-probability to train the SR network.
3.  **AffLL:** A baseline method guided by the log-likelihood of a state-of-the-art explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant).

Through experiments on a toy dataset, textures, faces, and natural images, the authors demonstrate that the AffGAN approach is most effective, producing sharp and perceptually convincing results that significantly outperform traditional methods in visual quality. The paper also provides a valuable theoretical connection, showing that a stochastic version of AffGAN performs amortised variational inference.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Principled Framework and Novel Architecture:** The paper provides a strong theoretical motivation for moving beyond MSE-based training for SR, framing it as a MAP inference problem. The core contribution—the affine projection layer—is a novel and powerful architectural innovation that enforces the critical data consistency constraint by design, which is a significant improvement over soft penalty-based approaches.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The 2D Swiss-roll example offers a clear and convincing illustration of the method's correctness, while the qualitative results on real-world images (especially the grass textures and faces) effectively demonstrate the ability of the AffGAN model to generate sharp, plausible high-frequency details where other methods fail.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strength lies in its clear chain of reasoning, from identifying the fundamental problem with MSE loss to proposing a sound architectural solution and exploring multiple ways to optimize the resulting objective.

The affine projection mechanism is particularly compelling. By ensuring `A * f_theta(x) = x` is always satisfied, the network is free to focus entirely on the more challenging task: hallucinating plausible high-frequency details in the null-space of the downsampling operator `A`. The experiment in Section 5.2, which shows that this projection even improves performance for standard MSE training, is a strong piece of evidence for its general utility.

The exploration of three different methods (GAN, denoiser-guided, density-guided) to optimize the same underlying cross-entropy objective is thorough. The empirical finding that AffGAN works best is significant, providing further evidence for the power of adversarial training in capturing complex image priors. The honest reporting on the limitations of the AffDG and AffLL methods on complex image data strengthens the paper's credibility and highlights important challenges for future work in those areas.

Finally, the discussion in Section 5.6 on the potential limitations of MAP inference and the theoretical connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F show a high degree of academic rigor and place the work in a broader context, elevating its contribution beyond a purely empirical result.

### Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

These points are intended as constructive feedback and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

1.  **Clarity of Presentation:** While the paper is rich with ideas, its presentation is quite dense and can be difficult to follow at times, especially in Section 3. A slightly more detailed walkthrough, perhaps with a small diagram, illustrating how the affine projection separates the problem into a fixed low-frequency component (`A+x`) and a learned high-frequency residual `(I - A+A)f_theta(x)`, could improve readability for a broader audience.
2.  **Importance of Instance Noise:** The "instance noise" technique (Sec 3.2.1, Appendix C) appears to be a crucial practical contribution for stabilizing GAN training, yet it is somewhat understated in the main text. Given the notorious instability of GANs, this is a valuable finding. If space permits, a brief mention in the main experimental section about its necessity for the ImageNet results could better highlight its importance.
3.  **Analysis of Learned Features:** The qualitative results are impressive, but the analysis is primarily visual. The paper would be even stronger with some analysis of what the generator `f_theta` is learning. Since it operates in the null-space of `A`, it is effectively learning a prior over high-frequency image content. Visualizing the filters or activations of this network could provide fascinating insights into the kinds of textures and details it has learned to generate.

### Questions for the Authors

Answering these questions would help clarify some details and strengthen my confidence in the assessment.

1.  The method relies on computing the pseudo-inverse `A+` of the downsampling operator. The appendix mentions a numerical optimization procedure. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the final image quality to the accuracy of this `A+` approximation? How critical is it to compute a near-perfect pseudo-inverse versus using a simpler, standard upsampling kernel (e.g., a learned transposed convolution initialized randomly)?
2.  The denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods are theoretically appealing but produced blurry results on real images. Do you have further intuition as to why they failed? For AffDG, was the gradient signal from the denoiser too noisy or biased? For AffLL, do you believe the primary issue lies with the quality of the density model itself, or with optimization difficulties when backpropagating through such a model?
3.  Regarding the instance noise used to stabilize AffGAN: how sensitive is the training stability and final performance to the specific annealing schedule of the noise variance? Is the proposed heuristic (keeping the discriminator's loss constant) straightforward to implement and tune in practice?
