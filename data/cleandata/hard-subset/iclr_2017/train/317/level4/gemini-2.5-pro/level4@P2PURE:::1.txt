This paper introduces a novel framework for super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference. The approach utilizes a pre-trained affine projection layer to ensure the high-resolution output is consistent with the low-resolution input, and it explores three different methods for minimizing the cross-entropy objective. Overall, this is a strong paper; however, I have several comments and questions:

1) The proposed amortized MAP inference is a novel approach that departs from previous SR methods. When combined with a GAN, this framework achieves plausible and high-quality results. However, a question arises regarding the contribution of this new formulation over other state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods, such as "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network." It would be beneficial for the authors to clarify the specific advantages of their formulation.

2) The authors claim that by using an affine projection layer as a constraint, the main model does not require paired {HR, LR} images for training. However, the affine projection layer itself requires pre-training on such paired data. Does this not simply shift the requirement for paired data from the main model's training to the pre-training of the affine projection layer?

3) The paper presents several results on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a direct and fair comparison with prior work, it would be beneficial for the authors to also report results on standard SR benchmark datasets, such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100.

4) The natural image results presented in the paper appear to be limited to a size of 128x128. Could the authors comment on the framework's performance and scalability on larger image sizes? This is an important consideration, as real-world SR applications often encounter inputs of arbitrary dimensions.

5) Typical GAN formulations include a noise vector input to the generator, which allows it to model a distribution of possible outputs. Did the authors experiment with incorporating a noise vector into their generator? If so, what was the effect on the diversity and quality of the results?

In summary, this paper presents a novel framework for SR supported by solid theoretical analysis. The core idea is innovative, and the authors have thoroughly explored several methods within this framework. While questions remain regarding its advantages over the state-of-the-art and more extensive experimental validation is needed, I believe this work offers valuable insights and will provide good inspiration for the community.
