### Summary of Contributions

This paper recasts the single-image super-resolution (SR) problem from the conventional empirical risk minimization framework to one of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core motivation is to generate perceptually plausible high-resolution (HR) images that have a high probability under the natural image prior, thereby avoiding the blurry outputs characteristic of models trained with pixel-wise MSE loss.

The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It proposes a novel convolutional neural network architecture featuring an **affine projection layer**. This layer is designed to project any output onto the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, elegantly enforcing the hard constraint that the downsampled output must be identical to the low-resolution (LR) input.
2.  With this architecture, the MAP inference problem is cleverly reduced to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of true HR images. This formulation removes the need for paired LR-HR training data, framing SR as a generative modeling task.
3.  The authors explore three distinct methods to solve this cross-entropy minimization problem:
    *   **AffGAN**: A variant of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) that minimizes the KL-divergence.
    *   **AffDG**: A denoiser-guided method that backpropagates gradient estimates from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder.
    *   **AffLL**: A baseline method guided by the log-likelihood of an explicit, pre-trained density model (PixelCNN-MCGSM).
4.  Through experiments on a toy dataset and real-world images (textures, faces, and natural scenes), the paper demonstrates that the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) produces the most visually compelling and sharp results.
5.  The paper also provides valuable theoretical insights, including a proof that their GAN training objective minimizes KL-divergence, a practical trick called "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training, and a connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-written and insightful paper that presents a novel, theoretically-grounded framework for image super-resolution. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Principled Approach to Perceptual SR:** The formulation of SR as amortised MAP inference provides a strong theoretical justification for moving beyond pixel-wise losses. It directly addresses the core problem of perceptual quality by seeking solutions that are probable under the natural image prior, offering a clear rationale for why methods like GANs are suitable for this task.
2.  **Elegant Architectural Constraint:** The affine projection layer is a significant technical contribution. It replaces the common practice of using soft penalties to enforce consistency and instead satisfies this crucial constraint by construction. The experiments convincingly show this not only works but can also accelerate training and lead to better solutions, even under a standard MSE objective.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are well-supported by a combination of theoretical analysis and empirical results. The motivation to move away from MSE is clearly articulated using the Swiss-roll toy example, which provides an excellent intuition for why MSE-optimal solutions can lie in low-probability regions of the data manifold, whereas MAP estimates do not. The experiments on this toy problem (Table 1, Figure 1) effectively validate that the proposed AffGAN and AffDG methods do indeed find solutions closer to the true MAP estimate compared to MSE/MAE baselines.

The introduction of the affine projection layer is a standout feature. The proof-of-concept experiment in Section 5.2, where an affine-projected network outperforms a standard CNN on an MSE task, provides strong evidence for the layer's general utility beyond the MAP inference framework. It demonstrates that a correctly structured model that incorporates prior knowledge about the problem can lead to better and faster optimization.

The exploration of three different methods to approximate the MAP objective is thorough. Although the denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches were less successful on complex image data, their inclusion demonstrates a rigorous investigation of the problem space and provides valuable insights into their respective limitations. The success of the AffGAN model on textures and faces clearly shows the potential of the overall framework to generate sharp, plausible details that are fundamentally difficult to produce with MSE-based methods.

Finally, the paper contributes beyond its main proposal. The analysis of GANs for KL-divergence minimization and the introduction of instance noise are useful contributions to the broader generative modeling community. The connection to amortised variational inference in the appendix is thought-provoking and opens up promising avenues for future work.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Comparison with Concurrent Work:** The paper focuses on a principled derivation for using GANs in SR. Concurrent work (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016, "SRGAN") also demonstrated impressive results using GANs. A more explicit discussion comparing the theoretical motivation and architectural choices (e.g., affine projection vs. soft penalty) and potentially a qualitative comparison on a few examples would further strengthen the paper's positioning and highlight the specific advantages of the proposed framework.
*   **Evaluation on Standard Benchmarks:** For a more comprehensive evaluation and to facilitate comparison with the extensive body of SR literature, reporting results on standard benchmark datasets (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100) would be highly valuable. While PSNR/SSIM are not the targets of this work, reporting them is standard practice, and qualitative comparisons on these widely-used images would be very informative.
*   **Scalability to Larger Images:** The experiments show results on images up to 128x128. Could you comment on the scalability of the method to higher resolutions? Since practical SR applications often deal with images of arbitrary and larger sizes, a brief discussion of potential computational or memory constraints would be helpful.
*   **Stochastic vs. Deterministic Generation:** Appendix F establishes a compelling link between a stochastic version of AffGAN (where the generator takes a noise vector `z` as input) and amortised variational inference. This seems like a promising way to address the potential "atypical mode" problem you raise in Section 5.6. Did you run experiments with a stochastic generator? It would be fascinating to see if this leads to more diverse or qualitatively different reconstructions compared to the deterministic MAP estimate presented in the main paper.
*   **Training of the Pseudo-Inverse:** Appendix B describes a numerical method for learning the pseudo-inverse `A+` for the projection. Could you clarify that this process only depends on the known downsampling operator `A` and does not require any paired LR/HR data? This would reinforce the claim that the main model training can be done in an unsupervised fashion.
