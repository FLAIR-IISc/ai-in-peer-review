### Summary

This paper tackles the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR), arguing that standard methods based on empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses (like MSE) lead to overly smooth and perceptually unsatisfying results. The authors reframe the problem as one of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, aiming to find the most probable high-resolution (HR) image that is consistent with the given low-resolution (LR) input.

The core contributions are threefold. First, the paper introduces a novel neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection layer. This layer elegantly enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled output must exactly match the LR input, effectively handling the likelihood term of the MAP objective. Second, with this constraint satisfied, the problem simplifies to maximizing the log-prior of the network's output, which is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real images. This recasts the supervised SR task as an unsupervised generative modeling problem. Third, the authors propose and evaluate three distinct methods to solve this generative problem: (1) a variant of Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), (2) a denoiser-guided method that uses gradients from a pre-trained denoiser as a proxy for the data log-probability gradient (AffDG), and (3) a baseline using an explicit, pre-trained density model (AffLL). Through experiments on toy data and natural images, the paper demonstrates that the AffGAN approach is the most effective, producing perceptually sharp and plausible results.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Principled Approach:** The paper's main strength lies in its principled reframing of the SR problem. The introduction of the affine projection layer is a particularly elegant and novel contribution. It provides a hard, architectural constraint for consistency, which is more theoretically sound than the soft penalties used in prior work. This projection cleanly separates the problem into a consistency component (the projection) and a plausibility component (the prior), allowing the SR network to focus solely on learning a powerful prior over natural images.

2.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The proposed GAN-based method (AffGAN) produces compelling qualitative results that are visibly sharper and more realistic than those from traditional MSE-based methods, especially on challenging textures like grass. The experiments are well-designed, starting with a 2D toy problem that clearly validates the core claim that the methods are optimizing the MAP objective, and then scaling up to real-world datasets of faces and natural scenes.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are well-supported by a logical progression of theoretical arguments and empirical results.

The central idea of using an affine projection `Π_Ax` to enforce `A * f_θ(x) = x` is theoretically sound. By design, this constrains the solution space to only those HR images that could have produced the LR input. The subsequent reduction of the MAP objective to a cross-entropy minimization problem (Equation 9) is a direct and powerful consequence of this design. The proof-of-concept experiment in Section 5.2, which shows that adding the affine projection even improves performance for a standard MSE-trained network, provides strong evidence for the utility of this architectural component.

The evaluation of three different methods to optimize the resulting objective is thorough. While the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods ultimately failed to produce sharp images, their inclusion is valuable. It highlights the practical difficulty of optimizing based on gradient estimates from auxiliary models and strengthens the conclusion that the adversarial approach is currently the most viable for this task. The success of AffGAN is clearly demonstrated in Figures 3, 4, and 5. The grass texture results (Figure 3) are particularly convincing, as generating plausible high-frequency, stochastic textures is a known weakness of MSE-based models. While the AffGAN results are not perfect and sometimes exhibit artifacts (e.g., high-frequency noise), they represent a significant step towards more perceptually realistic super-resolution.

Finally, the paper includes thoughtful discussions that add to its depth, such as the motivation for "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training (Appendix C) and a candid critique of the limitations of MAP inference itself (Section 5.6). The connection drawn between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference (Appendix F) is insightful and opens up promising avenues for future work.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is quite dense, and the core ideas could be made more accessible. For instance, a diagram illustrating the architecture of the affine projection layer—showing the roles of `f_θ(x)`, `A+x`, and the null-space projection `(I - A+A)`—would greatly aid understanding. The conceptual leap from maximizing the expected log-prior (Eq. 8) to minimizing cross-entropy (Eq. 9) could also be explained more explicitly for a broader audience.

*   **Analysis of AffDG and AffLL Failures:** The paper notes that the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods produced blurry results, with some discussion in Appendix E. This is a very interesting result. Expanding on this in the main text would be valuable. Is the failure due to noisy gradient estimates from the denoiser? Or is the explicit density model simply not accurate enough to provide a useful learning signal? A deeper analysis of these failure modes would strengthen the paper's conclusions about the relative advantages of the GAN-based approach.

*   **Analysis of Learned Features:** The current evaluation is primarily based on visual inspection and standard metrics. It would be insightful to include some analysis of what the generator network `f_θ` learns. For example, visualizing the "residual" component `(I - A+A)f_θ(x)` could reveal what kind of high-frequency details the network is learning to add to the baseline `A+x` upsampling.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you elaborate on the failure modes of the denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches on real images? Is this a fundamental limitation of using these proxy objectives for this task, or an issue of optimization and hyperparameter tuning that might be overcome with more effort?
*   The instance noise technique for stabilizing GAN training is interesting. Have you experimented with the annealing schedule or different noise distributions (e.g., heavy-tailed)? How sensitive are the final results to these choices?
*   How does the computational cost (both for training and inference) of an AffGAN model compare to a standard MSE-trained SR network of a similar capacity? Does the numerical optimization for the pseudo-inverse `A+` need to be re-run for different downsampling factors or kernels, and is this process computationally expensive?
