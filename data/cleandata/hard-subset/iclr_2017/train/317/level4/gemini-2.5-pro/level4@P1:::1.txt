### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is to frame SR not as a regression problem minimizing a pixel-wise loss, but as a problem of finding the most probable high-resolution (HR) image consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they propose a neural network architecture that incorporates an affine projection layer, which enforces the constraint that the downsampled output must be identical to the LR input. This architectural choice elegantly handles the likelihood term in the MAP objective. Second, with this constraint satisfied, the training objective reduces to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated HR images and the distribution of real HR images. The paper explores three methods to achieve this: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), a denoiser-guided approach, and a baseline using a pre-trained density model. Experiments on synthetic and real image data demonstrate that the GAN-based approach produces perceptually plausible and sharp results.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-written and thought-provoking paper that provides a solid theoretical foundation for using generative models in super-resolution. The key reasons for my decision are:
1.  The formulation of SR as an amortized MAP inference problem is novel and provides a principled motivation for moving beyond traditional MSE-based objectives.
2.  The affine projection layer is a clever architectural contribution that directly enforces output consistency, which is a more rigorous approach than the soft penalty terms used in related work.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its new perspective on the SR problem. By framing it as MAP inference and introducing the affine projection, the authors derive a clear objective: matching the distribution of generated images to the true data distribution. This provides strong theoretical justification for using techniques like GANs, moving beyond their use as just a "perceptual loss."

However, I would like the authors to address two key points to further strengthen the paper's claims:

1.  **Comparison with Prior GAN-based SR:** The paper's GAN-based method (AffGAN) invites comparison with other contemporary work, particularly Ledig et al. (2016), "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network." While the authors cite this work, it would be beneficial to explicitly delineate the contributions of the current paper. Is the primary advantage the theoretical grounding of amortized MAP inference, or the practical benefit of the hard-constrained affine projection over the soft L1/L2 consistency penalties used by others? A more direct comparison would help situate the paper's contributions within the state-of-the-art.

2.  **Requirement for Paired Data:** The paper suggests that by using the affine projection, the model no longer requires corresponding {HR, LR} image pairs for the main training phase. However, the affine projection layer itself (specifically, the pseudo-inverse A+) is pre-trained numerically using what appears to be paired data (as described in Appendix B). This raises the question of whether the requirement for paired data is simply being shifted to the pre-training of the projection layer, rather than being eliminated entirely. Clarification on this point is needed to accurately assess the data requirements of the proposed framework.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Standard Benchmarks:** The experiments are conducted on grass textures, CelebA, and ImageNet. While these are useful for qualitative assessment, it would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation to include results on standard SR benchmark datasets like Set5, Set14, and BSD100. This would allow for a more direct and fair quantitative comparison with a wider range of existing methods.

*   **Scalability to Larger Images:** The results presented are for relatively small output images (up to 128x128). Real-world SR applications often encounter inputs of arbitrary and much larger sizes. Could the authors comment on the scalability of their framework? Does the performance hold for higher-resolution outputs, and can the model be applied to images of varying sizes without retraining?

*   **Stochasticity in the Generator:** Standard GANs utilize a noise vector `z` as input to the generator, allowing them to model a distribution. The proposed generator, however, is deterministic given an LR input `x`. Given that the SR problem is ill-posed (many HR images can map to the same LR image), have the authors considered incorporating a noise vector into the generator, i.e., `fθ(x, z)`? This would allow the model to capture the multi-modal nature of the posterior distribution `p(y|x)` and generate diverse, plausible solutions for a single input. Appendix F provides a compelling theoretical link between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortized variational inference, and an empirical exploration of this would be a fascinating direction.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that provides a novel and well-motivated framework for super-resolution. The ideas are clearly presented, and the exploration of different training methods is thorough. I believe this work will be a valuable contribution to the community.
