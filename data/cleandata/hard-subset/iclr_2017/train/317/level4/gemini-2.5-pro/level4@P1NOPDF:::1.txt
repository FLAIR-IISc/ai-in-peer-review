This paper presents a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference. The authors employ a pre-trained affine projection layer to ensure that the high-resolution output is consistent with the low-resolution input. The paper also explores three distinct methods for minimizing the cross-entropy loss during training. Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling new approach. However, I have several comments and questions for the authors:

1) The proposed amortized MAP inference framework is a novel departure from previous SR methods. When combined with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), the framework achieves plausible and high-quality results. However, when compared with other recent GAN-based SR methods, such as Ledig et al.'s "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network" (SRGAN), it is not immediately clear what this new formulation adds to the state-of-the-art. Could the authors elaborate on the key advantages of their formulation over SRGAN and clarify its specific contributions?

2) The paper suggests that by using an affine projection constraint, the model does not require paired low-resolution (LR) and high-resolution (HR) images for training. However, the affine projection layer itself must be pre-trained on such {HR, LR} pairs. This seems to suggest that the requirement for paired training data is not eliminated, but rather shifted to a preliminary training stage. Could the authors clarify this point? Is this interpretation correct?

3) The paper presents numerous results on natural images from the ImageNet dataset. To facilitate a more direct and fair comparison with prior work, it would be beneficial for the authors to provide quantitative and qualitative results on standard SR benchmark datasets, such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100.

4) The natural image results presented in the paper appear to be limited to a resolution of 128x128. In practical applications, SR models must handle inputs of arbitrary and often much larger resolutions. Could the authors comment on the performance, scalability, and computational feasibility of their method on higher-resolution images?

5) Typically, GANs incorporate a random noise vector as input to the generator, enabling the model to learn a distribution of possible outputs. The proposed model does not appear to use such a noise vector. Was this a deliberate design choice? Did the authors experiment with including a noise term, and if so, what was its effect on the results?

In summary, this paper introduces a new framework for SR supported by solid theoretical analysis. The core idea is novel, and the authors thoroughly explore several methodological variations. While some questions remain regarding the method's specific advantages over the state-of-the-art and the need for more extensive experimental validation on standard benchmarks, I believe this work offers a valuable new perspective and is likely to provide significant inspiration to the research community.
