This paper presents a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR), reformulating the task as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that this approach overcomes the characteristic blurriness of common Mean Squared Error (MSE) trained models by seeking solutions that are inherently plausible under the natural image prior. The core technical contribution is a new convolutional neural network architecture that incorporates an affine projection layer. This layer elegantly ensures that any generated high-resolution image is perfectly consistent with the low-resolution input. This architectural constraint simplifies the MAP objective into a cross-entropy minimisation problem between the distribution of generated images and the true data distribution. The paper then thoughtfully proposes and investigates three different methods to solve this problem: one based on Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), one guided by a pre-trained denoiser (AffDG), and a third guided by an explicit density model (AffLL). Through comprehensive experiments, the paper demonstrates that the GAN-based approach yields the most visually appealing and sharp results.

I recommend accepting this paper. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper provides a principled and well-motivated reframing of the super-resolution problem, supported by a novel and effective affine projection architecture that rigorously enforces data consistency.
2.  The proposed methods are thoroughly validated through well-designed experiments on both a tractable toy problem and challenging real-world image datasets, convincingly demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed framework.

The theoretical motivation for approaching SR as an amortised MAP inference problem is exceptionally clear and well-argued. The derivation that transforms the problem into minimising cross-entropy (Eq. 9) is elegant. The introduction of the affine projection layer is a significant architectural contribution. It provides a hard constraint for data consistency, which is a more principled solution than the soft penalties often used in related work. The proof-of-concept experiment in Section 5.2, showing that this layer can even improve performance and training speed for standard MSE-based models, highlights its value as a standalone contribution.

The experimental evaluation is rigorous and insightful. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a powerful sanity check in a low-dimensional setting where the ground-truth MAP solution is computable, clearly demonstrating that the proposed GAN and denoiser-guided methods successfully optimize the intended objective, unlike traditional losses. The qualitative results on grass textures, faces, and ImageNet images compellingly support the central claim that the AffGAN model produces perceptually superior results that are sharper and more plausible than those from MSE-based methods. The thoughtful discussion on the limitations of MAP inference and the theoretical connection drawn between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference in the appendix further strengthens the paper's contribution.

The paper is very well-written and the arguments are presented with clarity. The following points are intended to help improve the final manuscript and are not critical to my decision.
*   A final proofread would be beneficial to correct a few minor typos and grammatical errors.
*   The discussion of the results for the AffDG and AffLL models on real images is brief. While the blurry outputs justify focusing on AffGAN, a slightly more detailed discussion on why these methods might have underperformed would be insightful. Is this a fundamental limitation, or perhaps a practical issue related to the difficulty of learning sufficiently accurate denoisers or density models in such high-dimensional spaces?
*   The "instance noise" technique for stabilizing GAN training is a useful practical contribution. Could the authors briefly comment on the sensitivity of the results to the noise annealing schedule? This could provide valuable guidance for practitioners seeking to reproduce or build upon this work.

I would be interested to hear the authors' thoughts on the following question:
*   The connection established in Appendix F between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference is fascinating. Based on your experience, what might be the practical trade-offs (e.g., in terms of training stability, computational cost, or visual quality of samples) when moving from the deterministic AffGAN model to this stochastic, sampling-based approach?
