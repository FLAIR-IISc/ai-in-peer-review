This paper proposes a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference. A key component is a pre-learned affine projection layer, which enforces data consistency between the high-resolution output and the low-resolution input. The authors also explore three different methods for minimizing the cross-entropy objective. While this is a strong paper, I have the following comments:

1.  The proposed amortized MAP inference framework is a novel approach to the SR problem. When combined with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), the method produces plausible and high-quality results. However, a question remains regarding the contribution over the current state-of-the-art. It would be beneficial for the authors to clarify the specific advantages of this formulation compared to other recent GAN-based SR methods, such as Ledig et al.'s "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network".

2.  The paper claims that by using the affine projection layer as a constraint, the model does not require paired {HR, LR} images for training. However, the affine projection layer itself must be pre-trained on such paired data. Could the authors clarify this point? It appears that the requirement for paired training data is not eliminated, but rather shifted to the pre-training of the projection layer.

3.  The paper presents extensive results, including those on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a more direct and fair comparison with previous work, the authors are encouraged to evaluate their framework on standard SR benchmark datasets, such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100.

4.  The results on natural images presented in the paper appear to be limited to a resolution of 128x128 pixels. Since real-world SR applications must handle inputs of arbitrary and often larger sizes, it would be valuable to understand how the proposed framework performs on higher-resolution images.

5.  Typical GAN architectures incorporate a noise vector as an input to the generator to help model a distribution over possible outputs. Did the authors experiment with including a latent noise vector in their framework?

Overall, this paper introduces a novel framework for super-resolution with a solid theoretical foundation. The core idea is interesting, and the authors have commendably explored multiple methodological variations. While there are open questions regarding the necessity of the proposed formulation over existing methods and a need for more extensive experimental validation, this work is thought-provoking and likely to provide valuable inspiration to the research community.
