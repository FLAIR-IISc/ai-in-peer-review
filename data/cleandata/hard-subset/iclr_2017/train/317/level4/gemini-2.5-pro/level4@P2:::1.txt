### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors correctly identify that standard methods trained with pixel-wise Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss tend to produce blurry and perceptually implausible results by averaging over the many possible high-resolution (HR) solutions. To address this, they reframe the SR task as finding an HR image that has a high probability under the natural image prior, conditioned on the low-resolution (LR) input.

A key contribution is a network architecture that incorporates an affine projection layer. This layer enforces a hard constraint ensuring that the downsampled output is always perfectly consistent with the LR input. This elegant design simplifies the learning objective to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the network's outputs and the true distribution of HR images. The paper then proposes and evaluates three methods to solve this objective: a Generative Adversarial Network (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided method (AffDG), and a baseline using an explicit density model (AffLL). Experiments on both a toy problem and real image datasets demonstrate that the AffGAN approach is particularly effective, producing sharp and visually compelling results.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper's main strength is its shift from standard empirical risk minimization to a theoretically sound framework of amortized MAP inference for SR. This provides a strong justification for why generative approaches are well-suited for this task.
2.  **Elegant Architectural Constraint:** The introduction of the affine projection layer is a significant contribution. It elegantly transforms a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one focused on matching the image prior, while guaranteeing that the model's output is a valid super-resolution of the input.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper is well-motivated and provides a fresh perspective on the SR problem. By framing the goal as MAP inference, the authors move beyond the limitations of MSE and other pixel-wise losses that fail to capture the complex, multi-modal nature of the distribution of natural images.

The proposed affine projection architecture is a core insight. By building the consistency constraint (`Af(x) = x`) directly into the network, the authors simplify the training objective into one of pure generative modeling. This makes the connection to techniques like GANs not just a heuristic choice for better perceptual quality, but a direct consequence of the problem formulation. This provides important theoretical grounding for the success of recent GAN-based SR methods.

The claims are well-supported by a series of thoughtful experiments. The 2D Swiss-roll example provides clear and convincing intuition for why the proposed methods succeed where MSE fails. The qualitative results on challenging grass textures and faces effectively demonstrate the ability of the AffGAN model to generate sharp, plausible details that are absent in the MSE-trained counterparts. The theoretical analysis, including the introduction of "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training and the connection to amortized variational inference in the appendix, adds significant depth to the work.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision. I would appreciate the authors' response to these questions to clarify my understanding.

*   **Comparison to Concurrent GAN-based SR:** The paper provides a strong theoretical basis for using GANs in SR. To better contextualize this contribution, a more direct discussion or comparison with contemporaneous work, such as Ledig et al. (2016), would be beneficial. What specific advantages does this amortized MAP formulation with a hard affine constraint offer over an adversarial loss combined with a soft L1/L2 consistency penalty?

*   **Training Data for the Affine Projection Layer:** The paper states that the proposed model does not require paired {HR, LR} data for training the main generator. However, the affine projection layer's pseudo-inverse `A+` is pre-trained numerically. Could you clarify the data requirements for this pre-training step? Does it still rely on paired image data, or is it trained on synthetic data based on knowledge of the forward downsampling operator `A`?

*   **Evaluation on Standard Benchmarks:** The qualitative results are compelling. To facilitate a more direct comparison with the broader SR literature, it would be valuable to report results on standard benchmark datasets (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100). While PSNR and SSIM are not the primary focus, reporting these conventional metrics would help position the work more clearly within the field.

*   **Scalability to Larger Images:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small image patches (e.g., 128x128 outputs). Have you investigated the scalability of the approach to larger, arbitrary-sized images? Are there any computational or architectural challenges that arise when moving beyond fixed-size patches?

*   **Role of a Latent Noise Vector:** The generator in the AffGAN model takes the LR image `x` as its primary input. In contrast, traditional GANs often use a random noise vector `z` to sample from the latent space and generate diverse outputs. Appendix F makes a connection to a stochastic version of the model for variational inference. Could you comment on whether you experimented with explicitly including a noise vector in the main experiments, and what effect this had on the diversity and quality of the generated outputs for a single LR input?
