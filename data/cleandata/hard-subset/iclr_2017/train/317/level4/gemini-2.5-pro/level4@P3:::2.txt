### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to single-image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that conventional methods based on empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise MSE loss produce blurry and perceptually implausible results because they average over the many possible high-resolution solutions. To address this, they propose a framework that seeks a single, high-probability solution consistent with the natural image prior.

The paper's core contributions are threefold:
1.  A novel convolutional neural network architecture that incorporates an "affine projection layer." This layer analytically enforces the constraint that the downsampled high-resolution output must exactly match the low-resolution input, elegantly converting a constrained optimisation problem into an unconstrained one.
2.  The formulation of the amortised MAP inference task as a cross-entropy minimisation problem between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of true high-resolution images.
3.  A thorough investigation of three distinct methods to solve this problem: one based on Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), one guided by a pre-trained denoiser (AffDG), and a baseline guided by an explicit density model (AffLL).

Through well-designed experiments on both a toy dataset and real-world images, the authors demonstrate that the AffGAN approach is the most effective, producing significantly sharper and more perceptually convincing results than traditional MSE-based methods and the other proposed techniques.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a well-written and thoughtfully constructed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of super-resolution. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper provides a strong theoretical motivation for moving beyond MSE loss and reformulates the SR problem as amortised MAP inference. The proposed affine projection layer is an elegant and effective method for ensuring consistency between the low-resolution input and the high-resolution output, which simplifies the learning objective in a principled way.
2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The authors empirically validate their claims with a series of well-chosen experiments. The toy example clearly illustrates the conceptual advantage of their approach, while experiments on challenging textures and natural images convincingly demonstrate that the GAN-based method produces visually superior results compared to alternatives.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully supports its claims through clear motivation, sound theoretical development, and rigorous experimentation.

*   **Motivation and Problem Framing:** The introduction clearly explains the shortcomings of MSE loss for an underdetermined problem like SR, using the Swiss-roll toy example as an effective illustration. This provides a strong rationale for pursuing MAP inference to find plausible, high-probability solutions instead of blurry, low-probability averages.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation in Section 3, which moves from the general MAP objective (Eq. 2) to the unconstrained cross-entropy minimisation problem (Eq. 9), is logical and clear. The affine projection (Eq. 7) is a clever architectural contribution that directly addresses the likelihood term in the MAP objective, allowing the models to focus entirely on learning the natural image prior.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experiments are well-designed to answer key questions.
    *   The 2D Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) provides quantitative evidence that AffGAN and AffDG successfully approximate the true MAP solution, unlike MSE or MAE models.
    *   The proof-of-concept experiment (Section 5.2) serves as a crucial sanity check, demonstrating that the affine projection layer does not harm, and may even accelerate, standard MSE-based training.
    *   The qualitative results on grass textures, faces, and ImageNet images (Sections 5.3-5.5) are compelling. They clearly show that the AffGAN model generates sharp details and plausible textures that are absent in the outputs of other methods, supporting the central claim that this approach yields perceptually superior results. The paper is also transparent about the failure cases of the AffDG and AffLL models, which strengthens the overall empirical story.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Discussion on AffDG and AffLL:** The AffDG and AffLL methods produced blurry results and were not pursued further. Appendix E provides some valuable discussion on why these methods might have failed (e.g., instability of gradients, inaccurate density models). It would strengthen the main paper to briefly summarize these insights in Section 5.3, as it provides a more complete picture of the challenges in optimising the cross-entropy objective.
*   **Instance Noise:** The "instance noise" technique introduced in Section 3.2.1 is an interesting and practical contribution to stabilizing GAN training. While discussed in detail in the appendix, its importance could be slightly more emphasized in the main text, perhaps by briefly contrasting it with other stabilization methods beyond one-sided label smoothing.
*   **Clarity on `A+`:** The paper explains that the pseudo-inverse `A+` is found numerically. It would be helpful to clarify if this is a one-time, pre-computation step for a given downsampling kernel `A` or if it adds significant overhead to the overall process.
*   **Minor Typos:** The manuscript is very well-written, but a final proofread would be beneficial to catch a few minor typographical errors.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you comment on the computational cost of your approach? Specifically, how does the training time of AffGAN compare to a similarly-sized CNN trained with a standard MSE loss?
*   The affine projection relies on knowing the exact downsampling operator `A`. How robust is the method if `A` is unknown or only approximately known, as might be the case in real-world applications?
*   In the stochastic version of AffGAN presented in Appendix F, you establish a compelling connection to amortised variational inference. Have you performed any preliminary experiments with this stochastic model? It seems like a promising direction for generating diverse, plausible reconstructions rather than a single MAP estimate, which could address some of the criticisms of MAP inference you thoughtfully raise in Section 5.6.
