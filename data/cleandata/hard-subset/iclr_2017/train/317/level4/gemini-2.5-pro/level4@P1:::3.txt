### Summary

This paper frames the single-image super-resolution (SR) task as a problem of amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that conventional methods based on empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses (e.g., MSE) produce overly smooth and perceptually implausible results because they effectively average all possible high-resolution solutions. In contrast, a MAP-based approach seeks a single, high-probability solution that is consistent with the image prior, leading to sharper and more realistic outputs.

The core technical contribution is a novel neural network architecture that incorporates an affine projection layer. This layer enforces the hard constraint that any generated high-resolution (HR) image must downsample back to the original low-resolution (LR) input. By satisfying this consistency constraint by design, the problem of amortized MAP inference simplifies to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the true data distribution.

To solve this objective, the paper proposes and investigates three distinct methods:
1.  **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) that minimizes the KL-divergence between the generated and real image distributions.
2.  **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided method that leverages a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradients of the log-probability of the data distribution.
3.  **AffLL:** A baseline method guided by the log-likelihood of an explicit, pre-trained density model (a PixelCNN variant).

Through experiments on toy data, textures, faces, and natural images, the authors demonstrate that the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) is most effective, producing visually impressive results that are significantly sharper and more detailed than those from traditional methods.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with novel contributions on both a conceptual and a technical level. The two key reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Principled Reframing of the SR Problem:** The paper provides a well-motivated and theoretically grounded framework for super-resolution based on amortized MAP inference. This approach directly targets the primary weakness of MSE-based methods—their tendency to produce blurry images—by instead optimizing for plausibility under the natural image prior.
2.  **Elegant Architectural Constraint and Effective Solution:** The proposed affine projection layer is an excellent method for enforcing input-output consistency, which elegantly transforms the SR problem into a generative modeling task. The subsequent application of GANs proves to be a highly effective way to solve this task, with empirical results that clearly demonstrate a substantial improvement in perceptual image quality.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's motivation is clear and compelling. The introductory example using a Swiss-roll distribution effectively illustrates why minimizing MSE or MAE can lead to low-probability outputs, while MAP inference correctly identifies a mode of the posterior. This provides strong intuition for the rest of the paper.

The main technical novelty, the affine projection layer, is well-conceived. It enforces the `x = Ay` constraint by construction, which is a more rigorous approach than the soft L1/L2 penalties used in concurrent work. The experiments in Section 5.2 compellingly show that this layer is not just a theoretical construct; it can accelerate training and improve final performance even when used with a conventional MSE loss.

The exploration of three different methods (GAN, denoiser-guided, density-guided) for optimizing the MAP objective is thorough. While the GAN-based approach was ultimately the most successful, the investigation of alternatives adds depth to the paper. The empirical results, particularly on the challenging grass texture and CelebA datasets, provide strong evidence for the central claim: that the proposed AffGAN method generates perceptually superior images that are sharp and rich in realistic detail. Finally, the theoretical connections drawn between GANs, KL-divergence, and (in the appendix) amortized variational inference provide welcome theoretical grounding for what is often a heuristic-driven field.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the core ideas are excellent, the paper could be strengthened with improvements to its presentation and analysis. These suggestions are intended to help improve the final version and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and, at times, difficult to follow. The theoretical development in Section 3 is crucial but could be explained more intuitively. A clearer roadmap at the beginning of the section, explicitly outlining how the MAP objective leads to the three proposed optimization strategies, would help guide the reader. The transition from Eq. (8) to Eq. (9), which recasts the problem in terms of cross-entropy, is a key step that would benefit from more detailed exposition.
*   **Analysis of Results:** The qualitative results for AffGAN are impressive. The paper would be even stronger with more analysis of the network's behavior. For instance, what types of textures or features does the generator learn to create? The "snake into water" example in Figure 5 is a fascinating failure case; a more systematic discussion of when and why the model hallucinates plausible but incorrect details would be highly valuable.
*   **Discussion of "Instance Noise":** The instance noise technique described in Appendix C appears to be a critical component for stabilizing training on complex datasets like ImageNet. Given the general difficulty of training GANs, this is a valuable contribution in its own right. I would suggest briefly mentioning it and its importance in the main body of the paper (perhaps in Section 3.2), rather than leaving it entirely to the appendix.
*   A final proofread for minor grammatical errors and typos would also be beneficial to polish the manuscript.

### Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods were found to produce blurry results on the grass texture dataset and were not explored further. Could you provide more insight into why these methods failed? Was the primary issue related to optimization instability, or do you believe there is a more fundamental limitation in using these particular gradient/likelihood estimators for this generative task?
2.  How sensitive is the AffGAN model to the hyperparameters of the instance noise, particularly its annealing schedule? Your success on ImageNet is encouraging, and it would be helpful to understand how much tuning was required to achieve stable training.
3.  The appendix establishes an exciting connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortized variational inference. Could you comment on the potential practical benefits of implementing this stochastic version? Do you hypothesize that sampling from the learned posterior `q(y|x)` would help address the "atypical mode" problem you raise in Section 5.6 and produce more diverse, yet still plausible, reconstructions?
