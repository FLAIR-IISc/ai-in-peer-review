## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that existing methods based on empirical risk minimization (e.g., MSE loss) often produce blurry results due to their averaging behavior, and propose a novel approach that leverages neural networks to directly estimate the MAP solution while maintaining consistency between low- and high-resolution images via an affine projection. They explore three optimization strategies – GANs, denoising-guided SR, and a density-model approach – to solve this amortised MAP inference problem, and demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real image datasets.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and explores a compelling approach to SR, the experimental results do not demonstrate a substantial or consistent improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods, and the presentation suffers from clarity issues that hinder full evaluation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of formulating SR as an amortised MAP inference problem is well-motivated and addresses a known limitation of traditional MSE-based approaches. The introduction of the affine projection layer is a clever way to enforce consistency between the LR and HR images, reducing the ill-posed nature of the SR problem. The connection drawn between GANs and amortised variational inference is also a valuable contribution.

However, the experimental evaluation, while extensive, is not fully convincing. While the authors show improvements on a synthetic Swiss-roll dataset and demonstrate the theoretical correctness of the affine projection, the performance gains on real image datasets (CelebA and ImageNet) are often marginal and not consistently superior to existing methods, particularly when considering standard metrics like PSNR and SSIM. The visual examples, while showing some improvement in perceptual quality with the GAN-based approach, are not always dramatically different from those produced by other recent SR methods. The paper focuses heavily on the AffGAN approach, and the results from AffDG and AffLL are less thoroughly explored, weakening the claim of a universally beneficial approach.

Furthermore, the paper is densely written and, at times, difficult to follow. The theoretical explanations are thorough, but could benefit from more intuitive explanations and visual aids. The notation is heavy, and the flow between sections feels disjointed. This makes it challenging to fully grasp the subtleties of the proposed methods and critically assess their effectiveness.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper would benefit significantly from a more streamlined presentation. Consider adding more visual explanations, breaking down complex equations, and improving the overall narrative flow.
* **Comparative Evaluation:** A more direct and comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, SRGAN) is needed, with a clear demonstration of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach. Providing a fair comparison with robust statistical significance testing would strengthen the claims.
* **Ablation Studies:** Investigate the individual contributions of different components of the proposed method, such as the affine projection layer and the specific optimization strategy employed. Which aspects are crucial for performance, and which are less impactful?
* **Feature Visualization:** Provide some analysis of the learned features within the network. What kind of patterns is the network learning to extract from the LR images? This could provide insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** While Section 5.6 briefly touches upon limitations, a more in-depth discussion of potential failure cases and future research directions would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* The paper mentions instance noise as a stabilization technique for GAN training. What is the sensitivity of the results to the annealing schedule of the noise? Were other noise distributions considered?
* In the CelebA experiments, the GAN models exhibit color drifting. Is this a known issue with the GAN formulation, and are there any strategies to mitigate it?
* Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the AffDG and AffLL models did not perform as well as the AffGAN model on the grass texture dataset? What are the potential bottlenecks in those approaches?
* The connection to variational inference is interesting. Have you explored any specific benefits of leveraging this connection for improved sampling or uncertainty estimation in the SR task?
