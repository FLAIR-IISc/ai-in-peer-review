This paper introduces a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference, coupled with a pre-learned affine projection to enforce low-resolution (LR) consistency. The authors further propose three distinct approaches for cross-entropy minimization. Overall, this is a strong contribution. However, I have several points for consideration:

1) The proposed amortized MAP inference represents a departure from existing SR techniques and, when integrated with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), yields compelling results. While building upon GAN-based SR methods like Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network, it would be beneficial to clearly articulate the specific advantages and contributions of this new formulation relative to the current state-of-the-art.

2) The use of an affine projection layer as a constraint allows training without paired high-resolution (HR) and LR images. However, the training of this projection layer itself requires {HR, LR} pairs. Clarification is needed on whether the training process for the affine projection is essentially a transfer of existing paired training procedures.

3) The paper presents extensive results on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a more direct comparison with previous SR work, I recommend including results on standard benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100.

4) The presented results for natural images are limited to a resolution of 128x128. Investigating performance on larger images is important, given that SR algorithms often encounter inputs of arbitrary size in practical applications.

5) Traditional GANs utilize a noise vector to define a latent space and represent learning a distribution. Have the authors explored the inclusion of such a noise vector within their framework?

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for SR, demonstrating a strong level of exploration and innovation. While questions remain regarding the necessity of certain components and further experimentation is warranted, I believe this work has the potential to significantly inspire future research in the field.
