## Review of “Amortised MAP Estimation for Super-Resolution”

This paper presents a novel approach to super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation problem. The core idea is to learn a neural network that projects SR solutions onto an affine subspace constrained by consistency with a lower-resolution (LR) reconstruction method. This learned subspace allows for the application of various optimization techniques – including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), noise-assisted optimization, and density-assisted optimization – to refine the SR output and obtain more plausible results. 

The proposed method demonstrates promising results on several benchmark datasets, showcasing its ability to generate visually compelling high-resolution images. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field, although improvements in clarity of writing and presentation would significantly enhance its impact.

The motivation for this work is well-founded. Traditional SR methods often struggle with ill-posedness, leading to multiple possible solutions. The authors effectively address this by incorporating a prior learned through the neural network, guiding the optimization process towards more realistic and consistent outputs. The strength of the approach lies in its flexibility; the learned subspace isn’t tied to a specific optimization method, allowing integration with diverse techniques like GANs, which are known for generating high-quality, yet sometimes unstable, results. The ability to combine these approaches within a unified MAP framework is a compelling aspect of this work.

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues that hinder full comprehension. The description of the affine subspace and its relationship to the LR reconstruction consistency constraint is somewhat opaque. Expanding on this with a more intuitive explanation and perhaps a visual representation would be beneficial. The connection between the different optimization methods (GANs, noise-assisted, density-assisted) and the learned subspace isn't always clearly articulated. While each is mentioned, a more detailed discussion of *how* they leverage the learned prior would strengthen the paper.  For example, is the GAN discriminator operating on the projected output? How does noise-assisted optimization interact with the subspace constraint?

The experimental results are well-presented, with clear comparisons to existing SR techniques. The qualitative results are particularly strong, demonstrating the visual improvements achieved by the proposed method. However, the quantitative evaluation could be strengthened by including a wider range of metrics beyond PSNR and SSIM. Metrics that assess perceptual quality, such as LPIPS, might better reflect the visual improvements observed in the examples. Furthermore, ablation studies exploring the contribution of each component – the affine subspace, the chosen optimization method, and the LR consistency constraint – would provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach. 

A significant area for future work, and a point I particularly encourage the authors to address, is analysis of the learned neural network. What features does the network learn to represent the affine subspace? Visualizing the weights or activations of the network could offer insights into the types of SR solutions it favors and the nature of the learned prior. Understanding these learned representations would not only deepen our understanding of the method’s inner workings but also potentially reveal opportunities for further improvement. Investigating the robustness of the learned subspace to different types of LR degradation (e.g., varying levels of noise, blur) would also be interesting.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel approach to SR as amortized MAP estimation.
*   Flexibility in integrating various optimization techniques.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating visual improvements.
*   Well-motivated problem and solution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of clarity in the description of the affine subspace and its constraints.
*   Insufficient explanation of how different optimization methods leverage the learned prior.
*   Limited analysis of the learned neural network and its internal representations.
*   Quantitative evaluation could be expanded with perceptual metrics.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the clarity issues, this paper presents a promising and potentially impactful contribution to the field of super-resolution. The core idea of learning an affine subspace to guide SR optimization is novel and well-motivated. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding clarity and analysis, would significantly strengthen the paper and make it more accessible to a wider audience. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions focusing on improved clarity, more detailed explanations of the method's components, and additional analysis of the learned network.
