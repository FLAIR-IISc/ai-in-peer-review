This paper proposes a novel approach to super-resolution (SR) by framing it as amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation. A key contribution is a projection step designed to enforce consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and their high-resolution (HR) counterparts. The authors then explore three methods – based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoising, and density modeling – to minimize the resulting cross-entropy problem.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach, supported by thorough experimentation. The results demonstrate a clear improvement in perceptual quality, particularly with the GAN-based method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper’s core idea – moving beyond empirical risk minimization (like MSE) towards MAP inference – is strongly justified by the inherent ambiguity of the SR problem and the limitations of pixel-wise loss functions. The introduction effectively explains why standard approaches often yield blurry results. The proposed affine projection is a clever technique to constrain the solution space and ensure consistency between LR and HR images. 

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The 2D Swiss-roll example provides a clear illustration of the benefits of MAP inference. The results on both texture and real-world image datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the AffGAN model, which consistently produces visually appealing results. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing AffGAN and SoftGAN) are informative and help isolate the impact of the affine projection. The connection established between AffGAN and amortized variational inference is a significant theoretical contribution.

**Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, the paper occasionally uses dense notation. Defining key terms and variables more explicitly could improve readability.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper briefly acknowledges potential limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6), but expanding on these would be valuable. For instance, a more detailed discussion of the challenges associated with accurately modeling the image prior would be helpful.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares favorably to existing methods, a more detailed comparison to the very latest SR techniques (beyond those cited) would strengthen the argument. Specifically, recent transformer-based SR models are showing impressive results.
*   **Instance Noise Details:** The section on instance noise (Section 3.2.1) is quite technical. A more intuitive explanation of *why* this technique stabilizes training would be beneficial.
*   **Typos/Proofreading:** While generally well-written, a final proofread to eliminate minor typos and grammatical errors would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   In Section 5.6, you mention that the MAP estimate may not be unique due to transformations. How sensitive are your results to the choice of image representation (e.g., different color spaces)?
*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the affine projection step? Does it significantly increase the training time or memory requirements?
*   You mention that AffGAN sometimes produces high-frequency noise. Have you explored techniques to mitigate this issue, such as post-processing or modifications to the GAN architecture?
*   Regarding instance noise, what is the sensitivity of the results to the annealing schedule and the initial noise level?
*   While you establish a connection to variational inference, are there any plans to directly leverage variational techniques (e.g., using a variational lower bound) for training the model?
