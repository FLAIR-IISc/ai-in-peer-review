This paper introduces a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is to leverage a convolutional neural network architecture with an affine projection layer, ensuring consistency between low- and high-resolution images, and then employ different techniques – Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser guidance, and a maximum-likelihood trained prior – to approximate the MAP estimate. The work is well-motivated, aiming to address the limitations of empirical risk minimization approaches (like MSE) which often produce blurry results by averaging over plausible solutions. The proposed approach is a promising direction for generating more perceptually realistic SR outputs.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach, concerns regarding the limited scope of evaluation and the incremental nature of the contribution relative to existing GAN-based SR methods lead me to recommend rejection. The experimental validation, while extensive, lacks comparison to standard benchmarks and exploration of scalability.

My primary concern centers on the empirical validation. The experiments demonstrate promising results, particularly with the GAN-based approach (AffGAN), but the lack of comparison to established SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) makes it difficult to assess the true performance improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods. While the authors acknowledge related GAN-based work (Ledig et al., 2016), a direct quantitative comparison is crucial to establish the advantages of the proposed affine projection and amortized MAP inference framework. The results on ImageNet are presented at a relatively low resolution (128x128); demonstrating performance on larger, more realistic images is important.

Secondly, while the paper argues that the affine projection eliminates the need for {HR, LR} pairs for *training the generative model*, the initial training of the affine projection layer *itself* requires paired data, as highlighted in the original review. Clarification is needed on the extent to which this initial training impacts the overall unsupervised learning claim. The connection to variational inference (established in the Appendix) is interesting, but further exploration and empirical validation in that context would be beneficial.

Finally, the exploration of the latent space with a noise vector (as with standard GANs) is a worthwhile consideration, and the absence of this element raises a question about the full potential of the proposed GAN-based approach.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Benchmarking:** Please include quantitative results on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) using commonly reported metrics (PSNR, SSIM).
*   **Scalability:** Investigate the performance of the proposed framework on larger images to demonstrate its scalability and practical applicability.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study to isolate the contributions of the affine projection and the different optimization methods (GAN, denoiser guidance, density model).
*   **Noise Vector:** Explore the inclusion of a noise vector as input to the generator (similar to standard GANs) to enable exploration of the latent space and potentially improve the diversity of generated outputs.
*   **Clarify Affine Projection Training:** Provide more detail on the training procedure for the affine projection layer. Does this initial training step significantly impact the subsequent unsupervised learning phase? How sensitive is the performance to the quality of this initial training?
*   **Theoretical Connection to VAEs:** Expand on the connection to variational autoencoders established in Appendix F. Could the stochastic extension of AffGAN be further explored and evaluated as a novel VAE variant?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a quantitative comparison of AffGAN against state-of-the-art SR methods like EDSR or SRGAN on standard benchmark datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100)?
*   How does the performance of AffGAN scale with increasing image size? Are there any limitations or challenges associated with applying this framework to very high-resolution images?
*   What is the sensitivity of the AffGAN performance to the initialization and training of the affine projection layer?
*   Have you explored the inclusion of a noise vector as input to the generator, and if so, what impact did it have on the results?
*   Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference? Are there any advantages to this perspective?
