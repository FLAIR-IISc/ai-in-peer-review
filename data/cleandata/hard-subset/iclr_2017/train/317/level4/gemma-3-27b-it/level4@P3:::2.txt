## Review of AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

This paper argues for approaching Super-Resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation. A key contribution is a projection step designed to ensure consistency between low-resolution (LR) and high-resolution (HR) dependencies. The authors experimentally verify this projection and then propose and test three distinct methods to solve the resulting cross-entropy problem outlined in Eq. 9.

**Summary:** This is a very good paper, well-written and clearly presented. The experimental results are sufficient to support the claims, and the inclusion of both a well-chosen toy example and real-world applications strengthens the evaluation. From my understanding, the core contributions regarding the projection and its application to different optimization strategies (Sections 3.2, 3.3, and 3.4) are novel. While some techniques for GAN training may have appeared in variations elsewhere (as the authors acknowledge), the integration into an amortised MAP inference framework for SR appears to be a significant advance. I believe this paper will be relevant to future work in super-resolution, and the finding that GAN-based models yield the most visually appealing results suggests a promising direction for further research.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to super-resolution, supported by convincing experimental results. The theoretical grounding and clear explanation of the proposed methods are particularly strong.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper tackles a fundamental issue in SR – the limitations of pixel-wise loss functions in capturing perceptual quality. Framing SR as an amortised MAP inference problem is a conceptually sound and well-motivated approach. The introduction of the affine projection layer to enforce consistency between LR and HR images is a clever and effective solution.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The three proposed methods (GAN-based, denoiser-guided, and density-guided) provide a comprehensive exploration of how to leverage the amortised MAP framework. The thorough experimental evaluation across different datasets and loss functions demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach, particularly the GAN-based method.
*   **Strong Experimental Validation:** The results on both the 2D Swiss-roll dataset and the natural image datasets are compelling. The quantitative results (Table 1, Table 2) and qualitative examples (Figures 3-5) clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed methods over traditional approaches. The ablation studies (Figure 2) provide valuable insights into the importance of the affine projection.
*   **Theoretical Connections:** Establishing the connection between the proposed AffGAN and amortised variational inference (Appendix F) adds significant depth and theoretical grounding to the work.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Typos and Grammar:** While generally well-written, a final proofread could address minor typos and grammatical inconsistencies.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While Section 5.6 touches on limitations, expanding this discussion would be beneficial. For example, elaborating on the computational cost of the affine projection and its potential impact on scalability could be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, noise levels) would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares to relevant baselines, a more direct comparison to the very latest state-of-the-art SR methods would further solidify the contribution.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   In the AffGAN implementation, how sensitive is the performance to the choice of instance noise annealing schedule? Could you elaborate on the heuristic used for adjusting the noise level during training?
*   The paper mentions the potential for the MAP estimate to be atypical in high dimensions (Section 5.6).  Could you explore whether incorporating techniques from variational inference (e.g., KL divergence regularization) might help address this issue and improve the perceptual quality of the generated images?
*   The computational cost of the affine projection is not explicitly addressed. Could you provide some insights into the overhead introduced by this step and how it might affect the scalability of the method to higher-resolution images?
*   You mention that the density model used for AffLL doesn't provide a sufficiently accurate representation of pY. What specific aspects of the density model's performance limit its effectiveness, and what potential improvements could be explored?
