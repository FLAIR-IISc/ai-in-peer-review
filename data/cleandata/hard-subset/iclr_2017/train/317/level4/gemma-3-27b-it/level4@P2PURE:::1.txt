The paper introduces a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference, employing a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency between high- and low-resolution outputs. It also proposes three distinct approaches for minimizing cross-entropy. Overall, this is a strong paper; however, several points warrant further clarification.

1) The proposed amortized MAP inference represents a departure from existing SR techniques and, when combined with a GAN, achieves compelling results. However, given the recent advances in GAN-based SR – such as Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network – it is unclear what specific advantages this new formulation offers over the current state-of-the-art.

2) The use of an affine projection architecture allows the model to be trained without paired {HR, LR} image data. However, the training of the affine projection layer itself still requires these paired examples. Does this simply represent a transfer of the standard training procedure to the affine projection layer?

3) The paper presents extensive results, including those obtained on natural images from ImageNet. To enable a fair comparison with prior work, could the authors also provide results on standard SR benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100?

4) The presented results for natural images are limited to a resolution of 128x128. Given that SR algorithms often encounter inputs of arbitrary size, can this framework effectively handle larger images?

5) Typical GANs utilize a noise vector to define a latent space, which better represents learning a distribution. Did the authors explore incorporating a noise vector into their framework?

In summary, this paper presents a new and theoretically sound framework for SR, with a novel idea and comprehensive exploration of different methods. While questions remain regarding its necessity and further experimentation is needed, this work has the potential to inspire further research in the field.
