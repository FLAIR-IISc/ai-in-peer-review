The paper presents an amortised MAP estimation method for image super-resolution (SR). By learning a neural network that projects solutions to an affine subspace consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input, the method enables finding plausible high-resolution (HR) solutions using various approaches: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser-guided optimisation, and density-guided optimisation. Results are demonstrated on several datasets.

**Decision: Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem in SR – the generation of more plausible and less blurry results – and proposes a novel and theoretically grounded approach. The experimental results support the claims, showing improved performance, particularly with the GAN-based method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of amortised MAP inference is well-motivated, addressing the limitations of empirical risk minimisation with losses like MSE. The affine projection constraint is a clever way to enforce consistency between LR and HR images and elegantly reduces the problem to minimizing a cross-entropy. The analysis connecting AffGAN to amortised variational inference is a valuable contribution, highlighting the broader implications of the work. The ablation studies (e.g., Table 1 and the comparison of AffGAN vs. SoftGAN) provide convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed affine projection. The results on the Swiss-roll toy problem clearly illustrate the benefits of MAP inference over MSE, and the experiments on real images demonstrate the practical impact of the approach.

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

While the technical content is strong, the writing could be refined for clarity and readability. The paper is dense and occasionally difficult to follow, particularly in the theoretical sections. Consider breaking down complex sentences and adding more intuitive explanations. 

*   **Introduction:** The motivation could be strengthened by providing more concrete examples of the perceptual shortcomings of existing SR methods.
*   **Theory Section:** The mathematical notation is correct, but the explanations could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key equations. For example, explaining the significance of the affine projection more visually, alongside the equation (7), would be helpful.
*   **Experiments Section:** The experimental setup could be more clearly described. Providing more details about the network architectures used in each experiment would improve reproducibility. More detailed feature analysis of the learned representations would be welcome.
*   **Discussion:** While the paper acknowledges potential limitations of the MAP approach, a more in-depth discussion of these limitations and potential future directions would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the potential for the MAP estimate to be atypical (Section 5.6). Could you elaborate on how this might manifest in practice, and what strategies could be employed to mitigate this issue?
2.  In the instance noise experiment (Section 3.2.1), what criteria were used for selecting the annealing schedule for the noise level? Were different schedules explored, and if so, what were the results?
3.  The density-guided approach (AffLL) seems to have shown less promising results than AffGAN. Was the performance limited by the capacity of the density model, or were there other factors at play? Could a more powerful density model potentially improve the results?
4.  How sensitive is the affine projection to the accuracy of the downsampling kernel A? Would using a slightly inaccurate A significantly affect the performance of the proposed methods?
