This paper argues for approaching Super-Resolution as amortised MAP estimation. A projection step is proposed to ensure consistency between high- and low-resolution dependencies, and is experimentally verified to yield improved results. Furthermore, three different methods for solving the resulting cross-entropy problem (Eq. 9) are proposed and tested.

Summary: This is a very good paper, well-written and presented. The experimental results are sufficient, and the paper includes well-chosen toy examples and real-world applications. To the best of my understanding, the contributions to the field of super-resolution are novel (Sections 3.2, 3.3, and 3.4). While certain aspects relating to GAN training may have appeared in various forms previously (as also discussed in the paper), I believe this work will be relevant to future research on super-resolution, and the finding that GAN-based model training yields the most visually appealing results suggests further investigation in this area.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel approach to super-resolution with a strong theoretical foundation and compelling experimental results. The method addresses a key limitation of existing approaches (blurriness due to MSE loss) and offers a promising alternative.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Soundness:** The paper introduces a compelling framework for super-resolution based on amortised MAP inference. The affine projection layer is a clever design choice that enforces consistency between LR and HR images and simplifies the problem formulation. The connection drawn between GANs and variational inference is also a valuable contribution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods through comprehensive experiments. The 2D Swiss-roll example provides a clear illustration of the benefits of MAP inference. The results on grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet demonstrate the practical applicability of the approach, particularly the superior performance of the GAN-based method (AffGAN).
3.  **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is remarkably well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear motivation for their work, thoroughly explain their methods, and present their results in a concise and understandable manner.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting decision):**

*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper briefly touches upon limitations in Section 5.6, a more in-depth discussion of potential drawbacks and future research directions would be beneficial. For example, the computational cost of training the GAN-based models and the sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning could be addressed.
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares different methods, more detailed ablation studies could help isolate the impact of individual components (e.g., the affine projection layer, instance noise).
*   **Clarify Log-likelihood for AffLL:** It is mentioned that the density model used for AffLL does not provide accurate enough density estimation, leading to divergence. Providing more detail on this and potential alternatives would be valuable.
*   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** A final careful proofread would be helpful to address minor grammatical errors and typos. (e.g., "Eqn." should be consistent as "Equation", "Section 5.6" appears to be missing in one instance).

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding the limitations discussed in Section 5.6, could you elaborate on how the choice of representation might affect the MAP estimate and whether there are strategies to mitigate this issue?
*   The AffLL model seemed to underperform. Could you elaborate on the challenges in achieving accurate density estimation for natural images and whether exploring alternative density models (e.g., normalizing flows) might be a fruitful avenue for future research?
*   The instance noise technique seems effective in stabilising GAN training. Is there a principled way to determine the optimal annealing schedule for the noise level, or is it largely based on empirical experimentation?
*   Could you provide some insight into the computational resources (e.g., GPUs, training time) required to train the AffGAN model on the ImageNet dataset?
