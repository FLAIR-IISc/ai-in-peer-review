This paper introduces a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference, coupled with a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency between low-resolution (LR) and high-resolution (HR) outputs. The authors also explore three distinct methods for minimizing cross-entropy loss. Overall, this is a strong contribution with a promising approach. However, I have several points for the authors to address.

First, the proposed amortized MAP inference represents a departure from conventional SR techniques. When combined with a generative adversarial network (GAN), the framework achieves compelling and visually plausible results. However, given the existing landscape of GAN-based SR methods, particularly SRGAN (Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network), it would be beneficial to clearly articulate the specific advantages and novel contributions of this new formulation relative to the current state-of-the-art. What specific improvements does this approach offer?

Second, the utilization of an affine projection layer as a constraint allows for training without requiring paired {HR, LR} images *during the SR reconstruction phase*. However, the paper states that {HR, LR} pairs *are* required to train the affine projection layer itself. This raises a question: is the affine projection layer training simply a transfer learning step, leveraging existing paired data to establish the necessary mapping before the unsupervised SR training begins? Clarification on this point would be helpful.

Third, the paper presents extensive results on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a more rigorous and fair comparison with existing SR methodologies, I recommend including quantitative results on standard benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100. Reporting performance on these commonly used datasets is crucial for establishing the framework's effectiveness within the broader SR research community.

Fourth, the presented results for natural images are currently limited to a resolution of 128x128. While this provides initial validation, it is important to investigate the performance of the framework on larger images. Real-world SR applications often involve inputs of arbitrary size, and demonstrating scalability is essential. Can the framework maintain performance with larger input images, and are there any computational limitations that arise?

Finally, traditional GANs incorporate a noise vector as a latent space, enabling the model to represent and learn a distribution. Did the authors consider incorporating a similar noise vector into their framework? Exploring this option could potentially improve the diversity and realism of the generated high-resolution images.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for SR, and the authors demonstrate a commendable effort in exploring various components and techniques. While questions remain regarding the necessity of the affine projection training procedure and a more thorough experimental evaluation is needed, I believe this work has the potential to inspire significant advancements in the field of single image super-resolution.
