## Review of “Amortized MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution”

This paper presents a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. A key component is the introduction of a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency between low-resolution (LR) and high-resolution (HR) outputs. The authors further explore three distinct approaches for minimizing cross-entropy within this framework. Overall, this is a strong contribution with a promising approach, though some aspects require further clarification and investigation.

The proposed amortized MAP inference represents a departure from traditional SR methods and, when combined with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), yields visually plausible and compelling results. However, in the context of recent advancements, particularly Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network (SRGAN), it’s crucial to articulate the specific advantages and contributions of this new formulation. What novel capabilities or improvements does this framework offer beyond existing state-of-the-art GAN-based SR techniques? A more detailed comparative analysis addressing this point would significantly strengthen the paper.

The use of an affine projection architecture to constrain the model is a clever design choice, enabling training without requiring paired {HR, LR} images for the core SR network. However, the authors acknowledge that training the affine projection layer *does* require such paired data. This raises a question: is the process of learning the affine projection simply a transfer of the traditional paired training procedure to a separate, preliminary step? Clarifying the role and impact of this affine projection training is essential. Specifically, demonstrating its contribution to the overall performance gain, beyond what might be achieved through standard paired training, would be beneficial.

The paper presents extensive results on natural images from ImageNet, which is encouraging. However, to facilitate a fair and direct comparison with previous SR work, the authors should include results on standard benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100. Reporting performance metrics on these datasets is critical for assessing the framework’s competitiveness within the SR research community.

The currently presented results are limited to 128x128 images. While this provides an initial demonstration, it’s important to evaluate the framework's scalability and performance on larger images. SR algorithms are often applied to inputs of arbitrary size, and the ability to handle higher resolutions effectively is a crucial consideration. Exploring the performance of this framework on larger images would address this practical concern.

Finally, traditional GANs typically employ a noise vector as a latent space representation, allowing for a better characterization of the learned distribution. Did the authors consider incorporating such a noise vector into their framework? If so, what were the results? If not, a brief justification for its omission would be helpful. Exploring the impact of a latent noise vector could potentially improve the diversity and realism of the generated super-resolution images.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for SR. The core idea is innovative, and the authors demonstrate a commendable exploration of different training methodologies. While questions remain regarding the advantages over existing approaches and the need for more comprehensive experimentation (particularly on standard benchmarks and larger image sizes), the potential impact of this work is substantial. This research offers valuable insights and should provide strong inspiration for future work in the field of image super-resolution. I believe this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the community.
