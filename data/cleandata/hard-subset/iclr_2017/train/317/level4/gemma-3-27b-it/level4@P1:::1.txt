Image super-resolution (SR) is an underdetermined inverse problem, and this paper presents a novel framework for addressing it: amortized MAP inference. The authors introduce an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between high-resolution (HR) outputs and low-resolution (LR) inputs, and propose three methods—based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoising, and density models—to minimize cross-entropy. Overall, this is a strong paper with a promising approach. However, I have several comments and questions.

1) The proposed amortized MAP inference is a novel and distinct approach compared to previous SR methods. When combined with a GAN, it achieves plausible and visually appealing results. A natural question arises regarding the added value of this new formulation relative to state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods like Ledig et al.’s work. What specific advantages does this approach offer?

2) By utilizing an affine projection architecture as a constraint, the model seemingly reduces the need for paired {HR, LR} image data during training. However, the training of the affine projection layer itself requires these paired images. Does this mean the training procedure for the affine projection merely shifts the reliance on paired data to this specific layer?

3) The paper presents extensive results, including those on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a fairer comparison with prior work, could the authors also provide results on standard SR benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100?

4) The reported results for natural images are limited to a size of 128x128. Can this framework effectively handle images of larger sizes? SR algorithms often encounter inputs of arbitrary dimensions.

5) Traditional GANs employ a noise vector as a latent space, which aids in representing and learning a distribution. Did the authors explore the incorporation of such a noise vector into their framework?

Overall, this paper provides a new framework for SR with robust theoretical analysis. The idea is novel, and the authors explore a variety of methods. While questions remain regarding the necessity of certain components and further experimentation is needed, I believe this work will provide valuable inspiration to the research community.

**Decision:** Accept. The approach is novel, theoretically sound, and demonstrates promising results. While further validation on standard datasets and clarification of certain implementation details would be beneficial, the core contribution is significant and warrants acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a significant problem in image processing—super-resolution—with a fresh perspective. The concept of amortized MAP inference, coupled with the affine projection constraint, offers a compelling alternative to existing empirical risk minimization approaches. The three proposed methods (GAN, denoising, and density modeling) showcase the versatility of the framework. The theoretical grounding provided for using GANs in SR is particularly valuable. The experimental results, while needing expansion to include standard datasets, are encouraging. The connection established between AffGAN and variational inference is a noteworthy theoretical contribution.

**Additional Feedback (for paper improvement):**

*   **Clarify the advantages over existing GAN-based SR methods:** A more direct comparison, outlining the specific benefits of this approach (e.g., improved stability, better perceptual quality, faster convergence) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Elaborate on the training of the affine projection layer:** A detailed description of the training process for this layer, and how it influences the overall framework, would be helpful.
*   **Include standard benchmark datasets:** Adding results on Set5, Set14, and BSD100 will allow for a more comprehensive and objective evaluation of the proposed methods.
*   **Investigate performance on larger images:** Demonstrating the scalability of the framework to higher resolutions is important for practical applicability.
*   **Explore the use of a noise vector:** Investigating the incorporation of a latent noise vector could potentially improve the diversity and realism of the generated HR images.
*   **Consider the limitations of MAP inference:** The authors briefly mention limitations of MAP inference in the conclusion. Expanding on these limitations and discussing potential future directions to address them would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific advantages of your framework compared to existing state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods, such as Ledig et al.'s work?
2.  Can you provide more details on the training process for the affine projection layer? How does the training of this layer interact with the training of the rest of the network?
3.  What were the primary challenges encountered when attempting to scale the framework to larger image sizes, and what potential solutions are being considered?
4.  Have you explored the incorporation of a latent noise vector into the AffGAN model? If so, what were the results?
5.   Given your discussion of potential limitations of MAP inference, what future research directions do you envision to address these challenges?
