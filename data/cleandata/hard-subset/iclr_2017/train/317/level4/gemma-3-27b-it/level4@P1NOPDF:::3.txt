The paper presents an amortized MAP estimation method for super-resolution (SR) problems. By learning a neural network that projects solutions onto an affine subspace of plausible SR outputs – those consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input – the method enables the discovery of good solutions using a variety of optimization techniques, including GANs, noise-assisted optimization, and density-assisted optimization.

Results are nicely demonstrated on several datasets.

Overall, I like this paper, though I believe the writing could be significantly polished and the presentation clarified. I found it difficult to follow at times, and given the complexity of the subject matter, increased clarity would be highly beneficial. Additionally, I would appreciate more analysis of the learned networks – specifically, what kinds of features do they learn?

The core idea of constraining the solution space to an affine subspace is interesting and potentially impactful. The authors successfully leverage this constraint with different optimization strategies, showcasing the flexibility of their approach. The experimental results appear convincing and demonstrate the method's effectiveness across different datasets. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of detailed explanation in several areas. For example, the connection between the affine subspace and the LR input isn’t immediately obvious and requires a more thorough discussion. How is the subspace defined, and why is this particular definition appropriate for SR? A visual example illustrating the projection onto the subspace might be very helpful.

Furthermore, the motivation for using an amortized MAP estimation is not fully explained. While the benefits of speed and scalability are implied, a more explicit comparison to traditional MAP estimation methods would strengthen the argument. How does this approach compare in terms of solution quality and computational cost?

Regarding the experiments, while the quantitative results are good, a more detailed ablation study would be beneficial. For example, how does performance change when using different optimization techniques *without* the affine subspace constraint? This would isolate the contribution of the proposed method more effectively.  Also, it would be valuable to see results on a wider range of SR factors (e.g., x8) to further assess the generalizability of the approach.

Finally, the discussion of the learned features is a missed opportunity. Understanding what the network learns to represent could provide valuable insights into the underlying SR process and potentially lead to further improvements. Visualizing feature maps or conducting feature ablation studies could reveal important information about the network's behavior.



In summary, this is a promising paper with a novel approach to SR. Addressing the clarity issues and providing more detailed analysis, particularly regarding the learned features and the benefits of the amortized MAP estimation, would significantly strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
