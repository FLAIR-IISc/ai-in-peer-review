The paper presents a novel framework for solving the single image super-resolution (SR) problem, based on amortized MAP inference, and employs a pre-learned affine projection layer to ensure consistency between the high-resolution (HR) output and the low-resolution (LR) input. It also proposes three distinct methods for minimizing cross-entropy. Overall, this is a strong paper. However, I have several comments and questions for the authors.

1) The proposed amortized MAP inference is a novel approach, differentiating it from previous SR methods. When combined with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), this framework achieves plausible and compelling results. However, in comparison to other GAN-based SR methods, such as Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network, it’s unclear what this new formulation adds to the current state-of-the-art. A clearer articulation of the advantages over existing GAN approaches would be beneficial.

2) The use of an affine projection architecture as a constraint allows the model to be trained without requiring paired {HR, LR} images. However, the training of the affine projection layer itself *does* require these paired images. Does this simply mean the training procedure for the affine projection layer is a transfer of existing HR/LR training methods? Clarification on this point would be helpful.

3) The paper presents extensive results, including those for natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a fair comparison with prior work in the field, could the authors also provide results on standard SR benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100? 

4) The presented results for natural images are limited to a size of 128x128. Can this framework effectively handle images of larger sizes? SR methods often encounter inputs of arbitrary size, and performance at higher resolutions is an important consideration.

5) A typical GAN incorporates a noise term representing a latent space, allowing it to be interpreted as learning a distribution. Did the authors explore the use of a noise vector as input to their generator? Including such a vector might improve the diversity and realism of the generated high-resolution images.

Overall, this paper introduces a new and promising framework for SR, supported by solid theoretical analysis. The idea is innovative, and the authors explore a range of methods. While questions remain regarding the necessity of the proposed formulation and further experimentation is warranted, I believe this work will provide valuable inspiration to the SR community.
