This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by proposing an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference approach. The key idea is to learn a convolutional neural network that projects solutions to the affine subspace of valid SR outputs, ensuring consistency with the low-resolution input. This allows the authors to formulate SR as minimizing the cross-entropy between the model’s output distribution and the true image prior, enabling training using techniques like Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoising-guided optimization, and density models.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form. While the core idea is promising and the experimental results demonstrate improvements, the paper suffers from significant clarity and presentation issues that hinder understanding and evaluation. The theoretical contributions, while interesting, are buried in dense text and require substantial effort to decipher.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of clarity in the presentation. The paper is densely written and jumps between theoretical justifications and experimental details without sufficient explanation. Key concepts, like the affine projection and its benefits, are introduced but not thoroughly explained with intuitive examples beyond the initial Swiss-roll illustration. The connections between the different proposed methods (GAN, denoising, density modeling) are not clearly articulated, and their relative strengths and weaknesses are not adequately discussed. The theoretical motivation feels disconnected from the practical implementation.

A second reason is the limited analysis of the learned models. While the paper presents quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM, cross-entropy), it lacks a qualitative analysis of *what* the network is learning. Visualizing the learned filters, activation maps, or performing ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components would strengthen the paper. The analysis focuses heavily on quantitative metrics, overlooking the perceptual benefits expected from a MAP-based approach.  It’s also unclear how sensitive the results are to hyperparameters; a more comprehensive ablation study would address this.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents a novel approach with potential. The affine projection concept is intriguing, and the GAN-based results are encouraging. 

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Rewrite for Clarity:** Substantially revise the paper for clarity and readability. Break down complex concepts into smaller, more digestible pieces. Use more illustrative examples.
*   **Strengthen the Connection Between Theory and Practice:** Explain more explicitly how the theoretical motivations translate into the practical implementation of the proposed methods.
*   **Provide a More Thorough Analysis:** Include a qualitative analysis of the learned features and activation maps. Perform ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different components.
*   **Clarify the Advantages of Each Method:** Provide a clearer comparison of the GAN, denoising, and density-guided approaches, outlining their respective strengths and weaknesses.
*   **Expand on the Discussion of Limitations:** The paper briefly mentions potential limitations of MAP inference. Expand on this discussion, addressing potential drawbacks and areas for future research.
*   **Address Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss how sensitive the results are to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Visualizations:** Include more visualizations of the results, including examples of the affine projection in action, and comparisons between different SR methods.

I have several questions for the authors:

*   Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the affine projection, perhaps with a simple analogy or visual illustration beyond the Swiss-roll example?
*   What is the computational overhead of incorporating the affine projection into the network architecture?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the noise distribution and annealing schedule in the denoising-guided approach?
*   Why did the AffDG and AffLL models perform poorly on the grass texture dataset?
*   Could you elaborate on the potential drawbacks of MAP inference and how they might be addressed in future work?
*   How does the performance of your method compare to state-of-the-art SR methods that do *not* use GANs, but instead rely on perceptual loss functions (e.g., those based on SSIM)?
