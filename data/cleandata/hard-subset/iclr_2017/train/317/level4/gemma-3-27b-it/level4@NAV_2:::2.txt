This paper argues for framing Super-Resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation. A novel projection step is proposed to ensure consistency between high-resolution (HR) and low-resolution (LR) dependencies, and is experimentally verified to improve results. Furthermore, three distinct methods are presented to address the resulting cross-entropy minimization problem outlined in Eq. 9.

Summary: This is a strong paper, exceptionally well-written and presented. The experimental results are comprehensive, with well-chosen toy examples and real-world applications. To the best of my understanding, the proposed contributions to the field of super-resolution are novel (Sections 3.2, 3.3, and 3.4), although aspects related to GAN training may have appeared in variant forms previously (as also discussed within the paper). I believe this work will be highly relevant to future research in super-resolution, and the finding that GAN-based model training yields the most visually appealing results suggests a promising direction for further investigation. 

Manuscript should undergo a final proofread; I noted a small number of typographical errors that would benefit from correction.

Decision: Accept with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem in image super-resolution and provides a compelling and theoretically sound approach. The experimental validation is thorough and the results are convincing.

Supporting Arguments:
The core idea of formulating SR as an amortised MAP inference problem is well-motivated, particularly given the limitations of empirical risk minimization with losses like MSE. The introduction of the affine projection is a clever way to enforce consistency between LR and HR images and simplifies the optimization problem. The exploration of three different methods (GAN, denoiser-guided, and density-guided) to solve the resulting cross-entropy minimization offers a valuable comparative analysis. The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly the GAN-based method, in generating perceptually more pleasing SR images. The connection drawn between the AffGAN and amortised variational inference is a noteworthy theoretical contribution.

Additional Feedback:
1. The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is insightful and demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the potential drawbacks.
2. The ablation study in Section 5.2 clearly demonstrates the benefit of the affine projection layer.
3. The detailed explanation of the experimental setup in Appendix D is appreciated and facilitates reproducibility.
4. While the paper provides a thorough comparison of different loss functions, it might be helpful to include a brief discussion of computational complexity and training time for each method.

Questions for the Authors:
1. In Section 3.2.1, the discussion of instance noise mentions annealing the noise level during training. Could you provide more details on the specific annealing schedule used?
2. In the context of the affine projection, are there any specific considerations or limitations when applying this technique to different downsampling operators beyond the Gaussian kernel used in your experiments?
3. The authors mention the potential for the MAP estimate to be atypical, even if it has high probability under the prior. How might this be addressed in future work, perhaps through exploration of sampling-based approaches or alternative prior distributions?
4. The connection to variational inference is established in Appendix F. Could you elaborate on the potential benefits of explicitly leveraging variational inference techniques within the AffGAN framework, and whether you have preliminary results in this direction?
