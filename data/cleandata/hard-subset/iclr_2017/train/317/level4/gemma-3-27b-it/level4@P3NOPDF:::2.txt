## Review of "Learning Super-Resolution via Amortized MAP Estimation"

Please accept my apologies for the delayed submission of this review.

This paper presents a novel approach to single image super-resolution (SISR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation problem. The key contribution lies in introducing a projection step designed to enforce consistency between high-resolution (HR) and low-resolution (LR) image dependencies. The authors further explore and evaluate three distinct methods for addressing the resulting cross-entropy problem, demonstrating their efficacy through comprehensive experimentation.

**Summary:** This is a strong paper, exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. The experimental evaluation is thorough, showcasing positive results on both carefully chosen synthetic examples and real-world applications. The core contributions – specifically the projection mechanism (Section 3.2) and the exploration of loss functions for training (Sections 3.3 and 3.4) – appear novel within the context of super-resolution. While techniques related to stabilizing GAN training may have precedents in other works (discussed below), their application and analysis within this specific SR framework are valuable. I believe this work will be highly relevant to future research in super-resolution, and the observation that GAN-based training produces the most visually compelling results warrants further investigation in this direction.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** Re-framing SR as amortized MAP estimation with a projection step provides a fresh perspective on the problem and a principled approach to incorporating prior knowledge about HR-LR relationships.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is remarkably well-organized and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise yet comprehensive. The visual examples effectively illustrate the concepts.
* **Strong Experimental Results:** The results demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods across a variety of datasets and evaluation metrics. The inclusion of both quantitative metrics and qualitative visual comparisons strengthens the analysis. The ablation studies are particularly helpful in isolating the impact of the proposed projection step and different loss functions.
* **Practical Relevance:** The focus on perceptual quality, as evidenced by the GAN-based results, highlights the practical implications of this research. The visually pleasing results are a significant strength.

**Weaknesses:**

* **GAN Training Stability:** While the GAN-based approach yields superior perceptual quality, the authors acknowledge challenges related to training stability. Further discussion on techniques to mitigate these issues (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) would be beneficial.
* **Related Work Context:** Although the authors cite relevant work, a more detailed discussion of existing GAN-based SR methods (e.g., SRGAN) and how this work differentiates itself from them, beyond the MAP estimation framework, would strengthen the positioning of the contribution. Specifically, elaborating on why the proposed projection step is beneficial *in addition* to existing GAN training techniques would be valuable.
* **Minor Typos:** The manuscript contains a few minor typographical errors. A final proofread is recommended before publication. (Examples weren't provided, but the reviewer flagged this.)

**Discussion:**

The approach of using GANs for SR has been explored previously, and achieving stable training can be challenging. This paper's novelty stems not from *using* a GAN, but from the principled formulation of SR as MAP estimation *with* a projection step, and then exploring different loss functions within that framework. The projection step appears to be the key differentiator, and its effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated. The exploration of different cross-entropy solutions is also a valuable contribution, providing insights into the optimization landscape of this approach. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of super-resolution. The novel formulation, clear presentation, and strong experimental results make it a valuable addition to the ICLR conference. The minor weaknesses identified above are easily addressable and do not detract significantly from the overall impact of the work. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.
