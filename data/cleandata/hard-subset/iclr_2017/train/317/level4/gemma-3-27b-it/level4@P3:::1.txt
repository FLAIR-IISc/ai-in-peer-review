## Review of AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

This paper introduces a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The key idea is to leverage a convolutional neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection layer, ensuring consistency between low- and high-resolution images, and then employ different optimization strategies – GANs, denoiser guidance, and a maximum-likelihood prior – to approximate MAP estimation. Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling approach to a challenging problem.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound approach, the empirical evaluation lacks sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods and leaves critical questions unanswered regarding the practical benefits and limitations of the proposed framework.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary concern lies with the limited comparative evaluation. The authors demonstrate compelling results on a two-dimensional toy problem and grass textures, showcasing the benefits of the affine projection and MAP-based training. However, the comparison to existing SR methods, particularly recent GAN-based approaches like Ledig et al. (2016), is insufficient. While the authors acknowledge Ledig et al., they don't provide a direct performance comparison on standard benchmarks. The qualitative results on ImageNet are encouraging, but lack quantitative metrics for a fair assessment. The absence of results on commonly used datasets like Set5, Set14, or BSD100 hinders the ability to judge the practical impact of this work relative to the current state-of-the-art.

Secondly, the paper raises a question regarding the training of the affine projection layer itself. The authors state it removes the need for {HR, LR} pairs *after* training, but the initial training of the projection layer necessitates these pairs. This point requires further clarification: is the benefit primarily in the generative training phase, or is there a significant efficiency gain in the initial projection layer training? The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of this aspect.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Benchmarking:** Including quantitative results on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) is crucial to demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed methods. PSNR and SSIM scores should be reported and compared to leading SR techniques.
*   **Scalability:** The current experiments are limited to 128x128 images. Addressing the scalability of the framework to larger image sizes is important, as SR is often applied to images of arbitrary dimensions.
*   **Latent Space Exploration:** The authors mention GANs typically employ a noise term for latent space exploration. Investigating the incorporation of a noise vector into their GAN-based SR approach (AffGAN) and analyzing its impact on the generated images would be valuable.
*   **Clarify Affine Projection Training:** A more detailed explanation of how the affine projection layer is trained and its interplay with the overall framework would be helpful. Specifically, discussing the data requirements and computational cost of training the projection layer is needed.
*   **Ablation Study:** Provide an ablation study demonstrating the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (e.g., the affine projection, the different optimization methods).
*   **Connection to Ledig et al. (2016):** A more in-depth discussion of how this work builds upon and differs from Ledig et al. (2016) would strengthen the contribution. What specific limitations of existing GAN-based SR methods does this approach address?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide quantitative results (PSNR/SSIM) on standard SR benchmark datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100) comparing your best performing method (AffGAN) to state-of-the-art SR algorithms, including Ledig et al. (2016)?
2.  Can you elaborate on the data requirements and computational cost associated with training the affine projection layer? Is this a significant overhead compared to training a standard SR network?
3.  Have you experimented with incorporating a noise vector into AffGAN to explore the latent space and potentially improve the diversity of generated images? If so, what were the results?
4.  Could you clarify the benefit of the affine projection when compared to simply adding a soft constraint like an L1 loss, as used in other methods (e.g. Garcia, 2016)? Does the affine projection yield substantially better performance or stability?
5.  What are the limitations of the proposed approach? Are there specific image types or scenarios where it might not perform well?
