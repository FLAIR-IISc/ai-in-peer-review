The paper presents a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) – amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference – and introduces an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between high- and low-resolution images. The authors further propose three methods to approximate the cross-entropy minimisation required for MAP inference: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) approach, denoiser-guided SR, and a density-model guided approach. Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling theoretical foundation and promising experimental results. However, several points merit further clarification.

**Reject** – while the ideas are novel and potentially impactful, the paper lacks sufficient quantitative comparison to established state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks, and some aspects of the approach require further justification.

The core contribution of proposing amortised MAP inference for SR is significant and represents a departure from traditional empirical risk minimisation. The affine projection layer is a clever constraint that ensures valid solutions and simplifies the learning problem. The exploration of GANs, denoising, and density modelling as solution methods demonstrates a thorough investigation of the proposed framework. The connection established between GANs and amortised variational inference is also a valuable theoretical insight.

However, the manuscript could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper demonstrates qualitative improvements and provides comparisons to simpler baselines (MSE, MAE), a quantitative comparison against recent, high-performing GAN-based SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, Real-ESRGAN) on standard benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) is crucial for establishing the method’s true performance and contribution. This would allow for a fairer assessment of its advantages and disadvantages.
*   **Affine Projection Training:** The claim that the framework eliminates the need for paired {HR, LR} images is misleading. While the MAP inference *utilises* unpaired data through the generative modelling aspects, the initial training of the affine projection layer *requires* paired data to learn the correct projection. Clarify this distinction and discuss how this initial training phase impacts the overall unsupervised learning claim. Is this merely a transfer learning step?
*   **Standard Benchmarks & Scalability:** The current experiments primarily focus on texture patches and 128x128 images. Including results on standard SR benchmarks and exploring the method’s performance on larger images is essential. SR applications frequently involve arbitrary input sizes, and demonstrating scalability is important.
*   **Latent Space Exploration:** The introduction of a noise vector, akin to traditional GANs, could provide a latent space for exploring different plausible HR solutions and potentially improving the diversity of the generated outputs. While not strictly necessary, investigating this could be beneficial.
*  **Discussion of limitations:** The paper briefly mentions possible limitations of the MAP inference approach. Expand this section, providing a more thorough discussion of scenarios where the approach might falter.

To better assess the proposed framework, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

*   What is the computational cost of training the affine projection layer, and how does it scale with image size and downsampling factor?
*   How does the performance of the proposed methods degrade when the quality of the LR input is degraded (e.g., by adding noise or blur)?
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of architecture for the density model and its influence on the overall performance?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the noise distribution and annealing schedule in the instance noise technique?
