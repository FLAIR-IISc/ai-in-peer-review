The paper presents an amortised MAP estimation method for image super-resolution (SR). By learning a neural network that projects solutions to an affine subspace consistent with the low-resolution input, the method enables finding plausible high-resolution solutions using generative adversarial networks (GANs), denoising, or density-based optimisation. The results are demonstrated on several datasets.

I recommend rejecting this paper in its current form, primarily due to a lack of clarity in the presentation and insufficient analysis to fully support the claims. While the core idea of amortised MAP inference and the affine projection are novel and potentially valuable, the paper is difficult to follow in parts, hindering a thorough evaluation of the technical contributions.

The primary issue is the density of the writing and the complexity of the concepts presented. Several sections require significant effort to parse, and the connections between different ideas are not always clearly articulated. For instance, the motivation for the affine projection and its theoretical grounding are explained, but the practical implications and benefits beyond consistency are not fully explored. Similarly, the rapid introduction of three different optimisation methods (GAN, denoising, density modeling) without a comparative analysis of their strengths and weaknesses detracts from the overall impact. The empirical results, while present, do not provide enough insight into *why* the proposed methods perform as they do. The authors establish a connection to variational inference in the appendix, but fail to properly discuss the implications and differences compared to standard variational autoencoders. 

Further, the claims of improved perceptual quality need stronger support. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of PSNR and SSIM, they do not present compelling perceptual studies or qualitative comparisons that convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods. The ablation studies, such as comparing AffGAN to SoftGAN, are helpful, but more extensive analysis is needed. 

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

*   **Improve clarity and organisation:** Rewrite sections with complex theoretical arguments to be more accessible. Use more visuals to illustrate key concepts, such as the affine projection. Break down long paragraphs into shorter, more focused segments.
*   **Provide a more in-depth comparative analysis:**  Systematically compare the performance of the three proposed optimisation methods (GAN, denoising, density modeling) across different datasets and metrics. Discuss the trade-offs between them.
*   **Enhance the empirical evaluation:** Include perceptual studies (e.g., user studies) to assess the subjective quality of the generated images. Conduct more detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the proposed framework.
*   **Expand the discussion of the connection to variational inference:** Elaborate on the implications of the relationship between AffGAN and variational autoencoders. Discuss potential advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach compared to standard variational methods.
*   **Analyze learned features:** Provide insights into the features learned by the networks. Visualization techniques (e.g., activation maps) could be valuable.

I have several clarifying questions for the authors:

*   Regarding the affine projection, what specific criteria were used to choose the deconvolution kernel A+? How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of A+?
*   In Section 5.6, the authors discuss potential limitations of MAP inference. Could they elaborate on the conditions under which these limitations are most likely to occur, and how they might be mitigated?
*   The instance noise technique appears to improve GAN training stability. What is the theoretical justification for this approach, and how does it differ from other methods for stabilizing GANs, such as label smoothing?
*   What is the computational cost of the affine projection, and how does it compare to the computational cost of other SR methods?
*   For the density-guided approach, what are the limitations of the chosen MCGSM model, and how might these limitations affect the performance of the overall method?
