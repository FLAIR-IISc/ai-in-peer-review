This paper introduces a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference, coupled with a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency between high-resolution (HR) and low-resolution (LR) outputs. The authors also investigate three distinct approaches to cross-entropy minimization. Overall, this is a strong contribution. However, I have several points for consideration:

1) The proposed amortized MAP inference represents a departure from existing SR techniques and, when integrated with a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN), yields promising results. While comparable to recent GAN-based SR methods such as Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network, it would be beneficial to clearly articulate the specific advantages and contributions of this new formulation relative to the current state-of-the-art.

2) The use of an affine projection architecture as a constraint allows training without requiring paired {HR, LR} images. However, the training of this affine projection layer itself necessitates the use of such paired data. Clarification is needed regarding whether the training process for the affine projection is simply a transfer of existing training procedures.

3) The paper includes extensive results on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a more direct and equitable comparison with prior work in the field, I recommend including results on standard SR benchmark datasets such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100.

4) The presented results for natural images are currently limited to a size of 128x128. Given that SR methods often encounter inputs of arbitrary size, it would be valuable to demonstrate the framework’s performance on larger images to assess its scalability and generalizability.

5) Traditional GANs utilize a noise vector to define a latent space, effectively representing the learning of a distribution. Have the authors explored the inclusion of such a noise vector within this framework?

In summary, this paper presents a new and theoretically sound framework for SR, with a novel core idea and thorough exploration of different methodologies. While questions remain regarding the necessity of certain components and further experimentation is warranted, I believe this work has the potential to significantly inspire future research in the SR community.
