1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes amortised MAP inference for single-image super-resolution (SR). The key idea is a neural architecture that projects outputs onto the affine subspace of HR images consistent with the LR input, ensuring exact data fidelity (Aŷ = x). With this projection, training for MAP reduces to matching the marginal distribution of reconstructed images to the natural image prior via cross-entropy, eliminating the need for paired LR–HR training data.

Building on this, the authors present three training strategies:
- Affine-projected GAN (AffGAN), with a discriminator guiding the generator to minimise KL[q‖p] (and thus cross-entropy), plus an “instance noise” trick to stabilise training.
- Denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), which uses a denoiser to estimate ∇y log pY (y) and backpropagates these gradients through the affine-projected network.
- Density-guided SR (AffLL), which uses a differentiable, maximum-likelihood image prior (PixelCNN-like with MCGSM likelihood) to guide training via log-likelihood.

They also show the affine projection does not harm—and can improve—MSE-trained SR performance, and present results on a 2D Swiss-roll toy problem, textures, faces (CelebA), and ImageNet. Empirically, AffGAN produces the most plausible, sharp outputs. The paper further connects a stochastic variant of AffGAN to amortised variational inference.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated formulation of amortised MAP SR with an elegant affine projection that guarantees LR–HR consistency and reframes training as distribution matching.
- Solid empirical evidence that the approach works in practice, with AffGAN delivering perceptually convincing results across datasets. The theoretical links to GANs and to amortised VI add further value.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: SR is an underdetermined inverse problem; MSE-trained models average over plausible HR solutions, yielding blurry, implausible outputs. The paper seeks plausible reconstructions by approximating MAP inference while retaining the efficiency of amortised inference.
- Positioning and motivation: The approach is grounded in classical MAP reasoning and modern generative modelling. The affine projection is a principled alternative to soft fidelity constraints (e.g., L1/L2 penalties), exactly enforcing Aŷ = x. The GAN formulation is related to f-divergence minimisation; “instance noise” is a plausible stabilisation mechanism. The denoiser gradient connection relies on known theory linking denoising and score estimation.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - Swiss-roll: AffGAN and AffDG achieve cross-entropy close to the brute-force MAP solution; MSE/MAE fall in low-density regions. Affine projection ensures zero LR-consistency error; soft constraints do not.
  - Affine projection and MSE: With or without learning A+, affine-projected networks train faster and reach better MSE/SSIM in CelebA, while maintaining exact consistency.
  - Textures: AffGAN yields sharp, plausible textures; AffDG and AffLL produce blurry outputs despite convergence, suggesting challenges in using denoiser/density guidance here.
  - Faces and ImageNet: AffGAN produces sharper images with good global structure; some high-frequency noise and occasional semantic drift (e.g., snake to water) are observed—consistent with adversarial training trade-offs. PSNR/SSIM favour MSE as expected; affine models maintain exact data fidelity.
Overall, the experiments and analyses support the central claims, with clear advantages for AffGAN and meaningful insights into the other two methods’ limitations.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the decision)
- Clarity and organization: The paper is conceptually dense. Tighten the exposition around the affine projection (define A and A+ early; give more intuition for ΠAx, null-space projection, and why A+ is not simply A⊤). Consider moving the most essential parts of Appendices A–C into the main text, especially the instance noise rationale.
- Analysis of learned models: Provide qualitative/quantitative analysis of what the affine-projected networks learn. Examples: visualise residuals in the null space (I − A+A)fθ, intermediate feature maps, or frequency spectra of outputs; examine how instance noise affects discriminator features.
- Evaluation: In addition to PSNR/SSIM, consider perceptual metrics (e.g., deep feature distances or human studies) to better quantify plausibility. Include more ablations: with/without affine projection; varying strength of soft constraints; effect of instance noise schedules; sensitivity to A/A+ choices.
- Practicalities and reproducibility: Clarify training stability tricks (learning rates, discriminator updates per generator step), hyperparameters for instance noise annealing, and release the A+ estimation procedure/code. Discuss computational cost and inference latency.

Questions for the authors
- Affine projection:
  - How sensitive is performance to mis-specification of A (e.g., blur kernel mismatch)? Can the method handle unknown degradations or jointly estimate A?
  - Is A+ fixed after numerical estimation, or can it be learned without harming fidelity? Any results across different downsampling kernels or scales?
- Instance noise:
  - How do you set and anneal the noise variance in practice? Did you compare to other stabilisation methods (e.g., label smoothing) quantitatively?
  - Does instance noise impact final sharpness or introduce the observed high-frequency noise?
- Denoiser/density guidance:
  - Can you elaborate on why AffDG/AffLL were blurry on textures? Is this due to gradient accuracy early in training, or limitations of the priors? Would multi-scale priors or improved density models help?
  - How robust are these methods to the choice of denoising noise levels and annealing schedules?
- Generalisation and limitations:
  - Since MAP depends on representation, have you explored alternative colour spaces or feature spaces for MAP, and how do results change?
  - Could the stochastic AffGAN variant be used to sample from p(Y|X), rather than just MAP-like reconstructions?
- Comparisons:
  - How does AffGAN compare to contemporary perceptual-loss SR methods that combine feature-space distances with adversarial losses, under both fidelity and perceptual metrics?
  - Any results at other upscaling factors or on standard SR benchmarks to contextualise performance?
