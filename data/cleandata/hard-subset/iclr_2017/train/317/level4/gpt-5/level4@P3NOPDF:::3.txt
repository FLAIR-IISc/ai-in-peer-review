Summary:
The paper proposes an amortized MAP framework for single-image super-resolution (SR). The core idea is to learn a neural network that parameterizes an affine subspace of high-resolution (HR) images that are consistent with the low-resolution (LR) observation under a known degradation model. This parameterization enables efficient inference while enforcing measurement consistency, and it can be combined with different objectives and training/inference strategies (e.g., adversarial, noise-assisted, and density-assisted optimization). The approach is evaluated on multiple datasets, showing promising results.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Learning an affine subspace of LR-consistent HR solutions is a compelling way to enforce data consistency while amortizing inference. This neatly separates the physical constraint (consistency with the LR measurement) from the learned prior within the feasible set.
- Flexibility: The same parameterization supports several inference/training paradigms (GAN-based, noise-assisted, density-assisted), making the method applicable to different goals (e.g., perceptual quality vs. fidelity).
- Empirical promise: The results on multiple datasets indicate the approach can produce visually plausible reconstructions with adherence to the degradation model.

Weaknesses and clarity concerns:
- Exposition: The paper is difficult to follow in places. Key definitions (the image formation model, the precise form of the affine subspace, and how projection/consistency is enforced) should be presented earlier and more clearly. A compact algorithmic overview for each variant would help.
- Methodological specifics: It is not fully clear how the affine subspace is constructed and constrained during training/inference. For instance, do you explicitly parameterize the null space of the downsampling operator, or do you apply an analytic projection step? If the latter, where does it appear in the computational graph, and how do gradients flow?
- Empirical analysis: While results are shown on several datasets, the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of:
  - Measurement consistency (quantify LR reconstruction error).
  - Ablations on the subspace dimension, projection strength, and choice of inference strategy.
  - Trade-offs between perceptual quality and fidelity across the different variants.
  - Runtime and scalability versus non-amortized baselines.
- Understanding the learned representation: The paper does not analyze what the network/subspace learns. This is a missed opportunity to provide insights into how the affine basis captures frequencies, textures, and structures relevant to SR.
- Positioning relative to prior work: The relationship to MAP with learned priors, SRGAN/ESRGAN, deep image prior/generative priors, plug-and-play/RED, and data-consistent generative models is not thoroughly discussed. Clarifying similarities and differences would strengthen the contribution.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the model:
  - State the forward operator and noise model up front and use consistent notation.
  - Define the affine subspace precisely (e.g., x = x0 + U z with H x = y). Explain how x0 and U are obtained or parameterized, and how LR consistency is enforced during both training and inference.
  - Provide concise pseudocode for each variant (GAN, noise-assisted, density-assisted), including losses, projection steps, and optimization details.
- Expand empirical evaluation:
  - Report standard metrics (e.g., PSNR/SSIM for fidelity, LPIPS/NIQE for perception) and, critically, an LR consistency error.
  - Include ablations on subspace dimension and projection strength; show how these affect diversity, fidelity, and perceptual quality.
  - Compare against strong baselines that enforce data consistency (e.g., iterative projection methods, plug-and-play priors, deep generative priors with projection, and SRGAN-like models with a consistency term).
  - Evaluate robustness to mismatch in the degradation model (e.g., unseen kernels, additive noise levels) and generalization across scales.
  - Provide runtime comparisons and memory footprints to substantiate the amortization benefits.
- Analyze what is learned:
  - Visualize basis directions/U columns, their frequency responses, and their sensitivity maps with respect to the LR image.
  - Explore the spectrum/conditioning of the learned subspace and its alignment with the null space of the downsampling operator.
  - Show diversity within the subspace for a fixed LR input and how that diversity translates into texture/perceptual variation.
  - Use linear probes or feature attributions to understand which structures (edges, textures, repeated patterns) are emphasized.
- Improve readability:
  - Tighten notation, avoid overloading symbols, and include a figure that illustrates the affine subspace, the LR-consistency manifold, and the role of each component.
  - Group related objectives and clearly delineate how the GAN, noise-assisted, and density-assisted variants differ in losses, inference, and guarantees.
  - Proofread for numerous minor issues (e.g., “amortised” vs. “amortized” consistency, “LR method” should be “LR measurement/observation,” and typos like “propoer”).

Questions for the authors:
- How is the affine subspace constructed in practice? Is there an explicit projection operator, and does training guarantee exact LR consistency or only penalize deviations?
- What is the effective dimensionality of the learned subspace, and how sensitive is performance to this choice?
- Can the method handle unknown or spatially varying degradations, and if so, how is the operator identified or adapted?
- How do the three variants compare under a controlled fidelity–perception trade-off (e.g., via a Pareto frontier)?
- Does amortization reduce or increase mode collapse relative to a purely adversarial SR model?

Overall assessment:
This is an interesting and promising approach that combines data consistency with amortized inference in a flexible framework. The empirical results are encouraging, and the idea of learning an LR-consistent affine subspace is elegant. However, the paper’s clarity and depth of analysis need improvement. With clearer exposition, stronger positioning relative to prior work, and more thorough quantitative and representation-level analyses, I would be inclined toward acceptance. In its current form, I lean positive but recommend revisions to address the above points.
