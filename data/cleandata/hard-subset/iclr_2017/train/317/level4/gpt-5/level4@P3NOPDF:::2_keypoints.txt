- Formulates single-image SR as amortized MAP and adds a projection step enforcing exact HR-LR consistency under the known degradation model, which improves results.
- Proposes three strategies to optimize the cross-entropy objective (Eq. 9); GAN-based training yields the most perceptually appealing reconstructions.
- Experiments combine diagnostic toy problems and realistic SR scenarios, emphasizing visual quality and supporting the paper’s claims.
- Clear, well-organized, and well-written; projection step is intuitively appealing and practically effective.
- Thoughtful experimental design with compelling results that consistently support HR-LR consistency and perceptual gains.
- Contributions in Sections 3.2–3.4 appear novel; the observation that GAN-based training produces the best visuals is valuable.
- Amortized MAP framing is a unifying perspective with potential applicability to other inverse problems.
- Needs clearer positioning and explicit comparisons to prior GAN-based SR and perceptual-loss methods (novelty clarification and citations/table).
- The three optimization strategies require deeper comparative analysis: computational cost, convergence behavior, hyperparameter sensitivity, and failure modes; include ablations and runtime/memory metrics.
- Should report quantitative HR-LR consistency (e.g., degradation-model reconstruction error) and standard distortion metrics (PSNR/SSIM) alongside perceptual metrics to show trade-offs.
- Discuss robustness to degradation-model mismatch (blur kernel, noise level) and behavior of the projection under model misspecification or heavy noise.
- Minor typos present; recommend careful proofreading.
- Presentation is very good; consider adding an intuition box or schematic for the projection operator and its interaction with Eq. 9.
- Reproducibility would be improved by releasing code and pretrained models and providing full training details (architectures, losses, optimization settings, seeds, preprocessing, degradation specs).
- High significance: ideas convincingly improve SR outcomes and are likely relevant for future SR and other inverse-problem research.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—clarify novelty vs prior GAN work, expand comparisons among optimization methods, and fix typos.
