This paper proposes a new super-resolution (SR) framework based on amortized MAP inference, augmented with a pre-learned affine projection layer to enforce consistency with the low-resolution (LR) observation. It also introduces three approaches for optimizing the cross-entropy objective. Overall, the paper is strong and well-motivated, but I have several comments:

1) The amortized MAP inference formulation is novel within SR and, when combined with a GAN, yields plausible high-quality results. However, compared with prior GAN-based SR methods (e.g., “Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network”), it is unclear what concrete advantages this new formulation provides. Please clarify the distinctive contributions and provide head-to-head comparisons or ablations that isolate the benefits of amortized MAP relative to existing GAN-based SR approaches.

2) With the affine projection constraint, the main model does not require paired {HR, LR} images for training. Yet the affine projection layer itself must be trained using such pairs. Does this simply shift the supervision burden to the affine projection pretraining? Please detail the data requirements and training pipeline, including how often the affine projection needs to be retrained or adapted across datasets, and whether its pretraining limits generalization.

3) The paper reports results on natural images from ImageNet. To enable fair comparison with prior SR work, please include evaluations on standard benchmarks such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100 (and corresponding metrics) alongside recent baselines.

4) The qualitative results for natural images appear limited to 128×128. Can the framework scale to larger images and arbitrary input sizes typical of SR applications? Please discuss any architectural or computational constraints, and provide evidence (or analysis) of performance and runtime on higher resolutions.

5) Standard GANs typically include a latent noise input to better capture output diversity. Did the authors experiment with injecting a noise vector? If so, how did it affect perceptual quality, diversity, and training stability within the amortized MAP framework?

Overall, this paper introduces a compelling framework for SR with solid theoretical grounding. The ideas are novel and thoroughly explored. Addressing the questions above—especially clarifying the incremental contribution over existing GAN-based SR, the practical necessity and impact of the affine projection pretraining, and adding standard benchmark results—would strengthen the paper further. Despite these open points, the work is promising and likely to inspire future research in SR.
