This paper presents a new framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized MAP inference, and introduces a pre-learned affine projection layer to ensure consistency with the LR observation. It also proposes three methods for minimizing cross-entropy. Overall, this is a strong paper, but I have several comments:

1) The proposed amortized MAP inference approach is novel relative to prior SR methods. In combination with a GAN, the framework yields plausible, high-quality results. However, given prior GAN-based SR work—e.g., “Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network”—it remains unclear what substantive advantages this new formulation offers over the latest state-of-the-art. Please clarify the incremental contributions (both theoretical and empirical) beyond SRGAN and similar methods.

2) By enforcing an affine projection constraint, the model reportedly does not require paired {HR, LR} images for training. Yet the affine projection layer itself appears to require {HR, LR} pairs for training. Does this simply shift the need for paired data from the SR network to the projection module? Please clarify the data requirements and training pipeline: which components are trained with paired data, which without, and what practical benefit this decomposition brings.

3) The paper reports results on natural images from ImageNet. For a fair comparison with prior SR literature, please also include evaluations on standard benchmarks such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100, following common protocols and metrics. This would allow readers to assess progress against established baselines.

4) The natural-image results seem limited to 128×128 outputs. Can the framework scale to larger images effectively? Since SR is typically applied to inputs of arbitrary size, please discuss scalability, memory/runtime behavior, and any architectural or training modifications needed for high-resolution inference.

5) In standard GANs, a stochastic latent input (noise) helps model a distribution. Did you experiment with a noise vector in the generator or inference mechanism? If so, how does it affect diversity, fidelity, and training stability? If not, please explain the design choice and whether the deterministic amortized MAP objective suffices.

Overall, this paper proposes a compelling SR framework with solid theoretical grounding. The ideas are novel, and the paper explores multiple optimization strategies. Nevertheless, questions remain about necessity and comparative advantage relative to existing GAN-based SR approaches, and additional experiments on standard benchmarks and higher resolutions would strengthen the case. I believe this work will stimulate useful discussion and inspire further research in the community.
