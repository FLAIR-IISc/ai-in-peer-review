Summary and contributions
This paper tackles single-image super-resolution (SR) from a principled probabilistic perspective, aiming to avoid the blurriness induced by empirical risk minimization with convex pixel losses. The core idea is amortised MAP inference: learn a function that directly outputs the MAP estimate for any low-resolution (LR) input, privileging high-prior-probability solutions. The key technical contribution is an affine projection layer that maps network outputs to the affine subspace of HR images consistent with the LR observation (Afθ(x) = x), implemented via a learned Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse A+. This guarantees strict LR–HR consistency and turns training into matching the marginal distribution of outputs to the HR data distribution, i.e., minimizing H[qθ, pY]. The paper then develops three training strategies leveraging this view:
- AffGAN: an affine-projected GAN that (under a modified update rule) approximately minimizes KL[qθ‖pY]; it also proposes “instance noise” to stabilize GAN training.
- AffDG: denoiser-guided training that uses gradients from a denoising autoencoder as a score estimator ∂y log pY(y).
- AffLL: density-guided training that optimizes cross-entropy under a differentiable PixelCNN/MCGSM likelihood.
Empirically, AffGAN produces sharper, perceptually more plausible results than MSE/MAE baselines on textures, faces, and ImageNet crops. The affine projection does not degrade—and sometimes improves—MSE/SSIM when used with standard MSE training. On a 2D Swiss-roll toy problem the proposed methods achieve cross-entropy close to brute-force MAP. The paper also sketches a connection between a stochastic extension of AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Methodological novelty and clarity: the affine projection for strict LR–HR consistency and the amortised MAP framing are elegant and well-motivated, with solid links to GAN training and score matching.
- Empirical evidence that AffGAN produces perceptually superior SR while maintaining exact consistency, with ablations showing benefits of the affine projection over soft constraints and instance noise for stability.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? The paper addresses the mismatch between MSE training and multimodal SR posteriors, proposing a principled way to prefer plausible HR reconstructions that respect the observation model exactly.

2) Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature? Yes. The motivation (MAP vs. conditional mean) is clearly explained with the Swiss-roll example. The work connects to SRGAN/perceptual losses, but improves by enforcing exact consistency via affine projection rather than soft penalties, and by framing training as distribution matching rather than supervised risk minimization. The links to f-divergence GANs, denoising-score estimation, and PixelCNNs are appropriate and up to date for the venue.

3) Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The toy example directly evaluates H[qθ, pY], showing AffGAN/AffDG approach MAP. On images, qualitative results demonstrate sharper textures and faces than MSE/MAE, and the affine projection achieves zero LR-consistency error versus soft constraints. The MSE baseline with and without affine projection shows the projection does not harm capacity and can speed convergence. That said, perceptual evaluation is mostly qualitative; quantitative perceptual metrics or user studies are limited, and AffDG/AffLL underperform in high dimensions. Some failure cases (hallucinations/high-frequency noise) are acknowledged.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Clarity and structure: The paper is dense. Consider a higher-level overview figure that contrasts empirical risk vs. amortised MAP, and a schematic of the affine projection layer (A, A+, residual in null-space) with pseudo-code for AffGAN/AffDG/AffLL. Define A and A+ early; briefly summarize how A+ is identified and its sensitivity to kernel choices.
- Evaluation:
  - Add perceptual evaluations beyond PSNR/SSIM, e.g., human preference tests or task-based metrics. If feasible, include feature-based distances (e.g., deep feature matching) to corroborate visual gains.
  - Provide ablations for instance noise (noise level vs. stability/quality), and for the effect of fixing vs. training A+ after correct initialization.
  - Compare to more SR baselines that target perceptual quality (e.g., perceptual/content losses) and to SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016) with the same downsampling operator for fairness.
  - Report runtime and memory at training and inference; discuss throughput advantages of amortisation.
- Robustness and scope:
  - Analyze sensitivity to mismatch between the assumed downsampling operator A and the true one (non-Gaussian blur, unknown kernels). Discuss prospects for blind SR (learning or inferring A jointly).
  - Explore other scales (×2, ×3) and operators to demonstrate generality.
  - Quantify hallucination risk: when the prior dominates, how often does AffGAN produce semantically wrong but plausible textures? Can confidence or uncertainty be estimated?
- Alternative formulations:
  - The coordinate-dependence of MAP is rightly discussed; consider presenting stochastic AffGAN outputs to approximate posterior samples, and report diversity/entropy vs. quality trade-offs.
  - For AffDG/AffLL, expand on why they struggle in high dimensions and whether curriculum strategies (noise/likelihood annealing) or modern continuous normalizing flows could help.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to misspecification of A? If the blur kernel or stride differs at test time, how do AffGAN’s quality and consistency degrade? Could A or A+ be learned end-to-end (with regularization) to handle mild kernel uncertainty?
- Instance noise: What annealing schedule worked best in practice? Can you show training curves and final quality with/without instance noise, and comment on discriminator overfitting?
- Evaluation: Do you have any user preference study or pairwise comparisons on CelebA/ImageNet? If not, could you report a simple two-alternative forced choice result?
- Affine projection and capacity: After initializing A+ to the Moore–Penrose inverse, is keeping it fixed versus trainable materially different across datasets? Any risk of drifting away from exact consistency if trainable?
- AffDG/AffLL: What bits-per-dimension (or NLL) does your density model achieve on the HR training data, and how correlated is that with AffLL’s gradients and downstream SR quality? Could a stronger score estimator (e.g., modern score-based models) improve AffDG?
- Failure modes: Can you quantify and/or detect hallucinations (e.g., the “snake→water” example)? Would adding weak content losses or cycle-consistency help reduce semantic drift without reintroducing blur?
- Stochastic AffGAN: Have you tried sampling with z at inference to produce multiple plausible HR images per LR input? How does diversity affect perceptual judgments?

Overall, this is a timely and well-motivated contribution that advances the probabilistic foundations of SR and delivers a practical, consistent, and perceptually strong method via AffGAN. I recommend acceptance after strengthening the exposition and adding a bit more quantitative perceptual evaluation and ablation.
