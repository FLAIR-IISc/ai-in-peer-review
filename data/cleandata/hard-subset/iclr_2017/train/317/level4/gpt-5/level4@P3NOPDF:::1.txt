Summary
The paper proposes an amortized MAP framework for single-image super-resolution (SR), coupled with a pre-learned affine projection layer to ensure consistency with the observed low-resolution (LR) input. It also explores three strategies for cross-entropy minimization. When combined with adversarial training, the method produces visually plausible outputs. The approach is conceptually novel for SR and is supported by solid theoretical motivation.

Strengths
- Novelty: Introducing amortized MAP inference to SR is a fresh perspective relative to conventional regression-based deep SR. The coupling with a learned affine projection to enforce LR consistency is elegant.
- Theoretical grounding: The formulation is clearly motivated, and the projection constraint directly encodes the forward image formation model.
- Promising visual quality: The qualitative results indicate that adversarially trained versions of the model can produce realistic textures.

Concerns and questions
1) Relation to prior GAN-based SR:
- The paper’s connection and added value relative to “Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network” (SRGAN) are not sufficiently clear. What concrete benefits does amortized MAP bring beyond adversarial training alone? For example, does LR-consistency via affine projection yield measurable improvements in fidelity or stability? A direct head-to-head comparison and ablation isolating amortized MAP and the projection layer would clarify the contribution.

2) Training without paired data vs pre-training the projection:
- The manuscript states that the affine projection architecture obviates the need for paired {HR, LR} data when training the SR mapping, yet the projection layer itself is learned using paired data. This raises questions:
  - Are we merely shifting the supervision burden to the projection pre-training step?
  - How sensitive is overall performance to mismatch between the degradation process used to train the projection and the degradations encountered at test time?
  - What data volume and diversity are required to learn a projection that generalizes broadly? Please clarify the training protocol and provide results under degradation mismatch.

3) Benchmarking on standard datasets:
- While ImageNet examples are informative, SR papers are typically evaluated on Set5, Set14, and BSD100 (and often Urban100). Please include quantitative comparisons (PSNR/SSIM and, if available, perceptual metrics) and visual comparisons on these benchmarks to enable fair comparison to prior work.

4) Resolution and scalability:
- The presented natural image results are limited to 128×128. Can the framework handle larger, arbitrary-sized inputs at test time? Please discuss scaling behavior, memory usage, and any tiling or border-handling strategies. Empirical results on higher-resolution images (e.g., 512×512 and above) would strengthen the case for real-world applicability.

5) Role of stochasticity:
- Standard GANs often incorporate latent noise to capture output variability. Does the proposed model include (or can it benefit from) a noise input? If not, is the approach fundamentally deterministic due to its MAP objective? If you experimented with a noise vector, please report its effect on perceptual quality, diversity, and LR-consistency.

6) Cross-entropy minimization strategies:
- The paper proposes three methods to minimize cross-entropy, but their relative merits are not fully disentangled. A clear motivation for each, computational trade-offs, and a thorough ablation (quantitative and qualitative) would help readers understand which is preferable under which conditions.

7) Stability and training dynamics:
- GAN-based SR can be sensitive to loss balancing. How does amortized MAP plus projection affect stability? Please include training curves, variance across seeds, and sensitivity to key hyperparameters (e.g., adversarial/fidelity weights).

8) Clarity of the projection constraint:
- Please provide more details on the form of the affine projection, its architecture or parameterization, and how exact LR-consistency is enforced in practice (e.g., is it strictly enforced or approximated?). An analysis of projection error and its impact on final SR quality would be useful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add direct comparisons against SRGAN and strong non-adversarial baselines on Set5/Set14/BSD100 (and preferably Urban100), reporting PSNR/SSIM and perceptual metrics, with user studies if feasible.
- Provide ablations isolating the contributions of amortized MAP, the affine projection, and adversarial training.
- Demonstrate performance on larger images and multiple scales, with runtime and memory profiling.
- Clarify the training pipeline: data used to train the projection, whether it is fixed or updated during SR training, and robustness to degradation mismatch.
- If applicable, explore or discuss stochastic variants (latent noise) and their compatibility with the projection constraint and MAP objective.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and well-motivated contribution that brings amortized MAP inference and a principled projection constraint to SR. The ideas are novel and the qualitative results are promising. However, to substantiate the benefits over existing GAN-based SR methods and to ensure fair comparison with the literature, the paper would benefit from (1) rigorous benchmarking on standard datasets, (2) ablations clarifying the contributions of each component, and (3) evidence of scalability to higher resolutions. With these clarifications and experiments, the work could have strong impact and provide valuable insights to the SR community.
