The paper proposes an amortized MAP approach to super-resolution (SR). It trains a neural network to map observations to an affine subspace of high-resolution solutions that are consistent with the low-resolution measurements. Within this subspace, the method supports several inference strategies—GAN-based, noise-assisted, and density-assisted optimization—to recover plausible SR reconstructions. The approach is demonstrated on multiple datasets with convincing results.

Overall, I like the paper: the idea is interesting and the empirical results are solid. However, the exposition needs substantial polishing. The core intuition, the construction of the affine subspace, and the relationships among the different inference modes are often difficult to follow. A clearer problem setup, more precise notation, and a step-by-step description of the training and inference procedures (ideally with a schematic figure or pseudocode) would significantly improve readability. The related work section could also be tightened to better position the contribution relative to amortized inference methods and existing SR approaches.

I would also like to see a deeper analysis of the learned networks. What features or structures do they capture? Are there visualizations of learned filters, basis elements of the affine subspace, or example trajectories within the subspace during optimization? How sensitive is performance to the subspace dimension and to the choice among GAN-, noise-, and density-assisted objectives? Ablation studies and error analyses—particularly under mismatch between the assumed and true degradation models—would strengthen the empirical claims and clarify when each inference mode is preferable.

Minor comments:
- Fix typos and terminology (e.g., “amortized,” “proper,” and consistent use of LR = low-resolution).
- Standardize hyphenation (e.g., “noise-assisted,” “density-assisted”).
- Improve figure captions and cross-references to guide readers through key results and design choices.

In summary, the paper presents a promising framework with compelling results, but the clarity of presentation and the analysis of what the networks learn should be improved to fully convey the contribution.
