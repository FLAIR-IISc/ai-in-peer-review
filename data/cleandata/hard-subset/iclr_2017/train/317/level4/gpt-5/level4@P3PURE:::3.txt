This paper introduces an amortized MAP estimation approach for super-resolution (SR). The authors train a neural network to project inputs onto an affine subspace of SR solutions that are consistent with the low-resolution (LR) observation model. Within this learned subspace, they can then recover plausible high-resolution outputs using multiple strategies, including GAN-based, noise-assisted, and density-assisted optimization. The method is demonstrated on several datasets, with clear and generally convincing results.

Overall, I like the paper. The idea of amortizing the search for LR-consistent solutions via a learned affine subspace is compelling, and the flexibility to plug in different optimization criteria (GANs, noise-assisted, density-assisted) is appealing in practice. The empirical results are nicely presented across multiple datasets.

That said, the paper would benefit from significantly improved writing and clearer presentation. The exposition is often hard to follow, which is especially problematic given the conceptual complexity of the approach. A more structured narrative, clearer notation, and tighter explanations would greatly improve readability.

I would also appreciate more analysis of the learned networks themselves. What kinds of features do they capture? How does the learned affine subspace relate to known SR priors or structures in natural images? Providing qualitative visualizations or quantitative probes of intermediate representations would make the contribution more interpretable and strengthen the paper.

In summary:
- Strengths: A principled amortized MAP framework; projection to an LR-consistent affine subspace; flexibility to use GAN, noise-assisted, and density-assisted optimization; solid demonstrations on several datasets.
- Areas for improvement: Writing and presentation should be polished for clarity; include deeper analysis of the learned features and the resulting networks to better understand what the model captures.

I am positive about the contribution, contingent on improvements to clarity and additional analysis of the learned representations.
