Summary and contributions
This paper proposes amortized MAP inference for single-image super-resolution (SR) and introduces an affine projection architecture that guarantees consistency between the generated high-resolution (HR) output and the low-resolution (LR) input (Aŷ = x). The key theoretical step shows that amortized MAP reduces to minimizing the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the implicit distribution of outputs and the HR data distribution, reframing SR training as a form of generative modeling without requiring paired {LR, HR} data. Three training strategies are explored: (1) AffGAN, a GAN trained on affine-projected outputs with an “instance noise” technique to improve stability; (2) denoiser-guided training (AffDG) that uses a denoiser to approximate ∇y log pY(y) and backpropagates through the SR network; and (3) density-guided training (AffLL) using a differentiable likelihood model (PixelCNN-like with MCGSM). The paper provides a principled motivation for using GANs in SR, introduces a projection layer implementable via deconvolution that enforces exact consistency, and gives a novel connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortized variational inference. Experiments on a 2D toy problem (with computable MAP), textures, faces (CelebA), and ImageNet illustrate that AffGAN produces sharper, more plausible images than MSE/MAE baselines and soft constraints, while maintaining LR consistency.

Decision: Weak accept
Reasons:
- The paper offers a clear, principled reformulation of SR as amortized MAP with an elegant architectural mechanism (affine projection) that enforces observation consistency and reduces reliance on paired data.
- Theoretical framing and analysis (cross-entropy objective, KL perspective for AffGAN, instance noise, and the variational inference connection) are insightful and likely to influence future SR and inverse-problem work.

Supporting arguments
- Specific problem and motivation: SR is underdetermined; MSE returns posterior means that are perceptually implausible. Casting SR as amortized MAP with an exact data-consistency constraint directly targets plausible reconstructions.
- Placement in literature: The work situates itself relative to SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016) and perceptual-loss methods, highlighting differences: exact enforcement of Aŷ = x via affine projection instead of soft penalties; training as distribution matching without paired data; and theoretical grounding (minimizing H[qθ, pY], KL for AffGAN).
- Correctness and rigor: The derivation showing the constrained MAP objective reduces to matching qθ to pY is sound, given the affine projector. The GAN update justified as minimizing KL[qθ‖pY], the role of instance noise, and the null-space projection properties are well argued. The toy problem validates that AffGAN/AffDG approach the MAP solution, and the affine projection achieves zero LR-consistency error as designed.
- Empirical support: Qualitative results on textures, faces, and natural images show sharper outputs for AffGAN relative to MSE/MAE and soft constraints. The ablation demonstrating benefits of initializing/using the correct Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse A+ is informative. However, the empirical section lacks standard SR benchmarks and broader quantitative comparisons.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all are decision-critical)
- Benchmarking and reproducibility:
  - Include results on standard SR datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100) under bicubic downsampling to enable direct comparison with prior work (PSNR/SSIM and perceptual metrics).
  - Report inference speed and model size; compare to common baselines (e.g., SRCNN/ESPCN/SRGAN) for practical relevance.
- Perceptual evaluation:
  - In addition to PSNR/SSIM, consider perceptual metrics (e.g., MS-SSIM) and user studies to substantiate “plausibility.” If possible, add a small human study on a subset.
- Scaling and generalization:
  - Demonstrate performance on larger images and arbitrary resolutions, clarifying how the affine projection handles boundaries and non-multiple-of-stride inputs.
  - Discuss sensitivity to mismatch between the assumed downsampling kernel A and the true degradation in real data, and whether the method can learn or adapt A.
- Comparisons and ablations:
  - Provide head-to-head comparisons against SRGAN using identical data and degradation models, with and without the affine projection and instance noise, to isolate contributions.
  - Detail the instance-noise schedule, sensitivity, and failure modes; include training curves for D/G losses.
- Unpaired training claim:
  - Clarify the exact data requirements: what distributions of LR and HR images are needed? Do LR inputs come from the same A applied to HR training data, or can LR be arbitrary in-the-wild?
- AffDG and AffLL:
  - The paper notes AffDG/AffLL produce blur on real images. Add diagnostics (e.g., gradient magnitude spectra, log-likelihood landscapes) to better explain failures and whether improved denoisers/density models could close the gap.
- Stochastic generators:
  - Given the multi-modality of SR posteriors, explore a stochastic AffGAN with latent z in experiments (not only in theory), and assess sample diversity and posterior coverage conditioned on fixed x.

Questions for the authors
- Training without paired data: Practically, what datasets are used for pX and pY? Must pX be produced by the same operator A applied to samples from pY, or can pX be arbitrary? How sensitive is training/inference to a mismatch between the assumed A and the LR data?
- Affine projection layer: How exactly is A+ estimated? Does it require any {HR, LR} pairs, or only knowledge of A and synthetic Gaussian inputs as in Appendix B? In large images, what kernel size/support is used for A+ and what is the impact on artifacts and runtime?
- Comparison to SRGAN: Under identical training data, architectures, and downsampling kernel, how does AffGAN compare to SRGAN in PSNR/SSIM, perceptual quality, and LR-consistency error?
- Instance noise: What noise levels and annealing schedules were used across datasets? How robust is convergence to these choices? Did you try alternative noise families?
- Stochastic AffGAN in practice: Did you experiment with a latent z at train/test time? If so, how diverse are conditional samples for a fixed x, and does diversity reduce artifacts or improve perceptual quality?
- Scalability and arbitrary input sizes: Can the affine projection be applied convolutionally to arbitrary-size images at test time without retraining? Any boundary handling needed?
- Evaluation on standard SR benchmarks: Can you provide results on Set5/Set14/BSD100 with bicubic downsampling to contextualize performance, even if your focus is perceptual quality?

Overall, this work presents a compelling, principled perspective on SR with a simple yet powerful architectural constraint and thoughtful theoretical analysis. With stronger experimental comparisons on standard benchmarks and a demonstration of stochastic inference, the paper would be even more impactful.
