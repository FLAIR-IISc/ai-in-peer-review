1. Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles single-image super-resolution (SR) as an underdetermined inverse problem and proposes amortized MAP inference as an alternative to empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses. The key idea is to learn a function that directly maps low-resolution (LR) images to high-resolution (HR) MAP estimates.
- A central architectural contribution is an affine projection layer that projects outputs onto the subspace consistent with the LR input, guaranteeing data fidelity exactly (Aŷ = x). This turns training into matching the model’s marginal output distribution to the HR data distribution, i.e., minimizing cross-entropy H[qθ, pY], and removes the need for paired LR–HR data for training the SR mapping.
- Three training strategies are explored: (i) AffGAN, a GAN formulation adapted to the affine projection; (ii) AffDG, which uses gradients from a denoiser to approximate ∇y log pY(y); and (iii) AffLL, which uses a learned likelihood model (PixelCNN/MCGSM) to guide training.
- The paper also proposes instance noise to stabilize GAN training and provides a theoretical connection between the AffGAN objective and amortized variational inference.
- Empirically, AffGAN yields visually sharper, more plausible results than MSE/MAE on toy and real image data; the affine projection improves consistency and does not hinder (and may speed up) MSE training.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novel and well-motivated reframing of SR as amortized MAP with a principled architectural mechanism (affine projection) that enforces data consistency and connects SR training to generative modeling.
- Strong theoretical grounding (cross-entropy interpretation, GAN variant minimizing KL, denoiser gradients, VI connection) and compelling qualitative results showing substantial perceptual gains over MSE/MAE.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and novelty: The paper squarely addresses the mismatch between MSE training and the multimodal nature of SR posteriors, arguing for MAP estimates that lie in high-probability regions of the image prior. The affine projection is an elegant way to encode the SR constraint exactly, which is often only softly enforced in prior GAN-based SR work.
- Motivation and placement in the literature: The approach is well positioned relative to MSE/SSIM-based SR, perceptual-loss methods, and prior GAN SR (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016). The hard constraint (affine projection) and the amortized MAP objective differentiate this work from “soft penalty” GAN SR formulations. The denoiser-gradient and density-model baselines are thoughtfully chosen and grounded in prior theory.
- Empirical and theoretical support: On a 2D toy problem, AffGAN and AffDG achieve cross-entropy close to the MAP solution, validating the objective. For faces and ImageNet patches, results look sharp and plausible, with perfect LR–HR consistency by construction. The observation that affine projection does not degrade and can even improve MSE training is useful. However, standard SR benchmarks (Set5/Set14/BSD100) and broader quantitative perceptual assessments are missing, and the experiments focus on up to 128×128 outputs; this limits direct comparison to prior SR literature.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Benchmarking: Include results on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) with both distortion metrics (PSNR/SSIM) and perceptual metrics. Even if GAN methods trade off PSNR, these benchmarks aid fair comparison and contextualization.
- Degradation model: The approach assumes a known, noiseless downsampling operator A. Please evaluate robustness to mismatch (e.g., small blur/noise perturbations, unknown A) and, if possible, include a variant that estimates or adapts to A.
- Scaling: Discuss and, if feasible, demonstrate performance on larger outputs (e.g., 256×256, 512×512) and arbitrary-sized inputs at test time. Since the model is convolutional with a fixed projection, clarify memory/computation scaling and any tiling/overlap strategies.
- Instance noise: Provide an ablation of instance noise (with/without, annealing schedules) and compare to one-sided label smoothing to substantiate the stability claims.
- Stochastic generators: Since the paper establishes a VI connection, consider including experiments with a stochastic AffGAN (injecting latent noise) to explore posterior sampling versus MAP and its effect on perceptual diversity and fidelity.
- Clarity: Expand on how A+ is obtained in practice and whether it requires paired data. The current appendix suggests a data-agnostic numerical procedure; making this explicit in the main text would preempt confusion.

5. Questions for the authors
- Added value over prior GAN SR: Beyond the hard constraint via affine projection and the amortized MAP framing, what concrete gains (stability, diversity, fidelity) does AffGAN provide over Ledig et al. (2016) when trained under identical data and degradation settings? Can you show side-by-side comparisons and ablations with/without projection?
- Training data requirements: The SR network is trained without paired LR–HR images thanks to the cross-entropy objective, but does learning A+ (or otherwise instantiating the projection) require paired data? If not, please clarify the exact procedure and its assumptions (e.g., knowledge of A).
- Standard datasets: Can you report results on Set5/Set14/BSD100 to compare against classical and deep SR baselines? If not included due to the mismatch in degradation models, can you provide results under bicubic downsampling for compatibility?
- Scalability and generalization: How does the method perform on larger images and arbitrary input sizes? Are there any artifacts at tile boundaries or degradation in global consistency?
- Latent noise: Have you tried adding a latent noise vector to the generator (stochastic AffGAN) to reflect posterior uncertainty? If so, how does it affect perceptual quality and consistency with the LR input?
- Degradation mismatch and noise: How sensitive is performance to small deviations from the assumed A, or to additive sensor noise? Could the framework incorporate a learned or adaptive A to handle real-world degradations?

Overall assessment
This is a thoughtful and well-supported contribution that reframes SR through amortized MAP, provides a principled architecture for exact data consistency, and offers both theoretical insights and strong qualitative improvements. Addressing the benchmarking and robustness questions would further strengthen the paper and its impact. I recommend acceptance.
