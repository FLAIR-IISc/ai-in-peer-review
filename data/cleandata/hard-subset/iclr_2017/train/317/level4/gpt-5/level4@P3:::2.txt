Summary and contributions
The paper reframes single-image super-resolution (SR) as amortised MAP inference and proposes an architecture that enforces exact LR–HR consistency via an affine projection layer. This projection ensures the network output ŷ always satisfies Aŷ = x for a known downsampling operator A, turning the amortised MAP objective into minimising the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the model’s marginal output distribution and the true HR image distribution. Building on this, the paper introduces three training strategies: (1) AffGAN, an affine-projected GAN variant with a principled generator update that targets KL[q‖p] and a practical stabilisation technique (instance noise); (2) AffDG, which uses a denoiser to estimate ∇y log pY(y) and backpropagates these gradients through the SR network; and (3) AffLL, which trains against a tractable density model (PixelCNN+MCGSM). A stochastic extension connects AffGAN to amortised variational inference. Experiments on a toy Swiss-roll, textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet) demonstrate that the affine projection preserves LR consistency, accelerates training even for MSE, and that AffGAN yields the most plausible, sharp reconstructions.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated formulation and novel architectural contribution: enforcing exact affine consistency is elegant, practical, and improves both optimisation and fidelity. The amortised MAP perspective is well-placed in the literature and tightly argued.
- Strong empirical and conceptual support: clear toy evidence that AffGAN/AffDG approach the MAP objective; convincing perceptual results on real data; and a useful theoretical link between GANs and amortised variational inference, plus the practical “instance noise” stabilisation.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the known limitation of MSE/PSNR-optimised SR (blurriness due to averaging under multimodal posteriors) and argues for MAP-like solutions to favour plausible outputs. This is appropriate for perceptual SR and consistent with recent GAN-based SR trends, but with stronger justification.
- Approach and placement: The affine projection layer is a simple, principled way to satisfy Aŷ = x exactly, reducing the learning problem to distribution matching in HR space. This avoids soft penalties that implicitly assume observation noise and are not guaranteed to enforce consistency. The three training routes (GAN, denoiser gradients, density models) connect SR to modern unsupervised learning; the GAN update targeting KL[q‖p] and the “instance noise” trick are timely and relevant given instability issues in GANs. The link to amortised variational inference for a stochastic AffGAN is insightful and novel.
- Evidence and rigor: On the Swiss-roll, AffGAN/AffDG achieve cross-entropies close to brute-force MAP, while MSE/MAE do not—directly supporting the central claim. The affine projection demonstrably preserves LR–HR consistency (zero error) and improves optimisation even for MSE training. On textures and faces, AffGAN yields sharper, more plausible images than MSE/MAE, with reasonable qualitative results on ImageNet. The limitations of AffDG/AffLL are discussed candidly (training stability and density sharpness), aligning with the paper’s conclusion that the GAN route currently works best. Theoretical arguments are clear about assumptions (e.g., Bayes-optimal discriminator), and instance noise is motivated to address non-overlapping supports.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Evaluation breadth: Consider adding perceptual metrics beyond PSNR/SSIM (e.g., MS-SSIM, NIQE/BRISQUE, or a small-scale user study). A classifier-based Turing test or diversity measures could also support claims about plausibility and variability.
- Unpaired training: While the theory allows training without matched LR–HR pairs, experiments appear to use paired data via synthetic downsampling. Please clarify this and, if feasible, include an experiment with unpaired LR and HR sets to demonstrate the practical benefit of amortisation.
- Instance noise ablations: Provide quantitative/qualitative ablations on the noise level and annealing schedule, and compare directly to one-sided label smoothing to substantiate the stabilisation claim.
- Operator mismatch: Real-world SR often involves unknown and spatially varying degradations. Discuss robustness to A mismatch and whether A+ can be learned/adapted from data (e.g., blind SR) while preserving the projection’s benefits.
- AffDG/AffLL: Given their weaker results, a brief analysis of failure modes would be useful. Would stronger denoisers (e.g., DnCNN) or alternative density models change outcomes? How sensitive is AffDG to the noise annealing schedule? Could score matching or modern score-based estimators be substituted?
- Diversity vs MAP: The paper notes H[q] differences between cross-entropy and KL objectives. A short empirical note on sample diversity (for stochastic AffGAN) versus determinism (MAP) would clarify practical trade-offs.
- Clarity/typos: A careful proof-read will help (a few minor typos and formatting glitches). Ensure equations and references are consistently formatted.

Questions for the authors
- Data pairing: Did you train AffGAN with strictly paired LR–HR images (x = Ay from the same y), or were HR samples for D unpaired/independent from LR inputs to G? If unpaired, please detail the protocol.
- Instance noise: What precise schedule and variance ranges were used across datasets? How sensitive is training to these choices, and how does it compare to label smoothing when D is trained to convergence?
- A and A+: How is A chosen in practice, and how robust are results to kernel/stride mismatch relative to the true degradation? Are boundary conditions important? Does learning A+ (rather than fixing it to the numerically computed pseudoinverse) degrade LR–HR consistency?
- Computational cost: What are training and inference times compared to strong MSE baselines (e.g., SRCNN/FSRCNN/sub-pixel CNN)? Does the affine projection materially affect runtime at inference?
- AffDG gradients: How many denoiser noise levels are used, and how are they scheduled? Did you try more recent score-estimation methods? Can AffDG be made competitive with stronger denoisers or better schedules?
- Variational connection: For the stochastic AffGAN, do you observe improved diversity or better coverage of pY|X in practice? Any empirical evidence that EpX KL[qY|Xθ || pY|X] decreases during training?
- Metrics vs perception: Can you provide any no-reference IQA or human preference study comparing AffGAN to MSE/MAE on faces and natural images?

Overall, this is a strong, well-argued paper that advances SR with a principled architectural constraint and a compelling amortised MAP perspective, supported by solid experiments and useful theoretical insights.
