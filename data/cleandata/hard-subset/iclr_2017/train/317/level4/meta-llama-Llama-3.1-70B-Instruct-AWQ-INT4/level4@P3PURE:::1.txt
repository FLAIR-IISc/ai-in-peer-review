The paper presents a new framework for solving the super-resolution (SR) problem using amortized MAP inference and a pre-learned affine projection layer to ensure the output is consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. It also proposes three different methods to minimize cross-entropy. Overall, this is a well-written paper with a solid theoretical foundation.

However, I have several comments and suggestions for improvement:

1. The proposed amortized MAP inference is novel and distinct from previous SR methods. When combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), this framework can produce plausible and good results. Compared to another GAN-based SR method, Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network, it is unclear what new insights or improvements this formulation brings to the state-of-the-art.

2. The use of an affine projection architecture as a constraint allows the model to be trained without corresponding high-resolution (HR) and LR image pairs. However, the affine projection layer itself still requires HR and LR pairs for training. Does this imply that the training procedure is simply being transferred from the main model to the affine projection layer?

3. The paper presents numerous results of the framework, including those on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a fair comparison with previous work, it would be beneficial to include results on conventional SR test datasets, such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100.

4. The results presented for natural images are limited to a size of 128x128 pixels. Can the framework perform well on larger images, as SR methods often need to handle inputs of arbitrary size?

5. A typical GAN includes a noise term as a latent space, allowing it to learn a distribution. Did the authors experiment with incorporating a noise vector into their framework?

In conclusion, this paper provides a new framework for SR with a solid theoretical analysis. The idea is novel, and the authors explore various methods to solve the problem. While there are still some questions regarding the necessity and effectiveness of certain components, and more experiments are needed, this work has the potential to inspire the community.
