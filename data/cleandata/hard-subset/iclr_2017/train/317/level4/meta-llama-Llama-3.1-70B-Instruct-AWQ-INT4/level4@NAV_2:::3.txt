The paper proposes a novel amortised MAP estimation method for image super-resolution (SR) problems. The approach learns a neural network that projects to an affine subspace of SR solutions, ensuring consistency with the low-resolution (LR) input. This enables the use of various methods for finding proper solutions, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), noise-assisted, and density-assisted optimisation.

The paper presents a clear and well-motivated approach to addressing the limitations of current single image SR methods, which often rely on empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise mean squared error (MSE) loss. The authors argue that a more desirable approach is to employ amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, preferring solutions that have a high posterior probability and thus high probability under the image prior.

The proposed method is well-placed in the literature, building upon recent work on GANs, denoising, and density models. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical foundations of their approach, including the use of affine projections to ensure consistency between the LR input and the high-resolution (HR) output.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on several datasets, including the challenging task of super-resolving grass textures. The authors also provide a comparison with other methods, including MSE-trained models and soft-constrained GANs, showing that their approach produces sharper and more plausible images.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, with one or two key reasons being the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed amortised MAP estimation method, as well as the thorough analysis and experimental evaluation provided.
