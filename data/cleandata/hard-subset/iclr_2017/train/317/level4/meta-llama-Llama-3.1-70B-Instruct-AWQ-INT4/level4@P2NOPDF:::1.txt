The paper presents a novel framework for solving the super-resolution (SR) problem by introducing amortized MAP inference and utilizing a pre-learned affine projection layer to ensure consistency with low-resolution (LR) inputs. Additionally, the authors propose three distinct methods for minimizing cross-entropy. Overall, the paper is well-written and contributes to the field of SR.

However, I have several comments and suggestions for improvement:

1. The proposed amortized MAP inference is innovative and distinct from previous SR methods. When combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), this framework can produce plausible and good results. Nevertheless, a question arises regarding what this new formulation adds to the latest state-of-the-art, particularly when compared to other GAN-based SR methods, such as Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network.

2. The use of an affine projection architecture as a constraint allows the model to be trained without corresponding high-resolution (HR) and LR image pairs. However, the training of the affine projection layer still requires these pairs. It is unclear whether this simply shifts the training procedure to the affine projection layer. Further clarification on this point would be beneficial.

3. The paper presents numerous results of the framework, including those on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a fair comparison with previous work, it would be helpful to include results on conventional test datasets for SR, such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100.

4. The results for natural images presented in the paper are limited to a size of 128x128 pixels. It is essential to demonstrate the framework's performance on images of varying sizes, as SR methods should be able to handle inputs of arbitrary size.

5. A typical GAN includes a noise term as a latent space, allowing it to learn a distribution. Did the authors experiment with incorporating a noise vector into their framework? This could potentially enhance the model's ability to learn a distribution.

In conclusion, the paper provides a novel framework for SR with solid theoretical analysis. The idea is innovative, and the authors explore multiple methods. Although some questions remain regarding the necessity of certain components and the need for additional experiments, this work has the potential to inspire the community.
