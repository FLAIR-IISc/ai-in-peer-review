This paper proposes a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing SR methods that use empirical risk minimization. The paper's main contribution is the introduction of an affine projection architecture that ensures the output is consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input, allowing for the use of a generative model to perform SR without the need for paired high-resolution (HR) and LR images.

The authors propose three methods to solve the problem of minimizing cross-entropy: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior. The GAN-based approach is particularly interesting, as it allows for the use of a stochastic extension to perform amortized variational inference.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed methods, including a proof that the GAN-based approach minimizes the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the generative distribution and the data distribution. The authors also provide an empirical evaluation of their methods on several datasets, including a 2D toy problem, grass textures, and natural images from ImageNet.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a solid theoretical analysis of the proposed methods. The idea of using amortized MAP inference for SR is novel and has the potential to provide good inspiration to the community.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons for this choice:

1. The paper proposes a novel framework for SR that is well-motivated and theoretically sound.
2. The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation of their methods on several datasets.

Supporting arguments for the reasons:

1. The paper provides a clear explanation of the limitations of existing SR methods and how the proposed framework addresses these limitations.
2. The affine projection architecture is a key contribution of the paper, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of its properties and how it can be used to perform SR.
3. The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides a good understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed methods.

Additional feedback:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing SR methods, particularly those that use GANs.
2. The authors should provide more results on conventional test datasets for SR, such as Set5, Set14, and BSD100, to allow for a fair comparison with previous work.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed methods and how they compare to existing methods.
4. The authors should provide more details on the implementation of the affine projection layer and how it is trained.

Questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on how the affine projection layer is trained and how it is used in the proposed framework?
2. How do the proposed methods compare to existing SR methods in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements?
3. Can you provide more results on conventional test datasets for SR to allow for a fair comparison with previous work?
4. Have you considered using other types of generative models, such as variational autoencoders, in the proposed framework?
