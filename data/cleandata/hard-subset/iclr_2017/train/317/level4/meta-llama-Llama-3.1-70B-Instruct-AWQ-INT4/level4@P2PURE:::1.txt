The paper presents a novel framework for solving the Super-Resolution (SR) problem by introducing amortized MAP inference, which is combined with a pre-learned affine projection layer to ensure consistency with Low-Resolution (LR) inputs. Additionally, the authors propose three different methods for minimizing cross-entropy. Overall, this is a well-written paper with a solid theoretical foundation.

However, I have several comments and suggestions for improvement:

1. The proposed amortized MAP inference is a novel approach that differs from previous SR methods. When combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), this framework can produce plausible and good results. Nevertheless, it is unclear what advantages this new formulation offers over the latest state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods, such as Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network.

2. The use of an affine projection architecture as a constraint eliminates the need for corresponding High-Resolution (HR) and LR image pairs during training. However, the training of the affine projection layer itself still requires these pairs. This raises the question of whether the training procedure is simply being transferred from the main model to the affine projection layer.

3. The paper presents numerous results for the proposed framework, including those on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a fair comparison with previous work, it would be beneficial to include results on conventional SR test datasets such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100.

4. The results presented for natural images are limited to a size of 128x128 pixels. It is unclear whether this framework can perform well on images of larger sizes, which is a critical consideration for SR tasks that often involve inputs of arbitrary size.

5. Typically, a GAN includes a noise term as a latent space, allowing it to learn a distribution. Did the authors experiment with incorporating a noise vector into their framework?

In conclusion, this paper provides a new framework for SR with a solid theoretical analysis, novel ideas, and explorations of multiple methods. Although some questions remain regarding the necessity and generality of the approach, and additional experiments are needed, this work has the potential to inspire the community and contribute to the advancement of SR research.
