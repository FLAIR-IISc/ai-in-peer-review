This paper presents a novel framework for solving the super-resolution (SR) problem through amortized MAP inference, incorporating a pre-learned affine projection layer to ensure consistency with low-resolution (LR) inputs. Additionally, the authors propose three distinct methods for minimizing cross-entropy. Overall, the paper is well-written and contributes to the field of SR.

However, I have several comments and suggestions to further improve the manuscript:

1. The proposed amortized MAP inference is innovative and differs from previous SR methods. When combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), this framework yields plausible and good results. A comparison with another GAN-based SR method, "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network," raises the question of what new insights this formulation brings to the state-of-the-art. It would be beneficial to provide a more in-depth discussion on the advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches.

2. The use of an affine projection architecture as a constraint eliminates the need for corresponding high-resolution (HR) and LR image pairs during training. However, the training of the affine projection layer still requires these pairs. It is unclear whether this simply shifts the training procedure to the affine projection layer. The authors should provide more details on the training process and how it affects the overall framework.

3. The paper presents numerous results for the proposed framework, including natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a fair comparison with previous work, it would be helpful to include results on conventional test datasets for SR, such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100.

4. The results for natural images presented in the paper are limited to a size of 128x128 pixels. It is essential to demonstrate the framework's performance on larger images, as SR methods often encounter inputs of arbitrary size. The authors should provide additional experiments to showcase the scalability of their approach.

5. A typical GAN includes a noise term as a latent space, allowing it to learn a distribution. The authors should consider incorporating a noise vector into their framework and discuss its potential impact on the results.

In conclusion, this paper provides a new framework for SR with solid theoretical analysis. The idea is novel, and the authors explore various methods to solve the problem. While some questions remain regarding the necessity and effectiveness of the approach, this work has the potential to inspire the community. With some additional experiments and clarifications, the manuscript can be further improved to make a more significant impact.
