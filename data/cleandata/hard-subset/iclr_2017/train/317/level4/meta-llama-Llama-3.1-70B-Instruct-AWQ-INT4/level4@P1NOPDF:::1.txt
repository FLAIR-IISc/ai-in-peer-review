The paper presents a novel framework for solving the super-resolution (SR) problem through amortized MAP inference, incorporating a pre-learned affine projection layer to ensure consistency with low-resolution (LR) inputs. It also proposes three distinct methods to address the issue of minimizing cross-entropy. Overall, this is a well-written paper with a solid theoretical foundation.

However, I have several comments and suggestions to further improve the paper:

1. The proposed amortized MAP inference framework is innovative and distinct from previous SR methods. When combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), this framework yields plausible and good results. A comparison with another GAN-based SR method, "Photo-Realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network," raises questions about the added value of this new formulation over the current state-of-the-art.

2. The use of an affine projection architecture as a constraint eliminates the need for corresponding high-resolution (HR) and low-resolution (LR) image pairs during training. However, the training of the affine projection layer still requires HR and LR image pairs. Does this imply that the training procedure is simply shifted from the main model to the affine projection layer?

3. The paper showcases numerous results of the proposed framework, including those on natural images from ImageNet. To facilitate a fair comparison with previous work, it would be beneficial to provide results on conventional SR test datasets such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100.

4. The results presented for natural images are limited to a size of 128x128 pixels. Can the framework perform well on images of larger sizes, as SR methods often encounter inputs of arbitrary sizes?

5. Typically, a GAN includes a noise term as a latent space, allowing for a better illustration of learning a distribution. Did the authors consider incorporating a noise vector into their framework?

In conclusion, this paper offers a novel framework for SR with solid theoretical analysis, presenting a fresh idea and exploring multiple methods. Although some questions remain, such as the necessity of certain components and the need for additional experiments, this work has the potential to inspire the community.
