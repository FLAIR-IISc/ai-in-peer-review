This paper proposes an amortized MAP estimation method for image super-resolution (SR) problems. The approach learns a neural network that projects to an affine subspace of SR solutions consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input, enabling the discovery of proper solutions using various methods, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), noise-assisted, and density-assisted optimization.

The paper presents a clear and well-motivated approach to addressing the limitations of current SR methods, which often rely on empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise mean squared error (MSE) loss. The authors argue that this approach can lead to blurry and over-smoothed outputs, and propose using amortized MAP inference as a more desirable alternative.

The method is well-placed in the literature, building on existing work on GANs, denoising, and density estimation. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical foundations of their approach, including the use of affine projections, GANs, and density models.

The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on several datasets, including 2D toy data, grass textures, CelebA faces, and natural images from ImageNet. The results show that the proposed method can produce perceptually superior SR results, with sharper images and more accurate reconstruction of textures and details.

However, the writing and presentation could be improved. At times, the text is dense and difficult to follow, particularly for readers without a strong background in the subject matter. The authors could benefit from breaking up long paragraphs, using clearer headings, and providing more intuitive explanations of complex concepts.

Additionally, I would like to see more analysis of the resulting networks, including what kind of features they learn and how they compare to other SR methods. This could provide further insight into the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper, but with revisions to address the writing and presentation issues.

Decision: Accept with revisions.

Reasons for decision:

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and clearly explained approach to addressing the limitations of current SR methods.
2. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on several datasets.

Revisions:

1. Improve the writing and presentation to make the text more accessible to a broader audience.
2. Provide more analysis of the resulting networks, including what kind of features they learn and how they compare to other SR methods.

Questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the affine projection layer and how it is implemented in practice?
2. How do the proposed methods compare to other SR methods in terms of computational complexity and training time?
3. Can you provide more insight into the features learned by the resulting networks and how they relate to the SR task?
