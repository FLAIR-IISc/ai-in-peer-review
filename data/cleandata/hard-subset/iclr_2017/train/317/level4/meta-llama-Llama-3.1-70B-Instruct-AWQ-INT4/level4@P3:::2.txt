This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by formulating it as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that traditional empirical risk minimisation methods, which often employ pixel-wise mean squared error (MSE) loss, can produce blurry and over-smoothed outputs. In contrast, MAP inference prefers solutions with high posterior probability, leading to more plausible and visually appealing results.

The paper's main contributions are threefold. Firstly, the authors propose a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that projects the model output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between high-resolution (HR) and low-resolution (LR) inputs. Secondly, they introduce three methods to approximate the cross-entropy problem that arises from MAP inference: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser-guided SR, and density-guided SR. Finally, they establish a connection between GANs and amortised variational inference, providing a theoretical grounding for using GANs in image SR.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the GAN-based approach, which produces the most visually appealing results. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the limitations of MAP inference and the potential of their approach.

The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear and concise. The experimental results are sufficient, and the toy examples and real-world applications are well-chosen. The contributions to the field of super-resolution are novel and relevant.

One minor suggestion is to proof-read the manuscript once more to fix the few typos that were noticed.

Decision: Accept

Reasons for the decision:

1. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to image super-resolution, addressing the limitations of traditional empirical risk minimisation methods.
2. The proposed methods, particularly the GAN-based approach, demonstrate state-of-the-art performance in producing visually appealing results.

Additional feedback:

* The connection between GANs and amortised variational inference is an interesting contribution, but it would be helpful to provide more context and explanation for readers unfamiliar with these concepts.
* The discussion on the limitations of MAP inference is thorough, but it would be beneficial to provide more guidance on how to address these limitations in future work.
* The experimental results could be further strengthened by including more comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and evaluating the performance of the proposed methods on more diverse datasets.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the affine projection layer and how it is integrated with the CNN architecture?
* How do you plan to address the limitations of MAP inference, particularly the issue of mode dependence on the representation?
* Can you discuss the potential applications of the proposed methods beyond image super-resolution, such as in other inverse problems or computer vision tasks?
