**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that projects the model output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between the high-resolution output and the low-resolution input. They also present three methods to solve the optimization problem of minimizing cross-entropy: Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN), denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the GAN-based approach, in producing perceptually superior SR results.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Based on the paper's contributions and the quality of the research, I recommend accepting the paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. Novelty of the amortized MAP inference framework, which provides a new perspective on the SR problem.
2. Solid theoretical analysis and derivation of the optimization problem and the proposed methods.
3. Effective experimental evaluation of the proposed methods, demonstrating their ability to produce plausible and good SR results.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's contributions are well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing SR methods. The proposed amortized MAP inference framework is novel and different from previous SR methods. The use of an affine projection architecture as a constraint is a clever idea, allowing the model to ensure consistency between the high-resolution output and the low-resolution input without requiring corresponding {HR, LR} image pairs for training.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the GAN-based approach. The authors also provide a comparison with other GAN-based SR methods, which helps to establish the novelty and contribution of their work.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

1. Provide more details on the training procedure for the affine projection layer, including the choice of hyperparameters and the computational cost.
2. Include results on conventional test datasets for SR, such as Set5, Set14, or BSD100, to allow for a fair comparison with previous work.
3. Investigate the scalability of the proposed framework to larger image sizes, as SR often encounters inputs with arbitrary sizes.
4. Consider adding a noise term as a latent space to the GAN-based method, as this is a common practice in GANs to better illustrate learning a distribution.

I would like the authors to address the following questions:

1. How does the proposed framework handle cases where the low-resolution input is highly degraded or noisy?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the choice of the affine projection architecture and its impact on the optimization problem?
3. How does the proposed framework compare to other SR methods in terms of computational cost and efficiency?
