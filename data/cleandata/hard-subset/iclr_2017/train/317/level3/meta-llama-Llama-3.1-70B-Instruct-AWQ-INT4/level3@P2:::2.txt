This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by reframing the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors introduce a projection step to maintain consistent high-resolution (HR) and low-resolution (LR) dependencies, which is shown to improve results experimentally. They propose and evaluate three methods to solve the cross-entropy problem in Eq. (9), including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior. The experiments demonstrate that the GAN-based approach yields the most visually appealing results, making it a relevant contribution to future SR work.

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason being the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach. The paper is well-written and presented, with sufficient experiments that include well-chosen toy examples and real-world applications.

The specific question tackled by the paper is how to perform amortised MAP inference for image super-resolution. The approach is well-motivated, as it addresses the limitations of empirical risk minimisation methods, which often produce blurry and over-smoothed outputs. The use of a projection step to maintain consistent HR-LR dependencies is a key contribution, and the evaluation of three methods to solve the cross-entropy problem in Eq. (9) provides a thorough analysis of the proposed approach.

The paper supports its claims through extensive experiments, including a 2D toy problem, grass textures, and real image datasets (CelebA and ImageNet). The results demonstrate that the GAN-based approach, AffGAN, produces the most visually appealing results, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the performance of each method.

To improve the paper, I suggest a few minor revisions. Firstly, the manuscript needs another proofread to fix a few minor typos. Secondly, the authors may want to consider providing more details on the computational cost of each method, as this could be an important factor in choosing the best approach for SR. Finally, the authors may want to discuss the potential limitations of the proposed approach, such as the requirement for a large dataset of HR images for training.

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the affine projection step and its impact on the performance of the SR methods.
* The authors may want to consider evaluating their approach on more diverse datasets, such as medical images or satellite images.
* The connection between GANs and amortised variational inference is an interesting one, and the authors may want to explore this further in future work.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the computational cost of each method, and how this affects the choice of approach for SR?
* How do you plan to address the potential limitations of the proposed approach, such as the requirement for a large dataset of HR images for training?
* Can you provide more insight into the connection between GANs and amortised variational inference, and how this relates to other approaches to SR?
