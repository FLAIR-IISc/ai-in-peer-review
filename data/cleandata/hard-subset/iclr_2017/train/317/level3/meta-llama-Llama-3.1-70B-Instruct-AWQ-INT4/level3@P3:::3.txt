This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that projects the model output onto an affine subspace of SR solutions consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. This approach ensures that the high-resolution output of the network is always consistent with the LR input. The authors also propose three methods to solve the optimization problem: Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN), denoiser-guided SR, and density-guided SR.

The paper is well-motivated, and the approach is well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of current SR methods and the benefits of using MAP inference. The proposed CNN architecture is novel and has the potential to improve the performance of SR methods.

The empirical results are well-demonstrated across several datasets, including the 2D Swiss-roll dataset, grass textures, CelebA faces, and natural images from ImageNet. The results show that the proposed methods can produce perceptually superior SR results compared to traditional methods.

However, the writing and presentation of the paper need substantial polishing and clearer exposition. The paper is sometimes hard to follow, given the complex subject matter. The authors could improve the clarity of the paper by providing more intuitive explanations of the proposed methods and by adding more visualizations to illustrate the concepts.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from more analysis of the learned networks, such as what features they learn and how they relate to the SR task. This would provide more insight into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed methods.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with the condition that the authors address the issues mentioned above.

Specifically, I would like to see:

* Improved writing and presentation to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
* More analysis of the learned networks, such as feature visualizations and ablation studies.
* More discussion on the limitations of the proposed methods and potential future work.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more insight into the learned features of the proposed networks and how they relate to the SR task?
* How do the proposed methods handle cases where the LR input is noisy or of poor quality?
* Can you provide more discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed methods and how they compare to traditional SR methods?
* How do the proposed methods handle cases where the SR task is more complex, such as in the presence of multiple objects or scenes?

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from more references to related work in the field of SR and image processing.
* The authors could provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the hyperparameters used for the proposed methods.
* The paper could benefit from more visualizations, such as images or plots, to illustrate the concepts and results.
