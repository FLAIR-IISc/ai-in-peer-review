This paper introduces a novel framework for amortised MAP inference in image super-resolution (SR) using a combination of affine projections and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors propose a new neural network architecture that projects the model output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring that the high-resolution output is always consistent with the low-resolution input. This allows for training without paired high-resolution and low-resolution images, making the problem more akin to unsupervised learning or generative modelling.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of current SR methods that use empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise mean squared error (MSE) loss. They argue that a desirable approach is to employ amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, which prefers solutions that have a high posterior probability and thus high probability under the image prior.

The authors propose three methods for amortised MAP inference in SR using the affine projection: (1) Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), (2) denoiser-guided SR, and (3) a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior. They provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the methods, including a connection between GANs and amortised variational inference.

The experimental results show that the GAN-based approach, AffGAN, produces the most visually appealing results, especially on natural images. The authors also demonstrate that the affine projection layer does not hurt the performance of CNNs for image SR and can even improve it.

However, there are some limitations and concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the paper does not provide a clear comparison with recent state-of-the-art (SOTA) GAN-based SR methods, making it difficult to assess the added value of the proposed framework. Secondly, the affine projection itself is trained using paired high-resolution and low-resolution images, which seems to transfer the need for pairs rather than eliminating it. Thirdly, the results are limited to 128×128 images, and it is unclear whether the framework can handle larger or arbitrary image sizes typical in SR.

Additionally, the authors do not provide evaluations on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) for fair comparison with prior work. It is also unclear whether a GAN noise latent vector was used or tried to better model distributions.

Overall, the paper presents a novel framework and solid theory, but more experiments and justification of necessity are required. The authors should provide a clearer comparison with SOTA methods, address the limitations of the affine projection, and demonstrate the scalability of the framework to larger image sizes.

Questions to the authors:

1. Can you provide a clearer comparison with recent SOTA GAN-based SR methods, including a discussion of the added value of the proposed framework?
2. How do you address the concern that the affine projection itself is trained using paired high-resolution and low-resolution images, which seems to transfer the need for pairs rather than eliminating it?
3. Can you provide evaluations on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) for fair comparison with prior work?
4. Can you demonstrate the scalability of the framework to larger or arbitrary image sizes typical in SR?
5. Did you use or try a GAN noise latent vector to better model distributions, and if so, what were the results?

Additional feedback:

* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed framework and its theoretical underpinnings.
* The experimental results are promising, especially on natural images.
* The connection between GANs and amortised variational inference is an interesting contribution that warrants further exploration.
* The authors should consider providing more details on the implementation of the affine projection and the GAN-based method, including the architecture of the neural networks and the training protocols used.
