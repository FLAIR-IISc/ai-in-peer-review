**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that projects the model output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring that the high-resolution output is always consistent with the low-resolution input. They then propose three methods to approximate the MAP objective: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on various datasets, including natural images, and show that the GAN-based method produces the most visually appealing results.

**Decision: Accept with Minor Revisions**

While the paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to image SR, there are some concerns that need to be addressed before acceptance. Specifically:

* The authors need to provide more clarity on what their approach adds over recent GAN-based state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods. A more detailed comparison with existing methods would strengthen the paper's contributions.
* The affine projection layer is a key component of the proposed architecture, but it is unclear whether it is truly necessary or if it is simply transferring the need for paired high-resolution (HR) and low-resolution (LR) images to the projection itself. More analysis or experiments are needed to justify the use of this layer.

**Supporting Arguments and Additional Feedback**

* The paper's approach is well-motivated, and the use of amortized MAP inference is a novel and interesting direction for image SR.
* The experiments are thorough, and the results are promising, especially for the GAN-based method.
* However, the paper would benefit from more analysis on the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly with regards to the affine projection layer.
* The authors should also consider evaluating their method on standard SR benchmarks (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100) to provide a fair comparison with prior work.
* Additionally, it would be interesting to see if the proposed framework can handle larger or arbitrary image sizes typical in SR.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you provide more details on the necessity of the affine projection layer and how it compares to other approaches that do not use this layer?
* How do you plan to address the limitation of the proposed approach, particularly with regards to the affine projection layer?
* Can you provide more analysis on the performance of the proposed method on larger or arbitrary image sizes typical in SR?
* How do you plan to extend the proposed framework to other image restoration tasks, such as denoising or deblurring?
