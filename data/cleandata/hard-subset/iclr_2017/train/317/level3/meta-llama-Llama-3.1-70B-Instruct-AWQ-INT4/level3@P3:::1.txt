This paper introduces a novel amortized MAP inference method combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for image super-resolution (SR), which produces plausible SR results. The proposed method uses an affine projection layer to ensure that the high-resolution output of the network is always consistent with the low-resolution input. The authors also propose three methods to solve the optimization problem: GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of current SR methods and the benefits of using MAP inference. The proposed method is well-placed in the literature, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of related work.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly the GAN-based approach, which produces visually appealing results. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with other methods and an analysis of the results.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of what the proposed method adds over recent GAN-based state-of-the-art methods. Additionally, the affine projection layer requires paired high-resolution and low-resolution images for training, which may limit its applicability.

Furthermore, the results are limited to 128×128 images, and it is unclear whether the framework can handle larger or arbitrary image sizes typical in SR. The authors also do not provide a clear explanation of whether a GAN noise latent vector was used or tried to better model distributions.

Overall, I decide to accept the paper with the following key reasons:

1. The paper proposes a novel amortized MAP inference method combined with GANs for image SR, which produces plausible SR results.
2. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison with other methods and an analysis of the results.

However, I would like the authors to address the following points to improve the paper:

1. Provide a clear explanation of what the proposed method adds over recent GAN-based state-of-the-art methods.
2. Clarify the applicability of the affine projection layer, particularly with regards to the requirement of paired high-resolution and low-resolution images for training.
3. Evaluate the proposed method on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) for fair comparison with prior work.
4. Investigate whether the framework can handle larger or arbitrary image sizes typical in SR.
5. Consider using a GAN noise latent vector to better model distributions.

I would like the authors to answer the following questions to clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence to support their claims:

1. Can you provide more details on how the affine projection layer is trained and how it affects the performance of the proposed method?
2. How does the proposed method compare to other MAP inference methods, such as those using variational inference or Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods?
3. Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the architecture of the generator and discriminator networks, the training procedure, and the evaluation metrics used?
