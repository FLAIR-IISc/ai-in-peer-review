## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper presents a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The core idea of projecting solutions to an affine subspace ensuring consistency between LR and HR images is interesting and theoretically sound. The authors explore different methods to solve the resulting optimization problem, including GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using an image prior.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a promising framework and solid theoretical grounding, the lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation on standard benchmarks and limited scope of experiments prevent a positive assessment at this time. The novelty of the approach isn’t convincingly demonstrated as superior to existing state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The paper lacks evaluation on standard SR benchmarks like Set5, Set14, and BSD100. Without these comparisons, it’s impossible to assess the performance of the proposed method relative to established techniques and determine if the added complexity yields tangible improvements. The current experiments on limited datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet crops) are insufficient to justify the claims of improved perceptual quality.
2. **Affine Projection Justification:** The affine projection is a key component of the framework, but the paper doesn’t fully justify *why* it’s necessary. While it ensures consistency, the authors acknowledge that training the projection itself relies on paired data, seemingly negating the benefit of avoiding paired data during inference. Is the improvement gained from the projection worth the initial paired training?
3. **Limited Image Size:** The experiments are limited to 128x128 images. This raises concerns about the scalability of the framework to larger, more realistic image sizes commonly encountered in SR applications. Can the affine projection be efficiently computed for high-resolution images?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **GAN Noise Latent Vector:** Did the authors explore using a GAN noise latent vector to better model the distribution of plausible HR images? This could potentially improve the diversity and realism of the generated results.
*   **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies are needed to isolate the contribution of each component of the framework (e.g., the affine projection, the specific GAN architecture, the loss function).
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method compared to existing SR techniques would be valuable.
*   **Clarify the connection to Variational Inference:** The connection to amortized variational inference is mentioned in the conclusion, but it would be beneficial to elaborate on this connection earlier in the paper and provide more details on how the stochastic extension of AffGAN relates to VAEs.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide quantitative results on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) to allow for a fair comparison with existing methods?
*   What is the computational overhead introduced by the affine projection, particularly for larger image sizes?
*   Can you elaborate on the necessity of the affine projection and explain why it provides benefits despite requiring paired data for its initial training?
*   Have you experimented with different noise distributions (e.g., heavy-tailed distributions) in the instance noise technique?
