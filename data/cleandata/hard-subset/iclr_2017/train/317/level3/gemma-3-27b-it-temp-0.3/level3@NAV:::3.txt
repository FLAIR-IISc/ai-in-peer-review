## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation problem. The core idea – projecting the SR solution onto an affine subspace consistent with the low-resolution input – is clever and well-motivated, addressing the common issue of blurry results from standard empirical risk minimization methods. The exploration of multiple optimization strategies (GAN-based, noise-assisted, and density-assisted) within this framework demonstrates a thorough investigation of the proposed approach. The empirical results across several datasets are compelling and showcase the potential of the method, particularly the GAN-based implementation. Overall, I find this to be a strong contribution with significant potential.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to SR, supported by strong empirical results. However, the presentation requires substantial polishing to improve clarity and accessibility.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully tackles the problem of generating plausible high-resolution images from low-resolution inputs by explicitly incorporating a prior through MAP estimation. The affine projection technique is a key innovation, ensuring consistency between LR and HR images and simplifying the optimization process. The extensive experimental validation on diverse datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the AffGAN approach, which consistently outperforms alternatives. The connection drawn between GANs and amortized variational inference is also a valuable theoretical contribution.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the core ideas are strong, the paper is often difficult to follow due to the complexity of the subject matter and the density of the writing. Several sections, particularly those detailing the theoretical derivations, could benefit from more intuitive explanations and visual aids. A clearer discussion of the limitations of the MAP approach, beyond the brief mention in the introduction, would also be beneficial. 

I would appreciate further analysis of the learned networks. Specifically:

*   **Feature Visualization:** What types of features are learned by the networks, particularly in the AffGAN model? Visualizing these features could provide insights into how the network is leveraging the affine projection and incorporating the image prior.
*   **Ablation Studies:** A more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different components (e.g., the affine projection, instance noise) on performance would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Qualitative Comparison:** A more direct visual comparison of the outputs from the different optimization strategies (GAN, denoising, density) on a consistent set of images would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational cost associated with each method, particularly the AffGAN, would be valuable for practical applications.



I believe addressing these points would significantly enhance the clarity and impact of this already promising work.
