### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically principled framework for single-image super-resolution (SR). The authors compellingly argue that traditional MSE-based methods produce blurry results by averaging over a multimodal posterior of plausible high-resolution (HR) images. To address this, they reframe SR as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, aiming to find the single most probable HR image for a given low-resolution (LR) input.

The core contributions are twofold:
1.  A novel neural network architecture featuring an **affine projection layer**. This layer elegantly enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled output must be perfectly consistent with the LR input. This is a significant departure from prior work that uses soft L1/L2 penalties.
2.  A reformulation of the training objective. With the affine projection in place, the MAP inference task simplifies to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated HR images and the distribution of real HR images. This insightfully recasts the SR problem into one of generative modeling, enabling training without requiring matched {LR, HR} image pairs.

The paper explores three methods to solve this objective: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). Empirical results demonstrate that AffGAN produces the most visually plausible and sharpest results, particularly on challenging texture and face datasets.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This paper is a clear accept due to its novel conceptual framing and strong theoretical contributions. The work provides a solid theoretical motivation for using generative models in SR and introduces an elegant architectural constraint to enforce data consistency. However, the acceptance is contingent on the understanding that the empirical validation needs to be significantly strengthened to fully substantiate the practical benefits of the proposed method.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel Framework:** The formulation of SR as amortized MAP inference, combined with the elegant affine projection layer to enforce hard data-consistency constraints, represents a significant conceptual advance for the field. It provides a principled alternative to the ad-hoc loss combinations often seen in perceptual SR.
2.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The paper's claims, while theoretically sound, are not fully supported by a rigorous and comparative empirical evaluation. The lack of experiments on standard SR benchmarks and the absence of a direct comparison to contemporary GAN-based state-of-the-art methods make it difficult to assess the practical impact and superiority of the proposed framework.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work is its theoretical foundation. The motivation to move from empirical risk minimization (e.g., MSE) to MAP inference is well-argued and directly addresses the known issue of blurriness in SR. The affine projection layer `Π(A,x)` is the paper's most significant technical contribution. It is a principled method to restrict the solution space to only valid HR images, which in turn simplifies the learning objective to matching marginal distributions. This is cleaner than the soft consistency penalties used in related works and opens up the possibility of training on unpaired data, a valuable direction for SR research. The exploration of three distinct optimization strategies (GAN, denoiser, density model) is thorough, even though only the GAN-based approach proved successful in practice.

However, the experimental section does not match the quality of the theoretical contributions. The main weaknesses are:
*   **Lack of SOTA Comparison:** The paper cites Ledig et al. (2016), whose SRGAN model was a key competitor at the time. SRGAN also uses a GAN but combines it with a perceptual loss in feature space and a soft content loss. A direct comparison is crucial to understand what practical advantages the proposed affine projection and MAP formulation offer over SRGAN's approach. Without it, the necessity of the proposed architectural changes remains unclear.
*   **Absence of Standard Benchmarks:** The SR community relies on standard datasets like Set5, Set14, and BSD100 for quantitative comparison. The experiments here are limited to CelebA, ImageNet crops, and a grass texture dataset. While useful for qualitative assessment, the lack of results on standard benchmarks prevents a fair comparison with the vast body of existing work and makes it hard to gauge the method's performance objectively.
*   **Limited Scale:** All experiments are conducted on relatively small images (e.g., upscaling 32x32 to 128x128). While the architecture is fully convolutional and should theoretically scale, GAN training is notoriously difficult on larger images. The authors should discuss the scalability of their framework to resolutions more typical in practical SR applications.

**4. Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the acceptance decision, but addressing them would significantly increase the paper's impact.
*   The discussion in Appendix F about a stochastic generator `f_theta(x, z)` is extremely interesting. This links the framework to amortized variational inference and directly addresses a potential limitation of MAP inference: collapsing to a single mode. A model that could sample multiple, diverse, plausible HR images for a single LR input would be a major step forward. Including even preliminary empirical results from this stochastic version would be a powerful addition.
*   The paper's claim of not needing paired data hinges on knowing the downsampling kernel `A` to compute its pseudo-inverse `A+`. In real-world scenarios, this kernel is often unknown (blind SR). It would be beneficial to discuss this limitation and potential future work on extending the framework to the blind SR setting.
*   The failure of the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods is an important negative result. A more in-depth analysis of why these theoretically plausible methods produced blurry outputs could provide valuable insights for the community.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding and help solidify my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
1.  Could you elaborate on the practical advantages of the hard-constrained affine projection layer over the soft content/perceptual losses used in SRGAN? An experimental comparison on a common dataset would be highly valuable.
2.  The method trains `f_theta` without paired data, but finding the projection `A+` requires knowing `A`. Is my understanding correct that the need for pairs is not transferred but instead circumvented by assuming a known degradation model, which is standard in non-blind SR?
3.  Could you provide the reasoning for omitting evaluations on standard benchmarks like Set5, Set14, and BSD100? Including PSNR/SSIM results on these benchmarks, even if they are not the main focus, would be essential for comparison.
4.  Have you explored applying your framework to larger image resolutions? Are there specific challenges (e.g., training stability, memory) in scaling the AffGAN approach?
5.  Appendix F presents a fascinating link to variational inference via a stochastic generator. Did you run experiments with this model? We are curious if it can produce a diverse set of plausible HR outputs for a given LR input, which would be a compelling demonstration of modeling a multi-modal posterior.
