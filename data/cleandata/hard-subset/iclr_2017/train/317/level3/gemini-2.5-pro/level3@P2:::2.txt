### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to single image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The key motivation is to overcome the characteristic blurriness of traditional methods trained with pixel-wise Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss, which tend to average over the many plausible high-resolution solutions. The authors argue that a MAP objective, which seeks a high-probability solution under the natural image prior, is better suited for producing perceptually convincing results.

The paper's main contributions are threefold:
1.  It introduces a novel neural network architecture featuring an "affine projection layer". This layer elegantly enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled high-resolution output must exactly match the low-resolution input, turning a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one.
2.  It shows that with this architecture, amortised MAP inference is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real high-resolution images.
3.  It proposes and empirically evaluates three distinct methods to solve this cross-entropy minimization problem: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) based approach (AffGAN), a method guided by gradients from a pre-trained denoiser (AffDG), and a baseline using an explicit density model (AffLL).

Through experiments on a toy problem and on challenging real-world image datasets (textures, faces, and ImageNet), the authors demonstrate that the GAN-based approach, AffGAN, produces visually sharp and plausible results that are perceptually superior to those from MSE-trained models and the other proposed MAP methods.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper provides a strong theoretical reframing of the super-resolution task as amortised MAP inference. This is a well-motivated and principled way to address the known perceptual failings of MSE-based objectives and provides a solid foundation for the proposed methods.
2.  The affine projection layer is a significant and practical contribution. It is an elegant architectural solution to enforce data consistency, and the experiments convincingly show that it improves model performance and training efficiency even in a standard MSE setting, highlighting its general applicability.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's core strength lies in its clear and logical progression from a well-defined problem (blurry SR images from MSE loss) to a principled solution (MAP inference). The derivation in Section 3, which connects the MAP objective to minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the data distribution, is sound and compelling. This provides a strong theoretical justification for moving away from paired training data towards techniques from generative modeling.

The affine projection layer is a standout contribution. By building the physical model of downsampling directly into the network architecture, the authors ensure perfect consistency between the low-resolution (LR) and high-resolution (HR) images, which many previous methods only encourage with a soft penalty term. The experiments in Section 5.2 provide excellent validation, showing that this layer not only works as intended but also accelerates training and improves final MSE and SSIM scores compared to unconstrained networks. This demonstrates the layer's value as a general tool for SR architectures, independent of the specific loss function used.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment effectively illustrates the conceptual difference between MSE and MAP solutions and validates that the proposed AffGAN and AffDG methods do indeed approximate the MAP objective. On real image datasets, the authors fairly compare their three proposed methods. While the denoiser- and density-guided approaches were ultimately unsuccessful in producing sharp images, the transparency about these negative results is commendable. The qualitative results for the AffGAN method are impressive, particularly on the grass texture and CelebA datasets, producing sharp details that are plausible even if not pixel-perfect matches to the ground truth. This successfully demonstrates that the MAP objective can lead to perceptually superior results, which is the central claim of the paper. Finally, the introduction of "instance noise" as a GAN stabilization technique is a useful practical insight.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for improving the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   The manuscript is well-written but would benefit from a final proofreading pass to correct a few minor typos and grammatical errors.
*   The discussion of why the AffDG and AffLL methods failed to produce sharp results is somewhat brief and relegated to the appendix. Expanding on this in the main text could provide valuable insights for the community. For instance, is the failure due to inaccurate gradient estimates, a difficult optimization landscape, or a fundamental limitation of using these priors?
*   The "instance noise" technique is interesting and appears crucial for the ImageNet experiments. A more direct comparison to other GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., one-sided label smoothing, which is mentioned) within the main experimental section could better highlight its relative advantages.
*   The connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference, presented in Appendix F, is a very insightful theoretical result. While a full empirical study is rightly left for future work, even a small qualitative example showing diverse samples for a single LR input would have been a powerful illustration of this connection.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you elaborate on the failure modes of the AffDG and AffLL models? Were the blurry results due to the optimization getting stuck in poor local minima, or was the guidance signal from the denoiser/density model fundamentally not sharp enough to encourage the generation of high-frequency details?
*   How sensitive was the training of AffGAN to the hyperparameters of the instance noise, particularly its annealing schedule? Does the optimal schedule vary significantly across different datasets?
*   The paper argues that MAP inference is preferable to MSE. A potential criticism of MAP is that the mode of a distribution can be atypical (e.g., the zero vector for a high-dimensional Gaussian). Your results look very plausible, suggesting this is not a major issue here. Do you have any intuition as to why the modes of natural image posteriors appear to be "typical" and perceptually plausible in the context of SR?
