### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to single image super-resolution (SR) by framing the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors convincingly argue that standard empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise MSE loss leads to blurry results because it averages over the many plausible high-resolution solutions. In contrast, a MAP objective seeks a single, high-probability solution under the natural image prior, which is more likely to be perceptually realistic.

The core contributions are threefold:
1.  A novel convolutional neural network architecture that incorporates an affine projection layer. This layer elegantly enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled output must be identical to the low-resolution input, ensuring consistency.
2.  The formulation of the amortised MAP problem as the minimisation of the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real high-resolution images.
3.  A thorough investigation of three distinct methods to solve this cross-entropy minimisation problem: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided method (AffDG), and a baseline using an explicit density model (AffLL).

The paper demonstrates through well-designed experiments on both a toy dataset and real-world images that this framework is effective. The GAN-based approach is shown to produce the most perceptually convincing results, generating sharp and plausible details that are absent in MSE-trained models.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for this recommendation are the insightful reframing of the super-resolution problem in a MAP inference framework and the introduction of a novel affine projection architecture that elegantly enforces consistency constraints. The paper is well-written, the theoretical motivation is sound, and the empirical results convincingly support the central claims. This work provides a strong theoretical grounding for using GAN-based losses in SR and is a valuable contribution to the field.

### Supporting Arguments

1.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The paper moves beyond simply applying a new loss function and instead builds a strong theoretical case for its approach. By starting from the goal of MAP inference (Eq. 2-4) and deriving the cross-entropy minimisation objective (Eq. 9), the authors provide a clear justification for why their method should be preferred over traditional MSE-based training. This formulation helps to unify the task of super-resolution with generative modeling.

2.  **Novel and Effective Architecture:** The affine projection layer is a simple yet powerful architectural innovation. It directly addresses a fundamental constraint of the SR problem—that any valid solution must downsample back to the original low-resolution input. The experiments in Section 5.2 clearly show that this hard constraint is superior to soft penalties, leading to faster training, better final performance (in both MSE and SSIM), and guaranteed consistency. This is a practical contribution that could be adopted by other SR models.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and progressively build the case for the proposed method. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a clear and convincing demonstration that the AffGAN and AffDG methods are indeed minimising the cross-entropy objective, unlike MSE or MAE. The experiments on grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet) effectively showcase the perceptual benefits of the AffGAN approach, which consistently produces sharper and more detailed results than the baselines. The finding that the GAN-based training is empirically superior to the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods is an important practical result.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the theory is developed step-by-step, and the results are presented with informative figures and tables. The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 shows a mature and thoughtful consideration of the problem.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper is of high quality, and the following points are intended as constructive suggestions rather than criticisms necessary for acceptance.

*   The manuscript would benefit from a final proofread to correct a few minor typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "varational autoencoders" in Sec 5.6, inconsistent capitalization of "ImageNet").
*   The empirical failure of the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods on real images is an interesting negative result. While Appendix E provides some details, a brief discussion in the main paper about the potential reasons for their poor performance (e.g., instability of gradients, insufficient model capacity for the denoiser/density estimator) would strengthen the paper's conclusions and provide valuable insights for future work.
*   The "instance noise" trick described in Section 3.2.1 is an interesting and practical contribution to stabilizing GAN training. The authors could consider highlighting this more, perhaps with an ablation study showing its effect on training stability or final performance on one of the image datasets.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The instance noise trick (Sec 3.2.1) seems effective for stabilizing training. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the results to the specific annealing schedule of the noise variance? Is there a principled way to set this schedule, or was it found through empirical tuning?
2.  Appendix F presents a fascinating connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference. Was this explored empirically? If not, could you speculate on the potential challenges or benefits of such an approach compared to the deterministic one presented in the main experiments? For instance, would it help address the "atypical mode" issue discussed in Section 5.6 by allowing sampling from the posterior?
3.  The denoiser-guided and density-guided methods performed well on the toy problem but failed to produce sharp results on real images. Beyond the discussion in Appendix E, do you have further intuition on why these theoretically-motivated approaches underperformed? For instance, do you believe the issue lies in the capacity of the denoiser/density models, or is it a more fundamental problem with the stability of the gradients they provide in high-dimensional spaces?
