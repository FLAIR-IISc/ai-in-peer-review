This paper presents a novel and principled approach to single-image super-resolution (SR) by framing the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is to generate outputs that are not only consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input but also have a high probability under the natural image prior, thus avoiding the blurry results typical of MSE-based methods. The authors introduce a key architectural innovation: an affine projection layer that mathematically enforces consistency between the generated high-resolution (HR) output and the LR input. To solve the resulting cross-entropy minimization problem, the paper proposes and evaluates three distinct methods: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) approach, a denoiser-guided method, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood-trained prior.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the super-resolution literature. The primary reasons for this decision are (1) the elegant reframing of super-resolution as an amortised MAP estimation problem, which provides a solid theoretical foundation for generating more plausible HR images, and (2) the introduction of a novel and effective affine projection layer to enforce output consistency, which is well-supported by experiments.

The paper successfully tackles the well-known problem of blurry outputs from SR models trained with MSE loss. The motivation is clearly articulated and well-placed in the literature, contrasting the proposed MAP framework with standard empirical risk minimisation. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The Swiss-roll toy example effectively demonstrates that the proposed methods (particularly AffGAN and AffDG) successfully approximate the MAP solution. The experiments on real-world data convincingly show that the GAN-based approach produces visually superior and sharper results, validating the paper's central hypothesis. The utility of the affine projection layer is also demonstrated in isolation, showing it can accelerate training and improve results even in an MSE context.

**Additional Feedback**
- The manuscript is well-written but would benefit from a final proofread to correct a few minor typos.
- While the contributions are novel for the SR domain, the discussion on GAN training stability (e.g., instance noise) could be strengthened by more explicitly comparing it to similar techniques in the broader GAN literature beyond the cited works.

**Questions for Authors**
- The AffGAN results are impressive but, as noted, sometimes exhibit high-frequency noise. Could you comment on the trade-off between sharpness and this artifact? Are there ways to control this, perhaps through the instance noise annealing schedule or architectural choices?
- The denoiser-guided and density-guided methods performed well on the toy problem but failed to produce sharp results on image data. Could you elaborate on why you believe these methods struggled in high-dimensional spaces? Was it an optimization challenge or a more fundamental limitation?
