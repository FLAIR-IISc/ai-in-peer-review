This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. Its core contribution is a neural network architecture featuring an affine projection layer. This layer elegantly ensures that any generated high-resolution (HR) image is perfectly consistent with the corresponding low-resolution (LR) input. This architectural constraint simplifies the MAP objective to a problem of matching the distribution of generated images with that of real data. The authors propose and evaluate three strategies to solve this: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided method, and a density-guided method. Experiments across a toy problem and several image datasets convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach, particularly AffGAN, generates sharp and perceptually plausible results that address the blurriness inherent in traditional MSE-based methods.

I recommend to **Accept** this paper. The decision is based on two key strengths: 1) The work provides a strong theoretical motivation for using generative models for SR by framing the task as amortized MAP inference, moving beyond purely empirical success. 2) The proposed affine projection layer is a clever and impactful architectural contribution that enforces a hard constraint central to the problem, improving both performance and training stability.

The paper’s arguments are well-supported. The theoretical connection between MAP inference and minimizing cross-entropy is clearly articulated. The empirical validation is methodical, beginning with a 2D toy problem that provides clear intuition and verification of the method's correctness. The qualitative results on real-world datasets like CelebA and grass textures effectively showcase the perceptual advantages of generating sharp, plausible details over the overly smooth outputs of MSE-trained models. The experiments also successfully validate the utility of the affine projection layer, showing it can improve performance even in a standard MSE-training regime.

To further improve the paper, I strongly encourage the authors to revise the manuscript for clarity. The writing is quite dense and the exposition of complex ideas is sometimes difficult to follow, which may limit the paper's impact. The work would also benefit significantly from more analysis of the learned networks. For instance, what specific features or structures does the network learn to add in the null-space of the downsampling operator? Visualizing these residuals could provide valuable insights.

I would also appreciate clarification on the following points:
1.  The denoiser-guided and density-guided methods produced blurry results. Could you elaborate on why you believe these approaches underperformed compared to the GAN? Is it a limitation of the specific models used or a more fundamental issue?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study on the impact of "instance noise" and its annealing schedule on GAN training stability and final image quality?
