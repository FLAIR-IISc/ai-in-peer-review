### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single image super-resolution (SR) by reframing the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors cogently argue that standard empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise MSE loss leads to perceptually poor, blurry results because it averages over the multi-modal posterior of plausible high-resolution (HR) images. Their key insight is to instead train a network to directly output the MAP estimate, which corresponds to a mode of the posterior and is therefore more likely to be a plausible natural image.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces a novel neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection layer. This layer elegantly enforces the hard constraint that any generated HR image must downsample exactly to the given low-resolution (LR) input, a physically motivated consistency that is often only approximated with soft penalties in other works. Second, with this architecture, the authors show that amortised MAP inference is equivalent to minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of true HR images. This insight connects SR directly to generative modeling. Third, the paper proposes and empirically evaluates three distinct methods to solve this cross-entropy minimisation problem: one based on Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), one guided by gradients from a pre-trained denoiser (AffDG), and a baseline guided by an explicit density model (AffLL). The comprehensive experiments, including a well-chosen toy example and real-world image datasets, demonstrate that the AffGAN approach produces the most visually appealing and sharpest results.

**2. Recommendation: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It provides two key contributions that advance the field of super-resolution:
1.  It offers a theoretically sound and well-motivated reframing of super-resolution as amortised MAP inference, moving beyond the limitations of standard MSE-based training and providing a clear objective function (cross-entropy minimisation) for generating perceptually plausible results.
2.  The proposed affine projection layer is a simple yet powerful architectural innovation that directly enforces the physical consistency between the LR input and HR output, which is shown experimentally to improve both performance and training stability.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous. The theoretical formulation is clear, elegant, and well-grounded. By identifying the shortcomings of MSE and motivating MAP estimation, the authors provide a principled justification for using generative models like GANs for SR, which at the time was a relatively new but promising direction. The derivation in Section 3, which transforms the MAP objective into an unconstrained cross-entropy minimisation problem via the affine projection, is a significant theoretical contribution.

This theoretical work is strongly supported by the proposed affine projection architecture. This is a novel contribution for SR that directly addresses the problem's core constraint (x = Ay). The experiments in Section 5.2 compellingly demonstrate its value: the projected networks not only enforce the constraint by definition but also converge faster and achieve better MSE/SSIM scores than their unconstrained counterparts. This shows the projection is not just theoretically pleasing but practically beneficial.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The 2D Swiss-roll toy problem is an excellent choice to validate the core hypothesis—that the proposed methods (especially AffGAN and AffDG) are indeed minimising the desired cross-entropy objective and finding the posterior mode, unlike MSE/MAE. On real image datasets (grass textures, faces, ImageNet), the qualitative results clearly show that AffGAN produces perceptually superior images that are sharper and more detailed than those from MSE-based methods. While the other two proposed methods (AffDG and AffLL) are less successful, their inclusion makes the investigation more complete and highlights the practical advantages of the adversarial training paradigm for this task. The finding that AffGAN is the most effective approach is a valuable result that provides guidance for future research in perceptual SR.

**4. Additional Feedback**

This is an excellent paper. The following points are intended as constructive suggestions for further improvement and are not critical to my decision.

*   The manuscript is generally very well-written, but a final proofread would be beneficial to correct a few minor typos and grammatical errors.
*   The discussion of the AffDG and AffLL methods in the main paper is brief. While the appendix provides more detail, a short analysis in the main text on *why* these methods might have failed on complex image data would strengthen the paper. For example, is the failure due to the difficulty of accurately estimating the score function (∇ log p(y)) from a denoiser in high dimensions, or the known challenges of explicit density models capturing the fine details of natural image manifolds?
*   The "instance noise" trick introduced in Section 3.2.1 is an interesting and practical contribution to stabilizing GAN training. While the theoretical motivation provided is good, its impact could be highlighted more clearly, perhaps with an ablation study showing training stability with and without it.
*   The connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference, derived in Appendix F, is a fascinating and important theoretical result. It elegantly bridges the gap between GANs and VAEs for this inverse problem. This connection feels significant enough that it could be mentioned more prominently, perhaps briefly in the main conclusion, to ensure readers do not miss it.

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you elaborate on the practical difficulties encountered with the AffDG and AffLL methods? Was their failure on image tasks a matter of optimisation and hyperparameter tuning, or do you believe there is a more fundamental limitation in using denoiser gradients or explicit likelihoods to guide generation for a complex task like SR?
*   In Section 5.6, you provide an excellent critique of MAP inference, noting that the mode of a high-dimensional distribution can be atypical. Given the connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F, have you performed experiments with a stochastic generator `f_θ(x, z)`? This seems like a natural and promising extension to not only find a plausible HR image but to sample from the posterior `p(y|x)`, which could generate diverse and more "typical" results.
