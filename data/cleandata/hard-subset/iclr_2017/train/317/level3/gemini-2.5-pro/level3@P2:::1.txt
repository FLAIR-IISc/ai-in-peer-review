### **1. Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that conventional empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise MSE loss leads to blurry results, and that finding a MAP estimate which is probable under the natural image prior is a more desirable goal for perceptual quality.

The core contributions of this work are threefold. First, the paper introduces a novel convolutional neural network architecture featuring an "affine projection layer." This layer is designed to project any output into the affine subspace of high-resolution (HR) images that are perfectly consistent with the given low-resolution (LR) input. This elegantly handles the likelihood term in the MAP objective, turning the problem into an unconstrained optimization of the prior term. Second, this formulation recasts SR training as a generative modeling task: minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated HR images and the distribution of true HR images. This has the significant benefit of not requiring paired LR-HR images for training the main generator network. Third, the paper explores three different methods to solve this optimization problem: using Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), guidance from a pre-trained denoiser (AffDG), and guidance from an explicit density model (AffLL). Through experiments, the AffGAN approach is shown to be the most effective, producing sharp and perceptually plausible results. The paper also provides valuable theoretical motivation for using GANs in SR and introduces "instance noise" as a practical technique for stabilizing training.

### **2. Decision**

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper proposes a novel and theoretically well-grounded framework for image SR. The formulation as an amortised MAP inference problem and the introduction of the affine projection layer to enforce data consistency are elegant and significant conceptual contributions.
2.  However, the empirical evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the proposed method's superiority over existing state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods. The lack of experiments on standard benchmarks makes it difficult to assess its practical performance relative to prior work.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its principled approach. The motivation to move away from MSE loss towards MAP inference is well-argued and addresses a known key problem in SR: the generation of overly smooth and perceptually poor images. The derivation that transforms the constrained MAP objective into an unconstrained cross-entropy minimization problem via the affine projection is clear and insightful. This provides a strong theoretical foundation for using generative models for SR, going beyond a purely empirical application of GANs. The connection made between the GAN objective and KL-divergence minimization further strengthens this theoretical grounding. The exploration of alternative methods like denoiser- and density-guided SR, even if ultimately less successful, adds to the thoroughness of the work.

Despite the strong theoretical contributions, the experimental validation is not yet on par with the claims. The main competitors for this work are other GAN-based SR methods, such as the concurrent work by Ledig et al. (2016). While this paper provides a compelling "why" for using GANs, it needs to more rigorously demonstrate that its specific formulation—AffGAN with the hard projection constraint—is practically superior to methods that use a soft content loss alongside an adversarial loss. The current qualitative results on grass, faces, and ImageNet crops are illustrative but not sufficient for a conclusive comparison.

A significant omission is the evaluation on standard SR benchmarks (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100). These datasets are the standard for quantitative and qualitative comparison in the field. Without results on these benchmarks, it is very difficult to situate the performance of AffGAN relative to the large body of existing SR literature. While PSNR and SSIM are rightly critiqued, they are still standard metrics that should be reported for completeness, alongside a more rigorous perceptual evaluation (e.g., a user study or established perceptual metrics) to substantiate the claims of improved visual quality.

### **4. Additional Feedback**

This feedback is intended to help improve the paper and is not a primary factor in the decision.
*   The experiments are conducted on relatively small images (upscaling to 128x128). It would strengthen the paper to show that the framework can be applied to larger, more arbitrarily sized images, as is often required in practical SR applications.
*   The "Criticism and Future Directions" section is excellent. It shows a deep understanding of the potential limitations of the MAP approach and thoughtfully points towards future work on posterior sampling.
*   The "instance noise" technique for stabilizing GAN training is an interesting practical contribution. A more direct ablation study comparing it to other stabilization techniques (like one-sided label smoothing) on this SR task would be a valuable addition.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to better understand the work and its implications:
1.  The SRGAN paper (Ledig et al., 2016) also demonstrates impressive perceptual results using a GAN framework. Could you elaborate on the practical advantages of your AffGAN with its hard affine projection constraint compared to SRGAN's use of a soft content loss? A direct comparison on a standard benchmark dataset would be very insightful.
2.  The affine projection layer depends on a known downsampling operator `A` and a numerically pre-computed pseudo-inverse `A+`. How sensitive is the final image quality to potential mismatches between the assumed kernel `A` and the true, often unknown, downsampling process in real-world images?
3.  The proposed generator is deterministic for a given LR input `x`, corresponding to finding a single MAP estimate. However, the true posterior `p(y|x)` is often multi-modal, meaning multiple plausible HR images could exist. As you allude to in Appendix F, have you experimented with incorporating a latent noise vector `z` into the generator to enable sampling from the posterior? This could potentially capture a richer diversity of textures and details.
