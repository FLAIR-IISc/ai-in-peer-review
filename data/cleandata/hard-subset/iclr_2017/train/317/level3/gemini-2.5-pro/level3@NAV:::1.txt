### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**Summary of Contributions**
This paper presents a novel and theoretically elegant framework for single-image super-resolution (SR), reformulating the task as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The core contribution is a neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection layer, which enforces that the high-resolution output is always mathematically consistent with the low-resolution input. This clever constraint transforms the learning objective into one of matching the distribution of generated images with that of real high-resolution images, thereby enabling training without strictly paired data and providing a principled justification for using generative models. The authors propose three methods to solve this objective, finding that a GAN-based approach (AffGAN) yields the most perceptually convincing results. The work provides a solid theoretical motivation for using GANs in SR, moving beyond simple empirical risk minimisation.

**Decision: Weak Accept**
I am recommending this paper for acceptance, though my decision is borderline. The key reasons for my recommendation are the novelty of the theoretical framework, which elegantly connects MAP inference and generative modeling for SR, and the innovative use of the affine projection for hard data-consistency constraints. However, this is balanced by a significant weakness in the empirical evaluation, which currently lacks comparisons on standard benchmarks, making it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed method against the state-of-the-art.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**
The main strength of this paper is its theoretical foundation. Framing SR as MAP inference and using the affine projection to reduce the problem to distribution matching is a strong conceptual contribution. It provides a more rigorous explanation for why GANs are a suitable tool for this task compared to prior work that often combines adversarial and pixel-wise losses more heuristically.

However, the empirical support for the claimed benefits is not yet sufficient. The primary weakness is the absence of evaluations on standard SR benchmark datasets like Set5, Set14, and BSD100. Without results on these datasets, the claims of producing superior results are difficult to validate, as no direct comparison can be made with the vast body of existing SR literature, including recent GAN-based methods like SRGAN. While the proposed AffGAN model is interesting, its practical advantage over a state-of-the-art GAN trained with a soft consistency penalty (e.g., L1/MSE loss) is not clearly demonstrated against established external baselines.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**
*   **Scalability:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small 128×128 images. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion or experiments on the framework's performance and computational feasibility on larger, more practical image resolutions.
*   **Generator stochasticity:** The generator seems to be a deterministic function of the low-resolution input. The authors could clarify if they experimented with introducing a latent noise vector `z` as an additional input to the generator. This is a common technique to help GANs model the multi-modal nature of the posterior distribution and generate diverse high-frequency details, which aligns well with the paper's stated goals.

**Questions for the Authors**
1.  The affine projection removes the need for paired {HR, LR} data during the main generator training. Could you clarify if learning the pseudo-inverse `A+` itself is entirely unsupervised, or if it requires paired data or assumptions that effectively transfer this dependency?
2.  Could you provide quantitative and qualitative results on standard benchmarks (Set5, Set14) to facilitate a fair comparison with prior work? This would be crucial for establishing the practical impact of your proposed framework.
