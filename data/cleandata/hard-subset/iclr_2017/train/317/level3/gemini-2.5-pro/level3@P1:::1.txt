This paper presents a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) by framing the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors rightly argue that traditional empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise MSE loss leads to overly smooth and perceptually unsatisfying results, as it encourages averaging over the many plausible high-resolution solutions. To address this, they propose a method that seeks a MAP estimate, which by definition corresponds to a high-probability solution under the natural image prior. The core contributions are twofold. First, the paper introduces a novel CNN architecture incorporating an "affine projection layer". This layer elegantly enforces data consistency by ensuring that downsampling the network's high-resolution output perfectly recovers the low-resolution input. This architectural constraint simplifies the MAP objective into a problem of matching the distribution of generated images to the distribution of real high-resolution images, akin to training a generative model. Second, the paper explores three distinct methods to solve this distributional matching problem: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided method (AffDG), and a method guided by an explicit density model (AffLL). Through experiments on a toy problem and various image datasets, the authors demonstrate that the AffGAN approach is the most effective, producing sharp and perceptually plausible results.

**Decision: Accept**

My decision is to accept this paper based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper introduces a novel, elegant, and theoretically well-grounded framework for super-resolution. Formulating the problem as amortised MAP inference and introducing the affine projection layer to enforce the likelihood constraint is a significant conceptual contribution that provides a principled justification for using generative modeling techniques in this domain.
2.  The proposed AffGAN method successfully generates perceptually convincing high-resolution images that are qualitatively superior to those from traditional MSE-based methods, directly addressing the well-known issue of blurriness and producing plausible high-frequency details.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its principled approach. By moving from simple loss minimisation to amortised MAP inference, the authors provide a solid probabilistic foundation for SR. The affine projection layer is a particularly clever innovation. It directly encodes the physical model of image downsampling into the network architecture, guaranteeing that any generated solution is consistent with the low-resolution input. This transforms the complex MAP objective into an unconstrained optimisation problem focused purely on matching the prior distribution of natural images (Eq. 9). This formulation elegantly connects the discriminative task of SR with the broader field of generative modeling and provides a strong theoretical motivation for using techniques like GANs, which have previously been applied to SR in a more ad-hoc manner.

The empirical results, particularly for the AffGAN model, effectively demonstrate the value of this approach. The qualitative examples on grass textures, faces, and ImageNet images (Figures 3, 4, 5) show a marked improvement in perceptual quality. The model generates sharp details and textures that, while not necessarily pixel-perfect reconstructions, are plausible and visually appealing. This successfully validates the paper's core hypothesis: optimising for a high-probability sample under the image prior (via the GAN objective) leads to better perceptual results than optimising for the mean of all possible solutions (via MSE).

Despite these strengths, the paper's empirical evaluation has some limitations. The primary concern is the lack of comparison against contemporary state-of-the-art methods, especially other GAN-based SR techniques like SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016). The experiments are conducted on custom--processed datasets (CelebA, ImageNet crops) rather than standard SR benchmarks (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100), which makes it difficult to assess the method's performance relative to prior work. While the theoretical framework is novel, its practical advantage over a combined perceptual and adversarial loss (as in SRGAN) is not clearly demonstrated.

Furthermore, there is a potential lack of clarity regarding the training data requirements. The paper suggests that the affine projection allows training without paired HR/LR images. However, the procedure for learning the pseudo-inverse `A+` for the projection layer (described in Appendix B) seems to require paired data or at least exact knowledge of the forward downsampling operator `A` to generate such pairs. This should be clarified, as it seems the need for paired data is shifted to a pre-computation step rather than being fully eliminated.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper.

*   **Standard Benchmarks:** To solidify the paper's contributions, I strongly encourage the authors to evaluate their method on standard SR benchmark datasets and provide quantitative comparisons (e.g., PSNR, SSIM, and ideally a perceptual metric like LPIPS or a user study) against other leading methods, including recent GAN-based approaches. This would provide a clearer picture of where the proposed method stands in the field.
*   **Scalability:** The experiments are limited to generating images up to 128x128. Since the architecture is convolutional, it should theoretically apply to images of any size. Could you comment on the scalability of the framework? Have you tested it on higher-resolution images, which are common in practical SR applications?
*   **Stochasticity in the Generator:** The proposed generator `fθ(x)` is deterministic, meaning it can only learn a single mapping from LR to HR. This forces the model to choose one mode from the posterior `p(y|x)`. In Appendix F, you brilliantly connect a stochastic version of AffGAN `fθ(x, z)` to amortised variational inference. Did you experiment with this stochastic generator? It seems like a promising direction to better model the inherent uncertainty of the SR problem and potentially generate diverse, plausible reconstructions for the same input.
*   **Clarity vs. SRGAN:** It would be beneficial to add a more direct discussion on the conceptual and practical differences between your AffGAN framework and the SRGAN approach. While the theoretical motivation is distinct, the end result is still an adversarially trained SR network. What are the key advantages of enforcing the consistency constraint via a hard projection versus a soft penalty in the loss function? The results in Table 1 and Figure 2c begin to address this, but a more explicit discussion in the main text would be valuable.
