### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to single image super-resolution (SR), framing the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The key motivation is to overcome the blurriness inherent in methods trained with pixel-wise Mean Squared Error (MSE), which tend to average over the many plausible high-resolution solutions. The authors make several significant contributions. First, they introduce a neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection layer. This layer elegantly enforces the constraint that the super-resolved output must be consistent with the low-resolution input, effectively handling the likelihood term of the posterior. This transforms the problem into one of maximizing the prior probability of the generated images, which is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real high-resolution images. Second, the paper thoroughly explores three distinct methods for solving this cross-entropy minimization problem: (1) a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) approach (AffGAN), (2) a denoiser-guided method that uses gradients from a pre-trained denoiser to approximate the gradient of the log-prior (AffDG), and (3) a baseline method guided by an explicit, pre-trained density model (AffLL). The empirical results, demonstrated on a toy problem and on real image datasets (textures, faces, and ImageNet), compellingly show that the GAN-based approach produces perceptually superior and sharper results than traditional methods and the other proposed techniques.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a strong contribution to the field of image super-resolution and generative models. My decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The core idea of framing SR as amortised MAP inference, and the introduction of the affine projection layer to enforce consistency, is a significant conceptual advance. It provides a principled framework for moving beyond simple pixel-wise losses towards generating perceptually plausible images.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The authors not only propose a new framework but also rigorously test three different ways to optimize within it. The experiments are well-designed, starting with a simple 2D case to verify the theoretical claims and scaling up to challenging real-world image datasets, which effectively supports the paper's central claims.

### Supporting Arguments

The main strength of this paper lies in its clear theoretical motivation. The limitations of MSE for SR are well-known, and the shift towards MAP inference is a logical step. The main hurdle for MAP inference is modeling the prior `p(y)`, and the paper's approach of instead matching the output distribution `q_theta` to `p_Y` via cross-entropy minimization is clever. The affine projection architecture is the key technical innovation that makes this possible, turning a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one that is amenable to modern generative modeling techniques. This provides a strong theoretical underpinning for using adversarial training in SR, which has been explored in other recent work but often with less formal justification.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment neatly demonstrates that the proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG) do indeed approximate the MAP solution, in contrast to MSE/MAE baselines. The subsequent experiments on grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet effectively highlight the practical benefits, showing that AffGAN can produce sharp, detailed results where other methods fail. The authors are also transparent about the limitations of the denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches, which, despite being theoretically sound, did not perform as well in practice. This honest reporting strengthens the paper. The finding that the affine projection layer can even accelerate and improve training for a standard MSE loss is a valuable secondary contribution.

The paper also introduces "instance noise" as a technique for stabilizing GAN training, which appears to be a useful practical trick with sound motivation. The discussion in the appendix connecting a stochastic version of AffGAN to amortised variational inference is thought-provoking and builds a valuable bridge between two major families of generative models.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, its presentation could be significantly improved. The writing is often dense and assumes a high level of familiarity with multiple advanced topics (e.g., GANs, variational inference, denoising autoencoders, pixelCNNs). This makes the paper challenging to follow in places, potentially limiting its impact. The following points are suggestions to improve clarity and are not part of my decision assessment.

*   **Clarity and Exposition:** The connection between the core objective in Eq. (9) and the three proposed methods could be stated more explicitly in the introduction. A clearer roadmap would help the reader understand that the affine projection is the foundational component, and the GAN/Denoiser/Density models are three different tools to solve the resulting optimization problem. Some of the dense theoretical derivations, particularly for the GAN variant in Section 3.2, might be better suited for the appendix to improve the flow of the main paper.
*   **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The paper demonstrates that the method works but provides little insight into *what* the network learns. The network output is composed of a baseline upsampling `A+x` and a learned residual `(I - A+A)fθ(x)`. It would be highly informative to visualize this residual term. Does it primarily capture high-frequency textures, edges, or other structures? Showing these visualizations could provide powerful intuition about the model's inner workings.
*   **Discussion of Failure Modes:** The paper shows an interesting failure case where a snake is super-resolved into water. This is a fascinating result that speaks to the model's reliance on the image prior. A more detailed discussion of such failure modes would be a valuable addition. When does the model "hallucinate" plausible but incorrect details, and why?

### Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The affine projection relies on computing the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse `A+`. How sensitive is the model's performance to the accuracy of this numerical estimation? In a real-world scenario, the downsampling kernel `A` might be unknown or vary. How do you believe the proposed framework would perform in such a setting?
2.  You note that the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods produced blurry results on real images, despite their theoretical promise. Could you provide more intuition on why these methods failed? For AffDG, is the gradient estimate from the denoiser too noisy or biased in high dimensions? For AffLL, is the issue that even state-of-the-art density models are not yet good enough to provide a useful training signal for this task?
3.  Regarding the instance noise trick for stabilizing GANs: Have you compared its effect to other stabilization techniques like weight clipping or gradient penalty on your specific task? Is it a complementary or alternative approach?
