### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to single-image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue compellingly that standard empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses like MSE leads to overly smooth and perceptually poor results, as such methods are driven to average over the many plausible high-resolution solutions. To address this, they propose training a neural network to directly find a MAP estimate, which by definition corresponds to a high-probability solution under the natural image prior.

The primary technical contribution is a network architecture that incorporates an affine projection layer. This layer ensures that any high-resolution output is perfectly consistent with the low-resolution input, elegantly satisfying the data-consistency constraint of the inverse problem. With this architecture in place, the MAP inference objective simplifies to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of network outputs and the distribution of true high-resolution images.

The paper then thoughtfully explores three distinct strategies to perform this minimization: (1) an approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), (2) a denoiser-guided method using gradient estimates from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder (AffDG), and (3) a baseline using an explicit, pre-trained density model to score generated images (AffLL). Through extensive experiments on a toy problem, textures, faces, and natural images, the authors demonstrate that their framework is effective and that the GAN-based approach, in particular, produces sharp, perceptually convincing results.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  The paper introduces a highly novel and well-motivated framework for super-resolution based on amortised MAP inference. The proposed affine projection layer is an elegant and principled method for enforcing data consistency, moving beyond the soft penalties used in prior work.
2.  The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. By systematically comparing three different optimization strategies on a variety of datasets, the paper provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of its framework and offers valuable insights into the practical challenges and successes of each approach.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical soundness and its rigorous empirical methodology.

The shift in perspective from MSE minimization to amortised MAP inference is well-justified and directly targets the key failing of many SR methods: the production of blurry images that lack realistic texture. The formulation of the problem as minimizing the cross-entropy `H[q_theta, p_Y]` is insightful and cleanly connects the SR problem to the field of generative modeling. The architectural contribution of the affine projection `Π_Ax` is a standout feature. It provides a "hard" constraint that guarantees consistency, which is theoretically cleaner than the soft penalties often used. The proof-of-concept experiment in Section 5.2, showing that this projection even improves performance for standard MSE training, is a strong piece of evidence for the value of this architectural element on its own.

The empirical work is comprehensive. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment is an excellent illustrative example that builds intuition and validates that the proposed methods (AffGAN and AffDG) are indeed minimizing the intended cross-entropy objective in a setting where it can be measured. The subsequent experiments on challenging image datasets like grass textures, where MSE-based methods are known to perform poorly, effectively highlight the perceptual advantages of the MAP approach. The head-to-head comparison of the GAN, denoiser-guided, and density-guided methods is a significant strength, and the finding that the GAN-based approach performs best is an important practical result for the community.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the core ideas and results are strong, the paper could be improved significantly with revisions focused on clarity and deeper analysis. These are suggestions to strengthen the work, not criticisms that undermine its acceptance.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and can be difficult to follow at times. The theoretical exposition in Section 3, particularly the derivation leading to Equation 9, could benefit from more intuition and a clearer step-by-step explanation. A summary table comparing the core ideas, requirements, and observed pros/cons of the three proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) would be extremely helpful for readers to navigate the contributions. A thorough editorial pass to simplify sentence structures and improve the overall flow would make the paper's excellent contributions more accessible.
*   **Analysis of Learned Representations:** The paper demonstrates *that* the method works but could benefit from more analysis of *why* it works. For instance, what does the generator network `f_theta` learn? Visualizing the "residual" component `(I − A+A)f_theta(x)` for various inputs could reveal whether the network is primarily learning to add plausible high-frequency details. For the successful AffGAN model, an analysis of the discriminator (e.g., via activation maps) could provide insight into what image statistics or artifacts it learns to identify as "fake".

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the rebuttal period:

1.  The "instance noise" trick for stabilizing GAN training is interesting and practical. Could you provide more detail on the annealing schedule used in the ImageNet experiments? How sensitive were the results to the specific parameters of this schedule?
2.  The denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods performed poorly on real image data, producing blurry results despite their strong theoretical grounding. Do you have a concrete hypothesis for this? Do you suspect it is primarily an optimization issue, or that the gradient/likelihood estimates from the auxiliary models were not accurate enough for this complex task?
3.  Could you comment on the relative computational costs of the three proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL)? In particular, how does the cost of pre-training the denoiser or the density model compare to the training time of the end-to-end AffGAN model?
4.  The paper finds the pseudo-inverse `A+` numerically. How robust is the final performance to the accuracy of this numerical estimate? Have you explored the performance impact of using simpler, non-optimal upsampling kernels (e.g., bilinear, nearest-neighbor) in the affine projection layer?
