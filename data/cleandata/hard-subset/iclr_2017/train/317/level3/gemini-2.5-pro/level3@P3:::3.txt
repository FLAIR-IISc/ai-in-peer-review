### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper addresses the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR), arguing that standard methods based on empirical risk minimization with an MSE loss lead to perceptually unsatisfying, blurry results. The authors propose a more principled approach by framing SR as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, which seeks solutions that are not only consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input but also have high probability under the prior distribution of natural images.

The core contributions of this work are threefold:
1.  **A novel network architecture featuring an affine projection layer.** This layer is designed to project any network output onto the affine subspace of high-resolution (HR) solutions that are perfectly consistent with the given LR input. This elegantly enforces the constraint `x = A*y_hat` by construction, rather than through a soft penalty.
2.  **Reformulation of amortized MAP inference as a cross-entropy minimization problem.** By using the affine projection architecture, the MAP objective is shown to be equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the network's outputs and the true distribution of HR images. This shifts the training paradigm from supervised learning on paired data to a paradigm similar to unsupervised generative modeling.
3.  **Exploration of three distinct methods to solve this optimization problem.** The authors propose and implement three strategies: (i) an affine-projected Generative Adversarial Network (AffGAN), (ii) a denoiser-guided (AffDG) method that uses the gradients from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder, and (iii) a density-guided (AffLL) method using a pre-trained PixelCNN-like density model.

The paper provides strong empirical validation on a toy Swiss-roll dataset, grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet), demonstrating that the GAN-based approach in particular produces sharp, perceptually convincing results that are superior to traditional methods.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**
**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel and theoretically well-grounded framework for super-resolution based on amortized MAP inference. The affine projection layer is a particularly strong contribution that provides a principled way to enforce the data consistency constraint, which is a core aspect of this inverse problem.
2.  The empirical results are convincing and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed approach. The visual quality of the super-resolved images, particularly from the AffGAN model, is a significant improvement over blurry MSE-based results, successfully producing plausible high-frequency details.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**
The paper's primary strength lies in its novel formulation of the SR problem. Moving from MSE minimization to amortized MAP inference is well-motivated, and the derivation in Section 3, which connects the MAP objective to minimizing the cross-entropy `H[q_theta, p_Y]`, is elegant. The architectural solution of an affine projection layer is a key enabler for this framework, directly satisfying the likelihood term of the MAP objective and simplifying the optimization. This hard constraint is a notable improvement over prior work that often uses soft L1 or L2 penalties on the downsampled output.

The comprehensive exploration of three different optimization strategies (GAN, denoiser-guided, density-guided) is another major strength. This provides a valuable comparative study within the proposed framework and lends credibility to the conclusion that the GAN-based approach is the most effective in this high-dimensional setting. The experiments are well-designed, starting with the 2D Swiss-roll example that provides clear quantitative evidence (Table 1) that the proposed methods successfully optimize the intended objective. The subsequent results on challenging real-world images (textures, faces) effectively showcase the perceptual benefits of the approach. The finding that the AffGAN model produces sharp and plausible textures is particularly compelling.

While the paper is strong, its main weakness is in its presentation. The exposition is often dense and can be difficult to follow without significant domain expertise. Key details, such as the numerical estimation of the pseudo-inverse `A+`, are deferred to the appendix, which can disrupt the flow of the main text. However, this weakness is outweighed by the novelty of the ideas and the strength of the results.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**
These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper would benefit greatly from a thorough revision aimed at improving clarity. The theoretical development in Section 3 is crucial but could be explained more pedagogically. Providing more intuition behind the steps would make the work more accessible. Bringing the core details of the affine projection implementation (Appendix B) into the main text would also help readers better understand the methodology.
*   **Analysis of Learned Networks:** The results are visually impressive, but the paper lacks an analysis of what the generator network is actually learning. It would be valuable to visualize the "residual" component `(I - A+A)f_theta(x)`, which represents the high-frequency details being added by the model. What kinds of structures or textures does it learn to generate? Such an analysis would provide deeper insight into the model's behavior.
*   **Discussion of Method Limitations:** The paper notes that the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods produced blurry results on real images and were not explored further. While Appendix E provides some details, expanding this discussion in the main text would be beneficial. A more in-depth analysis of *why* these methods failed (e.g., inaccurate gradients from the denoiser, optimization challenges, mode-seeking behavior of the density model) would strengthen the paper's contribution.

### **Questions for the Authors**
*   Could you elaborate on the failure modes of the AffDG and AffLL methods on complex image data? Do you believe this is a fundamental limitation of using denoisers or explicit density models to estimate the score function in high-dimensional spaces, or is it primarily an optimization challenge that could be overcome with different training strategies?
*   The affine projection layer relies on a pre-computed pseudo-inverse `A+`. How sensitive is the final image quality to the accuracy of this numerical approximation of `A+`? Have you experimented with learning `A+` as part of the end-to-end training, and how did that compare to fixing it?
*   In Section 5.5, you show an interesting example where a snake is super-resolved into water, which you attribute to water having a higher probability under the image prior. This highlights a potential challenge with MAP estimation. Could you discuss how one might guide the model towards a semantically correct, albeit potentially lower-probability, reconstruction in such ambiguous cases?
