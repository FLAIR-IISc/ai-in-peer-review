

# Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution by reframing image super-resolution (SR) as amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than empirical risk minimization. The authors propose a novel neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection layer that ensures the high-resolution output is always consistent with the low-resolution input, addressing a critical issue in SR where previous methods often produced inconsistent outputs. By leveraging this projection, the problem reduces to minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The paper then proposes and evaluates three methods to solve this optimization problem: Affine Projected GAN (AffGAN), Denoiser-Guided Super-Resolution (AffDG), and Density Guided Super-Resolution (AffLL). The authors provide strong theoretical justification for their approach, including a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference. The experiments demonstrate that AffGAN produces visually superior results on both toy examples and real-world image datasets, with the most compelling evidence being the clear visual improvement over MSE-based baselines.

## 2. Decision

I recommend accepting this paper.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's key strength is its principled theoretical framework for SR. The authors successfully demonstrate that MSE-based approaches produce blurry outputs because they find the mean of the posterior distribution rather than the mode, while MAP inference should produce more visually plausible results. The affine projection layer is a clever and well-motivated solution that ensures consistency between HR and LR images, which is a critical technical contribution. The three proposed methods provide a comprehensive exploration of different approaches to the cross-entropy minimization problem, with AffGAN emerging as the most effective solution. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with well-chosen toy examples (Swiss-roll) and real-world applications (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) that convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for why MAP inference is the right framework for SR.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The manuscript is well-written overall, but would benefit from one final proofread to fix minor typos (e.g., "shown ( )" in the abstract, "H[qθ, pY ] `MSE(x,Ay)" in Table 1). The connection between GANs and amortized variational inference is particularly insightful and should be emphasized more in the conclusion. The paper could also briefly discuss the computational cost of AffGAN compared to other methods, as this is important for practical applications.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that AffGAN produces slightly sharper images but with more high-frequency noise than SoftGAN (Section 5.4). Could you elaborate on how this trade-off between sharpness and noise might be addressed in future work?

2. In Section 5.6, you discuss limitations of MAP inference, particularly its dependence on the representation. How do you reconcile this with the observed visual quality improvements in your experiments?

3. The AffDG and AffLL methods produced blurry results that couldn't be improved with various optimization techniques. Could you share more details about why these approaches failed, and whether there might be modifications to these methods that could yield better visual results?
