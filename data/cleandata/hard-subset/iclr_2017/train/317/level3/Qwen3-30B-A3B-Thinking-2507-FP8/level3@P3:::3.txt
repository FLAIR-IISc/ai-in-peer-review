

# Review: AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a novel approach to single-image super-resolution (SR) by leveraging amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference instead of traditional empirical risk minimization. The authors propose a neural network architecture that projects outputs onto an affine subspace consistent with the low-resolution input, ensuring that the high-resolution output always matches the input when downsampled. This architectural innovation transforms the SR problem into minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The authors then present three distinct training approaches: AffGAN (GAN-based), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). Their experiments demonstrate that the AffGAN approach produces visually superior results compared to MSE-based methods and other baselines across multiple datasets, including synthetic 2D problems, grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet).

## 2. Decision
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of current SR methods: MSE-based approaches produce blurry, over-smoothed results because they average over the posterior distribution rather than finding high-probability solutions. The authors correctly identify that MAP inference would produce more plausible results but is difficult to implement directly. Their proposed amortized MAP inference approach via affine projection is theoretically sound and well-motivated. The key insight that SR can be framed as minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior is compelling and well-supported by their theoretical analysis.

The paper's empirical results are thorough and well-demonstrated across multiple datasets. The AffGAN method consistently produces sharper, more visually plausible results than MSE-based approaches, with the added benefit of exact consistency between low- and high-resolution inputs. The theoretical connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference (shown in Appendix F) is particularly valuable and represents a significant conceptual contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the technical content is strong, the paper suffers from poor exposition that makes it difficult to follow for readers unfamiliar with the subject matter. The writing is dense, with complex mathematical formulations that could benefit from more intuitive explanations. I recommend substantial polishing of the presentation, particularly in Sections 3 and 4, to improve clarity.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing more analysis of what features the learned networks capture. The authors should include visualizations of the intermediate representations or feature maps learned by AffGAN, comparing them to those learned by MSE-based models. This would provide deeper insight into why the MAP approach produces more plausible results.

The connection between GANs and amortized variational inference (Appendix F) should be more prominently featured in the main text, as it represents an important conceptual contribution that could be of broad interest to the machine learning community.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide more details about the learned features in the AffGAN model? How do these features differ from those learned by MSE-based models, and how do these differences explain the improved perceptual quality?

2. The paper mentions that AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations than standard MAP inference. Could you elaborate on this claim and provide evidence from your experiments?

3. In the density-guided approach (AffLL), you note that the density model achieves a log-likelihood of -4.10 bits per dimension. How does this compare to state-of-the-art density models for natural images, and why do you believe this is insufficient for effective training?
