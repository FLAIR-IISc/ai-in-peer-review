

**Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"**

This paper proposes a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce an affine projection layer that ensures network outputs are always consistent with the input low-resolution image, transforming the SR problem into minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and natural image statistics. They present three methods to achieve this: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided), with AffGAN showing the most promising results on real images. The theoretical foundation connecting GANs to MAP inference is well-developed, and the paper establishes an interesting link to variational inference.

**Decision:** Reject. While the framework is theoretically sound and novel, the empirical evaluation lacks critical comparisons needed to validate the approach's superiority.

**Supporting Arguments:** The paper fails to address several key concerns: (1) It does not clearly differentiate its approach from recent GAN-based SOTA methods (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016), making it unclear what novel contribution it offers; (2) It evaluates only on custom datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) without standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100), preventing fair comparison with prior work; (3) It limits results to 128×128 images without addressing scalability to larger, arbitrary image sizes common in SR applications; (4) The affine projection layer requires paired data for training, contradicting the claim that paired data is unnecessary.

**Additional Feedback:** To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) Conduct comprehensive comparisons on standard SR benchmarks; (2) Demonstrate scalability to larger image sizes; (3) Clarify the relationship between their approach and existing GAN-based SR methods; (4) Justify the necessity of their framework over simpler alternatives.

**Author Questions:** 
1. How does AffGAN specifically improve upon Ledig et al.'s GAN-based SR in terms of both quantitative metrics and perceptual quality?
2. Why does the paper not use a GAN noise latent vector (z) to better model the distribution of plausible high-resolution images?
3. Can the authors explain how the framework would handle arbitrary image sizes beyond 128×128, and what modifications would be required?
