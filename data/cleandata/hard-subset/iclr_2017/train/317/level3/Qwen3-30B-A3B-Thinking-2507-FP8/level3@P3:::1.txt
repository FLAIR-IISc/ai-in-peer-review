

# Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors propose a CNN architecture with an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, effectively projecting the output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions. This architectural innovation transforms the MAP inference problem into minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The authors present three methods to solve this optimization problem: an affine-projected GAN (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The theoretical grounding is strong, establishing a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference. The paper demonstrates promising visual results with AffGAN, producing sharper and more plausible SR outputs compared to MSE-based methods, particularly on challenging texture data.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's main contributions are not sufficiently justified against recent GAN-based SOTA methods, and critical experiments are missing for fair comparison with prior work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical framework is novel and well-motivated, but the key contributions lack sufficient justification against recent GAN-based SOTA approaches. The AffGAN method resembles Ledig et al.'s work (2016) but with an affine projection layer instead of soft constraints. However, the paper fails to clearly articulate what specific advantages this affine projection provides over the soft constraint approach used in prior work, especially since both approaches use paired data for training (the affine projection itself requires paired data to compute A+).

The most critical shortcomings are:
- The paper does not evaluate on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100), which are essential for fair comparison with prior work. Without these results, it's impossible to assess whether the method actually outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on standard metrics.
- All experiments are limited to 128×128 images, whereas most SR methods handle arbitrary image sizes. This limitation significantly reduces the practical relevance of the work.
- The paper does not address whether the affine projection layer truly eliminates the need for paired data, as the projection itself requires paired data to compute A+.

The theoretical contribution is strong, and the AffGAN approach shows visually plausible results on some datasets, but the lack of standard benchmark evaluations and the image size limitation prevent us from confidently assessing the method's true contribution to the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions
To strengthen the paper and justify its contribution, the authors should address the following points:

1. **Benchmark evaluation**: Please provide results on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) with quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) to enable fair comparison with prior work.

2. **Image size scalability**: Demonstrate that the framework can handle larger or arbitrary image sizes, as is standard in SR literature. Please include a discussion of how the affine projection layer scales to different input resolutions.

3. **Contribution justification**: Clearly articulate what specific advantages the affine projection provides over the soft constraint approach used in Ledig et al. (2016) and other recent GAN-based SR work. What specific problem does the affine projection solve that the soft constraint does not?

4. **Paired data requirement**: Address the question of whether the affine projection truly eliminates the need for paired data. Since A+ requires paired data to compute, the need for paired data is merely transferred to a different stage of the pipeline.

5. **Latent vector exploration**: The paper mentions a stochastic extension in Appendix F, but doesn't explore using a latent vector in the main AffGAN model. Please discuss whether using a latent vector z (as in standard GANs) would further improve the model's ability to capture the distribution of plausible SR solutions.

6. **Comparison to recent SOTA**: The paper cites Ledig et al. (2016) as the most relevant GAN-based SR work, but the field has advanced significantly since 2016 (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN, etc.). Please compare your approach to these more recent methods.

The paper presents a theoretically sound framework with promising initial results, but without addressing these critical points, it's difficult to assess whether the method represents a meaningful contribution to the field of image super-resolution.
