

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors address the fundamental limitation of current SR methods, which typically use empirical risk minimization with MSE loss and produce blurry outputs due to averaging over the posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution images. Instead, they propose training a neural network to directly calculate the MAP estimate, which should produce sharper and more perceptually plausible images by preferring solutions with high probability under the natural image prior.

The key contributions include: (1) a novel neural network architecture that projects the output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency with the low-resolution input; (2) three methods to solve the resulting optimization problem: Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and density-guided SR (AffLL); and (3) an interesting theoretical connection between GANs and amortized variational inference. The authors demonstrate that their AffGAN method produces visually superior results compared to MSE-based approaches and other methods on multiple datasets.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of image super-resolution with clear empirical validation. The key reason for acceptance is that the authors successfully address a fundamental limitation of current SR methods through a well-motivated theoretical approach that produces demonstrably better perceptual results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical foundation is strong and well-motivated. The authors clearly demonstrate why MSE-based approaches fail for SR (producing blurry outputs due to averaging over the posterior distribution), and why MAP inference would be preferable (producing outputs in high-probability regions of the image prior). Their derivation showing that MAP inference reduces to minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior is particularly compelling.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both a 2D toy problem (where exact MAP inference is tractable) and multiple real-world image datasets. The cross-entropy measurements in Table 1 provide quantitative evidence that AffGAN and AffDG correctly minimize the desired objective, while MSE and MAE-based methods do not. The visual results in Figures 3-5 clearly show that AffGAN produces sharper, more plausible images than MSE-based approaches, especially for challenging textures and natural images.

The authors also provide valuable theoretical insights by connecting GANs to MAP inference and establishing a relationship between AffGAN and variational inference in Appendix F. This connection to existing work in generative modeling strengthens the theoretical foundation of their approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper's technical content is strong, the writing and presentation require substantial polishing to improve clarity and accessibility. The paper is sometimes difficult to follow due to the complexity of the concepts and the density of the exposition. I recommend:

- Improving the organization of the paper to better guide the reader through the theoretical motivation, proposed methods, and empirical results.
- Adding more intuitive explanations of key concepts (e.g., the affine projection layer, why MAP inference produces better perceptual results).
- Providing more analysis of the features learned by the network, which would help readers understand why the method works well.
- Clarifying the practical implementation details, particularly regarding the numerical estimation of the pseudo-inverse (Appendix B), which is critical for the affine projection.

The paper's connection to recent work on GANs for SR (Ledig et al., 2016) is valuable, but the authors could strengthen this by explicitly comparing their theoretical justification to the empirical success of those methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that AffGAN and AffDG produce better results than AffLL, but AffLL is based on a state-of-the-art density model. Could you provide more insight into why the density-guided approach performs worse, and whether this is a limitation of the specific density model used or a more general issue with density-guided MAP inference?

2. The authors note that MAP inference can be sensitive to coordinate transformations (Section 5.6). How does AffGAN's performance compare to other methods when tested on data transformed to different color spaces or feature spaces?

3. The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces more perceptually plausible results than MSE-based methods. Could you provide more detailed perceptual evaluations (beyond visual inspection), such as human subject studies or additional perceptual metrics beyond PSNR/SSIM, to further validate this claim?

4. The affine projection layer is a key contribution. Could you provide more analysis of how this projection affects the network's capacity and what specific features the network learns to produce high-quality reconstructions?
