

# Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors propose an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, transforming the SR problem into a generative modeling task. They present three methods to train this architecture: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with clear connections to probabilistic inference and generative modeling. The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces visually more plausible SR results than MSE-based methods on both synthetic and natural image datasets, particularly for challenging textures.

## Decision
Reject. While the theoretical contributions are strong and the framework is novel, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation on standard SR benchmarks and fails to adequately justify the necessity of the proposed approach compared to recent GAN-based SOTA methods.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical foundation is solid, but the empirical evaluation is insufficient for a conference paper. Key limitations include:

1. The absence of evaluations on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) makes it impossible to fairly compare with prior work. The current experiments only use grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet natural images, which do not constitute standard SR evaluation protocols.

2. The paper claims to eliminate the need for paired HR/LR data during training, but the affine projection layer itself requires paired data for numerical optimization (Appendix B). This undermines the main claim of the paper and transfers the need for paired data to a different stage of the training process.

3. The paper does not clearly demonstrate how AffGAN improves upon recent GAN-based SR methods like Ledig et al. (2016), which already achieved state-of-the-art perceptual quality. Without such comparison, it's unclear what novel contribution the paper makes to the field.

4. The experiments are limited to 128×128 images, which is not representative of typical SR applications that handle larger image sizes. The paper doesn't address how the framework scales to larger resolutions.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
- Conduct experiments on standard SR benchmarks with quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) alongside qualitative results
- Clarify the training process for the affine projection layer and address the paired data requirement
- Provide a more detailed comparison with recent GAN-based SR methods, highlighting specific advantages
- Explore the framework's scalability to larger image sizes and discuss computational requirements
- Consider using a latent noise vector in the GAN framework to better model the distribution of plausible HR images

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your AffGAN approach specifically improve upon Ledig et al.'s GAN-based SR method (which is already state-of-the-art), and what quantitative evidence supports this improvement?

2. The affine projection layer requires paired HR/LR data for numerical optimization (Appendix B), yet the paper claims to eliminate the need for paired data during training. Can you clarify how this requirement fits within the overall framework?

3. Have you tried using a latent noise vector in the GAN framework (beyond the instance noise used for training stability) to better model the distribution of plausible HR images and potentially improve results?

4. How would your framework handle larger image sizes (e.g., 512×512 or higher), which are typical in SR applications, and what modifications would be necessary to scale the approach?
