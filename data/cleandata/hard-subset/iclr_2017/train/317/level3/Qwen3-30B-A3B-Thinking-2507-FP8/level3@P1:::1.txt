

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by introducing amortized MAP inference using a convolutional neural network architecture with an affine projection layer. The authors argue that current SR methods based on empirical risk minimization with MSE loss produce blurry results due to averaging over plausible high-resolution (HR) solutions. Instead, they propose to directly minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the HR image prior, which corresponds to MAP inference. The key contribution is a neural network architecture that projects outputs to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions (ensuring consistency with low-resolution inputs), enabling training without paired HR-LR data. The authors present three methods to approximate this optimization: AffGAN (a GAN variant), AffDG (denoiser-guided SR), and AffLL (density-guided SR). Their experiments show AffGAN produces visually superior results compared to MSE and other baselines, particularly for textures and natural images.

I recommend rejection. The paper's core contribution lacks sufficient novelty and the experimental validation is inadequate for a top-tier conference like ICLR. The most critical issue is that the proposed AffGAN approach appears to be a minor variation of Ledig et al.'s "Photo-realistic Single Image Super-Resolution Using a Generative Adversarial Network" (2016), which already established GAN-based SR as a strong baseline. The paper fails to clearly articulate how their affine projection layer provides meaningful advantages over existing GAN-based SR methods, particularly since their AffGAN results are not substantially better than the prior work.

Furthermore, the paper's claim that their approach "doesn't require paired HR-LR data" is misleading. While the training procedure for the SR network itself doesn't require paired data, the affine projection layer (A+) must be pre-trained using paired data, as shown in Appendix B. This undermines the paper's main selling point and transfers the need for paired data to a different component. The authors should explicitly clarify this limitation rather than presenting it as a major advantage.

The experimental evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive. The authors only report qualitative results on limited datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) without evaluation on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100), which are essential for fair comparison with prior work. Their quantitative results are limited to PSNR and SSIM, which are known to correlate poorly with perceptual quality, and they fail to provide statistical significance testing. The paper also restricts experiments to 128×128 images, while standard SR benchmarks typically use larger images (e.g., 480×640 for Set5), raising questions about the framework's scalability to practical applications.

The paper also lacks exploration of a key GAN component: the latent noise vector. The authors do not discuss whether they tried using a latent vector to better model the distribution of plausible HR images, which is standard practice in GAN-based SR. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether the authors fully leveraged the generative modeling potential of their approach.

The theoretical foundation is strong, and the affine projection architecture is well-motivated, but these contributions are insufficient to overcome the lack of novelty and inadequate experimental validation. The paper would benefit significantly from:

1. More comprehensive evaluation on standard SR benchmarks with quantitative metrics that correlate better with perceptual quality (e.g., LPIPS, FID)
2. Clearer articulation of the specific advantages of their affine projection layer over existing GAN-based SR approaches
3. Investigation of the latent noise vector in the GAN framework
4. Demonstration of scalability to larger image sizes and practical SR applications
5. Clarification about the need for paired data in training the affine projection layer

The paper presents a theoretically sound framework with some interesting ideas, but the lack of novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and misleading claims about the need for paired data make it unsuitable for acceptance at ICLR 2017. The authors should address these fundamental issues before resubmission.
