

This paper presents a compelling reframing of image super-resolution (SR) as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, moving beyond traditional empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors introduce a novel neural network architecture featuring an affine projection layer that guarantees consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and high-resolution (HR) outputs, a critical constraint often overlooked in prior work. This projection layer enables the problem to reduce to minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior, making the approach more akin to generative modeling than conventional SR training. The authors then propose and rigorously evaluate three methods to solve this optimization problem: an affine-projected GAN (AffGAN), denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and density-guided SR (AffLL). The paper's strengths lie in its clear theoretical motivation, elegant architectural innovation, and thorough empirical validation across both synthetic and real-world datasets.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the SR literature by addressing a fundamental limitation of current methods—blurry, over-smoothed outputs due to MSE minimization—and providing a principled alternative through MAP inference. The affine projection layer is a particularly elegant solution to the consistency constraint, and the three proposed methods offer distinct, well-motivated approaches to the optimization problem. The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, with the 2D Swiss-roll toy example providing an intuitive understanding of the theoretical advantages, while the real-world image experiments demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach. The AffGAN method consistently produces the most visually appealing results across multiple datasets, with clear evidence of improved perceptual quality compared to MSE and MAE baselines.

The paper's theoretical contributions are strong, particularly the connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference established in Appendix F. The authors provide thorough justification for their architectural choices, including the careful implementation of the affine projection layer using the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse, and demonstrate its importance through ablation studies. The experimental results convincingly show that the affine projected models outperform soft-constrained alternatives in maintaining LR-HR consistency while achieving superior perceptual quality. The inclusion of instance noise as a training stabilizer for GANs is a valuable practical contribution that could benefit other generative modeling applications.

The paper is well-written, logically organized, and makes a clear case for the importance of the problem and the novelty of the approach. The presentation of the theoretical foundations is accessible and well-motivated, and the empirical results are thoroughly analyzed. The only minor issue is a few typographical errors that would benefit from a final proofreading pass.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Clarifying the relationship between the affine projection layer and prior work on consistent SR solutions (e.g., the "consistent" SR approaches by Garcia, 2016).
2. Providing more detailed discussion on the computational overhead of the affine projection layer compared to standard SR networks.
3. Including a comparison with the most recent SR methods that use perceptual loss functions (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016) to better position the contribution.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1. In Section 3.2.1, you introduce instance noise as a training stabilizer for GANs. How sensitive is AffGAN to the specific noise distribution used, and would the same noise level work across different image datasets?
2. The AffDG method appears to have stability issues during training (Section 5.3 and Appendix E). Could you elaborate on why this occurs and whether the proposed annealing approach is sufficient to address these stability concerns for all datasets?
3. In Figure 5, the AffGAN results sometimes produce plausible but incorrect reconstructions (e.g., the snake becoming water). How does the paper's theoretical framework account for such "plausible but incorrect" outputs, and how might this be addressed in future work?
