

# Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary
This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of image super-resolution by reframing the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than empirical risk minimisation. The authors introduce a novel neural network architecture that projects outputs to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs. This architectural innovation enables the problem to be reduced to minimising the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The authors then propose three distinct methods for solving this optimisation problem: (1) an affine projected GAN (AffGAN), (2) denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and (3) density-guided SR (AffLL). The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides thorough experimental validation across both toy examples and real-world image datasets, demonstrating that the GAN-based approach yields the most visually plausible results.

## 2. Decision
I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a novel architectural approach that addresses a fundamental limitation in current super-resolution methods, and the experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses three key questions that determine its quality:

1. **What is the specific question/problem tackled by the paper?** The authors clearly identify the limitation of current MSE-based super-resolution methods (producing blurry, over-smoothed outputs) and propose MAP inference as a more desirable approach that produces visually plausible results. They provide a compelling theoretical justification using a two-dimensional Swiss-roll example that demonstrates how MAP inference avoids the averaging behavior of MSE.

2. **Is the approach well motivated, including being well-placed in the literature?** The paper is exceptionally well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, clearly distinguishing their approach from existing methods (particularly those using perceptual losses or soft constraints). Their theoretical foundation for using affine projections is strong and well-explained, and they successfully connect their work to broader concepts in generative modeling and variational inference.

3. **Does the paper support the claims?** The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The experiments on the toy Swiss-roll dataset clearly demonstrate that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the cross-entropy objective while MSE and MAE methods do not. The real-world experiments on grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images consistently show that AffGAN produces sharper, more visually plausible results than competing methods. The ablation studies (Section 5.2) convincingly demonstrate that the affine projection does not hurt performance and can even improve training dynamics.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper is already quite strong, but the following suggestions would further enhance its quality:

- The manuscript would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of MAP inference (beyond Section 5.6), particularly regarding the potential sensitivity to the representation (e.g., color spaces or feature spaces) and the potential for mode collapse in the GAN-based approach.

- While the paper mentions that AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations due to the H[qθ] term, a brief theoretical discussion of how this resilience manifests in practice would strengthen the argument.

- A more comprehensive comparison to recent state-of-the-art super-resolution methods (particularly those using GANs) would help position the work more clearly in the current landscape.

- A few minor typos were found (e.g., "a large number of plausible images can explain the LR input" should be "a large number of plausible high-resolution images can explain the same low-resolution input"), which would benefit from a final proofread.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In Section 5.5, you note that "the snake depicted in the third column is super resolved into water which is obviously wrong but still a very plausible image." Could you elaborate on how the model's prior knowledge (or lack thereof) about snakes versus water leads to this particular error, and whether this behavior is consistent with the theoretical properties of MAP inference?

2. The paper establishes a connection between AffGAN and amortised variational inference in Appendix F. Could you provide more insight into how this connection might inform future work on stochastic super-resolution methods, particularly regarding potential trade-offs between perceptual quality and diversity?
