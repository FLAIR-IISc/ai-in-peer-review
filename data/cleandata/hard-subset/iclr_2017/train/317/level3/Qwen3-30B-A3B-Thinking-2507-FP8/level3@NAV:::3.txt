

# Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper proposes a novel amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation method for image super-resolution (SR), addressing a key limitation of current approaches that rely on pixel-wise loss functions like MSE, which produce blurry and over-smoothed results. The authors introduce a neural network architecture that projects onto an affine subspace of valid SR solutions consistent with the low-resolution input, transforming the MAP inference problem into minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. They propose three methods to solve this optimization problem: GAN-based (AffGAN), noise-assisted (AffDG), and density-assisted (AffLL), with AffGAN demonstrating the best perceptual quality on real image datasets.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The work tackles a significant problem in SR with a well-motivated approach that is properly situated within the literature, building on recent advances in generative modeling while addressing key limitations of current SR methods. The empirical results across multiple datasets (2D toy problem, grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet) are well-demonstrated and convincingly show that AffGAN produces visually superior SR results compared to MSE and MAE baselines. The theoretical grounding connecting GANs to amortized MAP inference provides valuable insights into why GAN-based SR methods work well.

The paper's main weakness is its presentation, which needs substantial polishing. The complex subject matter is sometimes difficult to follow due to unclear exposition, particularly in the theoretical sections. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of the learned features in the network, such as what specific image statistics the model captures, to better understand why the MAP approach produces perceptually superior results. Additionally, the paper would benefit from more explicit comparisons with recent perceptual loss-based methods.

To improve the paper, I recommend: 1) Rewriting key sections with clearer explanations of the theoretical contributions, 2) Adding visualizations of learned features or activation patterns, and 3) Including more detailed ablation studies on the impact of different architectural choices. These improvements would make the paper significantly more accessible and impactful.
