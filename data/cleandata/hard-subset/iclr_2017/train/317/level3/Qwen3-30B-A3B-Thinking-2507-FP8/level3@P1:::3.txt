

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by leveraging amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors recognize that current SR methods using empirical risk minimization with MSE loss produce blurry outputs because they average over the plausible high-resolution solutions. Instead, they argue for MAP inference, which prefers solutions with high probability under the natural image prior. Their key contribution is a convolutional neural network architecture that projects outputs onto the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency with the low-resolution input. This allows them to reformulate the MAP inference problem as cross-entropy minimization between the generated and true high-resolution image distributions. They propose three methods to solve this optimization problem: an affine-projected GAN (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces visually superior results on multiple datasets compared to MSE-based methods and other SR approaches, while theoretically connecting GANs to amortized variational inference.

I recommend acceptance with a strong positive recommendation. The core idea of using MAP inference for SR is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation of current approaches. The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear derivation of the affine projection layer and the cross-entropy minimization perspective. The empirical results are thorough and convincing, with comprehensive experiments across synthetic and real-world datasets showing that AffGAN produces perceptually superior super-resolved images that better match human visual perception. The paper's connection between GANs and amortized variational inference is particularly novel and valuable.

The theoretical contribution is strong and well-supported. The affine projection layer is a clever technical solution to the consistency constraint in SR, and the paper provides clear mathematical justification for why this approach enables MAP inference. The authors effectively demonstrate that their methods minimize the desired cross-entropy objective (Table 1), while MSE-based methods fail to do so. The empirical validation across multiple datasets (Swiss-roll toy example, grass textures, CelebA faces, ImageNet natural images) provides convincing evidence for the superiority of their approach. The AffGAN results (Figures 3-5) clearly show sharper, more plausible reconstructions compared to MSE and even other GAN-based approaches.

The paper would benefit from substantial polishing of the writing and presentation. The theoretical sections are dense and sometimes difficult to follow, particularly the derivations in Section 3. The connection between the affine projection and the underlying mathematics could be made more intuitive. The paper also lacks sufficient analysis of what features the learned networks actually capture - a deeper investigation into the representations learned by the AffGAN model would strengthen the paper significantly. The description of the instance noise technique in Section 3.2.1, while theoretically sound, could be better motivated and explained in relation to the practical challenges of GAN training.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify: How do the features learned by the AffGAN model differ from those learned by MSE-based SR networks, and what specific aspects of the image prior do they capture? Additionally, the paper claims that AffGAN "produces the most visually appealing results" but does not provide a quantitative measure of perceptual quality beyond PSNR/SSIM (which correlate poorly with human perception). Could the authors provide a more robust evaluation of perceptual quality, such as a user study or comparison using established perceptual metrics beyond PSNR/SSIM? Finally, the connection to variational inference in Appendix F is intriguing but not fully explored - how would the stochastic version of AffGAN compare to standard variational inference methods in terms of performance and computational efficiency?
