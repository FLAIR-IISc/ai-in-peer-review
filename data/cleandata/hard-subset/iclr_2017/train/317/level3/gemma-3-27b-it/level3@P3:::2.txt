This paper presents a compelling reframing of image super-resolution as amortized MAP inference, introducing a novel network architecture with an affine projection to enforce consistency between LR and HR spaces. The authors thoroughly explore three methods to address the resulting cross-entropy minimization problem – GANs, denoiser guidance, and a density-model approach – and demonstrate superior performance, particularly with the GAN-based method, on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible.

**Decision:** Accept

**Reasons:** The paper tackles a significant problem in super-resolution – generating plausible, visually appealing results beyond simply minimizing pixel-wise error – with a novel and theoretically grounded approach. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, especially the GAN-based implementation, outperforming existing techniques in perceptual quality.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully addresses the limitations of empirical risk minimization (e.g., MSE) in super-resolution by shifting the focus to MAP inference. The introduction of the affine projection is a key contribution, ensuring consistency between the low- and high-resolution domains and simplifying the problem to cross-entropy minimization. The comprehensive evaluation of three different methods for solving this minimization problem demonstrates the flexibility and robustness of the proposed framework. The experimental setup is well-designed, including both a 2D toy problem for validation and more complex real-world image datasets. The quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM, cross-entropy) support the claims of improved performance, and the visual comparisons clearly illustrate the superior perceptual quality of the GAN-based results. The connection drawn between AffGAN and amortized variational inference further enriches the theoretical contribution of the work. 

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is excellent, a careful proofread would be beneficial to eliminate minor typos. I noticed a few instances where wording could be refined for clarity (e.g., slightly awkward phrasing in some sentences). The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is insightful but could be expanded. Specifically, addressing how the choice of prior distribution impacts the results would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The instance noise technique introduced in Section 3.2.1 appears promising. Could you elaborate on the heuristic annealing schedule used for the noise level and discuss its sensitivity to different settings? Did you explore alternative noise distributions beyond Gaussian?
2.  In the AffDG method, you mention loading parameters of the DAE trained at increasingly lower noise levels. Could you provide more detail on this process? Specifically, how frequently are the DAE parameters updated, and what criteria are used to determine when to switch to a DAE trained with lower noise?
3.  The paper states the density model achieves a log-likelihood of −4.10 bits per dimension. How does this compare to state-of-the-art density models trained on similar datasets? Is the performance of the density model a bottleneck for the AffLL approach, or are there other factors limiting its performance?
4.  The Affine projection enforces consistency between LR and HR. Does the choice of the downsampling kernel ‘A’ significantly impact the performance of the models? Have you experimented with different downsampling kernels beyond the Gaussian one?
