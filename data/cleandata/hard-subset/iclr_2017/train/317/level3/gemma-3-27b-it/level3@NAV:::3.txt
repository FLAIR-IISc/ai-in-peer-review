## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation problem. The core idea – training a neural network to project onto an affine subspace consistent with the low-resolution input – is compelling and offers a principled way to address the common issues of blurriness in traditional SR methods. The exploration of different optimization strategies (GAN-based, noise-assisted, and density-assisted) within this framework demonstrates a thorough investigation of the proposed method.  The empirical results across several datasets are well-presented and visually demonstrate the potential benefits of the approach, particularly with the GAN-based implementation. 

I **accept** this paper. The approach is innovative, well-motivated, and demonstrates promising results. The idea of enforcing consistency between LR and HR spaces via the affine projection is a strong contribution, and the exploration of different training paradigms is valuable.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical grounding and the clear connection made to MAP inference. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of moving beyond empirical risk minimization and provide a tangible solution. The results on the Swiss-roll toy problem effectively illustrate the core principle of finding solutions within a plausible subspace. The observed performance improvements on real image datasets, while not always dramatic in quantitative metrics, are visually noticeable and suggest a better perceptual quality. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its exposition. The complex subject matter is presented in a way that sometimes makes it difficult to follow the reasoning and the flow of ideas. Substantial polishing of the writing and presentation is needed. Specifically, more detailed explanations of the mathematical derivations and the implementation details would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the learned representations within the networks are not thoroughly analyzed. While the paper shows *what* the networks achieve, it doesn’t delve into *how* they achieve it – what features are being learned to enforce the consistency constraint and generate high-quality SR images?

**Additional Feedback & Questions:**

*   **Feature Visualization:** Can the authors provide visualizations of the learned filters in the initial convolutional layers? This would help understand what features the network is learning to identify and reconstruct high-resolution details.
*   **Ablation Study on Affine Projection:** The paper demonstrates that the affine projection improves performance. However, a more detailed ablation study varying the strength or implementation of the projection could provide further insights. What happens if the projection is only applied to certain layers or is softened?
*   **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:** While PSNR and SSIM are reported, exploring more sophisticated perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) would strengthen the quantitative evaluation of the method.
*   **Computational Cost:** What is the computational overhead associated with the affine projection layer? Is it significant compared to standard convolutional operations?
*   **Sensitivity to Noise Level (AffDG):** How sensitive is the denoising-guided approach (AffDG) to the choice of noise level during training? What strategies were used to select the optimal noise level?
