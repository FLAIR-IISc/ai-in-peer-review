This paper introduces a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, leveraging convolutional neural networks and generative adversarial networks (GANs). The core idea is to constrain the solution space to be consistent with the low-resolution input via an affine projection, allowing the network to learn an implicit image prior and generate more plausible high-resolution outputs compared to methods relying on pixel-wise loss functions like MSE. The authors present three methods – GAN-based, denoiser-guided, and density-model-guided – and demonstrate promising results, particularly with the GAN approach.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable approach, the lack of comparison to established SR benchmarks and the limited scope of the experiments raise concerns about the practical impact and true novelty of the work.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper effectively motivates the use of MAP inference for SR, highlighting the limitations of MSE-based approaches. The introduction of the affine projection is a clever idea, ensuring consistency between low- and high-resolution images and simplifying the problem formulation. The theoretical connection to GANs and variational inference is insightful. The results on the 2D Swiss-roll dataset convincingly demonstrate the ability of the proposed methods to find MAP estimates. However, the empirical evaluation on natural images is limited.  The primary issue is the absence of results on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100). Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the performance of the proposed methods relative to state-of-the-art SR techniques. The authors acknowledge Ledig et al. (2016) but do not provide a direct comparative evaluation.

Furthermore, the experiments are primarily conducted on relatively small images (up to 128x128 pixels). The scalability of the framework to larger, more realistic image sizes commonly encountered in SR applications remains unclear. The computational cost associated with the affine projection could become significant for higher-resolution images. Finally, while the authors mention the potential for instability in GAN training and introduce “instance noise,” the impact of this technique isn’t thoroughly explored, and its necessity in relation to other GAN stabilization methods isn’t clear.  The statement that "training the projection itself uses pairs — is this just transferring the need for pairs?" has not been sufficiently addressed within the context of training the initial A+ parameter.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Benchmark Comparisons:** The most crucial improvement would be to include comprehensive evaluations on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) and compare the results with relevant baseline methods, including recent GAN-based approaches (e.g., SRGAN, ESRGAN).  PSNR, SSIM, and ideally, perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) should be reported.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Investigate the performance and computational cost of the proposed framework on larger images (e.g., 512x512 or higher).
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more detailed ablation studies to assess the individual contributions of the affine projection, the different loss functions (GAN, denoising, density modeling), and the instance noise technique. Quantify the impact of each component on the overall performance.
*   **Latent Space Exploration:** Was a latent vector input to the generator explored, similar to standard GAN architectures? If not, could integrating a latent vector offer additional control over the generated high-resolution images and improve diversity?
*  **Clarify the data pair usage**: How is the initial 'A+' projection trained? Is this training process separate to the full network training and is it using paired data? This point needs to be made more clear.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Expand the discussion of the limitations of the MAP inference approach, as briefly touched upon in the conclusion. Consider the potential impact of the choice of image prior and the sensitivity of the results to the specific network architecture and training parameters.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why were standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) not included in the experimental evaluation?
2.  How does the computational cost of the affine projection scale with image resolution? Are there strategies to mitigate this cost for larger images?
3.  Can you provide more details on the training procedure for the initial A+ projection, and how this relates to the use of paired data in that stage?
4.  Were any alternative GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) explored in addition to instance noise? If so, what were the results?
5.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the image prior (e.g., the density model used in the density-guided approach)?
