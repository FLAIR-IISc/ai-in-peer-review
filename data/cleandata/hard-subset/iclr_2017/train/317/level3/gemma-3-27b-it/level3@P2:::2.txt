## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper proposes a novel approach to single image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation problem. The authors argue that traditional empirical risk minimization approaches, particularly those relying on pixel-wise losses like MSE, lead to blurry and implausible results due to their averaging behavior. To address this, they introduce a convolutional neural network architecture with a crucial projection step ensuring consistency between low-resolution input and high-resolution output, effectively constraining the solution space to valid SR solutions. They then explore three methods – GANs, denoiser-guided training, and a density-model approach – to minimize the resulting cross-entropy objective, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach with comprehensive experiments.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling argument, a novel architectural component, and strong experimental results. It tackles a significant problem in SR – the generation of perceptually plausible images – with a well-motivated and technically sound approach.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of leveraging MAP inference for SR is well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of MSE-based approaches and the benefits of seeking solutions with high prior probability. The introduction of the affine projection is a key contribution, elegantly enforcing consistency between LR and HR images and reducing the problem to a tractable cross-entropy minimization. This projection is not merely a technical detail but a fundamental shift in how the SR problem is approached, and the explanation in Section 3.1 is clear and convincing. 

The experimental evaluation is thorough. Starting with a well-defined 2D toy problem allows for a direct verification of the proposed methods’ ability to minimize the desired cross-entropy (Section 5.1).  The transition to real image datasets, including grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet images (Sections 5.3-5.5), demonstrates the practical applicability and effectiveness of the approach. The comparisons with baseline methods, including MSE-trained networks and variations of GANs, are appropriately chosen and presented.  The inclusion of quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) alongside visual comparisons provides a balanced assessment of performance. The fact that the AffGAN model consistently outperforms other approaches, particularly in perceptual quality, strongly supports the paper’s claims.

The connection established between the proposed AffGAN method and amortised variational inference (Appendix F) is a significant theoretical insight, broadening the perspective on adversarial training and suggesting avenues for future research. The detailed experimental setup and explanations provided in Appendix D are helpful for reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Typos/Proofreading:** The manuscript contains a few minor typos and grammatical errors. A careful proofread would improve clarity and professionalism. (e.g., "A+A" appears occasionally where it should likely be "(A+A)")
* **Clarify the role of Instance Noise:** While the explanation of instance noise in Appendix C is detailed, it might be beneficial to provide a more intuitive explanation within the main body of the paper. A clearer connection between the instability of GANs and the non-overlapping support of distributions would be helpful for readers less familiar with GAN training challenges.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper focuses primarily on performance and perceptual quality. A brief discussion of the computational cost associated with the proposed methods, particularly the affine projection, would be valuable.
* **Further Ablation Studies:** While the ablation study in Section 5.2 is helpful, further exploring the impact of different components (e.g., skip connections, network depth) on the performance of AffGAN could provide more granular insights.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper mentions limitations of MAP inference related to representation dependence (Section 5.6). Could you elaborate on how the affine projection might mitigate or exacerbate this issue in the context of your proposed approach?
2. In the denoiser-guided approach, how sensitive is the performance to the quality of the pre-trained DAE? Does the performance saturate with increasingly accurate denoising models, or is there a point of diminishing returns?
3. The instance noise technique appears effective in stabilizing GAN training.  Have you explored alternative noise distributions beyond Gaussian noise and, if so, what were the results?
4. While the connection to variational inference is interesting, what practical advantages (if any) does recognizing this connection offer for training or improving the AffGAN model?
