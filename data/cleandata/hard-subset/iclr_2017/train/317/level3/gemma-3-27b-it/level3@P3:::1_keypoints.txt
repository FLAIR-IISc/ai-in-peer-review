- Decision: Reject, with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.
- Contribution: Proposes an amortized MAP inference framework for SR using GANs and an affine projection to enforce LR–HR consistency.
- Theoretical grounding: Solid theoretical connection to amortized variational inference and formulation as cross-entropy minimization.
- Limited empirical validation: Experiments confined to 128×128 images and lack convincing quantitative evidence.
- Missing SOTA comparisons: No head-to-head evaluation against established SR baselines (e.g., Ledig et al. 2016) or standard benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100).
- Insufficient metrics: Qualitative results presented but PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS and other quantitative metrics are absent.
- Affine projection unclear benefit: Paper does not show whether the projection adds value beyond existing GAN-based SR methods or simply shifts the paired-data requirement.
- Need ablation: Requires controlled comparisons with and without the affine projection using the same training procedure.
- Scalability concerns: No evidence the method scales to larger, realistic image sizes or handles arbitrary resolutions efficiently.
- Computational cost: Lacks analysis of the computational overhead of the affine projection, especially for larger images.
- GAN latent space not explored: Fails to investigate using a latent noise vector to model multiple plausible HR solutions and inherent ambiguity.
- MAP advantage under-justified: Needs stronger motivation for choosing MAP inference over alternative SR approaches and explanation of how it overcomes known limitations.
- Related work coverage: Should expand discussion of recent SR advances, particularly GAN-based methods.
- Recommended experiments: Evaluate on standard benchmarks, report PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS, perform ablations, test larger images, and explore latent-variable integration.
- Key author questions: Explain absence of quantitative SOTA comparisons, clarify the distinct advantage of affine projection, justify omission of GAN latent variable, detail computational costs, and show how the design mitigates MAP limitations.
