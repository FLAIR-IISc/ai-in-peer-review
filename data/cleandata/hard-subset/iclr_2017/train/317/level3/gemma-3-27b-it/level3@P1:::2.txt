## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission: AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

**1. Summary of Claimed Contributions**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by reframing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that existing methods based on empirical risk minimization, particularly those employing pixel-wise loss functions like MSE, often produce blurry and implausible results due to their averaging behavior. To address this, they introduce a novel convolutional neural network architecture incorporating an affine projection, ensuring consistency between the low-resolution input and the high-resolution output. This projection simplifies the MAP inference problem to minimizing a cross-entropy between distributions. They then explore three approaches to solve this optimization: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) formulation, a denoiser-guided approach leveraging gradient estimates, and a baseline method using a maximum-likelihood trained image prior.  The authors demonstrate through experiments that the GAN-based approach performs best on real image data, and they establish a connection between their AffGAN model and amortised variational inference. 

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to image super-resolution. The introduction of the affine projection and the subsequent formulation of the problem as amortised MAP inference are significant contributions. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach, especially the GAN-based method.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper effectively motivates its approach by highlighting the limitations of traditional empirical risk minimization strategies in the context of image SR, where multiple plausible solutions exist for a given low-resolution input. The theoretical foundation, particularly the connection between the affine projection and minimizing cross-entropy, is clearly presented and well-justified. The proposed architecture is innovative, addressing a key constraint in the SR problem (consistency between LR and HR images) in a principled manner. 

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The initial experiments on a two-dimensional Swiss-roll dataset convincingly demonstrate that the proposed methods can effectively approximate MAP inference, as evidenced by the cross-entropy values shown in Table 1.  The subsequent experiments on image data demonstrate the visual superiority of the GAN-based approach over other methods, including those based on MSE loss. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing AffGAN to SoftGAN) and the analysis of the affine projection’s effect on training speed and performance provide valuable insights. The connection to variational inference in the appendix further strengthens the theoretical contribution.  The inclusion of different datasets (grass texture, CelebA faces, ImageNet) broadens the generalizability of the findings.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is excellent overall, the following points might help improve it further:

*   **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The affine projection adds computational overhead. A brief discussion about the complexity of the proposed approach compared to other SR methods would be beneficial.
*   **Robustness to Hyperparameters:** A sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, noise levels in the denoiser-guided method, GAN training parameters) would further strengthen the results.
*   **Visual Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** Providing a visual comparison with current state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, SRGAN) would provide a stronger benchmark for the proposed approach.
*   **Minor Typos/Grammatical Issues:** A final proofread to address some minor typos and grammatical inconsistencies would improve the overall polish.
*   **Clarify the role of Instance Noise**: While the benefits are mentioned, the intuitive reason why instance noise stabilizes training, beyond the points made in Appendix C, could be elaborated.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  In Section 5.6, you mention that the MAP estimate might not always appear plausible. Could you elaborate on how the choice of representation (e.g., pixel values vs. learned features) might influence the MAP estimate, and how this might be addressed?
2.  Regarding the instance noise, did you experiment with different noise distributions (e.g., Laplacian, uniform)? If so, what were the results?
3.  You mention in section 5.3 that the AffDG and AffLL models produced blurry results. Could you provide some insights into why these methods didn’t perform as well as AffGAN? Was it related to the limitations of the density model or the difficulty in accurately estimating gradients from the denoiser?
4.  What are the limitations of the affine projection? Are there scenarios where this assumption of linear downsampling might not hold, and how could the approach be adapted in such cases?
5.  How does the choice of the Gaussian kernel size in the affine projection affect the performance of the network? Did you perform any experiments to optimize this parameter?
