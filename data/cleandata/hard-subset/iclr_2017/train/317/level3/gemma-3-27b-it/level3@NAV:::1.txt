## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper presents a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) leveraging amortized MAP inference combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The core idea of projecting solutions to an affine subspace enforcing consistency between LR and HR images is interesting and theoretically well-motivated. The exploration of different optimization methods, including GANs, denoising-guided SR, and a baseline method, demonstrates a thorough investigation of the proposed approach.

**Reject** – While the paper presents a solid theoretical foundation and a potentially promising framework, the lack of comparison with established SR benchmarks and limited scope of experiments prevent a strong endorsement at this time. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation is necessary to demonstrate the practical benefits and establish the method's competitiveness.

The key strength of the paper lies in its theoretical justification for employing MAP inference and the elegant use of affine projections to constrain the solution space. The connection drawn between GANs and amortized variational inference is also noteworthy. However, the empirical evaluation is currently limited. The results are primarily demonstrated on 2D data and 128x128 image patches, hindering a clear assessment of scalability and performance on realistic SR tasks. The claim of achieving “best” results with the GAN-based approach needs to be substantiated with comparisons to state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, Real-ESRGAN) on standard benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100).

Furthermore, the paper’s argument that the affine projection reduces the need for paired HR/LR data is somewhat undermined by the fact that the projection itself is *trained* using paired data. It would be valuable to clarify whether the projection is learned independently or jointly with the rest of the network and if the performance is sensitive to the quality of the paired data used for training the projection. The discussion on whether a GAN noise latent vector was used/tried to better model distributions is also important. The manuscript also does not explicitly explore the impact of the affine projection on computational complexity.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Benchmarking:**  Please provide quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM, and ideally, perceptual metrics like LPIPS) on standard SR benchmarks.
*   **Scalability:**  Investigate the performance of the framework on larger image sizes and assess its computational efficiency.
*   **Projection Training:** Elaborate on the training process of the affine projection – is it pre-trained, jointly trained, and how sensitive is the performance to the quality of paired data used during projection training?
*   **Latent Space Exploration**: How does incorporating a latent vector in the GAN influence the SR results?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each component (affine projection, GAN loss, etc.) to the overall performance.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** Thoroughly discuss the limitations of the MAP inference approach, especially regarding the uniqueness of the mode and its representativeness.



Could you please clarify: 

1.  How does the computational cost of the affine projection layer scale with image size and network depth? 
2.  Was the affine projection initialized randomly or pre-trained, and how does this affect the training stability and final performance?
3.   Did you explore other generative models besides GANs for minimizing the cross-entropy, and if so, what were the results?
