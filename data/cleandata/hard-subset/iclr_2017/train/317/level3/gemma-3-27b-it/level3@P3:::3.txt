This paper proposes an amortized MAP inference method for image super-resolution (SR), leveraging a novel neural network architecture designed to project solutions onto an affine subspace consistent with the low-resolution input. The core idea is compelling – moving beyond empirical risk minimization (and its blurry results) towards a more principled MAP approach. The exploration of multiple optimization strategies (GAN-based, noise-assisted, and density-assisted) within this framework is a strength, and the empirical results demonstrate promising performance across several datasets.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to super-resolution, grounded in a solid theoretical framework. While the presentation requires polishing, the core ideas are significant and the experimental validation is convincing.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary reason for acceptance is the novelty of the approach. Framing SR as an amortized MAP inference problem, and particularly the clever use of the affine projection, is a significant contribution. This projection elegantly addresses the ill-posed nature of the SR problem by enforcing consistency between the LR input and HR output. The exploration of different optimization techniques – GANs, denoising, and density modeling – demonstrates a thoughtful investigation of the proposed framework. 

Empirically, the results support the claims. The 2D Swiss-roll example clearly illustrates the benefit of MAP estimation over MSE. The image-based results, while acknowledging limitations with some methods, show that the AffGAN model produces visually appealing and plausible reconstructions, often surpassing results from traditional approaches. The quantitative metrics, though not always favorable compared to MSE (as expected given the focus on perceptual quality), are reasonable and complement the qualitative observations. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing affine-projected vs. soft-constrained models) provide further support for the proposed method.

Finally, the connection drawn between AffGAN and amortized variational inference is a valuable theoretical insight, bridging the gap between adversarial methods and Bayesian inference.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several areas could benefit from further attention:

*   **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper is dense and sometimes difficult to follow, even for someone familiar with SR and GANs. Expanding explanations of key concepts (e.g., the affine projection, the relationship to variational inference) with more intuitive examples would significantly improve readability.
*   **Feature Learning Analysis:** The review specifically requests more analysis of what the learned networks are actually *doing*. What features are being extracted, and how do they contribute to the improved reconstruction quality? Visualization of activations, filter weights, or feature maps would be valuable.
*   **GAN Training Stability:** The discussion of instance noise is welcome, but a more detailed analysis of its impact on training stability (e.g., learning curves, comparison with other stabilization techniques) would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to baseline SR methods is adequate, a more thorough comparison to current state-of-the-art SR techniques (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) would strengthen the paper.
*    **Failure Cases:** A more in-depth discussion of failure cases, and the potential reasons for them, would enhance the paper's credibility. The example of the snake being reconstructed as water is intriguing and deserves further investigation.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Affine Projection Intuition:** Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the affine projection consistently improves performance, even when using a loss function like MSE? Is there a specific regularization effect at play?
2.  **Feature Visualization:** Are you able to share any visualizations of the features learned by the AffGAN network? This would help to understand what the network is focusing on during reconstruction.
3.  **Instance Noise Tuning:** What is the optimal strategy for tuning the annealing schedule for the instance noise parameter? Is there a principled way to determine the starting and ending noise levels?
4.  **Computational Cost:** What is the computational overhead associated with the affine projection layer? Does it significantly increase the training or inference time?
5.  **Scalability:** Have you investigated the scalability of this approach to higher resolution images and larger datasets? Are there any limitations in terms of memory or computational resources?
