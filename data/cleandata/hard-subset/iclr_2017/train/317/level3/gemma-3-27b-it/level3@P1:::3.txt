## Review for ICLR 2017 – AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

This paper introduces a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that standard empirical risk minimization, particularly with pixel-wise loss functions, leads to blurry results due to averaging over plausible solutions. Instead, they propose a method that directly estimates the MAP estimate using a convolutional neural network, enforcing consistency between the LR input and the HR output via projection onto an affine subspace. Three methods for solving the resulting optimization problem are explored: a GAN-based approach, a denoiser-guided method, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood trained image prior. The paper presents a compelling argument for a MAP-based approach to SR, and the experimental results demonstrate promising performance, particularly with the GAN-based method.

**Decision:** **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a significant problem in SR – the generation of perceptually plausible images – and presents a novel and well-motivated solution. The experimental results are convincing, and the connection drawn between GANs and amortized variational inference is an interesting theoretical contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of enforcing consistency between LR and HR images via the affine subspace projection is both clever and theoretically sound. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this projection helps the network avoid regions of low prior probability, leading to more plausible reconstructions. The exploration of three different optimization strategies – GAN, denoising, and density modeling – provides a comprehensive investigation of potential approaches to solving the amortized MAP inference problem. The results presented in Section 5, particularly the comparison of the different methods on the Swiss-roll toy problem and the grass texture dataset, strongly support the claim that the proposed approach, and particularly the GAN-based version, is effective. The inclusion of analysis of cross-entropy values and consistency errors further strengthens the empirical evaluation. The exploration of the theoretical link between the proposed GAN approach and amortized variational inference provides valuable insights and contextualizes the work within a broader theoretical framework.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper presents a strong technical contribution, the writing could be improved for clarity and readability. The introduction, while well-intentioned, is somewhat dense and could benefit from a more focused explanation of the core problem and the proposed solution. The theoretical sections (Section 3) are mathematically rigorous but can be challenging to follow without a strong background in the relevant areas. Breaking down the equations and providing more intuitive explanations would be helpful.

The presentation of experimental results could also be enhanced. While the figures are informative, providing more qualitative comparisons, perhaps with larger image examples, would strengthen the visual impact. Furthermore, a more detailed analysis of the learned network features – what types of features are learned by the affine projected CNNs, and how do they differ from those learned by standard CNNs? – would provide valuable insights into the efficacy of the proposed method. The ablation studies are not very detailed, and exploring the impact of different architectural choices or hyperparameters would be beneficial.

Finally, the discussion of the limitations of the MAP inference approach (Section 5.6) is appreciated, but could be expanded. Exploring potential alternatives or mitigation strategies for these limitations would further strengthen the paper. The description of experimental settings in Appendix D is extremely detailed and thorough.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Feature Analysis:** Could you provide some insights into the features learned by the affine projected CNNs? Are there any qualitative or quantitative differences in the learned features compared to those learned by standard CNNs trained with MSE loss?
2.  **Ablation Studies:** The paper mentions briefly exploring different configurations. Could you provide a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of different choices of the affine projection layer or the different optimization strategies used for training?
3.  **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of the proposed method compared to state-of-the-art SR techniques? Is the affine projection layer computationally expensive?
4.  **Generalization:** Has the approach been tested on datasets significantly different than the ones presented in the paper? How does it perform on datasets with different types of textures or image characteristics?
5. **Stochastic AffGAN and Variational Inference**: You state that a stochastic version of AffGAN performs amortised variational inference, but don't fully explain the specifics of this stochastic version. Could you expand on this connection, detailing how the stochastic element is implemented and how it relates to the standard variational inference framework?
