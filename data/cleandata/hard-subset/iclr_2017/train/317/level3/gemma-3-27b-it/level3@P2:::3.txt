This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is to constrain the SR network’s output to lie on the affine subspace of solutions consistent with the low-resolution input, effectively enforcing a consistency constraint. The authors then explore three methods – GAN-based, denoising-guided, and density-guided – to train the network within this constrained space. The work is theoretically sound, addresses a key limitation of traditional SR methods (blurriness due to MSE loss), and demonstrates promising results across several datasets.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-motivated approach to SR with encouraging empirical results. While the presentation could be improved, the core contribution is significant and warrants acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for moving beyond empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses like MSE is clearly articulated. The paper convincingly argues that MAP inference offers a more principled approach, favoring plausible solutions aligned with the image prior. The affine subspace projection is a clever mechanism to enforce consistency between LR and HR images, simplifying the MAP estimation problem. The exploration of three different training strategies (GAN, denoising, and density modeling) provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed framework. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment effectively demonstrates the ability of the AffGAN and AffDG models to align with the MAP solution, and the results on real image datasets, particularly the visual improvement in texture details (grass textures) and faces, are compelling. The connection drawn between AffGAN and amortized variational inference further strengthens the theoretical foundation of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, the presentation can be significantly improved. The writing is often dense and requires careful reading to fully grasp the concepts. More intuitive explanations of the mathematical formulations would be beneficial. For example, a more detailed explanation of the connection between the affine projection and the reduction to cross-entropy minimization would improve clarity.

The experimental section could be expanded with more detailed analyses of the learned networks. Visualizing the filters learned by the convolutional layers, particularly in the AffGAN model, could provide insights into the features the network is extracting and how it leverages the affine constraint. Additionally, exploring the impact of different noise levels in the denoising-guided approach and density models might reveal optimal configurations. The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6) is insightful but could be expanded. Discussing potential scenarios where the MAP estimate might be undesirable or misleading would add nuance to the presentation.

The notation throughout the paper could be made more consistent. While generally clear, some equations could benefit from better formatting and explanations of the variables.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. In Section 3.2.1, you mention instance noise being related to label smoothing but avoiding its bias. Could you elaborate on the specific bias introduced by label smoothing and how instance noise mitigates it? A visual representation might be helpful.

2. The results for AffDG and AffLL were less promising compared to AffGAN. Could you discuss potential reasons for this, particularly regarding the stability of training and the effectiveness of the gradient estimates from the denoising network and the density model?

3. In the conclusion, you mention a stochastic extension of AffGAN performing amortized variational inference. Could you provide a high-level overview of how this stochastic extension differs from the original AffGAN and why it leads to a variational inference formulation? Is this described in Appendix F? If so, pointing to the exact equations would be helpful.

4.  Regarding the performance on ImageNet (Figure 5), the example of the snake being "super-resolved" into water is intriguing.  Is this a common occurrence?  Could you quantify how often the model generates such plausibly incorrect but high-probability outputs?
