## Review for ICLR 2017 - AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that existing methods relying on empirical risk minimization (e.g., MSE loss) produce blurry results due to averaging over plausible solutions, and propose a framework that directly estimates the MAP estimate using convolutional neural networks. A key contribution is a novel network architecture incorporating an affine projection to ensure consistency between the low-resolution input and the high-resolution output. Three methods – GAN-based, denoiser-guided, and maximum-likelihood trained prior – are explored to solve the resulting optimization problem, with the GAN approach showing the best performance on real images. The paper also establishes a connection between GANs and amortized variational inference.

**Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially promising approach, the lack of comparison to established state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks, coupled with limited experimental scope, prevents a positive assessment at this time. The novelty and theoretical grounding are appreciated, but insufficient empirical evidence supports the claim of substantial improvement over existing techniques.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of moving beyond empirical risk minimization and directly optimizing for MAP estimation in SR is appealing. The proposed affine projection is a clever way to constrain the solution space and ensure consistency between input and output. The exploration of three different optimization methods within the amortized MAP framework demonstrates a good breadth of investigation. However, the primary weakness lies in the evaluation. The experiments are largely conducted on relatively simple datasets (Swiss-roll, grass textures) and limited to 128x128 images.  Crucially, results are not presented on standard SR benchmarks like Set5, Set14, or BSD100, making it difficult to assess the practical impact of the proposed methods compared to existing, highly optimized techniques (e.g., EDSR, RCAN). Without such comparisons, it's difficult to determine if the observed improvements are significant or simply a result of different experimental settings.

Furthermore, the paper states the affine projection removes the need for paired {HR,LR} data, yet the training of the projection itself (finding A+) appears to require paired examples (see Appendix B). This raises a question about whether the benefit of avoiding paired data during inference is offset by the need for it during the initial projection learning phase. The claim of improved perceptual quality based on visual inspection also lacks a quantitative assessment using established perceptual metrics beyond PSNR and SSIM.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Benchmark Evaluation:**  The most important improvement would be to evaluate the proposed methods on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100). This is essential for a fair comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Scalability:** The experiments are limited to 128x128 images. It would be beneficial to demonstrate the scalability of the framework to larger, more realistic image sizes.
*   **Latent Space Exploration:**  Did the authors explore incorporating a noise latent vector (z) into the GAN generator, as is common practice? This could potentially improve the diversity of the generated high-resolution images and address the issue of the model “dreaming up” plausible but incorrect details (as observed in the ImageNET example).
*   **Ablation Studies:** Provide a more thorough ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (e.g., the affine projection, the specific GAN training techniques, the choice of loss function).
*    **Clarify A+ Training:** Elaborate on the data requirements for training the affine projection (A+). If paired data is necessary, clarify the extent to which this requirement diminishes the benefit of the amortized MAP approach.
*   **Perceptual Metrics:**  Include evaluation using more advanced perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) to better quantify the perceptual quality of the generated images.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why were standard SR benchmark datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100) not used for evaluation?  What were the limitations that prevented their inclusion?
2.  The affine projection seemingly requires paired {HR,LR} data for initial training of A+. How does this impact the overall claim of avoiding paired data during training?
3.  Was a noise latent vector (z) explored in the GAN implementation? If not, why?  How might incorporating a latent vector affect the results?
4.  Beyond visual inspection, what quantitative metrics were used to assess the perceptual quality of the generated images, and why were PSNR/SSIM the only metrics reported?
5.  Can the proposed framework be easily extended to handle arbitrary image scales (e.g., 2x, 4x, 8x) without significant modifications?
