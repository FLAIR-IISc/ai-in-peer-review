## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper presents a compelling reframing of image super-resolution (SR) as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation problem. The core contribution lies in introducing an affine projection within the network architecture, ensuring consistency between low- and high-resolution representations. The authors then explore three methods – GANs, denoiser-guided training, and a baseline density model – to solve the resulting cross-entropy optimization, demonstrating that the GAN-based approach yields the most visually appealing results. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, with a strong theoretical grounding and comprehensive experimental validation. 

**Accept** – This is a very good paper that offers a novel and insightful perspective on SR. The theoretical justification is strong, and the empirical results are convincing. The exploration of different optimization methods, and the clear demonstration of the effectiveness of the GAN approach, are particularly noteworthy. 

The strength of this paper lies in its elegant connection between SR and MAP estimation, coupled with the practical implementation through the affine projection layer. The experiments are well-designed, ranging from a simplified 2D case to complex natural images, providing sufficient evidence to support the claims.  The ablation studies (e.g., comparing affine-projected vs. soft-constrained models) are particularly insightful. The authors correctly identify the limitations of MSE-based approaches and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of aligning the solution with the image prior.  The connection established between AffGAN and amortised variational inference in the appendix further enhances the significance of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The minor typos noted by the supervisor should be addressed.
*   While the explanation of instance noise is thorough, it might benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* it helps stabilize training, geared towards readers less familiar with GAN training intricacies.
*   The discussion of limitations in Section 5.6 is excellent, but could perhaps be expanded upon. Specifically, acknowledging potential biases introduced by the specific priors learned during training, and how these might affect generalization to different types of images, would strengthen the paper.
*   It would be helpful to visualise the affine projection more directly, perhaps showing examples of the constrained solution space for a few representative LR inputs.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper states the affine projection “ensures that the network always outputs estimates that are consistent with the inputs.” Could you elaborate on the implications of this constraint in scenarios where the LR input is severely degraded (e.g., very low bit-depth or significant noise)? Does the projection potentially discard valuable information in such cases?
2.  The instance noise technique seems particularly effective. Did you explore other noise distributions (e.g., Laplacian, uniform) and if so, how did their performance compare to the Gaussian noise used in the experiments?
3.  In the context of the connection to variational inference (Appendix F), what specific advantages does this perspective offer for future research directions beyond those already mentioned? Would it open doors to utilizing techniques from the variational autoencoder literature to further improve SR performance?
