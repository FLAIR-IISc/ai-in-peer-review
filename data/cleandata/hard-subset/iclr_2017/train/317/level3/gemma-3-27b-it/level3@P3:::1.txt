This paper introduces a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, combined with Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors propose a convolutional neural network architecture with an affine projection to enforce consistency between low- and high-resolution images, effectively reducing the SR problem to minimizing cross-entropy between distributions. They explore three approaches for this minimization: GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a density-model guided approach, with the GAN-based method showing the most promising results. The work establishes a connection between this approach and amortized variational inference.

**Decision: Reject** with strong encouragement for revision and resubmission. While the paper presents a novel framework and demonstrates solid theoretical grounding, the limited empirical evaluation and lack of convincing justification for its necessity prevent acceptance in its current form.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Validation & Lack of Comparison to SOTA:** The core claim of novelty hinges on improving SR performance through MAP inference. However, the experiments are largely confined to 128x128 images and lack comparison to established state-of-the-art (SOTA) SR methods on standard benchmarks like Set5, Set14, and BSD100. Without these comparisons, it’s difficult to ascertain the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The qualitative results, while visually appealing, are not sufficient to demonstrate superiority. The authors acknowledge Ledig et al. (2016), a key baseline, but don’t offer a direct head-to-head comparison.

2. **Affine Projection’s True Contribution:** The affine projection is a key component of the architecture. While it ensures consistency, the paper doesn’t fully clarify whether this consistency is *already* implicitly addressed in current GAN-based SR methods. The authors state the projection "transfers the need for pairs," but the training of the projection *itself* utilizes paired data. This raises the question: is the affine projection adding genuine value, or merely shifting *where* the paired data requirement exists? A more detailed analysis comparing performance with and without the projection, trained consistently, is needed.

3. **GAN Latent Space Exploration:** The paper doesn’t explore the use of a GAN noise latent vector to further model the distribution of possible high-resolution solutions. Given the focus on MAP inference and modeling the prior, incorporating a latent variable seems like a natural extension and a potentially powerful way to capture the inherent ambiguity in the SR problem. The paper doesn’t even mention this as a possible avenue for future work.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Extend Experiments:** A comprehensive evaluation on standard SR benchmarks (Set5, Set14, BSD100) is crucial. Report PSNR, SSIM, and ideally, perceptual metrics like LPIPS.
* **Ablation Study:** Conduct an ablation study to isolate the impact of the affine projection. Compare performance with and without the projection, using the same training procedure.
* **Address Pairwise Dependency:** Clearly articulate how the affine projection alleviates the need for paired data, considering it's trained *with* paired data initially.
* **Larger Image Sizes:** Demonstrate scalability to larger, more realistic image sizes. Address whether the framework can handle arbitrary image resolutions efficiently.
* **Latent Variable Investigation:** Explore the incorporation of a latent variable within the GAN framework to better capture the distribution of plausible solutions.
* **Clarify MAP Advantage:** Strengthen the justification for choosing MAP inference over other approaches. Specifically, explain why the proposed method overcomes limitations of existing GAN-based SR techniques.
* **Expand Discussion of Related Work:** Provide a more comprehensive overview of recent advances in SR, particularly GAN-based methods.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How do you explain the lack of quantitative comparison to established SOTA SR methods on standard benchmarks? Are there specific computational limitations preventing this?
2.  Can you elaborate on the extent to which existing GAN-based SR methods already implicitly enforce consistency between LR and HR images? How does the affine projection provide a distinct advantage?
3.  Why was the exploration of a GAN latent vector not considered in this work? Do you foresee any challenges in integrating such a variable into the proposed framework?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with the affine projection, particularly for larger image sizes?
5.  The paper mentions the potential limitations of MAP inference regarding representation. How do you believe the specific design of your framework mitigates these issues in the context of image SR?
