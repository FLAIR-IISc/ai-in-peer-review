## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors introduce a CNN architecture with an affine projection to ensure consistency between low- and high-resolution images, and then explore three methods – GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood trained prior – to solve the resulting optimisation problem. The core idea of directly estimating the MAP estimate using a neural network is compelling and addresses the limitations of traditional empirical risk minimisation approaches that often lead to blurry results.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper in its current form, but with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the proposed approach is interesting and theoretically sound, the experimental results presented do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods, particularly the GAN-based approach which is highlighted as performing best. The improvements observed in the 2D Swiss-roll example are clear, but the gains on real image datasets (CelebA, ImageNet) appear marginal and are not consistently supported by quantitative metrics like PSNR and SSIM.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical grounding and the clear explanation of the connection between the proposed affine projection and MAP inference. The derivation of the loss functions and the justification for using GANs are well-presented. However, the experimental section feels somewhat incomplete. The ablation studies comparing AffGAN to SoftGAN are limited, and the reasons for the observed high-frequency noise in the GAN results are not thoroughly investigated. The discussion of the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods is brief, and their poor performance is not adequately addressed. The connection to variational inference, while interesting, is presented as an afterthought and lacks detailed exploration.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following: 1) Conduct more extensive experiments with a wider range of state-of-the-art SR methods for comparison. 2) Provide a more in-depth analysis of the GAN results, including a visual comparison of the generated textures and a discussion of the observed artifacts. 3) Investigate the reasons for the poor performance of the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods and explore potential improvements. 4) Expand on the connection to variational inference and discuss its implications for future research. 5) Clarify the practical benefits of the affine projection – does it consistently lead to faster training or improved performance, and under what conditions?

Finally, could the authors elaborate on the choice of the Gaussian kernel for the affine projection? Would other kernel choices impact the performance of the model? Also, what is the computational overhead introduced by the affine projection layer, and is it significant in practice?
