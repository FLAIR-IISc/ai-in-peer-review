Summary and contributions
The paper proposes amortised MAP inference for single-image super-resolution (SR) and introduces an architecture and learning framework to approximate MAP solutions without requiring paired LR–HR data. The key ideas are:
- Architectural projection to the affine subspace of valid HR reconstructions: a fixed projection layer g(x) = (I − A+ A) fθ(x) + A+ x ensures exact data consistency Afθ(x) = x for a known downsampling operator A (implemented as strided convolution) with pseudo-inverse A+ (implemented as deconvolution and estimated numerically).
- Reformulation of amortised MAP as minimising H[qθ, pY], the cross-entropy between the model’s output distribution qθ and the HR data distribution pY, turning SR training into a generative modelling problem.
- Three training strategies to approximate the objective: (i) AffGAN, a GAN whose “generator” is the affine-projected SR network driven by LR samples; (ii) denoiser-guided training (AffDG) using a DAE to estimate ∇y log pY; (iii) a density-model-guided baseline (AffLL) using a differentiable PixelCNN/MCGSM prior. A stabilisation technique, instance noise, is proposed to regularise GAN training by convolving both pY and qθ with noise.
- Theoretical analysis: AffGAN minimises KL[qθ‖pY] (derivation provided) and a stochastic variant performs amortised variational inference (Appendix F).
- Empirical evidence: (a) a 2D Swiss-roll toy problem where AffGAN and AffDG approach MAP in terms of cross-entropy, (b) proof-of-concept on CelebA showing affine projection does not hurt and can help MSE training, (c) qualitative 4× SR on grass textures, faces (CelebA), and ImageNet, where AffGAN yields sharper, more plausible images than MSE/MAE.

Decision: Weak accept
Primary reasons: (1) The affine projection layer and the amortised-MAP reformulation provide a principled, novel perspective on SR with a clear advantage of guaranteed data consistency; (2) the theoretical grounding of GAN-based SR and the instance-noise stabilisation are insightful and broadly relevant; (3) empirical results, while not exhaustive, demonstrate perceptual benefits over MSE and support the theory on a controlled toy task.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the mismatch between pixel-wise losses and perceptual plausibility in SR, arguing for MAP solutions consistent with the image prior. This is well motivated and positioned relative to MSE/MAE SR and perceptual/GAN-based SR (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016).
- Novelty and placement: Exact data consistency via an affine projection layer is a clean architectural contribution that contrasts with prior soft LR-consistency penalties. The reduction of amortised MAP to matching qθ to pY connects SR to generative modelling without paired data. Instance noise offers a principled stabilisation distinct from one-sided label smoothing (Salimans et al., 2016) and is related to later ideas on regularising discriminator smoothness. The connection to amortised VI is conceptually interesting.
- Technical correctness: The constraint handling via projection is sound given known A; the derivations linking the GAN objective to KL[qθ‖pY] and to amortised VI in the stochastic case are reasonable. The numerical procedure to approximate A+ is described and validated via near-zero projection errors. The denoiser gradient link to ∇ log p is standard and correctly used.
- Empirical rigor: The Swiss-roll experiment convincingly verifies cross-entropy improvements relative to MAP. The MSE proof-of-concept shows the projection does not degrade and may accelerate training. On real images, AffGAN improves perceptual quality qualitatively and enforces Af(x)=x exactly. However, evaluation lacks standard SR benchmarks, perceptual user studies, and comparisons to strong baselines beyond MSE/MAE or soft-GAN, and the alternative amortised methods (AffDG, AffLL) underperform in high dimension.

Suggestions for improvement (to help strengthen the work; not all essential)
Essential:
- Benchmarking: Include standard SR datasets (e.g., Set5/Set14/BSD100/Urban100) with both distortion-oriented (PSNR/SSIM) and perception-oriented metrics available at the time (e.g., NIQE/BRISQUE) and qualitative side-by-side comparisons with SRResNet/SRGAN, VDSR, and other contemporaneous methods. A small-scale human study (MOS or pairwise preference) would substantially support the perceptual claims.
- Robustness to model mismatch: Evaluate sensitivity when the true degradation operator differs from A (e.g., bicubic vs Gaussian kernel, mild blur or noise). Report how strict projection behaves under mismatch, and whether a relaxed/learned A or hybrid soft constraint improves robustness.

Important but optional:
- Instance noise: Provide ablations on noise level and annealing schedule, compare against label smoothing and gradient penalty variants, and quantify training stability (discriminator loss trajectories, FID-like proxy if feasible).
- Data pairing claim: Demonstrate training without paired LR–HR by sampling LR and HR from different sources or by using unpaired datasets; clarify any alignment assumptions needed.
- Affine projection details: Release the code or provide more implementation specifics for A+ estimation (kernel sizes, convergence criteria), and analyse sensitivity to the kernel support of A+.
- AffDG/AffLL: Add diagnostics (e.g., gradient field visualisations, likelihood calibration) to explain why these underperform in high dimensions, and whether multiscale or noise-level curricula can narrow the gap.
- Artifacts: Characterise the high-frequency noise seen with AffGAN (spectral analysis), and explore simple priors (e.g., TV or discriminator feature regularisation) to mitigate it while preserving sharpness.

Questions for clarification
- Training without pairs: Were LR inputs generated exclusively by applying A to HR training images, or were HR and LR drawn independently during training? If the latter, how was domain mismatch handled?
- Degradation operator: How is A chosen for real data, and how sensitive are results to kernel misspecification? Can A (or A+) be learned jointly from data while retaining the projection guarantee?
- Instance noise: What precise annealing schedule and target discriminator loss were used? Was the discriminator trained “to convergence” per iteration, and how many D steps per G step were taken in practice?
- Diversity vs MAP: Since AffGAN minimises KL[qθ‖pY], it encourages higher-entropy qθ than strict MAP. How sensitive are reconstructions to LR input perturbations, and is there evidence of multimodal outputs for the same x?
- Computational cost: What is the runtime and memory overhead of the affine projection compared to standard architectures, and does projection accelerate convergence consistently?

Overall, the paper offers a principled, well-motivated framework with a clear architectural idea and useful theoretical insights. Strengthening empirical validation and robustness analysis would make the contribution significantly more compelling.
