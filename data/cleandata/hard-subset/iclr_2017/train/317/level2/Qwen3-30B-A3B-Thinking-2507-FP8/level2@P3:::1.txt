

This paper proposes amortized MAP inference for image super-resolution (SR), addressing the limitation of current methods that use MSE loss and produce blurry, over-smoothed outputs. The authors introduce a novel neural network architecture with an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs. They then formulate MAP inference as minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior, proposing three methods to solve this optimization problem: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoiser guidance), and AffLL (using density models). The paper provides theoretical grounding for their approach, demonstrates its effectiveness on both toy and real image datasets, and establishes a connection to variational inference.

The paper should be accepted. The key reasons are its strong theoretical motivation for MAP inference in SR and clear experimental evidence that the proposed methods produce perceptually superior results compared to existing approaches.

The theoretical contribution is significant. The paper correctly identifies that MSE-based SR produces outputs at the mean of the posterior distribution, which results in blurry reconstructions, while MAP inference targets the mode of the posterior, which should appear more plausible. The affine projection layer is a clever technical solution to the consistency problem in SR, and the derivation showing that MAP inference reduces to minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the image prior is well-justified. The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is a valuable theoretical contribution that broadens the paper's impact.

The experimental results provide strong support for the claims. The 2D toy experiment (Section 5.1) clearly demonstrates that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the desired cross-entropy objective while MSE and MAE models produce outputs in low-probability regions. The experiments on real images (Sections 5.3-5.5) show that AffGAN produces sharper, more plausible reconstructions than MSE-based methods, with quantitative metrics supporting this perceptual improvement. The ablation study in Section 5.2 confirms that the affine projection layer does not hurt performance and may even improve training speed and final quality.

To further improve the paper, the authors should provide more detailed analysis of computational costs for the proposed methods compared to existing approaches. While the paper mentions that AffGAN outperforms other methods, it would be helpful to include training time comparisons to contextualize the performance gains. The discussion of why AffDG and AffLL underperform compared to AffGAN (Section 5.3) could be strengthened with more specific analysis of the failure modes of these methods.

The paper could also benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art SR methods beyond those mentioned in the related work. While the paper references Ledig et al. (2016) as using GANs for SR, it would be valuable to directly compare AffGAN with more recent approaches that use perceptual losses or other techniques for improved quality.

The authors should consider expanding the discussion of the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6. While the paper correctly notes that MAP estimates can be representation-dependent, it would strengthen the argument to provide specific examples of cases where MAP inference might fail compared to other approaches.

The instance noise technique proposed in Section 3.2.1 is a promising contribution to GAN training stability, but the theoretical justification could be strengthened. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of how instance noise affects the convergence properties of AffGAN compared to other stabilization techniques.

The paper successfully addresses a significant limitation in image super-resolution by proposing a theoretically grounded approach that produces perceptually superior results. The combination of theoretical insights, novel architectural contributions, and strong experimental validation makes this work an important contribution to the field.
