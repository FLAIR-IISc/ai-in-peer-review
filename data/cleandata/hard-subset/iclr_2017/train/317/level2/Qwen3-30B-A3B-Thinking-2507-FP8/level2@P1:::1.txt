

# Review

## Summary
The paper introduces a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors address a fundamental limitation of current SR methods that use empirical risk minimization with MSE loss, which produces blurry, over-smoothed outputs. They propose a new neural network architecture that projects the output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between low-resolution input and high-resolution output. This architectural innovation allows the MAP inference problem to be reformulated as minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The authors then present three methods to solve this optimization problem: Affine Projected GAN (AffGAN), denoiser-guided SR (AffDG), and density-guided SR (AffLL). The paper demonstrates through both synthetic and real-world experiments that AffGAN produces visually superior results compared to MSE-based methods and other approaches, with a theoretical connection to variational inference.

## Decision
Accept

## Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a significant contribution to the image super-resolution field by addressing a fundamental limitation of current methods. The motivation for MAP inference is well-justified through a clear theoretical analysis of the limitations of MSE loss, illustrated with a compelling toy example. The authors' architectural innovation (affine projection layer) is elegant and well-explained, providing a principled way to handle the constraint that high-resolution outputs must be consistent with low-resolution inputs.

The experimental results strongly support the authors' claims, particularly for the AffGAN method. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides clear evidence that AffGAN and AffDG successfully minimize the MAP objective, while MSE and MAE methods produce outputs in low-probability regions. The real image experiments (grass textures, CelebA faces, ImageNet) demonstrate that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results compared to MSE-based methods and other approaches. The quantitative results (PSNR/SSIM) align with the qualitative observations, showing that while MSE methods achieve better numerical scores, they produce blurry outputs that are less perceptually appealing.

The theoretical connection between AffGAN and variational inference (shown in Appendix F) is a particularly strong contribution that elevates the paper beyond just a practical SR method. This connection provides a new perspective on GANs and their relationship to probabilistic modeling.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art SR methods, particularly those that use perceptual loss functions (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016, which is cited but not thoroughly compared). The authors should clarify how their method relates to and differs from these approaches.

2. The paper mentions that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results, but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why this occurred. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of these approaches would be valuable for future work.

3. The instance noise technique is an interesting contribution to GAN training stability, but the paper could better explain why this approach is preferable to other stabilization techniques like label smoothing.

4. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is strong, but the connection to the practical implementation of the affine projection layer could be more explicit. A simple example showing how the projection layer is implemented in a typical CNN architecture would help readers understand the practicality of this approach.

## Questions for Authors
1. In the ImageNet experiments, the AffGAN model sometimes produces plausible but incorrect reconstructions (e.g., snake to water). How does the authors' approach handle cases where the MAP estimate is visually plausible but factually incorrect? Is there a way to incorporate additional constraints to prevent such errors?

2. The paper mentions that the density-guided approach (AffLL) achieved a log-likelihood of -4.10 bits per dimension, which is comparable to previous work. How does this density model's quality compare to the density models used in other recent SR work, and why did the AffLL approach still fail to produce high-quality SR results?

3. In Section 5.6, the authors discuss limitations of MAP inference. How do the authors plan to address the issue that MAP estimates may be highly atypical compared to typical samples from the posterior distribution, especially as the dimensionality increases?
