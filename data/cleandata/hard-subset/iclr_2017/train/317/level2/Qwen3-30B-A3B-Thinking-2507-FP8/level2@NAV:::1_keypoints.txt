- Proposes shifting image super-resolution from MSE-based empirical risk minimization to amortized MAP inference to avoid blurry averaged outputs.  
- Argues MAP (mode-seeking) yields more plausible high-resolution images compared to MSE (mean-seeking).  
- Introduces a novel neural architecture with an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs.  
- Reduces the MAP inference problem to minimizing cross-entropy between distributions.  
- Proposes three methods built on this idea: AffGAN (affine-projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser-guided SR), and AffLL (density-guided SR).  
- Provides theoretical analysis and experiments showing AffGAN achieves superior perceptual quality over MSE-based and other baselines.  
- Empirical validation includes a 2D toy problem and real-world natural image datasets.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance, citing a significant and theoretically sound contribution.  
- Key strengths: clear motivation for MAP, elegant affine-projection architecture, and thorough empirical validation.  
- AffGAN demonstrates the practical advantage of mode-seeking over mean-seeking in perceptual quality.  
- Suggests clarifying computational efficiency relative to direct MAP estimation, which is expensive for high-dimensional images.  
- Recommends moving the intriguing connection to variational inference (currently in Appendix F) into the main text with more detail.  
- Notes GAN outputs exhibit high-frequency noise and asks for discussion of its practical impact and mitigation strategies.  
- Requests clarification of practical advantages beyond perceptual quality, especially computational efficiency and scalability to higher resolutions.
