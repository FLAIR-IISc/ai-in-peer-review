

# Review

## Summary
The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, addressing the limitation of current MSE-based methods that produce blurry outputs. The authors introduce an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between low-resolution (LR) input and high-resolution (HR) output, reducing the problem to minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. They present three methods to solve this: AffGAN (GAN-based), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with a clear explanation of the affine subspace structure of SR problems. The paper also introduces "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training and establishes a connection between AffGAN and variational inference.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to SR, but the empirical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the comparison to state-of-the-art methods is inadequate.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is theoretically sound and well-motivated. The key insight about the affine subspace structure of SR is correct and important. The 2D toy problem in Section 5.1 convincingly demonstrates that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the cross-entropy as intended, unlike MSE and MAE methods. The introduction of "instance noise" is a valuable technical contribution that addresses a known GAN training instability issue.

However, the paper fails to adequately demonstrate the practical advantage of their approach over existing state-of-the-art SR methods. While they compare to MSE-based methods, they do not properly compare to Ledig et al. (2016), which used GANs for SR and is cited in the paper. The quantitative results in Tables 1-2 show that AffGAN achieves lower PSNR than MSE-based methods, which contradicts the common understanding that GAN-based SR methods improve PSNR (though often at the cost of perceptual quality). The paper claims AffGAN produces "more plausible" images, but provides no human evaluation or metrics that correlate with perceptual quality (beyond qualitative examples). The authors acknowledge that PSNR/SSIM don't correlate well with human perception but don't provide alternative metrics.

A more serious issue is the poor performance of AffDG and AffLL methods, which the authors dismiss without thorough explanation. As shown in Figure 7 and Appendix E, these methods produce blurry results that don't improve with training, yet they are presented as equally valid approaches. The paper doesn't explain why these methods fail while AffGAN succeeds, which undermines the theoretical framework.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods, particularly Ledig et al. (2016), which is cited but not properly contrasted. The authors should clarify how their AffGAN differs from this prior work.

2. The paper claims to produce "more plausible" images but provides no human evaluation or perceptual metrics. The authors should include human evaluations or metrics like LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity) to support their perceptual quality claims.

3. The AffDG and AffLL methods are presented as equally valid approaches but perform poorly. The authors should either provide a thorough explanation for their failure or remove them from the main comparison.

4. The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is intriguing but not integrated into the main narrative. This could be a major contribution if properly developed.

5. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of MAP inference, particularly the criticism in Section 5.6 about coordinate transformation dependence. The authors should clarify why MAP inference in RGB space is preferable to other representations.

## Clarification Questions
1. The paper states that AffGAN produces "sharper images" but has lower PSNR than MSE methods. How do the authors reconcile this apparent contradiction? Is the lower PSNR due to the different loss functions, or is there another explanation?

2. The AffDG method uses denoising to estimate gradients of the log-prior. Why does this approach fail to produce good results while the GAN-based method succeeds? Is it due to the approximation error in the denoising step, or are there other factors?

3. The paper claims to have a connection between AffGAN and variational inference, but this is only in an appendix. Why isn't this connection emphasized in the main text, and how does it affect the interpretation of the method?
