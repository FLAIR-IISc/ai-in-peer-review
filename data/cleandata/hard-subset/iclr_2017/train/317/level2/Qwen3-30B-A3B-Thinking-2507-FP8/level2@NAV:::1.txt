

This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution by shifting from empirical risk minimization (using MSE loss) to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution, while MAP inference finds the mode, resulting in more plausible high-resolution images. They introduce a new neural network architecture with an affine projection layer ensuring consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs. Using this architecture, they reduce the MAP inference problem to minimizing cross-entropy between distributions, proposing three methods: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser-guided SR), and AffLL (density guided SR). The authors demonstrate through theoretical analysis and experiments that AffGAN produces superior perceptual quality compared to MSE-based methods and other baselines on real-world image datasets.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretically sound approach to address a well-known limitation of current super-resolution methods. The key strength lies in the clear motivation for MAP inference over empirical risk minimization, the elegant affine projection architecture that enforces solution consistency, and the thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets (including a 2D toy problem and real-world natural images). The AffGAN method achieves the best perceptual quality, with results that clearly demonstrate the advantage of mode-seeking over mean-seeking approaches.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should more clearly articulate the computational efficiency of their approach compared to direct MAP estimation (which would be prohibitively expensive for high-dimensional images). The connection between AffGAN and variational inference (Appendix F) is intriguing but requires more detailed explanation in the main text. Additionally, while the paper acknowledges the limitation of high-frequency noise in GAN outputs, it would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of how this noise impacts practical applications and potential mitigation strategies.

I recommend the authors clarify the practical advantages of their method beyond perceptual quality, particularly regarding computational efficiency and scalability to higher resolution images. The theoretical grounding for using GANs in image SR is compelling, and the empirical results provide strong support for the claims.
