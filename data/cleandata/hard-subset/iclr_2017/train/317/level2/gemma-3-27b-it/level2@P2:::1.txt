This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is to design a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture that projects the output to the affine subspace of valid SR solutions, ensuring consistency between low- and high-resolution images. The authors then explore three methods to train this network: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood-trained image prior. The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, addressing a significant issue in SR – the blurry results often produced by methods minimizing pixel-wise losses.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and demonstrates promising results, particularly with the GAN-based approach, the empirical evaluation lacks sufficient depth to convincingly support the claimed benefits. Specifically, the comparisons are often against relatively basic baselines, and the ablation studies are limited. The gains over these baselines, while visible, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at a competitive conference like ICLR. Furthermore, the connection to amortized variational inference, while intriguing, feels tacked on at the end and is not fully explored.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical grounding and the novelty of the affine projection constraint. The argument for MAP inference in SR is well-presented, and the idea of enforcing consistency between LR and HR images through the network architecture is clever. The analysis of the 2D Swiss-roll example effectively illustrates the limitations of MSE loss. The connection between the proposed approach and GANs is also valuable, providing a theoretical justification for using GANs in SR.

However, several weaknesses hinder the paper’s overall impact. First, the experimental evaluation primarily focuses on relatively simple datasets (grass textures) and doesn’t adequately demonstrate generalization to more complex, real-world scenarios. The ImageNet results are shown but are not thoroughly analyzed. Second, the comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods is limited. The authors mention Ledig et al. (2016) but don’t provide a direct comparison. A more thorough benchmark against recent SR techniques is crucial to establish the contribution of this work. Third, the ablation studies are insufficient. For instance, the impact of the affine projection layer is evaluated, but a more comprehensive ablation, including variations in network architecture and hyperparameters, would be beneficial. The denoiser and density guided methods are quickly dismissed due to poor results, but a deeper investigation into why these methods failed would add value. Finally, the discussion of the connection to variational inference feels underdeveloped and lacks a clear explanation of its practical implications.

**Additional Feedback/Suggestions:**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include comparisons against more recent and powerful SR methods, such as EDSR, RCAN, or RDN.
*   **More Comprehensive Ablation Studies:** Conduct a thorough ablation study to evaluate the individual contributions of the affine projection, different loss functions, and network architecture choices.
*   **Detailed ImageNet Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the results on the ImageNet dataset, including qualitative examples and quantitative metrics. Consider reporting results on different levels of downsampling.
*   **Expand on Variational Inference:** Elaborate on the connection to variational inference, providing a more detailed explanation of how the stochastic extension of AffGAN relates to VAEs. Can this connection be leveraged for improved performance or regularization?
*   **Investigate Failure Cases:** Analyze the cases where the proposed method fails, identifying potential limitations and areas for improvement.
*   **Qualitative Comparisons:** Provide more visually compelling comparisons, perhaps using zoomed-in regions of the reconstructed images to highlight the differences in texture and detail.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Why was the denoiser-guided method and the density-guided method abandoned quickly? Were specific hyperparameter searches performed to optimize their performance?
*   What is the computational cost associated with the affine projection layer compared to a standard CNN architecture?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the proposed method to the choice of the downsampling kernel A?
*   Can the affine projection layer be easily incorporated into other SR architectures, such as those based on residual learning?
*   Could you provide more insight into the limitations of the MAP inference approach as discussed in Section 5.6? What are the potential drawbacks of focusing solely on the mode of the posterior distribution?
