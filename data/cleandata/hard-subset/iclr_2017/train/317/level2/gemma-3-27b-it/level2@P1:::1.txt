This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is to leverage convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to directly estimate the MAP estimate, ensuring consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and high-resolution (HR) outputs via an affine projection. The authors explore three methods for solving the resulting optimization problem: GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood trained image prior. The paper is well-written and the motivation for exploring MAP inference as an alternative to empirical risk minimization (particularly MSE) is clearly articulated.

**Decision:** Reject. While the ideas presented are interesting and the technical details are generally sound, the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods, especially considering the complexity introduced. The gains observed, particularly with the GAN-based approach, are marginal and not consistently shown across different datasets.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Gains:** The most significant claim – that the proposed methods yield superior perceptual quality – isn’t strongly supported by the provided results. While the 2D Swiss-roll experiment effectively illustrates the theoretical benefits of MAP inference, the results on real images (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) are not drastically better than those achievable with standard SR techniques (even those based on MSE, as shown in the comparative tables). The quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) don’t show a clear advantage for the proposed methods, and the visual comparisons, while showing some improvements in sharpness, also reveal artifacts and inconsistencies.

2. **GAN Instability and Complexity:** The reliance on GANs, despite being the best-performing method, introduces inherent instability and sensitivity to hyperparameters. The authors acknowledge this and introduce “instance noise” as a mitigation strategy, adding further complexity to the training process. The experiments highlight difficulties in stabilizing the GAN training and achieving consistent results.  The benefits gained from the GAN approach don’t appear to justify the added computational cost and tuning requirements.

3. **Lack of Robustness and Generalization:** The denoiser-guided SR and density-guided SR methods are shown to struggle with convergence, limiting their practical applicability. The authors attribute this to challenges in accurately modeling the data distribution, but this suggests a fundamental limitation of these approaches. The performance on different datasets is not thoroughly evaluated, making it difficult to assess the generalizability of the proposed methods.



**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies would be beneficial.  Specifically, it would be helpful to isolate the impact of the affine projection layer. What performance is achieved *without* the affine projection but *with* the same loss function?  This would help determine whether the projection itself contributes significantly to the results.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** A comparison against more recent and established SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) would provide a more meaningful benchmark. It's unclear how the proposed methods compare to the current state-of-the-art in terms of both quantitative metrics and perceptual quality.
* **Qualitative Evaluation:** A more rigorous perceptual evaluation would strengthen the claims of improved visual quality. Consider conducting user studies to assess subjective preferences for images generated by the proposed methods versus baseline approaches.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper acknowledges potential limitations in Section 5.6, a more thorough discussion of the practical challenges and drawbacks of the proposed methods would be valuable. For example, how does the computational cost scale with image size and resolution?  What are the memory requirements?
* **Clarify the Role of the Affine Projection:** The mathematical formulation in Section 3.1 is sound, but the intuitive explanation of the affine projection could be improved. Providing a clearer explanation of how this projection enforces consistency and why it's beneficial would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and memory requirements of the proposed methods, particularly compared to standard SR techniques?
* How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, especially for the GAN-based approach? What strategies did you employ to optimize these hyperparameters?
*  You mention issues with the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods. Were any other techniques, beyond annealing the noise/likelihood, explored to address these convergence problems?
* Can you elaborate on the connection between the AffGAN model and amortized variational inference, as mentioned in Appendix F? What practical implications does this connection have for the design and training of SR models?
