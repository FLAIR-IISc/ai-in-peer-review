This paper addresses the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as a Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem and proposing methods for amortized MAP inference using convolutional neural networks. The core idea is to enforce consistency between low-resolution (LR) inputs and high-resolution (HR) outputs via an affine projection, reducing the problem to minimizing the cross-entropy between the generated distribution and the true image prior. The paper explores three approaches: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood trained image prior, finding the GAN-based approach to perform best on real image data.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framing of SR as MAP inference and introduces a novel architectural constraint (affine projection), the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate substantial improvements over existing methods. The presented advantages are not always consistent across experiments, and the theoretical connection to variational inference feels somewhat tacked on.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **Limited Empirical Gains:** While the 2D Swiss-roll experiment convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the affine projection in recovering the MAP solution, the gains on more complex image datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) are less pronounced. The paper acknowledges that AffGAN produces “slightly sharper” images than SoftGAN, but the quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM) often favor the baseline MSE model, indicating a trade-off between perceptual quality and traditional metrics. The claim of superior perceptual quality, a primary motivation, isn’t consistently supported by quantitative data, and a proper perceptual study is missing.
2.  **Instability and Tuning:** The discussion in Section 5.6 highlights the challenges of applying the proposed methods (especially AffLL and AffDG) and the need for careful tuning and stabilization techniques (e.g., instance noise). This suggests a fragility in the approach, making it harder to generalize and potentially less practical than more established SR methods. The reliance on specific annealing schedules and the sensitivity to noise levels are not thoroughly investigated.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison is limited, and a more thorough benchmarking against recent state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN, RDN) is necessary. Simply stating that the performance is comparable isn’t sufficient to demonstrate a significant contribution. It's unclear if the affine projection provides a real advantage *beyond* what’s achievable with optimized loss functions and network architectures in existing SR models.

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

*   **Perceptual Evaluation:** Include a thorough perceptual evaluation (e.g., user study) to support the claim of improved perceptual quality. This is critical, given the motivation for MAP inference.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more detailed ablation studies to isolate the impact of the affine projection. How much does it contribute to performance when combined with different loss functions and network architectures?
*   **Detailed Discussion of Failure Cases:** Provide a more detailed analysis of failure cases and the reasons why the proposed methods sometimes struggle.  The observation about AffGAN generating “water” instead of a “snake” is interesting but requires deeper investigation.
*   **Clarify the Connection to Variational Inference:** The connection to variational inference established in Appendix F is intriguing but feels underdeveloped.  Elaborate on the implications of this connection and potential avenues for further exploration. How can the stochastic AffGAN be practically implemented as a variational autoencoder?
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost associated with the affine projection and the proposed training procedures. Is there a significant overhead compared to standard SR methods?
* **Quantitative Justification:** Provide a more rigorous quantitative justification of the claimed advantages, possibly by performing statistical tests on the performance differences between different models.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What are the limitations of the affine projection? Are there cases where it might hinder performance?
*   How does the choice of the deconvolution kernel (A+) impact the performance of the affine projected models?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameters of the instance noise (e.g., annealing schedule)?
*   How does the performance of AffGAN compare to state-of-the-art SR methods on standard benchmarks?
*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of the connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference? What are the potential benefits of leveraging this connection?
