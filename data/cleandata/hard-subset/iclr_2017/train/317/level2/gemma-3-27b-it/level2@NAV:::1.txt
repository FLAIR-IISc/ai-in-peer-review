## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that current empirical risk minimization methods, particularly those using MSE loss, lead to blurry results and propose a framework leveraging neural networks to directly estimate the MAP solution. A key contribution is a CNN architecture enforcing consistency between LR and HR images via an affine projection, simplifying the amortised MAP problem to cross-entropy minimization. They explore three methods to solve this – GANs, denoiser-guided SR, and a baseline using a maximum-likelihood trained image prior, finding the GAN-based approach to be most effective.

I **reject** this paper. While the motivation for moving beyond MSE is sound, and the theoretical framing of MAP inference is well-presented, the experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate a significant advancement over existing GAN-based SR methods. Specifically, the performance gains over standard GAN approaches (SoftGAN) are marginal, and the qualitative results presented aren’t convincingly superior, especially considering the additional complexity of the proposed affine projection.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical justification for using GANs within a MAP inference framework. The explanation of the affine projection and its connection to enforcing consistency between LR and HR images is clear and insightful. However, the experiments feel somewhat limited. The comparison to Ledig et al. (2016) is indirect, and a more thorough evaluation on standard SR benchmarks (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100) with established metrics (PSNR, SSIM, and potentially perceptual metrics) is crucial. The ablation studies regarding the affine projection are helpful, but the benefits observed seem modest. The results using the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods were not competitive, and the reasons for this were not fully explored.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Perform a rigorous quantitative and qualitative evaluation on standard SR datasets and compare against state-of-the-art SR methods, including those beyond GANs.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Further investigate the impact of the affine projection. Does it consistently improve performance across different network architectures and datasets? What is the computational cost?
*   **Clarify the advantage:** More clearly articulate the specific benefits of the proposed AffGAN approach over existing GAN-based SR methods. Is the improved stability claimed in Section 3.2.1 consistently observed in practice?
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper briefly mentions limitations in Section 5.6, but a more thorough discussion of the potential drawbacks of MAP inference in this context is warranted.
* **Code availability:** Providing code would allow for independent verification of the results and facilitate further research.

I would encourage the authors to address these points and resubmit. The core idea of formulating SR as amortised MAP inference is promising, but more robust validation is needed to establish its practical benefits.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   What is the computational overhead introduced by the affine projection layer?
*   Why were the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods not competitive, and what steps were taken to optimize their performance?
*   Can you provide more examples showcasing the perceptual improvements offered by AffGAN compared to other methods?
