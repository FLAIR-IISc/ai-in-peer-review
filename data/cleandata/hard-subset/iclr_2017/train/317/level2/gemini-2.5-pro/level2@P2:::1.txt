### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR), aiming to produce perceptually plausible high-resolution (HR) images rather than the blurry results typical of methods trained with Mean Squared Error (MSE). The authors make a compelling case for reformulating the SR problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is that the network should learn a mapping from a low-resolution (LR) input to the most probable HR image, i.e., the mode of the posterior distribution.

The paper's main contributions are twofold. First, it introduces a novel neural network architecture featuring an affine projection layer. This layer elegantly enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled output must be identical to the LR input, ensuring consistency by projecting any intermediate HR estimate onto the affine subspace of valid solutions. Second, with this architecture in place, the paper shows that amortised MAP inference reduces to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of true HR images. To solve this, the authors propose and evaluate three distinct approaches:
1.  **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network that minimizes the KL-divergence between the two distributions.
2.  **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided method that uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data.
3.  **AffLL:** A baseline that uses a pre-trained, explicit generative model (a PixelCNN variant) to provide a likelihood signal.

Experiments show that the GAN-based approach produces the most visually appealing and sharp results on natural images. The paper also provides valuable theoretical insights, including a novel technique called "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training and a connection between a stochastic version of their model and amortised variational inference.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions on both a conceptual and a technical level. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides a novel and well-motivated framework for perceptual super-resolution based on amortised MAP inference. This moves the field beyond heuristic perceptual losses and provides a solid theoretical justification for using generative models like GANs for this inverse problem.
2.  **Novel and Effective Architecture:** The proposed affine projection layer is an elegant and powerful architectural contribution. It replaces the common practice of using soft penalties with a hard, explicit constraint that guarantees data consistency, which the authors show is beneficial even for standard MSE training.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous. The motivation for moving beyond MSE is clearly articulated with an excellent toy example (the Swiss-roll) that intuitively demonstrates why MSE leads to implausible outputs and why MAP inference is a desirable alternative.

The derivation that connects amortised MAP inference to minimizing the cross-entropy `H[q_theta, p_Y]` is clean and provides the theoretical backbone for the rest of the paper. This framing allows the problem to be treated as a form of generative modeling, where paired data is no longer strictly necessary.

The affine projection layer is a standout contribution. Formulating the projection as `(I - A+A)f(x) + A+x` is clever and directly implementable with standard deep learning components (convolution and deconvolution). The experiments in Section 5.2 convincingly show that this layer is not just theoretically sound but also practically beneficial, leading to faster training and better results even on a standard MSE task.

While only one of the three proposed methods (AffGAN) was empirically successful on complex image datasets, the comparative study is itself valuable. The success of AffGAN demonstrates that the overall framework is viable and produces state-of-the-art perceptual results that are qualitatively much sharper than MSE-based methods. The discussion of "instance noise" is a useful practical contribution for the broader GAN community. Finally, the theoretical connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference, presented in the appendix, is a deep and valuable insight that links two major families of generative models.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Analysis of Failed Methods:** The paper is transparent about the poor performance of the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and likelihood-guided (AffLL) methods on image textures, which is commendable. However, the discussion of *why* they failed is brief. A more in-depth analysis would strengthen the paper. For instance, were the gradients from the denoiser too noisy or biased? Did the generator get stuck in a poor local minimum of the learned log-likelihood surface? A deeper investigation into these failure modes could be very instructive for future work in this area.
*   **Quantitative Perceptual Evaluation:** The evaluation of the final SR results is purely qualitative. While the images are compelling, the claims of perceptual superiority would be significantly bolstered by a user study asking participants to rate the realism or quality of images generated by different methods (e.g., AffGAN vs. SoftGAN vs. MSE).
*   **AffGAN vs. SoftGAN:** The qualitative comparison between AffGAN and SoftGAN in Figure 4 is not entirely conclusive. The paper states AffGAN is "slightly sharper" but also contains "more high frequency noise". It would be helpful to expand on this trade-off and more clearly articulate the advantages of the hard affine projection over a soft penalty in the adversarial setting.
*   **Limitations of MAP:** The discussion in Section 5.6 on the potential pitfalls of MAP inference (e.g., representation dependence, the "atypical mode" problem) is excellent and shows great scientific maturity. It might be worth briefly connecting this to the success of the AffGAN model. Does the KL-divergence objective, which implicitly encourages entropy in the generator's distribution, offer a partial remedy to the atypical mode problem compared to a method that *only* seeks the mode?

### **Questions for Authors**

*   Could you provide more intuition on the failure of the AffDG model on textures? Was performance sensitive to the noise annealing schedule for the denoising autoencoder? Does the method work better for simpler, less multi-modal data distributions?
*   How critical was "instance noise" for successfully training the AffGAN models on ImageNet and CelebA? Did you observe evidence of mode collapse without it, and how sensitive was the final image quality to the noise annealing schedule?
*   The computation of the pseudo-inverse kernel `A+` is an interesting practical detail. How computationally intensive is this optimization, and does its quality depend heavily on the properties of the downsampling kernel `A` (e.g., its size or shape)?
