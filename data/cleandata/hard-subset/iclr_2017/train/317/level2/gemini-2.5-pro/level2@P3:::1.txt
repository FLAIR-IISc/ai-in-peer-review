**Summary**

This paper recasts the problem of single image super-resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The motivation is to overcome the characteristic blurriness of models trained with pixel-wise Mean Squared Error (MSE), which tend to output the conditional mean of plausible solutions. By seeking the mode of the posterior distribution (the MAP estimate), the aim is to generate outputs that are more plausible under the prior of natural images, and thus have higher perceptual quality.

The primary contribution is a principled framework for this task. First, a novel neural network architecture is introduced featuring an "affine projection" layer. This layer analytically enforces the constraint that the high-resolution output must downsample back to the low-resolution input, elegantly handling the likelihood term of the posterior. With this constraint satisfied by construction, the MAP objective simplifies to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the network's outputs and the distribution of true high-resolution images.

To solve this challenging unsupervised learning problem, the paper proposes and evaluates three distinct methods:
1.  **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) where the SR network acts as the generator.
2.  **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided approach that uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-prior, which is then backpropagated to train the SR network.
3.  **AffLL:** A density-guided approach that uses a pre-trained explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant) to provide a training signal.

Experiments on a toy dataset and natural images show that the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) is most successful, producing sharp and perceptually convincing results, while the other two methods yield blurry outputs. The work also contributes a practical technique called "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training and provides a theoretical connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically-grounded framework for super-resolution. The two key reasons for this recommendation are:

1.  The formulation of SR as amortised MAP inference, combined with the elegant affine projection layer to enforce data consistency, provides a principled and insightful alternative to standard empirical risk minimization. This framing moves the problem into the domain of generative modeling in a rigorous way.
2.  The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that the proposed GAN-based model (AffGAN) produces results with significantly higher perceptual quality than MSE-based methods, successfully demonstrating the practical value of the proposed framework.

**Supporting Arguments**

The work's main strength lies in its clear and coherent conceptual development. It correctly identifies a key limitation of MSE-based SR—its tendency to average plausible solutions—and proposes MAP inference as a well-justified remedy. The derivation in Section 3, which shows how an architectural constraint (the affine projection) transforms a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained cross-entropy minimization, is particularly insightful. This provides strong theoretical justification for using generative modeling techniques like GANs for SR, moving beyond purely empirical motivation.

The empirical validation is thorough and effective. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a clear and intuitive demonstration of the core idea, showing that AffGAN and AffDG correctly identify the posterior mode where MSE fails. The experiments on natural images (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) convincingly show the qualitative superiority of the AffGAN approach, which produces sharp details and realistic textures that are absent in the MSE results. The inclusion of an experiment (Section 5.2) showing that the affine projection layer can even improve performance for MSE-trained models is a nice touch, validating the architectural contribution independently of the MAP framework.

The paper is also notable for its thoughtful discussion of limitations. The authors are candid about the empirical failure of the denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches, and the discussion in Section 5.6 regarding the potential pitfalls of MAP inference (e.g., representation dependence, atypicality of the mode) demonstrates a mature understanding of the problem space. The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is a valuable theoretical extension that points toward promising future work.

While the paper is strong, the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods constitutes a weakness. The explanation provided in Appendix E—that the density/score estimates are not accurate enough—is plausible but leaves the reader wondering if these interesting avenues are truly dead ends or if they could be made to work with different optimization strategies or more powerful auxiliary models. A more detailed analysis of this negative result would have further strengthened the paper.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further improving the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** It would be beneficial to expand on the reasons for the blurriness of the AffDG and AffLL results. For instance, analyzing the variance of the gradients provided by the denoiser could offer insight into optimization difficulties. Is it possible that the score/density estimates are accurate for in-distribution samples but provide misleading gradients for the out-of-distribution samples produced early in the SR network's training?
*   **Instance Noise Ablation:** The "instance noise" technique is presented as an important component for stabilizing training, particularly on ImageNet. Its contribution would be clearer with a direct comparison, perhaps showing training curves or qualitative results for AffGAN with and without instance noise on one of the datasets.
*   **Comparison with Perceptual Losses:** The work positions itself as an alternative to using hand-crafted or learned perceptual loss functions. A direct qualitative and quantitative comparison against a leading perceptual loss method (e.g., using a VGG-based loss as in Johnson et al., 2016 or Ledig et al., 2016) would help to contextualize the performance of the AffGAN model more precisely within the landscape of perceptually-driven SR methods.

**Questions for Clarification**

*   The pseudo-inverse `A+` is learned numerically. How sensitive are the final results to the precision of this learned pseudo-inverse? Does a minor error in `A+` lead to a significant degradation in performance or visual quality?
*   Regarding the AffDG model, the training process involves annealing the noise level of the denoiser. Could the blurry results be related to the difficulty of this annealing schedule or the transition between different denoiser checkpoints? Was a curriculum-like strategy explored where the SR model is first trained with gradients from a high-noise denoiser before moving to finer-grained gradients?
*   The connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is very interesting. Has the work explored empirically training the stochastic generator `f_theta(x, z)`? This could potentially allow for sampling diverse, plausible reconstructions for a single input `x`, which would directly address the "atypicality" concern raised against MAP inference.
