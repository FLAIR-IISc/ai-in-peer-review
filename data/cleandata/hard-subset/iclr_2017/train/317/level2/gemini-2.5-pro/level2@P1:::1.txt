### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The primary motivation is to address the common issue of blurry and perceptually unconvincing results produced by standard SR methods trained with pixel-wise Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss. The authors argue that by seeking a MAP estimate, the model is encouraged to produce high-resolution (HR) outputs that are plausible under the prior distribution of natural images.

The core technical contribution is a novel neural network architecture that incorporates an "affine projection" layer. This layer is designed to analytically enforce the constraint that the downsampled version of the HR output must be identical to the low-resolution (LR) input. By satisfying this consistency constraint by construction, the MAP inference problem is elegantly transformed into an unconstrained optimization problem: minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the network's outputs and the true data distribution of HR images. This reframing effectively turns SR into a generative modeling task, where paired LR-HR data is no longer strictly necessary for the main objective.

The paper explores three distinct methods for solving this cross-entropy minimization problem:
1.  **AffGAN:** A method based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), where a discriminator is trained to distinguish between generated HR images and real ones.
2.  **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided approach that uses the gradients from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to approximate the gradient of the log-prior of natural images.
3.  **AffLL:** A baseline method guided by the log-likelihood of a pre-trained explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant).

Through experiments on a toy dataset, grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet), the authors demonstrate that the AffGAN approach produces significantly sharper and more perceptually convincing results than the other two proposed methods and traditional MSE-based models. Additionally, the paper introduces a useful technique called "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training and provides a compelling theoretical connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces a principled and novel approach to super-resolution by framing it as an amortised MAP inference problem. The affine projection layer is a simple yet powerful architectural contribution that elegantly enforces the data consistency constraint, providing a clear advantage over methods that use soft penalties. This reframing of a conditional image-to-image problem into an unconditional generative modeling task is a significant conceptual contribution.
2.  The work is well-supported by strong empirical evidence. The qualitative results clearly show that the AffGAN model produces images with superior perceptual quality compared to baselines. The paper also makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between their GAN framework and amortised variational inference, which helps to unify these two important families of generative models.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous. The motivation for moving beyond MSE loss to MAP inference is clearly articulated and well-illustrated with the 2D Swiss-roll example, which effectively demonstrates how MSE-based solutions can lie in low-probability regions of the data manifold.

The central idea of the affine projection layer is technically sound and elegant. It provides a hard constraint that is often desired in inverse problems but is typically handled with less principled soft penalty terms. The authors show in Section 5.2 that this layer not only enforces the constraint but can also lead to faster training and better final performance even when training with a standard MSE loss, suggesting it acts as a very effective architectural prior.

The exploration of three different methods (GAN, denoiser-guided, density-guided) to optimize the same underlying cross-entropy objective is thorough. While only the GAN-based approach was successful on complex image data, the systematic investigation and the honest reporting of the limitations of the other methods are commendable. The visual results, particularly on the challenging grass texture and CelebA datasets, are compelling and convincingly demonstrate the ability of the AffGAN method to generate sharp details and realistic textures that are absent in the MSE-trained counterparts.

Finally, the paper includes several valuable additional contributions. The "instance noise" technique for stabilizing GAN training is well-motivated and potentially of broad interest to the community. The discussion in Section 5.6 on the limitations of MAP inference is insightful and demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the problem space. The derivation in Appendix F, which connects a stochastic version of AffGAN to amortised variational inference, is a significant theoretical insight that opens up exciting avenues for future research.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my assessment.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The discussion regarding the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods on image data is insightful but relegated to Appendix E. It would be beneficial to summarize these findings in the main paper. Is the primary issue the difficulty of accurately estimating the score function or the density in high-dimensional spaces, or is there a more fundamental optimization challenge? A brief discussion in Section 5.3 would strengthen the narrative.
*   The "instance noise" technique is presented as a key factor for stabilizing training on ImageNet. Given its potential utility for the broader GAN community, an ablation study demonstrating its effect on training stability or final image quality would be a very strong addition to the paper.
*   The paper provides a thoughtful critique of MAP inference in Section 5.6. It might be worth briefly mentioning these limitations earlier in the introduction to better frame the choice of MAP as a pragmatic step towards perceptual quality, while acknowledging that it is not a perfect solution.

**Questions:**
1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the affine projection layer? Specifically, what is the overhead of pre-computing the pseudo-inverse `A+`, and does using the projection (`I - A+A`) add significant time to the forward and backward passes during training compared to a standard architecture?
2.  The result in Section 5.2, where the affine projected network trained with MSE outperforms a standard CNN, is quite interesting. Do you have further intuition as to why this hard constraint is so beneficial in an MSE setting? Does it act as a form of regularization that prevents the network from finding "shortcut" solutions that are good in terms of MSE but violate the consistency constraint?
3.  The theoretical link between the stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference is very exciting. Have you performed any preliminary experiments with this stochastic model? One might expect it to produce more diverse outputs and perhaps mitigate some of the typical GAN artifacts, moving from finding a single MAP mode to sampling from the posterior. Any initial findings or thoughts on this would be very interesting to hear.
