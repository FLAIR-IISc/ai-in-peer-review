This paper presents a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The goal is to generate perceptually plausible high-resolution images, avoiding the blurriness common in methods trained with pixel-wise MSE loss. The authors make several key contributions. First, they introduce a novel CNN architecture featuring an "affine projection" layer, which elegantly enforces the constraint that the downsampled output must match the low-resolution input. This transforms the MAP objective into an unconstrained cross-entropy minimisation problem. Second, they propose and compare three methods to solve this: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoiser-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided baseline (AffLL). Experiments demonstrate that the AffGAN model produces the sharpest and most visually appealing results.

I recommend that this paper be **Accepted**. The work is built on a strong theoretical foundation and addresses a significant problem in the field. The two key reasons for my decision are:
1.  The proposed affine projection layer is a principled and novel architectural contribution that directly models the physical constraint of the SR problem. This is a clear improvement over previous works that use soft penalties.
2.  The claims are convincingly supported by a well-designed set of experiments, including a clear toy-problem demonstration that validates the core MAP inference objective, and strong qualitative results on real-world images that show a marked perceptual improvement over baseline methods.

The paper’s core strength lies in its elegant mathematical formulation. By strictly enforcing data consistency via the projection layer, the problem simplifies to matching the distribution of generated images to the distribution of real images, providing a solid theoretical justification for using GANs in this context. The Swiss-roll experiment provides clear, quantitative evidence that the proposed methods successfully optimize the intended objective, which is a crucial validation step. Furthermore, the qualitative results on grass textures and faces effectively highlight the ability of AffGAN to generate plausible high-frequency details where traditional methods fail.

For improvement, I would suggest the authors consider the following points:
*   The discussion on why the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods failed to produce sharp results is brief and primarily in the appendix. A more detailed analysis of their limitations (e.g., optimization difficulties, inaccurate gradient estimates) in the main paper would be valuable.
*   The paper notes that AffGAN can introduce high-frequency artifacts. A brief discussion on potential techniques to mitigate this common GAN-related issue would strengthen the practical applicability of the method.

Finally, I have a few questions for the authors:
*   The theoretical connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference in Appendix F is fascinating. Were there any preliminary experiments or specific challenges that led to leaving an empirical study of this connection for future work?
*   How sensitive is the performance of AffGAN to the choice of the downsampling kernel A? Would the method be equally effective with a more standard bicubic downsampling kernel?
