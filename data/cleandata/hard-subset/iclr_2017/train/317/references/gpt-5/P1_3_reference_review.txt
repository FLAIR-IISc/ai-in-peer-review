Summary and contributions
The paper proposes amortised MAP inference for single-image super-resolution (SR). The key idea is to enforce the exact data-consistency constraint ŷ such that Aŷ = x by introducing an affine projection layer using the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse A+. This turns the MAP objective into maximising the prior log-probability of the output subject to the affine constraint and, in expectation over x, into minimising the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the distribution of model outputs and the true HR image distribution. Three training routes are proposed to approximate this objective:
- AffGAN: a GAN variant that, via a modified generator loss and under an optimal discriminator, minimises KL[qθ‖pY]. The paper also motivates and introduces “instance noise” (adding noise to both real and generated samples) to stabilise training and ensure support overlap.
- AffDG: a denoiser-guided approach that estimates score ∇y log pY(y) via a DAE and backpropagates through the affine projection to update the SR network.
- AffLL: training against a tractable density model (PixelCNN-like with a continuous MCGSM likelihood) to minimise cross-entropy directly.

Additional contributions include: (i) practical construction of A+ for strided convolutions; (ii) a proof-of-concept that affine projection can improve or match MSE-trained CNNs while guaranteeing exact LR consistency; and (iii) an appendix showing a connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical validation is limited and does not sufficiently support the central claims beyond proof-of-concept; (2) clarity and presentation issues (equation correctness, missing figure references, incomplete citations) hinder reproducibility and confidence.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The amortised MAP formulation and the use of an affine projection layer to guarantee exact data consistency are well motivated. The paper clearly explains why MSE-trained SR tends to be blurry and why MAP subject to a natural image prior is appealing. The relation to GANs, denoising, and density models is appropriate and the “instance noise” stabilisation is timely and insightful.
- Theory: The reduction of the amortised MAP objective to H[qθ, pY] with an affine projection is elegant. The AffGAN derivation (minimising KL[q‖p] with a logit-based generator loss) is plausible but the main text/appendix contain typographical issues that make the exact update rule ambiguous. The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is interesting, albeit high-level.
- Empirical evidence: 
  - On a 2D Swiss roll, AffGAN/AffDG approximate MAP and achieve low cross-entropy relative to MSE/MAE, which is a good sanity check.
  - The affine projection helps MSE-trained CNNs train faster and ensures exact LR consistency.
  - On real images (CelebA, ImageNet), AffGAN produces sharper, perceptually plausible outputs compared to MSE, but exhibits high-frequency noise and occasional semantic “hallucinations.”
  - However, the evaluation lacks strong baselines (e.g., SRGAN, VDSR/EDSR or perceptual losses), perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS), or human studies. The denoiser- and density-guided methods largely fail (blurry, instability), and are dropped early, weakening the claim that all three routes are practical.
  - The setting assumes a known downsampling operator and noiseless observations; robustness to mismatch or noise is not explored. This is a critical limitation for real-world SR.
- Novelty: The affine projection for exact consistency in SR networks is a neat architectural contribution compared to soft constraints; “instance noise” is a relevant stabilisation trick (overlapping with concurrent work). The amortised MAP perspective is a useful reframing. Nonetheless, the lack of comparative experiments against contemporaneous GAN-based SR (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016) diminishes the empirical novelty.

Constructive feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify and correct equations: the generator loss for AffGAN, the combined objective in Eqn. (17), and typos in the text/figures/references. Some figure placeholders and reference formatting are broken.
- Expand empirical evaluation:
  - Compare against SRGAN and strong supervised baselines; include perceptual metrics (LPIPS, FID/KID) and a small-scale user study.
  - Provide ablations for instance noise (noise level, annealing), and for A+ (fixed vs. trainable vs. incorrect A).
  - Report robustness to mismatch between assumed A and the true LR formation, and to additive noise.
  - Evaluate on standard SR benchmarks (Set5/14, Urban100, DIV2K) to contextualise results.
- DAE/density routes: either improve stability (e.g., modern score-based models, better likelihoods, multiscale training) or reposition them more explicitly as exploratory. Current negative results undermine the tri-partite claim.
- Clarify the “no paired data” claim: in experiments, pX is obtained by downsampling HR images; discuss training with genuinely unpaired HR and LR datasets.
- Discuss the implications of amortised optimisation: the objective encourages high-prior, feasible outputs on average, but does not guarantee per-x posterior modes; consider stochastic generators to capture multimodality and report diversity.
- Release code and A+/A construction details to improve reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide the exact generator objective you use for AffGAN and a clean derivation showing it minimises KL[qθ‖pY] under an optimal D. The current equations appear inconsistent.
- How sensitive is performance to mismatch between the assumed A (kernel/stride) and the true LR formation process? Can the method handle observation noise or unknown blur?
- How is A+ parameterised (kernel size/support), and how close is the projection to exact pseudoinverse in practice? Any ringing/aliasing artifacts?
- Can you quantify the impact of instance noise (with/without, different schedules) on stability and final quality?
- For AffDG/AffLL, can you stabilise training with modern score-based or flow models, or curriculum strategies? What were the main failure modes?
- Have you tried a stochastic generator (AffGAN with z) to approximate posterior sampling rather than point estimates, and if so, how do results differ?
- Beyond visual inspection, can you provide perceptual metrics or small-scale human studies to substantiate the perceptual gains?
