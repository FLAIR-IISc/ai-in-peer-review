Summary and contributions
The paper addresses single-image super-resolution (SR) from an amortized Bayesian perspective: rather than empirical risk minimization with MSE/MAE, the goal is amortized MAP inference, preferring outputs with high posterior under a natural image prior and exact consistency with the LR observation. The key ideas are:
- An affine projection layer that enforces the observation constraint Afθ(x)=x exactly by projecting any network output onto the affine subspace of valid HR solutions using the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse A+. This turns the constrained MAP objective into minimizing the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the distribution of model outputs and the HR data distribution.
- Three training strategies to approximate this objective without paired LR-HR supervision: (1) an affine-projected GAN (AffGAN) with a modified generator update that approximates minimizing KL[qθ||pY] and a stabilization technique called instance noise; (2) a denoiser-guided approach (AffDG) using DAE score estimates; (3) a density-guided approach (AffLL) using a differentiable PixelCNN/MCGSM to provide log-likelihood gradients.
- Empirical results on a toy Swiss-roll problem (where MAP is tractable) and on images (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet). AffGAN produces sharp, plausible results while satisfying the exact consistency constraint; AffDG and AffLL tend to be blurry/unstable. The affine projection can also improve optimization even when training with MSE.

Decision: Reject (promising conceptual contributions but insufficient empirical validation and comparisons)
Key reasons:
- The empirical evidence supporting the main claims is limited and lacks comparisons to strong, contemporary SR baselines (e.g., SRGAN/perceptual-loss methods) and perceptual evaluations.
- Only the GAN-based variant works well in practice; the other two proposed amortized-MAP surrogates underperform, weakening the central claim that amortized MAP (as instantiated here) improves SR broadly.
- The approach assumes a known, noise-free forward operator A and enforces it exactly, which is a strong assumption for practical SR. Robustness to model mismatch or noise is not evaluated.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The critique of MSE/PSNR for SR and the case for high-prior-probability reconstructions are well motivated and connected to prior work. The affine projection is a clean architectural idea that directly enforces consistency (in contrast to soft penalties used in SRGAN-like models), and recasts training as matching the marginal output distribution to the HR prior. The theoretical connections to f-divergences, score-based learning (DAE), and amortized variational inference (Appendix F) are interesting and potentially impactful.
- Correctness of theory: The decomposition to constrained MAP and the projection construction are sound (assuming an accurate A+). The GAN update that targets KL[q||p] follows prior notes (e.g., Huszár) under the ideal discriminator assumption. The DAE gradient connection is standard. The amortized VI view of stochastic AffGAN is elegant, albeit not validated empirically.
- Empirical rigor: 
  - Toy example: Clear evidence that AffGAN/AffDG approximate the posterior mode and minimize directly estimated H[qθ, pY]. Good diagnostic of LR-consistency (exact for affine models).
  - Images: Visuals suggest AffGAN yields sharp details and plausible textures; however, evaluations are qualitative, with PSNR/SSIM favoring MSE (as expected) and no perceptual metrics or user studies. The observation that AffGAN can hallucinate (snake→water) underscores the trade-off between perceptual plausibility and fidelity, but the paper does not quantify this or compare to baselines that balance perceptual and content losses.
  - Missing comparisons: No head-to-head against SRGAN (Ledig et al.) or perceptual-content losses (e.g., VGG feature loss) on the same datasets. The paper argues affine projection is superior to soft penalties but shows limited evidence beyond LR-consistency; comparative perceptual quality is not systematically assessed.
  - AffDG/AffLL: The negative results and training instability are acknowledged and partially analyzed, but the paper leaves these as underperforming without a convincing path forward; yet they constitute two of the three proposed routes to amortized MAP.
- Assumptions and scope: Exact Af=y consistency presumes known A and zero noise, which is often violated in real settings. The method’s performance under kernel/noise mismatch is not studied. While the paper notes that projection reduces hypothesis space, it does not assess whether this harms reconstruction under realistic degradations.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decisive)
- Strengthen empirical validation:
  - Compare AffGAN against SRGAN/perceptual-loss methods and strong MSE baselines (e.g., VDSR-like) on common benchmarks; include perceptual metrics (user study, learned perceptual similarity) and LR-consistency error.
  - Provide ablations: with/without affine projection; with soft LR penalties; effect of instance noise (including annealing schedule); discriminator capacity/updates; and A+ accuracy.
  - Evaluate robustness to noise and kernel mismatch; consider adding a soft consistency term for noisy settings or extending projection to estimated A (blind SR).
- Clarify and tighten theory:
  - Clearly state assumptions required for the KL-minimization view (discriminator optimality, overlapping supports). Discuss how instance noise addresses these and its relation to concurrent work (e.g., Arjovsky & Bottou).
  - For Appendix F, show a small empirical demonstration of the stochastic AffGAN/VI connection (e.g., diversity vs. fidelity).
- Improve clarity and presentation:
  - Fix typos and broken references; streamline notation around qθ, qY|X, and projections.
  - Make the A+ estimation procedure and its accuracy more prominent; quantify projection error vs. kernel size/stride.
- Discuss application trade-offs:
  - When is strict LR-consistency preferable to perceptual fidelity (hallucinations)? Consider downstream use-cases (medical/forensics vs. natural image enhancement).

Questions for the authors
- How does AffGAN compare quantitatively and qualitatively to SRGAN and perceptual-content losses on standard SR datasets? Does affine projection yield better perceptual quality at equal LR-consistency?
- How sensitive is performance to inaccuracies in A and to observation noise? Can your framework accommodate a probabilistic likelihood (soft constraints) without losing the benefits of projection?
- Can you report perceptual metrics (e.g., human MOS) or modern learned perceptual metrics to substantiate the claim of improved perceptual quality?
- What is the effect of instance noise hyperparameters and annealing schedule? Can you provide diagnostics or theory-guided tuning rules?
- For AffDG/AffLL, can techniques from score matching/diffusion models or improved density estimators address the blurriness/instability you observed? Would multi-scale training or curriculum help?
- Does the stochastic AffGAN (Appendix F) produce diverse, plausible samples conditioned on x? How does that compare to deterministic AffGAN in terms of fidelity and perceptual quality?
