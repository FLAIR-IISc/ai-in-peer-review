Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles single-image super-resolution as an underdetermined inverse problem and proposes “amortized MAP inference” driven solely by a natural image prior. The core idea is to enforce the observation constraint Ax = ŷ via an affine projection layer gθ(x) = (I − A+ A) fθ(x) + A+ x, where A is a known downsampling operator and A+ its Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse realized as a deconvolution. This guarantees consistency (A ŷ = x) for any network output.
- Under this constraint, maximizing Ex log pY(gθ(x)) is shown to reduce to minimizing the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY], where qθ is the model’s marginal distribution of reconstructions. This reframes SR as matching the HR distribution while remaining consistent with LR inputs.
- Three optimization routes are proposed: (i) a GAN variant (AffGAN) argued to minimize KL(qθ‖pY) via a modified generator loss and stabilized with “instance noise”; (ii) using a denoising autoencoder to estimate ∂y log pY(y) and backpropagate these gradients through the SR network (AffDG); (iii) training a tractable density model (PixelCNN+MCGSM) and minimizing −log pY(gθ(x)) (AffLL).
- Empirically: on a 2D Swiss roll toy problem, AffGAN and AffDG approximate MAP solutions and achieve lower cross-entropy than MSE/MAE; on grass textures and face/natural images (CelebA/ImageNet), AffGAN yields sharper, more perceptual outputs, while MSE scores best in PSNR/SSIM as expected. The affine projection is shown not to hurt (and sometimes improve) MSE-trained SR, and guarantees zero LR inconsistency.

Decision
Reject. While the affine projection idea and the MAP-to-cross-entropy derivation are appealing, the empirical validation is insufficient and the novelty over existing GAN-based SR is limited. Claims for two of the three proposed training routes (denoiser/density guided) are not supported by compelling results.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The critique of MSE/PSNR and the shift toward prior-driven perceptual SR are well motivated and consistent with recent literature (SRGAN and perceptual losses). The paper adds a clear architectural constraint (affine projection) and a principled derivation from MAP to cross-entropy. This is a meaningful perspective. However, the main practical method (AffGAN) is essentially SRGAN with an exact observation constraint; the denoiser/density routes—arguably the more novel components—do not deliver beyond the toy task.
- Theoretical correctness: The derivation that, under degenerate likelihood and exact projection, amortized MAP reduces to minimizing H[qθ, pY] is sound. The GAN objective variant and the KL(q‖p) connection hinge on idealized assumptions (unique optimal discriminator, overlapping support), which the authors acknowledge and attempt to mitigate via instance noise. The amortized VI link (Appendix F) is interesting but largely formal and not validated empirically. A key caveat remains: minimizing H[qθ, pY] with a deterministic generator corresponds to per-x MAP only in the limit of infinite capacity and perfect optimization; moreover, AffGAN minimizes KL(q‖p), not cross-entropy, introducing an entropy term that can deviate from strict MAP. The paper partially discusses this but still frames the method as “amortized MAP,” which feels overstated.
- Empirical rigor: 
  - Benchmarks and baselines: Experiments omit standard SR benchmarks (Set5/14, BSD100, Urban100, DIV2K) and state-of-the-art perceptual SR methods. A direct comparison to SRGAN/ESRGAN or feature/perceptual losses would be essential to substantiate perceptual gains beyond qualitative figures.
  - Metrics: No perceptual metrics (LPIPS, FID, NIQE) or user studies; reliance on PSNR/SSIM (expected to prefer MSE) and qualitative visuals weakens claims about perceptual superiority. The notable “snake becomes water” example illustrates the risk of hallucination under a strong prior with minimal data fidelity beyond Ax = x; this tradeoff is not systematically studied (e.g., distortion–perception curves).
  - AffDG/AffLL: These methods underperform on real images and are subsequently dropped. There is limited analysis of why (e.g., gradient quality in high dimension, density model mismatch). As a result, two of the three proposed approaches are not convincingly supported.
  - Realism of the observation model: The method assumes known A and zero observation noise. In realistic SR, A is often unknown/mismatched and noise is present. The paper does not evaluate robustness to A mismatch or noise, despite suggesting the projection as a central contribution.
  - Reproducibility: Many architectural/hyperparameter details are deferred; no code release is mentioned; quantitative tables are sparse.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Strengthen empirical validation: include standard SR datasets, compare to SRGAN/ESRGAN and perceptual-loss baselines, report perceptual metrics and possibly a small-scale MOS study. Provide ablations isolating the projection layer and instance noise.
- Study robustness: evaluate performance with mismatched/unknown A and with observation noise; consider a learned A (or blind SR) variant. Report the effect of mis-specified A+.
- Clarify the MAP claim: explicitly distinguish optimizing H[qθ, pY] (deterministic per-x solutions tending to MAP) from minimizing KL with GAN (adds H[qθ]); discuss when these coincide and when they do not. Consider a stochastic generator and demonstrate posterior sampling vs MAP tradeoffs empirically.
- Analyze AffDG/AffLL: provide deeper diagnostics (gradient field visualization, noise-annealing schedules, density calibration), and consider stronger density models or score-based estimators. If these routes remain weak, reposition them as exploratory.
- Evaluate hallucination/fidelity trade-offs: report LR consistency errors (which you do) and also semantic consistency w.r.t. ground truth; perhaps introduce a controlled knob trading off prior vs fidelity (e.g., slight relaxation of the constraint or noise modeling).

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to misspecification of A and to observation noise? Can the affine projection be extended to noisy or blind SR (e.g., via probabilistic constraints)?
2. In what sense does minimizing H[qθ, pY] with a deterministic mapping guarantee per-x MAP? Can you provide conditions or a proposition clarifying when amortized minimization recovers MAP for each x?
3. For AffGAN, how critical is instance noise vs other stabilization (e.g., spectral norm, gradient penalties, WGAN variants)? Any quantitative stability metrics?
4. Can you provide quantitative perceptual metrics (LPIPS/FID) and comparisons to SRGAN/perceptual-loss methods on standard datasets?
5. What failure modes did you observe (e.g., “plausible but wrong” hallucinations)? Can you characterize their frequency and dependence on A or content?
6. For AffDG, how does the choice of noise schedule affect gradient quality? Did you try modern score-matching/denoising diffusion estimators for ∇y log pY(y)?
7. For AffLL, how close is the learned density to pY (e.g., held-out log-likelihood, bits/dim) on your datasets, and how does density calibration impact SR training?
