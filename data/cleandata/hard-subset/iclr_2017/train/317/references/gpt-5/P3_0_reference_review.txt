Summary
The paper proposes amortised MAP inference for single-image super-resolution (SR) under a known, noiseless degradation x = Ay. The key idea is an affine projection layer that guarantees consistency Afθ(x) = x by projecting any network output onto the affine subspace consistent with the LR input using the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse A+. This turns learning into maximising Ex log pY(ΠAx fθ(x)), which is equivalent to minimising the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the marginal distribution of reconstructions and the HR prior. The authors instantiate this with three training strategies: (i) a GAN variant (AffGAN) with a generator update that targets KL[q||p] and an “instance noise” trick to stabilise training; (ii) denoiser-guided learning (AffDG) using a DAE’s score estimate to backpropagate log pY gradients; (iii) density-guided learning (AffLL) using a differentiable PixelCNN-like MCGSM prior. They present a theoretical connection between stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference. Empirically, they show the projection achieves exact LR consistency and yields speed benefits; on a toy Swiss-roll problem AffGAN/AffDG achieve cross-entropy near MAP; on textures/faces/ImageNet, AffGAN produces sharper, perceptually plausible results than MSE/MAE and their soft-constrained counterparts.

Decision: Reject
Main reasons: (1) The empirical validation is limited and does not convincingly support the central amortised-MAP claim beyond the toy task; only the GAN variant works well in practice, while the denoiser- and density-guided methods underperform. (2) The setting assumes a known, noiseless A and uses synthetic LR generated by that A; claims about unpaired training and practical utility are not demonstrated under realistic degradations or noise, nor compared against strong SR baselines using perceptual losses.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Architectural contribution: The affine projection layer is a clean, principled way to hard-enforce downsampling consistency, with a sound linear-algebraic derivation. The observation that A+ is not simply the transpose is important, and the numerical procedure to approximate A+ is practical.
- Clear derivation: The reduction of amortised MAP (with delta likelihood) to minimising H[qθ, pY] is well explained and motivates unpaired learning using HR-only priors.
- GAN analysis: The generator update targeting KL[q||p] and the instance noise idea have reasonable theoretical motivation; the discussion of support mismatch and discriminator instability is insightful.
- Interesting theoretical note: The link between stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference is novel and conceptually valuable.

Weaknesses
- Unrealistic observation model: All derivations and experiments hinge on p(x|y) = δ(x − Ay). Real SR often has unknown blur, sensor nonidealities, and noise. The hard projection may degrade performance under misspecified A or noise. No robustness experiments are provided (e.g., training with A but testing with slightly different A, or additive noise).
- Unpaired claim not substantiated: While the objective theoretically does not require paired data, the experiments generate LR from HR using the same A; there is no demonstration with independently collected LR and HR sets (domain gap), which is essential for the claimed benefit.
- Limited empirical rigor: Beyond the 2D toy, there is no quantitative evaluation of the core objective (H[q,p]) on images. Perceptual quality is judged mostly visually; PSNR/SSIM are reported but known to be misaligned with perceptual goals. There is no user study, no modern perceptual metrics (e.g., though LPIPS is later, no NR-IQA like NIQE/BRISQUE), and no comparison to strong concurrent baselines (e.g., Ledig et al.’s SRGAN beyond a “SoftGAN”, or later ESRGAN). The observation of high-frequency noise and semantic hallucinations (snake→water) raises concerns about faithfulness despite LR consistency.
- Two out of three methods underperform: AffDG and AffLL produce blurry outputs and face training instabilities. The paper attributes this to score/likelihood concentration but does not provide ablations that demonstrate they can be made competitive. This weakens the central “amortised MAP via three approaches” message.
- Instance noise novelty and evaluation: Adding input noise to smooth distributions has been contemporaneously advocated (Arjovsky & Bottou). The paper does not provide a thorough ablation of noise schedules, discriminator updates per generator step, or comparisons to alternative stabilisation methods.
- Clarity and presentation: Some references are incomplete or duplicated; several formatting glitches exist. Important experimental details (datasets for grass textures, exact A kernel, kernel sizes for A+) are scattered in the appendix; core comparisons are thin in the main text.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Robustness to model mismatch: Evaluate with additive noise in x and with degradations that differ from the training A (e.g., bicubic vs Gaussian, kernel width shift). Show the effect of hard projection vs soft penalties in these cases.
- Unpaired training: Train using HR images from one domain and LR images from another source (e.g., device LR photos), still using a known A, to demonstrate the claimed independence from paired supervision.
- Perceptual evaluation: Include NR-IQA metrics and, ideally, a small-scale user study. Calibrate the perception–distortion tradeoff by sweeping a tunable parameter (e.g., adding a soft MSE term) and plotting PSNR vs perceptual score.
- Comparisons: Provide direct comparisons to SRGAN-like models without projection and to classical back-projection methods. Include an ablation of A+ initialisation (correct vs random vs learned), fixed vs learnable A+, and with/without projection.
- AffDG/AffLL improvements: For AffDG, explore modern score-matching/denoising approaches (annealed noise schedules with variance-preserving scaling, multi-noise DAEs) and stabilise gradients. For AffLL, test stronger continuous-density priors and temperature scaling to mitigate overconcentration.
- Instance noise ablation: Report sensitivity to noise variance and annealing; compare to label smoothing, gradient penalty, spectral norm, or WGAN-GP.
- Faithfulness beyond LR consistency: While Afθ(x)=x guarantees low-frequency agreement, assess semantic drift at higher frequencies. Consider local cycle-consistency checks or patch-wise constraints.

Questions for the authors
1. How does the affine projection behave under noise or misspecified A at test time? Can you report quantitative and qualitative results when x = Ay + ε or when the blur kernel differs by, say, 10–20% in width?
2. Can you demonstrate truly unpaired training (disjoint HR and LR datasets) and report performance relative to paired training?
3. What is the perceptual quality according to NR-IQA metrics and/or a user study? How often do semantic hallucinations occur while still satisfying Afθ(x)=x?
4. For AffDG, what is the effect of the noise annealing schedule and the DAE architecture on stability and sharpness? Can you relate your method to modern score-based models and show improved results?
5. How sensitive is AffGAN to the instance noise schedule? Does training remain stable without noise if using other stabilisation tricks (e.g., spectral norm, gradient penalty)?
6. Could A+ be learned end-to-end with a regulariser enforcing AA+≈I on LR space, and does that help when A is only approximately known?
7. How large is the receptive field required for a good approximation to A+? Do truncation effects introduce artifacts?

Overall, the paper presents a neat architectural idea and an appealing amortised-MAP perspective, but the current empirical support and scope are insufficient for acceptance.
