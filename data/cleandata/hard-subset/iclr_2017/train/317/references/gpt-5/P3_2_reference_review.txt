Summary and contributions
- The paper reframes single-image super-resolution (SR) as amortised MAP inference with an explicit observation model x=Ay, replacing empirical risk minimization with an average log-posterior objective. Under a linear downsampling operator A, the likelihood is degenerate, so the MAP objective reduces to maximising a prior term under the hard constraint Afθ(x)=x.
- The key architectural idea is an affine projection layer ŷ=ΠAx fθ(x)=(I−A+A)fθ(x)+A+x that guarantees consistency with the LR input by projecting to the affine subspace {y:Ay=x}. This can be dropped into standard CNNs and, empirically, does not hurt MSE/SSIM, and even improves convergence and LR-HR consistency.
- The training objective is shown to be equivalent to minimising H[qθ,pY], the cross-entropy between the marginal distribution of model outputs and the HR data distribution. Three practical approximations are proposed: (1) AffGAN, a GAN variant with a generator loss designed to minimise KL[qθ||pY], plus “instance noise” for stability; (2) AffDG, training by backpropagating score estimates from a denoising autoencoder; (3) AffLL, training against a tractable continuous PixelCNN-like density model.
- On a 2D toy problem, AffGAN and AffDG approach the MAP solution as measured by estimated cross-entropy. On images (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet), AffGAN produces sharper, perceptually more plausible results than MSE or soft-constraint baselines; AffDG/LL underperform (blurry). The affine projection enforces exact LR-HR consistency and trains faster.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
- Reason 1: Despite interesting theory and a useful architectural constraint, the empirical evidence is limited and does not convincingly support the central claim that amortised MAP (via H[qθ,pY]) improves perceptual SR at scale. Key baselines and perceptual metrics are missing, and the best-performing method (AffGAN) shows noticeable artifacts and requires tricks whose novelty is limited.
- Reason 2: Several claims blur conceptual distinctions (MAP vs distribution matching; “no paired data”) and hinge on strong assumptions (known A, accurate priors) without sufficient empirical validation. The denoiser- and density-guided variants, which would better instantiate the MAP view, fail to deliver in practice.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning
  - The affine projection layer is a clean, practical contribution for enforcing exact observation consistency in SR. While similar “soft” constraints have appeared, the hard projection and the demonstration that it can speed/regularize training is valuable.
  - The amortised MAP objective Ex log pY(ΠAx fθ(x)) recasts SR training as matching qθ to pY. This is conceptually elegant, but not entirely new: related ideas exist in plug-and-play priors, deep generative priors, and conditional generative modeling. The paper would benefit from a tighter positioning relative to SRGAN (Ledig et al.), plug-and-play/RED/score-based priors, and modern conditional likelihood approaches.
  - The “instance noise” stabilization and the KL-targeted generator loss are incremental (cf. f-GAN, early GAN regularization, and later work like WGAN-GP); the paper acknowledges parallel developments but does not compare empirically.
  - The variational inference connection (Appendix F) is interesting, but essentially restates that minimising KL[qθ||pY] with a stochastic generator corresponds to amortised VI with an exclusive KL; conditional VAEs already formalize amortised inference for SR.

- Correctness and clarity
  - The derivation that minimising Ex log pY(ΠAx fθ(x)) is equivalent to minimising H[qθ,pY] is correct and provides motivation for unpaired training given known A and access to pY and pX.
  - The generator loss targeting KL[q||p] via a log-odds objective follows from Bayes-optimal D; however, this relies on strong assumptions (unique optimal D, sufficient capacity/optimization), which are not met in practice. The paper introduces instance noise to mitigate this gap but does not provide ablations vs. alternative stabilizers.
  - The claim that MAP is preferable because MSE averages across posterior modes is fair, but minimising H[q,p] does not guarantee per-x MAP—rather, it encourages outputs to lie in high-density regions of pY subject to Afθ(x)=x. For ambiguous x, this can bias toward globally common textures regardless of the true posterior mode. The “snake→water” example illustrates plausible but wrong hallucinations, echoing this concern.
  - The statement that “we don’t need matched HR/LR pairs” is somewhat overstated. Training still requires (i) knowing A and (ii) sampling x from pX consistent with A (often by applying A to HR data); truly unpaired regimes with unknown degradations are not addressed.

- Empirical evaluation
  - The 2D toy experiment is useful. On images, evidence is mostly qualitative; no human studies or modern perceptual metrics (LPIPS, FID/KID, DISTS) are reported, and comparisons to strong baselines (SRGAN variants, ESRGAN, RCAN+perceptual loss) under standard protocols (e.g., bicubic ×4 on Set5/Urban100/Div2K) are missing. The downsampling uses a Gaussian A, making it hard to compare to established literature.
  - The reported GAN artifacts (high-frequency noise, color drift) and the failure of AffDG/LL suggest that the “prior-driven” routes remain fragile. The ablations on the affine projection with MSE are positive and convincing.
  - The claim that AffGAN achieves lower cross-entropy is only demonstrated in 2D; in high-D, no quantitative proxy is provided.

Additional feedback and questions for the authors
- Please provide stronger empirical validation:
  - Compare against SRGAN/ESRGAN-like methods, both visually and using perceptual metrics (LPIPS, MOS) on standard datasets with bicubic degradation. Include user studies if possible.
  - Report the impact of the affine projection on GAN performance via ablations: with/without projection under identical training, and soft vs hard constraints under matched budgets.
  - Add ablations for instance noise vs. one-sided label smoothing, spectral norm, gradient penalty, and WGAN-GP. Plot discriminator losses, gradient norms, mode coverage, and final quality.
  - Evaluate robustness to misspecified A (blur kernel mismatch, unknown downsampling) and to real LR inputs. How sensitive is performance to errors in A+?
- Clarify theory and scope:
  - Make explicit the conditions under which minimising H[qθ,pY] recovers per-x MAP rather than global high-density textures. Can one derive conditions when the objective selects the true posterior mode?
  - The coordinate-transformation argument (Sec. 5.6) and the claim that AffGAN is more resilient due to the entropy term need formal support or empirical evidence.
  - For AffDG, quantify score estimation accuracy vs. σ; consider modern score models (NCSN/score matching) or diffusion-based priors, which might yield better gradients than DAEs.
  - For AffLL, provide density model calibration diagnostics and discuss whether the continuous PixelCNN+MCGSM is expressive enough; consider modern flow or diffusion models.
- Practicalities:
  - Provide compute/training costs and inference speeds vs. standard SR methods. Does affine projection add overhead at test time?
  - Release code/models for reproducibility.

Overall, the paper offers a compelling perspective and a useful architectural tool, but the empirical support for the key claims—especially beyond GANs—falls short for acceptance at this time. Strengthened experiments, clearer positioning, and tighter ablations could make this a strong contribution.
