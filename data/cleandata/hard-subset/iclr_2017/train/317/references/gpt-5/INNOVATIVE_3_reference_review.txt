Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes amortised MAP inference for single-image super-resolution. The key idea is to enforce exact data-consistency via an affine projection layer that projects any network output onto the affine subspace {y: Ay = x}, turning the amortised MAP objective into minimising the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the model’s marginal output distribution and the HR data distribution.
- This yields an unsupervised training setup (no paired LR-HR needed) and reframes SR as matching natural image statistics while guaranteeing LR consistency.
- Three training strategies are developed: (1) AffGAN, a GAN variant with a modified generator loss that approximately minimises KL[qθ‖pY]; (2) AffDG, using denoisers to provide score estimates ∇y log pY and backpropagating them through the projection; (3) AffLL, a likelihood-guided approach using a differentiable PixelCNN/MCGSM prior.
- The affine projection layer is implemented using the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse A+, shown to be practically realisable as a learned deconvolution and to preserve or improve MSE/SSIM even when training with MSE.
- The paper also introduces instance noise for GAN training, with a clear motivation (to ensure support overlap), an annealing schedule, and a divergence interpretation KL[pσ ∗ qθ‖pσ ∗ pY]. Appendix F connects a stochastic AffGAN to amortised variational inference.
- Empirically: on a 2D Swiss roll, AffGAN/AffDG approach the MAP cross-entropy; on textures, faces (CelebA), and ImageNet, AffGAN produces sharper, perceptually more plausible outputs than MSE, with zero LR inconsistency due to the projection.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper introduces a novel and coherent direction—amortised MAP inference for SR via an affine data-consistency projection—and ties SR training to generative modelling without paired data. The projection layer, the KL-minimising GAN objective, instance noise, and the VI connection make a compelling, forward-looking package despite some incomplete empirical validation.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and potential impact: Recasting SR as cross-entropy minimisation under an image prior with exact data consistency is elegant and influential. The affine projection “data-consistency layer” is a simple, general architectural idea applicable beyond SR. Training without paired data is attractive for many applications.
- Theoretical grounding: The decomposition of the amortised MAP objective, equivalence to H[qθ, pY], and the use of A+ to make constraints tractable are sound. The modified GAN loss and the instance noise analysis are well-motivated; the amortised VI view of stochastic AffGAN is a valuable conceptual bridge.
- Empirical evidence: While not exhaustive, the 2D toy shows the intended behaviour against MAP, and the image experiments demonstrate the qualitative wins (sharpness, plausibility) that the paper is optimising for. The ablation showing affine projection does not harm MSE training (and can help) strengthens the architectural claim.
- Relevance to community: The work provides principled justification for GAN-based SR while correcting a common weakness (lack of consistency), and suggests a path to posterior sampling variants.

Caveats (not blocking for acceptance)
- The AffDG and AffLL approaches underperform visually; the paper reports instability and blur but stops short of making them competitive. This somewhat weakens the “three approaches” story, though the negative results are informative.
- Evaluation is largely qualitative for natural images; no human studies or perceptual metrics are provided, and comparisons to contemporary SRGAN-style baselines are limited.
- Failure modes are apparent (e.g., plausible but semantically wrong reconstructions); controlling semantic drift is not addressed empirically.

Feedback and suggestions (to improve the paper; not all are part of the acceptance decision)
- Add perceptual evaluations: user studies or modern perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID/KID, NIQE/PI) alongside PSNR/SSIM to quantify the claimed improvements in perceptual quality.
- Stronger baselines: include SRGAN/ESRGAN-like baselines trained with and without the affine projection; show measurable benefits of exact data consistency. Also compare to recent data-consistency layers in other inverse problems to contextualise novelty.
- Analyze instance noise: provide sensitivity to noise schedule, show discriminator loss trajectories, and ablate “train D to convergence” vs partial updates.
- Unpaired training: demonstrate the no-pair requirement explicitly (e.g., use HRs from a different but related domain) to validate the claim.
- Address semantic drift: consider stochastic AffGAN with multiple samples per LR input, or add feature-level constraints to preserve content. Report diversity vs fidelity trade-offs.
- Robustness to A mismatch: real degradations are often unknown; study performance under kernel/scale mismatch and discuss learning/estimating A jointly while preserving (approximate) projection.
- Score-based priors: given the denoiser connection, consider modern score/diffusion models to provide high-quality ∇y log pY, which may improve AffDG substantially.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the exact A and A+? Can the method handle unknown or varying degradations, and can A (or A+) be learned while maintaining exact or near-exact consistency?
- Can you quantify the impact of instance noise (ablation of σ schedule, final σ, number of D steps) on stability and final quality?
- Do you have evidence that training truly does not require paired LR-HR? For example, training with HRs from a related domain and LRs from the target—how does performance degrade?
- Can you provide multiple stochastic reconstructions for the same LR input (stochastic AffGAN) to assess posterior multimodality and mitigate mode-seeking behaviour of reverse KL?
- How does the affine projection interact with clipping/sigmoid at the output—does it ever induce minor consistency errors due to quantisation or activation bounds?
- For AffDG/AffLL, could you share more diagnostics (gradient norms, step sizes, likelihood curvature) to explain the observed blur and to guide future improvements?

Overall, this is a bold, well-motivated direction with clear conceptual advances and promising qualitative results. I recommend acceptance.
