1. Summary of contributions (positive and generous)
The paper tackles single-image super-resolution (SR) by advocating amortised MAP inference instead of empirical risk minimization with convex losses (e.g., MSE/MAE). The authors make three main contributions:
- Architectural: They introduce an affine projection layer that enforces exact data-consistency Afθ(x)=x by projecting to the affine subspace defined by the observed LR image x via ŷ = (I−A+A)fθ(x)+A+x. This turns SR with a deterministic observation model into an unconstrained optimisation and guarantees consistency for any generator fθ.
- Objective: They show that amortised MAP for SR reduces to minimising the cross-entropy H[qθ,pY], where qθ is the marginal distribution of the projected outputs over LR inputs, thus enabling training from unpaired HR/LR data and framing SR as matching the HR marginal.
- Algorithms: They propose three approaches to approximate this objective:
  • AffGAN: a GAN variant with a generator given by the affine-projected SR network, and a modified generator update that minimises KL[qθ‖pY]; they also propose “instance noise” to stabilise GAN training.
  • AffDG: denoiser-guided training using a DAE to estimate ∇y log pY(y), backpropagated through the affine-projected SR network.
  • AffLL: likelihood-guided training using a differentiable density model (PixelCNN with a continuous MCGSM likelihood).
They present theory connecting AffGAN to KL minimisation and, in an appendix, relate a stochastic variant of AffGAN to amortised variational inference. Empirically, they demonstrate on a toy Swiss-roll problem that AffGAN and AffDG approximate MAP and achieve low cross-entropy; they show that affine projection does not harm (and can improve) training under MSE; and they provide qualitative SR results on grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet), with AffGAN producing perceptually sharper outputs.

2. Decision
Reject. Key reasons: (i) empirical support is limited for the central claims on real images (qualitative comparisons dominate; strong SR baselines are missing; two of the three proposed approaches underperform), and (ii) the novelty over concurrent GAN-based SR is modest, with the main practical gains driven by AffGAN plus a hard consistency layer and instance noise.

3. Supporting arguments
- Clarity: The core derivations (affine projection, conversion to cross-entropy, GAN/KL update, DAE gradient estimator) are clearly presented and reference established results. Implementation details for A+ estimation and training procedures are provided. Some formatting/typo issues in the draft do not impede understanding.
- Technical soundness:
  • The projection-based handling of a degenerate likelihood is sound; using A+ to define ΠAx ensures exact consistency, and the Monte Carlo objective for approximating A+’s pseudoinverse is reasonable.
  • The reduction of amortised MAP to minimising H[qθ,pY] over the generator’s marginal is correct and conceptually useful.
  • The AffGAN generator update aligning with KL[q‖p] follows Huszár (2016); the instance noise rationale matches known issues with support mismatch and is coherent.
  • The DAE gradient estimator is a standard, valid approximation of ∇ log pY.
  • The variational inference interpretation in the appendix is plausible for the stochastic extension.
- Novelty: The combination of (i) an exact hard projection layer for SR, (ii) an explicit amortised-MAP viewpoint (cross-entropy over marginals), and (iii) applying DAE/density gradients to train SR is interesting. However, adversarial training for SR with global perceptual objectives is already known (e.g., SRGAN), and instance noise was concurrently proposed. The practical contribution largely reduces to “GAN-based SR + exact LR-consistency,” which is an incremental advance relative to prior soft constraints.
- Empirical support and rigor:
  • The 2D Swiss-roll convincingly illustrates the MAP behavior vs. MSE/MAE.
  • The MSE proof-of-concept shows affine projection does not hurt and improves consistency; good control.
  • On real images, evaluation is mostly qualitative. PSNR/SSIM are reported for CelebA but favor MSE as expected; no perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) or human studies are provided. No comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., SRGAN with VGG perceptual loss, ESRGAN/feature-based objectives) are shown.
  • AffDG and AffLL underperform (blurry outputs) and are dropped; the paper provides hypotheses but lacks robust remedies or ablations demonstrating viability.
  • The GAN results show artifacts (high-frequency noise; hallucinations like “snake→water”), underscoring the need for calibrated evaluation and robustness analysis.
  • The claim that the approach enables training without paired LR-HR is attractive but not explored with truly unpaired datasets or realistic degradation mismatches; A is assumed known and Gaussian, limiting practical applicability.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Include strong baselines: SRGAN (adversarial + perceptual feature loss), more recent perceptual-loss models. Report perceptual metrics (LPIPS, FID/KID) and, if possible, a small-scale user study.
- Provide ablations: effect of affine projection vs. soft penalty; instance noise schedule and magnitude; alternative GAN losses (e.g., hinge, WGAN-GP); entropy term’s impact (H[qθ]) on diversity vs. sharpness.
- Strengthen AffDG/AffLL: clarify training protocols and failure modes; explore multi-scale DAEs, score-based models, or annealed σ schedules with principled continuation; try modern continuous likelihoods or flow-based models for stronger gradients; show at least one setting where they yield competitive perceptual results.
- Evaluate robustness: mismatch between assumed A and real downsampling; different blur/downsample operators; real LR inputs (e.g., camera pipeline) without synthetic A.
- Quantify exact consistency: Report A ŷ − x errors across datasets; compare to soft penalties under equal training budgets.
- Benchmarks: Evaluate on standard SR sets (Set5, Set14, BSD100, Urban100, DIV2K) with both distortion (PSNR/SSIM) and perception-oriented metrics; include 4× and other scales.
- Discuss computational costs and stability: discriminator updates, convergence behavior with and without instance noise; training time vs. MSE baselines.
- Clarify the variational inference connection with a minimal empirical demonstration using stochastic generators.

Questions for the authors
- What is the precise training/evaluation protocol regarding paired vs. unpaired data? Do any experiments use truly unpaired HR and LR from different sources?
- How sensitive are results to misspecification of A? Can the method learn A (or A+) jointly, or adapt to unknown degradations?
- Can you quantify the impact of instance noise (ablation across σ schedules) on stability and final perceptual quality?
- For AffDG, what are the relative contributions of σ-annealing, DAE capacity, and projection to convergence? Have you tried score networks trained with denoising score matching at multiple σ (NCSN-like) for more reliable gradients?
- For AffLL, how does performance change with stronger density models (e.g., flows) and temperature scaling to prevent overly peaky gradients early in training?
- Can you report perceptual metrics (LPIPS/FID) and comparisons to SRGAN with VGG feature loss on CelebA/ImageNet subsets?
- Does the affine projection restrict texture diversity (due to null-space projection) or introduce artifacts across different choices of A (e.g., bicubic vs. Gaussian)?
