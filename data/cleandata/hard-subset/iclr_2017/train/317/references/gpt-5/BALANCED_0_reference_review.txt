1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes amortised MAP inference for single-image super-resolution, arguing that maximising the posterior under a natural image prior (with a known, noiseless downsampling operator) should yield perceptually plausible solutions. The key architectural idea is an affine projection layer that enforces exact data consistency: the network output is projected onto the affine subspace {y: Ay = x} using a numerically estimated Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse A+. This turns the MAP objective into minimising the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the model’s output distribution and the HR data distribution, which allows training without paired LR-HR data. The paper explores three strategies to approximate this cross-entropy:
- AffGAN: a modified GAN objective claimed to minimise KL[qθ‖pY], with “instance noise” to stabilise training.
- AffDG: backpropagating gradients from a denoiser to approximate ∇y log pY(y).
- AffLL: using a differentiable density model (PixelCNN + MCGSM) to provide log-likelihood gradients.

Experiments include a 2D Swiss-roll toy problem, grass textures, CelebA (4× SR from 16→64), and ImageNet (32→128). AffGAN gives the most visually appealing results; the affine projection improves data consistency and often speeds training even for MSE-trained models. The paper also shows a derivation connecting a stochastic variant of AffGAN to amortised variational inference.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited and uneven empirical validation: strong qualitative focus, sparse quantitative comparisons, and no human perceptual studies or modern perceptual metrics; important baselines are missing.
- The SR problem setting (noiseless known A, exact data consistency) and objective (prior-driven MAP) encourage hallucinations inconsistent with the original scene; this is acknowledged (e.g., “snake→water”) and undermines SR fidelity without compensating perceptual validation.

3) Supporting arguments
On the three guiding questions:

Q1. Specific problem? The paper aims to learn amortised MAP inference for SR given a known, deterministic downsampling operator A, avoiding MSE-induced blur by leveraging natural image statistics. The contribution of an affine projection layer to enforce exact consistency is clear and useful.

Q2. Motivation and placement in the literature? The case against MSE is well-motivated. The move to amortised MAP via cross-entropy is interesting, and the projection layer is a principled way to impose data consistency (similar in spirit to data-consistency steps in inverse problems). The GAN angle relates to SRGAN; the novelty is in (i) hard affine projection and (ii) the specific KL-oriented generator update plus instance noise. However, comparisons to SRGAN (Ledig et al. 2016) and other GAN-stabilisation methods (e.g., later WGAN/WGAN-GP) are absent, making it hard to judge the incremental value. The denoiser and density-guided variants are conceptually appealing but empirically weak here.

Q3. Do the results support the claims? On the 2D toy problem, cross-entropy measurements support the MAP narrative. For images, the evidence is mostly qualitative; PSNR/SSIM are reported only for MSE/GAN on CelebA, and AffGAN does not outperform in these fidelity metrics (as expected), but there is no systematic perceptual evaluation to substantiate the key claim of improved perceptual quality. The “snake→water” example highlights a risk of the proposed prior-driven MAP: hallucination that violates semantic fidelity. This is acceptable only if the goal is unconditional photo-realism rather than SR faithful to the input. The AffDG and AffLL variants are reported to be unstable or blurry and then de-emphasised; this weakens the claim that the general amortised MAP program works robustly beyond GANs. Finally, the assumption of a known, noiseless A is strong; real degradations (unknown kernels, noise, camera pipeline effects) are not addressed.

Strengths
- Affine projection layer is simple, principled, and practically useful; the data-consistency guarantee is valuable and the demonstration that it can be implemented with learned A+ is nice.
- Clear derivation reducing amortised MAP to cross-entropy minimisation; insightful perspective.
- Instance noise is a reasonable stabilisation idea with a defensible rationale.
- The variational inference connection for a stochastic AffGAN is an interesting theoretical note.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited: few datasets/sizes, sparse metrics, no user studies, no comparison to strong contemporaneous baselines (e.g., SRGAN) or later stabilisation techniques.
- The chosen SR setting (deterministic A, no noise) limits practical relevance; no experiments with kernel mismatch or noise.
- Hallucinations undermine SR fidelity; the work lacks a principled way to navigate the fidelity–perceptual quality tradeoff.
- The denoiser/density-guided routes are not convincingly demonstrated.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all affect the accept/reject decision)
- Add rigorous perceptual evaluation: two-alternative forced choice, MOS, or no-reference perceptual metrics; report diversity/fidelity tradeoffs if using stochastic generators.
- Include strong baselines: SRGAN (and ablations), perceptual-feature losses, and data-consistency-enforced variants of those methods. Compare also to simple “projection + MSE” baselines beyond what you show.
- Evaluate robustness to realistic degradations: unknown blur kernels, noise, and camera pipelines; study kernel mismatch and show how A/A+ can be learned or adapted.
- Quantify hallucinations: evaluate LPIPS (or similar), FID/KID between outputs and HR distributions, and identity preservation for faces.
- Provide ablations: effect of affine projection, accuracy of A+, instance noise schedule, discriminator overtraining, entropy of qθ.
- Clarify the modified GAN objective and its relation to f-GAN; provide theoretical assumptions and failure modes; contrast to WGAN/WGAN-GP or spectral norm regularisation.
- For AffDG/AffLL, deepen the analysis: annealing schedules, gradient variance, and why they blur; try score-based models or modern diffusion priors to estimate ∇ log pY.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to misspecification of A? Can A and A+ be learned jointly from data? What happens with mild noise in x?
- Do you have human perceptual studies comparing AffGAN to SRGAN or feature-loss methods? If not, can you provide them?
- Can the stochastic variant (Appendix F) produce multiple diverse SR outputs for the same x? How do you evaluate the conditional distribution quality (e.g., coverage vs. fidelity)?
- How do you select and anneal instance noise in practice? Does training remain stable if the discriminator is trained to near-convergence each step?
- Could you report results on standard SR benchmarks (Set5/14, BSD100, Urban100) with both fidelity and perceptual metrics, and include comparisons using bicubic-downsampled pairs?
- For AffDG/AffLL, can you use modern score/diffusion models as priors to revisit the approach? Do gradients remain stable when the prior is stronger?
