Summary and contributions
The paper tackles single-image super-resolution (SR) from an amortized Bayesian perspective. The authors argue that pixel-wise convex losses (e.g., MSE/MAE) are ill-suited under multimodal priors because they average plausible solutions and produce blurry outputs. They propose amortized MAP inference: for each LR input x, predict an HR image ŷ that maximizes log pY|X(y|x), but learn a single predictor fθ amortizing the computation. Key ideas:
- Affine projection layer: enforce exact consistency Afθ(x)=x by projecting arbitrary fθ(x) onto the affine subspace {y: Ay=x} using the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse A+. This avoids soft penalties and guarantees consistency.
- Reformulation: amortized MAP reduces to minimizing cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] where qθ is the distribution of model outputs on random LR inputs. This reframes SR training as matching the marginal distribution of outputs to the HR prior, without paired LR-HR data.
- Three training strategies to (approximately) minimize H[qθ, pY]: (i) AffGAN (a GAN variant whose generator update targets KL[q||p]; with “instance noise” to stabilize training), (ii) AffDG (backpropagating score estimates from a denoising autoencoder), and (iii) AffLL (using a differentiable density model akin to PixelCNN with MCGSM).
- Theory: proof sketch that their generator objective minimizes KL[q||p], analysis of instance noise as minimizing KL between noise-smoothed distributions, and a connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortized variational inference.
- Empirics: toy Swiss-roll demonstrates that AffGAN/AffDG recover posterior modes with low cross-entropy; affine projection does not hurt MSE-based SR and improves consistency; qualitative SR results on grass textures, CelebA, and ImageNet show sharper, more plausible images for AffGAN vs MSE/SSIM baselines.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Insufficient empirical rigor and comparisons for SR: evaluation is largely qualitative, lacks human studies or modern perceptual metrics, and does not compare against strong contemporary SR baselines (e.g., SRGAN/perceptual losses) beyond superficial references. The observation model is idealized (known A, no noise), limiting practical relevance.
2) Two of the three proposed training routes (AffDG, AffLL) underperform and are abandoned; the paper’s main empirical contribution reduces to AffGAN, where novelty and benefit over existing GAN-based SR methods are not convincingly demonstrated.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Well motivated. The MAP vs MSE/MAE argument is clear and illustrated by the Swiss-roll. The consistency constraint via affine projection is intuitive and practically useful; turning supervised SR into marginal distribution matching is a neat conceptual reframing.
- Placement in literature: Reasonable coverage (GANs, f-divergences, denoising-score connections, perceptual losses). The theoretical generator objective aiming at KL[q||p] is related to known GAN variants; instance noise is closely related to techniques that later became standard (e.g., input noise, spectral smoothing), and the authors cite contemporaneous work.
- Theory: The decomposition from amortized MAP to cross-entropy minimization is elegant (Eq. 8–9). The KL-minimizing generator update is plausible under standard GAN optimality assumptions; however, the practical conditions (optimal/unique discriminator, support overlap) are delicate. The instance-noise analysis addresses this and is a strength. The amortized VI connection (Appendix F) is interesting but high-level and not empirically validated.
- Empirical support:
  - Swiss-roll: appropriate to validate the cross-entropy objective; AffGAN/AffDG match MAP better than MSE/MAE.
  - Affine projection: good ablation showing exact consistency and no loss in MSE/SSIM; initializing with correct A+ matters.
  - Real images: Results are qualitative and mixed. AffGAN produces sharper images but with noticeable high-frequency noise; an ImageNet example hallucinates plausible but incorrect content (snake→water), illustrating the prior-dominant behavior of MAP. AffDG and AffLL yield blurry results and training instability; the paper offers reasonable hypotheses but no remedy.
  - Missing quantitative perceptual evaluation (e.g., LPIPS, FID/KID, user studies), and no comparisons to strong SR methods using perceptual/VGG losses or SRGAN-like pipelines. The restrictive/noiseless observation model (fixed Gaussian A, no degradation mismatch) is a major gap for real-world SR.
- Clarity and presentation: The core ideas are clear, but the paper has formatting issues, inconsistent notation and references, and some derivations relegated to appendices that rely on fragile assumptions. Experimental details are extensive.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen empirical evaluation:
  - Include modern perceptual metrics (LPIPS), human preference studies, and comparisons against SRGAN/perceptual-loss methods with matched backbones and identical consistency constraints. Evaluate on widely used SR benchmarks (Set5/14, BSD100, Urban100, DIV2K) and with realistic degradations (unknown blur, noise, JPEG).
  - Quantify the benefit of affine projection beyond zero consistency error: e.g., content fidelity metrics under kernel mismatch, color drift, and artifact rates.
  - Provide ablations on instance noise (schedule, magnitude) and discriminator training budget; report stability statistics across random seeds.
- Revisit AffDG/AffLL:
  - Consider modern score/density estimators (score matching with Langevin, diffusion models) that provide better gradients; your affine projection could pair well with score-based priors for conditional sampling/MAP via gradient ascent in y-space.
  - Improve curriculum for noise/likelihood annealing; quantify how score accuracy vs σ impacts SR training.
- MAP critique and alternatives:
  - Since MAP can be atypical in high dimensions, consider stochastic generators to sample from approximations of p(y|x) and evaluate sample diversity/consistency trade-offs. The amortized VI connection is promising—demonstrate it empirically.
- Practicality:
  - Discuss and test robustness to A-mismatch and observation noise; explore learning A jointly or handling unknown degradations.
  - Release code for the A+ computation and affine projection layer; this could be broadly useful.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is AffGAN to mismatch between the assumed A (Gaussian kernel, stride) and the true degradation? Can you quantify performance under misspecification and with additive noise?
2) Can you report perceptual metrics (LPIPS) and user studies comparing AffGAN to SRGAN and VGG-perceptual-loss SR, with and without your affine projection?
3) For AffGAN, how is the instance-noise schedule chosen in practice? Do you train D “to convergence” per iteration, and how many D:G steps are used? How critical is the modified generator loss vs the standard non-saturating loss?
4) Does affine projection help GAN-based SR even when using standard SR backbones and losses (e.g., SRResNet/SRGAN)? Ablate projection vs soft constraints under identical architectures.
5) For AffDG, what is the measured accuracy of the score estimate vs σ on held-out data, and how does that correlate with SR training stability? Have you tried modern score-based priors (e.g., diffusion models) to guide ŷ updates?
6) Can you provide quantitative evidence that minimizing H[qθ,pY] yields per-input outputs close to the posterior mode along the affine slice, beyond the toy example?

Overall, the paper presents appealing ideas (affine projection, cross-entropy framing, stabilization via instance noise) but falls short in empirical validation and comparisons necessary for ICLR.
