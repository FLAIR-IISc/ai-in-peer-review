Summary and contributions
The paper tackles single-image super-resolution (SR) by reframing it as amortized MAP inference under a natural image prior, rather than empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses. The key ideas are:
- Enforce exact data fidelity via an affine projection layer: ŷ = (I − A+ A) fθ(x) + A+ x, guaranteeing A ŷ = x for a known downsampling operator A (modeled as strided convolution) and its Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse A+ (a learned deconvolution initialized numerically).
- Show that amortized MAP reduces to minimizing the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the distribution of model outputs qθ and the HR data distribution pY, thereby decoupling training from paired LR–HR supervision.
- Propose three ways to minimize H[qθ, pY]: (i) AffGAN, a GAN variant with a generator defined by the affine-projected SR function, argued to approximately minimize KL[qθ‖pY] (and connect to amortized VI in a stochastic extension); (ii) AffDG, training with gradients from a denoising autoencoder to approximate ∇y log pY; and (iii) AffLL, training against a tractable density model (PixelCNN-like with MCGSM likelihood).
- Introduce “instance noise” (adding noise to both real and generated samples) to stabilize GAN training by ensuring overlapping supports, with an annealing schedule.
- Empirically: (a) on a 2D Swiss-roll toy problem MAP is tractable and AffGAN/AffDG closely match it in cross-entropy; (b) the affine projection does not hurt (and can improve) MSE training on CelebA while ensuring perfect consistency A ŷ = x; (c) on grass textures and faces/natural images, AffGAN yields perceptually sharper results than MSE/MAE baselines, though PSNR/SSIM remain lower as expected.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) The affine projection layer is a simple, principled architectural contribution that enforces exact data fidelity and can be broadly useful in inverse problems. (2) The amortized MAP framing with clear links to GAN training, plus the instance noise stabilization argument, are well-motivated and insightful. While empirical evaluation is not exhaustive, the combination of theoretical framing and practical improvements merits acceptance.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Motivation and placement: The limitations of MSE/MAE for perceptual SR are well established; reframing SR as amortized MAP provides a coherent objective (cross-entropy to pY) and obviates the need for paired LR–HR supervision. The paper situates itself relative to SRGAN and feature-metric losses, and clarifies the distinction between soft constraints and exact enforcement via projection.
- Technical clarity: The affine projection derivation and equivalence to H[qθ, pY] are clean. The AffGAN update targeting KL is justified (under Bayes-optimal D) and the amortized VI connection for a stochastic generator is interesting and timely.
- Practical insight: Instance noise is well-motivated (support overlap, uniqueness of optimal D), and the discussion is more principled than typical “GAN tricks.”
- Empirics: The toy problem convincingly shows AffGAN/AffDG approximate MAP and achieve low cross-entropy. The affine projection improves training dynamics and guarantees consistency. Qualitative results on faces/ImageNet demonstrate plausible, sharp reconstructions compared to MSE.

Weaknesses
- Empirical rigor: On real images, evaluation is largely qualitative. There is no perceptual study or modern perceptual metrics; comparisons to SRGAN or recent SR baselines on standard benchmarks (DIV2K, Set5/Set14, BSD100, Urban100) are missing. The denoiser/density-guided variants underperform and are not thoroughly explored; conclusions there are mostly negative.
- Assumptions/Generality: The approach assumes known, noise-free A and enforces exact consistency; real camera pipelines introduce unknown blur/noise and other degradations. Soft variants are mentioned but not evaluated as thoroughly, and affine projection may be brittle if A is misspecified.
- Theoretical caveats: The jump from minimizing H[qθ, pY] to producing per-x MAP estimates is intuitive but not fully formalized; the discussion of representation dependence of MAP is noted but not resolved empirically.
- Clarity/polish: Some equations (e.g., generator objective form), references, and notational consistency could be improved. Details on boundary handling for A/A+ and the implications of localized deconvolution kernels are limited.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen the empirical section with:
  - Standard SR benchmarks, including perceptual metrics (LPIPS, NIQE/PI) and, ideally, a small user study.
  - Direct comparisons to SRGAN and feature-based perceptual losses under identical data fidelity constraints.
  - Ablations isolating the impact of affine projection and instance noise (including training stability plots, with/without noise).
  - Robustness to mismatch/noise in A; soft-constraint variants with a learned noise model.
- Elaborate on computing A+ for strided convolutions: boundary conditions, kernel sizes, and when A+ must be large/long-range to be accurate. Provide code or pseudo-code and empirical error of AA+A and A+AA+ on images (not just random vectors).
- For AffDG/AffLL, provide sharper analyses of failure modes: visualize DAE gradient fields at different σ; measure density model calibration; try score-matching/energy models or modern score-based models as a stronger baseline for ∇ log pY.
- Clarify the precise generator loss used (log-odds vs standard non-saturating loss) and reconcile with the KL vs JS objectives literature. Explicitly state the conditions under which each divergence is minimized.
- Clean up references and notation, and consider releasing code/pretrained models to foster adoption of the affine projection module.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is the affine projection to mismatch in A or to sensor noise? Can you quantify degradation when A is slightly wrong, and compare exact projection vs soft-constraint under realistic degradations?
2. Can you provide an ablation showing the effect of instance noise (magnitude and annealing schedule) on training stability and final perceptual quality?
3. Do the affine-projected GANs generalize to different upscaling factors or blur kernels without retraining A+ (e.g., if A changes at test time)?
4. For AffDG, how do you select σ during training, and how does the DAE gradient magnitude/distribution behave across scales? Have you tried modern score-based models as denoisers?
5. Can you quantify the trade-off between enforcing exact consistency and perceptual sharpness, e.g., via controlled noise models or learned A?
