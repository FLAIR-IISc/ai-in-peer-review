Summary
This paper proposes amortised MAP inference for single-image super-resolution. The key idea is to enforce exact data consistency Afθ(x)=x (with A the downsampling operator) by inserting an affine projection layer ŷ=Π_Ax fθ(x)=(I−A⁺A)fθ(x)+A⁺x, where A⁺ is the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse of A. Under this constraint, maximising Ex log pY(ŷ) reduces to minimising the cross-entropy H[qθ,pY], where qθ is the marginal distribution of model outputs when x∼pX. The paper presents three training strategies to approximate this objective without paired LR-HR data: (i) AffGAN, a GAN variant claimed to minimise KL[qθ||pY] and introducing “instance noise” for stability; (ii) AffDG, using a denoiser to provide score estimates ∇y log pY(y); and (iii) AffLL, using a PixelCNN+MCGSM likelihood to guide training. A numerically learned deconvolution A⁺ is used to implement Π_Ax. Experiments on a 2D Swiss roll, grass textures, CelebA 16→64, and ImageNet 32→128 show that AffGAN produces sharper images than MSE, while AffDG and AffLL tend to blur; affine projection yields zero LR-consistency error and sometimes faster MSE training. Appendix F claims a connection between stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

Decision: Reject
Despite interesting ideas (affine projection for exact consistency; the “instance noise” stabilisation; a unifying objective), the paper has substantial issues in theoretical claims, experimental validation, baselines, and presentation/formatting. The empirical evidence is not rigorous enough to support the main claims, and the writing contains numerous technical and formatting flaws that impede verification.

Supporting arguments
- Objective and MAP claim: The reduction to minimising H[qθ,pY] over the marginal qθ (Eq. 9) does not, in general, guarantee amortised MAP per x. It ensures that the aggregate distribution of outputs matches high-density regions under pY subject to LR consistency in distribution, but it can prefer high-prior solutions that sacrifice content fidelity. The “snake→water” failure illustrates this. The paper acknowledges representation-dependence of MAP but still positions the approach as delivering “plausible” SR; however, SR typically requires fidelity to input content. No experiment evaluates fidelity beyond LR-consistency (e.g., LPIPS or identity preservation for faces).

- Affine projection novelty and correctness: Enforcing exact data consistency via a learned A⁺ is a useful architectural constraint. However, A⁺ as a local deconvolution is only an approximation to the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse for strided convolutions; the true pseudoinverse is generally nonlocal. The paper claims extremely small errors (10^-12–10^-8) for A−ABA and B−BAB with small kernels, which seems implausible for stride-4 Gaussian blur with 5×5 upkernels. Details (kernel sizes, channels, boundary handling) are insufficient to verify this. “42 kernels of size 5×5” for A⁺ is unexplained (why 42? color/grayscale? relation to sub-pixel factors). This undermines the exactness of the claimed projection.

- GAN theory claims: The generator loss (Eq. 10) is unusual and poorly typeset; the derivation that training minimises KL[qθ||pY] relies on an optimal, unique discriminator and perfect optimisation at each step, which is contradicted by practical instability and by adding instance noise (which changes the divergence). The claim that dσ(q,p)=KL[pσ∗q||pσ∗p] is a Bregman divergence “if pσ is Gaussian” is stated without proof and is nonstandard. The link to amortised VI (Appendix F) is interesting but also rests on strong assumptions (optimal D; divergence equivalence; stochastic generator) and is not empirically validated.

- Instance noise: Adding Gaussian noise to inputs of D is sensible and has appeared in concurrent/related work. The paper acknowledges parallel methods but presents no ablation isolating instance noise vs other stabilisers (e.g., label smoothing, spectral norm, gradient penalty). The annealing schedule heuristic is not specified quantitatively; no stability curves/discriminator losses are shown beyond a qualitative statement.

- Experimental evaluation: 
  - Datasets/benchmarks: No standard SR benchmarks (Set5/14, BSD100, Urban100, DIV2K) or bicubic degradation; ImageNet 32→128 and CelebA 16→64 are nonstandard; “grass textures” are ill-defined. 
  - Metrics: Predominantly qualitative; PSNR/SSIM reported only for CelebA and unsurprisingly favour MSE. No perceptual metrics (LPIPS, FID, NIQE/PI) or human studies to substantiate “perceptual quality”.
  - Baselines: No comparisons to strong SR networks (SRResNet/EDSR/VDSR/SRGAN), nor to recent GAN/perceptual-loss SR methods. The MSE baseline is an in-house CNN only.
  - Failure analysis: The admitted hallucinations (snake→water) question suitability for SR use-cases; no mechanism to trade off fidelity vs realism (e.g., with a content term) is provided beyond noting soft constraints performed worse.
  - Reproducibility: Important hyperparameters (learning rates, batch sizes, training steps, optimizer settings) are missing. No code. The A⁺ learning procedure is difficult to replicate from the current description.

- Clarity/formatting: The manuscript contains many typos, broken equations, missing symbols, and malformed references (e.g., “? \Q2016\E”, incomplete equations, footnotes in text, inconsistent figure/table references). This significantly impairs readability and raises concerns about correctness.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide a rigorous analysis of when minimising H[qθ,pY] under Afθ(x)=x approximates amortised MAP per x, and when it may sacrifice content fidelity. Consider adding a content alignment term or a mechanism to sample from q(y|x) rather than produce a single mode.

- Validate A⁺: precisely specify convolution strides, padding, channels, and show quantitative reconstruction errors for Afθ(x)−x across datasets. Compare learned A⁺ to true pseudoinverse in Fourier domain for the chosen A.

- Strengthen experiments: evaluate on standard SR benchmarks with bicubic A; include strong baselines (SRResNet/EDSR, SRGAN) and perceptual metrics and/or user study. Report runtime and memory.

- Instance noise: add ablations comparing with/without noise, with label smoothing, gradient penalty, spectral normalisation; report discriminator calibration and stability metrics; detail the annealing schedule.

- Denoiser/density guidance: either substantially improve or tone down these contributions. If kept, include stronger density models (modern autoregressive/flow models), proper score matching (Sliced Score Matching, NCSN), and a careful annealing schedule; otherwise, present them as negative results.

- Clarify the KL-minimising GAN objective and ensure equations are correct and readable; reconcile with the use of instance noise.

- Fix references, notation, and figures; release code and pretrained models.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you precisely quantify the approximation quality of your learned A⁺ (per image LR-consistency error, and AA⁺A and A⁺AA⁺ residuals) under your convolutional/stride/padding settings?

2. How does AffGAN perform on standard SR benchmarks (DIV2K, Set14) with bicubic downsampling, against SRGAN/EDSR in both distortion and perceptual metrics?

3. Can you provide ablations for instance noise (noise level, schedule), and comparisons to other stabilisation techniques? Does your method still “minimise KL” under instance noise?

4. Does minimising H[qθ,pY] lead to content drift systematically? Can you quantify identity preservation on CelebA (face recognition score vs HR ground truth)?

5. For the denoiser-based method, what σ schedule and DAE capacity were used? Did you try modern score-based training or DSM/SSM alternatives?

6. Is the “unpaired” training claim actually exercised (true unpaired LR and HR sources), or are LR images always generated by A from HR? If the latter, please qualify the claim.
