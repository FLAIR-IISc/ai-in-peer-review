**Summary of Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) by casting the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. This is a departure from the common empirical risk minimisation framework with MSE loss, aiming to produce more perceptually plausible high-resolution (HR) images that have high probability under the natural image prior. The core technical contribution is an "affine projection" layer, a network architecture component that enforces the output HR image to be perfectly consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. This constraint simplifies the MAP objective to maximising the log-prior of the output, which is equivalent to minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of true HR images. To solve this, the authors propose three distinct methods: 1) AffGAN, based on Generative Adversarial Networks; 2) AffDG, guided by the score function (gradient of the log-density) estimated by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder; and 3) AffLL, guided by a pre-trained explicit density model (PixelCNN-style). The paper also introduces "instance noise" as a technique to stabilise GAN training.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a theoretically appealing framework and some interesting ideas, I am recommending rejection due to two primary reasons. First, the manuscript is rife with significant formatting errors, typos in key equations, and inconsistencies that severely undermine its credibility and suggest a lack of thorough preparation. Second, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the paper's broad claims; two of the three proposed methods are dismissed prematurely based on a single experiment, and the main method is not rigorously compared against contemporary state-of-the-art perceptual SR models.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Significant Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper suffers from a startling number of errors that hinder readability and reflect a lack of diligence.
    *   **Citations:** The reference list is improperly formatted, with numerous entries containing BibTeX-like error strings (e.g., "Alain and Bengio.,? \Q2014\E, Alain and Bengio.,", "Bruna et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Bruna et al\.,"). This is a pervasive issue that should have been caught in any proofreading pass.
    *   **Incomplete Captions and Tables:** The caption for Figure 1 is incomplete, containing empty parentheses where plot symbols should be described (e.g., "AffGAN( )"). In Table 1, the consistency error for affine models is listed as exactly "0.0", which is only true in theory; stating it up to numerical precision would be more accurate.
    *   **Equation Typos and Inconsistencies:** There appears to be a typo in the GAN objective for the generator in Section 3.2, with a stray `-D(G(z))` term. More critically, there is a major inconsistency in the GAN objective between the main text and the appendix. Section 3.2 proposes `L(G;D) = −Ez∼pZ log D(G(z))`, which is a well-known non-saturating loss. However, Appendix A, which purports to show this objective minimises `KL[qG‖pY]`, analyzes a completely different objective: `Ey∼qG log (1−Dψ(y))/Dψ(y)`. This discrepancy confuses the theoretical claims of the paper. Is the algorithm in the paper minimising the KL divergence as claimed, or a different objective?

2.  **Insufficient and Incomplete Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation of the proposed framework is weak.
    *   **Premature Dismissal of AffDG and AffLL:** The paper introduces three methods for amortised MAP inference, but AffDG and AffLL are discarded after a single experiment on grass textures for producing "blurry results" (Section 5.3). This is a weak basis for dismissal. The paper should have provided a more thorough analysis of why these principled methods failed, as this is a significant (and interesting) negative result. As it stands, the paper promises a comparison of three methods but effectively only evaluates one.
    *   **Lack of State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The primary adversarial baseline, SoftGAN, is based on "unpublished work". A rigorous evaluation would require comparison against published, state-of-the-art perceptual SR methods, most notably SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016), which this work is clearly inspired by. Without this comparison, it is impossible to assess the practical benefit of the proposed affine projection over a standard content loss paired with a soft consistency penalty.
    *   **Evaluation Metrics:** The primary motivation is improving "perceptual quality," yet no quantitative perceptual metrics (e.g., MOS, NIQE, LPIPS) are used. The evaluation relies on qualitative visual inspection and standard metrics like PSNR/SSIM, on which the proposed model performs worse than the MSE baseline, as expected. The "Consistency" metric in Table 2 is self-serving, as it is designed to measure the very constraint the affine models enforce by construction.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The manuscript requires a thorough round of proofreading to fix the numerous formatting and typographical errors. The state of the citations is unacceptable for a formal submission.
*   The theoretical analysis, especially regarding the GAN objective in Appendix A, must be clarified and made consistent with the method described in the main text.
*   The connection to amortised variational inference presented in Appendix F is highly compelling and arguably a more significant contribution than the main MAP inference story. The authors might consider reframing the paper to highlight this connection more prominently.
*   The "instance noise" trick is interesting but needs more detailed exposition to be reproducible. The proposed heuristic annealing schedule ("keep the optimal discriminator’s loss constant") is vague. Providing a concrete algorithm or implementation details would be valuable.
*   The authors provide an excellent critique of MAP inference in Section 5.6. This discussion should be better integrated into the paper's motivation, perhaps to justify why the GAN-based KL-minimising approach is preferable to the other two more direct MAP approaches that failed.

**Questions for Authors**

1.  Can you clarify the discrepancy between the generator's objective function presented in Section 3.2 and the one analyzed in Appendix A? Which one was used in the experiments, and does it truly minimize `KL[qG‖pY]` as claimed?
2.  Could you provide more insight into why the AffDG and AffLL methods failed to produce sharp results, while AffGAN succeeded? Does this suggest a fundamental limitation of using explicit score/density models for this task, or was it an issue of implementation and optimisation?
3.  Why was a direct comparison to SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016) omitted? Given the similarity in goals and methods (using GANs for perceptual SR), this seems like the most relevant baseline. How do you expect AffGAN to perform against SRGAN, both visually and in terms of perceptual metrics?
