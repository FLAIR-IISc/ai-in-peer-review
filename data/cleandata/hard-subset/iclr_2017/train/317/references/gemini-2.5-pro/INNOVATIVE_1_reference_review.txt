### Summary of Contributions
This paper recasts the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR) from the standard empirical risk minimization framework into one of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that this is a more principled approach for achieving high perceptual quality, as it seeks solutions that are probable under the prior distribution of natural images, thus avoiding the blurry outputs typical of MSE-trained models. The paper makes several significant and novel contributions:

1.  **A Principled MAP Framework:** It formally derives the objective for amortised MAP inference in SR, showing it is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the distribution of true high-resolution images. This provides a strong theoretical foundation for using generative approaches in SR.

2.  **The Affine Projection Layer:** A novel architectural component is proposed that enforces the consistency constraint (i.e., that the downsampled output must exactly match the low-resolution input) by construction. This is achieved through a clever projection onto the affine subspace of valid solutions, `gθ(x) = (I − A+A)fθ(x) + A+x`, elegantly converting a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one.

3.  **Three Methods for Prior Optimization:** The paper explores three distinct and creative methods for optimizing the prior term (i.e., minimizing the cross-entropy):
    *   **AffGAN:** An adversarial approach using a GAN to minimize the KL-divergence.
    *   **AffDG:** A novel method that uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the score (gradient of the log-probability) of the data distribution, which is then backpropagated to train the SR network.
    *   **AffLL:** A more direct approach using a pre-trained, explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant) to provide the optimization signal.

### Decision and Key Reasons
**Decision:** Accept

This paper presents a bold and highly novel conceptual reframing of the super-resolution problem. My decision is based on two key points:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Elegance:** The formulation of SR as amortised MAP inference, coupled with the elegant affine projection layer, is a significant conceptual leap. It provides a principled theoretical grounding for a class of problems previously tackled with more heuristic or purely empirical methods. The architectural idea of enforcing the consistency constraint by projection is particularly powerful and could have impact beyond SR to other inverse problems.

2.  **Ambitious Exploration of New Directions:** The paper does not just propose one solution but courageously explores three different ways to model the image prior, drawing deep connections to GANs, score matching via denoising, and explicit density modeling. The denoiser-guided (AffDG) approach is especially creative, proposing a novel mechanism for training networks. Even though not all three methods were successful on complex images, their proposal opens up fascinating new avenues for research. This is precisely the kind of ambitious, idea-driven work that the community should encourage.

### Supporting Arguments
The core strength of this paper lies in its ideas. The shift from minimizing a pixel-wise loss to matching a distribution via MAP inference is compelling and well-motivated by the excellent Swiss-roll toy example, which clearly visualizes the pitfalls of MSE/MAE and the promise of the MAP approach.

The affine projection layer is a standout contribution. It is a clean, architectural solution to a fundamental constraint of the SR problem. The experiments in Section 5.2 convincingly show that this layer not only enforces consistency but can also accelerate and improve training even in a standard MSE framework. This is a powerful result on its own.

While the empirical results for the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods on real images are disappointing, the success of the AffGAN method validates the overall framework. The visual results on grass, faces, and ImageNet are sharp and plausible, clearly demonstrating the benefit over MSE-based methods. The failure of AffDG and AffLL is a weakness, but I view it not as a refutation of the core idea, but as an honest exposition of the significant challenges in optimizing with gradients derived from learned models in high-dimensional spaces. The paper's value is in proposing these bold directions, not in perfecting their execution.

Finally, the discussion of the limitations of MAP inference (Sec 5.6) and the connection to amortised variational inference in the appendix are signs of deep thinking and intellectual honesty, strengthening the paper considerably.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement
While I strongly support acceptance, the paper could be improved.
*   **Investigating the Failure of AffDG/AffLL:** The paper would be strengthened if the authors could provide more intuition or analysis as to why the AffDG and AffLL methods produced blurry results on textures. The current explanation is brief. Is the issue poor gradient estimates from the denoiser/density model, optimization instability, or something more fundamental? The blurriness seems to indicate a collapse towards a mean, ironically betraying the paper's initial motivation. Further investigation here would be highly valuable for future work in this direction.
*   **Highlighting the VI Connection:** The link between the stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference, currently in Appendix F, is a profound insight. It connects two major families of generative models in a novel context. I would encourage the authors to consider bringing a summary of this finding into the main body of the paper (perhaps in the conclusion or future work section) as it significantly broadens the paper's impact.
*   **Instance Noise:** The introduction of instance noise is an interesting practical contribution to stabilizing GANs. A more direct empirical comparison to other techniques like one-sided label smoothing on this specific SR task would make its benefits more concrete.

### Questions for the Authors
1.  The failure of the AffDG method is particularly intriguing given the theoretical appeal of the idea. Did you experiment with different denoiser architectures or training schedules? Is it possible that for very small noise levels σ, the denoiser provides gradients that are too sharp and localized, leading to poor optimization dynamics when training the SR generator?
2.  The "snake to water" example in Figure 5 is a fascinating case of the prior overwhelming the likelihood. It produces a plausible image, but one that is semantically incorrect. This suggests a potential tension in MAP inference for SR. Do you have any thoughts on how one might control this trade-off, perhaps for applications where preserving the original semantic content is critical?
