### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper formulates the single-image super-resolution (SR) task as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The central contribution is an architectural component, an "affine projection layer," which enforces the constraint that the downsampled output of the network must exactly match the low-resolution input. The authors show that by satisfying this likelihood constraint through architecture, the MAP inference problem reduces to an unconstrained optimization of the image prior, equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated high-resolution (HR) images and the true data distribution.

To solve this objective, the paper proposes and evaluates three distinct approaches:
1.  **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) variant that uses the affine projected SR network as its generator, thereby approximately minimizing the KL-divergence between the generated and real image distributions. The paper also introduces "instance noise" as a general technique for stabilizing GAN training.
2.  **AffDG:** A method that uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the score (gradient of the log-probability) of the data distribution, and backpropagates this gradient signal to train the SR network.
3.  **AffLL:** An approach that uses a pre-trained, explicit generative model (a PixelCNN variant) to directly provide a log-likelihood signal for training the SR network.

The methods are evaluated on a 2D toy problem and on natural image datasets (grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet). The results demonstrate that the affine projection layer improves performance even for standard MSE training and that the AffGAN method produces perceptually sharp and plausible images, outperforming the other two proposed MAP approaches. The paper concludes with a theoretical argument in the appendix connecting a stochastic version of AffGAN to amortised variational inference.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and principled framework for super-resolution by formulating it as amortised MAP inference. The proposed affine projection layer is a technically sound and elegant method for enforcing the data consistency constraint, which distinguishes it from prior work that relies on soft penalties.
2.  The empirical results for the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) demonstrate a clear and significant improvement in perceptual quality over traditional methods. This provides strong support for the proposed framework, even though the alternative denoiser and density-guided methods were shown to be less effective in practice.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and structured logically. The motivation for MAP inference is clearly articulated with an effective 2D toy example (Figure 1) that illustrates the shortcomings of MSE and MAE losses. The theoretical derivation in Section 3, which connects amortised MAP inference under the affine constraint to cross-entropy minimization, is presented clearly and is straightforward to follow.

**Technical Soundness:** The core technical proposal—the affine projection layer—is sound. It correctly identifies the set of valid HR solutions as an affine subspace and implements a projection onto it using standard neural network components (convolution and deconvolution). This provides a hard constraint that is more principled than the soft consistency losses used in related work. The three proposed methods for optimizing the resulting objective are well-grounded in existing literature on generative modeling, score matching, and adversarial learning. The experiments are well-designed, starting with a low-dimensional problem where the ground truth MAP solution can be approximated, which serves as a valuable sanity check for the proposed methods. The empirical results on image datasets support the main claim that AffGAN can produce high-quality perceptual results.

**Novelty and Significance:** The paper's main contributions are novel and significant.
*   The formulation of SR as amortised MAP inference, and the insight that an architectural constraint simplifies the objective to matching the prior distribution, provides a new theoretical lens through which to view perceptual SR.
*   The affine projection layer is a novel architectural element for this problem that could be adopted in other inverse problems with linear forward models.
*   While GANs have been used for SR previously, this paper provides a strong theoretical justification for their use from the MAP inference perspective.
*   The exploration of denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches, while ultimately less successful, represents a valuable scientific exploration of alternative ways to optimize the image prior.
*   The paper also makes secondary contributions, such as the "instance noise" technique for GAN stabilization and the theoretical connection between a stochastic AffGAN and variational inference, which are of broader interest.

A notable weakness is the empirical failure of the AffDG and AffLL models, which are reported to produce blurry results and are quickly discarded after one experiment. While this limits the paper's contribution to primarily the AffGAN method, the success of AffGAN is strong enough on its own to warrant acceptance.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   The empirical failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods deserves a more detailed analysis in the main text rather than being relegated to the appendix. A deeper investigation into why these methods failed (e.g., optimization instability, inaccuracy of the gradient/density estimates) would provide valuable insights for the community and make the paper a more complete scientific study.
*   The "instance noise" contribution could be strengthened with a direct empirical comparison. An ablation study showing training curves or output quality with and without instance noise, or in comparison to other stabilization tricks like label smoothing, would more concretely demonstrate its value.
*   The visual results from AffGAN, while sharp, occasionally contain high-frequency artifacts characteristic of GANs. A brief discussion on the potential causes and mitigation strategies for these artifacts would be a useful addition.
*   The discussion on the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is excellent. It shows a nuanced understanding of the problem and preempts potential criticisms, strengthening the paper's overall argument.

### 5. Questions for the Authors
*   Could you provide more insight into the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods on the grass texture dataset? Do you believe these approaches are fundamentally limited by the accuracy of current denoisers and density models, or could they potentially be made to work with architectural improvements or more sophisticated training procedures?
*   In the AffDG method, the gradients for the SR network are derived from a pre-trained denoising model. How sensitive is the training process to the quality of the denoiser? Did you observe instability or poor performance when using denoisers that were not trained to full convergence or with suboptimal noise schedules?
*   How does the proposed AffGAN method compare specifically to SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016)? Both use GANs for perceptual SR. A direct comparison of the objective functions (hard affine constraint vs. feature-space content loss) and the resulting image quality would help to precisely situate your contribution relative to this key piece of prior work.
*   The paper mentions that instance noise was essential for stabilizing the ImageNet experiments. Was this technique also used or beneficial for the CelebA experiments? What properties of the ImageNet dataset made the training particularly unstable without this regularization?
