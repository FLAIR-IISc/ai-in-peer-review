### **Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of single image super-resolution (SR), arguing that standard approaches based on empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses (e.g., MSE) produce blurry and perceptually unsatisfying results. The authors reframe SR as a problem of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, with the goal of finding a high-resolution (HR) output that is maximally probable under the posterior distribution given the low-resolution (LR) input. The paper's primary contributions are twofold. First, it introduces a novel "affine projection" layer, an architectural constraint that ensures any generated HR image is perfectly consistent with the LR input by projecting the network's output onto the affine subspace of valid solutions. This elegantly handles the likelihood term in the MAP objective. Second, with the consistency constraint satisfied, the problem reduces to maximizing the prior probability of the output. The paper proposes and investigates three distinct methods to achieve this by minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real HR images: (1) **AffGAN**, a Generative Adversarial Network approach; (2) **AffDG**, a method guided by the score (gradient of the log-probability) estimated from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder; and (3) **AffLL**, a method guided by the log-likelihood from a pre-trained explicit density model (PixelCNN-style).

### **Decision and Key Reasons**
**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Principled and Novel Framework:** The paper presents a very clear, elegant, and theoretically sound framework for perceptual super-resolution based on amortised MAP inference. The introduction of the affine projection layer is a significant and novel contribution that cleanly disentangles the data consistency requirement from the image plausibility objective, moving beyond soft penalties to a hard architectural constraint.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation of the Core Idea:** The proposed GAN-based variant, AffGAN, is shown to be highly effective, producing visually compelling and sharp results that significantly outperform traditional MSE-based methods in perceptual quality. The experiments are well-designed, including an excellent toy problem that provides clear intuition and a crucial control experiment validating the utility of the affine projection layer itself.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**
**Strengths:**
*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly articulates the shortcomings of MSE loss and provides a strong motivation for MAP inference. The 2D Swiss-roll example is a powerful and intuitive illustration of the core concepts, demonstrating precisely why MAP estimation is preferable to seeking the mean or median of the posterior.
*   **Technical Novelty:** The affine projection layer is a clever architectural innovation that rigorously enforces the `Ax=y` constraint. This is a more principled approach than the soft L1/L2 consistency penalties used in prior work. Furthermore, the paper provides solid theoretical grounding for using GANs in this context by connecting the training objective to KL-divergence minimization. The proposed "instance noise" technique for stabilizing GAN training is also an interesting contribution.
*   **Rigorous and Insightful Experiments:** The experimental design is thorough. The 2D toy problem successfully validates that AffGAN and AffDG are indeed minimizing the intended objective (Table 1). The experiment in Section 5.2, which shows that the affine projection layer can even improve performance for MSE-trained models, is a strong piece of evidence for its general utility. The qualitative results on grass, faces, and natural images convincingly demonstrate AffGAN's ability to generate plausible high-frequency details, even if it sometimes "hallucinates" content (e.g., the snake becoming water), which powerfully illustrates that the model has learned a strong prior over natural images.
*   **Thoughtful Discussion:** The "Criticism and Future Directions" section (5.6) is excellent. The authors show a deep understanding of the potential limitations of MAP inference (e.g., dependence on representation, atypicality of the mode) and thoughtfully connect their work to the alternative of posterior sampling via a stochastic model (Appendix F). This self-awareness and forward-looking discussion significantly strengthens the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Limited Empirical Success of All Proposed Methods:** While the paper proposes a general framework with three methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL), the empirical results show that only AffGAN produces high-quality results on complex image datasets. The AffDG and AffLL methods, while theoretically interesting, are shown to produce blurry outputs on the grass texture task and are subsequently dropped. This means the paper's main empirical success rests on one of the three proposed instantiations, which slightly weakens the claim of a broadly successful general framework.
*   **Lack of Direct Comparison for Instance Noise:** The paper introduces instance noise as a technique for stabilizing GAN training and provides good theoretical motivation. However, its effectiveness is not directly compared against other popular stabilization techniques like one-sided label smoothing, spectral normalization, or a gradient penalty. Such a comparison would have made the contribution of this specific technique clearer.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**
*   The empirical failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods is an important finding. The discussion in Appendix E is useful, but it would be beneficial to summarize these insights more prominently in the main text (perhaps in Section 5.3). This would provide a more balanced perspective on the practical challenges of implementing the different variants of the proposed framework.
*   The paper notes that the pseudo-inverse `A+` is found numerically. It could be helpful to briefly comment on the sensitivity of the model's performance to the precision of this numerical approximation.
*   The connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference drawn in Appendix F is fascinating. While a full empirical study is rightly left for future work, even a small qualitative experiment showing diverse samples from a stochastic generator for a single LR input would have been a powerful addition to demonstrate the potential of this direction.

### **Questions for Authors**
1.  Could you elaborate further on the reasons for the poor performance of the AffDG and AffLL methods on image data? The appendix suggests issues with the accuracy of the gradient estimates. Do you believe this is a limitation of the specific denoiser/density models used (i.e., they were not powerful enough), or do you suspect there is a more fundamental difficulty in optimizing SR networks using score/likelihood-based gradients in such high-dimensional spaces?
2.  Instance noise appears to be an effective tool for stabilization in your experiments. Have you performed any direct empirical comparisons against other contemporary GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., one-sided label smoothing, WGAN-GP) on your SR task to isolate the benefits of your proposed method?
3.  The link to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is very exciting, as it suggests a way to sample from the posterior, addressing the "atypical mode" critique of MAP inference. Have you conducted any preliminary experiments with this stochastic AffGAN? If so, does it appear capable of generating diverse yet plausible high-resolution outputs for a single low-resolution input?
