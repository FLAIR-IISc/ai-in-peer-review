### **Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a principled probabilistic framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) that aims to generate perceptually plausible high-resolution (HR) images, moving away from the blurry results typical of methods trained with pixel-wise MSE loss. The core idea is to frame SR as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, seeking solutions that have high probability under the natural image prior.

The paper makes several key contributions:
*   It proposes a novel network architecture featuring an **affine projection layer**. This layer elegantly enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled output must be identical to the low-resolution (LR) input. This is a significant improvement over previous work that used soft penalties.
*   By enforcing this consistency constraint, the MAP objective simplifies to maximizing the log-prior of the generated HR image. This insight reframes the SR task as a generative modeling problem: training the network to match the distribution of its outputs to the true distribution of HR images, which is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the two.
*   The paper then proposes and investigates three distinct methods for minimizing this cross-entropy objective, all built upon the affine projection architecture:
    1.  **AffGAN:** A GAN-based approach that minimizes the KL-divergence between the generated and real image distributions.
    2.  **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder, which leverages the connection between optimal denoising and the score function (gradient of the log-probability) of the data distribution.
    3.  **AffLL:** A method that directly uses a state-of-the-art density model (PixelCNN with MCGSM) to provide a training signal via its log-likelihood.

The authors validate their framework first on a 2D toy problem, showing that the proposed methods successfully find the posterior mode, and then demonstrate that the AffGAN variant produces sharp, perceptually convincing results on several real-world image datasets.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear and compelling core idea. The two primary reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper provides a rigorous and well-motivated probabilistic framework for super-resolution. Framing the problem as amortised MAP inference and connecting it to generative modeling via cross-entropy minimization provides a solid theoretical justification for using techniques like GANs, moving beyond purely heuristic choices.
2.  **The Affine Projection Layer:** This is an elegant and impactful architectural contribution. It directly solves the problem of input-output consistency in a clean, parameter-free way. The empirical validation shows it not only guarantees consistency but can also improve training speed and performance even for standard MSE-based models.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

**Strengths:**
*   The theoretical development is clear and sound. The derivation that transforms the constrained MAP objective into an unconstrained cross-entropy minimization problem (Eq. 6 to 9) is the cornerstone of the paper and is very well-explained.
*   The 2D Swiss-roll experiment is an excellent piece of supporting evidence. It provides a clear, low-dimensional visualization and quantitative confirmation (Table 1) that the proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG) do what they claim: they find solutions close to the posterior mode and minimize cross-entropy, unlike traditional MSE/MAE approaches.
*   The AffGAN method produces high-quality qualitative results. The images in Figures 3, 4, and 5 are visibly sharper and more detailed than those from MSE-based models, especially on the challenging grass texture dataset, which is a known failure case for pixel-wise losses.
*   The paper shows strong intellectual honesty and depth in Section 5.6 by discussing the potential theoretical limitations of MAP inference itself (e.g., the atypicality of the mode). Furthermore, the connection drawn between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference in Appendix F is insightful and opens up promising avenues for future work.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper introduces three promising methods for amortised MAP inference, but only one (AffGAN) is shown to be effective on real image data. The AffDG and AffLL methods are reported to produce blurry results and are quickly dismissed after the grass texture experiment. This is the most significant weakness of the paper. It undermines the generality of the proposed framework and leaves the reader wondering why these theoretically-motivated approaches failed. A more detailed investigation into their failure would have substantially strengthened the paper.
*   The quantitative evaluation relies on PSNR and SSIM, metrics that the paper itself argues are poorly correlated with the target of perceptual quality. While this is understandable for comparison with baseline methods, the lack of modern perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID) makes it difficult to quantitatively assess the claimed perceptual improvements of AffGAN over its competitors.

Despite these weaknesses, the strengths of the core ideas—the MAP formulation and the affine projection layer—are significant enough to warrant acceptance. The success of the AffGAN variant alone is a valuable contribution.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions and are not central to my decision.

*   The discussion on the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is excellent. It would be fascinating to connect this discussion more explicitly to the empirical results. For example, could the "high-frequency noise" sometimes seen in GAN results be interpreted as an artifact of the mode being "atypical" compared to a random sample from the prior?
*   The instance noise technique for stabilizing GAN training is an interesting practical contribution. While the appendix provides good intuition, an empirical ablation study comparing it to other techniques like one-sided label smoothing on your specific task could make its benefits clearer.
*   The failure of the AffDG method is particularly surprising given the strong theoretical link between denoising and score matching. I would strongly encourage the authors to investigate this further in future work. Is the failure due to approximation errors in the learned denoiser, optimization instability when backpropagating the estimated gradients, or a more fundamental issue with applying this technique to train a deep generator?

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  Could you elaborate on the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods on the image datasets? Was the primary issue related to optimization (e.g., unstable or vanishing gradients), or do you suspect a more fundamental limitation in using score/density-based guidance for training a deep generative model in this SR context?
2.  For the AffLL method, what was the performance (e.g., bits per dimension) of the trained PixelCNN/MCGSM density model on the HR image data? This would help clarify whether the issue was with the generator's optimization or with the quality of the guidance signal from a potentially imperfect density model.
3.  Have you considered evaluating the perceptual quality of the AffGAN results using quantitative metrics like LPIPS, which are designed to correlate better with human judgments than PSNR/SSIM? Such results could provide stronger quantitative backing for the visual improvements shown.
4.  The connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is very compelling. Have you performed any preliminary experiments with a stochastic version of AffGAN? It would be interesting to know if this approach can generate diverse solutions for a single LR input and perhaps mitigate some of the "atypical mode" issues discussed in Section 5.6.
