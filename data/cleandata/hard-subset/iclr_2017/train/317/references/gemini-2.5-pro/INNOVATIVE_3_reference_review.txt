**Summary of Contributions**

This paper recasts the problem of single image super-resolution (SR) from the standard empirical risk minimization framework into one of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The central motivation is that traditional MSE-based methods produce blurry results by averaging over a multimodal posterior of plausible high-resolution (HR) images, whereas a MAP estimate seeks the single most probable HR image, which should be sharper and more perceptually realistic.

The core technical contribution is a novel neural network architecture incorporating an "affine projection layer". This layer elegantly enforces the hard physical constraint that the downsampled output must exactly match the low-resolution (LR) input. This turns the MAP inference problem into an unconstrained optimization of the log-prior of the network's output distribution. To optimize this objective, the authors propose three distinct and creative methods that do not require paired HR-LR data:
1.  **AffGAN:** An adversarial approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) that is shown to minimize the KL divergence between the output distribution and the true data distribution.
2.  **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder, which leverages the connection between optimal denoising and the score function (the gradient of the log-density) to perform gradient ascent on the log-prior.
3.  **AffLL:** A method guided by a pre-trained, explicit deep generative model (a PixelCNN variant), which directly optimizes the log-likelihood of the generated images under this model.

The paper demonstrates through experiments on a toy problem and real image datasets that the affine projection is beneficial and that the AffGAN model produces perceptually high-quality results that are significantly sharper than those from MSE-trained models.

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The work is built on a strong, novel conceptual foundation—reframing SR as amortised MAP inference—which is a significant departure from the dominant paradigm of empirical risk minimization with hand-crafted perceptual loss functions. The introduction of the affine projection layer is an elegant and powerful architectural idea for inverse problems that I believe will be influential. While not all of the proposed optimization strategies were empirically successful, the creativity of the ideas and the success of the GAN-based approach are more than sufficient to merit acceptance. This is exactly the kind of bold, conceptually-driven research that moves the field forward.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Novel and Principled Framework:** The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical rigor and novelty. Instead of heuristically combining multiple loss terms (pixel-wise, adversarial, feature-space) as is common in perceptual SR, this work derives the training objective from first principles under a clear probabilistic goal: MAP inference. This provides a much-needed theoretical justification for using generative modeling techniques like GANs for SR, grounding them in the language of Bayesian inference. The connection drawn in the appendix between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference is particularly insightful.

2.  **Elegant Architectural Contribution:** The affine projection layer is a fantastic idea. By reformulating the SR network to explicitly project its output onto the affine subspace of all valid solutions, it guarantees input-output consistency by construction. This is a far more principled approach than the soft L1/L2 consistency penalties used in prior work. The experiment in Section 5.2, which shows that adding this layer can even improve the performance of a standard MSE-trained network, is a compelling validation of its general utility.

3.  **Bold Exploration of Optimization Strategies:** I commend the authors for proposing and testing three very different ways to optimize the MAP objective. The denoiser-guided (AffDG) approach is particularly creative, proposing to literally backpropagate the score function estimated by a denoiser through the SR network. While the AffDG and density-guided (AffLL) methods ultimately produced blurry results, their inclusion does not weaken the paper. On the contrary, they demonstrate the breadth of the core MAP inference idea and represent a valuable, albeit negative, scientific result. The paper is honest about their failure, and the success of the AffGAN method is sufficient to prove the value of the overall framework. The visually compelling results from AffGAN, especially on the challenging grass texture, convincingly demonstrate that the MAP objective leads to sharp, plausible images.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While I am enthusiastic about this paper, the following points could help improve it further:

*   **Analysis of Failed Methods:** The discussion of why AffDG and AffLL failed is relegated to the appendix and is somewhat brief. I would encourage the authors to bring some of this analysis into the main paper. Is the failure due to optimization difficulties? Or do the gradient estimates from the denoiser/density model, while theoretically sound, lack the fidelity needed to guide a powerful generator network in high-dimensional image space? A deeper discussion here would be very valuable for researchers who wish to build on these novel ideas.
*   **KL vs. Cross-Entropy:** The paper notes that the AffGAN objective (minimizing KL[q||p]) differs from the pure MAP objective (minimizing cross-entropy H[q, p]) by an entropy term H[q]. This is an important distinction. The authors suggest this might encourage diversity, which is an interesting hypothesis. It would be beneficial to expand on the implications of this. Does this mean AffGAN is not strictly performing MAP inference but something else, perhaps something closer to mode-seeking but regularized by output diversity?
*   **Instance Noise:** The instance noise trick for stabilizing GANs is a useful practical contribution. Given its apparent importance for the ImageNet experiments, a small ablation study showing training with and without it would strengthen this part of the contribution.

**Questions for Authors**

1.  Regarding the AffDG and AffLL methods, do you have a stronger intuition for their failure? For instance, in the AffDG case, the score is estimated around a noisy version of the data. Could the discrepancy between the score of the clean data manifold (which is what we want) and what the denoiser provides be a fundamental issue?
2.  The MAP estimate is known to be dependent on the data representation (as you rightly discuss in 5.6). Have you considered whether the MAP objective in raw pixel space is truly the right goal for *perceptual* quality? Could a similar MAP framework be developed in a learned perceptual feature space (e.g., from a VGG network), and how might that change the results?
3.  How was the downsampling kernel `A` chosen for the real-world experiments, and how sensitive is the performance of the affine projection to a mismatch between the assumed `A` and the true (unknown) downsampling process of a given LR image?
