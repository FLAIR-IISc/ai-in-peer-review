### 1. Summary

The paper proposes a new framework for single image super-resolution (SR) aimed at improving perceptual quality. It argues that traditional methods based on minimizing pixel-wise Mean Squared Error (MSE) produce overly smooth and blurry results because they effectively average over the multi-modal posterior distribution of high-resolution (HR) images consistent with a given low-resolution (LR) input. To address this, the paper reframes SR as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, seeking solutions that have high probability under the natural image prior.

The primary contribution is a novel CNN architecture component called the "affine projection layer". This layer is designed to explicitly enforce the constraint that the downsampled output of the SR network must match the LR input. This is achieved by projecting the output of an arbitrary network onto the affine subspace of all valid HR solutions. By enforcing this constraint architecturally, the MAP inference objective simplifies to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the true distribution of HR images.

Based on this framework, the paper develops three distinct methods to approximate this cross-entropy minimization:
1.  **AffGAN**: A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) variant where the generator is the affine-projected SR network. This approach is shown to approximately minimize the KL-divergence KL[q_G || p_Y]. The paper also introduces "instance noise" as a technique to stabilize GAN training.
2.  **AffDG**: A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder (DAE). It leverages the theoretical connection that an optimal denoiser can estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data distribution (the score). This gradient is then backpropagated through the SR network to perform optimization.
3.  **AffLL**: A method guided by a pre-trained, explicit generative model (a PixelCNN with an MCGSM likelihood). This model provides a direct, differentiable estimate of the log-likelihood of the generated images, which is used as the optimization objective.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a strong and principled theoretical framework by recasting the perceptual super-resolution problem as amortized MAP inference. This provides a solid justification for using generative approaches over traditional pixel-wise losses.
2.  The proposed affine projection layer is a novel and technically sound architectural contribution. It elegantly transforms a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one, strictly enforces data consistency, and is shown to improve performance even in a standard MSE-based setting.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The decision is based on the following assessment of the paper's clarity, technical soundness, and novelty.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and motivated. The introduction uses a simple 2D Swiss-roll example to provide a clear and intuitive explanation for the failure of MSE loss and the desirability of MAP inference. The theoretical derivations that connect amortized MAP inference to cross-entropy minimization via the proposed affine projection layer are presented logically and are easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are also clearly described.

**Technical Soundness:** The core theoretical claims are well-supported. The mathematical derivation in Section 3 appears correct. The affine projection layer is a sound method for enforcing the `x = Af(x)` constraint, and the numerical method for finding the pseudo-inverse `A+` is reasonable. The claims are backed by rigorous experiments. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) provides a clean, quantitative validation of the core hypothesis: the proposed GAN and Denoiser-Guided methods successfully minimize the cross-entropy and find posterior modes, unlike MSE/MAE. The ablation study in Section 5.2 successfully demonstrates that the affine projection layer is beneficial on its own, improving convergence speed and final performance for a standard MSE-trained network. While the Denoiser-Guided (AffDG) and Likelihood-Guided (AffLL) methods are shown to perform poorly on complex image data, the paper is transparent about these negative results. The success of the AffGAN variant is sufficient to validate the overall framework.

**Novelty:** The paper makes several novel contributions.
1.  **Affine Projection Layer:** While residual learning is common in SR, this specific formulation of an exact projection onto the affine subspace of consistent solutions is novel and powerful.
2.  **Principled Framework:** The paper provides a clear theoretical grounding for using GANs in SR, framing it as a method for amortized MAP inference. This moves beyond the more heuristic applications of adversarial losses in prior work.
3.  **Novel Methods:** The Denoiser-Guided (AffDG) approach, which backpropagates gradients estimated by a denoiser, is a highly creative and novel optimization technique, even if its empirical success was limited. The "instance noise" trick for GAN stabilization is also a useful practical contribution with a clear motivation.
4.  **Theoretical Insight:** The connection established in Appendix F between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortized variational inference is a valuable theoretical insight that links two major families of generative models.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

**Feedback:**
*   The failure of AffDG and AffLL on the grass texture dataset is a significant result. The discussion in Appendix E is insightful but brief. It would strengthen the paper to bring a more detailed analysis of *why* these methods fail into the main text. Is the issue primarily the difficulty of training accurate high-dimensional denoisers/density models, or is there a fundamental optimization challenge when using their gradients/likelihoods as a learning signal?
*   The discussion on the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6) is excellent and shows a mature understanding of the problem. This section adds significant value to the paper.
*   While the paper argues against PSNR/SSIM, these are still the primary quantitative metrics reported. Given the focus on perceptual quality, a small-scale user study or the inclusion of no-reference image quality assessment metrics (e.g., NIQE) could provide stronger quantitative evidence for the perceptual claims.

**Questions for the Authors:**
*   The pseudo-inverse upsampling kernel `A+` is learned numerically. How sensitive is the performance of the affine projection to the accuracy of this learned kernel? For standard downsampling operations like bicubic interpolation, is there an analytic form or a simpler way to derive a suitable `A+`?
*   The paper states that instance noise was critical for stabilizing training on ImageNet. Was this technique also necessary or beneficial for the CelebA and Grass experiments? Could you provide more detail on the annealing schedule used in practice?
*   Could you comment on the relative computational costs of the three proposed methods? Specifically, how does the pre-training time for the denoiser (AffDG) and density model (AffLL) compare to the end-to-end training time of AffGAN?
