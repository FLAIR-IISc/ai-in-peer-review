### Summary

This paper frames the task of single-image super-resolution (SR) as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that conventional methods based on minimizing pixel-wise error (MSE) lead to blurry results because they average over the posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution images. Instead, they propose to find the mode of this distribution (the MAP estimate), which should correspond to a more plausible and perceptually pleasing image.

The core of their contribution is twofold. First, they introduce an "affine projection layer" into a neural network architecture. This layer enforces a hard constraint that the downsampled output of the network must exactly match the low-resolution input, elegantly handling the likelihood term of the posterior. This transforms the problem into maximizing the prior probability of the generated high-resolution image. Second, since the true prior of natural images is unknown, they propose and investigate three distinct methods to approximate this objective: (1) AffGAN, which uses a Generative Adversarial Network to align the distribution of generated images with the real data distribution; (2) AffDG, which uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-prior; and (3) AffLL, which uses a pre-trained explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant) to score the generated images. The methods are evaluated on a toy dataset and several natural image datasets.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation Against State-of-the-Art:** The paper proposes a new framework for perceptual super-resolution but fails to compare its primary working method (AffGAN) against the most relevant and established baselines, such as SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016). Without this comparison, the claims of producing "perceptually superior" results are unsubstantiated. The evaluation relies on qualitative inspection and metrics (PSNR/SSIM) that are acknowledged to be misaligned with the paper's perceptual goals.

2.  **Failure of Proposed Alternative Methods:** Two of the three proposed methods for MAP inference, the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) approaches, are shown to produce blurry, unsatisfactory results on a simple texture dataset and are subsequently abandoned. This significant negative result undermines the credibility and robustness of the proposed MAP inference framework. If only the GAN-based variant is viable, the paper's contribution is reduced to an incremental modification of existing GAN-based SR methods rather than a successful new general framework.

### Supporting Arguments

The theoretical motivation for this work is compelling. Framing SR as MAP inference and introducing the affine projection layer to strictly enforce data consistency is a methodologically clean and interesting idea. The experiment in Section 5.2, which shows that this projection layer can even improve standard MSE-based training, is a valuable finding.

However, the paper's overall claims are not supported by the necessary empirical rigor.

**Lack of Critical Baselines:** The central claim is that the proposed MAP inference approach yields perceptually superior results. At the time of this work, the leading method in this domain was SRGAN, which also uses a GAN to improve perceptual quality. SRGAN combines an adversarial loss with a "perceptual loss" based on VGG feature distances. AffGAN is conceptually similar but replaces the perceptual and pixel-wise losses with a hard consistency constraint. A direct, rigorous comparison between AffGAN and SRGAN is therefore essential to evaluate the paper's contribution. Is the affine projection superior to a combination of VGG and pixel losses? The paper does not provide the evidence to answer this. Simply comparing against MSE and a "SoftGAN" variant is not sufficient to position this work within the literature. Furthermore, claims of perceptual quality should be supported by appropriate metrics (e.g., MOS from a user study, or established no-reference metrics like NIQE), which are absent here.

**Instability of the Proposed Framework:** The paper introduces three methods to approximate the MAP objective, presenting them as parallel contributions. However, the empirical results show that AffDG and AffLL fail. The paper states, "The AffDG and AffLL models both produced blurry results which we where unable to improve upon," and relegates further discussion to an appendix. This is a critical weakness. It suggests that the theoretical connection between the MAP objective and its approximation via score-matching (denoising) or explicit density models is fragile and does not hold up in practice for this high-dimensional problem. If the general framework only works when instantiated with a GAN, it significantly narrows the scope and impact of the contribution. The paper then becomes a theoretical re-justification for using GANs in SR, which was already an established practice.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The affine projection layer appears to be a genuinely useful architectural component. The paper could be strengthened by focusing more on this contribution. I would suggest a more thorough investigation of this layer's effect when integrated into various existing SR architectures, including established methods like SRResNet/SRGAN. This could be a more impactful contribution than the MAP framework, whose practical utility remains questionable given the failure of two of its variants.
*   The discussion on the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is a thoughtful and honest critique. However, it also weakens the paper's central motivation. If the mode of a distribution can be "highly atypical" and not resemble a random sample, it begs the question of why MAP inference is the desired goal over, for instance, posterior sampling. This internal tension should be addressed more directly.
*   The connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference in Appendix F is theoretically interesting. However, as it is purely theoretical and detached from the empirical results of the main paper (which are deterministic), it feels like an afterthought. If this connection is important, it should be integrated more centrally and supported by experiments.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please justify the omission of a comparison to SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016)? This seems to be the most direct and relevant baseline for evaluating the performance of AffGAN.
2.  The AffDG and AffLL methods are presented as core contributions but fail in practice. Do you believe this is due to fundamental limitations of using score-matching or explicit likelihoods for this task, or were there specific optimization and implementation challenges? More insight into this failure would be crucial for assessing the viability of your proposed framework.
3.  Have you experimented with quantitative perceptual metrics to support your claims of visual improvement? Qualitative "side-by-side" comparisons can be subjective.
4.  The results in Figure 5 show a case where the model "dreams up" water instead of a snake. While you interpret this as generating a plausible image, it is semantically incorrect. Does this highlight a failure mode of prioritizing the image prior over the likelihood, even with the hard constraint? How often do such semantic errors occur?
