### **1. Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of single image super-resolution (SR), arguing that standard methods based on empirical risk minimization with MSE loss lead to perceptually poor, blurry results. The authors propose to reframe the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, with the goal of finding the most probable high-resolution (HR) image consistent with a given low-resolution (LR) input.

The core contributions are:
1.  A theoretical framework showing that amortised MAP inference for SR is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the true distribution of HR images, subject to the hard constraint that the model's output must downsample back to the LR input.
2.  A novel and principled architectural component, the "affine projection layer," which enforces this consistency constraint exactly. This layer decomposes the output into a base upsampling of the LR input and a residual component learned in the null-space of the downsampling operator.
3.  Three distinct methods for performing the cross-entropy minimization, since the true data distribution is unknown:
    a. **AffGAN:** An approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks, where the SR network acts as the generator. This method is shown to minimize the KL-divergence, which is closely related to the cross-entropy objective. A stabilization technique called "instance noise" is also introduced.
    b. **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder, which is used to estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data (the score function).
    c. **AffLL:** A method guided by a pre-trained explicit density model (PixelCNN with MCGSM), which is used to directly evaluate the log-likelihood of generated samples.
4.  Empirical validation on a toy dataset and several image datasets (textures, faces, natural images), demonstrating that the affine projection layer is beneficial on its own and that the AffGAN approach, in particular, produces sharp, perceptually convincing results that are superior to MSE-based methods.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that provides a novel and well-motivated theoretical perspective on super-resolution, supported by a clever technical contribution and convincing empirical results.

1.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The paper's main strength is its reframing of SR from a problem of minimizing a pixel-wise error to one of amortised MAP inference. The derivation that connects this goal to minimizing the cross-entropy between output and data distributions under a hard consistency constraint is insightful and provides a solid theoretical foundation for pursuing generative approaches to this problem.
2.  **Novel and Effective Architecture:** The proposed affine projection layer is an elegant solution to enforce the data consistency constraint `A*f(x)=x`. This is a significant improvement over common approaches that use soft penalties, which do not guarantee consistency. The experiments in Section 5.2 convincingly show that this architectural choice is not a restrictive prior but is actively helpful, improving convergence speed and final performance even when training with a standard MSE loss.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's claims are well-supported by its structure and experiments. The motivation to move beyond MSE is clearly established with the classic argument against averaging in multi-modal distributions, which is effectively illustrated by the 2D Swiss-roll example. This toy problem then serves as an excellent testbed to verify that the proposed AffGAN and AffDG methods do indeed approximate the MAP solution, in contrast to MSE/MAE baselines (Figure 1, Table 1).

The core technical contribution, the affine projection layer, is validated in isolation (Section 5.2), which is a mark of rigorous experimental design. This experiment demonstrates that the layer is a generally useful architectural component for SR.

The main empirical results for the proposed MAP inference methods show that the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) is highly effective, producing sharp and plausible results on challenging grass textures, faces, and general natural images. While the denoiser and density-guided methods were less successful, the success of AffGAN is sufficient to validate the overall framework. The paper also includes a thoughtful discussion on the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6), which shows a deep understanding of the problem space and adds to the paper's credibility. The theoretical connection drawn between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference in the appendix is a fascinating and valuable addition.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** The denoiser-guided (AffDG) and likelihood-guided (AffLL) methods are dismissed rather quickly after the grass texture experiment due to producing blurry results. The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion in the main text on the potential reasons for this failure. Is it due to the inherent difficulty of accurately estimating the score function or density in high-dimensional image space? Or is it more of an optimization problem? Expanding on the discussion in Appendix E would provide valuable lessons for the community.
*   **Ablation Study for Instance Noise:** "Instance noise" is introduced as a key technique for stabilizing GAN training, particularly for the ImageNet experiments. While the motivation in Appendix C is plausible, the paper would benefit from a direct empirical ablation study. Showing a comparison of training stability or final image quality with and without instance noise would make a much stronger case for its general utility.
*   **Positioning Relative to SRGAN:** The paper cites Ledig et al. (2016) (SRGAN), which is a highly influential work that also uses GANs for perceptual SR. A more explicit comparison would be very helpful. While AffGAN is founded on a more rigorous MAP inference framework with a hard consistency constraint, SRGAN combines adversarial loss with a VGG-based perceptual loss. A brief discussion of the methodological differences and a qualitative comparison of results would better position this paper's contribution within the literature.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

*   The objective function for the generator `L(G;D)` in Equation (10) appears to contain a typo in the second term. The derivation in Appendix A uses a different, albeit also non-standard, formulation in Equation (17). Could you please clarify the precise generator objective used in your experiments and correct the equations if necessary?
*   The connection to amortised variational inference via a stochastic AffGAN (Appendix F) is very interesting. Does this formulation empirically lead to more diverse outputs, potentially addressing the "atypicality of the mode" issue discussed in Section 5.6? Have you performed any preliminary experiments in this direction?
