### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a new framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) aimed at improving perceptual quality. The authors argue that conventional methods based on empirical risk minimization with MSE loss lead to overly smooth and perceptually poor results. They reframe the SR problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, which seeks to find the high-resolution (HR) image that is most probable under the posterior distribution given the low-resolution (LR) input.

The paper makes three primary contributions. First, it introduces a novel neural network component, the "affine projection layer," which architecturally enforces the consistency constraint that the downsampled output must exactly match the LR input. This is achieved by projecting the output of an arbitrary convolutional neural network (CNN) onto the affine subspace of all valid HR solutions. Second, the authors show that with this constraint enforced, the amortised MAP inference objective simplifies to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the true data distribution of HR images. This insight recasts the SR problem into a generative modeling task. Third, the paper proposes and evaluates three different methods to approximate this cross-entropy minimization:
1.  **AffGAN:** An approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) that minimizes the KL-divergence. A stabilization technique called "instance noise" is also introduced.
2.  **AffDG:** A method that uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data (the score function) and uses it to guide the SR network.
3.  **AffLL:** A method guided by the log-likelihood provided by a powerful pre-trained generative density model (a PixelCNN variant).

Through experiments on a 2D toy problem and on real-world image datasets (grass textures, faces, and ImageNet), the authors demonstrate that their proposed methods, particularly AffGAN, successfully perform MAP inference and produce sharp, perceptually plausible images that are superior to those from MSE-trained models.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Principled Framework:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for super-resolution by formalizing it as amortised MAP inference and introducing an affine projection layer to strictly enforce data consistency. This provides a clear and rigorous motivation for using generative models for this task, moving beyond more heuristic approaches.
2.  **Strong Technical Contributions and Empirical Support:** The affine projection layer is a significant and practical architectural innovation, shown to improve performance even in a standard MSE setting. The primary proposed method, AffGAN, is shown to produce compelling qualitative results on multiple challenging datasets, clearly demonstrating its ability to generate perceptually superior images compared to baseline methods.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The motivation is established from the outset with an intuitive 2D toy example that perfectly illustrates the failure mode of MSE loss and the conceptual advantage of MAP inference. The theoretical development in Section 3 is logical, well-paced, and easy to follow, successfully connecting the high-level goal of MAP inference to the concrete objective of minimizing cross-entropy via the affine projection.

**Technical Soundness and Novelty:**
*   The **affine projection layer** is a key novelty. It provides an elegant, hard-constrained alternative to the soft L1/L2 consistency penalties used in prior work. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the demonstration in Section 5.2 that this layer can accelerate training and improve final performance even for an MSE objective is a strong piece of evidence for its general utility.
*   The reformulation of constrained MAP inference into an unconstrained cross-entropy minimization problem (Eq. 9) is a powerful theoretical insight. It cleanly separates the problem into satisfying the likelihood (handled by the architecture) and matching the prior (handled by the generative training objective).
*   The three proposed training schemes (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) are all well-grounded in established principles from machine learning (GANs, score matching, density modeling). Applying them within this new constrained SR framework is novel. While only AffGAN proved successful on complex image data, the systematic exploration of these principled alternatives is a valuable contribution.
*   The proposed "instance noise" trick for stabilizing GAN training is well-motivated and provides a clearer theoretical justification than many other GAN stabilization heuristics.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed.
*   The Swiss-roll experiment provides a crucial "sanity check" in a low-dimensional setting where the MAP solution and cross-entropy can be computed, confirming that AffGAN and AffDG behave as theoretically predicted.
*   The experiments on diverse and challenging image datasets (textures, faces, natural images) effectively demonstrate the capabilities and limitations of the proposed methods. The qualitative results in Figures 3, 4, and 5 provide compelling evidence for the perceptual quality of the images generated by AffGAN.
*   The quantitative results, while showing lower PSNR/SSIM for AffGAN (as expected for perceptual methods), correctly highlight the near-perfect input-output consistency achieved by the affine projection models (Table 2).

### **4. Additional Feedback**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The practical failure of AffDG and AffLL on complex image datasets is a significant finding. While discussed in Appendix E, a brief summary of this result and potential reasons (e.g., difficulty of estimating scores or densities accurately in high dimensions) in the main text would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights for future research in this direction.
*   The connection to amortised variational inference established in Appendix F is a very interesting and non-obvious theoretical result. It provides a novel link between GANs and VAEs in this specific constrained setting. This could be highlighted more prominently, perhaps with a sentence in the main conclusion, as it is of potential interest to a broader audience beyond the SR community.
*   The discussion of instance noise in Appendix C is excellent. Given its apparent importance for training on ImageNet, it might merit a slightly more detailed treatment in the main body of the paper.

### **5. Questions for Authors**

*   Regarding the performance of AffDG and AffLL: Could you elaborate on why you believe the gradient estimates from the denoiser (AffDG) failed on complex textures? The theory suggests `f*(y) - y` should point towards higher-density regions. Does this approximation break down for high-dimensional data on a complex manifold, or is the issue primarily one of optimization?
*   The instance noise technique was critical for training on ImageNet. Was it also necessary for the CelebA or Grass texture experiments? The appendix mentions a heuristic annealing schedule that aims to keep the discriminator's loss constant. Could you provide more details on how this was implemented?
*   In Section 5.4, you note that AffGAN produces slightly sharper images than SoftGAN but with more high-frequency noise. Do you have an intuition for why enforcing the exact consistency constraint with the affine projection might lead to this specific artifact compared to using a soft penalty?
