### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel and principled approach to single image super-resolution (SR) aimed at improving perceptual quality. The authors argue that standard methods based on minimizing MSE loss result in blurry images because they effectively average over the manifold of plausible high-resolution solutions. To address this, they reframe SR as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, seeking the single most probable high-resolution image consistent with the low-resolution input.

The core contributions are threefold:
1.  An architectural innovation called the "affine projection layer," which is a parameter-free layer that can be added to any CNN to strictly enforce the constraint that the downsampled output matches the low-resolution input. This elegantly transforms the constrained MAP optimization problem into an unconstrained one.
2.  A reformulation of the MAP objective as minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the network's outputs and the true data distribution of high-resolution images. This shifts the problem from supervised learning to a generative modeling task.
3.  The exploration of three distinct methods to solve this generative task: (i) **AffGAN**, an approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks, which provides a theoretical justification for using GANs in SR; (ii) **AffDG**, a method guided by the gradients of the data log-density estimated from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder; and (iii) **AffLL**, a method guided by an explicit, pre-trained density model (a PixelCNN variant).

Through experiments on a toy dataset and several natural image datasets (textures, faces, ImageNet), the authors demonstrate that their framework, particularly the AffGAN variant, successfully produces sharp and perceptually convincing results that are superior to traditional MSE-based methods. They also introduce "instance noise" as a general technique for stabilizing GAN training.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision:** Accept

This is a well-written and thoughtful paper that makes significant contributions to the field of image super-resolution and, more broadly, to solving inverse problems with deep learning. The two primary reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **A Principled and Novel Framework:** The paper provides a clear and theoretically sound framework for super-resolution based on amortised MAP inference. The introduction of the affine projection layer is an elegant solution to enforce data consistency, moving beyond the ad-hoc soft penalty terms common in other works. This provides a strong theoretical foundation for using generative models for SR and clarifies the objective as matching the distribution of natural images.
2.  **Strong and insightful Empirical Investigation:** The paper not only proposes a new method but also thoroughly investigates different ways to realize it (GAN, denoiser, density model). The experiments are well-designed, starting with a simple 2D toy problem that perfectly illustrates the core concepts, and scaling up to challenging real-world image datasets. The success of AffGAN and the reported failure of the denoiser and density-guided methods provide valuable insights into the practical challenges and trade-offs of these different generative modeling paradigms for this task.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The motivation for moving beyond MSE is exceptionally well-articulated in the introduction, using the Swiss-roll toy problem as a powerful visual aid to explain why MAP inference is preferable to finding the mean (MSE) or median (MAE) of the posterior. The theoretical development in Section 3 is logical and easy to follow.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivations are correct. The architectural proposal of the affine projection layer is novel and practical. The connection made between the MAP objective and cross-entropy/KL-divergence minimization is a key insight that bridges the gap between inverse problems and generative modeling. The proposed "instance noise" trick for stabilizing GANs is also well-motivated by addressing the issue of non-overlapping supports between the generator and data distributions.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experiments convincingly support the paper's claims. The 2D experiment confirms that AffGAN and AffDG do approximate the MAP solution as intended. The MSE experiment (Sec 5.2) provides an important control, showing that the affine projection layer is not detrimental and can even be beneficial (faster training, better final score) in a standard setting. The qualitative results on textures, faces, and ImageNet clearly show that AffGAN generates images with significantly better perceptual quality than MSE-based methods, producing sharp details and plausible textures where MSE models produce blur. The snake-to-water example (Fig 5) is a particularly interesting case that highlights how the model prioritizes the natural image prior when the LR input is ambiguous.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** The paper notes that the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods produced blurry results and were not pursued further. This is an important negative result. The discussion in Appendix E is a good start, but expanding on this in the main text would be valuable. A deeper analysis of *why* these principled methods failed (e.g., difficulty in accurately estimating scores/densities in high dimensions, optimization instability) would provide a more complete picture and be very useful for the community.
*   **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:** The paper rightly critiques PSNR/SSIM. However, the quantitative evaluation relies solely on these metrics (Table 2), while the core claims of perceptual superiority are supported only qualitatively. Including a quantitative perceptual metric like LPIPS or even a small-scale user study would provide stronger, more objective evidence for the main claims.
*   **Prominence of "Instance Noise":** This technique appears to be critical for training on complex datasets like ImageNet. The explanation in Appendix C is excellent, but given its practical importance, a brief summary of the motivation and method in the main body (Section 3.2.1) would be beneficial.
*   **Connection to Variational Inference:** The derivation in Appendix F connecting a stochastic AffGAN to amortised variational inference is a fantastic theoretical result. This connection is significant and could be highlighted more prominently, perhaps with a sentence in the main introduction or conclusion, to ensure readers don't miss this important insight.

### Questions for the Authors

*   Could you elaborate on the failure of AffDG and AffLL? Do you believe these approaches are fundamentally flawed for this task, or could they be made to work with more powerful, state-of-the-art denoisers or density models?
*   How was the annealing schedule for instance noise determined? The appendix mentions a heuristic based on keeping the discriminator loss constant. Could you provide more detail on this and comment on the model's sensitivity to this schedule?
*   In Section 5.4, you observe that AffGAN produces slightly more high-frequency noise artifacts compared to the SoftGAN. Do you have an intuition for why enforcing the hard affine constraint might lead to this behavior?
*   The pseudo-inverse upsampling kernel `A+` is computed numerically. How sensitive is the final performance to the quality of this approximation? Does a small residual error in the projection `(I - A+A)` cause any practical issues?
