### **Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR), aiming to generate perceptually plausible high-resolution (HR) images rather than the overly smooth outputs typical of models trained with pixel-wise MSE loss. The authors propose to frame the SR task as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is to find the HR image that is most probable under the posterior distribution, given the low-resolution (LR) input.

The main contributions are:
1.  An architectural constraint, the "affine projection layer," which is designed to enforce consistency between the LR input and the downsampled HR output. This is achieved by projecting the network's output onto the affine subspace of all HR images that are consistent with the LR input.
2.  A theoretical reformulation showing that, with this affine projection in place, the amortised MAP objective is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the distribution of true HR images. This shifts the problem from supervised learning on paired data to a generative modeling task.
3.  Three distinct methods are proposed to approximate this cross-entropy minimization objective:
    *   **AffGAN:** A GAN-based approach where the generator incorporates the affine projection.
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by the gradients of the log-data-density, which are estimated using a pre-trained denoising autoencoder.
    *   **AffLL:** A method that directly uses a pre-trained, explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant) to provide a training signal.

The methods are evaluated on a toy 2D dataset and on SR tasks for grass textures, faces (CelebA), and natural images (ImageNet).

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The paper claims to produce perceptually superior results, but this claim is not substantiated through rigorous evaluation. The experiments lack comparisons to key, established baselines in perceptual super-resolution (e.g., SRGAN) and do not employ any quantitative perceptual metrics or user studies. Visual inspection alone is not sufficient to validate the claimed improvements.
2.  **Failure of Proposed Methods:** Two of the three core technical methods proposed (AffDG and AffLL) are shown to be ineffective for image SR, producing "blurry results" in the authors' own experiments. They are subsequently abandoned for the main image experiments. This significantly weakens the paper's overall contribution, as it suggests the proposed framework is not robust and only the GAN-based variant is viable.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's theoretical foundation is interesting, and the affine projection layer is a sound architectural idea. However, the work is let down by a lack of rigorous empirical evidence and the failure of a majority of its proposed methods.

**On Empirical Validation:** The standard for evaluating perceptual image generation models requires more than showing qualitative examples and reporting PSNR/SSIM, especially when the explicit goal is to move beyond such metrics. By the time of this work, methods like SRGAN had already established the use of GANs combined with perceptual feature losses for this task. A direct comparison to SRGAN is a critical omission. Without it, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed AffGAN offers any practical advantage over existing, well-established techniques. Furthermore, to support claims of perceptual quality, the authors should have reported no-reference quality scores (e.g., NIQE, BRISQUE) or, ideally, conducted a user study to compare their results against baselines like bicubic interpolation, an MSE-trained network, and SRGAN. The provided qualitative results for AffGAN show promise but also contain noticeable high-frequency artifacts, which are a common failure mode for GANs; a user study would be essential to determine if human observers prefer these results despite the artifacts.

**On the Proposed Framework:** The paper introduces a general framework for amortised MAP inference and proposes three distinct instantiations: AffGAN, AffDG, and AffLL. However, in Section 5.3, the AffDG and AffLL models are reported to produce blurry results on a relatively simple texture synthesis task and are not evaluated further. This is a major weakness. If two-thirds of the proposed methods do not work on real image data, it undermines the central claim that amortised MAP inference via cross-entropy minimization is a practical approach for super-resolution. The paper then pivots to focus solely on AffGAN, making the contribution much narrower than initially claimed. The failure of AffDG and AffLL suggests that accurately estimating gradients of the data log-density, a cornerstone of the proposed framework, is prohibitively difficult in practice for these approaches. This practical limitation is not sufficiently analyzed or discussed.

While the affine projection layer is a valuable contribution on its own and is shown to improve even standard MSE training, its primary role in this paper is to enable the MAP inference framework. The failure of AffDG and AffLL raises questions about whether this architectural element is sufficient to make the underlying optimization problems tractable.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The theoretical derivation connecting constrained MAP inference to cross-entropy minimization is clear and elegant. This is a strength of the paper. The affine projection layer is also a well-motivated and solid piece of engineering that could have impact beyond this specific application.
*   The paper would be significantly stronger if it were reframed to focus exclusively on the one working method, AffGAN. This would allow for a more thorough investigation, including the critical comparisons to state-of-the-art baselines and the inclusion of proper perceptual evaluation metrics mentioned above.
*   The discussion in Section 5.6 regarding the limitations of MAP inference is insightful and shows a good understanding of the potential pitfalls of the approach (e.g., dependency on representation, atypicality of the mode). This is a welcome addition that adds nuance to the work.
*   The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is intriguing but feels disconnected from the main paper, as it is not empirically tested. This could be a direction for future work but, in its current state, does not add substantial weight to this submission.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a quantitative and qualitative comparison of AffGAN against SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016) on a standard SR benchmark dataset (e.g., Set14, BSD100)? This would be the most direct way to situate your work relative to the established state-of-the-art in perceptual SR.
2.  Could you elaborate on the failure modes of the AffDG and AffLL methods? Was the problem primarily the instability of the optimization process, or were the gradient signals from the pre-trained denoiser and density models fundamentally too noisy or biased to guide the generator towards sharp, realistic images? A more detailed analysis would be very instructive.
3.  The paper introduces "instance noise" as a technique to stabilize GAN training. How does this method compare conceptually and empirically to other prominent stabilization techniques from that era, such as the use of the Wasserstein distance in WGANs?
