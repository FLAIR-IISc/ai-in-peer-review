This paper reframes the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR), moving away from the dominant empirical risk minimization paradigm with pixel-wise losses (e.g., MSE) towards a principled framework for amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that finding the most probable high-resolution (HR) image given a low-resolution (LR) input is a more suitable objective for achieving perceptual quality than finding the mean of all possible solutions. The core contribution is twofold. First, they introduce a novel "affine projection" layer, an architectural constraint that forces any network output to be perfectly consistent with the LR input, thereby exactly satisfying the likelihood term of the posterior. Second, with the likelihood handled, the problem elegantly reduces to maximizing the log-prior of the network's output, `log p(y)`. This transforms SR into an unsupervised learning problem that can be trained on unpaired HR images. The paper then explores three creative methods for optimizing this prior term: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a method guided by the gradients of a pre-trained denoiser (AffDG), and one guided by an explicit density model (AffLL).

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The work presents a bold and highly novel conceptualization of the super-resolution problem. The introduction of the affine projection layer is a particularly elegant and powerful idea that provides a hard, principled constraint where previous methods used soft penalties. This reframing of a classic regression problem into one of generative modeling is a significant contribution that opens up new and exciting research directions. While the empirical validation is not exhaustive and some of the proposed methods fall short, the strength and novelty of the core framework more than compensate for these limitations.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **A Novel and Principled Framework:** The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical formulation. The shift from minimizing a perceptual loss to performing amortised MAP inference is well-motivated and compelling. The decomposition of the MAP objective into likelihood and prior terms is standard, but the method for handling each is highly innovative. The affine projection `ΠAx` is a standout contribution. It directly embeds the physics of the degradation process into the network architecture, guaranteeing that `A f(x) = x`. This is a much cleaner and more rigorous approach than the commonly used soft L1/L2 consistency losses, as demonstrated by the experiments in Figure 2 and Table 1.

2.  **A New Direction for Super-Resolution:** By perfectly satisfying the likelihood constraint, the authors transform SR into a problem of matching the distribution of network outputs to the distribution of natural images. This effectively turns SR into an unsupervised, generative modeling task. This is a powerful shift, suggesting that SR models can be trained on vast corpora of high-resolution images without needing corresponding low-resolution pairs. The AffGAN method successfully demonstrates the viability of this approach, producing sharp and perceptually convincing results that are qualitatively superior to the blurry outputs of MSE-based methods.

3.  **Intellectual Depth and Honesty:** The paper demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem. The authors provide a thoughtful critique of their own MAP-based approach in Section 5.6, discussing the representation-dependence of the mode and its potential to be "atypical" compared to random samples. Furthermore, the appendix (Section F) draws a fascinating theoretical connection between a stochastic version of their model and amortised variational inference. This link between GANs and variational methods in the context of inverse problems is a significant insight and points towards rich avenues for future work.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Failure of Denoising and Density-Guided Methods:** The paper proposes three exciting methods for optimizing the prior, but only the GAN-based approach seems to work well for real images. The blurry results from AffDG and AffLL are disappointing, especially given the theoretical appeal of these methods. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough investigation into *why* these methods failed. Were the auxiliary denoiser and density models not powerful enough to capture the complex manifold of natural images? Or is there a fundamental optimization difficulty in backpropagating gradients from these models? The discussion in Appendix E is a good start, but a deeper analysis would be valuable.
*   **Instance Noise:** The proposed "instance noise" for stabilizing GAN training is an interesting idea. While the motivation provided in Appendix C is intuitive, the paper would benefit from a more direct empirical comparison to other contemporary stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, different GAN loss functions) on the specific SR tasks presented.
*   **The Nature of MAP Artifacts:** The AffGAN results, while sharp, exhibit some of the high-frequency, noise-like artifacts common in GAN-generated images. It would be interesting to hear the authors' thoughts on whether these are limitations of the GAN optimization procedure, or if they are an inherent feature of the MAP solution itself (i.e., the mode of the posterior is "spiky" and noisy).
*   **Sensitivity to Downsampling Kernel:** The affine projection requires knowledge of the downsampling operator `A` to compute its pseudo-inverse `A+`. How robust is the method if the assumed `A` during training does not perfectly match the true degradation kernel used to create the test images? This is a crucial question for real-world applicability.
*   **Stochastic Extension:** The connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is perhaps one of the most exciting parts of the paper. Have the authors experimented with this stochastic version of AffGAN? It seems like a very promising path to address the "atypical mode" problem by allowing for sampling from the posterior, which could lead to more diverse and potentially more realistic reconstructions.
