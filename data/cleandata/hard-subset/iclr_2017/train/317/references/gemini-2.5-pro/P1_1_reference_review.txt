This paper frames the problem of single image super-resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. It argues that standard pixel-wise losses like MSE fail because they produce the mean of the posterior distribution over plausible high-resolution (HR) images, which results in blurriness. Instead, the authors propose to find the mode of this posterior. The paper's primary contribution is a novel and principled framework to achieve this.

First, it introduces an "affine projection layer" that can be added to any SR network. This layer is designed to analytically enforce the physical constraint that the downsampled output of the SR network must exactly match the low-resolution (LR) input. This transforms the problem into an unconstrained one of maximizing the log-prior of the network's output, which is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of real HR images.

Second, the paper explores three distinct methods to solve this cross-entropy minimization problem without access to paired LR-HR data:
1.  **AffGAN:** An approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), with a modified objective shown to minimize the KL-divergence. A technique called "instance noise" is also introduced to stabilize training.
2.  **AffDG:** A method guided by the score function (`∇ log p(y)`) estimated from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder.
3.  **AffLL:** A method guided by the log-likelihood computed by a pre-trained explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant).

The paper empirically validates these ideas, showing on a toy problem that the proposed methods find the posterior mode, unlike MSE. It demonstrates that the affine projection layer is beneficial even for standard MSE training and then shows that on real image datasets, the AffGAN approach produces sharp, perceptually convincing results, while the AffDG and AffLL approaches struggle. An appendix further provides a compelling theoretical link between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that provides a strong, theoretically grounded framework for super-resolution and inverse problems in general. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The formulation of SR as amortised MAP inference combined with the elegant affine projection layer provides a novel and principled alternative to the ad-hoc loss engineering common in the field. This architectural contribution is significant and broadly applicable.
2.  The paper's claims are well-supported by a rigorous and well-designed set of experiments. The clear success of the AffGAN method, coupled with an honest reporting of the practical difficulties with the other two proposed methods, makes for a convincing and scientifically sound contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**
*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper starts with a clear motivation using a 2D toy problem that perfectly illustrates why MSE fails and MAP inference is desirable. The theoretical development from the MAP objective to the final cross-entropy minimization objective is logical and easy to follow.
*   **Novelty and Significance:** While GANs have been used for SR, this work provides a much deeper justification, framing them as a tool for MAP inference rather than just a "perceptual loss". The affine projection layer is a key innovation, solving the consistency problem exactly rather than with a soft penalty term. This is a more principled approach than seen in prior work. The proposed "instance noise" is also a useful and well-motivated trick for stabilizing GAN training. The theoretical connection to variational inference in Appendix F is a significant conceptual insight that bridges two major families of generative models.
*   **Empirical Rigor:** The experimental design is very strong. The authors wisely include a control experiment (Sec 5.2) to show that their proposed architectural change does not harm performance on standard metrics, which validates the projection layer as a general tool. The comparison of the three proposed methods on the grass texture dataset is a critical part of the paper, as it clearly demonstrates the practical superiority of the GAN-based approach over the denoiser and density-guided methods. The qualitative results on CelebA and ImageNet are compelling and effectively demonstrate the ability of AffGAN to generate sharp and plausible details.

**Additional Feedback and Questions:**
The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** The paper concludes that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results, with more details in Appendix E. This discussion could be strengthened and partially moved to the main text. It appears the gradient signal from the guidance models is not robust enough, especially when the SR network's output is far from the data manifold. Could you elaborate on whether the issue lies with the accuracy of the score/density estimation itself, or if the optimization landscape induced by these objectives is inherently difficult?
*   **Comparison to SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016):** This is a key piece of concurrent work. A more explicit comparison would be beneficial. SRGAN uses a combination of adversarial loss, a VGG-based perceptual loss, and a content (MSE/L1) loss. Your work presents a more "pure" approach, relying solely on matching the data distribution via the adversarial objective while enforcing content consistency with the affine projection. Emphasizing that your hard projection is a principled replacement for SRGAN's soft content loss would strengthen your paper's positioning.
*   **Instance Noise Schedule:** You mention that the variance of the instance noise was annealed during training. Could you provide more details on the specific schedule used? Appendix C mentions a heuristic of adapting the noise to keep the discriminator's loss constant. Was this heuristic used in the experiments? Details on this would be highly valuable for reproducibility.

I would also appreciate clarification on the following questions:
1.  The connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is based on a stochastic generator `f_theta(x, z)`. Did you experiment with this stochastic version of AffGAN? If so, did it successfully produce a diversity of plausible HR outputs for a single LR input, as the theory would suggest?
2.  For the AffDG method, you describe a process of loading DAE models trained with progressively smaller noise levels. Does this mean you train multiple DAEs, one for each noise level, and swap them out during the training of the SR generator? How sensitive was the final SR quality to this complex training procedure?
3.  Regarding the numerical estimation of the pseudo-inverse `A+`: you note that the exact `A+` may have an infinite-support kernel. How does the quality of the finite-kernel approximation affect the exactness of the projection? Is there a trade-off between the kernel size of `A+` and the resulting consistency error?
