### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper tackles the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR), aiming to produce perceptually convincing high-resolution (HR) images rather than optimizing for pixel-wise metrics like MSE. The authors argue that conventional MSE-based methods produce overly smooth results because they effectively average all possible HR solutions. As an alternative, they propose framing SR as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, seeking the single most probable HR image consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input.

The core contributions are:
1.  An "affine projection" layer, a novel architectural component that enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled output must exactly match the LR input. This is motivated by the linear nature of the downsampling operator.
2.  The theoretical insight that, with this constraint, the amortised MAP objective reduces to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated HR images and the distribution of true HR images. This reframes the problem as a generative modeling task.
3.  Three distinct methods for minimizing this cross-entropy objective:
    *   **AffGAN:** A GAN-based approach that uses an adversarial discriminator to match the output distribution to the real data distribution.
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoiser, which is used to estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data.
    *   **AffLL:** A method guided by a pre-trained explicit density model (PixelCNN-style), which directly provides a loss based on the log-likelihood of the output.

The authors demonstrate their methods on a toy 2D problem and on SR for textures, faces, and natural images, claiming that the GAN-based approach produces visually superior results compared to MSE baselines.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation of the Core Framework:** The paper presents a general framework for amortised MAP inference and proposes three methods to achieve it. However, two of these three methods (AffDG and AffLL) are reported to fail on a key task (grass textures), producing "blurry results" that led the authors to abandon them for further experiments. The failure of two-thirds of the proposed methods severely undermines the central claim that this MAP framework is a robust and effective approach to SR. The paper is left with only one working implementation (AffGAN), which makes the overall contribution feel more like an exploration of GANs for SR with a specific architectural constraint, rather than a successful demonstration of a new general principle.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Relevant State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The primary goal is to improve perceptual quality, moving beyond MSE. The experiments compare the proposed methods mainly against MSE-trained networks and "soft-constrained" versions of their own models. This is insufficient. At the time of this work, other methods also used generative models and perceptual losses for SR, most notably SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016), which is cited by the authors. A rigorous comparison against SRGAN or similar established perceptual SR methods is essential to properly situate the contributions of AffGAN. Without it, it is difficult to judge whether the proposed affine projection and specific GAN formulation offer a tangible improvement over existing techniques that also aim for perceptual realism.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The theoretical motivation for this work is elegant. The formulation of the SR problem as constrained MAP inference and its reduction to a cross-entropy minimization problem is well-reasoned and provides a solid foundation. The introduction of the affine projection layer is also a methodologically sound contribution, and the experiment in Figure 2, which shows its benefit even for standard MSE training, is convincing.

However, the paper's strengths are overshadowed by its empirical weaknesses. A framework is only as strong as its implementations. The fact that the denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches failed suggests that directly optimizing the proposed cross-entropy objective is extremely challenging. The paper does not provide a satisfactory explanation for these failures. Is the gradient from the denoiser too noisy? Is the density model not accurate enough to provide a useful learning signal? The lack of analysis here leaves the reader questioning the practicality of the entire MAP framework. It seems plausible that the success of AffGAN is due more to the implicit regularization and dynamics of adversarial training than to the explicit optimization of the stated objective.

Furthermore, the qualitative results for AffGAN, while sharper than MSE, exhibit characteristic GAN artifacts, such as high-frequency noise and color shifts (as noted in Section 5.4). The example of the snake being reconstructed as water (Section 5.5) highlights a critical failure mode of this generative approach: it can produce a plausible but semantically incorrect image. While the authors frame this as the model "dreaming up reasonable data," for many practical applications this would be an unacceptable error. These trade-offs are not sufficiently benchmarked against other perceptual SR methods that may be more robust.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**

The affine projection layer is a very interesting contribution in its own right. It provides a principled way to enforce data consistency. This idea could potentially be separated from the more speculative MAP inference framework and evaluated more thoroughly across different loss functions and network architectures. It might be a valuable tool for the community, independent of the success of the GAN-based training.

The discussion in Section 5.6 on the limitations of MAP inference is commendable for its honesty and insight. This critical perspective strengthens the paper, but it also reinforces the need for more rigorous empirical comparisons to alternative approaches, such as those based on sampling from the posterior.

To help clarify my assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the failure of the AffDG and AffLL models? Was the training unstable, or did it consistently converge to blurry solutions? This could provide crucial insight into the difficulty of optimizing the cross-entropy objective directly.
2.  How does AffGAN's performance compare directly to SRGAN on the same datasets? A side-by-side qualitative comparison and evaluation with a perceptual metric would be necessary to demonstrate a clear advance over the existing state of the art.
3.  The "instance noise" trick is presented as a key component for stabilizing AffGAN training. How sensitive are the results to the specific noise level and annealing schedule? Its robustness is critical for the method's reproducibility and general applicability.
