### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a principled framework for single image super-resolution (SR) by casting the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that standard MSE-based methods produce blurry results because they effectively average over a multimodal posterior of plausible high-resolution (HR) images. In contrast, a MAP estimate seeks a mode of this posterior, which corresponds to an HR image that is both consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input and has high probability under the natural image prior.

The core contributions are threefold:
1.  **A Novel Architecture:** An "affine projection layer" is introduced to enforce hard consistency between the SR output (`y_hat`) and the LR input (`x`), such that `A * y_hat = x`, where `A` is the downsampling operator. This is achieved by projecting the output of an arbitrary CNN onto the affine subspace of valid solutions. This elegantly separates the problem into satisfying the data likelihood constraint and modelling the natural image prior.
2.  **A Principled Objective:** By using the affine projection layer, the amortised MAP objective is shown to be equivalent to minimising the cross-entropy H[q_G, p_Y] between the distribution of generated images (q_G) and the true data distribution (p_Y). This reframes SR as a generative modelling task that does not require paired HR-LR data for training the prior component.
3.  **A Comparative Study of Methods:** The paper proposes and empirically evaluates three distinct methods to minimise this cross-entropy objective:
    *   **AffGAN:** An adversarial approach based on GANs that minimises the KL-divergence KL[q_G || p_Y].
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by the gradients of the log-data-density, which are estimated using a pre-trained denoising autoencoder.
    *   **AffLL:** A method guided by the log-likelihood of a pre-trained, explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant).

The paper also introduces "instance noise" as a technique for stabilising GAN training and provides a compelling theoretical connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference in the appendix.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that provides a clear and insightful theoretical framework for applying generative models to the ill-posed problem of super-resolution. The two primary reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Elegant Problem Formulation:** The introduction of the affine projection layer is a significant conceptual contribution. It moves beyond the common practice of using soft L1/L2 penalties for data consistency and provides a hard constraint that is architecturally enforced. This clarifies the problem by cleanly separating the enforcement of the likelihood term from the optimisation of the prior term, leading to the elegant cross-entropy minimisation objective.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Investigation:** The authors don't just propose a single method but rigorously explore three different, well-motivated strategies for optimising their objective. The experiments are well-designed, starting from a clear 2D toy problem that validates the core premise, and scaling up to challenging natural image datasets. The honest reporting on the limitations of the AffDG and AffLL methods on complex data strengthens the paper's conclusions and highlights the practical advantages of the adversarial approach in this high-dimensional setting.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's claims are well-supported by its theoretical development and empirical results. The formulation in Section 3, which progresses from the MAP objective (Eq. 2) to the unconstrained cross-entropy minimisation (Eq. 9), is logical and well-explained. The derivation connecting their specific GAN loss to KL[q_G || p_Y] in Appendix A is sound and provides a solid theoretical basis for the AffGAN method, distinguishing it from more heuristic applications of GANs to SR.

The experiments provide compelling evidence. The Swiss-roll toy problem (Fig. 1, Table 1) is an excellent illustration of the core thesis: AffGAN and AffDG successfully find the modes of the posterior, while MSE/MAE regress to the mean/median in low-density regions. The proof-of-concept experiment in Section 5.2, showing that the affine projection does not harm (and may even help) standard MSE training, is a crucial sanity check that validates the architectural choice.

The qualitative results on grass, faces, and ImageNet demonstrate that AffGAN produces perceptually sharp and plausible results that are qualitatively superior to MSE-based methods, even if they score lower on traditional metrics like PSNR/SSIM. The failure case of the snake being reconstructed as water is particularly insightful, as it supports the claim that the model is learning a strong natural image prior and "dreaming up" a plausible high-frequency interpretation of the ambiguous LR input.

Finally, the discussion in Section 5.6 and the connection to variational inference in Appendix F show a deep level of critical thinking and place the work in a broader context, elevating the paper beyond a simple application of existing techniques.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** The paper concludes that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results on complex images and abandons them. While the appendix offers some hypotheses (e.g., peaked densities, inaccurate gradients), this is a crucial result that deserves more prominent discussion in the main text. Is the failure due to the limited expressive power of current denoisers/density models, or is it an inherent optimisation difficulty? A deeper analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's comparative aspect.
*   **Comparison with Contemporary Work (e.g., SRGAN):** The paper cites Ledig et al. (2016), whose SRGAN is a highly relevant contemporary work. While this paper provides a more rigorous theoretical motivation (MAP inference vs. a perceptual loss), a more direct comparison would be beneficial. For instance, does the hard affine projection in AffGAN prevent certain artifacts (like the noted color drifting in SoftGAN) that might occur with SRGAN's soft consistency loss? Highlighting these differences would better position the paper's specific contributions.
*   **Impact of Instance Noise:** The instance noise trick is well-motivated in Appendix C. However, its empirical impact could be made clearer in the main paper. The text states it was used for the ImageNet experiments, but an ablation study showing training stability or final image quality with and without it would be a valuable addition.
*   **The "Atypical Mode" Problem:** The authors astutely point out in Section 5.6 that the mode of a high-dimensional distribution can be an atypical sample. This is a powerful critique of the entire MAP inference paradigm. They hint that the KL objective used by AffGAN might be preferable to the pure MAP objective due to its entropy term H[q_theta]. This is a fascinating point that could be expanded upon, as it suggests the GAN-based approach may have a built-in advantage over methods that would more purely seek the mode (like a perfected AffDG/AffLL).

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the failure modes of AffDG and AffLL? Was the performance of the underlying denoiser and density models state-of-the-art? Does this suggest that accurately estimating the score function or density for natural images at the required precision for this task is still an open problem?
2.  Your results for SoftGAN show it struggles more with consistency and color drift compared to AffGAN. Do you believe the architectural enforcement of consistency via the affine projection is the primary reason for AffGAN's superior stability and image quality?
3.  The connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is very exciting. Does this framework open the door to sampling from the posterior p(y|x) rather than just finding the mode? This could directly address the "atypical mode" criticism from Section 5.6 by allowing for the generation of multiple, diverse, and plausible HR solutions for a single LR input. Have you explored this possibility?
