### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper frames the problem of single image super-resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that conventional methods based on minimizing pixel-wise Mean Squared Error (MSE) produce blurry images because they average over the multimodal posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution (HR) solutions. In contrast, a MAP approach seeks the mode of this posterior, which by definition lies in a high-probability region of the natural image prior, leading to more perceptually plausible results.

The paper's main contributions are:
1.  **A Novel Architectural Constraint:** The authors introduce an "affine projection" layer that can be added to any SR network. This layer is designed to enforce the physical constraint that the down-sampled version of the network's HR output must exactly match the low-resolution (LR) input. This is achieved by projecting the output of an arbitrary CNN onto the affine subspace of valid solutions.
2.  **A Principled MAP Framework:** By using the affine projection layer, the problem of amortised MAP inference is shown to be equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the distribution of true HR images. This formulation shifts the training paradigm from supervised learning on paired LR-HR images to an unsupervised, generative modeling task that only requires samples from the true HR image distribution.
3.  **Three Methods for MAP Inference:** The paper proposes and evaluates three different methods to approximate the cross-entropy minimization objective:
    *   **AffGAN:** A variant of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) that uses the affine projected SR network as the generator. The paper provides a theoretical argument that its specific GAN objective minimizes the KL-divergence KL[q_G || p_Y]. A stabilization trick called "instance noise" is also introduced.
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder (DAE). It leverages the known connection that an optimal denoiser can estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data, which is then backpropagated to train the SR network.
    *   **AffLL:** A method guided by a pre-trained, explicit density model (a variant of PixelCNN). The SR network is trained to maximize the likelihood of its outputs under this learned density model.

The paper provides empirical validation on a 2D toy problem, as well as on natural image datasets (grass textures, faces, and ImageNet), demonstrating that the AffGAN approach produces perceptually sharp and plausible results.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and well-motivated theoretical framework that recasts perceptual super-resolution as amortised MAP inference. This provides a principled foundation for using generative models for this task, moving beyond purely heuristic approaches.
2.  The proposed affine projection layer is an elegant and effective architectural contribution that enforces a hard physical consistency constraint. The experiments demonstrate that this layer is not only theoretically sound but also practically beneficial, improving training and performance even for standard MSE-based models.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strength lies in its clarity, technical soundness, and the novelty of its core ideas.

**Clarity and Motivation:** The motivation for moving beyond MSE is clearly articulated using both established arguments and a highly effective 2D Swiss-roll toy example. This example visually and quantitatively demonstrates why MSE-based solutions can fall into low-probability regions of the data prior, whereas MAP inference correctly identifies the mode. The theoretical derivation that connects amortised MAP inference to cross-entropy minimization under the affine projection constraint is logical and easy to follow.

**Technical Soundness and Novelty:**
*   The core contribution, the **affine projection layer**, is a significant one. It formalizes the consistency requirement `x = A*y_hat` as a structural component of the network rather than a soft penalty in the loss function. Section 5.2 provides strong evidence for its utility, showing that it can accelerate training and improve final MSE/SSIM scores for a standard supervised task, while perfectly satisfying the consistency constraint (Figure 2c). This makes the layer a valuable contribution in its own right, independent of the MAP inference framework.
*   The **MAP framework** itself is a novel and compelling perspective on perceptual SR. It provides a solid theoretical justification for why a GAN-based approach (which aims to match distributions) is a suitable choice. The empirical results on the 2D toy problem (Section 5.1, Table 1) provide strong, quantifiable evidence that the proposed GAN and Denoiser-guided methods do indeed approximate the MAP objective far better than MSE or MAE.
*   The **AffGAN** method is shown to be effective on challenging real-world image datasets, producing qualitatively sharp and plausible results (Figures 3, 4, 5) that are superior to the blurry outputs of MSE-trained models. The introduction and motivation for "instance noise" as a GAN stabilization technique is also a valuable practical contribution.

While two of the three proposed methods (AffDG and AffLL) failed to produce compelling results on image textures, the paper is transparent about this limitation. The success of the AffGAN model, combined with the strength and generality of the underlying theoretical framework and the affine projection layer, is sufficient to warrant acceptance. The failure of the other methods highlights the practical difficulty of the problem but does not invalidate the core contributions.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of why the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods performed poorly on textures. The current explanation points to optimization challenges and model inaccuracies. It would be insightful to investigate if the gradients provided by these models are inherently noisy, biased, or otherwise ill-suited for this high-dimensional optimization landscape compared to the adversarial signal from a discriminator.
*   **Impact of Instance Noise:** The instance noise trick is presented as an important factor for stability, particularly in the ImageNet experiment. Its contribution could be made more concrete with a small ablation study, for instance, by showing training curves (e.g., discriminator loss or a metric of sample quality over time) with and without instance noise to empirically demonstrate its stabilizing effect.
*   **Details on A+ Estimation:** The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse A+ is found via numerical optimization. It would be helpful to include more details on this process. How sensitive are the final SR results to the precision of this A+ estimation? Is the optimization process stable and guaranteed to converge to a good solution?

### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you offer further intuition on the failure of the AffDG and AffLL models on the grass texture dataset? The appendix notes that the AffLL model achieved high log-likelihood values under the density model, suggesting the generator was successful in optimizing that objective. Does this imply that even a state-of-the-art density model like PixelCNN+MCGSM is not a sufficiently accurate proxy for the true natural image prior to guide MAP inference in this context?
2.  In Appendix F, you establish a compelling theoretical link between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference. Have you conducted any preliminary experiments with this stochastic generator? If so, does it produce a diverse set of plausible HR outputs for a single LR input, as one might expect from sampling from an approximate posterior?
