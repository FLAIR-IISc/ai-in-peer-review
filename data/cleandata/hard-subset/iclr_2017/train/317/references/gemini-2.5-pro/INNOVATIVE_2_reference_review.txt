**Summary of Contributions:**
This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single-image super-resolution (SR), reframing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue compellingly that traditional MSE-based methods are fundamentally flawed for tasks requiring high perceptual quality, as they average over a distribution of plausible solutions, leading to blurriness. Instead, they propose to find the mode of the posterior distribution.

The core contributions are threefold:
1.  A novel neural network architecture featuring an "affine projection layer". This is an elegant architectural constraint that analytically enforces consistency, ensuring that any high-resolution output, when downsampled, perfectly reconstructs the low-resolution input. This transforms the problem from a constrained optimization into one of matching the distribution of generated images to the distribution of real images.
2.  The formulation of the amortised MAP objective as minimizing the cross-entropy between the generated and real image distributions. This provides a clear, unsupervised-like learning signal that does not require paired HR-LR images.
3.  The exploration of three distinct and creative methods to approximate this cross-entropy minimization:
    a. **AffGAN:** A GAN-based approach, which provides a strong theoretical justification for recent successful uses of GANs in SR. The paper also introduces "instance noise" as a well-motivated technique to stabilize training.
    b. **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder, which leverages the theoretical connection between optimal denoising and the score function (gradient of the log-probability) of the data distribution.
    c. **AffLL:** A method guided by an explicit, state-of-the-art density model (a PixelCNN variant), which directly uses the model's log-likelihood as the optimization objective.

Finally, the appendix establishes a fascinating theoretical link between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

**Decision and Key Reasons:**
**Decision: Accept**

This paper is an excellent example of the kind of ambitious, conceptually-driven research that this conference should champion. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  **High Novelty and Principled Approach:** The paper's main strength is its fundamental rethinking of the super-resolution problem. Moving from empirical risk minimization to amortised MAP inference is a significant conceptual leap. The theoretical framework is clean, well-motivated, and provides a powerful new lens through which to view and tackle inverse problems in imaging.
2.  **Significant Architectural Innovation:** The affine projection layer is a brilliant and practical idea. It elegantly solves the consistency problem with a hard constraint, rather than a soft penalty. The experiments showing that this layer can even improve performance and training speed for standard MSE models (Figure 2) demonstrate its value as a standalone contribution that could be widely adopted.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision:**
The paper is bold in its exploration of three different paths to achieve its goal. While only the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) yielded perceptually impressive results on complex image datasets, this does not diminish the value of the work. On the contrary, the honest reporting on the limitations of the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and likelihood-guided (AffLL) methods is scientifically valuable. It highlights the practical difficulties of using score estimates or explicit density models for this type of optimization, which is an important finding in itself. The success of AffGAN provides a strong proof-of-concept for the overall MAP framework.

Furthermore, the paper is rich with valuable secondary contributions. The introduction and motivation for "instance noise" as a GAN stabilization technique is insightful and more principled than some ad-hoc "tricks". The theoretical connection drawn in the appendix between this framework and variational inference is profound, bridging two major families of generative models and opening up exciting avenues for future research.

I value the paper's ambition. Rather than presenting a minor tweak to an existing architecture for a 0.1 dB PSNR improvement, it proposes a completely new way of thinking about the problem. While the execution is not flawless (e.g., the failure of two methods, GAN artifacts), the core ideas are powerful, novel, and likely to be influential. This is precisely the type of work that pushes the field forward.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**
-   The failure of the AffDG and AffLL models is interesting and deserves more discussion in the main text. The appendix provides some clues, but a more prominent analysis of *why* these theoretically appealing methods struggled in practice would strengthen the paper. For instance, were the gradients from the denoiser too noisy? Did the density model create a "spiky" loss landscape that was difficult to optimize? A deeper dive here would be very instructive for the community.
-   The criticism of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is excellent and demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of the limitations. It might be beneficial to briefly foreshadow these points earlier in the introduction to provide a more nuanced motivation for the MAP objective from the outset.
-   The connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is a major result. While the appendix is the appropriate place for the full derivation, I would encourage the authors to highlight this connection more strongly in the main body of the paper (perhaps in the introduction or conclusion), as it significantly broadens the paper's impact.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  Could you provide more intuition on the failure modes of the AffDG and AffLL approaches? The appendix suggests the density model may not be accurate enough, but the reported log-likelihood seems strong. Could the issue be related to the optimization dynamics when backpropagating through a separately pre-trained, fixed model?
2.  The AffGAN results show impressive sharpness but also some high-frequency artifacts, which is common for GANs. Do you believe the instance noise trick, or perhaps the specific KL[qG‖pY] objective, contributes to these artifacts compared to other GAN variants?
3.  Regarding the affine projection, you learn the pseudo-inverse `A+` numerically. How sensitive is the final performance to the accuracy of this approximation? Does a small error in `A+` significantly impact the consistency guarantee or the training stability?
