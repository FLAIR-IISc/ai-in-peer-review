### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper frames the task of single image super-resolution (SR) as a problem of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The goal is to generate high-resolution (HR) images that are not only consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input but also have high probability under the prior distribution of natural images, thereby avoiding the blurry outputs typical of MSE-based methods. The core technical contributions are threefold. First, the authors introduce a novel affine projection layer that can be integrated into any SR network to enforce exact consistency between the downsampled output and the LR input. Second, they reformulate the amortised MAP objective as minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the true data distribution. Third, they propose and evaluate three distinct methods to approximate this objective: (1) AffGAN, an adversarial approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks; (2) AffDG, a method guided by the gradients of the log-data-density estimated from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder; and (3) AffLL, a method guided by a pre-trained explicit density model (a variant of PixelCNN). The methods are evaluated on a toy 2D problem and on several natural image datasets.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation Against State-of-the-Art:** The paper's primary successful method, AffGAN, is not compared against the most relevant and established baselines for perceptual super-resolution, such as SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016). Without this comparison, the practical contribution and performance of the proposed method in the context of existing literature are impossible to assess.
2.  **Failure of Proposed Core Methods:** Two of the three proposed methods for amortised MAP inference, AffDG and AffLL, are shown to perform poorly, yielding blurry results. As these methods represent more direct implementations of the paper's core theoretical proposal (optimizing the data log-likelihood), their failure casts significant doubt on the practical viability of the proposed MAP inference framework.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper is built on a theoretically appealing premise: shifting from empirical risk minimization to a more principled probabilistic framework of MAP inference. The proposed affine projection layer is an elegant architectural innovation for enforcing data consistency. However, the experimental support for the overall framework is weak and falls short of the standards required for publication at ICLR.

*   **Lack of Crucial Baselines:** The central claim is that the proposed methods yield perceptually superior results. While comparison to an MSE baseline is necessary, it is no longer sufficient in the field of perceptual SR. The work by Ledig et al. (SRGAN) established GANs as a powerful tool for this task and serves as the de-facto baseline. This paper cites SRGAN but provides no qualitative or quantitative comparison. This is a critical omission. It is unclear if AffGAN offers any advantage over existing, well-established adversarial techniques.

*   **Inconclusive Performance of the Main Method (AffGAN):** While AffGAN produces visually sharper images than the MSE baseline, the authors themselves note the presence of "high-frequency noise" (Sec 5.5), a common artifact in GAN-based models. The claims of perceptual improvement are based solely on visual inspection of a handful of selected images. No objective perceptual quality metrics (e.g., NIQE, Ma's score) or user studies (e.g., MOS) are provided to substantiate these claims. Furthermore, the benefit of the affine projection layer over a simple soft consistency penalty within the GAN framework is not convincingly demonstrated; the qualitative difference between AffGAN and SoftGAN in Figure 4 appears marginal.

*   **Failure of Denoising and Density-Guided Approaches:** The paper honestly reports that both the AffDG and AffLL methods failed to produce sharp results and were abandoned after initial experiments (Sec 5.3). This is a major negative result that fundamentally undermines the paper's narrative. The paper proposes amortised MAP inference as a general solution, but only the adversarial variant—which optimizes a KL-divergence, not strictly the posterior mode—shows any promise. This suggests that either the more direct MAP optimization approaches are technically flawed in their implementation or, more problematically, that the MAP objective itself is not suitable for generating high-quality images, a point the authors allude to in their critique (Sec 5.6). The paper does not provide a satisfactory investigation into the reasons for this failure.

In summary, the paper presents a novel framework but only provides convincing evidence for an architectural component (the affine projection layer). The main methodological proposal (amortised MAP inference) is not well-supported, as two of its three instantiations fail, and the one that succeeds (AffGAN) is not rigorously evaluated against established state-of-the-art methods.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The affine projection layer is a strong and well-motivated contribution. The analysis in Section 5.2, showing its benefits for MSE-based training, is solid. This component could be valuable in other contexts and is worth highlighting.
*   The discussion in Section 5.6 ("Criticism and Future Directions") is insightful and shows a commendable level of self-awareness regarding the limitations of MAP inference (e.g., representation dependence, atypicality of the mode). This discussion could be strengthened by linking it more directly to the empirical failure of the AffDG and AffLL models.
*   The connection to amortised variational inference presented in Appendix F is very interesting and potentially more promising than the MAP framework. Approximating the full posterior `p(y|x)` rather than just its mode is a more robust objective that naturally encourages diversity and could avoid the "atypical mode" problem. This could be a stronger foundation for a future submission.
*   To strengthen the paper, a thorough comparison of AffGAN with SRGAN is essential. This should include multiple datasets and be supported by both qualitative examples and quantitative perceptual metrics.

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you please justify the omission of a comparison against SRGAN, which appears to be the most direct and relevant prior work?
2.  The failure of AffDG and AffLL is a key result. Can you provide more insight into why these methods failed? For instance, did the gradients from the denoiser/density model prove too noisy or unstable for effective optimization? Was an extensive search over hyperparameters (e.g., noise schedules for the DAE) conducted?
3.  The main demonstrated benefit of the affine projection layer was in accelerating MSE training. In the adversarial setting, what is the clear perceptual advantage of AffGAN over SoftGAN? The visual results appear similar, with AffGAN perhaps being slightly sharper but also noisier.
4.  How was the pseudo-inverse `A+` computed for the downsampling kernels used in the experiments on real images? How robust is the method to inaccuracies in the estimation of `A+`?
