### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a principled probabilistic framework for single image super-resolution (SR), arguing that the goal should be amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than the standard empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise losses like MSE. The authors make several key contributions:

1.  **A Novel Architecture:** They introduce an "affine projection" layer into a CNN architecture. This layer is designed to mathematically enforce that any high-resolution (HR) output, when downsampled, is perfectly consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. This elegantly handles the likelihood term in the MAP objective, turning the problem into one of maximizing the prior probability of the generated image.

2.  **A Unified Training Objective:** By using the affine projection, the training objective for amortised MAP inference simplifies to minimizing the cross-entropy H[q_G, p_Y] between the distribution of generated images (q_G) and the distribution of real HR images (p_Y). This reframes SR as a generative modeling problem, removing the need for paired HR-LR data during the optimization of the prior term.

3.  **Three Methods for Optimization:** The paper proposes and evaluates three distinct methods for minimizing this cross-entropy objective:
    *   **AffGAN:** An adaptation of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) that uses the affine projected generator. They provide a derivation showing this setup minimizes the KL-divergence KL[q_G || p_Y]. They also introduce "instance noise" as a practical technique to stabilize GAN training.
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained Denoising Autoencoder (DAE). It leverages the principle that an optimal denoiser can estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data, which is then backpropagated through the SR network.
    *   **AffLL:** A method guided by a pre-trained, explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant). The SR network is trained by directly maximizing the log-likelihood of its outputs under this density model.

The paper provides strong empirical evidence, including a tractable 2D toy problem and experiments on textures, faces, and natural images, showing that the AffGAN approach produces perceptually superior results compared to MSE-based methods and the other proposed MAP inference techniques.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes both conceptual and practical contributions to the field of super-resolution. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The paper's core idea of framing perceptual SR as an amortised MAP inference problem, and the subsequent introduction of the affine projection layer to enforce the likelihood constraint, is both elegant and novel. It provides a solid theoretical grounding for why generative approaches like GANs are well-suited for this task, moving beyond the heuristic combination of adversarial and pixel-wise losses seen in some prior work.

2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The use of a 2D toy problem where the ground truth MAP solution is accessible provides a crucial sanity check for the proposed methods. The honest reporting of the failures of the AffDG and AffLL methods is a sign of scientific maturity and provides valuable insights into the practical difficulties of applying these gradient and density estimation techniques in high-dimensional spaces.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The conceptual framework is the paper's main strength. By decomposing the MAP objective (Eq. 4) and proposing the affine projection (Eq. 7) to satisfy the likelihood constraint, the authors neatly reduce the problem to maximizing the log-prior (Eq. 8), which is equivalent to minimizing cross-entropy (Eq. 9). This is a clean and compelling narrative that successfully connects the underdetermined inverse problem of SR to the machinery of generative modeling. The affine projection itself is a significant architectural contribution, and the control experiment in Section 5.2, which shows it can even slightly improve standard MSE training, convincingly demonstrates its value and benign nature.

The empirical results for the AffGAN method are impressive. The grass texture experiment (Fig. 3) is a particularly powerful demonstration, as this is a classic case where MSE-based methods fail completely by averaging high-frequency details into a blur. The AffGAN result is sharp and plausible. While the results on faces and natural images show some typical GAN artifacts (high-frequency noise), they are clearly sharper and more detailed than the MSE-based counterparts.

Furthermore, the paper contains several smaller but valuable contributions. The "instance noise" technique (Sec. 3.2.1, Appendix C) is a well-motivated and practically useful trick for stabilizing GAN training, and the discussion comparing it to label smoothing is insightful. The derivation in Appendix F connecting a stochastic version of AffGAN to amortised variational inference is a very interesting theoretical result that builds a bridge between two major families of generative models.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The discussion on the failure of AffDG and AffLL could be expanded slightly in the main text. While Appendix E provides details, a concise summary of the hypothesized reasons for failure (e.g., noisy gradient estimates from the denoiser, or insufficiently accurate density models for providing useful training signals) in Section 5.3 would strengthen the paper's narrative. It would highlight the practical superiority of adversarial training for this particular task.
*   The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is fascinating and seems very significant. While I understand it's presented as future work, it feels like a major theoretical insight. I would encourage the authors to consider highlighting this connection more prominently, perhaps even briefly in the introduction or conclusion, as it substantially broadens the paper's impact.
*   The quality of the figures is generally good, but in Figure 4 (CelebA), the perceptual difference between AffGAN and SoftGAN is subtle. Perhaps a zoomed-in region on a key feature like an eye or hair texture would make the claimed sharpness improvement of AffGAN more apparent.

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  The failure of the AffDG method is particularly interesting. The theory connecting denoising to score matching is well-established. Do you believe the failure is due to practical optimization issues, the specific architecture of the denoiser, or a more fundamental problem with the accuracy and variance of the log-probability gradient estimates in very high-dimensional image space?
2.  Regarding the affine projection layer: You calculate the pseudo-inverse `A+` numerically. How sensitive is the final SR performance to the precision of this pseudo-inverse calculation? Is a "good enough" approximation sufficient, or does the method's success rely on a highly accurate `A+`?
3.  The snake-to-water example in Figure 5 is a fantastic illustration of the model "hallucinating" a plausible but incorrect texture. This seems to be an inherent property of MAP-based generative SR. Do you have any thoughts on how one might control this behavior or trade-off plausibility with fidelity to the (unseen) ground truth in applications where factual correctness is critical?
