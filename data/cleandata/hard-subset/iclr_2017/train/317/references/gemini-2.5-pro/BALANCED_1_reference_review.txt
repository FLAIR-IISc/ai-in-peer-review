### 1. Summary

This paper frames the single-image super-resolution (SR) task as a problem of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that traditional MSE-based methods fail to produce perceptually realistic images because they average over the many possible high-resolution (HR) solutions, leading to blurry outputs. In contrast, a MAP approach seeks a single, high-probability solution under the natural image prior, which is more likely to be perceptually plausible.

The paper's main contributions are:
1.  A novel architectural component, the **affine projection layer**, which enforces that the network's output is always consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. This is achieved by projecting the output of a standard CNN onto the affine subspace of all HR images that downscale to the given LR input. This elegantly handles the likelihood term in the MAP objective.
2.  With this projection, the MAP objective reduces to maximizing the log-prior of the generated images, which is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the output distribution and the true data distribution.
3.  Three distinct methods are proposed to approximate this objective without access to the true data prior:
    *   **AffGAN:** A GAN-based approach where the SR network acts as the generator. An analysis shows this minimizes the KL-divergence, which is closely related to the cross-entropy objective.
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder, which leverages the connection between optimal denoising and the score (gradient of the log-probability) of the data distribution.
    *   **AffLL:** A method guided by a pre-trained explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant), which directly maximizes the likelihood of the generated images under the learned model.

The methods are evaluated on a toy 2D dataset and on natural image SR tasks. The results demonstrate that the affine projection is beneficial, and that the AffGAN approach produces sharp, perceptually compelling results that are qualitatively superior to MSE-based methods.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and principled framework for super-resolution based on amortised MAP inference. This provides a clear theoretical motivation for using generative approaches and moves beyond simple empirical risk minimization.
2.  The proposed affine projection layer is an elegant and powerful architectural contribution for solving inverse problems. It enforces hard physical constraints of the imaging model, which is a significant improvement over the more common soft-penalty approaches and is shown to improve performance even in a standard MSE setting.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core strengths lie in its clear theoretical formulation and the novelty of the affine projection layer. The derivation from the MAP objective in Eq. (2) to the unconstrained cross-entropy minimization in Eq. (9) is clean and insightful. This formulation provides a solid theoretical justification for why GANs are a suitable tool for perceptual super-resolution, something that was previously motivated more heuristically.

The affine projection layer is a standout contribution. It directly addresses the ill-posed nature of SR by restricting the solution space to only those images consistent with the input. The experiment in Section 5.2 provides convincing evidence of its benefits: faster training and better final MSE/SSIM scores, even for a simple MSE objective. This suggests the projection acts as a strong and useful architectural prior. This idea is general and could be fruitfully applied to other inverse problems like inpainting or deblurring where a linear forward model exists.

The empirical validation on the 2D Swiss-roll toy problem is excellent, providing a clear proof-of-concept where the ground truth is accessible. It convincingly shows that AffGAN and AffDG do indeed approximate the MAP solution, whereas MSE/MAE do not. The qualitative results for AffGAN on real images (grass, faces, ImageNet) are impressive, producing sharp details and plausible textures where MSE-based methods fail.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. The most significant is that two of the three proposed methods for optimizing the prior term—AffDG and AffLL—fail to produce good results on real images, yielding blurry outputs. The paper reports this honestly, but the analysis in Appendix E is brief and doesn't provide a deep investigation into the failure modes. This undermines the generality of the proposed framework, as it seems only the GAN-based approach is viable in practice for high-dimensional data. This raises the question of whether the failure is due to practical optimization difficulties or a more fundamental issue with using score/density estimates from imperfect models in this adversarial setting.

Furthermore, for a paper focused on perceptual quality, the quantitative evaluation is lacking. While PSNR and SSIM are reported (Table 2), the authors themselves correctly state that these metrics correlate poorly with human perception. The primary evidence for AffGAN's success is qualitative. The inclusion of modern perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID) or a small-scale user study would have made the claims about perceptual superiority much more rigorous and convincing.

Despite these weaknesses, the conceptual contributions are strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper introduces a new and valuable way of thinking about the super-resolution problem, and the affine projection layer is a concrete, useful, and generalizable technical innovation.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not primary reasons for the decision.
*   **Investigating Failure Modes:** A more thorough analysis of why AffDG and AffLL fail would be a valuable contribution. Is the issue the accuracy of the gradient/density estimates from the denoiser/PixelCNN, or is it an optimization landscape problem? For example, are the gradients from these models too noisy or biased to be useful for the generator? Exploring this could lead to important insights about the limitations of such methods.
*   **Perceptual Metrics:** To strengthen the empirical claims, I highly recommend adding quantitative results using metrics designed for perceptual similarity, such as LPIPS. This would provide stronger, more objective evidence to support the qualitative examples.
*   **Instance Noise:** The instance noise trick is interesting. A more detailed ablation study on its effect and the sensitivity to the annealing schedule would be beneficial. It would also be helpful to more clearly differentiate the contribution from contemporaneous work (Arjovsky & Bottou, 2017) on stabilizing GANs.
*   **Connection to Variational Inference:** The connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference, detailed in Appendix F, is a very interesting theoretical result. This could be highlighted more prominently in the main paper, as it connects two major families of generative models and further strengthens the paper's theoretical grounding.

### 5. Questions for Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:
1.  Could you provide more intuition or analysis on why the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) approaches, which work on the toy problem, fail on complex image data? Is this a fundamental limitation of using imperfect score/density functions for optimization in high dimensions, or do you believe it could be solved with better-scaled denoisers/density models and more careful tuning?
2.  Given that the central claim is improved perceptual quality, have you considered evaluating your models with established perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) or conducting a user study? Such results would provide much stronger quantitative support for the visual examples shown.
3.  Regarding the "instance noise" trick used to stabilize AffGAN training: How sensitive is the final model's performance to the specific annealing schedule of the noise variance? Is the proposed heuristic of keeping the discriminator's loss constant robust across different datasets?
