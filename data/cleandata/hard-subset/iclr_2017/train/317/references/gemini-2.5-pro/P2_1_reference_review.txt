### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper frames the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The central argument is that traditional Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss produces blurry images by averaging over a multimodal posterior of plausible high-resolution (HR) solutions. In contrast, MAP inference seeks the mode of this posterior, which should correspond to a more plausible and perceptually sharp image.

The authors make several key contributions:
1.  **A Principled Framework:** They decompose the MAP objective into a likelihood term and a prior term. The likelihood term, which enforces consistency between the downsampled HR output and the low-resolution (LR) input, is handled with a novel architectural component: an **affine projection layer**. This layer projects any network output onto the affine subspace of valid HR images consistent with the LR input, elegantly turning a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one.
2.  **Methods for Prior Optimisation:** With the likelihood handled, the problem reduces to maximising the log-probability of the output under the unknown prior distribution of natural images (`pY`). The paper proposes and experimentally evaluates three distinct methods to achieve this:
    *   **AffGAN:** Using a Generative Adversarial Network where the generator is the SR network with the affine projection. This is shown to approximate the minimisation of KL divergence `KL[qG||pY]`.
    *   **AffDG:** Using a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-prior (`∇ log pY`) and using this gradient to train the SR network.
    *   **AffLL:** Using a powerful, pre-trained explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant) to directly provide the log-likelihood of the SR network's output as a training objective.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The methods are validated first on a 2D toy problem to demonstrate that they successfully find the posterior mode, unlike MSE. They are then tested on real-world image datasets (grass textures, faces, ImageNet), where the AffGAN approach is shown to produce perceptually superior and sharper results compared to both MSE and the other proposed MAP methods (AffDG, AffLL). The paper also includes a practical contribution for stabilising GAN training called "instance noise".

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and well-motivated conceptual framework for super-resolution. The two primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  The formulation of SR as amortised MAP inference, combined with the elegant **affine projection layer**, provides a principled and powerful alternative to the common practice of using soft penalties for LR consistency. This architectural contribution is novel and shown to be effective even in a standard MSE setting.
2.  The paper conducts a rigorous and insightful comparative study of three different, well-motivated approaches (GAN, denoiser, density model) for optimising the natural image prior. The finding that the adversarial approach (AffGAN) succeeds where the others produce blurry results is a valuable contribution to the community, providing both a successful method and important negative results that guide future research.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's core strength lies in its clear theoretical motivation. The critique of MSE loss is standard, but the proposed solution is not merely another perceptual loss function. Instead, it re-frames the problem entirely.

The affine projection layer is a key innovation. It enforces the constraint `A*y_hat = x` by construction, rather than by a soft penalty. This is theoretically more satisfying and, as shown in Section 5.2, empirically beneficial. The experiment showing that an MSE-trained network with this projection trains faster and achieves a better final MSE/SSIM score is a compelling proof of concept that validates the architecture's utility independent of the MAP objective.

The exploration of three different methods to approximate the prior is a major strength. It demonstrates a thorough investigation of the problem. While AffDG and AffLL ultimately fail to produce sharp images on complex textures, this is an important scientific finding. The authors honestly report these results and provide plausible hypotheses for their failure (e.g., inaccurate gradients from the denoiser or a density model that is not precise enough). This strengthens their conclusion that the implicit density modelling of GANs is, at present, the most effective approach for this task. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a clear and intuitive validation of the core hypothesis before moving to the much harder high-dimensional image domain.

Finally, the paper is well-situated in the literature, provides strong theoretical grounding for its methods (e.g., the derivation showing AffGAN minimises KL-divergence and the connection to variational inference in Appendix F), and introduces a useful practical trick (instance noise) for stabilising GAN training. The qualitative results on CelebA and ImageNet clearly demonstrate that the proposed AffGAN method produces sharp and plausible details, even if sometimes generating plausible but factually incorrect textures (the "snake to water" example), which is perfectly in line with the MAP inference objective.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

1.  **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** The paper concludes that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results on grass textures and then abandons them for subsequent experiments. While the discussion in Appendix E is helpful, the main text could benefit from a more in-depth analysis. Is the failure due to an optimization challenge (e.g., vanishing/exploding gradients from the pre-trained models) or a more fundamental limitation in using explicit gradient/density information for this high-dimensional generative task? A deeper investigation could provide very valuable insights.
2.  **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:** The evaluation relies heavily on visual inspection, which is appropriate for a paper focused on perceptual quality. However, the claims could be further substantiated by including no-reference image quality metrics (e.g., NIQE, BRISQUE) which are designed to assess perceptual quality without a ground truth image. This would provide a quantitative complement to the qualitative results.
3.  **Ablation Study on Instance Noise:** Instance noise is proposed as a method for stabilising GAN training. The paper would be more compelling if it included a direct ablation study (e.g., AffGAN training curves and final image quality with and without instance noise) to clearly demonstrate its effect on stability and performance.

### **Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate further on the failure modes of AffDG and AffLL? You suggest the density model might not provide an "accurate enough representation of pY". Does this imply that with a theoretically perfect density model, AffLL should work, and the failure is purely a limitation of current density models? Or is there a more fundamental optimization issue when backpropagating through such models?
2.  The affine projection relies on numerically estimating the pseudo-inverse `A+`. How sensitive are the results to the quality of this approximation? Is there a performance drop-off if the reconstruction error of the `A+` optimisation is higher?
3.  In your ImageNet experiments, you mention being "unable to stably train models" without instance noise. Did you compare its effectiveness directly against other common GAN stabilisation techniques like one-sided label smoothing or spectral normalisation on your specific SR task?
