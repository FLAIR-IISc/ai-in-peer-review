### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that traditional methods based on minimising pixel-wise MSE loss produce blurry images because they are incentivised to output the conditional mean of all possible high-resolution (HR) solutions, which often lies in a low-probability region of the natural image manifold. In contrast, a MAP estimate finds the mode of the posterior distribution, which is by definition a high-probability, and thus more plausible, solution.

The core contributions are:
1.  **An Affine Projection Layer:** A specific neural network architecture is proposed that enforces consistency between the low-resolution (LR) input and the downsampled HR output. This is achieved by projecting the network's output onto the affine subspace of all valid HR solutions. This elegant architectural constraint transforms the problem into an unconstrained optimization of matching the marginal distribution of generated HR images to the true data distribution.
2.  **Three Methods for Amortised MAP:** The paper presents and evaluates three different methods to approximate the intractable MAP objective, which is equivalent to minimising the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the true data distribution:
    *   **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) variant is used to minimise the KL-divergence `KL[q_G || p_Y]`. The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for its GAN objective and introduces "instance noise" as a technique to stabilise training.
    *   **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided method that leverages the connection between optimal denoising and the score function (the gradient of the log-data-density). The gradient estimates from a pre-trained denoiser are backpropagated through the SR network.
    *   **AffLL:** A density-guided approach where a state-of-the-art density model (a PixelCNN variant) is first trained on HR images, and its log-likelihood is then used as the objective to train the SR network.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** Experiments on a 2D toy problem confirm that the proposed methods (especially AffGAN and AffDG) successfully approximate the true MAP solution. On real image datasets (grass textures, CelebA faces, ImageNet), the AffGAN model is shown to produce perceptually sharp and plausible results that are qualitatively superior to MSE-based methods, particularly on challenging textures.

### **Decision and Justification**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a principled and novel perspective on image super-resolution. The core ideas of framing SR as amortised MAP inference and introducing the affine projection layer are elegant and well-motivated. The paper is thorough in its exploration of different ways to achieve this goal, providing solid theoretical grounding and insightful experiments. While not all proposed methods were successful on real images, the success of the AffGAN approach, combined with the strength of the overall framework, constitutes a significant contribution to the field.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Framework:** Moving from empirical risk minimisation (with flawed perceptual losses like MSE) to a probabilistic inference framework (amortised MAP) is a significant conceptual shift. The paper provides a clear and compelling argument for why this is desirable for improving perceptual quality.
2.  **Elegant Technical Contribution:** The affine projection layer is a clever architectural solution to the consistency problem in SR. It cleanly separates the problem of satisfying the LR constraint from the problem of learning the natural image prior, turning a constrained optimization into an unconstrained one focused on generative modelling.
3.  **Comprehensive Exploration:** The authors propose and test three distinct and well-motivated strategies (adversarial, score-matching via denoising, and likelihood-based) to tackle the core problem. This comprehensive approach strengthens the paper, even though only the GAN-based method proved fully effective in practice.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear theoretical development in Section 3. The derivation that amortised MAP inference under the consistency constraint is equivalent to minimising the cross-entropy `H[q_theta, p_Y]` is clean and provides a solid foundation for the rest of the work. This reframes SR as a generative modelling task, which neatly explains why GANs have been empirically successful in this domain.

The affine projection layer is a standout contribution. The proof-of-concept experiment in Section 5.2, showing that this layer can actually improve MSE/SSIM performance and training speed compared to unconstrained networks, is convincing evidence that this architectural constraint is not just theoretically sound but practically beneficial.

The empirical results for AffGAN are impressive. The grass texture example (Figure 3) is a powerful demonstration of the failure of MSE and the success of the proposed method in a regime where multiple valid HR solutions exist. The qualitative results on faces and natural images further support the claim that the method produces sharp, plausible images, even if it sometimes hallucinates details (e.g., snake into water), which is an expected and interesting outcome of a prior-driven method.

The main weakness is the reported failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods on the grass texture dataset. The paper states they produced "blurry results" and moves on. This is a significant negative result that deserved more analysis. It raises the question of whether the overall "amortised MAP" framework is sound, or if the paper is primarily a success story for a specific GAN-based approach. While the success of AffGAN is valuable, a deeper discussion on why the other principled methods failed would make the paper's conclusions more robust. Nevertheless, the intellectual honesty in Section 5.6, which critically discusses the fundamental limitations of MAP inference itself (e.g., dependence on representation, atypicality of the mode), is commendable and adds significant value.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** It would be very insightful to include a more detailed discussion on why the denoiser and density-guided methods failed on complex image data. Was the pre-trained denoiser/density model not powerful enough? Or is the loss landscape induced by these gradient/likelihood estimates simply too difficult to optimise for a generator network? Showing some visualisations of the gradient fields from the denoiser might be helpful.
*   **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:** While PSNR/SSIM are correctly identified as poor correlates of human perception, the paper would benefit from including more modern quantitative metrics designed for perceptual quality, such as LPIPS or FID scores. This would provide quantitative support for the qualitative improvements shown in the figures.
*   **Sensitivity to Downsampling Kernel:** The affine projection requires perfect knowledge of the downsampling operator `A`. In many real-world applications, this kernel is unknown. How robust is the method to a mismatch between the `A` used in the network and the true degradation kernel? Some analysis of this would improve the practical relevance of the work.
*   **Instance Noise:** The instance noise trick is interesting. How was the annealing schedule determined? Is it crucial for good performance, or does a constant, small amount of noise also provide sufficient stabilisation?
*   **Connection to Variational Inference:** The appendix establishes a fascinating connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference. This suggests that instead of finding a single MAP point estimate, one could sample from the approximate posterior `q(y|x)`. Did you run any experiments with this stochastic version? It seems like this could address the "atypicality of the mode" criticism from Section 5.6 and might produce even more realistic and diverse results. This feels like a very promising direction for future work that could be briefly explored or highlighted more.
