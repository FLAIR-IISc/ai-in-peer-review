**Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a principled approach to single image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The core argument is that traditional MSE-based methods produce blurry results by averaging plausible solutions, whereas MAP inference seeks a single, high-probability solution under the natural image prior, leading to more perceptually realistic outputs. The main technical contribution is an "affine projection" layer, a specific network architecture that enforces the constraint that the downsampled output must exactly match the low-resolution input. This elegant constraint transforms the SR problem into an unconstrained task of matching the distribution of generated HR images to the true data distribution. The authors propose three distinct methods to achieve this: 1) AffGAN, an adversarial approach based on GANs; 2) AffDG, a method guided by gradients from a pre-trained denoising autoencoder; and 3) AffLL, a method guided by the log-likelihood of a pre-trained explicit density model. The paper empirically shows that the AffGAN approach produces sharp and plausible results, outperforming other variants.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Reject**

My recommendation is to reject this paper, primarily for two reasons:

1.  **Lack of Professional Polish and Rigor:** The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors, typographical mistakes, and inconsistencies. The bibliography, in particular, is unusable in its current state. This pervasive lack of attention to detail severely undermines my confidence in the technical rigor and correctness of the work.
2.  **Incomplete and Unconvincing Empirical Evaluation:** Two of the three proposed methods (AffDG and AffLL) are demonstrated to fail on a single texture dataset and are subsequently abandoned without sufficient analysis. The evaluation of the one successful method, AffGAN, is conducted against a weak baseline from an unpublished work, omitting a crucial comparison to the well-established state-of-the-art (e.g., SRGAN), which makes it impossible to assess the practical significance of the proposed contributions.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

**Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
A manuscript submitted to a premier conference like ICLR is expected to be polished and meticulously prepared. This paper falls far short of that standard.

*   **Bibliography:** The reference section is deeply flawed. It is littered with placeholder characters (`?`), formatting tags (`\Q`, `\E`), and malformed entries (e.g., the final entry for "Salimans, 2015" is nonsensical). This makes it difficult to verify citations and suggests a rushed preparation.
*   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** Multiple errors are present throughout the text, such as "we where unable" (Sec 5.3), "and and variational inference" (Appendix F), and an incomplete sentence structure in the introduction (`However the ∗Work done... shown ( ).`).
*   **Inconsistent Notation and Formatting:** In Equation 7, the affine subspace is defined as `{y : yA = x}`, which implies row vectors, contradicting the `x = Ay` notation used elsewhere, which assumes column vectors. In Section 3.2, Equation 10 presents two conflicting definitions for `L(G;D)`. The caption for Figure 1b refers to legend symbols `( )` that are missing in the text. These inconsistencies create confusion and betray a lack of careful proofreading.

While these may seem like minor points individually, their sheer number suggests a lack of diligence that is concerning for a research paper.

**Weaknesses in Methodology and Evaluation:**
The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by the experiments presented.

*   **Failure of AffDG and AffLL:** The paper introduces three methods for amortised MAP inference, but two of them are quickly dismissed after the grass texture experiment for producing "blurry results". The explanation provided in Appendix E is superficial, blaming an "inaccurate representation of pY" by a state-of-the-art density model (PixelCNN/MCGSM). This is a significant negative result that warrants a much deeper investigation in the main paper. Why do these principled, gradient-based methods fail? Is there a fundamental flaw in using denoiser or density model gradients for this task? As it stands, the paper effectively presents one working method (AffGAN) while the other two feel like underdeveloped filler, weakening the overall contribution.
*   **Inadequate Baselines:** The primary baseline for comparing AffGAN is "SoftGAN," a model that uses a soft MAE constraint based on an unpublished manuscript. A far more relevant and stronger baseline would be SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016), which is cited but never compared against. SRGAN also uses a GAN loss but combines it with a perceptual loss in VGG feature space to maintain content fidelity. A direct comparison is essential to disentangle the benefits of the proposed hard affine projection from the benefits of using a GAN framework in general, and to see how it fares against a content loss that is not pixel-wise MSE.
*   **Subjective and Ambiguous Results:** The qualitative improvements of AffGAN over SoftGAN are described as "slightly sharper" but also with "slightly more high frequency noise" (Sec 5.4). The "snake-to-water" example (Sec 5.5) is presented as a positive ("dreams up reasonable data"), but it is fundamentally a failure of content reconstruction, highlighting a potential weakness of relying solely on the prior without a stronger fidelity term. The lack of objective perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) makes the evaluation highly subjective.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite my recommendation, I believe the core ideas in this paper have merit and could form the basis of a strong future submission.

*   The affine projection layer is an elegant architectural idea for enforcing consistency in inverse problems. It is well-motivated and potentially very useful.
*   The connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference, detailed in Appendix F, is a very interesting theoretical result that deserves more prominence and empirical validation.
*   I strongly urge the authors to perform a thorough proofreading and correction of the entire manuscript. The formatting and citation issues must be completely resolved.
*   The paper would be significantly strengthened by focusing on AffGAN and conducting a rigorous comparison against SRGAN and other state-of-the-art perceptual SR methods. This should include both qualitative examples and quantitative evaluation using modern perceptual metrics.
*   A deeper analysis of why the denoiser and density-guided approaches failed would be a valuable scientific contribution in its own right.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you clarify why the pseudo-inverse `A+` was estimated numerically (Appendix B) rather than computed directly, given that `A` is a known linear convolution operator? How robust is the performance of the affine projection to the accuracy of this numerical approximation?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed hypothesis for the failure of the AffDG and AffLL models? Given that these methods are based on state-of-the-art techniques for modeling data distributions, their poor performance is surprising and counter-intuitive. Does this suggest that accurate local gradients of the log-probability are insufficient or perhaps too sharp for guiding a generator network in this high-dimensional setting?
3.  The main difference between AffGAN and SRGAN seems to be the method for ensuring content fidelity (hard affine projection vs. soft VGG perceptual loss). Have you performed any experiments to directly compare these two approaches? It would be illuminating to understand the trade-offs between enforcing exact mathematical consistency versus perceptual feature-space consistency.
