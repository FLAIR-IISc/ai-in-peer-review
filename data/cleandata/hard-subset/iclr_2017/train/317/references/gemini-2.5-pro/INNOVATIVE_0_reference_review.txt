### Summary of Contributions

This paper reframes the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR), moving away from the standard empirical risk minimization framework with pixel-wise losses towards a more principled approach of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that finding the mode of the posterior distribution p(HR|LR) is a desirable objective for generating perceptually plausible high-resolution images. The key contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Architectural Constraint:** The paper introduces an "affine projection layer" that enforces the physical constraint that the down-sampled output must be identical to the low-resolution input. This is achieved by projecting the network's output onto the affine subspace of all valid solutions, which elegantly transforms a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one.

2.  **MAP Inference as Cross-Entropy Minimization:** By using the affine projection layer to handle the likelihood term in the MAP objective, the problem simplifies to maximizing the log-prior of the generated image. This is shown to be equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the true data distribution. This formulation recasts SR as a generative modeling task, removing the need for paired HR-LR data during the optimization of the prior term.

3.  **Exploration of Three Optimization Strategies:** The paper proposes and evaluates three distinct methods to minimize this cross-entropy objective:
    *   **AffGAN:** An approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks, where the discriminator implicitly models the data distribution.
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by a denoising autoencoder, which leverages the connection between optimal denoising and the score (gradient of the log-probability) of the data distribution.
    *   **AffLL:** A method that uses a pre-trained, explicit, and differentiable density model (a PixelCNN variant) to directly provide the optimization objective.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept due to its bold and highly novel reconceptualization of the super-resolution problem.

1.  The formulation of SR as amortised MAP inference provides a strong theoretical foundation for using generative models to improve perceptual quality, moving beyond ad-hoc applications of GANs.
2.  The affine projection layer is a powerful and elegant architectural contribution that directly embeds a core physical constraint of the problem into the model. This idea is significant and could be broadly applicable to other ill-posed inverse problems.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper lies in its conceptual novelty. While prior work has used GANs to improve perceptual quality in SR, this paper provides a principled motivation rooted in Bayesian inference. The derivation that starts from the MAP objective and, through the clever use of the affine projection, arrives at a cross-entropy minimization objective is compelling. This reframing is a significant contribution that brings clarity and theoretical grounding to the field.

The affine projection layer itself is a standout idea. Enforcing the consistency constraint `x = A * f(x)` through a soft penalty is a common but imperfect solution. By building this constraint directly into the network architecture via a projection to the null-space of the downsampling operator `A`, the authors present a far more elegant and rigorous solution. The empirical validation in Section 5.2, which shows that this layer does not harm—and may even improve—performance in a standard MSE setting, is a convincing proof of concept for this architectural innovation.

While only the AffGAN variant proved successful on complex natural images, the exploration of the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and likelihood-guided (AffLL) approaches is still valuable. The paper's candid reporting on their failure to produce sharp images is scientifically honest and, in itself, an interesting result. It highlights the immense practical difficulty of using explicit score or density-based models as objectives for high-dimensional image generation, suggesting that the implicit gradients provided by a GAN discriminator are currently more effective. I value the paper's ambition in exploring these different paths, even if not all were successful. The success of the framework on the 2D toy problem is a crucial piece of evidence that the theory is sound, and the challenges on real images are practical, not fundamental.

Finally, the connection established in the appendix between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference is a fascinating theoretical insight that links two major families of generative models and points towards exciting future work in posterior sampling for SR.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Failure Analysis of AffDG/AffLL:** The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion on the failure of the denoiser and density-guided methods. Is the primary issue the quality of the gradient estimates in high-dimensional space? For AffDG, how much does the practical implementation with finite noise deviate from the theory which assumes infinitesimal noise? For AffLL, could the discrete nature of the PixelCNN-style model, even with a continuous likelihood, result in a "bumpy" loss landscape that is difficult for a generator to navigate? A deeper analysis would provide valuable lessons for the community.
*   **Instance Noise:** The instance noise trick is a nice practical contribution. It would be helpful to connect it more explicitly to other GAN stabilization techniques in the main text. For instance, explaining how adding noise to inputs relates to changing the divergence measure being optimized (e.g., as in Wasserstein GANs which are robust to non-overlapping supports) would provide richer context.
*   **Highlighting the VI Connection:** The link to variational inference in Appendix F is a very powerful idea that feels somewhat buried. It suggests the framework can be extended beyond finding a single MAP estimate to full posterior sampling. I would recommend highlighting this potential more prominently in the conclusion or future work section, as it represents a very promising direction.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  How robust is the affine projection layer to inaccuracies in the estimated pseudo-inverse `A+`? Since `A+` is found numerically, what is the impact of a slightly imperfect pseudo-inverse on the enforcement of the consistency constraint and on final image quality?
2.  The AffGAN results, while sharp, exhibit some of the high-frequency, noisy artifacts common to GANs. Do you believe these artifacts are inherent to the adversarial objective, or could they be exacerbated by the affine projection, which forces the generator to dedicate its entire capacity to modeling information in the null-space of `A`?
3.  Regarding AffDG and AffLL, did you experiment with different training schedules, such as a more gradual annealing of the noise level for the denoiser or starting the generator from a pre-trained MSE solution? Do you believe these methods are fundamentally limited for this task, or could they be made to work with more extensive tuning?
