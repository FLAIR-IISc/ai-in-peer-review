**Summary**
This paper proposes a new framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that conventional MSE-based methods are flawed as they produce blurry images corresponding to the mean of the posterior distribution. Instead, they propose to find the mode of the posterior (the MAP estimate), which should correspond to a plausible, high-probability image. The core of their contribution is twofold. First, they introduce an "affine projection layer" architecture that strictly enforces the constraint that the down-sampled high-resolution (HR) output matches the low-resolution (LR) input. Second, by enforcing this constraint, they show that amortised MAP inference reduces to minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of true HR images. They then propose and evaluate three distinct methods to approximate this cross-entropy minimisation: 1) AffGAN, using a Generative Adversarial Network; 2) AffDG, using a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the score function (gradient of the log-prior); and 3) AffLL, using a pre-trained explicit density model (PixelCNN) to directly provide the log-prior. Experiments on a toy dataset and natural images show that the AffGAN approach can produce perceptually sharp and plausible results.

**Decision: Reject**
My recommendation is to reject this paper. While the core idea of framing super-resolution as amortised MAP inference with a hard constraint is elegant and well-motivated, the paper is undermined by two primary issues:

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Support:** Two of the three proposed methods for MAP inference (AffDG and AffLL) are shown to perform poorly on image data and are effectively abandoned halfway through the experiments. This significantly weakens the paper's central claim of providing a general and effective framework.
2.  **Lack of Technical and Editorial Rigor:** The paper contains critical technical inconsistencies, particularly regarding the GAN objective function, and is plagued by a substantial number of formatting and citation errors, which casts doubt on the overall quality and reliability of the work.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Failure of Proposed Methods:** The paper introduces three methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) as parallel approaches to the same goal of minimising cross-entropy. However, the results on grass textures (Section 5.3) show that both AffDG and AffLL produce "blurry results," and the authors state, "Due to these findings we choose not to perform any further experiments with these models." This is a significant negative result. A paper that proposes three methods but only finds one to be viable has not successfully demonstrated a robust framework. The discussion in Appendix E attributes the failure to imprecise gradients or inaccurate density models, but this does not excuse the fact that the contribution is effectively reduced to just the AffGAN model, which is a less general and impactful claim than the one the paper sets out to make.

2.  **Technical Inconsistencies in GAN Formulation:** There is a critical discrepancy in the definition of the GAN objective for the generator.
    *   In Section 3.2, the generator loss is stated as `L(G;D) = −Ez∼pZ log D(G(z))`.
    *   However, in Appendix A (Eq. 17), the objective to be maximised for the generator contains the term `log(Dψ(Gθ(z))) − log(1−Dψ(Gθ(z)))`, which is equivalent to `log(D/(1-D))`.
    *   The derivation that follows in Eq. 18-20 relies on this `log(D/(1-D))` formulation to show a connection to `KL[qG || pY]`.
    These are two different objectives that result in different gradient updates for the generator. This inconsistency makes it unclear which method was actually implemented and whether the theoretical claim of KL-divergence minimisation holds for the reported experiments. This is a serious flaw in the technical exposition.

3.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Errors:** The manuscript is unpolished to a degree that is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Citations:** The bibliography is filled with errors, such as `Alain and Bengio.,? \Q2014\E, Alain and Bengio.,` and `Salimans,? \Q2015\E, Salimans,`. This suggests a problem with the build process that was not caught or corrected. In-text citation formatting is also inconsistent (e.g., `(Dong et al., 2016; Shi et al., 2016)` vs. `Bruna et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Bruna et al\.,`).
    *   **Figure/Table Clarity:** The caption for Figure 1b is confusing; the plot symbols `( )`, `( )` etc. are not explicitly defined. The table header `H[qθ, pY ] `MSE(x,Ay)` is poorly formatted.
    *   **Typos and Grammar:** Minor errors are present throughout (e.g., "the AffGAN and AffDG achieves" in Table 1 caption). In Equation 10, two different lines are presented for `L(G;D)`, which is confusing.

These issues, while seemingly minor individually, collectively give the impression of a rushed and careless submission, which erodes confidence in the correctness of the more complex technical details.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The affine projection layer is a very interesting contribution. The results in Section 5.2, showing that it improves even standard MSE training, are strong. This idea could potentially be the focus of a more successful paper.
*   The "Criticism and Future Directions" section is excellent and shows a thoughtful consideration of the method's limitations. Similarly, the connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is insightful and a promising direction for future work.
*   I would strongly advise focusing the paper on the single successful method (AffGAN with affine projection) and providing a much more thorough analysis of its properties, failure modes (e.g., the high-frequency noise mentioned in Section 5.5), and a more rigorous comparison against other GAN-based SR methods like SRGAN. The failed methods (AffDG, AffLL) should be moved to an appendix or presented as negative results with a deeper analysis of why they failed.
*   The numerical estimation of the pseudo-inverse `A+` in Appendix B is practical, but it would strengthen the paper to discuss the conditions under which a true pseudo-inverse can be derived analytically (e.g., for specific kernels like box filters) and how the numerical approximation compares.

**Questions for Authors**
1.  Could you please clarify the exact generator loss function used in your GAN experiments? Was it `-log(D)` as stated in the main text, or `log(D/(1-D))` as used in the KL-divergence derivation in the appendix?
2.  Given the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods, do you believe the general framework of amortised MAP inference for SR is flawed, or do you see a clear path to making these other approaches competitive with AffGAN (e.g., with more advanced denoisers or density models)?
