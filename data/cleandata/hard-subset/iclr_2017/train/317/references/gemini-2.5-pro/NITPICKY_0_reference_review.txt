### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper recasts the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR) from the standard empirical risk minimisation framework to one of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The central argument is that MAP inference is better suited for producing perceptually plausible high-resolution (HR) images than minimising pixel-wise losses like MSE, which tend to average over the manifold of possible solutions and produce blurry results. The primary contributions are threefold:
1.  An "affine projection layer" is proposed as a novel architectural component. This layer is designed to enforce the hard constraint that the down-sampled version of any generated HR image must exactly match the low-resolution (LR) input. This elegantly handles the likelihood term in the MAP objective.
2.  By enforcing this constraint, the authors show that MAP inference reduces to minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the true distribution of natural HR images, framing SR as a generative modelling problem that does not require paired data.
3.  Three distinct methods are proposed to solve this cross-entropy minimisation problem: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a method guided by the score of the data distribution estimated by a denoiser (AffDG), and a method guided by a tractable density model (AffLL).

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a novel and theoretically appealing framework for super-resolution, my recommendation is to reject it in its current form. The decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper suffers from a significant number of severe formatting errors, inconsistencies, and typos that fundamentally undermine its clarity and perceived rigor.
2.  The empirical support for the proposed framework is weak, as two of the three core proposed methods (AffDG and AffLL) are shown to perform poorly on image data and are abruptly discarded, leaving only the GAN-based approach as viable.

**Supporting Arguments**

**1. Pervasive Formatting and Presentation Flaws:**
The manuscript is replete with errors that go far beyond minor typos and suggest a lack of careful preparation.
*   **Broken Text Flow:** The first paragraph of the introduction is interrupted by a misplaced footnote and an incomplete figure reference: "However the ∗Work done while CKS was an intern at Twitter shown ( ).". This is a critical formatting failure at the very beginning of the paper.
*   **Inconsistent and Incorrect Equations:** The objective function for the generator in the AffGAN model is presented inconsistently. Section 3.2 shows `L(G;D) = −Ez∼pZ log D(G(z)) -D(G(z))`, which appears to contain a typo (a division symbol seems missing) and an extra term. Appendix A (Eqn. 17), however, presents the objective as `log(D) - log(1-D)`, which correctly corresponds to minimising `KL[qG||pY]`. This inconsistency regarding a central equation of the proposed method is a major flaw.
*   **Garbled References:** The entire bibliography section is malformed. Entries appear as `Alain and Bengio.,? \Q2014\E, Alain and Bengio.,`, indicating a catastrophic BibTeX failure. A bizarre, non-standard entry `Salimans, Consider the following idealised GAN algorithm,, Salimans,? \Q2015\E, Salimans,` is also present. A manuscript with a completely unreadable reference list is not acceptable for publication.
*   **Minor Issues:** There are numerous other small errors, such as the unconventional use of symbols in the caption for Figure 1 ("AffGAN( ) and AffDG( ) models"), inconsistent matrix notation ("{y : yA = x}" should be `Ay=x`), and grammatical mistakes ("which we where unable to improve").

While a few minor errors can be overlooked, the sheer number and severity of these issues make the paper difficult to follow and critically evaluate. It reflects poorly on the diligence of the authors and prevents the reader from having confidence in the technical correctness of the work.

**2. Weak Empirical Validation of Proposed Methods:**
The paper introduces a general framework with three instantiations: AffGAN, AffDG, and AffLL. However, the experimental section reveals that this is misleading. In Section 5.3 (Grass Textures), the AffDG and AffLL models are reported to produce "blurry results" that the authors were "unable to improve upon". Consequently, these two methods are abandoned for all subsequent experiments on faces and natural images.

This is a significant weakness. The paper's contribution is framed as a general methodology with three potential solutions, but in practice, only one is shown to be effective for image SR. The failure of two-thirds of the proposed methods suggests that the overall framework is either not robust or is extremely sensitive to the quality of the underlying density/score estimator, a critical point that is only briefly touched upon in Appendix E. Presenting these failed approaches as core contributions rather than as negative results or ablations inflates the perceived scope of the work.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Focus the Contribution:** The paper would be much stronger if it focused on the most successful components: the affine projection layer and its application within a GAN framework (AffGAN). A more thorough analysis comparing the hard constraint of the affine projection against various soft-constraint penalties would be a valuable contribution on its own.
*   **Strengthen Theoretical Sections:** The reliance on an "Unpublished note" (Huszár, 2016) for the GAN objective is a minor weakness. The connection between a stochastic AffGAN and variational inference (Appendix F) is very interesting but feels disconnected from the main paper, as no stochastic model is ever implemented. This could be a compelling direction for future work or a separate publication if empirically validated.
*   **Clarify Technical Details:** The numerical estimation of the pseudo-inverse `A+` deserves more attention. Since `A+` may only be an approximation, the claim that the affine projection provides an *exact* constraint satisfaction is not strictly true. The impact of this approximation error should be quantified and discussed. Furthermore, the description of the "instance noise" annealing heuristic is too vague to be reproducible.
*   **Proofreading:** A thorough proofreading and correction of all formatting, grammatical, and mathematical errors is absolutely essential before any resubmission.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact, correct form of the generator's loss function used for AffGAN in your experiments?
2.  Given their poor performance on image data, why were the AffDG and AffLL methods included as primary contributions? Do you believe their failure reveals a fundamental limitation in using explicit score or density models for this optimisation task, and if so, could you elaborate?
3.  Table 2 provides a "Consistency" metric. Is this the `MAE(x, Aŷ)` from Table 1? The values for the affine models are non-zero (e.g., 6.42 · 10−3), which contradicts the claim of exact constraint satisfaction. Could you comment on the source and significance of this residual error? Is it due to the numerical approximation of `A+`?
