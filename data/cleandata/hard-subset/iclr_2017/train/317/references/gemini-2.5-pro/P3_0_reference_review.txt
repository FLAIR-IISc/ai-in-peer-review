### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core motivation is to overcome the well-known issue of blurry outputs produced by models trained with pixel-wise MSE loss, which tend to predict the mean of the posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution (HR) images. The authors argue that seeking the mode of this posterior (the MAP estimate) is a more desirable objective for generating perceptually sharp and plausible images.

The main contributions are:
1.  **An Affine Projection Layer:** The authors introduce a novel architectural constraint that projects the output of any SR network onto the affine subspace of HR images that are perfectly consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. This is elegantly implemented using a fixed deconvolution (the pseudo-inverse of the downsampling operator) and a projection to the null-space of the downsampling operator.
2.  **Reformulation of SR as Cross-Entropy Minimization:** By enforcing this consistency constraint, the MAP inference problem simplifies to maximizing the log-prior probability of the generated HR image. This is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy H[q_G, p_Y] between the distribution of the generator's outputs (q_G) and the distribution of true HR images (p_Y), framing SR as a generative modeling task.
3.  **Three Methods for Amortised MAP Inference:** The paper explores three distinct approaches to minimize this cross-entropy:
    *   **AffGAN:** A GAN-based approach where the SR network acts as the generator and a discriminator is trained to distinguish its outputs from real HR images. The paper provides a theoretical justification for a specific GAN objective that minimizes KL[q_G || p_Y].
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder (DAE), which leverages the fact that the optimal denoiser can estimate the score function (the gradient of the log-probability of the data), allowing for direct gradient-based optimization of the prior term.
    *   **AffLL:** A method guided by a pre-trained, explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant), which allows direct calculation and backpropagation through the log-likelihood of the generated image.
4.  **Instance Noise:** A technique for stabilizing GAN training by adding noise to both real and generated samples, which is argued to prevent the discriminator from becoming overconfident when the supports of the two distributions do not overlap.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong Conceptual Contribution:** The paper provides a clear, elegant, and theoretically sound framework for perceptual super-resolution. The formulation of the problem as amortised MAP inference, enabled by the novel affine projection layer, is a significant contribution that provides a strong theoretical underpinning for using generative models in this domain. It successfully connects a practical problem (blurry SR images) to a principled inference objective.
2.  **Novel and Effective Architectural Component:** The affine projection layer is a simple yet powerful idea. It moves beyond soft penalty terms for consistency and enforces it by construction. The experiment in Section 5.2, showing that this layer even improves convergence and final performance for standard MSE training, is a compelling demonstration of its utility as a general architectural component for inverse problems.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**Strengths:**
*   **Clarity of Motivation and Theory:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the problem with the 2D Swiss-roll example, which provides powerful intuition for why MSE fails and MAP inference is desirable. The derivation from the MAP objective (Eq. 2) to the unconstrained cross-entropy minimization (Eq. 9) is clear and well-justified.
*   **Thorough Exploration of Solutions:** The investigation of three different generative modeling paradigms (adversarial, score-based, and likelihood-based) to solve the same underlying objective is a major strength, demonstrating a deep command of the field.
*   **Insightful Discussion:** The "Criticism and Future Directions" section (5.6) is excellent. It shows a high degree of intellectual honesty by discussing the potential pitfalls of MAP inference itself (e.g., its dependence on representation and the atypicality of the mode). The connection to amortised variational inference in the appendix is also very insightful and forward-looking.
*   **Compelling Toy-Problem Results:** The results on the Swiss-roll dataset (Fig 1, Table 1) provide strong evidence for the central claim: that AffGAN and AffDG successfully approximate the MAP objective, whereas MSE and MAE do not. This controlled experiment is crucial for validating the theoretical framework.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Underwhelming Performance of AffDG and AffLL:** The paper is candid that the denoiser-guided and likelihood-guided methods produced blurry results on real image textures and were subsequently dropped. While the honesty is appreciated, this significantly weakens the empirical story for two of the three proposed methods. The paper would be stronger with a more in-depth analysis of *why* these methods failed. Is the gradient estimation from the denoiser too noisy? Is the PixelCNN density model not accurate enough to provide a useful training signal for this task?
*   **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The experiments primarily compare against an MSE baseline and a "SoftGAN" variant. While this is sufficient to demonstrate the value of the affine projection, the paper lacks quantitative and qualitative comparisons to other prominent perceptual SR methods, most notably SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016), which is a key related work. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical impact of AffGAN relative to the state of the art in perceptual SR. Standard perceptual metrics like LPIPS are also absent.
*   **High-Frequency Artifacts:** The qualitative results for AffGAN (Figs 4 & 5), while sharp, exhibit some of the high-frequency, noisy artifacts commonly associated with GANs. The paper acknowledges this but does not explore it further. This is a practical limitation that affects the perceived quality.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   The numerical estimation of the pseudo-inverse A+ is an interesting detail. How critical is the accuracy of this estimation to the final performance? An ablation study showing the effect of a less-optimised A+ (e.g., a simple transposed convolution) versus the numerically optimized one would be valuable.
*   In Section 5.5, the example of the snake being super-resolved into water is fascinating. You suggest this is because water is more common in the prior. This highlights a key tension in MAP inference for SR: prioritizing prior plausibility over fidelity to the true, but perhaps rare, underlying HR image. This would be a great point to expand upon in the discussion.

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:
1.  Could you provide more insight into the failure of AffDG and AffLL on the grass texture dataset? Do you believe this points to a fundamental limitation of using pre-trained score or density models as loss functions in this context, or was it an issue of optimization or the specific model architectures used for the denoiser/density model?
2.  How does AffGAN compare, both quantitatively (using metrics like PSNR, SSIM, and a perceptual metric like LPIPS) and qualitatively, to established perceptual SR methods like SRGAN on standard datasets (e.g., Set14, BSD100)?
3.  The proposed connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is very compelling. Have you run any preliminary experiments with this stochastic version of AffGAN? Does it help mitigate the mode-collapse or artifact issues sometimes seen in GANs, or produce more diverse and plausible outputs as hypothesized?
