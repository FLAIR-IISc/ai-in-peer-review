### 1. Summary of the paper's contributions

This paper frames the task of single-image super-resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that conventional methods based on empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise losses (like MSE) lead to perceptually poor, blurry results because they average over the multi-modal posterior distribution of high-resolution images. In contrast, a MAP estimate seeks a mode of this posterior, which corresponds to a plausible high-resolution image that is consistent with the low-resolution input.

The paper's core contributions are threefold:
1.  **A Principled Framework:** It proposes to train a neural network to perform amortised MAP inference by optimising an objective that is equivalent to minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of true high-resolution images.
2.  **Affine Projection Layer:** A novel and elegant architectural component is introduced that enforces consistency between the low-resolution input and the downsampled high-resolution output by design. This is achieved by projecting the output of an arbitrary CNN onto the affine subspace of all valid solutions, turning a constrained optimisation problem into an unconstrained one.
3.  **Three Optimisation Strategies:** The paper explores three distinct methods to approximate the cross-entropy objective, which relies on the unknown prior distribution of natural images. These methods are based on: (a) Generative Adversarial Networks (AffGAN), (b) gradients derived from a pre-trained Denoising Autoencoder (AffDG), and (c) direct optimisation of the log-likelihood under a pre-trained explicit density model (AffLL).

Additionally, the paper introduces a technique called "instance noise" to stabilise GAN training and provides a theoretical connection in the appendix between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

### 2. Decision and key reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **The central claim of a general framework for amortised MAP inference is not fully substantiated.** While the paper introduces three promising, theoretically-motivated methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL), two of them (AffDG and AffLL) are shown to produce poor, blurry results on image tasks and are subsequently abandoned. The failure of 2/3 of the proposed instantiations undermines the claim of a broadly applicable framework and suggests that the success is specific to the GAN-based approach.
2.  **The empirical evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate a significant advance over the state of the art.** The paper's primary successful method, AffGAN, is closely related to prior work on GAN-based super-resolution (e.g., SRGAN by Ledig et al., 2016). However, the paper lacks any direct comparison, either qualitative or quantitative, to this or other relevant perceptual SR methods, making it difficult to assess the practical impact and superiority of the proposed contributions.

### 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper is built on a strong and insightful theoretical foundation. The formulation of perceptual SR as amortised MAP inference is compelling, and the affine projection layer is a particularly clever contribution that elegantly solves the consistency problem. The experiments on the 2D Swiss-roll dataset provide a clear and convincing demonstration that the proposed methods can indeed approximate MAP inference in a controlled setting.

However, the transition from this low-dimensional proof-of-concept to high-dimensional image data reveals significant issues. The main weakness lies in the experimental validation of the overall framework. The paper presents AffGAN, AffDG, and AffLL as three parallel approaches to the same goal, but the results on the grass texture dataset (Section 5.3) show that only AffGAN produces sharp, plausible images. The AffDG and AffLL models yield blurry outputs, contrary to the goal of the paper. This is a critical negative result that is not adequately addressed. The discussion is brief and relegated to Appendix E, where the failure is attributed to potential inaccuracies in the learned density models without a deep investigation. This is unsatisfying and casts significant doubt on the viability of the Denoising-Guided and Density-Guided approaches for this task, thereby weakening the paper's main story about a general framework. If only the adversarial approach works, the paper is less a novel framework and more an improvement to GAN-based SR.

Furthermore, positioning this improvement requires comparison to existing work. SRGAN is a highly relevant baseline that also uses a GAN to achieve perceptual quality. The proposed AffGAN differs mainly in its use of the affine projection layer instead of a soft content loss in pixel space. This is a well-motivated difference, and the experiments in Section 5.2 show the benefits of the affine projection for an MSE-trained model. However, to establish the value of AffGAN, a direct comparison with SRGAN is necessary. Without it, we cannot know if AffGAN produces qualitatively better images, is more stable to train, or offers other advantages over existing state-of-the-art perceptual SR methods. The current experiments only compare against a standard MSE baseline and a "SoftGAN," which appears to be the authors' own implementation, not a recognised SOTA model.

In summary, while the paper contains excellent ideas, the empirical evidence does not fully support its broad claims. The failure of two of its core methods on image data and the lack of comparison to relevant baselines are major shortcomings that prevent a confident assessment of the work's contribution to the field.

### 4. Additional feedback for improvement

I believe this paper has the potential to be a very strong contribution to the field with revision. My feedback is intended to be constructive towards that goal.

*   **Reframing the Contribution:** The most straightforward path to improvement would be to narrow the paper's focus. Instead of claiming a general framework, the paper could be framed around the novel affine projection layer and a deep dive into its most successful application, AffGAN. This would allow for a more thorough analysis and comparison of AffGAN against other perceptual SR methods.
*   **Investigating Failure Modes:** Alternatively, a deeper investigation into *why* AffDG and AffLL fail would be a valuable scientific contribution in its own right. Is the gradient from the denoiser too noisy? Is the log-likelihood surface of the density model too difficult to navigate for the generator? Answering these questions would provide important insights into the limitations of using score-matching and explicit density models for complex conditional generation tasks.
*   **Strengthening Empirical Evaluation:** A direct and fair comparison with SRGAN is essential. This should include side-by-side qualitative comparisons on standard test sets (e.g., Set14, BSD100) and reporting of perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS), which are more aligned with the paper's goals than PSNR/SSIM.
*   **Ablation Study for Instance Noise:** The instance noise technique is interesting. Its contribution would be much clearer with a dedicated ablation study that shows training dynamics (e.g., discriminator loss, gradient norms) with and without it, demonstrating the claimed stability improvement.
*   **Promote the VI Connection:** The theoretical connection between stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference (Appendix F) is very insightful. I would encourage the authors to consider moving this to the main paper to further strengthen the theoretical grounding of the adversarial approach.

### 5. Questions for the authors

1.  Could you elaborate further on the failure of the AffDG and AffLL models? Beyond the hypothesis in the appendix, did you experiment with different architectures for the denoiser/density model, or different training schedules (e.g., different noise annealing for the DAE)? Is it possible the problem lies in the optimization challenge of backpropagating through these pre-trained models rather than the models themselves?
2.  What was the reasoning for not including a comparison to SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016)? Given the similarity in using a GAN for perceptual SR, it seems like the most critical baseline for evaluating AffGAN.
3.  Regarding the affine projection layer, what is the computational overhead of computing the pseudoinverse `A+` and applying the projection `(I - A+A)` during the forward and backward passes, compared to a standard learned deconvolution layer?
4.  How sensitive is the training stability of AffGAN to the specific annealing schedule of the instance noise? Is there a principled way to set this schedule, or was it found through extensive tuning?
