This paper presents a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on the principle of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that standard methods based on minimising Mean Squared Error (MSE) lead to perceptually poor, blurry results because they average over the multi-modal distribution of possible high-resolution (HR) images. Instead, they propose to find the mode of the posterior distribution, which should correspond to a plausible, high-probability image.

The core contributions are threefold. First, the paper introduces an "affine projection layer" that can be incorporated into any neural network architecture. This layer enforces the physical constraint that the downsampled output must exactly match the low-resolution (LR) input, projecting the network's output onto the affine subspace of valid solutions. Second, by using this projection, the authors show that the amortised MAP objective simplifies to minimising the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the true data distribution of HR images. This elegantly reframes the SR task as a generative modelling problem that does not require paired LR-HR data for training. Third, the paper explores three distinct methods to minimise this cross-entropy objective: (1) AffGAN, a method based on Generative Adversarial Networks; (2) AffDG, a method guided by the gradients of a pre-trained denoising autoencoder; and (3) AffLL, a method guided by the log-likelihood of a pre-trained explicit density model. The paper also introduces "instance noise" as a practical technique to stabilise GAN training. Experiments on toy data and natural images demonstrate that the AffGAN approach, in particular, can generate sharp and perceptually convincing results.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear and compelling theoretical narrative, a novel architectural contribution, and solid empirical validation. The core reasons for my recommendation are:
1.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides an elegant and well-motivated derivation that connects the goal of perceptual super-resolution to amortised MAP inference and, subsequently, to generative modelling via cross-entropy minimisation. This places prior work using GANs for SR on a much stronger theoretical footing.
2.  **Novel and Effective Architectural Constraint:** The affine projection layer is a simple but powerful idea. Enforcing the physical consistency between LR input and HR output as a hard architectural constraint is more direct and effective than using a soft penalty loss. The experiments convincingly show this layer improves performance and convergence even in a standard MSE setting.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear, principled approach. By starting from the MAP objective, the authors derive a loss function (cross-entropy) that directly targets the statistical properties of natural images rather than relying on pixel-wise fidelity metrics known to correlate poorly with human perception. The affine projection is the key enabler for this, cleanly separating the problem into satisfying the data constraint (`Ax=y`) and satisfying the image prior (`y ~ p_Y`).

The exploration of three different methods (GAN, Denoising, Density Model) to optimize the objective is scientifically rigorous. Although the denoiser- and density-guided approaches were ultimately unsuccessful on complex image data, the inclusion of these experiments is valuable, highlighting the practical difficulties of using score estimates from denoisers or explicit density models in high-dimensional adversarial settings. The success of the AffGAN model demonstrates the practical utility of the overall framework.

Furthermore, the paper contains several valuable secondary contributions. The "instance noise" technique for stabilising GANs is well-explained and appears to be a useful tool for practitioners. The discussion section is refreshingly honest, acknowledging the theoretical limitations of MAP inference itself (e.g., its representation-dependence and the potential for the mode to be atypical). Finally, the connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference, detailed in the appendix, is a significant theoretical insight that links two major families of generative models.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are several areas where it could be improved:
*   **Analysis of AffDG/AffLL Failure:** The paper quickly discards the denoiser and density-guided methods after they produce blurry results on grass textures. The appendix provides some intuition, but a more in-depth analysis in the main text would be beneficial. Why do these methods, which theoretically optimize the same objective, fail where the GAN succeeds? Is it an optimization issue, where the gradients provided by the denoiser/density model create a more difficult landscape? Or is it an estimation issue, where the models are not accurate enough to provide useful guidance? A deeper investigation would strengthen the paper's conclusions.
*   **Quantitative and Qualitative Comparisons:** The quantitative evaluation relies on PSNR and SSIM, which the paper rightly critiques. To better support the claim of improved perceptual quality, including no-reference image quality metrics (e.g., NIQE) or, ideally, a user study would be highly beneficial. Additionally, a direct comparison to other perceptual SR methods, most notably SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016), would be very valuable. Both use GANs, but this work's affine projection is a key difference. Comparing them on standard benchmark datasets would help situate the contribution within the literature.
*   **Reproducibility Details:** More detail on the "instance noise" schedule would be helpful. The paper states it was "linearly annealed," but the exact schedule, its justification, and its sensitivity could be described more thoroughly to aid reproducibility.

**Questions for Authors**
*   Could you elaborate on the practical challenges encountered with the AffDG and AffLL models? Was the primary issue the quality of the gradient estimates from the pre-trained models, or did you face fundamental optimization challenges like vanishing/exploding gradients when backpropagating through them?
*   The connection to amortised variational inference in Appendix F is fascinating. Have you experimented with the proposed stochastic version of AffGAN? Could it potentially generate a diverse set of plausible HR solutions for a single LR input, thereby addressing the criticism that the MAP estimate can be an "atypical" sample from the posterior?
*   The current framework assumes a known, fixed downsampling kernel `A`. How do you see this approach being extended to the more realistic "blind SR" setting where the kernel is unknown? Could `A` and `A⁺` potentially be estimated as part of the learning process?
