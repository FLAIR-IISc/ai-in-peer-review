### **1. Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of single image super-resolution (SR) by moving away from standard empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses (e.g., MSE) towards a more principled probabilistic approach. The authors frame SR as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, aiming to find a high-resolution (HR) image that is both consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input and has a high probability under the natural image prior.

The primary contributions are:
1.  A novel neural network component, the "affine projection layer," which enforces the hard constraint that the downsampled output must exactly match the LR input. This is achieved by projecting the output of an arbitrary network onto the affine subspace of all valid solutions, turning a constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one.
2.  A theoretical reformulation showing that with this projection, amortised MAP inference is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of the model's outputs and the true data distribution of HR images.
3.  Three distinct methods for minimizing this cross-entropy objective:
    a. **AffGAN**: A variant of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) that provides a theoretical justification for using adversarial training in SR. It also introduces "instance noise" as a technique for stabilizing training.
    b. **AffDG**: A denoiser-guided method that uses a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data (the score function) and backpropagates this gradient to train the SR network.
    c. **AffLL**: A density-guided method that uses a pre-trained explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant) to directly optimize the log-likelihood of the SR network's output.

The authors validate their methods on a 2D toy problem, demonstrating that they successfully approximate the MAP solution. On real image datasets (textures, faces, and ImageNet), they show that the affine projection is an effective architectural choice and that the AffGAN model produces perceptually sharp and plausible images, unlike the blurry results from MSE-based methods.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Novel and Principled Framework:** The paper introduces a theoretically sound and novel framework for super-resolution based on amortised MAP inference. The key technical innovation, the affine projection layer, is an elegant solution to enforce data consistency as a hard constraint, which is more appropriate for the SR problem than common soft penalties.
2.  **Strong Conceptual Contribution and Clarity:** The work provides a clear and insightful connection between MAP inference in an inverse problem and generative modeling (specifically GANs). This provides a strong theoretical motivation for recent empirical successes in the field and is supported by well-designed experiments on a toy problem that clearly illustrate the core concepts.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

**Strengths:**
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation in Section 3, which transforms the MAP objective into an unconstrained cross-entropy minimization problem via the proposed affine projection, is a significant strength. It provides a clear, principled foundation for the rest of the work. Similarly, the connection established between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference (Appendix F) is an insightful theoretical contribution.
*   **The Affine Projection Layer:** This is a strong and practical contribution. It directly addresses a core aspect of the SR problem—consistency with the input. The experiments in Section 5.2 provide a crucial proof of concept, showing that this architectural constraint does not hinder performance when using a standard MSE loss and can even accelerate training, while perfectly satisfying the consistency constraint.
*   **Effective Validation on Toy Problem:** The Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) is an excellent and clear demonstration of the core thesis. It empirically validates that the proposed AffGAN and AffDG methods do indeed find solutions close to the MAP estimate, unlike MSE or MAE, and that the affine projection improves performance over soft constraints.
*   **Qualitative Results:** For the AffGAN method, the qualitative results on grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet images effectively demonstrate the ability to generate sharp, perceptually plausible high-resolution details, which is the primary goal of the paper. The honest discussion of failure modes (e.g., the "snake to water" example) adds to the paper's scientific rigor.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Limited Empirical Success of All Proposed Methods:** A notable weakness is that two of the three proposed optimization methods, AffDG and AffLL, are reported to produce blurry results on the grass texture dataset and were subsequently not evaluated on more complex image data. While the paper is transparent about this, it means that the practical success of the overall framework is demonstrated for only one of the three approaches (AffGAN). The explanation in Appendix E is brief and leaves questions about the fundamental viability of these score- and density-based guidance methods in high-dimensional spaces.
*   **Novelty of Training Stabilization:** The proposed "instance noise" technique for stabilizing GANs is well-motivated. However, the paper acknowledges parallel work from the same period exploring similar ideas (e.g., adding noise to inputs), which slightly tempers the novelty of this specific contribution, though it remains a useful practical trick.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The discussion regarding the poor performance of the AffDG and AffLL models in Appendix E could be expanded. It would be highly valuable for future work to understand whether the failure is due to fundamental limitations (e.g., inaccurate score/density estimates in high dimensions) or practical optimization challenges. A more detailed analysis of this negative result would strengthen the paper.
*   The connection to amortised variational inference presented in Appendix F is a powerful theoretical insight. This connection could be more prominently highlighted in the main text (perhaps in the conclusion or future work section) as it elegantly unifies GAN-based and VAE-based approaches for this problem class.
*   The paper claims to have used an annealing schedule for the instance noise but provides few details. Including more specifics about the schedule (e.g., the function used for annealing, how the discriminator's loss was used to adapt it) would significantly improve the reproducibility of the ImageNet experiments.

### **5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you elaborate on the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods on the grass texture dataset? The appendix suggests the density model might not be accurate enough. Do you hypothesize that the score estimates from the denoiser are similarly inaccurate or noisy in high dimensions, leading to blurry results, or was this primarily an issue of optimization stability?
*   The affine projection layer relies on a numerically estimated pseudo-inverse `A+`. How sensitive is the final SR performance to the precision of this estimation? Did you experiment with simpler approximations for `A+`, such as a standard learned transposed convolution initialized randomly, and how did that compare to using the pre-computed pseudo-inverse?
*   The use of instance noise appears to be critical for stabilizing AffGAN training on ImageNet. Could you quantify its impact? For instance, were you able to train the model at all without it, and if so, what was the quantitative or qualitative performance degradation?
