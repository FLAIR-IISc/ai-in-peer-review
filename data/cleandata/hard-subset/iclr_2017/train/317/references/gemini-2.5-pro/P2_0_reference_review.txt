### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper frames the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that conventional methods based on minimising pixel-wise MSE loss produce blurry images because they are Bayes-optimal for finding the mean of the posterior distribution, which is not a plausible natural image. Instead, they propose finding the mode (MAP estimate), which by definition lies in a high-probability region of the natural image prior.

The paper's core technical contributions are:
1.  **An Affine Projection Layer:** The authors observe that the downsampling process is a linear transformation (`x = Ay`). Consequently, all valid high-resolution (HR) solutions `y` for a given low-resolution (LR) input `x` lie on an affine subspace. They design a novel neural network layer that projects any output onto this subspace, thus enforcing by construction that the generated HR image is perfectly consistent with the LR input.
2.  **MAP as Cross-Entropy Minimisation:** By incorporating the affine projection layer, the MAP inference objective simplifies to an unconstrained problem of minimising the cross-entropy `H[q_θ, p_Y]` between the distribution of the network's outputs (`q_θ`) and the true distribution of HR images (`p_Y`).
3.  **Three Methods for Optimisation:** The paper explores three methods to minimise this cross-entropy:
    a. **AffGAN:** A GAN-based approach where the generator incorporates the affine projection. The authors provide a derivation showing their GAN training objective minimises `KL[q_θ || p_Y]`, which is closely related to the cross-entropy objective.
    b. **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder (DAE), which leverages the fact that the optimal denoiser can estimate the gradient of the log-probability of the data.
    c. **AffLL:** A method guided by a pre-trained, explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant).

The paper empirically validates these ideas, showing that the GAN-based approach (AffGAN) is the most successful at producing perceptually sharp and plausible results on natural images, outperforming MSE-based methods and soft-constrained GANs.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a clear, theoretically well-motivated, and novel framework for image super-resolution. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Elegant Problem Formulation and Novel Architectural Contribution:** The reframing of SR as amortised MAP inference constrained to an affine subspace is highly compelling. The proposed affine projection layer is a novel and principled way to enforce the hard physical constraint of the SR problem, which is a significant improvement over the commonly used soft L1/L2 consistency losses. This formulation provides a strong theoretical justification for using GANs in this domain, connecting them directly to the goal of finding high-probability solutions.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed to support the paper's claims. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a clear and intuitive validation of the core hypothesis. The experiment showing that the affine projection layer even improves performance for standard MSE training is a powerful proof of concept for the architecture itself. Finally, the qualitative results on challenging texture and natural image datasets demonstrate that the proposed AffGAN method successfully achieves its primary goal: generating perceptually superior images.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper excels in its clarity and logical flow. The introduction effectively motivates the shift from MSE to MAP inference using a simple 2D example. The theoretical development in Section 3 is sound, building from the MAP objective to the constrained optimisation problem, and finally to the elegant solution via the affine projection, which turns it into a cross-entropy minimisation problem. This provides a much-needed theoretical underpinning for using generative models for SR.

The affine projection layer is the standout contribution. It directly addresses a fundamental property of the SR inverse problem. The demonstration in Section 5.2 that this layer accelerates training and improves final performance even for a standard MSE loss is convincing evidence of its general utility. It shows that enforcing this constraint is not just beneficial for the proposed MAP framework but for SR networks in general.

The empirical results for AffGAN are impressive. The grass texture experiment (Figure 3) is a canonical example where MSE fails catastrophically, and AffGAN produces sharp, plausible results. The results on CelebA and ImageNet (Figures 4 and 5) further confirm that the method generates high-frequency details that are absent in MSE-based reconstructions. The authors are also commendably transparent about the limitations and failure modes of their other proposed methods (AffDG and AffLL), which strengthens the paper's scientific rigor. The "snake-to-water" example is a particularly insightful demonstration of the model's behaviour: it produces a plausible image from the prior, even if it is semantically incorrect, highlighting the nature of the MAP objective.

Finally, the discussion in Section 5.6 ("Criticism and Future Directions") is excellent. The authors show a deep understanding of the potential pitfalls of MAP inference, such as its dependence on representation and the "atypicality" of the mode in high dimensions. This self-critique adds significant intellectual depth to the paper.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help improve it further:

*   **Failure Analysis of AffDG and AffLL:** The paper states that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results and were abandoned for larger experiments. The discussion in Appendix E is helpful but could be expanded upon in the main text. A more in-depth analysis of *why* these methods failed would be valuable. Is it due to the difficulty of accurately estimating the score function (for AffDG) or the density (for AffLL) in high-dimensional image space with sufficient precision to guide the generator? This could provide important insights for future work in this direction.
*   **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:** The authors correctly argue that PSNR and SSIM are poor correlates of perceptual quality. However, the evaluation relies almost entirely on qualitative examples. The inclusion of more modern quantitative perceptual metrics, such as LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity), could provide stronger, more objective evidence to support the claims of perceptual improvement.
*   **Analysis of "Instance Noise":** The introduction of instance noise to stabilise GAN training is an interesting practical contribution. While Appendix C provides good intuition, a small ablation study showing training stability or final image quality with and without instance noise would make this contribution more concrete and impactful.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate further on the failure modes of the denoiser-guided (AffDG) and density-guided (AffLL) methods? Do you believe this is a fundamental limitation of using score/density models to guide generation in this context, or is it an issue of optimisation challenges and model capacity that might be overcome with different architectures or training techniques?
2.  The discussion in Section 5.6 on the mode of a distribution being potentially "atypical" is very insightful. Did you observe any visual artifacts in your AffGAN results that you would attribute to this phenomenon (i.e., finding a high-probability but unrepresentative solution), distinct from typical GAN artifacts like high-frequency noise?
3.  The affine projection relies on knowing the exact downsampling operator `A`. How sensitive is the method's performance to mismatches between the assumed `A` and the true (and potentially unknown) downsampling kernel used to create the LR images in a real-world scenario?
