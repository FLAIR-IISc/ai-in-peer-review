### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper frames the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR) as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that traditional MSE-based methods produce blurry results because they effectively average over the manifold of plausible high-resolution (HR) images, resulting in outputs with low probability under the true data distribution. To address this, they propose finding a single, high-probability HR image consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input.

The paper makes three main contributions:
1.  **A Novel Architecture with an Affine Projection Layer:** The authors observe that the downsampling process is a linear transformation (`x = Ay`). They design a network architecture that enforces this consistency as a hard constraint. This is achieved by projecting the output of an arbitrary network `f_θ(x)` onto the affine subspace of all valid solutions, ensuring that the final output `y_hat` always satisfies `A*y_hat = x`. This is an elegant architectural innovation.
2.  **Reframing SR as Cross-Entropy Minimization:** By enforcing the consistency constraint, the MAP inference objective simplifies to minimizing the cross-entropy `H[q_θ, p_Y]` between the distribution of the model's outputs (`q_θ`) and the distribution of true HR images (`p_Y`). This insight connects the SR problem directly to generative modeling and removes the need for paired LR-HR data during training.
3.  **Three Methods for Approximate MAP Inference:** The paper proposes and evaluates three different methods to minimize this cross-entropy objective:
    *   **AffGAN:** An approach based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) where the affine-projected SR network acts as the generator. They also introduce "instance noise" as a technique to stabilize GAN training.
    *   **AffDG:** A method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder (DAE). It leverages the theoretical result that an optimal denoiser can estimate the gradient of the log-data-density (`∇_y log p(y)`), which is then backpropagated through the SR network.
    *   **AffLL:** A method guided by an explicit, pre-trained density model (a PixelCNN variant), which is used to directly evaluate and maximize the log-likelihood of the SR network's output.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong and well-written paper that makes several significant contributions. The core ideas are creative, theoretically well-grounded, and effectively demonstrated through experiments.

1.  **Principled and Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper provides an elegant and compelling theoretical framework for super-resolution. Reframing the problem from empirical risk minimization (minimizing MSE) to constrained MAP inference (minimizing `H[q_θ, p_Y]`) is a significant conceptual shift. The architectural solution—the affine projection layer—is a novel and direct implementation of this principle, moving beyond the soft penalties used in prior work to a hard, verifiable constraint.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support for the Core Idea:** The experiments, particularly on the 2D Swiss-roll and texture datasets, provide clear and convincing evidence for the central claims. They demonstrate that the proposed GAN-based approach (AffGAN) successfully approximates the MAP solution and produces perceptually superior results compared to both MSE baselines and the other proposed MAP methods (AffDG, AffLL). The control experiment showing that the affine projection layer does not harm, and may even improve, standard MSE training further strengthens the case for its general utility.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strength lies in the tight coupling between its theoretical arguments and its practical implementation.

The introduction of the affine projection layer is a key contribution. It directly addresses the consistency problem (`x = Ay`), which is often handled with an auxiliary loss term. By building the constraint into the network architecture, the model is freed to focus entirely on optimizing the prior term (`log p_Y(y)`), which is the source of perceptual quality. This is a more principled approach than simply adding a GAN loss to a standard MSE-trained network. The numerical method for finding the pseudo-inverse `A+` is well-described and practical.

The connection established between this constrained optimization and minimizing cross-entropy is clear and powerful. It provides strong theoretical justification for using techniques from the generative modeling literature, such as GANs, for this discriminative task. The paper's analysis of GANs as minimizing KL-divergence (Appendix A) and the introduction of "instance noise" to stabilize training are valuable additions.

Empirically, the paper makes a strong case. The 2D Swiss-roll toy problem (Fig. 1, Table 1) is an excellent illustration of why MSE/MAE fails and how the proposed methods succeed in finding the mode of the posterior. The grass texture experiment (Fig. 3) is particularly telling; it's a domain where MSE notoriously fails, and the AffGAN results are strikingly sharper and more realistic than all other methods. While the AffDG and AffLL methods perform poorly on these complex images, the authors are transparent about this and the success of AffGAN is sufficient to validate the overall framework. The results on CelebA and ImageNet further confirm that the approach generalizes to structured, natural images, producing sharp and plausible details even if they are not pixel-perfect matches to the ground truth (e.g., the snake-to-water example). Finally, the "Criticism and Future Directions" section shows impressive maturity and self-awareness, acknowledging the conceptual limitations of MAP inference itself.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Analysis of AffDG and AffLL Failures:** The paper notes that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results on complex image data. The appendix suggests this is due to optimization difficulties and inaccurate gradients/density estimates. This is a significant finding and warrants more discussion in the main paper. Is this a fundamental limitation of using current denoisers/density models for high-dimensional gradient estimation, or could it be overcome with more powerful models or different optimization strategies? A deeper analysis would strengthen the paper by providing insight into the practical limits of these alternative approaches.
*   **Clarity on "Instance Noise":** While the motivation for instance noise is well-explained in the appendix, its relationship to other stabilization techniques could be briefly clarified in the main text. Also, the practical details of the annealing schedule are important for reproducibility and could be highlighted more.
*   **Computational Overhead:** A brief discussion of the computational cost of the affine projection layer would be helpful. How much overhead does calculating `A+` and applying the projection add to training and inference compared to a standard SR architecture of similar size?
*   **Stochastic Extension (Appendix F):** The connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference is fascinating and provides a deep link between two major generative modeling paradigms. While it is rightly placed in the appendix, this insight is powerful enough that it could be briefly foreshadowed in the main introduction or conclusion to highlight the richness of the proposed framework.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods? Do you believe this is an inherent flaw in using score-matching or likelihood-based objectives for this task in high dimensions, or do you think that with more advanced denoisers (e.g., diffusion models) or density estimators, these approaches could become competitive with AffGAN?
2.  The AffGAN results are perceptually sharp but sometimes exhibit high-frequency artifacts common to GANs. Have you experimented with combining the AffGAN objective with a small, weighted perceptual loss (like LPIPS) or even an L1 loss to see if this can regularize the output and reduce artifacts without reintroducing blurriness?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of AffGAN to the choice of the downsampling operator `A` and the accuracy of its numerically-estimated pseudo-inverse `A+`? Does the method hold up if `A` represents a more complex, non-ideal degradation model (e.g., including blur and noise)?
