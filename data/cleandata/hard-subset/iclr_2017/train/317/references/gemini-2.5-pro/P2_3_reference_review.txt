### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single-image super-resolution (SR) that aims to maximize perceptual quality by casting the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that standard Mean Squared Error (MSE) based methods produce blurry results because they are Bayes-optimal for an L2 loss, leading to an average of all plausible high-resolution (HR) solutions. In contrast, a MAP estimate seeks a mode of the posterior distribution, which corresponds to a solution that is highly plausible under the prior distribution of natural images.

The core contributions are:
1.  **An Architectural Constraint:** The introduction of an "affine projection layer" that enforces the output HR image to be perfectly consistent with the input low-resolution (LR) image. This is achieved by projecting any network output onto the affine subspace of valid solutions. This elegant move simplifies the MAP objective from maximizing the posterior `p(y|x)` to maximizing the prior `p(y)` subject to a hard constraint.
2.  **Objective Reframing:** The paper shows that with the affine projection, training for MAP inference is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy `H[q_G, p_Y]` between the distribution of the model's outputs (`q_G`) and the true data distribution (`p_Y`).
3.  **Three Training Methods:** Since `p_Y` is unknown, the paper proposes and evaluates three distinct methods to approximate this objective:
    *   **AffGAN:** A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) approach where a discriminator is trained to distinguish between generated and real HR images, providing a learning signal to the generator to match the data distribution.
    *   **AffDG:** A denoiser-guided approach that leverages the theoretical connection between optimal denoising and the score function (`∇ log p(y)`) to estimate gradients for the generator.
    *   **AffLL:** A density-guided approach that first trains an explicit, powerful density model (PixelCNN-MCGSM) on HR images and then uses its log-likelihood to train the SR network.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides extensive experiments on a 2D toy problem and several natural image datasets (grass, faces, ImageNet). The results demonstrate that the proposed methods, particularly AffGAN, successfully find mode-seeking solutions and produce perceptually sharper and more realistic images than traditional MSE-based methods.

---

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a clear, theoretically-grounded, and compelling new perspective on the image super-resolution problem. The two key reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper's main strength is its rigorous reframing of SR as an amortised MAP inference problem. The affine projection layer is a particularly insightful contribution, providing an architectural guarantee of data consistency that elegantly simplifies the learning objective. This provides a strong theoretical justification for using generative models for this task, moving beyond the more ad-hoc "perceptual loss" engineering common in the field.
2.  **Convincing Empirical Support and Analysis:** The experiments are well-designed and systematically support the paper's claims. The 2D toy problem provides a clear and intuitive validation of the core hypothesis. The experiments on real images convincingly demonstrate that the AffGAN approach produces visually superior results. The paper is also intellectually honest, thoroughly discussing the limitations of the MAP approach and reporting the negative results for the AffDG and AffLL methods.

---

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper excels in its clarity of motivation and execution. The initial discussion using the Swiss-roll example effectively illustrates the fundamental flaw of MSE/MAE for multimodal posteriors. This sets the stage perfectly for the proposed MAP inference approach.

The affine projection layer (Sec 3.1) is the paper's standout technical contribution. By building the `x = Ay` constraint directly into the network architecture, the authors transform a difficult constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one focused purely on matching the image prior. The demonstration in Sec 5.2 that this layer not only enforces the constraint but also accelerates training and improves final performance for a standard MSE loss is a powerful result in its own right. It shows that the proposed architecture is not just theoretically sound but practically beneficial.

The exploration of three different methods (GAN, Denoising, Density Model) to optimize the MAP objective is thorough. While AffGAN is ultimately the most successful, the inclusion of AffDG and AffLL serves as a rigorous comparison. The failure of AffDG and AffLL is an important scientific finding, suggesting that current denoisers and explicit density models may not yet provide stable or accurate enough gradient signals for this type of high-dimensional generative task, whereas the adversarial signal from GANs is more effective.

The qualitative results (Figs 3, 4, 5) are compelling. The AffGAN model produces images that are significantly sharper and more detailed than the MSE baseline. The "snake to water" example (Fig 5) is a fantastic illustration of the model's behavior: faced with an ambiguous LR input, it generates a sample from a high-density region of the natural image prior (water) that is consistent with the input, even if it is semantically incorrect. This highlights both the power and the potential pitfalls of the MAP approach, which the authors thoughtfully discuss in Section 5.6. The introduction of "instance noise" is also a valuable practical contribution to stabilizing GAN training.

Finally, the theoretical connection between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference (Appendix F) is a significant insight that bridges two major families of generative models and adds considerable depth to the paper.

---

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, a few points could be clarified or strengthened:

*   **Failure of AffDG and AffLL:** The paper relegates the discussion of why AffDG and AffLL failed to Appendix E. This is a very interesting result and deserves more prominence in the main text. A brief discussion of the potential reasons (e.g., noisy gradient estimates from the denoiser, or the density model being too sharply peaked) in Section 5.3 would strengthen the paper's conclusions and provide valuable insights for future research in this direction.
*   **Computational Cost of A+:** The paper details the numerical estimation of the pseudo-inverse `A+` in Appendix B. It would be helpful to include a brief comment in the main text about the practicality of this step. Is it a one-time pre-computation? How does its complexity scale with different downsampling operators?
*   **Instance Noise:** The instance noise trick is well-motivated in Appendix C. Given its importance for stabilizing the ImageNet experiments, it might be beneficial to briefly introduce and motivate it in the main methods section (3.2.1) rather than just referencing it.

---

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate further on your hypothesis for the poor performance of the AffDG and AffLL models? For AffDG, do you believe the issue is that the score approximation from the denoiser is too noisy for stable optimization, especially far from the data manifold? For AffLL, did you find that the generator quickly found a mode of the learned density model and then refused to move, leading to the blurry results?
2.  The "snake-to-water" result is fascinating. Does this suggest a fundamental limitation of the MAP objective for SR, where the model might prefer a "generic" high-probability texture over a "rare" but correct structure if both are consistent with the LR input? How might a sampling-based approach (as hinted at in Sec 5.6 and Appendix F) address this specific failure mode?
3.  How sensitive was the AffGAN model's performance and stability to the annealing schedule for the instance noise? Is there a principled way to set this schedule, or was it determined empirically?
