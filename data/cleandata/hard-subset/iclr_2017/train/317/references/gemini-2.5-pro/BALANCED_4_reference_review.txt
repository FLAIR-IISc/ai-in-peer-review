### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and principled framework for single image super-resolution (SR) by formulating the problem as amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The key motivation is to overcome the characteristic blurriness of models trained with pixel-wise MSE loss, which arises from averaging over the multimodal posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution (HR) images. The authors' primary contribution is an elegant architectural constraint, an "affine projection layer," which enforces that any generated HR image is perfectly consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. This projection cleanly handles the likelihood term in the MAP objective, transforming the problem into one of maximizing the prior probability of the generated images. This is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the distribution of generated images and the distribution of true HR images.

To tackle this new objective, the paper explores three distinct methods for learning the natural image prior without requiring paired LR-HR data: 1) a Generative Adversarial Network (AffGAN), which uses a discriminator to distinguish real from generated images; 2) a denoiser-guided approach (AffDG), which leverages a pre-trained denoising autoencoder to estimate the gradient of the log-prior; and 3) a density-guided approach (AffLL), which uses a pre-trained explicit density model (PixelCNN-style) to directly score the generated images. Through experiments on a toy dataset and real-world images (textures, faces, and natural scenes), the paper demonstrates that the AffGAN approach is highly effective, producing perceptually sharp and plausible results that significantly outperform traditional MSE-based methods in visual quality.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The paper provides a strong theoretical contribution by reframing the perceptual SR problem as amortised MAP inference. The proposed affine projection layer is a novel and elegant technical solution that cleanly enforces the data consistency constraint, providing a more principled alternative to the common practice of using soft penalty terms. This formulation provides a solid theoretical foundation for the use of generative models in SR.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The 2D toy problem provides clear intuition and validates the core hypothesis. The experiments on real images convincingly demonstrate the superior perceptual quality of the proposed AffGAN method over baselines. The honest reporting on the limitations of the denoiser and density-guided approaches further strengthens the paper's scientific rigor.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and motivated. The introduction, using the Swiss-roll example, clearly illustrates the fundamental problem with MSE loss in the context of multimodal posteriors and provides a strong justification for pursuing MAP inference. The theoretical derivation in Section 3, which moves from the MAP objective to the cross-entropy minimization under the affine projection, is logical and easy to follow.
*   **Technical Novelty:** The affine projection layer is a significant technical contribution. It guarantees that the model output lies within the affine subspace of valid solutions, directly satisfying the likelihood term `p(x|y)`. This is a much "harder" and more principled constraint than adding a soft L1/L2 reconstruction loss on the downsampled output, as shown by the superior performance of "Aff" models over "Soft" models in the experiments. The method for numerically computing the pseudo-inverse `A+` is also a useful practical detail.
*   **Comprehensive Experimental Design:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed to validate the paper's claims step-by-step.
    *   The Swiss-roll experiment (Sec 5.1) confirms that the proposed methods do indeed approximate the MAP solution and minimize cross-entropy, unlike MSE/MAE.
    *   The "proof of concept" experiment with an MSE loss (Sec 5.2) is an excellent sanity check, demonstrating that the affine projection architecture is not only a valid constraint but can actually improve performance and training speed even in a standard setting.
    *   The qualitative results on challenging datasets like grass textures and faces (Sec 5.3, 5.4) clearly show the perceptual benefits of AffGAN, producing sharp details where MSE-based methods produce blur.
*   **Insightful Discussion:** The "Criticism and Future Directions" section (5.6) is a mark of strong research, as the authors proactively discuss the potential theoretical limitations of MAP inference itself (e.g., dependency on representation, atypicality of the mode). This critical self-reflection adds depth to the paper and nicely motivates future work on posterior sampling, which is further explored in the appendix.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Success of DG and LL methods:** While the failure of the AffDG and AffLL methods is reported honestly, the analysis of why they failed is somewhat brief. The blurry results suggest that either the gradient estimates from the denoiser were not accurate enough for high-dimensional image data, or that the optimization landscape created by these methods is exceptionally difficult. A deeper investigation into this would have strengthened the paper.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation relies primarily on visual inspection, which is appropriate for a paper focused on perceptual quality. However, the quantitative evaluation is limited to PSNR and SSIM, which are known to correlate poorly with human perception and on which the proposed method is expected to perform worse. The inclusion of a no-reference perceptual quality metric (e.g., NIQE, BRISQUE) or a small-scale human study would have provided stronger quantitative evidence for the claimed perceptual improvements.

### **Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to be constructive suggestions for improving the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   The introduction of "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training is an interesting practical contribution. The theoretical motivation provided in Appendix C is compelling. To make a stronger claim, it would have been beneficial to include a small ablation study comparing its effect to other stabilization techniques like one-sided label smoothing.
*   The connection drawn in Appendix F between a stochastic version of AffGAN and amortised variational inference is fascinating and provides a tantalizing glimpse into future work. This connection is quite significant and could potentially have been highlighted more prominently, perhaps in the main discussion section, to further underscore the richness of the proposed framework.
*   The visual results in Figure 5 show some high-frequency artifacts, which is a common issue with GANs. While the results are still impressive, it would be useful to acknowledge this and briefly discuss potential remedies, such as modifications to the discriminator architecture or loss function.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if you could clarify the following points in your rebuttal:

1.  Could you provide more detail on the optimization difficulties encountered with the AffDG and AffLL methods? You hypothesize that the denoiser provides imprecise gradients and the density model is too peaked. Did you observe specific issues during training, such as unstable, vanishing, or exploding gradients?
2.  The instance noise was critical for training on ImageNet. Was it also necessary for the CelebA experiments? The appendix mentions an annealing schedule that keeps the discriminator's loss constant. Could you elaborate on how this adaptive schedule was implemented?
3.  The paper rightly argues against simple convex losses. However, recent work in perceptual SR has found success in combining an adversarial loss with a pixel-space loss (e.g., L1) to balance perceptual realism and fidelity to the ground truth. Did you experiment with such a hybrid loss within your AffGAN framework?
4.  Regarding the affine projection, how sensitive is the final model's performance to the accuracy of the numerically estimated pseudo-inverse `A+`? Is a rough approximation sufficient, or is it critical to minimize the objective in Appendix B.1 to a very low tolerance?
