### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper reformulates the problem of single-image super-resolution (SR) from the standard empirical risk minimization framework to one of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The central idea is to generate high-resolution (HR) images that have a high probability under the natural image prior, while remaining perfectly consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input. The authors make several key contributions. First, they introduce a novel "affine projection layer" for convolutional neural networks, which acts as a hard constraint ensuring that downsampling the network's output always reproduces the original LR input exactly. This elegantly handles the likelihood term in the MAP objective. Second, with the likelihood satisfied by construction, the problem reduces to maximizing the prior probability of the output images. To achieve this, the paper proposes and investigates three distinct methods for approximating and optimizing this objective: 1) AffGAN, a Generative Adversarial Network variant that minimizes the KL-divergence between the distribution of generated images and the data distribution; 2) AffDG, a method guided by a pre-trained denoising autoencoder, which leverages the connection between denoising and the score function (gradient of the log-probability); and 3) AffLL, an approach that directly uses a pre-trained explicit density model (a PixelCNN variant) to score the generated images. The methods are evaluated on a toy 2D problem and on standard image datasets for SR.

**Decision and Key Reasons:**

**Decision:** Reject

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation Against Established Baselines:** The paper proposes a new framework for SR, but the primary successful method, AffGAN, is not adequately compared against relevant and established state-of-the-art methods. The comparisons are limited to a simple MSE baseline and a "soft constraint" version of their own model, which is not a competitive benchmark. For a paper focused on perceptual quality, a direct comparison with prominent perceptual SR methods like SRGAN is necessary to properly situate the contributions.

2.  **Incomplete Investigation of Proposed Methods:** The paper introduces three parallel approaches for MAP inference (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) but fails to demonstrate the viability of two of them on real image data. The denoiser-guided and density-guided methods produce blurry results on the grass texture experiment and are subsequently abandoned without sufficient analysis. This significantly weakens the paper's central claim of presenting a general framework for amortised MAP inference, reducing its effective contribution to a single GAN-based method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core theoretical motivation of the paper—framing SR as MAP inference and using an affine projection to enforce data consistency—is methodologically sound and compelling. The experiment in Section 5.2, showing that the affine projection layer can even improve performance for a standard MSE loss, is a strong piece of evidence for this architectural contribution.

However, the paper's subsequent empirical support is lacking in rigor. The main body of work in SR has moved beyond simple MSE-trained CNNs. The authors cite Ledig et al. (2016), the work that introduced SRGAN, but do not compare their results to it, either quantitatively or qualitatively. SRGAN provides the most direct and relevant baseline, as it also uses a GAN-based objective to improve perceptual quality. Without such a comparison, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed AffGAN offers any tangible benefits over existing, well-established adversarial training techniques for SR. The qualitative results in Figures 4 and 5, while sharp, appear to exhibit the kind of high-frequency noise artifacts common to many GAN-based methods, and it is unclear if they represent an advance in perceptual quality over the state of the art.

Furthermore, the paper's contribution is presented as a suite of three methods derived from the same MAP inference principle. The failure of AffDG and AffLL on the first real image task (grass textures) is a major negative result that is not adequately explored. The appendix (Section E) offers a brief explanation, suggesting the pre-trained models were not accurate enough, but this is insufficient. This lack of analysis undermines the paper's broader claims. A rigorous paper would investigate *why* these theoretically motivated approaches failed in practice. Is the optimization landscape intractable? Are the gradients from denoisers or density models too noisy or biased for this task? By simply dropping these methods, the paper feels incomplete and opportunistic, focusing only on the one approach that yielded visually plausible results. This makes the contribution much narrower than initially claimed.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   The affine projection layer is a strong and well-motivated contribution on its own. The paper would be significantly stronger if it were reframed to focus more on this component, perhaps by demonstrating its utility across a wider range of loss functions (e.g., MSE, L1, VGG perceptual loss, adversarial loss) and comparing it systematically to the common "soft constraint" approach.
*   The introduction of "instance noise" is presented as a general trick for stabilizing GANs. While interesting, its effectiveness is not isolated or compared to other widely-used stabilization techniques like spectral normalization or gradient penalty (WGAN-GP). A small ablation study would be needed to substantiate the claim of its utility.
*   The theoretical connection between a stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference presented in Appendix F is intriguing. However, without any empirical results from this stochastic model, it remains speculative. If this connection is a key point, it should be supported by experiments.
*   While PSNR and SSIM are known to correlate poorly with human perception, they are standard metrics that should still be reported on benchmark datasets for completeness and to quantify the trade-off being made against perceptual quality. Reporting a perceptual metric like LPIPS would also strengthen the evaluation.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why established and highly relevant baselines, particularly SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016) or other contemporary perceptual SR models, were not included in your experimental comparisons?
2.  Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the failure of the AffDG and AffLL models? Were experiments conducted to diagnose the issue, for example, by visualizing the gradient fields produced by the denoiser or analyzing the log-likelihood scores of generated images throughout training?
3.  The qualitative results for AffGAN show sharper details but also appear to contain some artifacts (e.g., the noise in the faces in Fig. 4, the texture on the hat in Fig. 5). Have you considered or conducted any form of human perceptual study to formally assess whether your results are preferred over those from other methods?
