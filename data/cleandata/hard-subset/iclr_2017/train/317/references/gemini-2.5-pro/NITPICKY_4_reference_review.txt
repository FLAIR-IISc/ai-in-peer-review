### Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper presents a novel framework for single image super-resolution (SR) based on the principle of amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that conventional MSE-based methods produce blurry results because they average over a multimodal posterior, and propose instead to find solutions that have high probability under the natural image prior. The core technical contribution is an "affine projection layer" for neural networks, which strictly enforces consistency between the low-resolution (LR) input and the downsampled high-resolution (HR) output. This architectural constraint reframes the SR problem as one of matching the distribution of generated HR images to the true data distribution, which can be achieved by minimizing the cross-entropy between them. The paper then proposes and evaluates three methods to approximate this objective: (1) AffGAN, a GAN-based approach; (2) AffDG, a method guided by gradients from a pre-trained denoiser; and (3) AffLL, a method guided by a pre-trained explicit density model. Experiments on a toy dataset and natural image datasets demonstrate that the GAN-based approach can produce perceptually sharp and plausible results.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper is built on a strong and well-motivated theoretical premise—the affine projection for enforcing consistency is an elegant idea—the execution is marred by a substantial number of methodological and presentational flaws. Two of the three proposed methods are shown to be ineffective in practice, and the remaining method (AffGAN) is not sufficiently validated against appropriate baselines. More critically, the paper contains inconsistencies, typos, and what appear to be errors in key formulas that severely undermine confidence in the work's technical correctness.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision:**

1.  **Major Methodological and Clarity Issues:**
    *   **Incorrect Denoiser Gradient Formulation:** The core formula for the denoiser-guided method (AffDG) in Eq. (12) appears to be incorrect or at least highly misleading. It states that `f*(y) - y / σ^2 ≈ ∂/∂y log pY(y)`, where `f*` is the optimal denoiser. However, established theory (e.g., Alain & Bengio, 2014) indicates that the gradient is approximated by `(f*(y_tilde) - y_tilde) / σ^2`, where `y_tilde` is the *noisy* input. The paper's formulation `f*(y)-y` is ambiguous about the input to `f*` and seems to misstate the result. This confusion is propagated to the implementation in Eq. (29), which uses `f_DAE(y_tilde) - y`, mixing noisy and clean variables in a way that does not match the theory. This potential error calls into question the entire basis for the AffDG method.
    *   **Inconsistent GAN Objective:** There is a critical inconsistency in the objective function for the generator in the GAN formulation. Section 3.2 presents `L(G;D) = -Ez~pZ log D(G(z)) - D(G(z))`. The second term, `-D(G(z))`, appears to be a typo and does not correspond to standard GAN objectives. Appendix A then derives a justification for a *different* objective, `Ey~qG log(D(y)/(1-D(y)))`, which is claimed to be equivalent to the generator terms in Eq. (17). These conflicting and unconventional formulations create significant confusion about what was actually implemented and optimized.
    *   **Failure of Proposed Methods:** The authors report in Section 5.3 that both the AffDG and AffLL methods produced "blurry results" on the grass texture task and were subsequently abandoned for further experiments. This means that two of the three primary methods proposed in the paper are effectively non-starters. While reporting negative results can be valuable, presenting these failed approaches as parallel contributions gives a misleading impression of the paper's actual contribution, which narrows to just the AffGAN variant.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:**
    *   **Weak Baselines:** The primary baseline for the GAN approach is "SoftGAN," which is based on an unpublished manuscript (Garcia, 2016). A more rigorous comparison would involve reimplementing and evaluating against established contemporary methods like SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016), which the authors cite but do not compare against directly.
    *   **Subjective Evaluation:** The central claim is improved perceptual quality, yet the evaluation is almost entirely qualitative, relying on visual inspection of a few selected examples. No user studies or quantitative perceptual metrics (e.g., NIQE, or a precursor to LPIPS) were used to substantiate these claims. While PSNR/SSIM are acknowledged to be poor correlates, their inclusion as the only quantitative metrics highlights the lack of rigorous perceptual validation.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The discussion of failure cases, such as the snake being super-resolved into water (Figure 5), is accompanied by speculative reasoning ("water will likely have a higher density under the image prior") without any supporting evidence.

3.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Flaws:**
    *   The paper suffers from a distracting number of typos and formatting errors. The bibliography is completely broken, with entries like `Alain and Bengio.,? \Q2014\E, Alain and Bengio.,`. Figure captions are malformed (e.g., `shown ( ). b)`). Key theoretical ideas, like the connection to variational inference, are relegated to an appendix (Appendix F) that contains typos ("varational", "emperical") and remains purely theoretical without any empirical support. Such a lack of polish is not acceptable for a top-tier publication and erodes the reader's trust in the work's diligence.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   **Refocus the Paper:** The most significant contribution is the affine projection layer combined with an adversarial loss. The paper would be much stronger if it focused exclusively on this (AffGAN), removed the sections on the failed AffDG and AffLL methods, and used the freed space to provide a more rigorous and convincing experimental evaluation.
*   **Correct Technical Details:** Thoroughly review and correct all formulas, particularly the GAN objective and the denoiser gradient estimator, providing clear citations and derivations. Ensure the text, equations, and appendices are consistent.
*   **Strengthen Experiments:** Compare AffGAN against a faithful implementation of a strong, published baseline like SRGAN. Conduct a user study to provide quantitative evidence for the claims of improved perceptual quality.
*   **Develop Theoretical Connections:** The link between the stochastic AffGAN and amortised variational inference is intriguing. This idea could be the basis of a separate, more focused paper if accompanied by a full derivation and empirical validation. As it stands, it feels like an underdeveloped afterthought.
*   **Thorough Proofreading:** The entire manuscript requires meticulous proofreading to fix the pervasive formatting errors, typos, and especially the broken reference list.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you please clarify the denoiser gradient formulation in Eq. (12) and Eq. (29)? Specifically, can you reconcile your use of `f*(y) - y` with the standard result that the score is proportional to `f*(y_tilde) - y_tilde`?
2.  What is the correct generator objective function for AffGAN? The one listed in Section 3.2 appears to have a typo, and it does not seem to match the one justified in Appendix A.
3.  Given that two of your three proposed methods were found to be ineffective on image data, could you elaborate on why they were retained as central contributions of the paper rather than being presented as negative results in a more focused study of AffGAN?
