This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The core idea is to constrain the solution space to be consistent with the downsampling process, achieved through an affine projection layer within a convolutional neural network. Three methods are then explored to minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the true data distribution: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) variant (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The authors motivate their approach by arguing that pixel-wise losses like MSE lead to blurry results due to averaging over plausible solutions, and that MAP inference favors higher-probability, more realistic outputs.

**Reject.** While the motivation for moving beyond pixel-wise losses is sound, the paper presents a complex set of methods with limited and insufficiently convincing evidence of their superiority, especially considering the established success of existing SR techniques. The experimental validation lacks the necessary rigor to support the claims made.

The primary concern lies in the lack of a comprehensive comparison against strong, established SR baselines. While comparisons are made to MSE and MAE trained networks, these are not state-of-the-art. Crucially, there's no comparison to more recent GAN-based SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, Real-ESRGAN) or other perceptual loss-based approaches. The claim of improved perceptual quality based on visual examples is subjective and requires quantitative evaluation using established perceptual metrics (beyond PSNR and SSIM) and ideally, user studies. 

Furthermore, the results presented are not consistently compelling. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a good conceptual demonstration, but its relevance to complex natural images is limited. The grass texture experiment shows promise for AffGAN, but the subsequent experiments on CelebA and ImageNet lack clear evidence of significant perceptual improvement. The instability observed with AffDG and AffLL, and the reliance on heavy regularization (instance noise) to stabilize AffGAN, suggests underlying issues with the proposed methods. The explanation regarding the inherent problems with GAN training and the introduction of instance noise is insightful, but feels like a post-hoc justification for observed instability rather than a foundational element of the approach.

Finally, the theoretical connection drawn to variational inference via a stochastic AffGAN is intriguing but underdeveloped. A more detailed analysis and empirical validation of this connection would be required to strengthen this claim.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Baselines:** The authors *must* include a comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods, including recent GAN-based and perceptual loss-based techniques.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:**  Supplement visual results with quantitative perceptual metrics such as LPIPS, NIQE, or BRISQUE. User studies would be ideal.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of the affine projection layer and other key components of the proposed methods.
*   **Stability Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the training stability issues observed with AffDG and AffLL. Explore different regularization techniques or architectural modifications to address these issues.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Elaborate on the theoretical connection to variational inference. Provide more rigorous mathematical analysis and empirical evidence to support this claim.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices, especially the learning rate and noise level.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Why were state-of-the-art SR methods not included in the experimental comparisons?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the choice of the instance noise distribution and the annealing schedule?
*   What specific modifications were made to the DAE and density models to address their instability during training?
*   How does the performance of AffGAN degrade as the upscaling factor increases (e.g., 8x or 16x)?
*   Regarding the MAP inference critique in Section 5.6, could you elaborate on how the proposed methods address the potential issue of the MAP estimate being atypical?
