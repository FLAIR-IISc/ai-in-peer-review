This paper proposes a method for image super-resolution based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, motivated by the limitations of pixel-wise loss functions like MSE. The core idea is to enforce consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs via an affine projection, effectively restricting the solution space to plausible high-resolution images. The authors explore three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) variant (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL). They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, particularly AffGAN, on both a 2D toy problem and real image datasets.

**Reject.** While the motivation for moving beyond MSE is sound, and the idea of enforcing consistency through affine projection is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to convincingly demonstrate the superiority and robustness of the proposed methods, particularly compared to well-established baselines. The experimental evaluation feels incomplete, and the theoretical justification, while present, is not fully leveraged to explain the observed results.

The primary concern lies with the experimental validation. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment is useful for illustrating the core concept, but it’s a highly controlled scenario. The results on natural images are more compelling visually, but the quantitative comparisons (Table 2) show that the proposed AffGAN doesn’t outperform MSE in terms of PSNR and SSIM, the standard metrics for SR. The authors dismiss this by focusing on perceptual quality, but there’s a lack of rigorous perceptual evaluation beyond visual inspection. Furthermore, the comparisons to other GAN-based SR methods (e.g., SRGAN, ESRGAN) are missing, making it difficult to assess the contribution of the affine projection specifically. The ablation study (Figure 2) showing the benefit of the affine projection is positive but doesn’t fully justify the complexity of the overall framework. The inconsistent performance of AffDG and AffLL, and the decision to focus solely on AffGAN, raises concerns about the robustness of the approach. The claim that AffGAN “dreams up reasonable data” (e.g., turning a snake into water) is concerning and suggests potential artifacts rather than meaningful super-resolution. Finally, the discussion of the theoretical connection to variational inference (Appendix F) feels tacked on and underdeveloped.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **More Comprehensive Quantitative Evaluation:** Include comparisons to state-of-the-art SR methods (SRGAN, ESRGAN, etc.) using standard metrics (PSNR, SSIM) *and* perceptual metrics (LPIPS, MS-SSIM).
*   **Rigorous Perceptual Evaluation:** Conduct user studies to assess the perceptual quality of the generated images.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform more thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of the affine projection and other components of the framework.  Specifically, what is the impact of initializing A+ correctly?
*   **Analysis of Failure Cases:** Investigate and discuss the failure cases of the proposed method. Why does AffGAN sometimes generate implausible results?
*   **Clarify Theoretical Contribution:** Expand on the connection to variational inference, and discuss how this connection could be exploited for further improvements.
*   **Address the Discrepancy with PSNR/SSIM:** Explain why the method doesn’t achieve superior PSNR/SSIM scores despite its purported perceptual benefits.

Here are some questions for the authors:

*   Why aren’t the quantitative results (PSNR/SSIM) competitive with existing methods?
*   What is the computational overhead introduced by the affine projection?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the downsampling kernel A?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the artifacts observed in the generated images?
*   Could the "dreaming up reasonable data" phenomenon be mitigated by incorporating additional constraints or regularization techniques?
