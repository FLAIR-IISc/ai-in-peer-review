This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by proposing to move away from empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses like MSE and instead employ amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is that MAP inference, by preferring high-posterior probability solutions, avoids the blurry results often produced by minimizing MSE. The authors motivate this approach by demonstrating how MSE leads to solutions with low prior probability, particularly in a simple 2D toy example. They contribute a CNN architecture incorporating an affine projection layer ensuring consistency between LR and HR images, and three methods for approximating MAP inference: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL).

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to resubmit after significant revisions. The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and a novel architectural constraint (affine projection). However, the empirical results are not compelling enough to justify the complexity of the proposed methods, and critical details about the performance of the different components are lacking. Specifically, the key claim that AffGAN significantly outperforms other methods is not consistently supported by quantitative and qualitative results across all experiments.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of convincing empirical validation. While the 2D Swiss-roll experiment demonstrates that AffGAN minimizes the target cross-entropy, this setup is overly simplistic. The results on more complex datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) are less conclusive. The AffGAN model shows visually plausible results, but the quantitative comparison (PSNR/SSIM) often favors the baseline MSE model, which contradicts the paper’s central premise. The ablation studies comparing AffGAN to SoftGAN are insufficient; for instance, the authors note slightly sharper images with AffGAN but also mention higher-frequency noise – a crucial trade-off that needs more detailed analysis. The abandonment of AffDG and AffLL after initial testing further weakens the overall argument. The gains achieved do not appear to justify the additional architectural and training complexity.

A second reason for rejection is the lack of clarity and completeness in the experimental details. The description of the density model (AffLL) is brief, and its poor performance isn't thoroughly investigated. The annealing schedule for instance noise is mentioned but not precisely defined. The justification for certain hyperparameters (e.g., network architectures) is weak. The ablation studies comparing different components are limited. It's difficult to assess the contribution of each element without a comprehensive experimental analysis. The paper refers to supplementary material but does not sufficiently integrate the essential details into the main text.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The authors should include more robust perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) alongside PSNR/SSIM. A user study comparing the perceptual quality of the generated images would strengthen the conclusions significantly.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to isolate the effects of the affine projection, instance noise, and the different training objectives. For example, how does the performance change with different noise levels? What is the impact of removing the skip connections?
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** Compare the proposed methods against more recent and established SR techniques, such as EDSR, RCAN, or RDN.
*   **Density Model Analysis:** Provide more details about the architecture and training of the density model (AffLL) and analyze the reasons for its underperformance.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Expand the discussion of the limitations of MAP inference, particularly regarding the potential for mode collapse or lack of diversity in the generated images.
*   **Clarity:** The theoretical derivations are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. Consider adding visual aids to illustrate key concepts.
*   **Specific Questions:**
    *   What is the specific impact of the affine projection layer on training speed and memory usage?
    *   What is the precise annealing schedule used for instance noise, and how sensitive are the results to this schedule?
    *   Why were the AffDG and AffLL methods abandoned after initial testing? Were any attempts made to improve their performance?
    *   How does the performance of AffGAN vary with different downsampling factors and image resolutions?
