## ICLR 2024 Review: Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by moving away from pixel-wise loss functions (like MSE) and instead proposes methods grounded in Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The core idea is to leverage natural image statistics to favor solutions with high posterior probability, leading to more plausible and less blurry reconstructions. The authors present three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) variant (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL), all utilizing an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between low- and high-resolution images.  The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of these methods, particularly AffGAN, on both a simple 2D toy problem and more complex natural image datasets, showing improved perceptual quality compared to traditional methods.  A particularly interesting connection is drawn between AffGAN and amortised variational inference.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting and theoretically motivated approach, the empirical results, particularly the comparison to state-of-the-art methods, are not sufficiently convincing. The gains demonstrated are often marginal, and the instability/difficulty in training AffDG and AffLL are significant drawbacks. The analysis, while thorough, doesn’t fully justify the complexity of the proposed methods over existing SR techniques, particularly given the tuning sensitivity and limited robustness demonstrated.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The 2D Swiss-roll experiment convincingly demonstrates the theoretical benefits of MAP inference. However, the performance gains on natural images (CelebA, ImageNet) are often subtle.  While qualitative results *suggest* improved perceptual quality for AffGAN, the quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) are not consistently superior to MSE-trained networks and even lag behind in some cases (Table 2).  A stronger comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN, Real-ESRGAN) is crucial.
* **Training Instability & Complexity:**  The authors acknowledge difficulties in training AffDG and AffLL, highlighting the sensitivity of these methods.  This raises concerns about their practical applicability.  The need for careful tuning and the reliance on a complex training procedure, including annealing schedules and specific network architectures, make the approach less accessible and less robust than simpler alternatives.  The instance noise trick helps with GAN training but adds another hyperparameter to tune.
* **Insufficient Ablation/Analysis:** The ablation study comparing affine projected networks to non-projected networks is valuable, but more detailed ablation studies exploring the impact of different components (e.g., the instance noise, the specific choice of discriminator architecture) would strengthen the paper. It is not completely clear why AffGAN performs best and whether the improved results are solely due to the MAP objective or other factors.
* **Limited Novelty in GAN Application:** While the theoretical grounding of using GANs for MAP inference is valuable, the application of GANs to SR is not novel. The paper needs to more clearly articulate *what* specifically makes their GAN variant (AffGAN) unique and superior beyond the affine projection.



**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Stronger Baselines:** A critical improvement would be a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art SR models like EDSR, RCAN, and Real-ESRGAN.  These benchmarks are essential for establishing the significance of the proposed methods.
* **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:**  In addition to PSNR and SSIM, consider using more perceptually relevant metrics like LPIPS or NIQE to better assess the perceptual quality of the generated images.
* **Detailed Ablation Studies:** Conduct more detailed ablation studies to analyze the contributions of each component of the proposed methods (e.g., affine projection, instance noise, different loss functions). This will help pinpoint the factors driving performance improvements.
* **Analysis of Failure Cases:**  Discuss the limitations of the proposed methods and provide examples of failure cases.  The snake-to-water example in Figure 5 is intriguing – a deeper analysis of such scenarios would be insightful.
* **Clarify the Connection to Variational Inference:** The connection to variational inference is briefly mentioned in the conclusion.  Expanding on this connection and explaining its implications would strengthen the paper. Specifically, discuss how the stochastic AffGAN can be seen as an approximate inference algorithm.
* **Address the MAP Criticism:** The authors briefly acknowledge the potential issues with MAP inference in Section 5.6. Expanding on this discussion and providing potential mitigation strategies would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a direct quantitative comparison (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) of AffGAN against state-of-the-art SR models (EDSR, RCAN, Real-ESRGAN) on the CelebA and ImageNet datasets?
* What specific choices were made to stabilize the training of AffGAN, and how sensitive are the results to these choices?
* Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the affine projection layer and its impact on training/inference speed?
*  You mention that the AffDG and AffLL methods were difficult to train. Why do you think this is the case, and what steps have you taken to mitigate these issues?
*  What are the specific advantages of using the affine projection compared to simply enforcing a soft L1/L2 constraint during training?  Is there a theoretical justification for the improved performance?



In conclusion, while the paper proposes a theoretically sound approach to SR, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and the training challenges make it difficult to recommend acceptance at this time.  Addressing the feedback provided and strengthening the empirical evaluation would significantly improve the paper.
