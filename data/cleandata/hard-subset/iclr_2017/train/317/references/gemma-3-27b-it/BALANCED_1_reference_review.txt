This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that minimizing pixel-wise losses like MSE leads to blurry results due to averaging over plausible solutions, and propose methods to directly optimize for the posterior probability, leveraging natural image statistics. Their contributions include a CNN architecture enforcing consistency between LR and HR images via affine projections, and three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL).

**Decision: Reject**

The core idea of moving towards MAP inference for SR is promising and theoretically sound. However, the paper presents a somewhat fragmented set of approaches, and the empirical validation, while demonstrating some advantages, isn’t sufficiently convincing to justify acceptance at a competitive conference like ICLR.  Specifically, the improvements demonstrated over baseline methods are not substantial enough, and the explanation of *why* certain methods succeed while others fail lacks depth.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Gains:** While the 2D Swiss-roll experiment clearly demonstrates the ability of AffGAN and AffDG to find solutions in high-probability regions (as intended), the gains on real image datasets (CelebA, ImageNet) are modest. The visual improvements observed are often subtle, and the quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM) don't consistently outperform existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly when considering the complexity added by the proposed techniques. Table 2 on CelebA, for example, shows the AffGAN performing comparably to or slightly worse than the MSE baseline in terms of PSNR and SSIM.

2. **Insufficient Ablation and Analysis:** The paper introduces multiple components (affine projection, different loss functions, instance noise).  A more thorough ablation study is needed to disentangle the contributions of each component.  For instance, the benefit of the affine projection is demonstrated (Figure 2), but it’s not entirely clear how it interacts with the different loss functions. Why does AffGAN consistently perform better than AffDG or AffLL? A deeper investigation into this is crucial. The authors mention issues with AffDG and AffLL but simply state they focused on AffGAN, without sufficient justification. 

3. **Theoretical Connection is Not Fully Exploited:** The connection drawn between AffGAN and amortized variational inference in Appendix F is interesting, but not sufficiently integrated into the main body of the paper. It feels like an afterthought. More discussion on the implications of this connection for understanding the behavior of AffGAN and potentially improving its performance would be beneficial.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clearer Motivation for Affine Projection:** While the theoretical justification for the affine projection is presented, its practical advantages in the context of deep learning are not fully explored. A discussion on how it affects the optimization landscape, gradient flow, or the network’s ability to learn complex mappings would be valuable.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** A more direct comparison to the current state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) is necessary.  The current comparisons are mainly against simpler baselines (MSE).
* **Analysis of Failure Cases:**  The paper briefly mentions that AffGAN sometimes "dreams up" plausible but incorrect reconstructions (e.g., the snake example). A more systematic analysis of these failure cases would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and potential areas for improvement.
* **Instance Noise Details:** More details about the instance noise technique, including its implementation and hyperparameter tuning, would be helpful.
* **Code Availability:**  Making the code publicly available would significantly increase the reproducibility and impact of the research.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The results on real datasets are modest. How do you explain the limited improvement compared to baseline methods, and what steps could be taken to enhance the performance of AffGAN?
2.  The paper briefly mentions issues with AffDG and AffLL. Could you elaborate on the challenges encountered during their training and why you ultimately focused on AffGAN?
3.  The connection to variational inference is intriguing. How could this connection be leveraged to develop new algorithms or improve the training of AffGAN?  Specifically, can insights from VI be used to address the mode collapse issues often observed in GANs?
4.  Can you provide more details on the choice of the Gaussian kernel for the affine projection and justify its appropriateness for different types of images (e.g., textures vs. faces)?
5.  Regarding the instance noise, what is the rationale behind the specific annealing schedule used, and how sensitive are the results to changes in this schedule?
