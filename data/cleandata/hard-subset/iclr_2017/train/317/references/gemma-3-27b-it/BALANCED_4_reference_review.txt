This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that standard empirical risk minimization approaches, particularly those relying on pixel-wise losses like MSE, struggle to produce perceptually realistic results due to their averaging behavior. Instead, they propose methods that aim to directly estimate the posterior distribution and favor solutions with high probability under a natural image prior. Their contributions include a CNN architecture with an affine projection layer ensuring consistency between LR and HR estimates, and three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL).

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and explores potentially valuable approaches to SR, the empirical results presented are not convincing enough to justify acceptance at ICLR. The performance gains observed, particularly for the AffGAN model, are often marginal or not statistically significant compared to existing methods, and the results on natural images are limited. The presentation and justification of the experimental design and analysis are also lacking in rigor.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Evidence:** The primary weakness lies in the empirical validation. While the 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a clear illustration of the benefits of MAP inference, the jump to real images feels substantial without more compelling evidence. The grass texture experiment shows some improvements with AffGAN, but the qualitative comparisons are not decisive. The CelebA and ImageNet results are presented briefly, and the quantitative metrics (PSNR/SSIM) do not consistently demonstrate a clear advantage over standard methods. Specifically, the MSE model often *outperforms* the proposed methods in terms of these metrics, which contradicts the core argument of the paper.
2. **Lack of Strong Baselines & Ablation Studies:** The comparison to baseline methods is insufficient. While MSE is included, other strong SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) are missing, making it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed approach. More importantly, the paper lacks thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of the affine projection layer. While the authors mention initial observations, a more systematic evaluation of its influence (e.g., varying its implementation, removing it entirely, comparing different initialization strategies) is crucial.
3. **Unclear Connection to Variational Inference:** The connection to amortized variational inference established in Appendix F feels tacked on and isn't fully integrated into the main narrative. The authors state it "for the first time establishes a connection," but the discussion lacks depth and doesn't explain how this connection impacts the practical implementation or interpretation of the AffGAN model.
4. **Overly Complex Methodology and Presentation:** The paper presents a large number of methods and theoretical justifications. While comprehensive, this makes it difficult to follow the core arguments and appreciate the key contributions. A more focused presentation, concentrating on the most promising approach (AffGAN) and providing a more detailed analysis of its strengths and weaknesses, would be more effective.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Strengthen Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct more comprehensive experiments, including comparisons to state-of-the-art SR methods (EDSR, RCAN, etc.). Include more quantitative metrics (e.g., LPIPS) that better correlate with perceptual quality. Perform a statistically significant comparison of the proposed methods with baselines.
* **Thorough Ablation Studies:**  Systematically investigate the impact of the affine projection layer. Explore different implementations and initialization strategies. Quantify the benefits of instance noise.
* **Clarify Theoretical Contributions:** Refine the theoretical arguments and provide a more intuitive explanation of why MAP inference is desirable for SR. Address the limitations of the MAP approach as mentioned in the conclusion.
* **Focus and Streamline Presentation:**  Concentrate on the most promising approach (AffGAN) and provide a more detailed and focused analysis. Simplify the presentation and remove unnecessary details.
* **Elaborate on the Connection to Variational Inference:** Explain how the connection to amortized variational inference influences the practical implementation and interpretation of AffGAN. Explore the potential benefits of this connection.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Beyond the initial observations, can you provide a more quantitative analysis of the benefits of the affine projection layer? What is the impact on training speed, convergence, and final performance?
* Why does the MSE model often achieve better PSNR/SSIM scores than the proposed methods? How does this reconcile with your argument against MSE-based approaches?
* What specific choices were made for the hyperparameter settings (e.g., learning rate, batch size, network architecture) for each of the proposed methods, and how were these settings determined?
* Can you elaborate on the limitations of the density model (AffLL) and explain why it did not perform as well as AffGAN?
* What are the computational costs associated with the affine projection layer and the proposed methods compared to standard SR techniques?
