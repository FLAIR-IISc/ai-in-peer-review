## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by moving away from pixel-wise loss functions (like MSE) and instead advocating for a Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference approach. The core idea is that SR should prioritize solutions that have high probability under a natural image prior, rather than simply averaging plausible solutions as MSE often does. The authors propose several methods to achieve this, including: 1) a CNN architecture with an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between LR and HR images, 2) a GAN-based approach (AffGAN) leveraging the affine projection, 3) a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and 4) a density-guided approach (AffLL). The paper provides both theoretical justification and empirical results demonstrating the benefits of their approach, particularly in terms of perceptual quality and consistency.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and explores a potentially fruitful direction for SR research, the empirical results are not convincing enough to warrant acceptance. The improvements over standard methods are incremental, and the justification for the observed gains, especially in the real image experiments, feels weak given the complexity of the proposed methods.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The most compelling results (in terms of cross-entropy) are shown on the 2D Swiss-roll dataset, which is a highly controlled and simplified scenario.  While this demonstrates the method *can* work in principle, it doesn’t translate strongly to the more complex natural image datasets. The gains on CelebA and ImageNet appear modest, and the qualitative results, while sometimes better, are not consistently superior to existing GAN-based SR approaches (like SRGAN).
* **Lack of Robust Evaluation:** The comparison to baseline methods feels incomplete. While MSE is included, the paper lacks a comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods like EDSR, RCAN or recent transformer-based SR models.  Without these comparisons, it's difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed methods. The authors mention PSNR and SSIM scores are not as good as MSE but do not elaborate on why perceptual quality is better.
* **Theoretical Justification vs. Practical Implementation:** The paper spends a significant amount of space on theoretical derivations, which are valuable for understanding the motivations. However, the connection between the theoretical framework and the practical implementation (e.g., the choice of network architecture, training procedures) isn't always clear. For example, the choice of annealing schedules and hyperparameters is not well justified.
* **Instability & Sensitivity:** The text notes instability in training the AffDG and AffLL models, and the AffGAN model benefits from instance noise for stabilization. This suggests the methods are sensitive to hyperparameter tuning, which might hinder reproducibility and generalization.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify the Advantage:** The authors should more clearly articulate *why* their approach is expected to outperform existing methods in a real-world scenario. A more detailed analysis of the mode collapse issues in GANs and how the affine projection mitigates these would be helpful.
* **More Thorough Evaluation:** Include comparisons to stronger baseline SR models (EDSR, RCAN, etc.).  Provide a more detailed ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of the proposed methods (e.g., the affine projection layer, the instance noise).
* **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:** While visual inspection is helpful, the authors should incorporate quantitative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) to provide a more objective assessment of the perceptual quality of the generated images.
* **Explain the High Frequency Noise:** Address the observation of high-frequency noise in the AffGAN outputs. Is this an inherent limitation of the approach?
* **Detailed Training Details:** Provide more detailed training details (hyperparameter settings, optimization algorithms, learning rate schedules) to ensure reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* You mention that the affine projection “ensures that our CNNs always output estimates that are consistent with the inputs.” Does this constraint significantly limit the expressiveness of the network? How does the affine projection compare to other consistency constraints used in SR?
* The AffDG and AffLL models exhibited training instability. What specific modifications were made to stabilize training, and how sensitive are the results to these modifications?
*  In the ImageNet experiments, can you show more examples where the AffGAN model generates significantly different (and arguably more plausible) results compared to a standard GAN-based SR method? This would strengthen the claim of perceptual improvement.
* You mention a connection to amortized variational inference. Can you elaborate on the specific variational objective being approximated and how the AffGAN model relates to existing variational autoencoders?
