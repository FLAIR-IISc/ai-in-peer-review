## ICLR 2024 Review: Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by shifting away from empirical risk minimization (e.g., MSE loss) towards an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference framework. The authors argue that MAP inference, by prioritizing high-posterior probability solutions, can avoid the blurry outputs common with MSE-trained models.  They present a novel CNN architecture leveraging affine projections to ensure consistency between LR and HR images, and then explore three methods for approximating the MAP objective: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of these methods, particularly AffGAN, on both a 2D toy problem and real image datasets, showcasing improved perceptual quality and a closer alignment with the desired MAP behavior.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling argument for moving beyond traditional loss functions in SR, and the proposed methods demonstrate promising results, especially AffGAN. The theoretical grounding is solid, and the experimental validation is thorough.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of MSE-based SR and provides a well-reasoned motivation for exploring MAP inference. The toy problem effectively illustrates the shortcomings of MSE and the benefits of MAP.
* **Novelty:** The introduction of the affine projection layer is a clever idea that elegantly addresses the constraint of consistency between LR and HR images. This architectural choice simplifies the optimization problem and directly connects to the MAP objective.
* **Empirical Results:** The results on both the 2D Swiss-roll dataset and the image SR tasks are convincing. The quantitative analysis (cross-entropy, PSNR, SSIM) and qualitative results (visual comparison of SR images) consistently show that AffGAN outperforms models trained with standard loss functions, especially in terms of perceptual quality.  The fact that AffGAN achieves cross-entropy values close to the theoretical MAP solution is particularly strong evidence of its effectiveness.
* **Theoretical Grounding:**  The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation connecting the proposed methods to MAP inference and variational inference (as demonstrated in the Appendix). The connection to GANs and the explanation of instance noise are well-articulated.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarity of Affine Projection:** While the concept of the affine projection is well-motivated, its implementation details could be clarified. A more intuitive explanation of how the deconvolution operation (A+) is determined and its relation to the downsampling kernel (A) would be helpful.
* **Ablation Studies:** While the results demonstrate AffGAN's superiority, further ablation studies could strengthen the paper. Specifically, analyzing the impact of the instance noise technique in isolation (without the affine projection) would provide valuable insights into its effectiveness.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper briefly touches on limitations in the conclusion. Expanding on these, perhaps discussing scenarios where MAP inference might not be optimal or potential drawbacks of the affine projection, would enhance the paper’s completeness.
* **Visual Comparisons:**  Providing side-by-side comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art SR methods (beyond those used in the experiments) would further contextualize the performance of AffGAN.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost associated with the proposed methods, especially compared to standard SR approaches, would be useful.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Regarding the affine projection, how sensitive is the performance to the accuracy of the pseudoinverse A+?  Is there a threshold beyond which inaccuracies in A+ significantly degrade the results?
*  The paper mentions instability issues with AffDG and AffLL. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges encountered during training and the reasons for not pursuing them further?  Were different optimization strategies explored?
*  The instance noise technique seems to be crucial for GAN training.  What is the sensitivity of the results to the annealing schedule of the noise level? Are there guidelines for selecting an appropriate schedule for different datasets?
*  In the conclusion, you mention potential issues with the MAP estimate being atypical.  Have you explored methods to sample from the posterior distribution (as suggested) and compared those results to the MAP estimate obtained with AffGAN?
* Is the performance of AffGAN consistent across different downsampling factors (e.g., 2x, 8x)?  Are there any limitations related to the scaling of the affine projection with increasing downsampling factors?



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of image super-resolution. Addressing the minor feedback points will further strengthen the paper and solidify its impact.
