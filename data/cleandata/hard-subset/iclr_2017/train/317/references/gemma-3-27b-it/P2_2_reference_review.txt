## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by moving away from pixel-wise loss functions like MSE, which are known to produce blurry results. The authors argue for employing amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, favoring solutions with high posterior probability. They propose a novel CNN architecture incorporating an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between LR and HR images, and explore three methods for approximating MAP inference: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL).  The paper demonstrates, through experiments on both a 2D toy problem and real images, that the proposed methods, especially AffGAN, achieve promising results in terms of perceptual quality and minimizing cross-entropy.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting idea and a technically sound approach, the empirical results, particularly on the real image datasets, are not convincingly superior to existing methods. The qualitative improvements claimed are not consistently supported by quantitative metrics, and the instability observed with the AffDG and AffLL methods is a significant concern.  The theoretical contribution, while interesting, doesn't fully translate to practical improvements demonstrated through sufficiently rigorous experimentation.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Limited Empirical Gains:** The paper claims improved perceptual quality with AffGAN, but this isn't consistently reflected in the quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) presented in Table 2. While the AffGAN achieves a slightly better visual result in some examples, the gains aren't substantial enough to justify the architectural complexity and training challenges.  The comparison is somewhat limited; more thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) would be crucial.
*   **Instability of AffDG and AffLL:** The authors acknowledge the training instability of AffDG and AffLL and largely discount them. This is a significant weakness.  If the proposed methods struggle to converge reliably, their practical applicability is severely limited. While the paper attempts to address this through the annealing scheme, it is not sufficient to yield competitive results.
*   **Insufficient Justification for Toy Experiment:** The initial experiment on the 2D Swiss Roll is helpful for illustrating the core concept, but it doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that the approach would generalize to complex natural images. The claim that AffGAN/AffDG closely approach the MAP solution relies on evaluating the cross-entropy on a simple, synthetic dataset.
*  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper states that the affine projection consistently improves performance, but doesn't include a detailed ablation study showing the impact of removing this component under different training setups and architectures. This makes it hard to assess the real value of this contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

*   **More Robust Evaluation:** A more comprehensive evaluation using standard SR benchmarks (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100, Urban100) and a wider range of state-of-the-art SR methods is essential.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:**  While some visual comparisons are provided, a more systematic and statistically significant subjective evaluation (e.g., using a user study) would strengthen the claim of improved perceptual quality.
*   **Investigate Instability:** A more detailed analysis of the training instability of AffDG and AffLL is needed. What specific factors contribute to the instability?  Can it be mitigated through different optimization strategies or architectural modifications?
*   **Clarify Connection to VAEs:** The connection to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) in the appendix is intriguing.  Expanding on this connection and providing more theoretical insight would be valuable.
*   **Address Limitations:**  The discussion of limitations (Section 5.6) is good, but it could be expanded to discuss the computational cost of the affine projection and its potential impact on scalability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a detailed breakdown of the training time and computational resources required for the AffGAN model compared to a similar-sized GAN trained without the affine projection?
*   What specific hyperparameters were tuned to stabilize the AffDG and AffLL models, and what sensitivity analysis was performed to ensure the results are not overly sensitive to those settings?
*   Beyond the annealing schedule, did you experiment with other techniques to mitigate the training instability of AffDG and AffLL, such as gradient clipping or spectral normalization?
*   How does the affine projection layer interact with the receptive field of the convolutional layers? Does it potentially limit the model's ability to capture long-range dependencies in the image?
