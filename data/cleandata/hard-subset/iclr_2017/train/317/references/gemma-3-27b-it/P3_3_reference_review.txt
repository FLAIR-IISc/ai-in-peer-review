## ICLR 2024 Review: Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by moving away from traditional empirical risk minimization (ERM) with pixel-wise losses (like MSE) towards an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference framework. The authors argue that ERM struggles with the inherent multi-modality of natural image distributions, leading to blurry results. They introduce a novel CNN architecture incorporating an affine projection to ensure consistency between LR and HR estimates, and then explore three methods – GAN-based, denoising-based, and density-model-based – to approximate MAP inference.  The central idea is to learn a generator that outputs HR images with high posterior probability, effectively aligning with the natural image prior. The work provides theoretical justification for using GANs in SR and explores practical techniques like "instance noise" to improve training stability.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents a conceptually interesting and theoretically grounded approach, the empirical results, particularly outside the simplified 2D Swiss roll example, don't convincingly demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods. The gains are modest, and the training instability issues (especially with the denoising and density guided methods) are not adequately addressed. The connection to variational inference, while intriguing, feels tacked on at the end and isn't fully developed or demonstrated practically.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The core claim of superior performance via MAP inference is not consistently supported by the experimental results. While the 2D Swiss roll demonstrates the principle, the gains on more complex datasets (CelebA, ImageNet textures) are relatively small. The qualitative comparisons (Figures 3, 4, and 5) are not overwhelmingly convincing, and in some cases (e.g., the snake example in Figure 5), the results are arguably *worse* than what current state-of-the-art methods would produce.  The quantitative results (Table 2) show competitive, but not surpassing, performance compared to standard methods.
* **Training Instability:** The authors acknowledge difficulties in training the denoising and density-guided models. While the "instance noise" trick seems to help with GAN training, its effectiveness on the other methods is not clearly demonstrated.  The need for extensive hyperparameter tuning and careful annealing schedules raises concerns about the robustness and practicality of the proposed approaches.  The statement that they were "unable to stable train models" without instance noise for the ImageNet experiment is a significant red flag.
* **Theoretical Connection to VI is Weak:** The appendix detailing the connection to variational inference is interesting, but it feels underdeveloped.  The authors state it leaves "emperical studies as further work" which suggests that this connection is not yet a strong justification for the proposed method.  A stronger link, perhaps with concrete experimental validation, would strengthen the paper significantly.
* **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons to MSE-trained models are included, the paper lacks a thorough comparison against modern SR methods utilizing perceptual losses (e.g., those incorporating SSIM or GANs with more advanced architectures like ESRGAN). This makes it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approaches.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify the Affine Projection's Role:** While the authors explain the theoretical motivation behind the affine projection, its practical impact on the SR process could be discussed more thoroughly.  How does it affect the receptive field? Does it introduce any artifacts? A more detailed analysis of its benefits and drawbacks would be helpful.
* **Ablation Studies:**  Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (e.g., affine projection, instance noise, different loss functions).
* **Quantitative Metrics:** Provide more comprehensive quantitative evaluations, including metrics that better correlate with perceptual quality (e.g., LPIPS) in addition to PSNR and SSIM.
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of the proposed methods compared to existing SR techniques.  The added complexity of the affine projection and the training instability could potentially make these methods less practical.
* **Address the MAP Criticism:** The authors briefly touch upon the potential limitations of MAP inference. Expanding on this discussion and exploring alternative approaches (e.g., sampling from the posterior) would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the AffGAN consistently produces sharper images than the other proposed methods (AffDG, AffLL)? What specific aspects of the GAN training process contribute to this result?
*  How sensitive are the results to the choice of noise distribution and annealing schedule for "instance noise"?  Have you explored other noise distributions beyond Gaussian?
*  Can you provide a more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art SR methods, including those utilizing perceptual losses and more advanced GAN architectures?
*  Regarding the connection to variational inference, what specific practical benefits does this connection offer? Could it potentially lead to improved sampling or uncertainty estimation in the SR process?



In conclusion, while the paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution and an interesting exploration of MAP inference for SR, the limited empirical evidence and unresolved training instability issues prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.  With significant revisions and further experimentation, this work has the potential to become a more impactful contribution to the field.
