This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by proposing methods based on amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, moving away from traditional empirical risk minimisation with pixel-wise losses like MSE. The authors argue that MAP inference, by preferring high-probability solutions under the image prior, can mitigate the blurry results often produced by MSE-trained models. They present a CNN architecture incorporating an affine projection to ensure consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, and explore three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL).

**Reject.** While the paper presents a well-motivated and technically interesting approach, the empirical results, particularly regarding the AffDG and AffLL models, are not compelling enough to justify acceptance at ICLR. The primary strength—AffGAN—shows promising results on the 2D Swiss Roll dataset and produces visually plausible, though sometimes noisy, results on real images. However, the justification for choosing AffGAN over other GAN-based SR methods is not convincingly established through rigorous comparison, and the reported quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) do not demonstrate a clear advantage. The lack of detailed ablation studies to assess the contribution of the affine projection, beyond the initial proof-of-concept experiment, further weakens the empirical validation.

The paper's core argument, grounded in Bayesian reasoning and the limitations of MSE loss, is solid and well-explained. The mathematical formulation connecting MAP inference, the affine projection, and cross-entropy minimization is clear and theoretically sound. The affine projection itself is a novel contribution, ensuring consistency between input and output, and the connection drawn between AffGAN and amortised variational inference is insightful. However, the practical impact of these theoretical advancements remains somewhat unclear due to the limited scope of the empirical evaluation.

The experimental section, while exploring different datasets (Swiss Roll, Grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet), lacks sufficient depth. The results on the Grass textures dataset are particularly inconclusive, with AffDG and AffLL yielding blurry results. The comparison with state-of-the-art SR methods is limited. The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is appreciated but doesn’t adequately address the potential drawbacks observed in the experiments (e.g., high-frequency noise, "hallucinations" in the ImageNet experiment).

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct a more thorough analysis of the affine projection. How does its removal impact the performance of the GAN and other models? Does the specific initialization of the deconvolution A+ significantly affect the results?
*   **Comparative Evaluation:** Include comparisons with more recent and widely adopted SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) using standard evaluation protocols, along with comprehensive quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS).
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide more detailed qualitative analysis, potentially including user studies or perceptual metrics (e.g., NIQE) to assess the visual quality of the generated images.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the proposed methods to various hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, noise level, and network architecture.
*   **Denoising and Density Guided Methods:**  Investigate why AffDG and AffLL models performed poorly. Could adjustments to the DAE or density model architecture, training procedure, or loss function improve their performance?
*   **Instance Noise:** Provide more details on the annealing schedule for instance noise and justify the chosen parameters.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper emphasizes the theoretical advantages of AffGAN. However, the quantitative results are not decisively superior to other GAN-based SR methods. Could you elaborate on the specific design choices in AffGAN that contribute to its performance and how they differentiate it from other approaches?
2.  The paper mentions the connection to variational inference. Could you expand on the potential benefits of viewing AffGAN through this lens and how it might inform future research directions?
3.  The “hallucination” observed in the ImageNet experiment (snake transforming into water) is intriguing. Is this a common occurrence, and what mechanisms within AffGAN might be responsible for such behaviour?
4.  Regarding the limited success of AffDG and AffLL, what steps have been taken to stabilize their training, and are there specific challenges associated with their implementation that make them less competitive than AffGAN?
5.  What is the computational cost associated with the affine projection and does it significantly impact the training time compared to standard SR models without this projection?
