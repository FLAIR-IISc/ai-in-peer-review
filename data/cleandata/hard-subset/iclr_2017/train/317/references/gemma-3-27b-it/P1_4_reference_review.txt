## ICLR 2024 Review: Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution

**1. Summary**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that directly minimizing pixel-wise losses like MSE leads to blurry results due to averaging over plausible solutions, and propose methods to directly optimize for high posterior probability. They introduce an affine projection layer that enforces consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, and explore three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL). The paper provides theoretical grounding for their approach, and demonstrates its effectiveness on both a 2D toy dataset and real image datasets.  The key contributions are the affine projection, the theoretical analysis linking GANs to MAP inference in this context, and the empirical demonstration that AffGAN outperforms standard SR methods in terms of perceptual quality.

**2. Decision**

**Reject**. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and shows promising results, particularly with AffGAN, the empirical evaluation feels incomplete and lacks a sufficiently compelling demonstration of substantial improvement over established methods, especially considering the complexity of the proposed approach. The practical benefits of the affine projection and the superiority of the proposed methods, beyond the 2D toy example, aren’t convincingly established.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The quantitative results (Table 1, Table 2) are not conclusive. While AffGAN shows improvements on the grass texture dataset, its performance on CelebA, evaluated by standard metrics like PSNR/SSIM, is comparable to or slightly worse than standard methods. The paper relies heavily on visual inspection, which is subjective. A more thorough comparison against strong baseline SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) with proper hyperparameter tuning is needed.
* **Inconsistent Claims:** The authors claim AffGAN achieves "perceptually superior" results, yet the quantitative metrics don’t fully support this. The discussion of high-frequency noise in the generated images (Section 5.5) raises concerns about the perceptual quality. The paper also points out cases where the GAN "dreams up" plausible but incorrect solutions (e.g., snake to water), which is a significant drawback.
* **Complexity vs. Benefit:** The theoretical framework and the proposed affine projection layer add significant complexity to the SR pipeline. It's unclear whether the benefits obtained justify this added complexity, particularly given the marginal improvements observed in the empirical evaluation. The ablation study (Figure 2) showing the benefit of the affine projection is helpful, but not definitive enough to justify the full complexity of the system.
* **Unclear Guidance for AffDG and AffLL:** The authors state these models produced blurry results and decided to focus on AffGAN. This raises concerns about the effectiveness of the broader theoretical framework and suggests that the core ideas aren't robustly transferable to different implementation strategies. The lack of detailed analysis and troubleshooting for these approaches weakens the overall contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement)**

* **Stronger Baselines:** Compare against state-of-the-art SR models (EDSR, RCAN, SwinIR) with properly tuned hyperparameters. Report more comprehensive quantitative metrics, including learned perceptual image patch similarity (LPIPS).
* **User Study:** A perceptual user study would be crucial to definitively demonstrate the perceptual superiority of AffGAN.
* **Ablation Study:** Conduct a more detailed ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component of AffGAN (e.g., instance noise, affine projection) and understand their interactions.
* **Address Noise Issue:** Investigate methods to reduce the high-frequency noise observed in AffGAN's output.
* **Further Analysis of AffDG and AffLL:** Provide a more thorough analysis of why AffDG and AffLL failed to perform well. Explore potential improvements or modifications to these approaches.
* **Clarify Connection to Variational Inference:** The connection to variational inference presented in Appendix F is interesting but needs further elaboration and contextualization within the broader SR literature. What practical benefits does this connection offer?
* **More Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide more details about the training process, including learning rates, batch sizes, optimizer settings, and hardware used.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  Regarding the “dreaming up” phenomenon (snake to water), what mechanisms are in place to prevent the GAN from generating outputs that are semantically incorrect, even if they are plausible?
*  Can you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian) and the annealing schedule for instance noise?
*  Why were AffDG and AffLL abandoned, and what steps have been taken to understand their limitations and explore potential remedies?
*  What is the computational overhead introduced by the affine projection layer compared to standard SR architectures?
*  Do you believe the proposed method would benefit from incorporating more advanced GAN training techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, R1 regularization)?



In conclusion, while the paper explores a promising theoretical direction for image SR, the empirical evidence is not strong enough to support its claims. A more thorough evaluation, addressing the concerns raised above, would be necessary for acceptance.
