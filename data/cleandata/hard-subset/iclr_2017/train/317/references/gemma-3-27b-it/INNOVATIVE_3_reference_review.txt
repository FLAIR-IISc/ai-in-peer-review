This paper introduces a novel approach to image super-resolution by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The core idea is compelling: instead of minimizing pixel-wise loss functions that often lead to blurry results, the authors aim to directly maximize the posterior probability of the high-resolution image given the low-resolution input, leveraging the natural image prior. A key contribution is the introduction of an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between the low and high-resolution spaces, and three distinct methods—GAN-based, denoising-based, and density-guided—to approximate this MAP inference.

**Reject.** While the core idea is exceptionally novel and potentially impactful, the paper feels incomplete. The results, while promising, are not convincingly demonstrated across all proposed methods, and the experiments lack sufficient depth to fully validate the claims. The theoretical grounding is strong, but it isn't fully translated into consistently strong empirical performance.

The strength of this paper lies in its ambitious attempt to move beyond standard SR training paradigms. The argument for MAP inference is well-motivated, and the affine projection is a clever way to enforce consistency. The exploration of three different approaches (GAN, denoising, density estimation) is commendable, showcasing a broad investigation of the core idea. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment convincingly demonstrates the ability of the AffGAN and AffDG models to find high-probability solutions where MSE/MAE fail. 

However, the empirical results on natural images are less conclusive. While AffGAN performs reasonably well, it still exhibits high-frequency noise. The denoiser-guided and density-guided approaches were largely abandoned due to poor performance, without a thorough exploration of potential improvements or further analysis. The comparisons to baseline methods are limited. Specifically, a stronger, state-of-the-art SR method (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) should be included for a more meaningful evaluation.  The quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) don't tell the whole story, and a more comprehensive perceptual evaluation (e.g., using a user study or more advanced perceptual metrics) would be beneficial. The connection to variational inference, established in the appendix, is intriguing but underdeveloped, lacking concrete experimental validation. The discussion around the limitations of MAP inference, while present, could be expanded. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of the affine projection layer. How much does it contribute to performance gains?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the models to different hyperparameters, particularly those related to the GAN training process (e.g., learning rates, batch sizes, noise levels).
*   **Denoising and Density Guided Methods:** Revisit the denoising and density-guided methods. Perhaps explore different network architectures or training strategies to improve their performance. What specifically caused them to fail?
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of the proposed methods compared to traditional SR approaches.
*   **Variational Inference:** Elaborate on the connection to variational inference. Could the stochastic AffGAN be used as a variational autoencoder? Explore this connection empirically.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide more detailed qualitative analysis of the generated images, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each method. Show more examples, including failure cases.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Why do you think the AffGAN model still produces high-frequency noise, and what steps could be taken to mitigate this issue?
*   What were the primary challenges encountered when training the denoiser-guided and density-guided models, and what specific modifications were attempted to address these challenges?
*   Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with the affine projection layer and the overall training process?
*   How do you envision the connection to variational inference being further explored and utilized in future work?
