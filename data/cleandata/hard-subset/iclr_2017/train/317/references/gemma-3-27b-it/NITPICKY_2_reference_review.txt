This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the true data distribution leads to more plausible results than traditional pixel-wise loss functions like MSE. The authors introduce an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, and explore three methods – GAN-based, denoising-based, and density-guided – to approximate MAP inference. The core idea of aligning the generated distribution with the data prior is compelling and potentially valuable.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and attempts to connect GANs to variational inference, the implementation details are insufficiently robust, the experimental validation is lacking in rigor, and the presentation contains numerous minor but distracting flaws. The numerous issues, even if individually small, collectively undermine confidence in the reported results and the overall contribution.

The primary concern is the lack of convincing empirical evidence. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment, while illustrative, is overly simplistic and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods on complex image data. While the authors show results on grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet, the comparisons are often unclear or incomplete. For example, the claim that AffGAN produces “slightly sharper images” than SoftGAN on CelebA lacks quantitative support beyond PSNR/SSIM, which are acknowledged to be poor perceptual metrics. More importantly, the AffGAN model still generates high-frequency noise, a common GAN artifact, suggesting the method isn't fully addressing the core issues of perceptual quality. The reported inconsistencies in training stability, requiring tricks like instance noise, further point to implementation challenges. The dismissal of the denoiser and density guided methods without thorough investigation is also concerning. Simply stating they "did not improve" isn't sufficient justification without detailed analysis of why they failed.

Further, the paper suffers from numerous minor issues. There are several grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout. Table 1 lacks sufficient precision (e.g., reporting values to two decimal places when the standard deviation is unclear). The notation is inconsistent (e.g., varying uses of subscripts and superscripts). The figure captions are often uninformative. Appendix B, detailing the pseudo-inverse estimation, is crucial but lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. The connection to variational inference in Appendix F feels tacked on and isn't thoroughly explored. The theoretical motivations, while interesting, are not consistently applied across the different proposed methods. The claim that the affine projection "always guarantees" consistency feels strong without a more rigorous mathematical treatment, especially when considering numerical implementations.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide more detailed information about the network architectures, hyperparameter settings, training procedures, and implementation details to facilitate reproducibility. Code release would be highly beneficial.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** Conduct a more thorough quantitative evaluation using a wider range of metrics, including perceptual metrics like LPIPS, and perform statistically significant comparisons with state-of-the-art SR methods.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the affine projection layer and other key components of the proposed methods.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide a detailed error analysis of the generated images, identifying common failure cases and potential areas for improvement.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** Revise the paper for clarity, conciseness, and grammatical correctness. Improve the figure captions and provide more informative tables.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of hyperparameters used in the experiments?
*   What specific changes were made to address the training instability observed in the AffGAN model, and what impact did these changes have on the results?
*   Why were the denoiser-guided and density-guided methods abandoned without more extensive investigation?
*   What is the computational cost associated with the affine projection layer compared to traditional SR methods?
*   How does the performance of the proposed methods scale to higher upscaling factors (e.g., 8x)?
*   Could you provide more insight into the mathematical connection between AffGAN and variational inference, going beyond the brief mention in Appendix F?
