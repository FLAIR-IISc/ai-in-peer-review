This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, motivated by the limitations of pixel-wise loss functions like MSE which can lead to blurry results. The core idea is to leverage natural image statistics and design a CNN architecture that projects solutions to an affine subspace consistent with the low-resolution input. The authors then explore three approaches to minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the HR data distribution: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL).

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the motivation is sound and the theoretical framework is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods, particularly AffGAN, over existing approaches. The experimental results are presented in a fragmented and difficult-to-interpret manner, and the gains, where observed, are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the approach.

Here's a breakdown of my reasoning:

1. **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while covering a range of datasets (Swiss-roll, grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet), feel incomplete and lack rigorous comparison. The quantitative results (e.g., Table 1, Table 2) are often presented without sufficient context. For example, Table 2 shows PSNR/SSIM scores, but doesn’t provide standard deviations or comparisons to state-of-the-art SR methods beyond MSE. The claim of perceptual superiority is supported primarily by visual examples, which are subjective and don't offer definitive proof. The results on the CelebA and ImageNet datasets are particularly underwhelming – the improvement over MSE-trained models appears marginal and often accompanied by artifacts (high-frequency noise).

2. **Unclear Advantages of Affine Projection:** The paper emphasizes the importance of the affine projection layer, arguing it ensures consistency and improves performance. However, the benefits of this projection aren’t consistently demonstrated. The experiments in Section 5.2 show some improvement with the projection when using MSE, but this isn't replicated as strongly with the GAN-based approach. The reasons for this discrepancy are not explored. The statement that "the affine projected networks generally train faster" is not substantiated by timing data or learning curves.

3. **Fragmented Presentation & Lack of Clarity:** The paper is dense and difficult to follow. The transition between theoretical motivation and experimental results is often abrupt. The explanation of the three proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) feels rushed, and the reasons why AffGAN ultimately becomes the focus are not clearly articulated. The numerous references to appendices (A, B, C, D, E, F) create a disjointed reading experience. The discussion in section 5.6, while interesting, feels tacked on and doesn't fully address potential limitations of the MAP inference approach.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

* **Comprehensive Comparison:** Include comparisons against state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN, RDN) using standard metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) and, crucially, perceptual studies (e.g., user preference tests).
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to isolate the impact of the affine projection layer. Evaluate its performance with different network architectures and loss functions.
* **Learning Curves:** Provide learning curves to demonstrate the training dynamics of the different methods and justify claims about faster convergence.
* **Qualitative Analysis:** Instead of just presenting visual examples, conduct a more rigorous qualitative analysis, perhaps using metrics like FID or KID, to quantify the perceptual quality of the generated images.
* **Clarity and Organization:** Restructure the paper to improve clarity and flow. Streamline the explanation of the methods and provide more context for the experimental results. Avoid excessive reliance on appendices; integrate key details directly into the main text.
* **Investigate AffLL and AffDG:** Provide more detailed analysis of the performance of the AffLL and AffDG models and explain why they didn’t achieve results comparable to AffGAN.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Why does the affine projection layer not consistently lead to performance improvements across all experiments and loss functions?
2. Can you provide a more detailed justification for focusing on AffGAN over AffDG and AffLL? What specific limitations were encountered with the latter two approaches?
3. What is the computational overhead of incorporating the affine projection layer into the CNN architecture?
4. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the Gaussian kernel used for downsampling (A)? Have you experimented with other downsampling methods?
5. Could you elaborate on the potential for variational inference with the stochastic version of AffGAN, as mentioned in Appendix F? What specific advantages might this offer over the current approach?
