This paper proposes methods for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the HR data distribution leads to more plausible results than traditional pixel-wise loss functions like MSE. The authors introduce an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between LR and HR estimates and explore three approaches – GANs, denoising, and density modeling – to approximate the cross-entropy minimization.

**Reject.** While the core idea of framing SR as MAP inference and the introduction of the affine projection layer are interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings in presentation, experimental rigor, and justification of design choices. The review feels unnecessarily harsh and focuses on minor details, but this reflects a belief that the paper, as presented, does not meet the standards expected for ICLR publication.

The first, and most significant, issue is the lack of clarity and justification surrounding key methodological decisions. The reasoning behind choosing specific network architectures (e.g., layer numbers, filter sizes) is absent. The choice of the MCGSM for density estimation is presented without comparison to other, potentially more suitable, generative models.  The "instance noise" trick is introduced as a solution to GAN instability but is described vaguely and lacks a thorough ablation study demonstrating its effectiveness. The claim that it addresses the core issue of non-overlapping support between distributions requires more detailed explanation.  The abrupt dismissal of the AffDG and AffLL models after initial experiments is concerning, without sufficient analysis of *why* they failed to perform well. The explanation of the connection between the AffGAN and variational inference in Appendix F feels tacked on and lacks substantial elaboration.

Secondly, the experimental evaluation is flawed and often lacks sufficient detail.  The 2D Swiss-roll experiment, while useful for illustrating the principle, feels overly simplistic and doesn’t adequately translate to the complexities of natural images.  The results on grass textures are presented with limited quantitative analysis. The CelebA and ImageNet experiments show visual results, but the quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) favor the MSE model, directly contradicting the paper’s central premise. This discrepancy is not adequately addressed.  The claim that AffGAN produces "slightly sharper images" is subjective and unsupported by quantitative metrics. The statement about color drifting in the soft constrained models needs quantification.  Crucially, there’s no comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) to demonstrate the proposed approach’s competitive performance. The ablation studies are insufficient – for example, demonstrating the importance of initializing A+ correctly, but not exploring alternative initialization strategies.

Finally, the paper is riddled with minor technical inconsistencies and stylistic issues.  Equations are not always clearly explained. The notation is occasionally ambiguous.  The writing is verbose and contains unnecessary jargon.  There are instances of grammatical errors and awkward phrasing.  The references, while extensive, are not always integrated seamlessly into the text. The consistent use of phrases like “as expected” or “as shown” without providing supporting evidence is detrimental.  The organization of the results section is not optimal, jumping between different datasets without a clear narrative flow.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Justify architectural choices:** Provide a rationale for the specific CNN architectures used, including layer numbers, filter sizes, and activation functions.
*   **Thorough ablation studies:** Conduct extensive ablation studies to evaluate the impact of each component of the proposed methods (e.g., affine projection, instance noise).
*   **Comparison to state-of-the-art:** Compare the proposed approach to state-of-the-art SR methods (EDSR, RCAN, etc.) using standard evaluation metrics.
*   **Quantitative analysis:** Provide more rigorous quantitative analysis of the results, including statistical significance tests.
*   **Clarify the connection to variational inference:** Expand on the connection between AffGAN and variational inference in Appendix F, providing a more detailed mathematical derivation and discussing its implications.
*   **Address the discrepancy between perceptual quality and quantitative metrics:** Explain why the quantitative metrics favor the MSE model despite the claimed perceptual benefits of the proposed methods.
*   **Rewrite for clarity and conciseness:** Revise the paper for clarity, conciseness, and grammatical correctness.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Why were the AffDG and AffLL models abandoned after initial experiments? What specific issues prevented them from achieving competitive performance?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the noise level in the instance noise trick?
*   How does the affine projection layer affect the computational complexity of the SR model?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical justification for the instance noise trick, specifically how it addresses the issue of non-overlapping support between distributions?
*   Given the quantitative results favoring MSE, how can you confidently claim that the proposed methods produce perceptually superior results? Have you conducted any user studies to validate this claim?
