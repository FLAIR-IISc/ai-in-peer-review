This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that traditional loss functions like MSE lead to blurry results by averaging over plausible solutions, and instead advocate for finding the mode of the posterior distribution, which is more likely to align with the natural image prior. To achieve this, they introduce an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between the low-resolution input and the high-resolution output, and explore three methods for training: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The work is accompanied by a theoretical analysis connecting AffGAN to variational inference.

**Reject.** While the core idea of pursuing MAP inference for SR is intriguing and potentially impactful, the paper feels somewhat incomplete and lacks sufficient empirical validation to justify acceptance. The theoretical justification is strong, but the experimental results, while promising in the 2D toy example, are less convincing in the image SR tasks. The core contribution, AffGAN, shows some qualitative improvement but isn't demonstrably superior to existing GAN-based SR methods, and the ablation studies and comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods are limited. The abandonment of AffDG and AffLL after initial tests further weakens the overall impact.

The strength of this paper lies in the novelty of its perspective. Formulating SR as MAP inference is a refreshing departure from standard empirical risk minimization, and the affine projection layer is a clever way to enforce consistency. The connection to variational inference is also a significant theoretical contribution. However, the empirical results fail to fully capitalize on this strong foundation.

Specifically:

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The paper predominantly compares against MSE, which is a weak baseline. Comparisons to more recent and performant SR methods, such as EDSR, RCAN, or other GAN-based approaches (e.g., SRGAN), are crucial to establish the effectiveness of AffGAN.
*   **Qualitative Focus:** The results are heavily reliant on visual inspection. While perceptual quality is important, quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) should be reported more comprehensively and compared fairly against existing methods. The authors note that AffGAN produces "slightly sharper images" but this needs to be substantiated with numbers.
*   **Unclear Ablation Studies:** The experiment in Section 5.2 demonstrates the benefit of the affine projection but lacks sufficient detail about the architectural choices and training parameters.
*   **Incomplete Exploration of Methods:** The premature dismissal of AffDG and AffLL is a concern. Were these methods truly unsuccessful, or were they simply not pursued with the same vigor as AffGAN? Further investigation into these approaches could reveal valuable insights.
*   **Theoretical Claims vs. Empirical Evidence:** The connection to variational inference is presented as a key finding, but its practical implications for SR are not explored. How does this connection inform the design or training of the model?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6) is valuable and demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the approach.
*   The instance noise trick seems promising and deserves more attention. A more detailed ablation study on the impact of this technique would be beneficial.
*   The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the role of the affine projection in the context of the overall generative modeling framework.
*   More detailed training details (hyperparameters, optimization algorithms, etc.) would enhance reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How do the quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) of AffGAN compare to state-of-the-art SR methods on standard datasets (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100)?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why AffDG and AffLL were abandoned, and what steps were taken to optimize their performance?
*   What are the potential benefits of the connection to variational inference for the design or training of the AffGAN model?
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the Gaussian kernel for the affine projection, and have you experimented with other kernel functions?
*   How sensitive is the performance of AffGAN to the noise level in the instance noise trick?
