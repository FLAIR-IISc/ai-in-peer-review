This paper proposes methods for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, motivated by the limitations of pixel-wise loss functions like MSE. The authors argue that MAP inference, favoring solutions with high posterior probability, leads to more plausible reconstructions. They introduce an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between LR and HR images and explore three approaches for MAP inference: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL). The paper provides a theoretical grounding for using GANs in SR and presents experimental results on 2D toy data and natural images.

**Reject.** While the paper explores an interesting direction – moving beyond purely loss-driven SR towards a more probabilistic framework – the level of detail and rigor throughout is insufficient, and numerous minor flaws accumulate to a substantial concern. The experiments, while presented, lack thoroughness, and the claims often aren't convincingly supported. The writing is also occasionally unclear and suffers from redundancy.

The primary issue lies with the lack of compelling evidence that the proposed methods *consistently* outperform existing techniques. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment is useful for illustrating the core idea, but its relevance to realistic image SR is limited. The results on natural images are mixed, with AffGAN showing some promise but also exhibiting high-frequency noise. The quantitative results (Table 2) actually show *lower* PSNR/SSIM for AffGAN compared to the MSE method, directly contradicting the paper’s motivation. The ablation studies (Figures 2 and 7) are not sufficiently detailed to fully understand the impact of the affine projection or the various training strategies. The claims of superior performance are often qualified ("slightly sharper", "tends to find a better solution") and lack statistical significance.

Furthermore, the theoretical contributions are not fully fleshed out. The connection to variational inference, while intriguing (Appendix F), is presented as a "first step" and doesn't offer a concrete, immediately usable algorithm. The discussion of MAP inference limitations (Section 5.6) feels tacked on and doesn't adequately address the potential pitfalls of the approach. Numerous minor methodological points raise concerns, such as the specific implementation of the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse (Appendix B), the annealing schedule for instance noise (Appendix C), and the choice of hyperparameters. The writing also contains inconsistencies; for instance, the paper states "the AffGAN and AffDG solutions largely fit the dominant mode similar to MAP inference" yet later acknowledges AffGAN can "dream up" plausible but incorrect reconstructions (e.g., snake to water).

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Experimentation:** Conduct a more comprehensive evaluation on standard SR datasets (e.g., Set5, Set14, BSD100) using established evaluation protocols. Include comparisons to state-of-the-art SR methods, including recent GAN-based approaches and transformer-based models.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform more thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of each component (affine projection, instance noise, different loss functions). Quantify the benefits of the affine projection with statistical significance.
*   **Theoretical Clarity:** Elaborate on the theoretical connections between the proposed methods and existing probabilistic modeling frameworks. Provide a more rigorous justification for the choice of specific likelihood models and prior distributions.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the robustness of the methods to different types of noise and artifacts in the input images.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Writing and Presentation:** Proofread the paper carefully for clarity, conciseness, and consistency. Remove redundant phrases and clarify ambiguous statements. Ensure that figures and tables are properly labeled and easy to understand.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Given the lower PSNR/SSIM scores in Table 2 for AffGAN compared to the MSE method, can you provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between perceptual quality and quantitative metrics in your experiments?
2.  The affine projection layer is a key component of your approach. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the choice of the downsampling kernel A affects the performance of the model?
3.  The claim that AffGAN "dreams up" plausible but incorrect reconstructions is concerning. How can this be mitigated, and what steps can be taken to ensure that the generated images are faithful to the input data?
4.  The connection to variational inference (Appendix F) is intriguing. Can you elaborate on how this connection could be exploited to develop more robust and efficient SR algorithms?
5.  The noise annealing schedule in AffDG and AffGAN is not fully described. What specific criteria were used to determine the optimal annealing rate, and how sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?
