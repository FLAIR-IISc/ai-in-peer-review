This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution, arguing that directly minimizing pixel-wise losses like MSE leads to blurry results due to ignoring the underlying image prior. The authors propose a shift towards amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, enforcing consistency between low- and high-resolution images through an affine projection layer within a convolutional neural network. They explore three approaches to achieve this: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL).

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting and theoretically motivated idea – explicitly aiming for MAP inference in SR – the empirical results, particularly beyond the simple 2D Swiss-roll example, don’t convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods. The central contribution, the affine projection, seems more like a clever constraint than a breakthrough, and the gains observed are often marginal or accompanied by artifacts (e.g., high-frequency noise). The instability issues with AffDG and AffLL are also concerning.

Here’s a breakdown of my reasoning:

1. **Novelty vs. Impact:** The core idea of framing SR as MAP inference is intriguing and represents a shift in perspective. However, the implementation feels somewhat incremental. Existing GAN-based SR methods (e.g., SRGAN) already implicitly try to address the limitations of pixel-wise losses, and the presented AffGAN doesn’t demonstrate a clear, consistent perceptual improvement over them. The affine projection, while clever, feels like a technical detail rather than a fundamental advance. 
2. **Empirical Validation:** The 2D Swiss-roll experiment strongly supports the theoretical argument. However, the results on real images (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) are less compelling. While the authors show some qualitative improvements with AffGAN, the quantitative results (PSNR/SSIM) don’t consistently favor it over standard approaches. The claim of "slightly sharper images" in CelebA is subjective and not backed by convincing quantitative data. The appearance of plausible but incorrect reconstructions (snake becoming water) highlights a potential mode-collapse or overfitting issue.
3. **Methodological Concerns:** The training instability of AffDG and AffLL is a significant weakness. The ad-hoc solutions (annealing, continuous loading of parameters) suggest the approach isn’t robust. The authors mention instance noise as a stabilization technique, but its effectiveness isn’t thoroughly evaluated, and the justification feels somewhat hand-wavy. The dependence on a pre-trained DAE or density model also adds complexity and potential sources of error.
4. **Lack of Comparison:** A more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN, Real-ESRGAN) is needed. Simply stating that the AffGAN produces “plausible” results isn’t sufficient; it needs to be demonstrated that it outperforms these methods in terms of perceptual quality and/or PSNR/SSIM.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study is needed to isolate the impact of the affine projection. How much of the observed improvement (if any) is attributable to this constraint versus other aspects of the network architecture or training procedure?
* **Perceptual Metrics:** Relying solely on PSNR/SSIM is insufficient. The authors should include more sophisticated perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) to better assess the visual quality of the generated images.
* **Qualitative Evaluation:** Provide more detailed qualitative analysis, including comparisons to other methods, and a discussion of the types of artifacts that are generated by each approach. Include failure cases.
* **Theoretical Connection to VAEs:** The connection to variational inference in the appendix is interesting but underdeveloped. Expanding on this could strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work.
* **Clarify the role of the affine projection:** The paper states that the affine projection “ensures that our CNNs always output estimates that are consistent with the inputs.” What does “consistent” really mean in the context of real images? Is this consistency truly beneficial, or does it overly constrain the network and prevent it from generating realistic details?

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the high-frequency noise observed in the AffGAN outputs? Is this an inherent limitation of the approach, or can it be mitigated with further optimization?
* Why did the AffDG and AffLL models exhibit training instability? What specific hyperparameters or architectural choices were found to be most critical for stabilizing their training?
* How does the affine projection affect the receptive field of the network? Does it limit the network’s ability to capture long-range dependencies in the image?
* What is the computational cost associated with the affine projection layer? Does it significantly increase the training or inference time?
