This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution, arguing that existing methods relying on pixel-wise losses fall short due to their inability to capture the multimodal nature of plausible HR images given a single LR input. The authors propose a shift towards amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, framing SR as a problem of finding the most probable HR image consistent with the LR observation and a natural image prior. A key contribution is the introduction of an affine projection layer that enforces consistency between LR and HR images, simplifying the MAP inference objective. They explore three approaches to realizing this: GAN-based methods (AffGAN), denoising-guided methods (AffDG), and density-guided methods (AffLL).

**Reject.** While the idea of framing SR as MAP inference is intriguing and the affine projection is a clever observation, the paper feels somewhat exploratory and lacks the depth of validation needed for acceptance. The results, while promising, are not definitively convincing that the proposed methods outperform existing state-of-the-art approaches, and the ablation studies, while present, could be more rigorous. The paper introduces several ideas, but doesn't fully commit to and thoroughly explore the most promising one (AffGAN).

The core strength lies in the theoretical motivation – the Swiss-roll toy example effectively illustrates the limitations of MSE loss. The affine projection is also a valuable contribution, ensuring consistency and potentially simplifying the learning process. The connection drawn between AffGAN and variational inference in the appendix is interesting, hinting at a deeper theoretical grounding.

However, the empirical evaluation feels fragmented. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment is convincing for demonstrating the principle, but the results on natural images are less clear-cut. While AffGAN achieves visually plausible results, the quantitative comparisons (PSNR, SSIM) don’t consistently demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods. The paper mentions perceptual quality, but lacks a rigorous perceptual evaluation (e.g., using a user study or a more sophisticated perceptual metric than PSNR/SSIM). The abandonment of AffDG and AffLL after initial results is understandable, but leaves a sense of incompleteness. The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference, while appreciated, feels somewhat tacked on and doesn’t fully address potential drawbacks. The claims of improved training speed with the affine projection require more detailed analysis and benchmarking. The instance noise trick seems promising, but its impact isn't fully explored.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Focus:** The paper would benefit from a more focused approach. Instead of presenting three different methods, concentrate on AffGAN and explore its potential more thoroughly. A more comprehensive ablation study, systematically evaluating the impact of the affine projection and instance noise, would be valuable.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** Strengthen the quantitative evaluation by including comparisons with more recent and state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN). Consider reporting confidence intervals for the results.
*   **Perceptual Evaluation:**  Include a proper perceptual evaluation, such as a user study, to assess the visual quality of the generated images.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Elaborate on the theoretical implications of the affine projection. Does it impose any limitations on the expressiveness of the network? How does it interact with the choice of architecture?
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed methods, including training time and inference speed.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   You mention that AffGAN produces slightly sharper images but also more high-frequency noise. Can you quantify this trade-off and investigate techniques to mitigate the noise without sacrificing sharpness?
*   Why did you choose to abandon the AffDG and AffLL approaches? Were there specific challenges that prevented you from achieving good results with these methods?
*   The paper briefly touches on the limitations of MAP inference. Could you elaborate on how these limitations might affect the performance of the proposed methods in specific scenarios?
*   What is the impact of the affine projection on the receptive field of the network? Does it limit the network's ability to capture long-range dependencies?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the smoothing kernel used for downsampling and the corresponding deconvolution kernel (A+)?
