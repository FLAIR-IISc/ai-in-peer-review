This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, aiming to avoid the perceptual limitations of methods relying on pixel-wise losses like MSE. The authors introduce an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between LR and HR images and explore three methods – GAN-based, denoising-based, and density-guided – to approximate MAP inference. The core idea is to learn a generator that produces HR images with high posterior probability under the image prior.

**Reject.** While the motivation for moving beyond MSE-based SR is sound, and the theoretical justification for MAP inference is well-presented, the empirical evidence provided is insufficient to support the claims of significant perceptual improvement. The paper lacks a comprehensive and rigorous comparison against established SR methods, particularly more recent GAN-based approaches, and the reported results on real images are not compelling enough to justify the complexity of the proposed framework.

The paper’s key weakness lies in its evaluation. The 2D Swiss-roll example is a good illustration of the concept, but it’s a toy problem. The results on grass textures, while showing some improvement, aren't decisive and lack quantitative comparison to baseline methods. The CelebA and ImageNet experiments suffer from the same issue. While the authors acknowledge the presence of high-frequency noise in the GAN outputs, there’s no attempt to mitigate it or demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art SR techniques. The tables primarily show PSNR and SSIM which, as the authors themselves concede in the introduction, are imperfect perceptual metrics. Relying on these alone doesn’t validate the claim of improved *perceptual* quality. The limited ablation study (comparing with and without the affine projection) is a positive step, but more thorough comparisons are needed. The discussion of AffDG and AffLL is largely dismissive, claiming they don't perform well without offering sufficient investigation or attempted improvements beyond the initial setup. The claim of "establishing a connection between GANs and variational inference" in the appendix feels tacked on and doesn't fundamentally strengthen the core contribution.

The writing is generally clear, and the theoretical motivation is well-explained. However, the paper is dense and could benefit from more concise explanations of the mathematical formulations. The connection to variational inference, while interesting, requires further elaboration and justification to be considered a significant contribution.

**Additional Feedback & Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Quantitative Perceptual Metrics:** Include more sophisticated perceptual metrics like LPIPS or NIQE alongside PSNR/SSIM for a more robust evaluation.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** Compare AffGAN to recent, strong SR GANs (e.g., ESRGAN, Real-ESRGAN) using consistent evaluation protocols. Provide a quantitative comparison (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) and a subjective visual comparison.
*   **Ablation Study:** Perform a more comprehensive ablation study, systematically investigating the impact of different components of the AffGAN architecture (e.g., the instance noise, the affine projection).
*   **Noise Analysis:** Characterize the high-frequency noise observed in the GAN outputs. Is it a common artifact of GANs, or is it specific to the proposed method? Are there techniques to reduce it (e.g., regularization)?
*   **AffDG and AffLL:** Investigate the difficulties encountered with AffDG and AffLL in more detail. Provide specific details about the training process, hyperparameter settings, and potential reasons for their poor performance. Were different architectures or training procedures explored?
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of the proposed methods compared to existing SR approaches. Is the affine projection layer computationally expensive?
*   **Clarify the connection to Variational Inference:** Expand on the implications of the connection to variational inference. What new insights or benefits does this connection provide? How can this be leveraged to further improve the performance of the model?
*   **Regarding the MAP critique:** While the authors acknowledge the non-uniqueness of MAP solutions, the paper doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the results to different representations.
