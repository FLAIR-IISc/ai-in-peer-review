## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by proposing a shift from empirical risk minimization (typically using MSE) to an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference framework. The authors argue that MAP inference leads to more plausible and higher-probability image reconstructions by favoring solutions consistent with the natural image prior. They introduce a novel architectural constraint – an affine projection layer – that ensures consistency between low-resolution input and high-resolution output.  Furthermore, they present three methods for approximating MAP inference: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) variant (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The authors demonstrate, through both a 2D toy example and experiments on real image datasets, that their proposed methods can indeed minimize cross-entropy and produce perceptually superior SR results compared to standard techniques.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and a potentially valuable architectural constraint, the empirical results are not compelling enough to warrant acceptance. The improvements over baseline methods (especially in the image datasets) are not substantial enough, and the experiments lack sufficient detail and comparative analysis to confidently claim perceptual superiority.  Furthermore, the presentation, while ambitious, is dense and could benefit from clearer explanations of key concepts.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Empirical Gains:** While the 2D Swiss-roll experiment effectively demonstrates the theoretical advantages of MAP inference, the results on real image datasets (CelebA and ImageNet) are less convincing. AffGAN produces slightly sharper images, but the quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) do not show a significant advantage over standard approaches.  The qualitative comparisons are also subjective and don’t offer a clear win for the proposed methods.
* **Insufficient Ablation & Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to baseline methods is limited. While MSE is used as a benchmark, the paper lacks a comparison to more modern SR techniques like EDSR, RCAN, or SRGAN (beyond the soft-constrained GAN versions).  A more thorough ablation study, isolating the effect of the affine projection layer and each of the three MAP inference methods, is needed.  For example, how much does the affine projection layer contribute *without* the adversarial or denoising loss?
* **Unclear Justification for Architectural Choices:** While the affine projection layer is well-motivated theoretically, the details of the network architecture for AffGAN, AffDG, and AffLL are not sufficiently justified. Why were specific layer numbers, filter sizes, and activation functions chosen?  A more systematic exploration of these parameters could lead to improved performance.
* **Density Model Implementation & Limitations:** The choice of MCGSM as the density model seems justified. However, the paper acknowledges its potential limitations and the fact that the log-likelihood doesn't fully capture the data distribution, potentially hindering the performance of AffLL. This acknowledgment, while honest, raises concerns about the reliability of this approach.
* **Training Instability & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on “instance noise” to stabilize GAN training and the complex annealing schedule (mentioned in Appendix D) suggest the method is highly sensitive to hyperparameters.  This sensitivity is a practical concern and should be discussed more thoroughly.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is very dense and theoretically heavy. Consider breaking down complex concepts into smaller, more digestible chunks. Visual aids (diagrams, illustrations) could significantly enhance understanding.
* **Mathematical Notation:**  Ensure consistent and clear mathematical notation throughout the paper. Some equations could benefit from additional explanation.
* **Experiment Details:** Provide *much* more detail about the experimental setup. Specify the exact hyperparameters used for each experiment, the training schedule, and the hardware used. This is crucial for reproducibility.
* **Code Release:**  Releasing the code would significantly strengthen the paper and allow the community to verify the results and build upon the work.
* **Address the MAP limitations:** The discussion of limitations in Section 5.6 is valuable. Expand on the consequences of choosing a MAP estimate, particularly in the context of image SR.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond qualitative observations, can you provide a more objective evaluation of the perceptual quality of the generated images?  Perhaps using a learned perceptual metric (LPIPS) or conducting a user study?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the noise distribution and annealing schedule in the "instance noise" approach? Can you provide an ablation study demonstrating the effect of these parameters?
3.  Can you elaborate on why the affine projection layer sometimes leads to slightly *worse* quantitative performance (e.g., lower PSNR)? Is this a trade-off between perceptual quality and pixel-wise accuracy?
4.  You mention a connection to variational inference in Appendix F. Could you elaborate on the specific advantages and disadvantages of this connection, and how it might inform future research directions?
5.  How does the computational cost of the proposed methods compare to that of state-of-the-art SR techniques like EDSR or RCAN?



In conclusion, while the ideas presented in this paper are interesting and potentially promising, the lack of strong empirical evidence and the presentation issues prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.  With significant revisions and more rigorous experimentation, this work could become a valuable contribution to the field of image super-resolution.
