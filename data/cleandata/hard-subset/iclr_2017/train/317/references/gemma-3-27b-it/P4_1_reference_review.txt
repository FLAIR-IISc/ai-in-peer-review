This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem, aiming to generate plausible high-resolution images given low-resolution inputs, avoiding the typical blurry results of MSE-based methods. The approach is well-motivated, recognizing the limitations of pixel-wise losses and the benefits of aligning with the natural image prior. The paper builds upon existing work in GANs and denoising, adapting these techniques to directly optimize for MAP inference, a novel direction.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper. While the theoretical motivation is strong and the proposed methods are interesting, the empirical results presented lack sufficient clarity and compelling evidence to support the claims of significant improvement. The experimental setup feels fragmented and the benefits of the affine projection, a core contribution, are not convincingly demonstrated beyond the simple Swiss roll example.

The main arguments supporting this decision are:

1. **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The paper jumps between a toy 2D example, grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet images. While this explores different data types, the comparisons are not consistently thorough. The most convincing results (cross-entropy reduction in the Swiss roll) are for a highly controlled scenario. The results on natural images (CelebA and ImageNet) are presented visually, without robust quantitative metrics beyond PSNR and SSIM, which the authors themselves acknowledge are imperfect. A stronger comparison against state-of-the-art SR methods with appropriate metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID) is crucial. The claim that AffGAN produces “sharper” images is subjective without perceptual quality metrics.

2. **Unclear Benefit of Affine Projection:** The paper heavily emphasizes the affine projection layer as a key innovation. However, the empirical evidence demonstrating its advantage isn’t conclusive. The results in Figure 2 suggest a benefit, but the improvement appears marginal, and the explanation focuses on enforcing consistency rather than perceptual improvement. The AffDG and AffLL models are quickly dismissed due to blurry results, weakening the overall contribution. It's unclear if the instability issues are inherent to the method or simply due to hyperparameter tuning challenges. The fact that the paper moves the focus to AffGAN after these initial results suggests a lack of confidence in the other approaches.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

* **Quantitative Evaluation:** Provide a more comprehensive quantitative evaluation using state-of-the-art SR metrics (LPIPS, FID) on CelebA and ImageNet. Compare AffGAN against strong baselines (e.g., EDSR, RCAN, SRGAN) with fair comparisons (similar model size, training data).
* **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to rigorously assess the contribution of each component, especially the affine projection.  What is the performance gain solely from the affine projection, compared to a similar model *without* it, but trained with the same loss function?
* **Clarify Experimental Setup:** The description of the experimental setup is somewhat vague. Provide more details about the network architectures (number of layers, filter sizes), training parameters (learning rate, batch size, optimizer), and data preprocessing steps.
* **Address Criticisms:** The discussion of limitations in Section 5.6 is good, but could be expanded. Acknowledge the potential for the MAP estimate to be atypical and address how this might affect the perceived quality of the generated images.
* **Explain DAE Training:** The description of the denoising autoencoder (DAE) training is unclear. How was the noise level annealed? What are the specific hyperparameters used?
* **Figures:** Improve the clarity of the figures. For example, in Figure 3, the visual comparison is difficult to interpret due to the small image sizes and lack of a clear scale.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide a more detailed quantitative comparison of AffGAN against state-of-the-art SR methods using standard evaluation metrics (LPIPS, FID)?
* What is the computational overhead introduced by the affine projection layer? Does it significantly increase the training time or memory requirements?
* Could you elaborate on the challenges encountered when training AffDG and AffLL, and why they didn't perform as well as AffGAN?
* What specific architectural choices or hyperparameters were critical for achieving stable training with AffGAN?
