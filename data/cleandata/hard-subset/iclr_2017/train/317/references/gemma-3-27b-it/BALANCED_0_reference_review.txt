This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR), moving beyond pixel-wise losses like MSE and exploring methods based on natural image statistics. The core idea is to employ amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, favoring solutions with high posterior probability. The authors motivate this by demonstrating how MSE can lead to implausible solutions in a simple toy problem. They propose several approaches to implement this MAP inference, including a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL), all incorporating an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between LR and HR estimates.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and proposes novel approaches to SR, the empirical results, particularly those beyond the 2D Swiss-roll example, are not compelling enough to warrant acceptance. Specifically, the improvements over baseline GAN models (SoftGAN) and MSE-trained networks are often marginal, and the reported qualitative improvements are not consistently demonstrable. The paper feels somewhat incomplete in its experimental validation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Advantage:** The most significant results are presented in Section 5.1 with the Swiss-roll example, which convincingly demonstrates the benefit of MAP inference. However, this benefit doesn’t fully translate to real-world image SR. While the authors report perceptual improvements for AffGAN (e.g., in the grass texture example), these are not consistently confirmed across datasets (CelebA, ImageNet). Quantitative metrics like PSNR and SSIM often show comparable or even inferior performance compared to standard GAN approaches.

2. **Unclear Justification for Design Choices:** The choice of specific architectures (number of layers, filter sizes) seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic ablation study investigating the impact of different architectural choices and hyperparameters would strengthen the claims.  The authors mention difficulties training AffDG and AffLL, ultimately focusing on AffGAN. A more thorough investigation of these methods, or a clearer explanation of why they failed to converge, would be valuable.

3. **Inconsistent Reporting and Discussion:** The paper mentions high-frequency noise issues with AffGAN in Section 5.5 but doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of how to mitigate it. The connection made in Appendix F between AffGAN and variational inference, while interesting, feels tacked on and is not fully integrated into the main narrative of the paper. The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6) is insightful but doesn’t lead to concrete suggestions for future research.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a more detailed ablation study to assess the contribution of the affine projection layer. How much performance improvement does it offer compared to a standard CNN with the same loss function?
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:**  Report more robust quantitative metrics beyond PSNR and SSIM. Consider using perceptual metrics like LPIPS to better assess the perceptual quality of the generated images.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  Include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., those using transformers). This will provide a better benchmark for evaluating the performance of the proposed methods.
*   **Address Noise Issue:** Investigate the high-frequency noise observed in AffGAN and explore techniques to reduce it.
*   **Expand Variational Inference Connection:**  Elaborate on the connection between AffGAN and variational inference in the main body of the paper. Could this connection be leveraged to develop more stable and effective training algorithms?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the noise level in the instance noise technique.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the architectural choices made in the AffGAN model (e.g., number of layers, filter sizes)? How were these hyperparameters selected?
2.  In Section 5.3, you state that AffGAN produces the sharpest images for grass textures, but the visual examples in Figure 3 don't clearly demonstrate a significant improvement over other methods. Could you provide additional examples or a more quantitative analysis to support this claim?
3.  Why were the AffDG and AffLL models abandoned? What specific challenges did you encounter during their training?
4.  The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is insightful. Do you see any potential solutions to these limitations, perhaps by incorporating Bayesian modeling techniques?
5.  How does the performance of AffGAN degrade as the scaling factor (e.g., 2x, 4x, 8x) increases?
