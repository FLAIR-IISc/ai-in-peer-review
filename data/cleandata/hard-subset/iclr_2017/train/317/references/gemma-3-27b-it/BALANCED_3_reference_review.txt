This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by proposing a novel approach based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that it avoids the limitations of empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses like MSE. The core idea is to leverage the structure of the SR problem, where the set of HR images compatible with a given LR image forms an affine subspace, and to design CNN architectures that enforce this consistency through an affine projection layer. The authors then explore three methods for approximating MAP inference: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL).

I recommend **rejection** of this paper. While the theoretical motivation is sound and the idea of enforcing consistency through affine projections is interesting, the empirical results are not sufficiently compelling to justify the complexity of the proposed methods, and several aspects lack the rigor expected for ICLR publication. The primary reasons for this decision are a lack of convincing advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods, and insufficient ablation studies to isolate the contributions of the proposed affine projection.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the shortcomings of MSE-based SR and presenting MAP inference as a desirable alternative. The connection between SR and affine subspace projections is well-explained, and the theoretical derivation of the cross-entropy minimization objective is solid. The proposed AffGAN, AffDG, and AffLL methods build logically from this foundation. 

However, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat fragmented and incomplete. While the 2D Swiss-roll experiment effectively demonstrates the ability of AffGAN and AffDG to find solutions in high-probability regions, its simplicity limits its generalizability. The results on more complex datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) are mixed. AffGAN consistently performs well visually, but quantitative comparisons (PSNR, SSIM) often show it being outperformed by MSE-trained models, even though the authors argue these metrics correlate poorly with perception.  More critically, there’s a lack of rigorous comparison against other state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods (e.g., SRGAN, ESRGAN) to demonstrate a clear improvement. The claim that AffGAN produces “slightly sharper images” on CelebA without substantial quantitative support is unconvincing. The ablation studies are limited; it's not clear how much of the observed performance is due to the affine projection itself versus other architectural choices or training procedures. Specifically, a direct comparison of a standard CNN trained with the same loss function but *without* the affine projection would be invaluable.  The discussion of AffDG and AffLL is brief and their disappointing performance seems downplayed; more investigation into why they failed to perform well is needed. Finally, the connection to amortized variational inference established in the appendix feels tacked-on and doesn't significantly strengthen the main contribution of the paper.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Compare AffGAN against more recent and powerful GAN-based SR models (SRGAN, ESRGAN, Real-ESRGAN).
*   **Ablation Studies:**  Perform a thorough ablation study to isolate the impact of the affine projection layer. Compare a model *without* the affine projection but trained with the same loss function and architecture. Experiment with different ways of implementing the projection (e.g., trainable vs. fixed A+).
*   **Quantitative Metrics:** Supplement visual comparisons with more robust quantitative metrics, potentially including perceptual loss metrics (LPIPS).
*   **Detailed Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the performance of AffDG and AffLL. Why do they perform poorly? Are there specific hyperparameters or architectural choices that could improve their results?
*   **Clarify Contributions:**  More clearly articulate the specific contributions of the paper. Is it the affine projection layer, the specific training procedures, or the theoretical framework?
*   **Address Limitations:** More thoroughly discuss the limitations of the MAP inference approach, as briefly mentioned in the conclusion.
*   **Instance Noise Details:** Provide more implementation details regarding the "instance noise" technique, including how the noise level is annealed during training and its impact on performance.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Why was the choice made to focus on GAN-based methods for the main experiments, given the relatively poor performance of AffDG and AffLL?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of incorporating the affine projection layer into the network? Does it significantly increase training or inference time?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of AffGAN to the choice of the downsampling kernel (A) and the corresponding deconvolution kernel (A+)?
4.  In the 2D Swiss-roll example, how was the bandwidth of the Gaussian kernel density estimator chosen, and how does this choice affect the results?
5.  Regarding the claim that MSE-trained models produce generic, over-smoothed images, do you have any statistical measures (e.g., diversity metrics) to support this claim?
