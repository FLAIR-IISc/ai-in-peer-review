This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by proposing methods based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that it’s a desirable approach given the limitations of pixel-wise loss functions like MSE. The core idea is to leverage natural image statistics by favoring solutions with high posterior probability. The authors present three approaches: GAN-based methods, denoising-guided SR, and density-guided SR, all incorporating an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between LR and HR images.

**Decision: Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of convincing demonstration of significant improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly given the complexity of the proposed approaches. While the theoretical motivation is sound and the affine projection is a clever idea, the empirical results, even in the 2D Swiss-roll toy example, are not substantial enough to justify the paper's claims. The experimental section feels fragmented and lacks a clear, compelling comparison with established SR techniques beyond basic PSNR/SSIM values. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results presented do not convincingly demonstrate a clear perceptual or quantitative advantage over existing SR techniques. While the AffGAN model shows promise, the improvement in visual quality (Figures 3-5) appears marginal, and the quantitative metrics (Table 2) do not definitively favor the proposed methods. The comparison is often limited to MSE-trained models, which are known to be suboptimal, making it difficult to assess the true impact of the proposed approach.
2. **Overly Complex and Fragmented Presentation:** The paper introduces three distinct approaches (GAN, denoising, density) and analyzes each, but without a clear overarching narrative. The transitions between sections feel disjointed, and it's unclear which method ultimately provides the best results or the most promising direction for future research. The paper feels like a collection of ideas rather than a cohesive contribution.
3. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The impact of the affine projection layer, while theoretically motivated, is not rigorously assessed. The ablation study in Section 5.2 is helpful but limited. More thorough ablation studies are needed to understand the benefits and drawbacks of this crucial component. For example, how does the performance change with different kernel sizes or deconvolution layers in the affine projection? 
4. **Unclear Connection to Variational Inference:** The theoretical connection to variational inference (Appendix F) is presented as a “first step” but is not fully explored or integrated into the main body of the paper. The potential benefits of this connection remain largely unrealized.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Stronger Baselines:**  Compare against more recent and sophisticated SR methods, such as those based on transformer architectures or those winning recent SR challenges.  Simply comparing to MSE-trained models is insufficient.
* **Perceptual Metrics:**  Include more advanced perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) in the evaluation, as these are better correlated with human perception than PSNR/SSIM.
* **More Visual Examples:** Provide more visually compelling examples, ideally with side-by-side comparisons against state-of-the-art methods. Include examples where the proposed method demonstrably outperforms existing techniques.
* **Streamline the Presentation:**  Focus on the most promising approach (likely AffGAN) and provide a more in-depth analysis of its strengths and weaknesses.  Reduce the fragmentation by consolidating related sections.
* **Address the Limitations:**  The authors acknowledge potential limitations in Section 5.6, but these should be discussed more thoroughly and potential mitigation strategies explored.
* **Investigate the Noise Sensitivity:** The experiments indicate that GAN training is sensitive to noise. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different noise distributions and annealing schedules could be beneficial.
* **Clarify the Role of Instance Noise:** The explanation of "instance noise" is somewhat convoluted. A clearer explanation of its mechanism and benefits would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond the initial cross-entropy results in the Swiss-roll example (Table 1), can you provide quantitative evidence demonstrating that the proposed methods explicitly minimize the cross-entropy objective during training on real image datasets?
2.  The paper mentions instability in GAN training. What specific techniques (beyond instance noise) were employed to stabilize the AffGAN training process?
3.  Given the computational cost of the affine projection, how does it impact the inference speed compared to other SR methods? Is there a trade-off between accuracy and speed?
4.  Why did the AffDG and AffLL models perform poorly in the grass texture experiment? Were there specific challenges related to their implementation or optimization?
5.  You mention a connection to variational inference. Could you elaborate on how this connection could be exploited to develop more robust and efficient SR algorithms?
