## Review of “Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution”

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that traditional loss functions like MSE lead to blurry results due to their averaging behavior, and advocate for directly maximizing the posterior probability.  They introduce a CNN architecture with an affine projection layer ensuring consistency between LR and HR images and then explore three methods to approximate MAP inference: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL).  The paper presents theoretical justifications for these methods and demonstrates their performance on both a 2D toy dataset and real image datasets, showing promising results, particularly for AffGAN in terms of perceptual quality.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper presents a compelling theoretical framework and interesting ideas, the experimental validation is not fully convincing and lacks sufficient rigor to support the strong claims made.  The results are often presented qualitatively, and the quantitative comparisons, while present, don’t always demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, especially when considering standard metrics like PSNR and SSIM.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Quantitative Analysis:** The quantitative results, especially in Tables 1 and 2, are limited and don't provide a strong enough basis for concluding superiority. While the cross-entropy results in Table 1 are interesting for the toy problem, their relevance to real-image SR is not clearly established. The image quality metrics (PSNR, SSIM) in Table 2 show that the proposed methods do not outperform the MSE baseline, despite the claims of improved perceptual quality.
* **Qualitative Results Predominate:** The reliance on visual inspection of results is substantial. While visual quality is important, it’s subjective and doesn't allow for a fair comparison. A more systematic and user-study-based perceptual evaluation would be highly beneficial.
* **Instability and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention the instability of GAN training and the need for careful hyperparameter tuning (e.g., instance noise). However, the experimental details lack sufficient information regarding the specific settings used to achieve stable training, making it difficult to reproduce the results. The abrupt changes in AffDG training based on DAE parameters also raise concerns about the robustness of the approach.
* **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to existing SR methods (Ledig et al., 2016) is not comprehensive. A more thorough comparison against recent, high-performing SR architectures is necessary to position the proposed methods appropriately. It would be highly valuable to report results on standard benchmarks like Set5, Set14, BSD100, and Urban100.
* **The Connection to Variational Inference is Weakly Established:** The connection to variational inference established in the appendix feels tacked on and not central to the main narrative of the paper. Further exploration and integration would be required to elevate this connection.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Strengthen Quantitative Evaluation:** Conduct a more thorough quantitative evaluation using standard SR benchmarks and metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS). Perform statistical significance tests to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to chance.
* **Perceptual Evaluation:** Include a rigorous perceptual evaluation, such as a user study, to objectively assess the visual quality of the generated images.
* **Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including hyperparameters, training procedures, and hardware configurations, to ensure reproducibility.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to analyze the contribution of each component of the proposed methods (e.g., the affine projection layer, instance noise).
* **Address Limitations:** The paper acknowledges some limitations (e.g., potential issues with MAP inference and the sensitivity to representation). Discuss these limitations more thoroughly and suggest possible avenues for future research.
* **Clarify Theoretical Justification:** Expand on the theoretical justifications for the proposed methods, particularly the connection between MAP inference and GANs.
* **Expand on Failure Cases:** The authors observe "high-frequency noise" and hallucination (snake turning into water) in some results.  A deeper analysis of these failure cases, and potential remedies, would be valuable.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the Gaussian kernel width used in the affine projection? Did you explore different kernel sizes?
*  What specific strategies were employed to stabilize GAN training (learning rate schedules, batch sizes, etc.)? Please provide more details.
*  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of the proposed methods compared to existing SR techniques?
*  Given that PSNR and SSIM don't consistently favor your approach, what specific perceptual differences are you observing that justify the claim of improved perceptual quality? Can you quantify these differences?
*  How does the performance of AffGAN compare to other recent GAN-based SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN)?
* What is the justification for choosing the MCGSM for the density guided method instead of a simpler density estimator?



In conclusion, this paper presents a compelling idea, but requires significantly more rigorous experimental validation and a more comprehensive analysis to convincingly demonstrate its superiority over existing SR methods. I encourage the authors to address the above points and resubmit a revised version of the paper.
