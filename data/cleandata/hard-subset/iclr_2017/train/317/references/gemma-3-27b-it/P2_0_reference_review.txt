## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that traditional empirical risk minimization with losses like MSE leads to blurry results due to averaging over plausible solutions. They introduce an affine projection layer within a CNN architecture to ensure consistency between LR and HR images and then explore three methods – GAN-based, denoising-guided, and density-guided – to approximate MAP inference. The paper demonstrates, through both a 2D toy problem and image SR experiments, that their approach can minimize cross-entropy and produce perceptually more appealing results than standard methods, particularly using the GAN-based approach (AffGAN). A connection to variational inference is also established.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framing and some promising initial results, the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor and the improvements over strong baselines are not convincingly demonstrated. The results are often qualitative, and quantitative gains are either small or only observed in specific scenarios.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Limited Quantitative Results:** While Table 1 shows cross-entropy values for the 2D toy problem, supporting the theoretical claims, the quantitative results on image SR are limited to PSNR/SSIM (Table 2) and are not consistently better than existing methods. The small improvements observed don't justify the increased complexity of the proposed approach.
*   **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to existing SR methods, while present, isn't comprehensive enough.  The authors compare against MSE-trained models and variations of GANs, but lack comparisons to more recent and state-of-the-art SR architectures like EDSR, RCAN or RDN.  Without comparison to strong, established baselines, it is difficult to ascertain the true benefit of the proposed method.
*   **Unclear Ablation & Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The ablation study in Section 5.2, while demonstrating the benefit of the affine projection, doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the performance to hyperparameter choices or different network architectures. The impact of instance noise is also not thoroughly analyzed.
*   **Qualitative Results are Subjective:**  Many claims rely heavily on visual inspection (Figures 3-5). While the results *appear* promising, subjective assessment is not a strong form of evidence.
*   **Theoretical Justification vs. Practical Performance:** The strong theoretical motivation (MAP inference, cross-entropy minimization) doesn't fully translate into significant practical gains. This disconnect suggests potential issues with the approximations made in the practical implementation, or that the theoretical benefits are less impactful in the high-dimensional image domain.



**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include comparisons with EDSR, RCAN, or RDN in the experiments.
*   **More Robust Evaluation Metrics:** Consider incorporating perceptual metrics like LPIPS in addition to PSNR/SSIM.
*   **Comprehensive Ablation Study:**  Conduct a thorough ablation study to analyze the impact of each component of the proposed method (affine projection, different loss functions, instance noise) and different network configurations.
*   **Hyperparameter Optimization:**  Detail the hyperparameter tuning process and demonstrate the robustness of the results to different parameter settings.
*   **Analysis of Failure Cases:**  Discuss the limitations of the approach and analyze failure cases. The comment about generating water instead of a snake (Section 5.5) suggests the model can sometimes produce plausible but incorrect reconstructions.
*   **Clarify Connection to Variational Inference:** The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is intriguing, but requires further elaboration. Is this a true amortized VI framework, or simply a related observation?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why were specific, recent state-of-the-art SR architectures (e.g., EDSR, RCAN, RDN) not included in the experimental comparison?
2.  What is the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the proposed method compared to a standard SR CNN trained with MSE loss?
3.  The paper mentions instability in training for AffDG and AffLL. Could you provide more details on the strategies used to mitigate these issues?
4.  Can you explain in more detail how the AffGAN objective relates to the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) in variational inference? What approximations are being made?
5.  How sensitive is the performance of AffGAN to the choice of instance noise distribution and annealing schedule?
