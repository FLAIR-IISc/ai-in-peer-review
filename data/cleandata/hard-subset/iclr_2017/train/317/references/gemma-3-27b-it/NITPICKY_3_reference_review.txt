This paper proposes methods for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that directly minimizing perceptual loss functions is challenging and that leveraging natural image statistics is a promising alternative. The authors present a CNN architecture incorporating an affine projection to ensure consistency between low-resolution (LR) input and high-resolution (HR) output, and explore three approaches for approximating the cross-entropy loss: a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL). The work attempts to theoretically ground GANs in image SR and introduces a technique called "instance noise" to improve GAN training stability.

**Reject.** While the paper presents interesting ideas and attempts to provide theoretical justification for its approach, the execution is riddled with inconsistencies, insufficient experimental validation, and a level of detail that suggests a lack of thoroughness. The numerous minor flaws collectively undermine confidence in the reported results and the overall contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Inconsistent Reporting & Lack of Rigor:** The paper frequently makes claims without sufficient supporting evidence. For example, the claim that AffGAN and AffDG achieve cross-entropy values "close to the MAP solution" (Table 1) is not convincingly demonstrated. The setup for calculating the "true" MAP solution in the 2D toy example is not fully detailed, making replication and verification difficult. The descriptions of the experimental setups, especially concerning the density model (AffLL), are vague. Saying the log-likelihood is "comparable" to another work isn’t quantitative or helpful.
2. **Insufficient Ablation & Comparative Analysis:** The ablation study in Section 5.2, comparing affine-projected vs. non-projected networks, is weak. The discussion focuses primarily on MSE, neglecting other relevant metrics. The justification for the choice of architecture and hyperparameters isn’t clear. The authors fail to adequately address why AffDG and AffLL were abandoned after initial experiments (Section 5.3), merely stating that they produced blurry results. A more thorough investigation and comparative analysis are crucial.
3. **Overstated Claims & Lack of Critical Discussion:** The statement that the affine projection "exactly satisfies" the input-output consistency is mathematically correct, but its practical benefit appears limited, as demonstrated by the limited improvement in results. The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6) is superficial and doesn't engage with the existing literature on the challenges of mode collapse and posterior estimation. The connection drawn between AffGAN and variational inference in Appendix F feels tacked on and not fully integrated with the main body of the paper.
4. **Minor Details & Formatting Issues:** The paper contains numerous minor issues: inconsistent terminology (e.g., "soft-constrained" vs. "soft L2/L1 penalties"), unclear notation, and minor grammatical errors. While individually insignificant, these errors contribute to a sense of sloppiness. Tables and Figures are referenced inconsistently throughout the text. The lengthy appendixes contain a great deal of detail but feel disorganized.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the role of instance noise:** The theoretical justification for instance noise is compelling, but the experimental results don't clearly demonstrate its effectiveness. Provide a more rigorous analysis of its impact on training stability and performance.
*   **Provide more details on the density model:** The description of the PixelCNN-based density model is insufficient. Explain the architectural choices, training procedure, and hyperparameter settings in detail.
*   **Address the limitations of the affine projection:** Discuss the potential drawbacks of imposing the affine constraint, such as restricting the model's expressiveness or introducing artifacts.
*   **Conduct a more comprehensive evaluation:** Evaluate the proposed methods on a wider range of datasets and using a variety of perceptual metrics, including those specifically designed to assess image quality.
*   **Re-evaluate the significance of the MAP objective:** The paper emphasizes the importance of MAP inference, but the experimental results don't consistently demonstrate its superiority over other approaches. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between MAP inference and alternative optimization criteria.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the "true" MAP solution was calculated in the 2D toy example, including the specific parameters used and the computational procedure?
*   Why were the AffDG and AffLL models abandoned after initial experiments? What specific challenges did you encounter during training or evaluation?
*   How does the performance of AffGAN compare to state-of-the-art SR methods that do *not* rely on MAP inference or GANs?
*   What is the computational cost of the affine projection layer, and how does it affect the overall training time and memory usage of the models?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of noise distribution and annealing schedule for the instance noise technique?
