## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by moving away from traditional empirical risk minimization (e.g., MSE) and towards amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that MAP inference leads to more plausible results by favoring solutions with higher posterior probability, aligning better with the natural image prior.  They introduce a novel CNN architecture incorporating an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between LR and HR images, and explore three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL). The paper provides both theoretical justification and empirical validation of their approach, demonstrating improved performance on synthetic and real image datasets.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and explores a promising direction for SR, the empirical results, particularly regarding the proposed AffGAN method, are not sufficiently convincing to warrant acceptance. The improvements over baseline methods seem modest, and the practical benefits of enforcing the affine constraint are not clearly demonstrated.  The extensive theoretical development feels disproportionate to the demonstrable gain in performance.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Modest Empirical Gains:** The quantitative results (Table 1, Table 2) show only marginal improvements with AffGAN, often comparable to or slightly better than baseline methods. The claims of significant perceptual improvement, based on visual inspection of Figure 3, 4 and 5, are subjective and not supported by quantitative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS). While the authors acknowledge high-frequency noise in the AffGAN results, this remains a significant issue.
* **Unclear Benefit of Affine Projection:** The paper strongly emphasizes the affine projection layer. However, the experiments comparing with "soft" constraints (e.g., MSE with L1 regularization) don’t provide a compelling argument for the necessity or effectiveness of the affine projection. The analysis in Section 5.2 is insightful but doesn't translate to significant improvements in the final SR results.  The argument that maintaining the constraint exactly helps training is interesting but needs stronger evidence.
* **Complexity vs. Gain:** The theoretical framework is quite involved, requiring a significant amount of background knowledge. The payoff, in terms of demonstrated improvement over existing SR methods, doesn't appear to justify this complexity. The DAE and density estimation approaches (AffDG and AffLL) are largely dismissed in the results section, and the focus primarily remains on AffGAN, which, as noted above, shows limited improvement.
* **Inconsistent Results:** The results in Figure 3, while showcasing improvements for grass textures, don't generalize well to more complex images (CelebA, ImageNet) shown in Figures 4 and 5.  The "dreaming up" of water in the snake image in Figure 5 suggests potential issues with mode collapse or undesirable biases in the generated outputs.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Quantitative Perceptual Evaluation:**  Include established perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, NIQE) to provide a more objective assessment of the visual quality of the generated images. This is crucial to substantiate claims of improved perceptual quality.
* **Ablation Studies:**  Conduct a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed framework (e.g., affine projection, instance noise). Quantify the impact of each component on performance.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  Compare the results with more recent and established SR methods (e.g., RCAN, ESRGAN) to provide a more competitive benchmark.
* **Refine Analysis of Affine Projection:** Provide a clearer explanation of *why* the affine projection is advantageous and how it specifically addresses the limitations of existing methods.  Demonstrate its impact more convincingly in the experimental results.
* **Investigate AffDG and AffLL:** Provide more insights into why AffDG and AffLL did not perform well and explore potential improvements.
* **Clarify the Connection to Variational Inference:** While the connection to variational inference is mentioned in the conclusion, it's not deeply integrated into the main body of the paper. Expanding on this connection could strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* What is the computational overhead associated with the affine projection layer, and how does it impact training and inference speed?
* Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of noise distribution and annealing schedule in the instance noise method?
* Why did AffDG and AffLL perform comparatively poorly, and what steps could be taken to improve their performance?
* Beyond the visual examples provided, do you have any quantitative evidence that the AffGAN model produces more plausible reconstructions, according to human perceptual studies or other objective measures?
