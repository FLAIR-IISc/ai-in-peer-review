## ICLR 2024 Review: Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that minimizing pixel-wise losses like MSE leads to blurry results due to averaging over plausible solutions, and propose leveraging natural image statistics instead. They introduce an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between LR and HR images, and then explore three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL). The paper presents a strong theoretical grounding for their approach and empirical results on both a 2D toy problem and real image datasets. They demonstrate that AffGAN performs favorably, producing visually appealing results and achieving competitive performance.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to SR, grounded in a solid theoretical framework.  The experimental results are convincing, particularly the demonstration of AffGAN’s ability to generate plausible and sharp results, and the analysis in the 2D Swiss-roll example provides strong intuition for the benefits of MAP inference.  While the method doesn’t consistently outperform existing approaches on standard metrics, the qualitative improvements and theoretical justification make this a significant contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Theoretical Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of MSE-based SR and provides a compelling argument for MAP inference. The derivation of the affine projection and its connection to minimizing cross-entropy are well-explained.
* **Novel Approach:** The combination of affine projections with different generative modeling approaches (GAN, denoising, density estimation) is a novel and interesting direction.
* **Convincing Experiments:** The 2D Swiss-roll example clearly demonstrates the benefit of MAP inference. The experiments on grass textures and CelebA show that AffGAN can generate plausible and sharper results than methods based on MSE.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The paper provides detailed analysis of the results, comparing the performance of different methods and discussing the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
* **Connection to Variational Inference:** The identification of a link between AffGAN and amortised variational inference is a valuable theoretical insight.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Quantitative Results:** While the qualitative results are impressive, the quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM) are sometimes not competitive with state-of-the-art methods. It would be helpful to include a more thorough comparison with recent SR models, especially those using perceptual loss functions (e.g., RDN, ESRGAN).  A table summarizing the performance of various methods on standard datasets (Set5, Set14, BSD100) would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Instance Noise:** The introduction of instance noise is a promising technique for stabilizing GAN training, but the explanation could be more detailed. More discussion on how the annealing schedule for the noise level was chosen would be beneficial.
* **Ablation Studies:**  While the paper explores different training objectives, more detailed ablation studies examining the contribution of each component (e.g., the affine projection layer, instance noise) would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost associated with the proposed methods, particularly AffGAN, compared to standard SR models. This would help assess the practicality of the approach.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The discussion of limitations is good, but could be expanded.  For example, explore the potential for mode collapse in AffGAN, and how the affine projection might affect the model's ability to generate diverse outputs.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding quantitative results:**  Have you investigated the use of more recent perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) to evaluate the perceptual quality of the generated images? Do these metrics better reflect the visual improvements observed in the qualitative results?
* **Regarding instance noise:** What sensitivity analysis was performed on the instance noise hyperparameter? Did you observe significant differences in performance with different annealing schedules or noise levels?
* **Regarding the affine projection:**  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the affine projection? Are there scenarios where it might hinder performance or introduce artifacts?
* **Regarding future work:**  You mention a stochastic version of AffGAN and its connection to variational inference.  Could you provide more details on this connection and the potential benefits of this approach?  Are there any initial experimental results you can share?
* **Regarding failure cases:**  Can you provide examples of cases where the AffGAN model fails to produce plausible SR results?  What are the characteristics of these failure cases?



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that presents a compelling argument for using MAP inference in SR.  The proposed methods are novel and the experimental results are promising. With minor revisions addressing the points above, this paper has the potential to significantly advance the field of image super-resolution.
