This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR), arguing that Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference provides a desirable framework compared to empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise losses like MSE, which often lead to blurry results. The core contribution lies in developing a CNN architecture incorporating an affine projection that ensures output consistency with the low-resolution input, effectively restricting the solution space to plausible HR images. This projection enables framing the SR problem as minimizing cross-entropy between the model output distribution and the true HR image distribution, opening avenues for leveraging generative modeling techniques. The authors explore three approaches: GANs (AffGAN), denoising-guided methods (AffDG), and density modeling (AffLL).

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical argument and a novel architectural constraint (affine projection), the empirical results, particularly those beyond the 2D toy example, lack convincing evidence of substantial improvement over existing GAN-based SR methods. The practical benefit of the affine projection, beyond ensuring consistency, remains unclear, and the density-guided approach appears underdeveloped. 

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of compelling empirical validation. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment effectively demonstrates the theoretical advantage of MAP inference, showing AffGAN and AffDG aligning with the true posterior mode unlike MSE/MAE. However, the grass texture and CelebA experiments, while showing *some* improvement with AffGAN, don't definitively outperform state-of-the-art GANs, especially considering the additional complexity introduced by the affine projection. The quantitative results (PSNR, SSIM) are not demonstrably superior. The ImageNet results are presented qualitatively, making assessment difficult. Furthermore, the ablations and comparisons within the paper are not exhaustive enough to confidently claim the proposed methods are superior. Specifically, the paper states AffGAN produces “slightly sharper” images, which is a weak claim without more rigorous perceptual evaluation (e.g., user studies).

The second reason for rejection is insufficient clarity regarding the practical implementation details and the rationale behind certain design choices. While the paper attempts to motivate the theoretical framework, it lacks a clear explanation of why the affine projection consistently leads to improved performance. It's not entirely clear why enforcing consistency between LR and HR representations would inherently lead to better perceptual quality. The DAE/density model branches were under-developed and quickly abandoned, leaving questions about their potential.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **More Robust Evaluation:** Conduct a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., SRGAN, ESRGAN) using established evaluation metrics *and* perceptual quality assessments (e.g., LPIPS, learned perceptual image patch similarity). This should include detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the affine projection.
*   **Perceptual Metrics:** The reliance on PSNR and SSIM is insufficient. Incorporate perceptual metrics, ideally coupled with human evaluation, to better assess the visual quality of the generated images.
*   **Detailed Analysis of Affine Projection:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the impact of the affine projection. What is the computational cost? Does it introduce any artifacts? How does it interact with different network architectures?
*   **Complete the Density-Guided Approach:** The AffLL approach was prematurely abandoned. Investigate the reasons for its poor performance and explore potential remedies (e.g., more powerful density models, improved training strategies).
*   **Clarify the Connection to Variational Inference:** While the connection to amortized variational inference is mentioned in the conclusion, it could be strengthened by providing a more detailed explanation of how AffGAN relates to VAEs.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, the noise level in the DAE/density model, and the choice of network architecture.
*  **Address Limitations:** The paper touches on limitations, but a more thorough discussion of potential drawbacks (e.g., the sensitivity to the choice of A+, the potential for mode collapse) would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond the visual inspection, do you have quantitative evidence (e.g., LPIPS scores, user studies) demonstrating a statistically significant improvement in perceptual quality with AffGAN compared to SRGAN or ESRGAN?
2.  What is the computational overhead associated with the affine projection layer? Does it significantly increase the training time or inference time?
3.  Why did you abandon the AffDG and AffLL models so quickly? What specific challenges did you encounter during training?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of the Gaussian kernel for the downsampling operation A? How sensitive are the results to the kernel size and standard deviation?
5.  The theory suggests that minimizing the cross-entropy is desirable. However, minimizing KL-divergence (as in standard GANs) is closely related. What is the practical difference in performance and training stability between AffGAN (minimizing cross-entropy directly) and a standard GAN with affine projection?
