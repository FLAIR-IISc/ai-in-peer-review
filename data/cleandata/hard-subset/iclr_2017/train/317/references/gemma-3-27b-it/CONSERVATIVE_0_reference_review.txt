This paper proposes methods for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, motivated by the limitations of pixel-wise losses like MSE. The authors argue that MAP inference favors solutions with higher prior probability, leading to more plausible reconstructions. They introduce an affine projection layer to enforce consistency between LR and HR images and explore three approaches to approximate MAP inference: a GAN-based method (AffGAN), a denoising-guided method (AffDG), and a density-guided method (AffLL).

**Reject.** While the motivation for moving beyond MSE is sound and the idea of enforcing consistency through an affine projection is reasonable, the paper presents an overly ambitious set of methods with insufficient validation. The empirical results, while demonstrating some advantages of the affine projection, lack the robustness and thoroughness expected for a strong ICLR submission. The extensive theoretical framing feels disconnected from the experimental evaluation, and the presented results don't convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over existing SR techniques.

The core issue is the lack of rigorous comparison to established SR methods. While the authors mention Ledig et al. (2016) and others, the comparisons are limited and often lack quantitative metrics beyond PSNR and SSIM, which the authors themselves acknowledge are imperfect. The claim of perceptual improvement isn’t convincingly demonstrated. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment is helpful for illustrating the principle, but doesn’t translate directly to the complexities of natural images. The grass texture experiment is more relevant but the AffDG and AffLL results are dismissed quickly. The CelebA and ImageNet results are presented without detailed ablation studies or comparisons to state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods like Real-ESRGAN or GFPGAN. The added “instance noise” is presented as a crucial stabilization technique, yet lacks a thorough analysis of its impact and hyperparameter sensitivity. The connection drawn to variational inference in the appendix feels tacked on and doesn’t fully integrate with the main contributions. Finally, the discussion of limitations feels cursory and doesn't adequately address potential issues with the MAP assumption.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include comparisons to more recent and powerful SR methods (e.g., Real-ESRGAN, GFPGAN, SwinIR).
*   **Perceptual Metrics:** Supplement PSNR/SSIM with perceptual metrics like LPIPS or FID to better assess visual quality.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to analyze the impact of each component (affine projection, instance noise, different loss functions).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings.
*   **Detailed Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the failure cases and limitations of the proposed methods.
*   **Clarify the Connection to Variational Inference:** Expand on the connection to variational inference and demonstrate its practical implications.
*   **Address the Mode Collapse issue:** The paper acknowledges high frequency noise in the GAN outputs. Address this issue thoroughly.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Why were the AffDG and AffLL models dismissed after the grass texture experiment? What specific challenges were encountered?
*   What is the computational cost of the affine projection layer compared to other SR architectures? Does it introduce significant overhead?
*   What is the optimal setting for the instance noise parameter, and how does it vary across different datasets and architectures?
*   Can you provide a more detailed discussion of the potential issues with the MAP assumption in the context of natural images, and how your approach attempts to mitigate them?
*   Can you elaborate on the practical implications of the connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference? Does it offer any advantages in terms of training stability or performance?
