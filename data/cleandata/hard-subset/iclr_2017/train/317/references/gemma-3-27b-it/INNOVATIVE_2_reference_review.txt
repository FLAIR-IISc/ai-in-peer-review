This paper introduces a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The core idea is to leverage the inherent structure of the SR problem – the fact that LR images lie on an affine subspace of possible HR solutions – and enforce consistency between LR and HR via a specifically designed network architecture. The authors then explore three methods for learning this MAP estimate: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The paper argues that these methods, by preferring high-probability solutions, can overcome the limitations of traditional pixel-wise loss functions like MSE.

**Reject.** While the core idea of approaching SR through amortized MAP inference and enforcing affine consistency is genuinely novel and potentially impactful, the paper feels somewhat incomplete and lacks the robust validation needed to justify acceptance. The results, while promising, are not consistently superior across all experiments, and the theoretical justification, while interesting, doesn't fully translate into clear practical advantages.

Here's a breakdown of my reasoning:

* **Novelty is High, Rigor is Moderate:** The central concept of using affine projections to constrain the SR problem is a significant contribution. It's a clever way to incorporate prior knowledge and address the limitations of conventional SR methods. However, the paper sometimes feels like a collection of ideas rather than a tightly integrated, rigorously validated system.
* **Inconsistent Results:** The 2D Swiss-roll experiment convincingly demonstrates the method's ability to find MAP estimates. However, the results on real image datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) are more mixed. AffGAN appears to perform best, but even then, the gains over existing methods aren’t dramatic, and the results still exhibit GAN-typical artifacts (high-frequency noise). The AffDG and AffLL approaches seem less successful, and are largely dismissed by the authors. More thorough analysis and comparison, particularly with state-of-the-art SR methods, is needed.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper demonstrates that the affine projection can improve training speed and consistency, a more comprehensive ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of each component (GAN loss, denoising guidance, density modeling). It’s unclear which aspects are truly crucial for performance.
* **Theoretical Claims Need Further Elaboration:** The connection to variational inference (Appendix F) is intriguing, but it feels somewhat tacked on and doesn't receive sufficient development. The discussion of the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6) is insightful, but doesn’t offer concrete solutions.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Quantitative Evaluation:** The paper relies heavily on visual inspection. More comprehensive quantitative metrics beyond PSNR and SSIM (e.g., LPIPS, learned perceptual image patch similarity) would be helpful for objectively comparing the performance of the proposed methods with existing SR techniques.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of the proposed methods. Are they more or less efficient than existing SR algorithms? This is an important consideration for practical applications.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, noise level)? A sensitivity analysis would help to assess the robustness of the proposed methods.
* **Clarify the Role of Instance Noise:** While the description of instance noise is intriguing, it could be better integrated into the overall narrative. How does it specifically address the challenges of GAN training in this context?
* **Density Model Details:** The description of the MCGSM density model is rather brief. Providing more details about its architecture and training procedure would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of AffGAN with state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN)?
* What is the rationale behind choosing the particular noise distribution for instance noise? Have you experimented with other distributions?
* In the CelebA experiments, you mention color drifting with the SoftGAN model. Can you elaborate on this issue and explain why the AffGAN model is more robust?
* Could you provide a more thorough analysis of the failure cases of the AffGAN model? What types of images or textures does it struggle with?
* Regarding the connection to variational inference, could you expand on the potential benefits of exploring this connection further, and what specific research directions you envision?
