## Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of image super-resolution (SR) by framing it as an amortised Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference problem. The authors argue that minimizing pixel-wise loss functions like MSE leads to blurry results because they average over plausible high-resolution solutions, failing to leverage a natural image prior.  They propose a novel approach utilizing affine projections to constrain the SR network's output to the space of plausible solutions consistent with the low-resolution input.  They explore three methods to implement this: a GAN-based approach (AffGAN), a denoising-guided approach (AffDG), and a density-guided approach (AffLL). The paper provides a theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating connections to variational inference, and presents empirical results on both a 2D toy problem and image datasets.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and a potentially promising direction for SR, the experimental results, particularly on real image datasets, are not compelling enough to warrant acceptance. The improvements shown are incremental at best, and the claims of perceptual superiority are not thoroughly substantiated.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Limited Empirical Gains:** The results in Figures 3-5 show only moderate improvements compared to existing methods, and in some cases, the visual quality doesn't appear significantly better. The quantitative results in Table 2 indicate that the MSE-trained model still achieves the best PSNR and SSIM scores. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of these metrics, they don’t present sufficiently convincing evidence of perceptual improvements using more robust measures or user studies.
*   **Complexity vs. Benefit:** The proposed affine projection introduces a significant architectural constraint and theoretical complexity. The benefits gained from this complexity—primarily observed in the 2D toy problem and not consistently reflected in the image results—do not seem to justify the added intricacy.
*   **Unclear Advantage of AffGAN:** The AffGAN method seems to be the most promising, but the discussion around the high-frequency noise issues and instability in training raises concerns. The statement that "we were unable to stable train models without this extra regularization" hints at inherent instability, undermining the robustness of the approach.
*   **Incomplete Comparison:** The paper largely focuses on comparing against MSE, which is a known weakness. A more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art SR methods, especially those employing perceptual losses (e.g., ESRGAN), is needed to establish the true value of the proposed approach.
*   **Theoretical Connection to VI feels tenuous**: While the connection to Variational Inference is mathematically intriguing, the practical implications and benefits of this connection are not clearly articulated.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Perceptual Evaluation:**  Include a more robust perceptual evaluation, such as a user study or the use of a learned perceptual metric (e.g., LPIPS).  Simply stating that the results "look better" is insufficient.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the affine projection. For example, evaluate the performance of the GAN and denoising approaches *without* the projection.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Explore the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings, particularly related to the affine projection and the instance noise.
*   **Broader Dataset Evaluation:** Evaluate the method on a wider range of datasets and SR factors (e.g., 2x, 8x) to assess its generalization ability.
*   **Clarify VI Connection:** Elaborate on the practical implications of the connection to Variational Inference. How can this connection be leveraged for further improvements or insights?
*    **Address Computational Cost**: Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the affine projection. Does it significantly increase training or inference time?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the high-frequency noise observed in the AffGAN results? What specific strategies could be employed to mitigate this issue?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of kernel for the affine projection? Did you experiment with different kernels, and if so, what impact did they have?
*   What is the computational cost (training and inference time) of using the affine projection compared to a standard SR model without it?
*   Given the instability of AffGAN training, what specific instance noise parameters (e.g., annealing schedule, noise distribution) were found to be most effective?
*   Why was the density-guided approach (AffLL) discontinued as a primary focus after the initial experiments? What were the main challenges encountered with this method?
