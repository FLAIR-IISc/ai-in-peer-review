

# Review: AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by proposing a novel approach based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors convincingly argue that current state-of-the-art methods using empirical risk minimization with MSE loss produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution (Bayes-optimal for MSE), rather than selecting the mode of the posterior distribution. The authors introduce an elegant architectural innovation - an affine projection layer that ensures model outputs are always consistent with the LR input. Building on this, they propose three methods for amortized MAP inference: Affine Projected GAN (AffGAN), Denoiser Guided Super-Resolution (AffDG), and Density Guided Super-Resolution (AffLL). The paper provides strong theoretical grounding for these methods, demonstrates their effectiveness on both toy problems and real image datasets, and establishes a novel connection between GANs and variational inference.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that is well-motivated and supported by compelling empirical evidence. The key reason for acceptance is that the authors successfully demonstrate a novel approach to image super-resolution that produces visually superior results by focusing on the mode of the posterior distribution rather than the mean, addressing a fundamental limitation of current approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical motivation is exceptionally strong. The Swiss-roll toy example (Figure 1a) clearly demonstrates why MSE produces blurry outputs while MAP inference would produce sharper, more plausible results. The authors provide rigorous mathematical justification showing that their affine projection layer transforms the constrained MAP inference problem into an unconstrained optimization problem, which is a significant technical contribution.

The three proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) are well-motivated and leverage different aspects of probabilistic modeling. AffGAN is particularly compelling as it builds on the successful GAN framework for SR (Ledig et al., 2016) while providing theoretical justification for why GANs work well for this task. The instance noise technique is a clever and practical solution to GAN training instability, and the paper provides a clear theoretical explanation for why it works better than label smoothing.

The experimental results strongly support the authors' claims. The 2D toy problem (Section 5.1) shows that AffGAN and AffDG correctly minimize the MAP objective, while MSE and MAE produce outputs in low-probability regions. The real-world experiments on grass textures (Section 5.3) and faces (Section 5.4) demonstrate that AffGAN produces sharper, more visually plausible results than MSE and MAE, even though it performs worse on PSNR/SSIM metrics. This is exactly as expected, since PSNR/SSIM correlate poorly with human perception, and the authors correctly argue that perceptual quality is more important than these metrics for their application.

The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is a particularly elegant theoretical contribution that further validates the approach and opens new research directions.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions to further improve it:

1. **Clarify the PSNR/SSIM vs. perceptual quality tension**: The paper correctly notes that AffGAN performs worse on PSNR/SSIM than MSE, but it would strengthen the argument to include a comparison to more modern perceptual quality metrics (like LPIPS) that better correlate with human perception. This would provide a more complete picture of why AffGAN's approach is superior.

2. **Explain why AffDG and AffLL underperformed**: The paper states that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results, leading the authors to focus on AffGAN. A more thorough discussion of why these methods failed (beyond just stating they did) would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights for future work.

3. **Expand the discussion of instance noise**: While the instance noise technique is promising, the paper could provide more details on how it's implemented in practice and why it works better than label smoothing in the context of image SR.

4. **Address scalability to higher downscaling factors**: The paper focuses on 4× super-resolution, but it would be valuable to discuss how the approach would perform with higher downscaling factors (8× or 16×), which are more challenging and common in practice.

5. **Clarify the relationship between AffGAN and other GAN-based SR methods**: While the paper mentions Ledig et al. (2016), it would be helpful to explicitly compare AffGAN to their method and other recent GAN-based SR approaches to better position the contribution.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that AffGAN produces visually superior results despite worse PSNR/SSIM scores. How would you expect your method to perform on the LPIPS metric, which has been shown to better correlate with human perception than PSNR/SSIM?

2. The paper mentions that AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations due to the H[qθ] term. Can you provide specific examples or additional evidence to support this claim?

3. How would you expect the performance of AffGAN to change with increasing downscaling factors (e.g., 8× vs. 4×), and what modifications might be needed to maintain performance?

4. The authors note that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results. Could you elaborate on why these methods might have failed to capture the high-probability regions of the posterior distribution, while AffGAN succeeded?

5. In Appendix F, the authors establish a connection between AffGAN and variational inference. How might this perspective influence the design of future SR methods that combine the strengths of both approaches?

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution. The authors successfully demonstrate a novel approach that produces more visually plausible results by focusing on MAP inference rather than MSE, and they provide both theoretical and empirical support for their claims. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR conference.
