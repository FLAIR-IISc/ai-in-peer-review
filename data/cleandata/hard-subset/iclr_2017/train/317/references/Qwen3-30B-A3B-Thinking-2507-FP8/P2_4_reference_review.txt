

# Review for ICLR: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of image super-resolution (SR) by introducing a novel framework based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors convincingly argue that current SR methods using MSE or similar losses produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution rather than finding the mode. Their key contributions include:

1. A novel affine projection layer that ensures consistency between LR input and HR output by projecting model outputs onto the affine subspace defined by the downsampling operation
2. Three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoising), and AffLL (using density modeling)
3. Theoretical grounding for why MAP inference is preferable to MSE for SR, with a connection to variational inference in Appendix F
4. Extensive empirical validation across synthetic and real-world datasets showing that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results

The paper is well-motivated, well-structured, and makes a significant contribution to the understanding of SR as a probabilistic inference problem.

## 2. Decision

Accept with minor revisions.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation of current SR methods. The core idea of using MAP inference with an affine projection layer is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The AffGAN approach demonstrates clear perceptual improvements over standard baselines while maintaining computational efficiency.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths are substantial:

**Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a clear explanation of why MSE-based approaches produce blurry results (by averaging over the posterior) and why MAP inference is preferable (by selecting the mode of the posterior). The 2D Swiss-roll example in Figure 1a is particularly effective at illustrating this point. The derivation of the cross-entropy minimization objective (Equation 9) is rigorous and well-justified.

**Novel architectural contribution:** The affine projection layer is simple yet powerful. The paper demonstrates through ablation studies (Section 5.2) that this layer improves training speed and final performance without degrading results. The numerical estimation of the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse (Appendix B) is a thoughtful practical implementation detail.

**Empirical validation:** The experiments across multiple datasets (Swiss-roll, grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) consistently show that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE, MAE, and even soft-constrained GAN approaches. The cross-entropy measurements (Table 1) provide quantitative evidence that the method is minimizing the intended objective.

**Theoretical connection to variational inference:** Appendix F establishes a valuable connection between AffGAN and variational inference, which is a significant theoretical contribution that could inform future work in the field.

The main limitations are that the paper doesn't thoroughly compare to the most recent state-of-the-art SR methods (beyond MSE/MAE baselines) and doesn't fully explain why AffDG and AffLL underperform compared to AffGAN. However, these are minor issues that can be addressed in revision.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

**Minor improvements:**
- The paper could strengthen its position in the current literature by including a more thorough comparison to recent SOTA SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN, etc.) in the main text.
- The paper mentions that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results but doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for this. Adding a brief discussion of why AffGAN might outperform these approaches for SR would strengthen the paper.
- The criticism of MAP inference in Section 5.6 is important but could be more concrete. Providing specific examples where MAP inference fails for SR would help balance the discussion.

**Questions for authors:**
1. How does your approach compare to recent SOTA SR methods like ESRGAN or RCAN? Could you provide a brief comparison in the main text?
2. The AffDG and AffLL methods don't perform as well as AffGAN. Is there a theoretical reason why the GAN-based approach is particularly suited for SR, or is this primarily an empirical observation?
3. Could you provide more concrete examples of when MAP inference might fail for SR, beyond the theoretical argument in Section 5.6?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by reframing the problem as a probabilistic inference task rather than a simple regression problem. The authors provide a compelling theoretical argument, a novel architectural component (affine projection), and three practical methods for implementing amortized MAP inference. The empirical results consistently show that their AffGAN approach produces more perceptually pleasing results than standard baselines, with the theoretical connection to variational inference being a particularly valuable addition.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a strong case for the value of MAP inference in SR. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable in revision, and the core contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance at ICLR.
