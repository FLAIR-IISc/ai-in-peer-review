

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents an interesting and theoretically grounded approach to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they minimize the mean of the posterior distribution rather than finding the mode (MAP solution), which should yield more perceptually plausible images. They introduce a novel architectural component - an affine projection layer that ensures the SR output always satisfies the downsampling constraint. Using this projection, they develop three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs to minimize KL divergence), AffDG (using denoising to estimate gradients), and AffLL (using density models). The paper provides theoretical justification, experimental validation on both toy 2D datasets and real image datasets, and establishes a connection between AffGAN and variational inference.

## 2. Decision

Reject. While the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the SR problem, it lacks sufficient empirical evidence to support its key claim that MAP inference produces perceptually superior results. The paper fails to adequately demonstrate that their methods outperform state-of-the-art approaches in terms of human perception, which is the primary motivation for their work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical foundation is strong and well-motivated. The derivation of the MAP objective and the affine projection layer are mathematically sound, and the connection to GANs and variational inference is insightful. The 2D toy experiment clearly demonstrates that AffGAN and AffDG find the mode of the posterior distribution, unlike MSE/MAE methods.

However, the empirical validation has critical limitations:

1. The paper claims AffGAN produces "visually appealing" results but fails to provide any perceptual evaluation beyond standard metrics (PSNR, SSIM) that the authors themselves acknowledge correlate poorly with human perception. The qualitative results in Figures 3-5 show AffGAN produces sharper images than MSE-based methods, but these images still contain high-frequency noise that is perceptually undesirable.

2. The comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods is limited. The paper doesn't compare to recent GAN-based SR methods like Ledig et al. (2016), which is the most relevant prior work. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether AffGAN represents an improvement over the current state of the art.

3. The paper doesn't address the fundamental limitation they acknowledge in Section 5.6: MAP inference is sensitive to coordinate transformations. While they theoretically argue AffGAN is more resilient, they provide no empirical evidence of this in practice.

4. The paper claims "the AffGAN model produces slightly sharper images" (Section 5.4), but the figures don't clearly show this is perceptually better - in fact, the high-frequency noise in AffGAN outputs is a known issue with GAN-based SR methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this paper for future submission, the authors should:

1. Conduct a comprehensive perceptual evaluation with human subjects to demonstrate that AffGAN outputs are truly more appealing to humans than MSE-based methods and state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods.

2. Compare AffGAN against recent SOTA methods (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2018) using both standard metrics and perceptual metrics like LPIPS or user studies.

3. Address the high-frequency noise issue in AffGAN outputs, which is a well-known limitation of GAN-based SR. The paper should discuss how their method handles this problem compared to other approaches.

4. Provide more detailed experimental comparisons, including ablation studies on the affine projection layer's contribution to performance.

5. Expand the discussion in Section 5.6 to provide empirical evidence supporting their claim that AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims AffGAN produces "visually appealing" results, but without human evaluation, it's difficult to assess. Could the authors provide results from a user study comparing AffGAN to MSE and other GAN-based SR methods?

2. How does AffGAN specifically compare to Ledig et al. (2016), which is the most relevant prior work using GANs for SR? The paper mentions Ledig et al. in the related work but doesn't compare to their method.

3. The paper identifies high-frequency noise as a limitation in AffGAN outputs (Section 5.5), but doesn't discuss how their method addresses this issue compared to other GAN-based SR approaches. Could the authors elaborate on this?

4. The theoretical argument in Section 5.6 about AffGAN being more resilient to coordinate transformations is compelling, but the paper lacks empirical evidence. Could the authors provide experiments demonstrating this property?
