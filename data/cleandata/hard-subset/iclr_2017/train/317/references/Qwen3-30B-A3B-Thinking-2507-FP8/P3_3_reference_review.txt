

# Peer Review: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling approach to image super-resolution (SR) that moves beyond the standard empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors correctly identify that MSE-based SR methods produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution images, rather than finding the most probable solution. They propose amortized MAP inference as a superior alternative, arguing that MAP estimates (the mode of the posterior) correspond to high-probability images under natural image priors. The key innovation is an affine projection layer that ensures SR outputs are consistent with the LR input (i.e., downsampling the SR output recovers the LR input), which transforms the problem into one of matching the marginal distribution of reconstructed images to that of natural high-resolution images. The paper then proposes three methods to train such models: AffGAN (using GANs with affine projection), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the SR literature by providing a theoretically sound alternative to MSE-based approaches that produces perceptually superior results. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) The paper correctly identifies a fundamental limitation of current SR methods (the averaging behavior of MSE loss), and (2) The proposed approach is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—amortized MAP inference for SR—addresses a critical problem in the field. The theoretical foundation in Section 3 is strong, particularly the derivation showing that MAP inference corresponds to minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The affine projection layer (Equation 7) is a clever and well-justified architectural modification that enables this approach to work while ensuring consistency between input and output.

The empirical results are convincing. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) clearly demonstrates that AffGAN and AffDG find the mode of the posterior distribution, while MSE and MAE models produce outputs in low-probability regions. The real image experiments (Figures 3-5) show that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE and other baselines, with the AffGAN model consistently producing visually superior outputs.

The paper also makes a valuable theoretical contribution by connecting GANs to variational inference in Appendix F, establishing a novel relationship between these two important frameworks in generative modeling.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements to enhance its impact:

1. **Perceptual Quality Validation**: The paper claims that AffGAN produces "visually appealing" results, but lacks quantitative perceptual evaluation beyond SSIM and PSNR. I recommend including a brief user study or additional perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) to better validate this claim.

2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper should more thoroughly compare to recent state-of-the-art SR methods, particularly other GAN-based approaches (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016). The claim that AffGAN produces "the most visually appealing results" needs stronger contextualization against current methods.

3. **Clarify Limitations**: The paper mentions limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6 but doesn't discuss how these limitations specifically affect the proposed methods. A more detailed analysis of when MAP inference might fail for SR would strengthen the paper.

4. **Technical Clarifications**: The explanation of why AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results (Section 5.3) is insufficient. The authors should provide more insight into why these methods underperformed compared to AffGAN, as this is crucial for understanding the relative strengths of the proposed approaches.

## Questions for Authors

1. In Section 5.3, you state that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results that you couldn't improve upon. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges with these methods, and whether these issues might be mitigated with different architectures or training procedures?

2. The paper claims AffGAN produces "slightly sharper images" but also "slightly more high-frequency noise." How do you balance this trade-off, and are there ways to control it in practice? Could this noise be reduced without sacrificing sharpness?

3. In Section 5.6, you mention that MAP estimates may not be unique under coordinate transformations. How does your affine projection layer affect this issue? Does it make the solution more or less dependent on the coordinate system?

4. You establish a connection between AffGAN and variational inference in Appendix F. Could you elaborate on how this insight might inform future work on SR, particularly regarding the trade-off between mode-seeking (MAP) and sampling-based approaches?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of image super-resolution. The theoretical insights are sound, the approach is well-motivated, and the empirical results are convincing. With the minor revisions suggested above, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
