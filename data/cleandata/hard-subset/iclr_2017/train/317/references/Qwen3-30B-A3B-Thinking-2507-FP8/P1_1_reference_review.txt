

# Peer Review: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, addressing a fundamental limitation of current SR methods. The authors correctly identify that MSE-based approaches produce blurry outputs because they optimize for the mean of the posterior distribution (Bayes-optimal for MSE), rather than the mode which aligns better with human perception. Their key contributions are:

1. An architectural innovation using an affine projection layer that ensures outputs are always consistent with the input (i.e., when downsampled, they exactly match the input), which they prove is crucial for MAP inference.

2. Three distinct methods to approximate MAP inference without requiring paired training data:
   - AffGAN: An affine-projected GAN that minimizes KL divergence
   - AffDG: A denoiser-guided approach that uses gradient estimates from a denoising network
   - AffLL: A density-guided approach using a generative model

The paper provides strong theoretical justification for their approach, supported by empirical results across multiple datasets (2D Swiss-roll, grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet). Notably, their AffGAN method produces visually sharper results than traditional MSE-based approaches while maintaining input consistency, with the authors showing how this approach connects to variational inference (Appendix F).

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the SR literature by addressing a fundamental limitation of current approaches (blurry outputs due to MSE optimization) with a well-motivated theoretical framework and empirically validated methods. The AffGAN approach represents a meaningful advancement that aligns with recent successful GAN-based SR work (Ledig et al., 2016) while providing stronger theoretical grounding.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper's main strengths are its theoretical soundness, clear problem statement, and compelling empirical results:

- **Strong theoretical motivation**: The 2D Swiss-roll example (Fig. 1a) clearly demonstrates why MSE optimization produces low-probability outputs while MAP inference finds high-probability solutions. The paper's derivation of how the affine projection ensures consistency (Section 3.1) is mathematically rigorous and well-justified.

- **Novel architectural contribution**: The affine projection layer (Eq. 7) is a clever solution to a fundamental problem in SR (consistency between input and output) that can be applied to any SR algorithm. The authors demonstrate this conceptually and empirically (Section 5.2).

- **Empirical validation**: The results across multiple datasets (5.1-5.5) consistently show that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible images than MSE, MAE, and even other GAN-based approaches. The cross-entropy measurements (Table 1) provide quantitative evidence that AffGAN minimizes the desired objective.

- **Theoretical connections**: The paper makes an important connection between GANs and variational inference (Appendix F), which is novel and potentially impactful for the broader generative modeling community.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution by introducing "instance noise" (Section 3.2.1) to stabilize GAN training, which could be useful beyond SR applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance its quality:

1. **Human evaluation**: The paper primarily uses quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) and qualitative visual comparisons. Adding a small human evaluation study would strengthen the claim about "perceptual quality" since PSNR/SSIM correlate poorly with human perception (as the authors correctly note in Section 1).

2. **Comparison to recent SR work**: The paper cites Ledig et al. (2016) but doesn't compare directly to more recent state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RDN, etc.). A brief comparison would help contextualize the contribution.

3. **Clarify limitations**: The paper mentions that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results (Section 5.3), but doesn't explore why this occurs or whether these limitations could be overcome. A brief discussion of these limitations would make the paper more balanced.

4. **Implementation details**: The paper mentions using "instance noise" with annealing, but doesn't specify how much it improved convergence or whether the noise schedule was sensitive to hyperparameters. More details would help reproducibility.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help me better assess the paper's contribution, I'd appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. In Section 5.6, you discuss the criticism that MAP estimates depend on the representation. You mention AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations due to the H[qθ] term. Could you provide a simple example (even in 1D) to illustrate this point more concretely?

2. In Figure 3, the AffGAN results for grass textures are visibly superior to other methods, but the paper doesn't quantify this difference beyond visual inspection. Could you provide a brief comparison of human evaluation scores or additional perceptual metrics?

3. For AffGAN, you mention that "the GAN model dreams up reasonable data" (Section 5.5, snake example). Could you elaborate on how this relates to the MAP inference objective, and whether this phenomenon occurs for other image classes?

4. The paper states that "the AffGAN model produces slightly sharper images which however also seem to contain slightly more high frequency noise." What is the relationship between this noise and the MAP inference objective? Does this noise represent a trade-off between sharpness and plausibility?

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the SR literature with a well-motivated approach that addresses a fundamental limitation of current methods. The theoretical insights, architectural innovation, and empirical results are compelling, and I believe this work would be of interest to the ICLR community.
