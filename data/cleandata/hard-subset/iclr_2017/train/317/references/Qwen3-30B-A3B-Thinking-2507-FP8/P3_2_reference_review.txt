

# Peer Review: "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that current methods using MSE loss produce blurry outputs due to averaging over the posterior distribution. The authors introduce an architectural innovation—the affine projection layer—that ensures outputs are always consistent with LR inputs. They then present three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (GAN-based), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The paper claims these methods produce sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE-based approaches, with empirical validation on synthetic and real datasets.

## 2. Decision: Reject

I recommend rejection of this paper primarily because the authors' central claim—that MAP inference produces superior perceptual results—is not sufficiently supported by the evidence. The paper fails to provide rigorous perceptual evaluation beyond PSNR/SSIM metrics (which the authors themselves acknowledge correlate poorly with human perception), and the theoretical justification for MAP inference is fundamentally flawed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Theoretical Flaw in MAP Justification**: The paper correctly identifies that MSE minimization produces blurry outputs by averaging the posterior. However, in Section 5.6, the authors themselves acknowledge the critical limitation that "the mode of a distribution is highly atypical" in high-dimensional spaces (using the d-dimensional normal example). This directly contradicts their main claim that MAP produces "plausible" reconstructions. The paper fails to provide a compelling argument for why this atypical mode is preferable to sampling from the posterior (which would produce more typical-looking images), despite acknowledging this problem.

**Lack of Rigorous Perceptual Evaluation**: The paper relies solely on qualitative examples and PSNR/SSIM metrics to claim perceptual superiority. As the authors note in the introduction, "MSE and the related peak-signal-to-noise-ratio (PSNR) metric have been shown to correlate poorly with human perception." The paper does not use any established perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID) or human evaluation studies to validate their claims. Figure 5's "water" example (where a snake is super-resolved into water) demonstrates the potential for MAP to produce plausible but incorrect reconstructions, yet the paper fails to address this critical issue.

**Inadequate Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares only with MSE, MAE, and SoftGAN, but fails to include comparisons with modern perceptual SR methods like ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2018) or the GAN-based approach by Ledig et al. (2016) that the paper cites. The AffGAN method appears very similar to Ledig et al.'s approach, but the paper doesn't clarify how it differs or improves upon this existing work.

**Methodological Concerns**: The paper presents AffDG and AffLL as viable alternatives but shows in Figure 3 that these methods produce "blurry" results that cannot be improved with various optimization methods. The paper provides no explanation for why these approaches fail, which undermines the claim that "three approaches" are equally valid. Additionally, the "instance noise" technique for GAN stabilization is presented as novel, but lacks rigorous justification or ablation studies.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct proper perceptual evaluation using human studies or established metrics like LPIPS, which correlate better with human perception than PSNR/SSIM.

2. Address the fundamental contradiction between the paper's claim of "plausible" reconstructions and the acknowledged atypicality of MAP estimates. The authors should either provide evidence that MAP estimates in image space are actually perceptually typical or compare their method with posterior sampling approaches.

3. Include comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art perceptual SR methods, not just MSE and SoftGAN.

4. Provide a clear explanation for why AffDG and AffLL fail to produce good results, and whether these methods can be salvaged with modifications.

5. Clarify the relationship between their AffGAN and Ledig et al.'s (2016) GAN-based SR method, explaining how their approach differs and why it might be better.

## 5. Key Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims AffGAN produces "perceptually superior" results but provides no evidence beyond PSNR/SSIM and qualitative examples. How would you validate this claim with metrics that correlate with human perception?

2. In Section 5.6, you acknowledge that MAP estimates can be highly atypical (e.g., the mode of a d-dimensional normal has norm 0 while typical samples have norm ~√d). How do you reconcile this with your claim that MAP produces "plausible" reconstructions?

3. Figure 5 shows AffGAN producing a plausible but incorrect reconstruction (snake → water). How do you evaluate the trade-off between perceptual plausibility and accuracy in your method?

4. The paper states that "AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations" due to the H[qθ] term. Can you provide evidence for this claim beyond the theoretical discussion in Appendix F?

5. Why did AffDG and AffLL fail to produce good results (Figure 3), and how would you modify these approaches to avoid the blurry outputs?

This paper presents an interesting architectural innovation (the affine projection layer) but falls short in its core claim about MAP inference producing superior perceptual results. Without rigorous perceptual evaluation and a stronger theoretical justification for why MAP is preferable to sampling from the posterior, the paper's main contribution lacks sufficient support.
