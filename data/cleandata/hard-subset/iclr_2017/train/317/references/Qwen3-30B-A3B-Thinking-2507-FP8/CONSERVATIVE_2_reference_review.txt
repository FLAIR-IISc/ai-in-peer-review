

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that current SR methods using MSE or MAE loss functions produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution of possible high-resolution images. The authors introduce an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, and present three methods to approximate MAP inference: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser guided), and AffLL (density guided). The paper claims these methods produce perceptually superior results compared to traditional MSE/MAE approaches, with AffGAN demonstrating the most promising results.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes significant claims about perceptual quality improvements without sufficient evidence, and the proposed approach lacks rigorous comparison against established state-of-the-art methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary issue with this paper is that it fails to provide adequate evidence for its core claim: that MAP inference produces perceptually superior SR results. While the theoretical motivation is sound, the empirical validation is insufficient for several key reasons:

First, the paper relies exclusively on PSNR and SSIM metrics for evaluation, which the authors themselves acknowledge correlate poorly with human perception. The paper claims to improve perceptual quality but provides no human evaluations, no comparative studies with established perceptual metrics (like LPIPS), and no ablation studies demonstrating the necessity of their key contribution (the affine projection layer).

Second, the paper presents three methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) but only AffGAN produces visually appealing results. The other two methods (AffDG and AffLL) consistently produce blurry outputs that the authors acknowledge are problematic and cannot be improved with various optimization methods. This raises serious questions about the robustness of the proposed framework, but the paper does not adequately address why only one of the three methods works well.

Third, the paper does not compare against recent state-of-the-art SR methods. For example, it doesn't compare against ESRGAN (which uses perceptual losses and GANs), RCAN, or other recent methods that have established perceptual quality improvements. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether AffGAN represents a meaningful advance over existing approaches.

The paper's theoretical arguments are strong, but the empirical evidence falls short of supporting the claims made. The authors claim to be addressing a significant limitation of current SR methods (blurry outputs due to MSE/MAE), but the evidence provided doesn't convincingly demonstrate that their approach solves this problem better than existing methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct human evaluation studies to validate their perceptual quality claims. Without human assessments, the paper cannot credibly claim perceptual improvements.

2. Provide comprehensive comparisons against recent state-of-the-art SR methods, not just MSE and MAE baselines. This would help establish whether the AffGAN approach represents a meaningful advance.

3. Address the fundamental question of why only AffGAN works well while AffDG and AffLL fail. This is a critical limitation of the proposed framework that needs explanation.

4. Provide a more thorough ablation study to demonstrate the necessity of the affine projection layer. The paper shows it improves consistency but doesn't convincingly demonstrate it improves perceptual quality.

5. Include results using standard perceptual metrics (LPIPS, FID) to complement PSNR/SSIM.

**Questions for the authors:**

1. Why does AffGAN produce visually superior results while AffDG and AffLL consistently fail? What fundamental difference in the underlying optimization process explains this discrepancy?

2. How does AffGAN compare to established GAN-based SR methods like ESRGAN or SRGAN? The authors reference Ledig et al. (2016) but don't provide a direct comparison.

3. Why does the paper not include any human evaluation studies to validate the claim of "improved perceptual quality"? Given that the authors acknowledge PSNR/SSIM correlate poorly with human perception, this omission is particularly problematic.

4. The paper claims that AffGAN minimizes cross-entropy H[qθ, pY], but the results in Table 1 show AffGAN has higher cross-entropy than the MAP solution. How can this be reconciled with the claim that AffGAN minimizes the desired quantity?

5. How does the proposed approach handle the issue of MAP being representation-dependent (discussed in Section 5.6)? The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't explain how their method addresses it.
