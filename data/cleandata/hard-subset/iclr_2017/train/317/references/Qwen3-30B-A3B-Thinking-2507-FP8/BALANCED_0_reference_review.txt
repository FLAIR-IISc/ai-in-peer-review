

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce an affine projection layer that ensures the SR network's output is always consistent with the low-resolution input, and present three methods for implementing amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoising), and AffLL (using density models). The paper provides theoretical motivation showing that these methods minimize the cross-entropy between the generated image distribution and the natural image prior, rather than minimizing MSE. The authors demonstrate that their approach produces sharper, more perceptually appealing results than MSE/MAE baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach to SR with a clear motivation, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the main claims, and the novelty of the approach is limited compared to recent work in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main weaknesses are:

First, the theoretical contribution is not sufficiently novel. The affine projection concept is straightforward and has been used in previous SR work (e.g., Shi et al., 2016). The connection between GANs and MAP inference has been discussed in prior work by Mohamed & Lakshminarayanan (2016), and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contribution from these existing results.

Second, the empirical evaluation is inadequate. The paper primarily compares against MSE and MAE baselines, but does not provide a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, SRGAN, or recent transformer-based approaches). While Figure 4 shows qualitative results for AffGAN, the paper lacks quantitative comparisons with these methods in terms of PSNR, SSIM, and perceptual metrics. The claim that AffGAN produces "visually appealing" results is not substantiated by objective metrics or human evaluations.

Third, the paper fails to address the limitations of MAP inference for SR. Section 5.6 rightly acknowledges that MAP estimates may not be perceptually realistic (as the mode of a distribution may not resemble a typical sample), but the paper does not discuss how this affects their approach or how they mitigate this issue. The example of the snake being reconstructed as water (Figure 5) demonstrates this limitation without providing a resolution.

Fourth, the paper's experimental design is limited. The AffDG and AffLL methods are presented as alternatives but are not competitive with AffGAN, and the paper does not provide a clear explanation for why these methods underperform. The authors also do not address the high-frequency noise observed in AffGAN outputs (mentioned in Section 5.4) or how this affects perceptual quality.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen this work for future publication, the authors should:

- Provide comprehensive quantitative comparisons against state-of-the-art SR methods, not just MSE and MAE baselines. Include metrics like PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, and FID to objectively evaluate performance.
- Conduct human evaluation studies to validate the perceptual quality claims. Without this, the paper cannot credibly claim that their method produces more "visually appealing" results.
- Expand the discussion of MAP inference limitations. Explain how their approach mitigates the issue raised in Section 5.6, or provide evidence that their method produces results that are perceptually indistinguishable from ground truth.
- Include ablation studies to better understand the contributions of each component (affine projection, GAN training, instance noise).
- Provide more details on the implementation of the affine projection layer and how it affects the network's learning dynamics.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How does AffGAN compare to state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, SRGAN) in terms of both quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) and perceptual quality? Have you conducted any comparisons against these methods?

2. The paper states that "the AffGAN model produces slightly sharper images which however also seem to contain slightly more high frequency noise." How do you address this issue of high-frequency noise in your method, and how does it impact perceptual quality?

3. The paper claims that MAP inference produces plausible reconstructions, but Figure 5 shows a case where the reconstruction (snake → water) is obviously wrong but "plausible." How do you reconcile this with the claim that MAP inference produces perceptually realistic results?

4. Why do AffDG and AffLL underperform compared to AffGAN, and what specific aspects of the training process or model architecture lead to this difference? Could these methods be salvaged with better hyperparameter tuning or architectural modifications?

5. The paper mentions that "the snake depicted in the third column is super resolved into water which is obviously wrong but still a very plausible image considering the LR input image." How do you measure or validate this "plausibility" beyond visual inspection?
