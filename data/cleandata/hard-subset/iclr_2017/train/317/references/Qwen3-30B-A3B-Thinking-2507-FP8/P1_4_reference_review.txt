

# Peer Review: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from the standard empirical risk minimization (ERM) framework to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors correctly identify that MSE-based SR methods produce blurry outputs because they average over all plausible high-resolution images consistent with the low-resolution input. Instead, the paper proposes to select the mode of the posterior distribution, which should correspond to more perceptually plausible images.

The key contributions are:
1. An affine projection layer that ensures the model output is always consistent with the input (i.e., downsampling the output gives back the input), which is a critical architectural innovation.
2. Three methods for amortized MAP inference: Affine Projected GAN (AffGAN), Denoiser Guided Super-Resolution (AffDG), and Density Guided Super-Resolution (AffLL).
3. Theoretical grounding showing that MAP inference minimizes the cross-entropy between the model output distribution and the natural image prior.
4. A connection between AffGAN and variational inference, establishing a novel link between GANs and amortized variational inference.

The paper provides thorough theoretical analysis, a convincing 2D toy experiment, and extensive experiments on real image datasets showing that AffGAN produces perceptually superior results compared to MSE, MAE, and other GAN-based methods.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:
1. The paper addresses a significant limitation in current SR methods with a well-motivated theoretical approach that is clearly positioned within the literature.
2. The proposed methods are novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated across multiple challenging datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical foundation is exceptionally strong. The authors clearly demonstrate that minimizing MSE leads to averaging over the posterior distribution, while MAP inference selects the mode, which by definition has high probability under the natural image prior. Their derivation of the affine projection layer is elegant and well-justified, ensuring consistency between input and output without requiring paired training data.

The empirical results are comprehensive and convincing. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) provides a clear demonstration that AffGAN and AffDG indeed minimize the cross-entropy objective, while MSE/MAE fail. The ablation study in Section 5.2 convincingly shows that the affine projection is beneficial for performance, not just a theoretical curiosity. The grass texture experiment (Section 5.3) is particularly compelling as it demonstrates a case where MSE/MAE fail catastrophically while AffGAN succeeds.

The connection to variational inference (Section F) is a significant theoretical contribution that elevates the paper beyond just a practical SR method. This is the first time a connection between GANs and variational inference has been established for SR, providing deeper theoretical grounding for the approach.

The paper also provides a valuable technical contribution with their "instance noise" trick (Section 3.2.1), which addresses the well-known instability of GAN training. This is a practical contribution that could benefit the broader GAN community.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance it further:

1. **Clarify the difference between AffGAN and other GAN-based SR methods**: The paper mentions Ledig et al. (2016) and Garcia (2016), but doesn't clearly articulate how AffGAN differs from these approaches in a way that explains its superior performance. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Expand the discussion of computational cost**: While the affine projection is theoretically elegant, it's important to understand the practical computational cost. How does the inference time compare to standard SR methods? Is the affine projection a significant bottleneck?

3. **Address the limitations of MAP inference more thoroughly**: The paper mentions the criticism in Section 5.6 about MAP estimates being atypical (e.g., the mode of a Gaussian has norm zero while typical samples have norm √d), but doesn't discuss how this might manifest in their results. A brief discussion of this limitation in the context of their results would strengthen the paper.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In Section 5.3, AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results while AffGAN produces sharp images. What specific aspect of AffGAN's approach (vs. the other methods) enables it to avoid this blurriness? Is it related to the KL divergence minimization vs. cross-entropy?

2. The authors mention that AffGAN "favors approximate MAP solutions that lead to higher entropy and thus more diverse solutions overall" (Section 3.2). How does this theoretical advantage manifest in the visual quality of the results, particularly in cases like the grass textures where the output is not pixel-perfect but has the correct statistical properties?

3. In the ImageNet results (Section 5.5), the snake is super-resolved into water. Given that water has higher density under the natural image prior, how do the authors interpret this result? Does this indicate that the model is actually finding a more probable reconstruction than the original snake, or is this a case where the model is "dreaming up" plausible data?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by providing a theoretically grounded alternative to the standard MSE-based approach. The proposed methods are novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated across multiple challenging datasets. The connection to variational inference is particularly exciting and opens up new theoretical directions for future work. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR conference.
