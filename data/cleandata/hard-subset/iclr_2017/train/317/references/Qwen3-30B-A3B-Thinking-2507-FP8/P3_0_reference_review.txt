

# Peer Review: "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

**Summary of Contributions:**
This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that standard MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution rather than finding the mode. The authors introduce an elegant affine projection layer that ensures SR outputs are consistent with the LR input (i.e., downsampling the SR output recovers the LR input), and propose three methods to implement amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoising), and AffLL (using density models). The paper provides strong theoretical motivation, including a clear derivation of the affine projection layer and its connection to MAP inference. Empirical results on a 2D toy problem and real image datasets demonstrate that AffGAN produces sharper images than MSE-based methods while maintaining input-output consistency.

**Decision:**
Accept with minor revisions.

**Key Reasons:**
1. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by rigorously connecting MAP inference to SR and introducing the affine projection layer as a key enabler.
2. The empirical results generally support the main claim that MAP inference produces sharper images than MSE-based methods, with AffGAN showing the most promising results.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The paper's theoretical foundation is exceptionally strong. The derivation of the affine projection layer (Section 3.1) is clear and well-motivated, and the connection to MAP inference is logically sound. The authors successfully demonstrate that their method minimizes the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the HR image distribution, which aligns with the goal of MAP inference.

The empirical results are generally convincing. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) clearly shows that AffGAN and AffDG find the mode of the posterior, while MSE and MAE models produce outputs in low-probability regions. The AffGAN results on real images (Section 5.5) show sharper outputs than MSE-based methods, which aligns with theoretical claims.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing before acceptance:

1. The authors discard AffDG and AffLL early in the experiments (Section 5.3) without providing a thorough explanation for their failure. They claim these methods produced "blurry results" but don't provide quantitative evidence or detailed analysis of why they underperform. This weakens the paper's claim that AffGAN is superior.

2. The perceptual quality claims are not sufficiently supported. While the paper claims AffGAN produces "visually appealing" results, it lacks human studies or rigorous perceptual metrics beyond PSNR and SSIM. The paper should either provide additional perceptual evaluation or temper its claims about perceptual quality.

3. The paper could better position its work in the literature. While it references Ledig et al. (2016), it doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs from or improves upon existing GAN-based SR methods. The connection between their work and prior GAN-based SR approaches needs more explicit discussion.

**Additional Feedback:**
- The paper should provide a more detailed analysis of why AffDG and AffLL fail, including quantitative comparisons of perceptual quality. This would strengthen the claim that AffGAN is the superior method.
- Include additional perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID) to better support claims about visual quality beyond PSNR and SSIM.
- Clarify how the affine projection layer specifically improves upon existing GAN-based SR methods, particularly in comparison to Ledig et al. (2016).
- Expand the discussion of MAP inference limitations (Section 5.6) to address how the affine projection might mitigate coordinate transformation issues.

**Questions for Authors:**
1. Why do AffDG and AffLL fail to produce sharp results while AffGAN succeeds? Can you provide quantitative metrics to support your perceptual quality claims?
2. How does your affine projection layer specifically improve upon existing GAN-based SR methods, particularly Ledig et al.'s approach?
3. Can you provide evidence that the "plausible" images produced by AffGAN (e.g., the snake-to-water example in Figure 5) are actually preferred by human observers?

**Conclusion:**
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to SR by rigorously connecting MAP inference to SR and providing a practical implementation through the affine projection layer. The empirical results support the main claim that MAP inference produces sharper images than MSE-based methods. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses in empirical evaluation and literature positioning, this paper would be a strong contribution to ICLR. The theoretical insights and practical implementation make this work particularly compelling for the computer vision and machine learning communities.
