

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that current SR methods using MSE loss produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution rather than finding the mode. The authors introduce an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs, and propose three methods to perform amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (affine projected denoiser guided), and AffLL (affine projected density guided). The paper provides theoretical justification for their approach and demonstrates on both a 2D toy problem and real image datasets that their methods produce sharper outputs than MSE-based approaches.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework but fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims, particularly regarding perceptual quality improvements, which is the main motivation for the proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is the theoretical justification for using MAP inference in SR, which is well-motivated by the known limitations of MSE-based approaches. However, the paper falls short on three critical aspects:

First, the paper claims that MAP inference produces "visually appealing" results but provides no perceptual evaluation beyond PSNR and SSIM, which are known to correlate poorly with human perception (Laparra et al., 2016). The authors themselves cite this limitation in the introduction, yet they fail to provide any perceptual metrics or human evaluation to validate their claim that their method produces superior perceptual quality. This is a fundamental flaw in the paper's empirical validation.

Second, the paper fails to adequately compare with state-of-the-art methods. The authors mention Ledig et al. (2016) but do not compare their results with this work or other recent GAN-based SR methods. For example, the paper doesn't compare with ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2018), which has become the standard for perceptual quality in SR. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine if the proposed approach represents a meaningful improvement.

Third, the paper's main claim that AffGAN produces "visually appealing" results is contradicted by the qualitative results shown in Figures 4-5, which reveal "high-frequency noise" in the outputs. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation but fail to provide any analysis of why this occurs or how it compares to other GAN-based SR methods. The paper also doesn't explain why AffDG and AffLL, which are theoretically sound, fail to produce good results on the grass texture dataset (Section 5.3).

The paper's theoretical contributions are interesting but insufficiently validated. The connection to variational inference (Section F) is only theoretical and not empirically verified, and the proposed "instance noise" trick (Section 3.2.1) is not rigorously evaluated beyond a single toy example.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide comprehensive perceptual evaluation using metrics like LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) or human evaluation, which would directly address their main motivation for the work.

2. Conduct thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art SR methods, including both MSE-based and GAN-based approaches.

3. Investigate why AffDG and AffLL fail to produce good results, as this is a significant limitation of their framework.

4. Address the high-frequency noise issue in AffGAN outputs and compare with methods that have successfully mitigated this problem.

5. Provide more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between perceptual quality and quantitative metrics like PSNR/SSIM.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. How does your method compare to ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2018) in terms of perceptual quality, and why does your method produce high-frequency noise while ESRGAN does not?

2. Given that PSNR and SSIM are known to correlate poorly with human perception (Laparra et al., 2016), why did you not use perceptual metrics or human evaluation to validate your main claim?

3. Can you provide a detailed explanation for why AffDG and AffLL fail to produce good results on the grass texture dataset, and why you chose to abandon these approaches?

4. How does your "instance noise" technique compare to other stabilization techniques for GANs, such as spectral normalization or gradient penalty?

5. What are the computational costs of your approach compared to standard SR methods, and how does this impact practical applicability?

The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework but fails to provide the necessary empirical validation to support its claims about perceptual quality improvements. Without addressing these critical shortcomings, the paper cannot be accepted.
