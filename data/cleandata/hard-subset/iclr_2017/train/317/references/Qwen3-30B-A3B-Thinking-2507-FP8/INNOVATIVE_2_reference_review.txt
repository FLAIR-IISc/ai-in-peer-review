

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a fundamentally new perspective on image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization (ERM) to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors correctly identify a critical limitation of current SR methods: MSE-based approaches produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution, while natural images tend to have high probability at the mode of the posterior. The paper introduces an affine projection layer that ensures outputs are consistent with the LR input (downsampling the output gives back the input), enabling the use of natural image statistics rather than pixel-wise losses. The authors then propose three novel methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs with affine projection), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The paper establishes theoretical grounding for why MAP inference is desirable and demonstrates through experiments that AffGAN produces perceptually superior SR results compared to MSE and soft-constrained GAN approaches.

## Decision
Accept.

## Key Reasons for Acceptance
1. **Novel Perspective**: The paper introduces a bold, novel conceptual shift from ERM to MAP inference for SR. This is not just an incremental improvement but a fundamental rethinking of the problem. The authors correctly identify that the core issue with current SR methods is their reliance on MSE (which produces the mean of the posterior) rather than the mode, which aligns better with human perception.

2. **Theoretical and Practical Innovation**: The affine projection layer is a clever technical contribution that enables exact consistency between input and output. More importantly, the paper establishes a connection between GANs and MAP inference (via KL divergence minimization) and demonstrates how this leads to perceptually superior results. The theoretical grounding in Section 3 is solid and well-motivated.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's novelty is substantial and aligns with ICLR's emphasis on bold, novel ideas over incremental methodological improvements. The authors demonstrate that their approach fundamentally changes how we think about SR, moving beyond pixel-wise losses to leveraging natural image statistics. The AffGAN method produces visually sharper results than MSE and soft-constrained GAN approaches, as shown in Figures 3-5, with the AffGAN outputs being the most plausible to human observers.

The paper's theoretical analysis (Section 3) is particularly strong. The authors derive how MAP inference relates to minimizing cross-entropy, and they show how the affine projection layer enables this minimization without requiring paired data. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could influence future work in inverse problems beyond SR.

The empirical results are compelling, especially in the 2D Swiss-roll toy example (Figure 1b) and the grass texture experiments (Figure 3), where AffGAN produces sharp, plausible results while MSE and soft-constrained methods fail. The cross-entropy values in Table 1 directly support the theoretical claims, showing that AffGAN and AffDG achieve values close to the MAP solution.

While the paper acknowledges that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results (Section 5.3), this doesn't undermine the main contribution, which is the novel perspective and the successful AffGAN approach. The paper establishes a clear connection between GANs and MAP inference that is theoretically sound and empirically validated.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement
1. The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art SR methods beyond MSE and GAN-based approaches. While the paper focuses on the conceptual shift, a brief comparison with recent SOTA methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) would strengthen the empirical claims.

2. The paper mentions that AffGAN produces high-frequency noise (Section 5.4), but doesn't explore this limitation in depth or propose solutions. A brief discussion of potential ways to address this would be valuable.

3. The connection between MAP inference and perceptual quality could be strengthened. While the paper acknowledges that "the mode lies in a high-probability region" (Section 5.6), it would be helpful to provide more intuition or evidence for why this leads to better perceptual quality.

4. The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of why AffGAN works better than AffDG and AffLL. The current explanation (Section 5.3) is somewhat vague; a more detailed analysis of the differences between the methods would strengthen the paper.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "the GAN based approach, AffGAN produced the most visually appealing results" (Section 6). Could the authors provide a qualitative comparison with recent SOTA SR methods (e.g., EDSR, RCAN) on standard datasets like Set5, Set14, and BSD100?

2. The paper mentions that "the snake depicted in the third column is super resolved into water which is obviously wrong but still a very plausible image" (Section 5.5). How does the authors' approach handle such cases where the MAP estimate produces a plausible but factually incorrect image? Is this a limitation of the MAP approach or a feature?

3. In Appendix F, the authors establish a connection between AffGAN and variational inference. Could the authors elaborate on how this connection might inform future work on stochastic SR methods, particularly in terms of addressing the high-frequency noise issue?
