

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper tackles a fundamental limitation in image super-resolution (SR): current methods using MSE loss produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution images, rather than finding the most probable solution. The authors propose using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, which finds the mode of the posterior distribution, leading to sharper and more perceptually plausible results. They introduce a novel affine projection layer that ensures SR outputs are consistent with the input LR image, and develop three methods to approximate MAP inference: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser guided), and AffLL (density guided). The paper provides strong theoretical grounding for their approach and demonstrates empirical success on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework and novel architectural innovation (affine projection), but the empirical evidence does not sufficiently support the main claim that MAP inference leads to perceptually superior results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim is that MAP inference produces perceptually better SR results than MSE-based methods. However, the empirical validation is insufficient for this claim. The authors primarily rely on PSNR and SSIM metrics, which they themselves acknowledge correlate poorly with human perception (Section 1). While they show that AffGAN produces sharper images visually, they do not provide any user studies or perceptual evaluation metrics (e.g., human ratings, FID scores) to support their claim that these images are perceptually superior.

Furthermore, the paper fails to compare against relevant state-of-the-art methods. Recent work such as SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016) and ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2018) have demonstrated significant improvements in perceptual quality using GANs with perceptual losses. The authors do not compare against these methods, making it impossible to assess whether their approach represents an improvement over current practice.

The paper's theoretical contribution is sound but not particularly novel. While the affine projection layer is clever, the connection between GANs and MAP inference has been established in prior work (e.g., Mohamed & Lakshminarayanan, 2016). The paper's main contribution appears to be an application of existing techniques rather than a fundamental advance.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct user studies to evaluate perceptual quality, as this is the core claim of the paper. The authors could recruit participants to rate image quality on a Likert scale or perform a preference test between their method and baselines.

2. Compare against recent state-of-the-art SR methods that use perceptual losses (e.g., SRGAN, ESRGAN, RCAN). Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess the significance of their contribution.

3. Provide a more thorough analysis of why AffDG and AffLL underperform compared to AffGAN. The paper notes they produce blurry results but doesn't explore the root causes.

4. Include computational complexity analysis comparing training time and inference speed with relevant baselines.

5. Clarify the relationship between their work and the recently proposed "Perceptual Loss" methods (Laparra et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2004), which have been shown to improve perceptual quality without requiring MAP inference.

## 5. Questions for Clarification

1. The paper claims AffGAN produces "visually appealing" results, but the evaluation relies on PSNR/SSIM. How do the authors justify using these metrics to support their perceptual quality claim, given that they acknowledge their poor correlation with human perception?

2. Why doesn't the paper compare against SRGAN (Ledig et al., 2016) or ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2018), which are the current state-of-the-art for perceptual quality in SR? What is the specific advantage of their approach over these methods?

3. In Section 5.3, the authors state they "choose not to perform any further experiments with these models [AffDG and AffLL] and concentrate on AffGAN instead" due to their blurry results. Can the authors provide a theoretical explanation for why AffGAN outperforms the other methods, rather than simply discarding the others?

4. The paper claims to provide "theoretical grounding for using GANs in image SR" (Section 1). How does this theoretical grounding differ from the work of Ledig et al. (2016), which also used GANs for SR without this specific theoretical justification?
