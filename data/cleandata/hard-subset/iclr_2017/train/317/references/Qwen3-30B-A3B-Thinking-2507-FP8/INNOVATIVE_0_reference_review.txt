

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary
This paper proposes a fundamentally new approach to image super-resolution by shifting from empirical risk minimization (using MSE loss) to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that current SR methods produce blurry outputs because they minimize the mean of the posterior distribution rather than finding the mode, which would yield outputs in high-probability regions of natural image priors. They introduce an elegant architectural innovation - an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs - and develop three methods to approximate MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoising), and AffLL (using density models). The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear connections to probabilistic modeling concepts, and the empirical results on synthetic and real datasets demonstrate that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually pleasing results compared to MSE/MAE baselines.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new concept that fundamentally reframes the image super-resolution problem, representing a bold and novel direction in the field. While some aspects of the proposed methods require further validation, the core idea is compelling and well-motivated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's strongest point is its conceptual novelty. The authors successfully identify a fundamental limitation in current SR methods (the mean vs. mode problem) and propose a principled solution that shifts the paradigm from MSE-based learning to MAP inference. This is not an incremental improvement but a new direction that could significantly impact the field. The theoretical grounding is exceptionally strong, with clear connections to established concepts in probabilistic modeling (e.g., the connection between denoising and gradient estimation of log probability), and the affine projection layer elegantly solves the consistency constraint problem.

The empirical results provide compelling evidence for the approach. On the synthetic Swiss-roll dataset, AffGAN and AffDG successfully approximate MAP inference, while MSE and MAE produce outputs in low-probability regions. On real image datasets (grass textures, faces, natural images), AffGAN consistently produces sharper, more perceptually pleasing results compared to MSE and MAE baselines. The visual results (Figures 3-5) clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.

While the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art SR methods, the core contribution stands on its own as a significant conceptual advance. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach addresses a fundamental limitation in current SR methods, which aligns with ICLR's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art SR methods, particularly those using perceptual losses (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016). While the authors cite this work, they don't directly compare their AffGAN method against it, which would provide a more complete picture of the contribution.

- The authors note that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results but don't thoroughly explain why. A deeper analysis of why these approaches underperform compared to AffGAN would provide valuable insights for the community.

- The paper could benefit from a clearer discussion of the computational cost of the affine projection layer and the proposed methods compared to standard SR approaches. While the paper mentions that affine projected networks train faster (Section 5.2), the exact computational overhead is not quantified.

- The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is intriguing but not fully leveraged. A brief discussion of how this might inform future work on stochastic SR would be valuable.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the computational complexity of AffGAN compare to standard SR methods like EDSR or RCAN? Does the affine projection layer add significant overhead?

2. In Figure 5, the snake example is super-resolved into water. Could the authors provide more insight into why the model made this specific error, and how it relates to the MAP inference approach?

3. The authors note that AffGAN produces slightly sharper images but with more high-frequency noise. How do they suggest addressing this trade-off in future work?

4. The paper mentions that AffDG and AffLL are not as successful as AffGAN. Could they provide a more detailed analysis of why these approaches fail to match the quality of AffGAN, particularly given the theoretical connections between denoising and log probability estimation?
