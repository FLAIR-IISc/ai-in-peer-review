

# Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors address a fundamental limitation of current SR methods: MSE-based approaches produce blurry outputs because they average over the multimodal posterior distribution of possible HR images. The key contributions include: (1) an affine projection layer that ensures SR outputs are consistent with LR inputs by projecting to the affine subspace of valid solutions; (2) three methods to approximate MAP inference (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) that minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior; and (3) theoretical analysis connecting MAP inference to cross-entropy minimization. The paper demonstrates that their approach produces sharper, more perceptually appealing results compared to MSE-based baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a compelling case for using MAP inference in SR, provides sound theoretical grounding, and demonstrates clear improvements in perceptual quality through rigorous empirical validation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper is well-motivated by a clear problem statement: MSE minimization leads to averaging over the posterior distribution, producing blurry outputs that lack perceptual quality. The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear derivation showing that MAP inference in SR corresponds to minimizing cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The affine projection layer is well-justified and provides a practical solution to ensure consistency between input and output.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous. The 2D Swiss-roll toy experiment provides clear evidence that AffGAN and AffDG successfully minimize the MAP objective, while MSE/MAE methods produce outputs in low-probability regions. On real image datasets (grass textures, CelebA faces, ImageNet), AffGAN produces visually sharper results that better match human perception, despite lower PSNR/SSIM scores. The ablation studies (Section 5.2) demonstrate that the affine projection layer improves training speed and final performance without compromising the network's representational capacity.

The paper also makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting GANs to variational inference (Appendix F), establishing a novel relationship between adversarial methods and amortized variational inference.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could provide more detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art SR methods beyond the ones tested (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN) to better establish the relative performance of AffGAN.
- The authors should clarify why AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results (Section 5.3) and provide deeper analysis of the failure modes for these methods.
- The computational cost of AffGAN compared to standard SR methods (MSE-based or GAN-based) should be explicitly discussed, as this is important for practical applications.
- The claim that "training becomes more akin to training generative models" should be more precisely qualified, as the authors still require HR images for training (to estimate the natural image prior).

## Questions for Authors
1. In Figure 5, the AffGAN model super-resolves a snake into water. How does the model determine that water is more probable under the natural image prior than snakes? Could you provide more details about the image prior used for training?

2. The paper states that AffGAN produces "slightly sharper images" than SoftGAN, but the difference appears subtle in Figure 4. Could you provide a more detailed perceptual comparison or quantitative metrics that better capture this difference?

3. The paper claims that their approach "doesn't need corresponding HR and LR image pairs," but they still require HR images to train the natural image prior. How does this compare to completely unsupervised SR methods that don't require HR images at all?

4. The authors use instance noise with annealing. What is the optimal noise schedule for different upscaling factors (e.g., 4x vs 8x), and how sensitive is the training to this hyperparameter?

5. The paper establishes a connection between AffGAN and variational inference (Appendix F). How does this relationship inform the design of future SR methods, and what are the limitations of this connection?
