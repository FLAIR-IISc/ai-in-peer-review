

# Review: AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than the standard empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors compellingly argue that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they average over the multimodal distribution of plausible HR images, resulting in outputs that lie in low-probability regions under the natural image prior. The paper introduces three key contributions: (1) an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs by projecting model outputs to the affine subspace of valid solutions; (2) three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser guided), and AffLL (density guided); and (3) a theoretical connection between AffGAN and variational inference. The paper provides strong theoretical motivation with a clear 2D toy example, thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets (grass textures, CelebA faces, ImageNet), and a promising instance noise technique to stabilize GAN training.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the SR literature with a well-motivated approach that addresses a fundamental limitation of current methods. The main reason for acceptance is the strong theoretical foundation for MAP inference in SR, which is both novel and well-justified. A minor concern is the lack of rigorous evidence for perceptual superiority beyond visual inspection, but this can be addressed with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in several critical aspects:

First, the theoretical motivation is exceptionally strong. The 2D Swiss-roll toy example (Figure 1) clearly demonstrates why MSE produces blurry outputs while MAP inference finds the mode in high-probability regions. This fundamental insight is missing from most SR work, which typically treats MSE as a practical necessity rather than a conceptual limitation.

Second, the affine projection layer is a clever architectural innovation that solves a critical consistency problem. By ensuring Afθ(x) = x through the projection ΠAx, the authors enable training without paired data and create a clean connection between SR and generative modeling. The mathematical derivation in Section 3.1 is rigorous and well-motivated.

Third, the AffGAN method delivers the most compelling empirical results. Figure 3 shows that AffGAN produces sharp, plausible grass textures where MSE and other methods produce blurry results. The quantitative results in Table 1 and Figures 2-5 consistently show that AffGAN achieves lower cross-entropy (closer to the MAP solution) while maintaining perfect input-output consistency.

The paper's connection to variational inference (Appendix F) is a particularly valuable theoretical contribution that establishes a novel relationship between GANs and variational inference methods. This is a significant advance beyond the common practice of using GANs as a black box.

The main weakness is the lack of rigorous evidence for perceptual superiority beyond visual inspection. While the paper acknowledges that PSNR/SSIM correlate poorly with human perception, it doesn't provide stronger evidence for the perceptual quality of AffGAN outputs (e.g., user studies, LPIPS scores, or other perceptual metrics). This is a common issue in SR literature, but it's important to address more thoroughly.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Provide more evidence for perceptual superiority**: Include quantitative results using perceptual metrics like LPIPS, or conduct a small user study comparing AffGAN with other methods. The paper states AffGAN produces "visually appealing results" but doesn't provide convincing evidence beyond visual inspection.

2. **Explain AffDG/AffLL underperformance**: The paper notes that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results that couldn't be improved, but doesn't provide a thorough explanation. A brief discussion of why these methods underperform compared to AffGAN would strengthen the paper.

3. **Address high-frequency noise**: The paper notes that AffGAN produces "slightly more high frequency noise" but doesn't discuss this trade-off. A more detailed analysis of this characteristic would be valuable for practitioners.

4. **Clarify the relationship between MAP and perceptual quality**: While the paper correctly notes that MAP finds the mode (which is in a high-probability region), it could better address the potential disconnect between high-probability and perceptually pleasing. The discussion in Section 5.6 on this topic is relevant but could be expanded.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide more details on why AffDG and AffLL underperform compared to AffGAN? Are there specific architectural modifications or training techniques that could improve their performance?

2. Have you tried using LPIPS or other perceptual metrics to quantify the perceptual quality of AffGAN outputs? How do these metrics compare to the PSNR/SSIM results presented in the paper?

3. The paper notes that AffGAN produces "slightly more high-frequency noise" - could you elaborate on whether this is a general characteristic of MAP inference approaches, and how it might be mitigated?

4. The paper claims "the AffGAN model produces slightly sharper images which however also seem to contain slightly more high frequency noise" - how do you balance this trade-off in practical applications, and would this noise affect downstream tasks like object recognition?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the SR literature by providing a theoretically sound alternative to MSE-based approaches. The AffGAN method delivers compelling results that are perceptually superior to current state-of-the-art, and the theoretical connection to variational inference is particularly valuable. With minor revisions addressing the perceptual quality evidence, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
