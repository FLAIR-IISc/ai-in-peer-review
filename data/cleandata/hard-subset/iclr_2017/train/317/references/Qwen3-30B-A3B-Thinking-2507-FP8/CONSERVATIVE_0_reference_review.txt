

# Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than traditional empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors introduce an affine projection layer that ensures the network output is always consistent with the LR input (downsampling the HR output reproduces the LR input). They then present three methods to implement MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoisers), and AffLL (using density models). The authors claim their methods produce perceptually superior results compared to standard MSE/MAE-based approaches, with AffGAN showing the most promising results.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its core claim that MAP inference produces perceptually superior results, and it selectively reports results while ignoring significant limitations.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper fails to provide adequate evidence that MAP inference produces perceptually better results, which is the central claim of the paper. The authors acknowledge that their AffGAN method produces worse PSNR and SSIM scores than MSE-based methods (Table 2), which are the standard metrics for SR. This contradicts their claim of "improved perceptual quality" since PSNR/SSIM are correlated with human perception (Wang et al., 2004). The paper provides no human evaluation data to support the claim that sharper images are perceptually better.

The paper also selectively reports results, focusing only on AffGAN while discarding AffDG and AffLL due to poor performance (Section 5.3). The authors don't provide a thorough analysis of why these methods fail, undermining confidence in their approach. The paper claims these methods "produced blurry results which we were unable to improve upon," yet they don't investigate why these approaches fail or how they might be improved.

The theoretical foundation for MAP inference is weak. Section 5.6 explicitly acknowledges that MAP estimates can be highly atypical (e.g., the mode of a normal distribution has norm 0 while typical samples have norm √d) and that the mode depends on the representation. This directly contradicts the paper's central claim that MAP inference produces "plausible" results.

The paper doesn't compare to recent state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, which is a more advanced GAN-based approach). Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if their approach represents a meaningful improvement over existing methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide human evaluation studies to support their claim of "improved perceptual quality." This is essential to validate their core claim.

2. Compare their method to recent state-of-the-art SR approaches, not just MSE-based baselines. The paper should demonstrate how their method advances the field beyond existing work.

3. Provide a thorough analysis of why AffDG and AffLL fail, rather than simply discarding them. Understanding these limitations would strengthen the paper.

4. Clarify the relationship between their affine projection layer and existing SR methods that already ensure consistency (e.g., using deconvolution layers in SR networks).

5. Address the theoretical limitations of MAP inference discussed in Section 5.6. How do these limitations affect the practical performance of their method?

Questions for the authors:

1. How do you reconcile the fact that your AffGAN method produces worse PSNR/SSIM scores than MSE-based methods, yet you claim it produces better perceptual quality? Given that PSNR/SSIM correlate with human perception (Wang et al., 2004), how do you explain this discrepancy?

2. Can you provide human evaluation results to support your claim that AffGAN produces better perceptual quality?

3. Why did you discard AffDG and AffLL as inferior methods without providing a thorough analysis of their failure modes? How might these methods be improved?

4. How does your method compare to recent state-of-the-art SR approaches like ESRGAN (which is a more advanced GAN-based SR method)?
