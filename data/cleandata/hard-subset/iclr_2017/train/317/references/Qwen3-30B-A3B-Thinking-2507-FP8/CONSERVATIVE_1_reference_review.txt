

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
The paper proposes an alternative approach to image super-resolution (SR) that moves away from traditional MSE-based empirical risk minimization toward amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that MSE training produces blurry outputs because it computes the mean of the posterior distribution, while MAP inference would produce outputs with higher probability under the natural image prior. They introduce three methods based on affine projection: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser guided), and AffLL (density guided). The paper claims these methods minimize the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] between the model output distribution and the natural image prior, and presents empirical results on a 2D toy problem and limited image datasets showing improved perceptual quality over MSE and MAE.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation against established baselines and fails to provide rigorous evidence supporting its key claims about MAP inference for SR.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim that MAP inference produces perceptually superior results lacks sufficient evidence. While the authors present a theoretical argument and a 2D toy example (Section 5.1), they fail to demonstrate this on standard image SR benchmarks where it would matter. The empirical results are limited to:

- A 2D toy problem (Section 5.1), which doesn't translate to real-world image SR
- Grass textures (Section 5.3), where AffGAN shows some improvement but no comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods
- CelebA faces (Section 5.4) and ImageNet (Section 5.5), with only qualitative comparisons against MSE and MAE

Most critically, the paper does not compare against established SR methods like ESRGAN, RCAN, or other GAN-based approaches (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine if AffGAN actually outperforms current state-of-the-art methods. The paper's claim that "the GAN based approach, AffGAN produced the most visually appealing results" is unsubstantiated by any quantitative metrics on standard benchmarks.

The theoretical justification is also inconsistent. The authors claim to minimize the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] but then state that AffGAN minimizes KL[qθ||pY]. As they acknowledge, these are different quantities (H[qθ, pY] = KL[qθ||pY] + H[qθ]), and the paper fails to clarify this important distinction. The theoretical foundation for their approach is therefore undermined by this inconsistency.

The paper also dismisses AffDG and AffLL as producing "blurry results" without providing adequate explanation or comparison to other methods that use denoising or density models. This is particularly concerning as the paper claims these methods were theoretically designed to minimize the same objective as AffGAN.

Finally, the paper acknowledges the limitations of MAP inference in high dimensions (Section 5.6) but doesn't adequately address how these limitations affect their approach for image SR. The mode of a high-dimensional distribution can be highly atypical (e.g., zero-norm for Gaussian), which contradicts the paper's claim that MAP produces "plausible" images.

## 4. Additional Feedback

**Key improvements needed:**

1. **Comprehensive benchmarking**: The paper must include quantitative comparisons against state-of-the-art SR methods on standard benchmarks (DIV2K, Set5, Set14) using established metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) and provide ablation studies on the proposed methods.

2. **Theoretical clarification**: The authors must clearly reconcile the claimed objective (minimizing cross-entropy) with the GAN objective (minimizing KL divergence). This is a fundamental theoretical inconsistency that undermines their approach.

3. **Addressing MAP limitations**: The paper should provide evidence that the MAP estimates produced by AffGAN are perceptually better than stochastic approaches (like variational inference, which they mention in Appendix F) rather than just qualitatively stating that they're "sharper."

4. **Comparison with other methods**: The authors should compare AffGAN with other GAN-based SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, SRGAN) to demonstrate why their affine projection approach is superior.

**Questions for authors:**

1. Can you provide quantitative comparisons of AffGAN against established SR methods on standard benchmarks, including metrics like LPIPS (which correlates better with human perception than PSNR/SSIM)?

2. How do you explain the theoretical inconsistency between minimizing cross-entropy and minimizing KL divergence, and how does this affect the performance of AffGAN?

3. Given the limitations of MAP inference discussed in Section 5.6, how do you justify using MAP inference for image SR when the mode may not produce perceptually plausible results, especially when compared to sampling from the posterior?

4. Why does AffGAN work better than AffDG and AffLL when both methods are theoretically designed to minimize the same objective (H[qθ, pY])? The paper states they "produced blurry results" but provides no analysis or comparison.

This paper's approach to image SR using MAP inference has potential, but the lack of rigorous empirical validation against established baselines and the theoretical inconsistencies render the claims unsubstantiated. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their empirical evaluation and theoretical justification to be considered for publication at ICLR.
