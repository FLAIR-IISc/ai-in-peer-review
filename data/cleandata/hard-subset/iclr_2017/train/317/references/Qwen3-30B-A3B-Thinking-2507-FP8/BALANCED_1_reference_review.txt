

# Review of "Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that current methods using empirical risk minimization with MSE loss produce blurry outputs due to averaging over the posterior distribution. The authors introduce an affine projection layer that ensures SR outputs are consistent with the LR input, and develop three methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) to minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The paper provides theoretical justification and empirical evidence on toy problems and real images, claiming that AffGAN produces sharper and more perceptually pleasing results than MSE and MAE baselines.

## 2. Decision
Reject.

Key reasons: The paper's core contribution lacks sufficient novelty compared to recent work, and the empirical evaluation does not adequately demonstrate the method's advantages over state-of-the-art SR approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main contribution—using MAP inference for SR via an affine projection layer—builds directly on existing work, particularly Ledig et al. (2016) which introduced GAN-based SR that already produces sharper results than MSE-based methods. The authors cite this work but don't sufficiently distinguish their approach, which appears to be a minor extension of existing GAN-based SR methods. The key innovation (affine projection) is not convincingly shown to provide meaningful improvements over existing approaches.

The empirical evaluation is insufficiently rigorous. The paper compares against MSE and MAE baselines, which is appropriate, but fails to compare against state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN, or other recent SOTA approaches). Without these comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether the proposed method actually advances the field. The claim that "AffGAN produced the most visually appealing results" is subjective and unsupported by quantitative perceptual metrics like LPIPS or human evaluations. The paper acknowledges AffGAN produces "more high-frequency noise" but doesn't provide evidence that this is an improvement.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art SR methods beyond MSE and MAE baselines. Current SOTA methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN) should be included in the evaluation.

2. Include quantitative perceptual metrics (LPIPS, FID, or human evaluations) to support claims about visual quality. Without these, the claim of "improved perceptual quality" remains unsubstantiated.

3. Clarify how their affine projection layer differs from similar techniques in recent SOTA SR methods (e.g., residual learning in ESRGAN or RCAN).

4. Address the computational cost of their method compared to existing approaches, which is important for practical applications.

5. Provide more detailed analysis of why AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results (Section 5.3), as this undermines the viability of two of their three proposed methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your affine projection layer differ from the residual learning framework used in recent SOTA SR methods (e.g., RCAN, ESRGAN)? What specific advantages does your approach offer?

2. Have you compared your AffGAN method with other GAN-based SR approaches beyond Ledig et al. (2016), such as ESRGAN or SRGAN? If so, what were the results?

3. Can you provide quantitative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID) to support your claim that AffGAN produces more visually appealing results than MSE and MAE baselines?

4. How does your method scale to higher super-resolution factors (e.g., 8x or 16x) compared to state-of-the-art methods? The current experiments only cover 4x.

5. The paper mentions that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results that couldn't be improved. Can you elaborate on why this is the case, and whether these methods could be salvaged with additional modifications?

The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach that builds on existing work, but the lack of novelty compared to recent SOTA methods and insufficient empirical validation prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, particularly a more rigorous comparison with current state-of-the-art methods and the inclusion of quantitative perceptual metrics, the paper could be significantly strengthened.
