

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, arguing that current methods using MSE loss produce blurry outputs because they optimize for the mean of the posterior distribution rather than the mode. The authors introduce an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between input LR and output HR images, and then propose three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoising), and AffLL (using density models). The paper provides theoretical grounding for their approach and demonstrates that their methods produce sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE and MAE baselines on both toy and real image datasets.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. While the paper has several strengths, it has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being accepted at ICLR. The main reasons for rejection are: (1) the paper does not adequately address the fundamental limitations of MAP inference, particularly its representation dependence and potential for producing unrepresentative modes; and (2) the empirical evaluation is limited, with insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods and inadequate analysis of the proposed methods' limitations.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution—the use of amortized MAP inference for SR—is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The affine projection layer is a clever technical solution that ensures consistency between input and output, and the three proposed methods (AffGAN, AffDG, AffLL) represent a novel application of existing techniques to SR.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. First, it fails to adequately address the fundamental limitations of MAP inference. Section 5.6 briefly mentions that the mode of a distribution is representation-dependent (e.g., transforming through the cumulative distribution function makes the mode non-unique) and that the mode may not be perceptually plausible (e.g., the mode of a normal distribution has norm 0 while typical samples have norm sqrt(d)). The paper does not provide evidence that their methods overcome these limitations or that the MAP estimate in pixel space is special. This is a critical flaw, as the entire approach relies on the assumption that the MAP estimate in pixel space is perceptually meaningful.

Second, the empirical evaluation is limited. The paper compares their methods only to MSE and MAE baselines, but does not include comparisons to state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN, or other GAN-based SR methods that use perceptual losses). The results in Figure 5 show high-frequency noise in AffGAN outputs, which is a known limitation of GAN-based SR methods. The paper does not discuss how their approach compares to these methods or how it addresses these limitations.

Third, the paper does not sufficiently analyze the limitations of their approach. For example, why does AffGAN work better than AffDG and AffLL? The paper mentions that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results but does not explain why this happened or how to fix it. The paper also claims AffGAN minimizes H[qθ, pY] but the theoretical derivation shows it minimizes KL[qθ||pY], which is a significant discrepancy that is not adequately addressed.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Address MAP inference limitations**: Provide empirical evidence that the MAP estimate in pixel space is perceptually better than other representations. Consider including comparisons with methods that use feature space MAP estimation.

- **Expand empirical evaluation**: Compare with state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN) using standard metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, FID). Include failure cases to better understand the limitations of your approach.

- **Clarify theoretical relationship**: Reconcile the discrepancy between the paper's claim that AffGAN minimizes H[qθ, pY] and the theoretical derivation showing it minimizes KL[qθ||pY]. Explain why minimizing KL[qθ||pY] would lead to better perceptual quality.

- **Analyze AffGAN's success**: Provide a detailed analysis of why AffGAN works better than AffDG and AffLL. What specific aspects of the AffGAN approach make it more effective for SR?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that AffGAN minimizes H[qθ, pY], but the theoretical derivation shows it minimizes KL[qθ||pY]. How do you reconcile this discrepancy? Is there a theoretical reason why minimizing KL[qθ||pY] would lead to better perceptual quality?

2. The paper mentions that AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations than other MAP methods. Can you provide empirical evidence for this claim, and explain why this is the case?

3. Why do AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results while AffGAN does not? What specific aspects of the AffGAN approach make it more effective for SR?

4. How does your approach compare to state-of-the-art SR methods that use perceptual losses (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN)? Can you provide quantitative comparisons with these methods?
