

# Peer Review: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than traditional empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors make several significant contributions:

1. They propose a theoretical framework showing that MAP inference for SR can be framed as minimizing the cross-entropy between the model output distribution and the natural image distribution, without requiring paired HR-LR data.

2. They introduce an architectural innovation called the "affine projection layer" that ensures outputs are consistent with LR inputs by projecting to the affine subspace of valid solutions. This is a clever solution to the SR problem's inherent constraint.

3. They develop three practical methods for implementing amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The AffGAN approach is particularly well-motivated and shows strong empirical results.

4. They provide theoretical grounding for using GANs in SR, including a novel "instance noise" trick to stabilize training that could benefit other GAN applications.

5. They establish a connection between their AffGAN approach and variational inference, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

The paper is well-written, with clear motivation, thorough theoretical analysis, and comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and problem settings.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of image super-resolution with a theoretically sound approach that addresses a well-known limitation of current methods. The key reason for acceptance is that the authors successfully demonstrate that their MAP inference approach produces perceptually superior results compared to standard MSE-based methods, with strong theoretical justification and empirical evidence to support their claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is well-motivated by the limitations of MSE-based SR methods, which produce blurry outputs by averaging over possible solutions. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation showing that minimizing cross-entropy between the model output and natural image distribution is equivalent to MAP inference (Equation 9). This is a significant contribution as it reframes SR as a generative modeling problem rather than an empirical risk minimization problem.

The affine projection layer is a clever architectural innovation that ensures consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs, and their experiments (Section 5.2) show that it doesn't hurt performance but actually improves convergence and final results. The implementation details are well-described, and they provide a principled way to compute the optimal deconvolution kernel (Appendix B).

The empirical results are convincing. On the 2D Swiss-roll toy problem (Section 5.1), AffGAN and AffDG achieve cross-entropy values close to the true MAP solution, while MSE and MAE models fail. On real image datasets (Section 5.3-5.5), AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE and other baselines. The ablation studies (Section 5.2) provide strong evidence for the importance of their affine projection layer.

The theoretical connection between AffGAN and variational inference (Appendix F) is particularly noteworthy and represents a significant contribution to the understanding of GANs and their relationship to probabilistic modeling.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, these suggestions could further improve it:

1. **Quantitative Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares to MSE and MAE baselines but doesn't include comparisons to other recent SR methods that use perceptual losses or GANs (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016). Adding such comparisons would strengthen the position of their method in the current literature.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions that training AffGAN requires instance noise and careful annealing, but doesn't provide details on computational overhead compared to standard SR methods. A brief discussion of inference time and training time would be valuable for practitioners.

3. **Limitations of MAP Inference**: The paper briefly discusses limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6, but could expand on practical limitations in real-world applications. For example, how does the method handle cases where the LR input is highly ambiguous?

4. **Visualization of Affine Projection**: While Figure 1a and 2d provide good intuition, a visualization showing the effect of the affine projection layer on a specific image would help readers understand its operation more concretely.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In Section 5.3, you state that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results that you couldn't improve with various optimization methods. Could you elaborate on why these methods failed to produce good results while AffGAN succeeded? Was this a limitation of the specific implementation or a fundamental issue with the denoiser/density-based approaches?

2. In the theoretical analysis (Section 3.2.1), you introduce "instance noise" as a technique to stabilize GAN training. How does this compare to other stabilization techniques like label smoothing or spectral normalization? Have you tested this technique on other GAN applications beyond SR?

3. In Appendix F, you establish a connection between AffGAN and variational inference. Could you provide more intuition about why the AffGAN objective minimizes the KL divergence in the amortized variational inference framework? How does this relate to the standard variational autoencoder objective?

4. In Figure 5, you mention that the snake was super-resolved into water, which is "obviously wrong but still a very plausible image." Could you elaborate on how the model determines "plausibility" in such cases? Is there a way to quantify the "plausibility" of these reconstructions beyond visual inspection?

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution. The theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical results are convincing, and the proposed methods address a well-known limitation of current SR approaches. I recommend acceptance.
