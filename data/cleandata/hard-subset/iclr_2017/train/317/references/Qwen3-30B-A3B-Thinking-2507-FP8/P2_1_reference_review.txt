

# Review: AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) that shifts from traditional empirical risk minimization (ERM) with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution of plausible high-resolution (HR) images, while MAP inference selects the mode of the posterior, which they claim corresponds to more perceptually realistic images. Their key contributions include:

1. A CNN architecture with an affine projection layer that ensures outputs are consistent with the input (downsampling the output yields the input)
2. Three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (affine projected denoiser guided), and AffLL (affine projected density guided)
3. Theoretical grounding showing that these methods minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior
4. Empirical validation on both a 2D toy problem and real image datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet)

The paper is well-motivated, well-placed in the literature, and provides a clear theoretical and empirical justification for their approach.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by providing a theoretically grounded alternative to MSE-based approaches. The key reasons for acceptance are:

1. The paper identifies a fundamental limitation of current SR methods (blurry outputs due to MSE optimization) and provides a compelling theoretical argument for why MAP inference is a better objective.
2. The affine projection layer is a novel architectural innovation that solves the consistency problem between input and output in a principled way, and the paper provides strong empirical evidence of its benefits.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical foundation is strong. The authors clearly derive that MAP inference for SR corresponds to minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. This is a significant contribution because it provides a principled alternative to the common MSE loss that has dominated the field for years.

The empirical validation is comprehensive and well-designed. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment provides a clear illustration of the theoretical claims, showing that AffGAN and AffDG produce outputs aligned with the posterior mode while MSE and MAE methods produce outputs in low-probability regions. The real image experiments (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) further validate the approach, with AffGAN producing sharper and more perceptually realistic results than MSE and other methods.

The paper's contribution to understanding GANs is also significant. By establishing a connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference (Appendix F), the authors provide new theoretical insights into the relationship between GANs and variational inference, which is a valuable contribution to the broader deep learning literature.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with a thorough related work section that clearly distinguishes their approach from existing methods. They correctly identify that previous GAN-based SR methods (like Ledig et al., 2016) use soft constraints (MAE) to enforce input-output consistency, while their approach uses an exact affine projection.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions for improvement:

1. **Theoretical Justification of MAP Selection**: The paper briefly discusses potential limitations of MAP inference in Section 5.6, but this is the only mention of the concern that the mode of a distribution is representation-dependent. The authors should more thoroughly address this concern, perhaps by discussing how their affine projection approach might mitigate this issue or providing evidence that their approach works well across different representations.

2. **Quantitative Comparison of Perceptual Quality**: While the paper shows qualitative improvements, it would be valuable to include more quantitative metrics specifically designed for perceptual quality (e.g., LPIPS, FID, or human evaluation results) rather than relying solely on PSNR and SSIM, which are known to correlate poorly with human perception.

3. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces better results, but it doesn't provide a comparison of computational efficiency (training time, inference time) with other methods. For a practical application, this would be important to include.

4. **Instance Noise Analysis**: The paper introduces "instance noise" as a technique to stabilize GAN training, but it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how this technique affects the quality of the final model or compare it to other stabilization techniques.

## Questions for Authors

1. In Section 5.6, you mention that MAP estimates might not be visually typical even if they're in high-probability regions. How does the AffGAN approach specifically address this concern compared to other MAP-based methods?

2. The paper mentions that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results (Section 5.3), but doesn't provide a clear explanation for this failure mode. Could the authors elaborate on why these methods didn't work as well as AffGAN, particularly given their theoretical connection to the MAP objective?

3. In Figure 5, the AffGAN model produces a snake that's super-resolved into water. The authors suggest this is plausible due to the prior, but how do they quantify the plausibility of such "dreamed-up" images? Could they provide a metric or qualitative assessment of when the model produces plausible versus implausible outputs?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by providing a theoretically grounded alternative to MSE-based methods. The authors have clearly demonstrated the superiority of their approach in both theoretical and empirical terms, and the paper is well-written and well-positioned in the literature. I strongly recommend acceptance.
