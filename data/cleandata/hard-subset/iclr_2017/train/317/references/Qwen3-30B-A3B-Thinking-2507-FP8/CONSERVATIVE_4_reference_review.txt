

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than the standard empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors argue that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they output the mean of the posterior distribution rather than the mode, which they claim leads to more perceptually pleasing results. The core contribution is a new architecture with an affine projection layer that ensures consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs. The paper then proposes three methods to implement amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (a variant of GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The authors claim their methods produce sharper images with better perceptual quality while maintaining computational efficiency.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation against established baselines and does not adequately demonstrate that the proposed approach provides meaningful improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim—that MAP inference produces more perceptually pleasing SR results than MSE-based methods—lacks sufficient evidence. While the 2D toy example is theoretically sound, the empirical validation on real images is limited and fails to address critical questions:

1. **Insufficient Baseline Comparisons**: The paper primarily compares against MSE and MAE baselines, but fails to include comparisons against recent SOTA methods like ESRGAN (which builds on Ledig et al., 2016), RDN, or RCAN. The authors acknowledge that "our work follows successful demonstrations of GAN-based algorithms for image SR (Ledig et al., 2016)," yet they don't compare against the specific GAN-based approaches they cite as predecessors. The paper claims AffGAN produces "more visually appealing results" but provides no human evaluation to support this claim.

2. **Lack of Rigorous Perceptual Validation**: The paper relies on PSNR and SSIM metrics, which the authors themselves acknowledge "correlate poorly with human perception." They present visual examples where AffGAN appears sharper, but these are subjective comparisons without any controlled human study. The paper does not provide evidence that the improvements are statistically significant or meaningful to human observers.

3. **Unjustified Claims of Novelty**: The paper's key innovation—the affine projection layer—is not sufficiently novel. The authors acknowledge that their approach is similar to Garcia (2016), and their "affine projection" is essentially a constraint that many previous works have used (e.g., through L1/L2 loss between LR input and downsampled output). The paper fails to demonstrate that this constraint provides meaningful advantages over simpler approaches.

4. **Theoretical Limitations Not Fully Addressed**: Section 5.6 acknowledges that MAP inference has limitations (e.g., sensitivity to coordinate transformations), yet the paper doesn't sufficiently explore how these limitations affect the practical utility of their method. The paper also doesn't address the well-known issue that MAP estimates can be highly atypical (e.g., the mode of a Gaussian distribution has norm 0 while typical samples have norm √d).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Expand Baseline Comparisons**: Include comparisons against recent SOTA methods (ESRGAN, RCAN, etc.) with proper metrics, especially perceptual metrics like LPIPS that correlate better with human judgment.

- **Provide Human Evaluation**: Conduct a controlled human study to validate the claimed perceptual improvements, rather than relying solely on visual examples.

- **Clarify Novelty**: More clearly distinguish the paper's contributions from existing work, particularly Garcia (2016) and Ledig et al. (2016), which already use similar constraints and GANs for SR.

- **Address Limitations**: Thoroughly investigate the limitations of MAP inference in the context of SR, including the coordinate transformation issue and the potential for high-frequency artifacts observed in the results (Figure 5).

- **Improve Theoretical Analysis**: The theoretical connection to variational inference in Appendix F is intriguing but underdeveloped. Expand this analysis to show how it relates to practical performance.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims AffGAN produces "more visually appealing results" but provides no evidence beyond visual comparisons. Can you provide quantitative metrics that correlate better with human perception (e.g., LPIPS, user studies) to support this claim?

2. The paper states that "the affine projected networks generally found a lower cross-entropy H[qθ, pY ] when compared to soft-constrained versions" (Section 5.1), but doesn't explain why this matters for the final SR task. How does minimizing cross-entropy directly translate to better SR performance?

3. The paper acknowledges that "the MAP estimate is not unique if one allows for alternative representations" (Section 5.6), yet the paper doesn't address how this limitation affects the practical implementation of their method. How do you handle this issue in your experiments?

4. The paper claims AffGAN is "more resilient to coordinate transformations" than standard MAP inference, but provides no evidence for this claim. Can you provide experiments demonstrating this resilience?
