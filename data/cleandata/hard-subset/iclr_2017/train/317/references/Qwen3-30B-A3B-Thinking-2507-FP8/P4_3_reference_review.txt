

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) using amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors correctly identify that current SR methods based on empirical risk minimization with MSE loss produce blurry outputs because they output the mean of the posterior distribution rather than the mode. They introduce an affine projection layer that ensures the SR output satisfies the downsampling constraint (y = Ax), allowing them to frame MAP inference as minimizing the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The authors then propose three methods to approximate this objective: AffGAN (using GANs with affine projection), AffDG (using denoising to estimate gradients), and AffLL (using a density model). The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces perceptually superior results compared to MSE and MAE baselines while maintaining input-output consistency.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a compelling theoretical argument for using MAP inference in SR, provides strong empirical evidence supporting their claims, and demonstrates significant improvements in perceptual quality over existing methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in theoretical grounding. The authors clearly derive how MAP inference can be implemented with affine projections (Section 3.1), showing that the problem becomes equivalent to minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. This is a significant theoretical contribution that properly situates their work within the broader literature on GANs, probabilistic modeling, and image restoration.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous. The authors demonstrate their approach on both a toy Swiss-roll dataset (Section 5.1) where exact MAP inference is computationally tractable, and on real image datasets (Section 5.3-5.5). Their ablation study (Section 5.2) convincingly shows that the affine projection layer does not hurt performance and actually improves training dynamics compared to unconstrained CNNs. The AffGAN method consistently produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE, MAE, and other baselines across multiple datasets (grasses, faces, natural images), with quantitative metrics (PSNR, SSIM) supporting their qualitative observations.

The paper demonstrates scientific rigor in addressing limitations. Section 5.6 thoughtfully discusses limitations of MAP inference, including coordinate transformation issues and the potential unrepresentativeness of the mode. The connection to variational inference established in Appendix F further strengthens the theoretical foundation.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of the computational cost of AffGAN compared to standard SR methods. While Section 5.2 shows AffGAN trains faster than MSE baselines, it would be helpful to quantify the inference time overhead of the affine projection.

- In Section 5.3, the paper notes that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results for grass textures while AffGAN produces sharp results. A more detailed explanation of why AffGAN is more successful than these alternatives would strengthen the paper.

- The paper mentions that AffGAN produces images with "high-frequency noise" (Section 5.5), but doesn't discuss how this relates to the perceptual quality improvements. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-off between sharpness and noise would be valuable.

- The authors should clarify whether the affine projection layer is necessary for the GAN-based approach or if it's just a way to enforce consistency with the downsampling constraint.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Section 5.3, AffGAN produces sharp results for grass textures while AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results. What specific aspects of the AffGAN method make it more effective for this task compared to the other approaches?

2. The paper mentions that AffGAN produces "slightly sharper images" but "also seem to contain slightly more high frequency noise" (Section 5.4). How do the authors reconcile this with their claim that MAP inference produces more plausible images? What evidence do they have that the high-frequency noise doesn't harm perceptual quality?

3. In Section 5.6, the authors discuss the limitation of MAP inference being dependent on the coordinate system. How does the inclusion of the entropy term H[qθ] (as mentioned in Section 3.2) specifically help with this issue, and what evidence do they have that the mode in 24-bit RGB pixel representation is indeed special for perceptual quality?

4. The paper states that AffGAN "is more resilient to coordinate transformations" (Section 5.6). Can the authors provide a concrete example demonstrating this resilience, or explain how they verified this property?
