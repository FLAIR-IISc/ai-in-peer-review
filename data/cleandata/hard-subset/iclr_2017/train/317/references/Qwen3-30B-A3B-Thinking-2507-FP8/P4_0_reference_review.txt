

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, rather than traditional empirical risk minimization with pixel-wise loss functions. The authors argue that MSE-based approaches produce blurry outputs because they average over possible high-resolution solutions, whereas MAP inference selects the most probable solution. The key contributions include: (1) an affine projection layer that ensures model outputs are consistent with the input low-resolution image; (2) three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoising), and AffLL (using density models); and (3) theoretical justification showing these methods minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces perceptually superior results compared to MSE-based baselines across multiple datasets.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with sound technical execution, and provides compelling empirical evidence supporting its claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
**Theoretical Soundness (Question 3):** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for using MAP inference in SR. The affine projection layer (Section 3.1) is well-motivated and technically sound, with clear mathematical derivation showing how it enables unconstrained optimization of the MAP objective. The connection between the proposed methods and existing approaches (GANs, denoising, density models) is well-established, with proper theoretical grounding for each. The analysis in Section 3.2-3.4 clearly demonstrates how each method approximates the desired objective.

**Empirical Support (Question 3):** The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. The 2D toy experiment (Section 5.1) provides clear evidence that AffGAN and AffDG successfully minimize the cross-entropy objective, while MSE/MAE models produce outputs in low-probability regions. The real-world experiments on grass textures (Section 5.3), faces (Section 5.4), and natural images (Section 5.5) demonstrate consistent perceptual improvements with AffGAN, with visual results supporting the claim of superior perceptual quality. The ablation studies (Section 5.2) show that the affine projection layer does not harm performance and actually improves training dynamics.

**Motivation and Literature Positioning (Question 2):** The paper is well-placed in the literature and provides a strong motivation for the approach. The limitations of MSE-based SR are clearly articulated with a compelling 2D toy example (Figure 1a). The authors effectively position their work relative to recent GAN-based SR approaches (Ledig et al., 2016), while providing a more principled theoretical foundation. The connection to denoising (Alain & Bengio, 2014) and density modeling (Oord et al., 2016) is well-explained, demonstrating good awareness of the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of why AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results while AffGAN does not (Section 5.3). This is a significant finding that deserves deeper analysis.
- A brief discussion of computational cost and inference speed would be valuable for practical adoption of the method.
- The theoretical connection to variational inference (Section 6 and Appendix F) is promising but could be expanded to highlight its significance as a contribution.
- The paper could clarify the relationship between the affine projection layer and existing SR architectures, particularly how it compares to the "skip connections" used in many SR methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In Section 5.3, AffDG and AffLL consistently produce blurry results while AffGAN does not. What is the theoretical or practical reason for this discrepancy, and can you suggest modifications to AffDG/AffLL that might improve their perceptual quality?
2. The paper claims AffGAN produces "the most visually appealing results" in high dimensions. Can you provide more quantitative evidence for this claim, such as user studies or perceptual metrics beyond PSNR/SSIM?
3. The computational cost of the affine projection layer and the training procedure for AffGAN is not discussed. How does this impact practical deployment, and how does it compare to standard SR methods in terms of training time and inference speed?
