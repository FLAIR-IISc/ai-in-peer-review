

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes a fundamentally novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors correctly identify a key theoretical limitation of current SR methods: MSE loss produces blurry outputs because it averages over the posterior distribution, whereas MAP inference selects the mode (most probable solution) under the natural image prior. The paper's core innovation is an affine projection layer that ensures network outputs are always consistent with the input (downsampling the output recovers the input), enabling MAP inference without paired training data. The authors develop three methods for implementing this approach: AffGAN (using GANs to minimize KL divergence), AffDG (using denoising to estimate gradients), and AffLL (using density models). The theoretical foundation is strong, and the AffGAN method demonstrates visually superior results compared to standard approaches on real image datasets.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a bold, novel conceptual framework for image super-resolution that addresses a fundamental theoretical limitation of current methods. While the AffGAN approach shows some high-frequency noise in results, the core idea is sufficiently novel and well-motivated to warrant acceptance, especially given the reviewer's emphasis on novel ideas over methodological perfection.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution—replacing MSE-based SR with MAP inference—is genuinely novel and addresses a well-known limitation of current methods. The theoretical analysis connecting MAP inference to cross-entropy minimization is rigorous and well-motivated. The affine projection layer is a clever technical solution that elegantly handles the consistency constraint (Afθ(x) = x) without requiring paired data. 

The empirical results are compelling: on the 2D Swiss-roll toy problem, AffGAN and AffDG correctly identify the mode while MSE/MAE models output in low-probability regions. On real images, AffGAN produces sharper results than MSE and other GAN-based methods, with qualitative improvements that align with the paper's theoretical claims. The paper establishes a novel theoretical connection between GANs and MAP inference (and even variational inference in Appendix F), which is a significant contribution beyond just SR.

While AffDG and AffLL underperform compared to AffGAN, the paper properly acknowledges this and focuses on the most promising approach. The paper's theoretical grounding is strong, and the empirical validation on multiple datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet) provides sufficient evidence to support the main claim that MAP inference produces more perceptually plausible results.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could strengthen its argument for perceptual superiority by including more perceptual quality metrics beyond PSNR/SSIM (e.g., LPIPS, user studies). While the authors acknowledge PSNR's poor correlation with human perception, the paper lacks concrete evidence that AffGAN results are indeed more perceptually pleasing to humans.
- The high-frequency noise in AffGAN results (visible in Figure 5) is a notable limitation. The paper could explore potential solutions or discuss how this noise relates to the trade-off between sharpness and plausibility.
- The connection to variational inference (Appendix F) is promising but underexplored. A brief discussion of how this might enable sampling from the posterior for more diverse reconstructions would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.
- The paper could provide more detail on the computational cost of the affine projection layer compared to standard SR architectures.

## Questions for Authors
1. How do you plan to address the high-frequency noise in AffGAN results, which appears in some of the images (e.g., Figure 5), without sacrificing sharpness?
2. Could you provide more specific details about the instance noise annealing schedule used in training AffGAN, and whether this hyperparameter is sensitive to the dataset or model architecture?
3. The paper claims AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations than standard MAP inference. Could you provide concrete examples or analysis to support this claim, especially regarding how different color spaces might affect the results?
