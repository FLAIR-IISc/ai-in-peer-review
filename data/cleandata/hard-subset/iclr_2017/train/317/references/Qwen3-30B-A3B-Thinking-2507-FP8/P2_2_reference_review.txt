

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in current image super-resolution (SR) methods: the use of MSE loss functions that produce blurry, over-smoothed outputs by computing the mean of the posterior distribution rather than the mode (MAP estimate). The authors propose a novel approach to amortized MAP inference for SR that leverages natural image statistics. Their key contributions include:

1. A theoretical framework showing that MAP inference in SR corresponds to minimizing cross-entropy between the model output distribution and natural image prior
2. An affine projection layer that ensures model outputs are always consistent with the LR input
3. Three novel methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided)
4. A practical technique called "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training
5. Comprehensive empirical validation on both synthetic and real image datasets

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions**. The paper makes a compelling theoretical argument for why MAP inference is preferable to MSE-based approaches for SR, and the proposed AffGAN method produces visually superior results that align with the theoretical motivation. The theoretical grounding for using GANs in SR is particularly strong.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Strong Theoretical Foundation

The paper's theoretical contribution is its strongest aspect. The derivation in Section 3 clearly shows that MAP inference in SR corresponds to minimizing cross-entropy between the model output distribution and natural image prior (Equation 9), which is a significant theoretical contribution. The affine projection layer (Equation 7) is a clever architectural innovation that ensures consistency between input and output without requiring paired data, which is a major practical advantage.

### Novel Methodology with Practical Impact

The AffGAN approach is particularly compelling as it connects GANs to MAP inference, providing theoretical justification for why GAN-based SR methods work well. The instance noise technique (Section 3.2.1) is a valuable contribution that addresses a key practical challenge in GAN training. The paper demonstrates that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually pleasing results than MSE-based methods across multiple datasets.

### Comprehensive Empirical Validation

The experimental validation is thorough, with experiments on both synthetic (2D Swiss-roll) and real-world image datasets (grass textures, CelebA, ImageNet). The results consistently show that AffGAN produces superior perceptual quality compared to MSE/MAE methods, while maintaining input-output consistency. The ablation studies (Section 5.2) demonstrate that the affine projection layer actually improves training speed and final performance even when using MSE as the objective.

### Key Strengths

1. The paper clearly identifies and addresses a fundamental limitation of current SR methods
2. The theoretical motivation is strong and well-supported by the toy example
3. The proposed methods are well-motivated and theoretically grounded
4. The empirical results consistently support the theoretical claims
5. The instance noise technique is a valuable contribution with potential broader impact

### Minor Weaknesses

1. The paper doesn't directly compare with the most recent state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN), making it difficult to assess how competitive AffGAN is with current SOTA.
2. The paper relies primarily on visual comparisons for perceptual quality, without quantitative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID).
3. The limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6) are acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed in the context of practical performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Add quantitative perceptual metrics**: Include LPIPS or similar metrics to provide objective support for the visual claims about perceptual quality.

2. **Compare with recent SOTA**: Add a comparison with recent state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN) to demonstrate AffGAN's competitiveness with current approaches.

3. **Clarify the density model**: The AffLL approach using PixelCNNs with MCGSM is mentioned but not thoroughly evaluated. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of why AffLL underperforms compared to AffGAN.

4. **Expand on the stochastic extension**: The connection between AffGAN and variational inference (Appendix F) is intriguing. A brief discussion of this in the main text would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

5. **Provide more implementation details**: The numerical optimization of the pseudo-inverse (Appendix B) is crucial but lacks detail. Clarify how sensitive the results are to the choice of downsampling kernel.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does AffGAN compare to recent state-of-the-art SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RCAN) in terms of both quantitative metrics (PSNR/SSIM) and perceptual quality?

2. Why do AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results while AffGAN does not? Is this a fundamental limitation of the denoiser and density model approaches, or could it be addressed with better implementation or training techniques?

3. The paper states that the optimal deconvolution A+ is not simply the transpose of A. Could you provide more intuition about why this is the case, and how the numerical optimization of A+ is performed in practice?

4. In Section 5.6, you discuss limitations of MAP inference. How do these limitations manifest in your experiments, and are there any practical ways to mitigate them in the AffGAN framework?

5. The instance noise technique is promising. Could you share any insights on how it might generalize to other GAN applications beyond SR?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a compelling theoretical argument and demonstrates practical benefits for image super-resolution. With minor revisions to address the points above, it would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
