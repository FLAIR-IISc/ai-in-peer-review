- Proposes amortized MAP inference for super-resolution to avoid MSE-induced blurriness by matching HR outputs to the natural image prior.  
- Introduces an affine projection layer that guarantees model outputs are consistent with the LR input.  
- Presents three practical amortized MAP methods: AffGAN (affine-projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (likelihood/density-guided).  
- Theoretical derivation: amortized MAP corresponds to minimizing cross-entropy between model output distribution and the natural image prior.  
- 2D Swiss-roll experiment validates that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the cross-entropy objective while MSE/MAE produce low-probability outputs.  
- Empirical results across textures, CelebA, and ImageNet show AffGAN yields sharper, more perceptually plausible images than MSE/MAE and soft-constrained baselines.  
- Ablation study shows the affine projection layer speeds training and improves final performance without paired LR–HR data.  
- Instance noise is proposed and used to stabilize GAN training, addressing a key practical challenge.  
- The paper draws a novel theoretical connection between GANs and variational inference, showing stochastic AffGAN approximates amortized variational inference.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — contribution is significant, well-motivated, and empirically validated.  
- Request to report computational efficiency: quantify additional training time and cost for AffGAN/AffDG versus MSE methods.  
- Ask for analysis explaining why AffDG and AffLL produced blurrier results than AffGAN.  
- Suggest clarifying differences from prior VAE-based SR work (e.g., Larsen et al., 2015).  
- Recommend explicit discussion of MAP inference limitations in high-dimensional spaces and how the method mitigates non-representative modes.  
- Request more quantitative perceptual metrics beyond PSNR/SSIM to support claims that AffGAN is sharper than SoftGAN.  
- Questions on how the method handles atypical high-dimensional modes, demonstrates resilience to coordinate transformations, and sensitivity to the choice/distribution of instance noise.
