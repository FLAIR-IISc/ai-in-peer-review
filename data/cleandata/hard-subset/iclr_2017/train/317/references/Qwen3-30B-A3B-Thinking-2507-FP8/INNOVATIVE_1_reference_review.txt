

# Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## Summary
This paper makes a compelling argument for a fundamental shift in how we approach image super-resolution (SR). Instead of the standard empirical risk minimization framework with pixel-wise losses like MSE, the authors propose amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference, which aims to find the most probable solution under the natural image prior rather than the average solution. The paper introduces a novel architectural innovation—the affine projection layer—that ensures the output is always consistent with the input (downsampling the output yields the input), and three methods for implementing amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoisers), and AffLL (using density models). The authors provide strong theoretical grounding for their approach and demonstrate through both toy problems and real image experiments that their methods produce perceptually superior results compared to standard approaches.

## Decision
Accept

## Supporting Arguments
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by proposing a fundamentally new direction for image super-resolution, moving beyond the standard ERM framework. The core idea—using MAP inference to avoid the blurring artifacts of MSE training—is bold and well-motivated, addressing a well-known limitation in current SR methods. The paper's theoretical development is sound, with clear connections to existing work in GANs, denoising, and density modeling.

The authors' most compelling contribution is the affine projection layer, which cleverly ensures consistency between input and output while enabling the minimization of the cross-entropy H[qθ, pY] without requiring paired data. This architectural innovation is both novel and practical, and the paper demonstrates its effectiveness through both theoretical analysis and empirical results.

The experimental results are convincing, particularly for AffGAN, which produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE-based methods. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment (Figure 1) provides clear, intuitive evidence that the proposed methods are correctly minimizing the desired objective. The real image results (Figures 3-5) show that AffGAN produces visually superior outputs, with the added benefit of the affine projection ensuring perfect input-output consistency.

While AffDG and AffLL methods don't perform as well as AffGAN, this doesn't diminish the paper's core contribution, which is the novel paradigm of amortized MAP inference. The paper wisely focuses on AffGAN as their primary method, acknowledging the limitations of the other approaches. The theoretical connection between AffGAN and amortized variational inference (Section F) is particularly exciting and opens up new research directions.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of why AffGAN outperforms AffDG and AffLL. While the paper notes that AffDG and AffLL produce blurry results, a more thorough analysis of the underlying reasons would strengthen the paper.

2. The connection to variational inference in Appendix F is promising but could be expanded. The authors should elaborate on how this theoretical connection might inform future work on stochastic SR methods.

3. The paper briefly mentions the limitations of MAP inference (Section 5.6), but this could be more thoroughly addressed. The authors should discuss how their approach compares to sampling-based methods (e.g., using a VAE to sample from the posterior) and whether their method could be combined with sampling for even better results.

4. The paper's theoretical analysis is strong, but the connection between the affine projection and the MAP inference objective could be made more explicit in the main text rather than buried in the appendix.

## Questions for Authors
1. In Section 5.5, you mention that AffGAN "dreams up reasonable data" (e.g., the snake becoming water). Could you elaborate on how the image prior influences these specific hallucinations, and whether this is a desirable or undesirable property?

2. The paper states that AffGAN "produces slightly sharper images which however also seem to contain slightly more high frequency noise." How do you balance the trade-off between sharpness and noise in practical applications, and do you see this as a fundamental limitation of the MAP approach?

3. In Figure 5, the AffGAN results for natural images show some artifacts (e.g., water instead of snakes). How does the MAP approach handle ambiguous inputs where multiple plausible solutions exist, and how does this compare to methods that sample from the posterior?

4. The paper mentions that AffGAN "is more resilient to coordinate transformations" than standard MAP inference. Could you provide a brief example or explanation of how this works in practice?

This paper presents a novel and compelling direction for image super-resolution that challenges the status quo. The theoretical grounding, architectural innovation, and empirical results make it a strong contribution to the field. I believe this work will inspire significant follow-up research and has the potential to change how SR is approached in the future.
