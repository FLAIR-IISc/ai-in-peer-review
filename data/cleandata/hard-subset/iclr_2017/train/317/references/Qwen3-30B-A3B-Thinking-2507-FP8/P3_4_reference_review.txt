

# Peer Review: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a fundamentally different approach to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization (ERM) with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors correctly identify that MSE-based SR methods produce blurry outputs because they output the mean of the posterior distribution, while natural images are better represented by the mode (MAP estimate). Their key contributions include: (1) a principled CNN architecture with an affine projection layer ensuring consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs; (2) three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs with affine projection), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided); and (3) theoretical grounding showing that MAP inference minimizes cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. The paper provides strong theoretical motivation, thorough experiments on both toy and real-world datasets, and establishes a novel connection between GANs and variational inference.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by repositioning SR as a MAP inference problem rather than an ERM problem. The affine projection layer is a clever and principled solution to a fundamental consistency issue in SR, and the AffGAN method produces visually superior results that align with human perception better than current state-of-the-art methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical motivation is exceptionally strong. The 2D Swiss-roll toy example (Figure 1a) clearly illustrates why MAP inference is superior to MSE for SR, showing how MSE outputs fall in low-probability regions while MAP stays in high-probability regions. This is a compelling argument that addresses a fundamental limitation of current SR methods.

The affine projection layer (Equation 7) is a significant technical contribution. It solves the consistency problem in SR by ensuring Afθ(x) = x exactly, which MSE-based methods can only approximate with soft constraints. This is a major improvement over previous work like Garcia (2016) that used soft L2/L1 penalties. The paper demonstrates that this constraint improves training dynamics (Figure 2) and ensures perfect consistency (Table 1).

The empirical results are rigorous and well-designed. The authors test on a 2D toy problem (Section 5.1), grass textures (Section 5.3), CelebA faces (Section 5.4), and ImageNet natural images (Section 5.5). AffGAN consistently produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results than MSE, MAE, and other methods. The cross-entropy measurements (Table 1) provide quantitative evidence that AffGAN and AffDG are successfully minimizing the desired objective.

The theoretical connection between AffGAN and variational inference in Appendix F is a particularly elegant contribution that strengthens the paper's theoretical foundation.

## 4. Additional Feedback

**Key questions for authors:**

1. The paper acknowledges MAP inference's limitations in Section 5.6 (mode being representation-dependent and potentially atypical), but doesn't provide strong counterarguments. Can you provide additional evidence or reasoning to support why MAP is better than sampling from the posterior for SR? For example, could you show that MAP outputs are more frequently rated as "natural" in human perception studies?

2. AffDG and AffLL don't perform as well as AffGAN (Figure 3, Table 1). Could you provide more detailed analysis on why these methods underperform? Is it related to the quality of the denoiser/density model, or is there a fundamental limitation to these approaches for SR?

3. The instance noise technique (Section 3.2.1) is interesting but not thoroughly evaluated in the main experiments. How does it compare to other GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., one-sided label smoothing) specifically for SR? Could you provide a brief comparison in the main text?

4. The paper doesn't compare to recent state-of-the-art SR methods like ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2018) or other advanced GAN-based SR approaches. How does AffGAN compare to these in terms of perceptual quality (e.g., using LPIPS or human evaluations) and computational efficiency?

**Minor suggestions:**

- In Section 5.5, the snake-to-water example is intriguing but could be more thoroughly analyzed. Is this a failure mode or a feature of the MAP approach? Could you provide more context on why water is a plausible output given the LR input?

- The paper mentions AffGAN is "more resilient to coordinate transformations" but doesn't provide evidence. A simple experiment showing this would strengthen the claim.

- Consider adding a brief discussion of computational complexity and training time comparisons between AffGAN and other methods.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by rethinking SR from a probabilistic inference perspective. The theoretical motivation is sound, the technical approach is novel and well-executed, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over current methods. The paper provides both theoretical depth and practical relevance, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
