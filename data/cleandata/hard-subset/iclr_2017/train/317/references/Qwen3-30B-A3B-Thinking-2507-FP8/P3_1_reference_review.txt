

# Peer Review: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than the standard empirical risk minimization (ERM) with MSE loss. The authors correctly identify that MSE-based SR methods produce blurry outputs because they output the mean of the posterior distribution (Bayes-optimal for MSE), which often corresponds to low-probability regions under natural image priors. Instead, they propose MAP inference, which finds the mode of the posterior distribution—corresponding to high-probability regions under the natural image prior.

The key contributions are:
1. A CNN architecture with an affine projection layer that ensures outputs are consistent with LR inputs (satisfying x = Ay where A is the downsampling operator).
2. Three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs), AffDG (using denoising), and AffLL (using density models).
3. Theoretical justification showing their objective corresponds to minimizing cross-entropy between model output distribution and natural image prior.
4. Empirical results demonstrating that their methods produce sharper, more perceptually plausible SR results compared to MSE, MAE, and soft-constrained approaches.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a fundamental limitation of current SR methods with a theoretically sound approach. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the empirical results are compelling.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength is its clear theoretical motivation for why MAP inference is preferable to MSE-based inference for SR. The 2D Swiss-roll toy example (Figure 1a) effectively illustrates how MSE can produce low-probability outputs while MAP finds the mode in a high-probability region. This is a strong argument that resonates with the broader literature on the limitations of MSE for perceptual tasks.

The affine projection layer is a clever architectural innovation that ensures consistency between LR input and HR output without soft constraints. The paper demonstrates this through quantitative (Table 1) and qualitative (Figure 2) results, showing that affine projected networks maintain exact consistency (error = 0) while soft-constrained models only approximately satisfy this constraint.

The AffGAN method is particularly compelling as it provides a clear theoretical connection between GANs and MAP inference (Appendix F). The empirical results (Figures 3-5) show that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually plausible images compared to MSE and soft-constrained methods. This addresses a critical limitation in current SR literature where perceptual quality lags behind PSNR metrics.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with thorough discussion of related work including GAN-based SR (Ledig et al., 2016), perceptual loss functions, and connections to denoising-based approaches. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous methods and position their work as addressing these limitations.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions for improvement:

1. **Theoretical justification for MAP inference**: Section 5.6 discusses the limitation of MAP inference being dependent on representation. The authors should more thoroughly address this concern and provide empirical evidence showing their method is robust to coordinate transformations (e.g., color space changes).

2. **Comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper compares against MSE, MAE, and soft-constrained GAN methods, but it would be valuable to include comparisons with recent state-of-the-art perceptual SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RDN) to better position their contribution.

3. **Instance noise details**: The "instance noise" technique (Section 3.2.1) is introduced as a stabilizer for GAN training, but the implementation details (noise schedule, annealing) could be more thoroughly explained. The authors should provide concrete details about the noise schedule used in their experiments.

4. **Why AffGAN works best**: The paper states AffGAN produced the most visually appealing results but doesn't fully explain why it outperforms AffDG and AffLL. A more detailed analysis of the differences between these methods would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for authors:**

1. The paper states that "the AffGAN model produces slightly sharper images which however also seem to contain slightly more high frequency noise." Can the authors provide a more nuanced analysis of this trade-off between sharpness and noise? How does this trade-off compare to other state-of-the-art SR methods?

2. In Figure 5, the snake is super-resolved into water. The authors state this is "obviously wrong but still a very plausible image considering the LR input." Could the authors elaborate on why this is plausible given the LR input? What specific features in the LR image lead to this interpretation?

3. The paper mentions AffGAN is more resilient to coordinate transformations due to the H[qθ] term. Can the authors provide empirical evidence supporting this claim, perhaps by showing AffGAN outputs on images transformed to a different color space?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the SR field by addressing a fundamental limitation of current methods with a theoretically grounded approach. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate clear improvements in perceptual quality. I believe this work will be of interest to the ICLR community.
