

# Peer Review: "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that MSE-based SR methods produce blurry outputs because they output the mean of plausible solutions, which often lies in low-probability regions under natural image priors. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) an affine projection layer that ensures model outputs are consistent with LR inputs by projecting to the affine subspace of valid solutions; (2) three methods to minimize the cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and natural image prior: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (affine projected denoiser guided), and AffLL (affine projected density guided); and (3) theoretical justification showing that AffGAN minimizes KL[qθ||pY] rather than H[qθ,pY], which explains why GAN-based SR methods work well. The paper provides compelling theoretical analysis and extensive empirical validation across toy problems and real image datasets, demonstrating that AffGAN produces perceptually superior results compared to MSE and other GAN-based baselines.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons: The paper addresses an important limitation of current SR methods with a well-motivated theoretical approach, provides a clean and elegant solution (affine projection), and demonstrates strong empirical results that align with the theoretical claims. The work makes a significant contribution to the field by providing theoretical grounding for GAN-based SR methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of MSE-based SR (Section 1), using the Swiss-roll toy example to illustrate why MAP inference would produce more plausible results than MSE/MAE. This theoretical motivation is strong and well-placed in the literature, with thorough discussion of related work including GAN-based SR (Ledig et al., 2016), perceptual losses, and connections to denoising.

The theoretical analysis is rigorous and novel. The derivation showing that affine projection enables minimization of cross-entropy (Section 3.1) is elegant and well-executed. The connection between GANs and MAP inference (Section 3.2) provides a solid theoretical foundation for why GAN-based SR methods work well, addressing a gap in the literature. The theoretical analysis is complemented by strong empirical results: the Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) clearly demonstrates that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the desired objective, and the real image experiments (Sections 5.3-5.5) show that AffGAN produces sharper, more perceptually pleasing results than MSE and other baselines, while maintaining consistency with the input (Table 1, Figure 2).

The ablation studies are particularly convincing. Figure 2 demonstrates that the affine projection layer improves training speed and final performance compared to unconstrained networks, and Table 1 shows that the affine projected models satisfy the consistency constraint exactly (error = 0) while soft-constrained models only approximately satisfy it. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of limitations (Section 5.6), acknowledging that MAP inference has potential issues with coordinate transformations and the atypical nature of modes.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify the relationship between KL divergence and cross-entropy**: The paper states that AffGAN minimizes "the desired quantity" (H[qθ,pY]), but the theoretical analysis (Section 3.2) shows it minimizes KL[qθ||pY]. Since H[qθ,pY] = KL[qθ||pY] + H[qθ], the paper should clarify why minimizing KL[qθ||pY] (which AffGAN does) is preferable to minimizing H[qθ,pY] for SR tasks. The authors note that AffGAN "favors approximate MAP solutions that lead to higher entropy," but this could be expanded upon.

- **Expand on AffDG/AffLL underperformance**: The paper notes that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results (Section 5.3), but doesn't fully explain why. A more detailed analysis of why these methods underperformed compared to AffGAN would strengthen the paper. For instance, is this due to the choice of denoising model or density model, or is it a more fundamental issue with the approach?

- **Computational efficiency discussion**: The paper mentions that the affine projection layer "ensures that our CNNs always output estimates that are consistent with the inputs," but doesn't discuss how this affects computational complexity compared to standard SR methods. A brief discussion of inference time and memory requirements would be valuable for practitioners.

- **Expand the connection to variational inference**: Appendix F establishes a connection between AffGAN and variational inference, but this is somewhat underdeveloped. A short discussion of how this connects to existing work on variational inference for SR would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Section 5.6, the paper notes that MAP estimates are not unique under coordinate transformations. How do you address this issue in practice, and have you conducted experiments to test the robustness of your method across different color spaces or representations?

2. The paper states that AffGAN minimizes KL[qθ||pY] rather than H[qθ,pY]. How does this affect the practical behavior of the model, and why is minimizing KL[qθ||pY] preferable to minimizing H[qθ,pY] for SR tasks?

3. In Section 3.2.1, the authors introduce "instance noise" to stabilize GAN training. How does this differ from other stabilization techniques like label smoothing, and why does the paper claim it "does not introduce a bias in the optimal discriminator"?

4. The paper claims that "the AffGAN and AffDG achieves cross-entropy values close to the MAP solution" (Table 1), but the MAP solution is calculated via brute-force Monte Carlo. How would this approach scale to high-dimensional real image data where brute-force computation is infeasible?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by providing a theoretically grounded approach to MAP inference that produces perceptually superior results. The work is well-executed, well-motivated, and makes a compelling case for why GAN-based SR methods work well. With the minor revisions suggested above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
