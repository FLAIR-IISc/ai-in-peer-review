- Paper reframes image super-resolution from ERM/MSE to amortized MAP inference, arguing MAP yields modes rather than blurry posterior means.  
- Introduces an affine projection layer in a CNN to enforce exact LR-to-HR consistency (Afθ(x) = x).  
- Proposes three amortized MAP methods: AffGAN (GAN-based), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided).  
- Provides theoretical result that MAP inference minimizes cross-entropy between model outputs and the natural image prior.  
- Establishes a novel connection between GANs and variational inference (Appendix F).  
- Strong theoretical motivation illustrated by a 2D Swiss-roll toy example showing MAP avoids low-probability outputs.  
- AffGAN produces visually sharper, more perceptually plausible SR results than MSE/MAE baselines across toy and real datasets.  
- Empirical evaluation includes toy data, grass textures, CelebA faces, and ImageNet, with cross-entropy measurements supporting objectives.  
- Affine projection layer is a significant technical contribution improving training dynamics and ensuring perfect consistency versus soft penalty approaches.  
- Decision: Accept — paper is a significant theoretical and practical contribution to SR.  
- AffDG and AffLL underperform relative to AffGAN; reviewers request analysis on whether this is due to denoiser/density model quality or inherent limitations.  
- Reviewer asks for stronger justification/evidence that MAP outputs are preferable to posterior samples, e.g., human perceptual studies.  
- Instance noise technique (Section 3.2.1) is interesting but not thoroughly compared to other GAN stabilization methods; more evaluation requested.  
- Paper lacks comparison to recent SOTA SR methods (e.g., ESRGAN); reviewer requests perceptual metrics (LPIPS/human studies) and efficiency comparisons.  
- Specific curious example (snake→water) needs deeper analysis to determine if it’s a failure mode or plausible MAP outcome.  
- Claim about AffGAN’s resilience to coordinate transformations is unsupported; a simple experiment would strengthen it.  
- Suggest adding discussion/measurements of computational complexity and training time versus other methods.
