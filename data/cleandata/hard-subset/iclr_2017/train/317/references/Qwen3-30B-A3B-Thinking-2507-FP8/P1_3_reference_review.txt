

# Peer Review: Amortised MAP Inference for Image Super-Resolution

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization (ERM) with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors argue that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution of high-resolution (HR) images consistent with the low-resolution (LR) input, resulting in outputs with low probability under the natural image prior. The authors propose an architectural constraint (affine projection) that ensures model outputs are always consistent with the LR input, and three methods for amortized MAP inference: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (denoiser guided), and AffLL (density guided). Their key contributions include: (1) theoretical motivation for MAP inference in SR, (2) a novel affine projection layer that guarantees consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs, (3) three practical approaches for minimizing the cross-entropy between the model output distribution and the natural image prior, and (4) empirical demonstration that their methods produce perceptually superior results compared to MSE and MAE baselines.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a compelling contribution to the SR field by addressing a fundamental limitation of current approaches (blurry outputs) with a theoretically sound and empirically validated method. The technical novelty of the affine projection layer and the three proposed methods for MAP inference are significant and well-motivated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-positioned in the literature with a thorough related work section that distinguishes their approach from previous GAN-based SR methods (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016). The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear derivations showing that amortized MAP inference corresponds to minimizing the cross-entropy between the model output distribution and the natural image prior. The 2D Swiss-roll experiment (Section 5.1) provides elegant theoretical validation of their approach, showing that AffGAN and AffDG successfully minimize the cross-entropy objective while MSE and MAE models produce low-probability outputs.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-designed. The authors conduct thorough experiments across multiple datasets (grass textures, CelebA faces, ImageNet) and demonstrate that their AffGAN method produces sharper, more perceptually plausible results compared to MSE, MAE, and soft-constrained baselines. The ablation study in Section 5.2 confirms that the affine projection layer improves training speed and final performance without requiring paired LR-HR data. The inclusion of instance noise (Section 3.2.1) as a training stabilization technique is a valuable contribution that addresses a key practical challenge in GAN training.

The paper also makes an important theoretical connection between GANs and variational inference (Appendix F), showing that a stochastic extension of AffGAN approximately minimizes the amortized variational inference criterion. This is a significant theoretical contribution that could inspire future work at the intersection of generative modeling and inference.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational efficiency of their methods compared to existing approaches. How much additional training time do AffGAN and AffDG require compared to MSE-based methods?
- The authors mention that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results in their experiments (Section 5.3), but don't provide a clear explanation for why these methods underperformed compared to AffGAN. A more thorough analysis of this discrepancy would strengthen the paper.
- While the theoretical connection to variational inference is promising, the authors should clarify how their approach differs from previous work on variational autoencoders for SR (e.g., Larsen et al., 2015).
- The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of MAP inference, particularly in high-dimensional spaces where the mode may not be perceptually representative (Section 5.6), and how their method addresses these limitations.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that AffGAN produces "slightly sharper images" than SoftGAN (Section 5.4), but the visual examples in Figure 4 don't clearly show this difference. Could the authors provide more quantitative evidence for this claim, such as additional perceptual metrics beyond PSNR and SSIM?

2. In Section 5.6, the authors note that "the mode of the distribution is highly atypical" in high dimensions. How does their method specifically address this issue in the context of image super-resolution, where the high-dimensional space is constrained to natural images?

3. The paper claims that AffGAN "is more resilient to coordinate transformations" than standard MAP inference (Section 5.6). Could the authors provide a brief explanation or example of how this resilience manifests in practice?

4. The authors use instance noise for training stabilization. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of noise distribution (beyond additive Gaussian noise), and what are the theoretical guarantees for this noise-based approach?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image super-resolution by providing a theoretically sound approach that produces perceptually superior results. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and the experiments are thorough and convincing. With minor revisions addressing the points above, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
