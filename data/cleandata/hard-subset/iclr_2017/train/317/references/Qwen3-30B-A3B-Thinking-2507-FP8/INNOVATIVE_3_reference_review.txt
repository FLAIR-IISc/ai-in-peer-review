

# Review for "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a bold and novel contribution to image super-resolution (SR) by shifting from empirical risk minimization with MSE loss to amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors convincingly argue that MSE-based methods produce blurry outputs because they average over the posterior distribution, while MAP inference (finding the mode) should produce sharper, more perceptually plausible images. The key innovation is introducing an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, which allows them to frame SR as minimizing cross-entropy between the model's output distribution and the natural image prior. They then develop three methods (AffGAN, AffDG, and AffLL) to implement this approach, with AffGAN showing particularly promising results. The paper also establishes a novel theoretical connection between GANs and variational inference in Appendix F, which is a significant contribution in itself.

## Decision

Accept. This paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to image super-resolution that addresses a well-known limitation of current methods. The theoretical motivation is strong, the approach is novel, and the empirical results support the claims. While the paper focuses on AffGAN as the primary method, the theoretical framework and the affine projection layer represent a significant conceptual advance.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is the conceptual shift from MSE-based SR to MAP inference with a theoretically grounded implementation. This is a bold approach that directly challenges the dominant paradigm in the field. The authors demonstrate with a clear toy example (Figure 1a) why MAP inference is preferable to MSE for SR, showing how MSE produces outputs in low-probability regions while MAP finds high-probability solutions. This theoretical motivation is compelling and well-placed in the literature.

The affine projection layer (Section 3.1) is a clever technical innovation that enables the authors to transform the constrained optimization problem into an unconstrained one. This is not just a minor tweak but a fundamental architectural change that enables their approach. The paper's theoretical analysis is rigorous and clearly explains how the affine projection ensures consistency between input and output.

The empirical results (Section 5) support the theoretical claims. The AffGAN method produces visibly sharper images than MSE-based approaches on grass textures, faces, and natural images. The cross-entropy values in Table 1 confirm that AffGAN and AffDG minimize the desired objective, while MSE and MAE do not. The paper also provides an important theoretical contribution in Appendix F, establishing a connection between GANs and variational inference.

This paper is not incremental - it's proposing a fundamentally different approach to image super-resolution that addresses a core limitation of current methods. While the AffDG and AffLL methods don't perform as well as AffGAN, the paper wisely focuses on AffGAN as the primary method, and the theoretical framework is sound regardless of the specific implementation details.

## Additional Feedback

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of why MAP inference produces perceptually better results beyond just being the mode. While the paper mentions that MAP finds high-probability regions, it would be valuable to include a qualitative analysis of what makes these images perceptually pleasing.

2. The snake-to-water example in Figure 5 is intriguing but not fully explained. A brief discussion of why the model produced this specific error (and why it's still "plausible" according to the image prior) would strengthen the argument for MAP inference.

3. The paper mentions that AffDG and AffLL produced blurry results but doesn't fully explain why. A brief analysis of the differences between the three approaches (AffGAN vs. AffDG vs. AffLL) would help readers understand why AffGAN works better.

4. The theoretical connection between GANs and variational inference in Appendix F is promising but could be expanded in the main text to highlight its significance for the broader field of generative modeling.

## Clarification Questions

1. The paper states that "the MAP estimate is not unique if one allows for alternative representations" (Section 5.6). How does the affine projection layer help mitigate this issue specifically in the context of image SR, and what guarantees does it provide for the uniqueness of the MAP estimate in practice?

2. In the instance noise section (3.2.1), the paper claims that instance noise makes the Bayes-optimal discriminator unique. Can you provide more intuition about why this is the case, particularly in the context of image distributions which are known to concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds?

3. The paper notes that AffGAN produces "slightly sharper images which however also seem to contain slightly more high frequency noise" (Section 5.4). How does the authors' theory account for this trade-off between sharpness and noise, and is this noise a necessary consequence of MAP inference or an artifact of the specific implementation?
