

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a novel framework for image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors introduce an affine projection layer that ensures model outputs are consistent with LR inputs, and develop three approaches to approximate MAP inference: AffGAN (using GANs with affine projection), AffDG (denoiser-guided), and AffLL (density-guided). The key contribution is showing that MAP inference can be performed without paired LR-HR data, potentially leading to perceptually superior results compared to standard MSE/MAE approaches. The paper provides theoretical grounding for using GANs in SR and demonstrates empirical improvements on synthetic and real image datasets.

## 2. Decision
Reject.

Key reasons: (1) The core idea of using MAP inference for SR is not novel, building directly on Ledig et al. (2016) which already used GANs for SR. (2) The affine projection layer, presented as a key contribution, is a straightforward adaptation of standard convolution/deconvolution operations with limited novelty.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical analysis is technically sound but does not provide significant new insights. The authors correctly show that with their affine projection layer, MAP inference reduces to minimizing cross-entropy between the model output distribution and HR image prior. However, this connection to GANs was already demonstrated in Ledig et al. (2016), which the authors acknowledge but do not substantially advance.

The empirical results show AffGAN outperforms MSE baselines in perceptual quality, which is expected given known limitations of MSE for perceptual quality. However, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that their approach represents a meaningful advancement over state-of-the-art SR methods. Crucially, the paper does not compare against recent SOTA methods (e.g., ESRGAN, RDN, or other GAN-based SR approaches) beyond MSE and MAE baselines. The comparison to "SoftGAN" (which uses soft constraints) is insufficient to demonstrate the value of their affine projection.

The theoretical MAP solution achieves a cross-entropy of 3.15, while AffGAN achieves 4.10 (Table 1), indicating a significant gap between the method's goal and its actual performance. This suggests the method does not effectively minimize the desired quantity as claimed.

The paper claims to "minimize the desired quantity" but fails to provide a rigorous convergence analysis or demonstrate that the affine projection is necessary for achieving better results. The "proof of concept" in Section 5.2 shows affine projected networks can achieve similar MSE performance to unconstrained networks, but this doesn't demonstrate why the affine projection is needed for MAP inference.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper should clarify how this work advances beyond Ledig et al. (2016), which already demonstrated GAN-based SR with good perceptual results. The theoretical justification provided is not sufficiently novel.
- More comprehensive comparisons against recent SOTA SR methods are needed to establish the paper's contribution.
- The paper claims "our approach assumes no observation noise," but it's unclear why this is a significant improvement over existing soft-constraint approaches (e.g., Garcia, 2016).
- The paper should provide more analysis of why AffDG and AffLL perform poorly compared to AffGAN, as this is a critical finding but not well-explained.
- The theoretical analysis in Appendix A is incomplete without a proper convergence proof for the AffGAN algorithm.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your theoretical analysis specifically advance beyond Ledig et al. (2016), which already used GANs for SR with good results? What new insights does your work provide?
2. In Table 1, AffGAN achieves a cross-entropy of 4.10 while the theoretical MAP solution achieves 3.15. What explains this significant gap, and what does it imply about the quality of your MAP approximation?
3. Why did AffDG and AffLL perform poorly in the grass texture experiments (Figure 3), while AffGAN produced sharp results? What specific aspects of the AffGAN approach make it more effective for MAP inference in practice?
4. The paper claims "training becomes more akin to training generative models," but provides no evidence that this leads to better training stability or efficiency compared to standard SR training methods. Can you provide quantitative evidence supporting this claim?
