

# Review of "AMORTISED MAP INFERENCE FOR IMAGE SUPER-RESOLUTION"

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims

The paper proposes a novel approach to image super-resolution (SR) based on amortized Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference rather than standard empirical risk minimization with MSE loss. The authors argue that MSE leads to blurry outputs because it averages over the posterior distribution, while MAP inference finds the mode of the posterior, which they claim leads to more perceptually pleasing results. They introduce an affine projection layer to ensure consistency between low-resolution inputs and high-resolution outputs, and propose three methods to approximate MAP inference: AffGAN (affine projected GAN), AffDG (affine projected denoiser guided), and AffLL (affine projected density guided). The paper claims these methods can be trained without paired data (for AffGAN), and they demonstrate improved perceptual quality on both synthetic and real image datasets.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains multiple significant methodological flaws and lacks proper experimental comparison to state-of-the-art methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary reasons for rejection are:

First, the paper fails to properly situate itself within the literature. The authors claim their affine projection layer is novel, but this is essentially identical to the skip connections used in standard SR networks like the one proposed by Shi et al. (2016), which they cite. The paper also incorrectly states that their method "doesn't need corresponding HR and LR image pairs," but this is only true for AffGAN, while AffDG and AffLL still require paired data for training the denoiser and density model. They fail to properly discuss the relevant literature, including Ledig et al. (2016), who already used GANs for SR with similar conceptual approaches.

Second, the theoretical justification contains significant gaps. In Section 3.2, they claim AffGAN minimizes KL[qG‖pY] but provide no concrete proof or key insights in the main text, merely referring to Appendix A. The appendix appears to be a standard GAN derivation rather than a specific proof for their affine projection approach. Additionally, in Table 1, they report "AffGAN 4.10 0.0" for cross-entropy values, where a value of 0.0 is theoretically impossible for a non-optimal solution, suggesting a data or calculation error.

Third, the experimental evaluation is insufficient. The paper fails to provide a proper comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods beyond MSE and MAE. They compare to Ledig et al. (2016) only in passing but don't include their results in any quantitative comparison. The paper also claims AffGAN produces "sharper images" (Section 5.4) but acknowledges "high-frequency noise" in GAN results (Section 5.5), creating a contradiction. Most critically, they don't provide quantitative perceptual metrics (like LPIPS or FID) that would properly support their claim of "improved perceptual quality."

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a proper comparison to state-of-the-art SR methods beyond MSE and MAE, including Ledig et al. (2016) and other recent GAN-based approaches.

2. Clarify the data requirements for each method. AffGAN doesn't need paired data, but AffDG and AffLL do. The paper should explicitly state this distinction.

3. Fix the theoretical justification in Section 3.2 with a concise proof of how AffGAN minimizes cross-entropy, rather than referring to a potentially irrelevant appendix.

4. Address the inconsistency in Table 1 (0.0 cross-entropy value) and clarify the experimental setup for the 2D Swiss-roll experiment.

5. Provide quantitative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, FID) to support claims about improved perceptual quality, not just qualitative comparisons.

6. Properly cite and discuss the relevant unpublished work by Garcia (2016) if it's relevant, or remove the reference if it's not properly documented.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does the affine projection layer in their method differ from the skip connections used in standard SR networks (e.g., Shi et al., 2016), given that both ensure consistency between LR inputs and HR outputs?

2. Why do they report a cross-entropy value of 0.0 for AffGAN in Table 1, which is theoretically impossible for a non-optimal solution?

3. Can you provide a quantitative comparison between AffGAN and state-of-the-art GAN-based SR methods (e.g., Ledig et al., 2016) using perceptual metrics like LPIPS or FID?

4. Why do they claim AffGAN produces "sharper images" in Section 5.4 but acknowledge "high-frequency noise" in Section 5.5, and how do they reconcile this apparent contradiction?

5. How exactly does the "instance noise" technique differ from label smoothing (Salimans et al., 2016), and what evidence do they have that it's superior for their specific application?
