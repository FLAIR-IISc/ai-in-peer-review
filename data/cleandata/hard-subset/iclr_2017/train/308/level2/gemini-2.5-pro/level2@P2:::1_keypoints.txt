- Under the manifold hypothesis, real and generated distributions likely have disjoint supports, enabling a perfect discriminator.  
- A perfect discriminator yields useless gradients; for the original GAN objective the generator gradients vanish as the discriminator improves.  
- The `-log D` objective optimizes a combination of KL and JSD, explaining mode dropping.  
- Under a noisy discriminator model, the `-log D` updates can have infinite variance (Cauchy), explaining extreme instability.  
- Adding continuous (instance) noise to discriminator inputs smooths distributions and yields meaningful gradients that push generator and data manifolds together.  
- Introducing Wasserstein distance as a metric for manifold-supported distributions and proving it is bounded by the JSD of noise-perturbed distributions justifies instance noise theoretically.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
