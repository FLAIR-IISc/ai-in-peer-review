This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution toward understanding the instability inherent in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors claim that when the real and generated data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate them, leading to gradients that are either zero or pathologically unstable. The core contributions are a rigorous proof of this phenomenon for both the original and alternative GAN cost functions, and a theoretically-grounded solution involving the addition of instance noise to smoothen the distributions. This practical solution is elegantly connected to minimizing the Wasserstein distance, a more suitable metric for comparing distributions on manifolds.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are: 1) it offers a novel and compelling theoretical explanation for a critical, widely-observed problem in the field, moving beyond heuristic-based solutions, and 2) the proposed fix is simple, practical, and directly motivated by the theoretical findings.

The theoretical analysis in Section 2 is the paper's core strength. By proving that a perfect discriminator with zero gradients exists when supports are disjoint or on non-aligned low-dimensional manifolds (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2), the paper provides a clear reason for training failure. The subsequent theorems (2.4 and 2.6) convincingly demonstrate how this leads to vanishing gradients for the original cost function and unstable, infinite-variance gradients for the popular `-log(D)` alternative. This analysis is thorough and scientifically rigorous. Furthermore, the proposed solution of adding noise is not presented as another ad-hoc trick; instead, Section 3 provides a clear rationale, showing how it yields continuous distributions and smooth gradients (Corollary 3.2). The connection established in Theorem 3.3, which bounds the Wasserstein distance by the JSD of the noisy distributions, provides a powerful theoretical justification for the entire approach.

For improvement, I would suggest the following:
*   The paper is theoretically dense. Adding more high-level intuition around some definitions, such as "perfectly aligning" manifolds, could improve accessibility for a broader audience.
*   While the theory is the main focus, a more detailed description of the experimental setup for Figures 2 and 3 in the main text would strengthen the empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  In the experiments measuring gradient instability (Figure 3), how was the discriminator trained "closer to optimality"? Was this controlled by the number of discriminator updates per generator update, and if so, what was the range explored?
2.  Regarding the proposed solution, how sensitive is the training stability to the choice and variance (σ²) of the added noise? Does Theorem 3.3 suggest a practical schedule for annealing the noise variance during training?
