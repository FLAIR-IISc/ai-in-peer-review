- Significant theoretical contribution explaining GAN instability when real and generated distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds.
- Claim that an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate such distributions, producing gradients that are zero or pathologically unstable.
- Core contributions: rigorous proofs for both original and alternative GAN cost functions showing this phenomenon.
- Practical, theoretically-grounded solution: add instance noise to smooth distributions and stabilize gradients.
- Connection: adding noise is linked to minimizing the Wasserstein distance; Theorem 3.3 bounds Wasserstein by the JSD of noisy distributions.
- Strong recommendation to accept due to a novel, compelling theoretical explanation and a simple, practical fix.
- Section 2 is the paper’s core strength: Theorems 2.1/2.2 show existence of a perfect discriminator for disjoint/non-aligned supports.
- Theorems 2.4/2.6 demonstrate vanishing gradients for the original cost and infinite-variance/unstable gradients for the -log(D) alternative.
- Section 3 and Corollary 3.2 show that instance noise yields continuous distributions and smooth gradients, justifying the fix.
- Suggestion: add high-level intuition for definitions like “perfectly aligning” manifolds to improve accessibility.
- Suggestion: provide a more detailed experimental setup for Figures 2 and 3 to strengthen empirical validation.
- Question: how was the discriminator trained “closer to optimality” in Figure 3 (e.g., # discriminator updates per generator update and range)?
- Question: how sensitive is training stability to the choice and variance (σ²) of added noise, and does Theorem 3.3 imply a practical annealing schedule?
