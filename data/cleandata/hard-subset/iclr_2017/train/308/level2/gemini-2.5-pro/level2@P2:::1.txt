**Summary of Contributions**

This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution towards understanding the difficulties in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors rigorously analyze the training dynamics of GANs under the well-motivated assumption that the data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The key claims are:
1.  Under the manifold hypothesis, the supports of the real and generated distributions are likely disjoint, allowing for a perfect discriminator that can separate them with 100% accuracy.
2.  Such a perfect discriminator provides useless gradients for training the generator. Specifically, the authors prove that for the original GAN cost function, the generator's gradients vanish as the discriminator improves.
3.  For the commonly used `-log D` alternative cost function, the authors show it optimizes a combination of KL and JSD divergences which explains mode dropping. They further prove that this alternative objective leads to unstable updates with infinite variance when the discriminator is a noisy approximation of the optimal one.
4.  As a solution, the paper proposes adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator, which smooths the distributions. The authors analyze the resulting gradients, showing they provide a meaningful signal that pushes the generator manifold towards the data manifold.
5.  Finally, the paper introduces the Wasserstein distance as a sensible metric for distributions on manifolds and proves a key theorem bounding this distance by the JSD of the noise-perturbed distributions. This provides a principled justification for training GANs with instance noise as a means of minimizing a more appropriate distance between the true distributions.

**Decision: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  It provides the first rigorous and compelling theoretical explanation for the instability and vanishing gradient problems that are widely observed in GAN training. This is a major step forward from the heuristic-based approaches that have dominated the field.
2.  The proposed solution of adding instance noise is grounded in a solid theoretical framework, culminating in an elegant connection to the Wasserstein distance, which opens up new avenues for both training and evaluating GANs.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its deep and careful theoretical analysis. The authors build their argument from a simple, plausible assumption—that real-world data and generator outputs are concentrated on low-dimensional manifolds.

The proofs in Section 2 are the paper's core. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 formalize the intuition that if two distributions have non-overlapping supports (which is highly likely for low-dimensional manifolds in a high-dimensional space), an optimal discriminator will be perfect. The critical consequence, that the gradient of this discriminator is zero almost everywhere on the data manifolds, provides a clear reason for learning failure. Theorem 2.4 then rigorously shows that for the original GAN objective, this leads to vanishing gradients, explaining a well-known empirical pathology.

The analysis of the `-log D` cost function is equally insightful. Theorem 2.5 reveals the objective being optimized, providing a theoretical justification for the empirically observed phenomena of high-quality samples at the cost of mode dropping. More strikingly, Theorem 2.6, which shows the generator's updates follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance, is a powerful explanation for the extreme instability many practitioners experience.

The proposed solution in Section 3 is not just a heuristic fix but is derived directly from the preceding analysis. Adding noise makes the distributions absolutely continuous, sidestepping the issues of disjoint supports. The analysis of the resulting gradient in Theorem 3.2 is excellent, showing it corresponds to a form of distance-weighted attraction/repulsion between the data and generator manifolds. The final connection to the Wasserstein metric via Theorem 3.3 is the capstone of the paper. It bridges the gap between a tractable training objective (minimizing JSD on noisy distributions) and a more desirable, but intractable, metric on the original distributions (Wasserstein distance). This provides a firm theoretical foundation for a new class of principled GAN training methods.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.

1.  **Assumptions in Instability Proof:** The assumption in Theorem 2.6 that the error in the discriminator and its gradient are modeled by independent white noise Gaussian processes is quite strong. While it leads to a clean result (Cauchy distribution), it would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the conclusion to this assumption. Perhaps a brief argument could be made for why this is a reasonable model for the error of a neural network approximator, or a discussion on whether similar instability would arise under more relaxed noise assumptions.

2.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper is primarily theoretical, and the experiments are "targeted" to verify specific theoretical claims (Figs. 2 & 3). This is perfectly valid. However, the paper's impact could be substantially increased by including experiments that demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed noise-based training method. For instance, training a standard GAN architecture (like DCGAN) on a dataset like CIFAR-10 with and without the proposed noise injection, and comparing training stability (e.g., gradient norms over time) and final sample quality (e.g., Inception Score) would provide compelling evidence for the practical utility of the theory.

3.  **Algorithmic Clarity:** Theorem 3.3 suggests a practical training algorithm: train a GAN on noisy inputs while annealing the noise variance. It would be helpful to explicitly state this algorithm in a separate box or paragraph and to provide some practical guidance on the choice and annealing schedule of the noise.

**Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you provide more details about the experimental setup for Figures 2 and 3? For example, how was the discriminator trained "closer to optimality" at different stages? Was this achieved by increasing the number of discriminator updates per generator update?

2.  The gradient analysis in Theorem 3.2 is performed for Gaussian noise. How does the choice of the noise distribution P(epsilon) affect the resulting gradients? Does it correspond to minimizing different notions of distance between the manifolds?

3.  Theorem 3.3 provides a theoretical upper bound on the Wasserstein distance. Do you have any intuition or empirical results on how tight this bound is in practice? Could this relationship be leveraged to create a new, more stable evaluation metric for GANs by computing or approximating JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) for a fixed noise level?
