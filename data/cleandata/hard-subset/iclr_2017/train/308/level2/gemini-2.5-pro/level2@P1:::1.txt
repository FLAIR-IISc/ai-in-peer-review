### **Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a thorough theoretical investigation into the training dynamics and common failures of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors' main contribution is not a new model architecture, but a principled analysis of why GAN training is often unstable. They begin by arguing that both the real data distribution and the generator's distribution lie on low-dimensional manifolds. This insight leads to their core theoretical results: if these manifolds do not overlap significantly, an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate them, leading to gradients that are zero almost everywhere.

The paper then rigorously analyzes the consequences of this for the two most common generator cost functions. It proves that for the original `log(1-D)` cost, a near-perfect discriminator results in vanishing gradients for the generator. For the alternative `-log(D)` cost, it proves that the updates are optimizing a combination of a reverse KL divergence and a negative JSD term, and more critically, that gradients from a near-optimal discriminator can have infinite variance, providing a theoretical explanation for the extreme training instability observed in practice.

As a remedy, the paper proposes adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This effectively smooths the distributions, ensuring their supports overlap and providing well-behaved, non-vanishing gradients. Finally, the authors connect this practical solution to a more suitable metric for comparing distributions on manifolds: the Wasserstein distance. They prove a key theorem that bounds the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions by a function of the noise variance and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noise-perturbed distributions, thus justifying the proposed training procedure as a principled way to minimize a meaningful distance between the true data and generator distributions.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent paper that provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for understanding the challenges of training GANs. My decision is based on two key strengths:

1.  **Rigorous and insightful diagnosis of GAN instability:** The paper moves beyond heuristics and folklore to provide a formal explanation for why GANs are difficult to train. The theoretical arc—from the low-dimensional manifold hypothesis to the existence of a perfect discriminator and the resulting vanishing or exploding gradients—is novel, coherent, and powerfully explanatory.
2.  **A theoretically-grounded and practical solution:** The proposed solution of adding instance noise is not presented as another ad-hoc trick, but as a direct consequence of the theoretical analysis. The connection drawn to the Wasserstein distance in Section 3 is particularly compelling, as it provides a new, sensible objective for GAN training and evaluation.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The primary contribution of this work is the clear and rigorous theoretical analysis presented in Section 2. The proofs that the generator's distribution lies on a low-dimensional manifold (Lemma 1) and that this leads to the existence of a perfect discriminator with zero gradients on the data manifolds (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are fundamental insights. These results formalize intuitions that many practitioners have, but had previously lacked a solid theoretical backing for.

The subsequent theorems (2.4 and 2.6) are the crux of the paper's diagnostic power. Theorem 2.4, on vanishing gradients for the original cost function, elegantly explains the trade-off where improving the discriminator (which is necessary for a meaningful loss signal) paradoxically destroys the learning signal for the generator. Even more impressive is Theorem 2.6, which provides a concrete explanation for the massive instability of the popular `-log(D)` objective by showing that its gradients can follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance. This is a significant step forward from simply observing that training is "unstable."

The proposed solution in Section 3 is a direct and logical remedy to the problems identified in Section 2. By adding noise, the supports of the distributions are guaranteed to overlap, making the JSD a well-defined and smooth objective. The final result, Theorem 3.3, is a highlight of the paper. It elegantly connects the practical training objective (minimizing JSD on noisy data) to a more robust theoretical metric (Wasserstein distance on the original data). This provides a principled justification for the proposed method and opens up new avenues for thinking about GAN objectives.

### **Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, I believe the following points could help improve it further. These are suggestions and not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Experimental Validation of the Solution:** The experiments provided are excellent for verifying the paper's theoretical claims about the *problem* (e.g., Figure 3 showing gradient norm explosion). However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by an experiment that validates the proposed *solution*. A simple demonstration of a GAN trained with instance noise, perhaps showing improved training stability or sample quality compared to a baseline DCGAN, would make the practical implications of the theory more concrete.
*   **Intuition for Theoretical Concepts:** The paper is quite dense and mathematically rigorous. While this is a strength, including some more intuitive explanations or simple diagrams could improve accessibility. For example, a 2D illustration showing two disjoint 1D manifolds, the effect of adding noise to create overlapping 2D distributions, and how this provides a meaningful gradient, could help readers grasp the core geometric intuition quickly.
*   **Discussion on the Wasserstein Objective:** The introduction of the Wasserstein distance as a sensible metric is very insightful. This naturally raises the question of whether it is possible to optimize this distance more directly, rather than optimizing an upper bound on it. A brief discussion of this possibility as a direction for future work would be a valuable addition.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following points during the discussion period to clarify my understanding:

1.  The model for discriminator error in Theorem 2.6 (independent white noise for both the function value and its gradient) is a simplification. How critical is the independence assumption for the result of infinite variance? Would the conclusion still hold under a more realistic model where the errors in D(x) and its gradient are correlated?
2.  Theorem 3.3 provides a valuable upper bound on the Wasserstein distance. However, the bound involves the term `C`, the diameter of the support of the noisy distributions. In high-dimensional spaces, `C` could be very large. Could you comment on how tight this bound is expected to be in practice and whether a large `C` could weaken the practical utility of minimizing JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) as a proxy for minimizing W(Pr, Pg)?
3.  Have you performed any experiments with different types of noise (e.g., uniform vs. Gaussian) or different noise annealing schedules? It would be interesting to know how sensitive the proposed training stability improvements are to these choices.
