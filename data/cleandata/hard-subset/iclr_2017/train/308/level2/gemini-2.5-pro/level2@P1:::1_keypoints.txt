Paper presents rigorous theoretical analysis attributing GAN instability to low-dimensional data/generator manifolds.  
If data and generator manifolds don’t overlap, an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate them, yielding zero gradients almost everywhere.  
For the original log(1-D) generator cost, a near-perfect discriminator provably causes vanishing gradients.  
For the -log(D) generator cost, gradients can have infinite variance (Cauchy-like), explaining extreme training instability.  
Proposes adding instance noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions, ensure overlap, and provide stable, non-vanishing gradients.  
Connects the instance-noise remedy to the Wasserstein distance and proves a bound relating W(Pr,Pg) to noise variance and JSD of noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3).  
Decision: Accept — paper provides a needed theoretical foundation and a principled practical remedy.  
Strength: Rigorous, novel diagnosis moving from manifold hypothesis to formal vanishing/exploding gradient results.  
Strength: Theoretically-grounded practical solution with a compelling link to Wasserstein distance.  
Supporting: Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1/2.2 formalize manifolds and the existence of a perfect discriminator.  
Supporting: Theorems 2.4 and 2.6 explain vanishing gradients for log(1-D) and infinite-variance gradients for -log(D).  
Supporting: Theorem 3.3 justifies minimizing JSD on noisy data as a proxy for reducing Wasserstein distance on original data.  
Suggestion: Add experiments validating that instance noise improves GAN training stability and sample quality.  
Suggestion: Include intuitive explanations or simple diagrams (e.g., 2D manifold visuals) to aid accessibility.  
Suggestion: Discuss prospects of optimizing Wasserstein distance directly rather than an upper bound.  
Question: How critical is the independence assumption in Theorem 2.6 for concluding infinite variance when D(x) and its gradient errors are correlated?  
Question: How tight/useful is the Wasserstein upper bound in practice given the diameter term C, especially in high dimensions?  
Question: Have you tried different noise types (Gaussian vs. uniform) or annealing schedules, and how sensitive are stability gains to these choices?
