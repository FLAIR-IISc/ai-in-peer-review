- Tackles GAN training dynamics, aiming to explain instability and saturation and propose remedies.  
- Paper is well-structured into problem intro, theoretical analysis, and a proposed solution with new tools.  
- Decision: reject due to insufficient empirical validation for the extensive theoretical claims.  
- Theoretical analysis is mathematically rigorous but its practical relevance is unproven.  
- Core claim: generator and real data supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds, enabling perfect discrimination and causing vanishing/unstable gradients.  
- Theorems are presented demonstrating perfect discrimination and its negative effects on gradients.  
- Proposed solution: add noise to smooth distributions and promote more stable training.  
- Empirical evidence is weak: Figures 1 & 2 are illustrative without quantitative rigor (Fig.2 improvement not quantified); Fig.3 shows instability but isn’t tied to the solution.  
- Recommendation: add extensive experiments comparing the method to SOTA GANs (e.g., WGAN, LSGAN) on benchmarks like CIFAR-10 and CelebA.  
- Experiments should report quantitative metrics (Inception Score, FID) and evaluate stability, sample quality, and mode collapse.  
- Analyze sensitivity to theoretical assumptions (e.g., what happens if data are not on low-dimensional manifolds).  
- Provide more intuitive explanations of core concepts, particularly the implications of the perfect discrimination theorems.  
- Expand related work discussion, especially detailing connections to Wasserstein GANs.  
- Clarify practical implementation details of noise addition (e.g., how to choose the noise standard deviation).  
- Specific questions for authors: explain practical noise implementation and selection; compare to existing methods on hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost; provide evidence about mode collapse; state limitations and failure conditions; clarify how theoretical perfect discrimination maps to empirical vanishing gradients and how noise mitigates it.
