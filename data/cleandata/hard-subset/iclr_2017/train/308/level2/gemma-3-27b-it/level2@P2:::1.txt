This paper tackles the fundamental problem of understanding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), moving beyond heuristic approaches to provide a theoretical foundation for their notorious instability. The authors aim to rigorously analyze the issues of instability and saturation during GAN training and propose a theoretically grounded direction for improvement.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the problem addressed is undeniably important and the paper presents some interesting theoretical insights, the arguments are presented with a significant lack of clarity, reliance on unmotivated claims, and insufficient experimental validation to support the core arguments. The paper feels more like a collection of theoretical observations than a cohesive, substantiated contribution.

The core issue is a lack of connection between the theoretical arguments and practical implications. The authors identify “perfect discrimination” scenarios and then claim these lead to vanishing gradients or unstable updates. However, the conditions required for these perfect discriminators (disjoint supports or low-dimensional manifolds) are stated without sufficient justification for *why* they frequently occur in practice, beyond a cursory mention of the generator's output being on a low-dimensional manifold. While the Lemma regarding the dimension of the generator’s output is interesting, its impact on the overall argument isn’t fully explained.

Furthermore, the theoretical analysis of the cost functions – the original GAN loss and the alternative – feels disconnected. The authors claim the alternative loss suffers from mode dropping, but this is asserted rather than demonstrated with compelling analysis or connection to existing literature on mode collapse. The claim that the generator gradient has a Cauchy distribution is strong and requires significantly more justification, especially given its implications. Simply stating it arises from noisy approximations isn’t enough; a more detailed analysis of the noise distribution and its impact on the gradient’s properties is needed.

Finally, the proposed solution – adding noise and using the Wasserstein metric – is presented as a logical consequence of the analysis, but the link remains weak. The theoretical benefits are stated, but there's no empirical evidence presented to demonstrate that this approach actually improves GAN training *in practice*. Figures 2 and 3, while mentioned, are not sufficient to establish the claimed instabilities; the axes are unlabeled and the results are presented without comparative performance metrics. The connection between the theoretical Wasserstein metric discussion and any actual implementation or results is entirely missing.

Here are additional feedback points to improve the paper:

*   **Clarity:** The writing is often dense and assumes a high level of prior knowledge. Many arguments are presented as facts without sufficient motivation or explanation. Consider adding more intuitive explanations and examples.
*   **Justification of Assumptions:** The core arguments rely on assumptions about the nature of the data distribution (Pr) and the generator’s output (Pg). These assumptions need to be better justified with empirical evidence or references to relevant literature. Why are disjoint supports or low-dimensional manifolds common in GAN training scenarios?
*   **Experimental Validation:** The paper lacks sufficient experimental validation. The authors should include experiments demonstrating the predicted instabilities and the effectiveness of the proposed noise addition and Wasserstein metric approach. Comparative results with standard GAN training techniques are essential.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work. For example, the connection between the “perfect discriminator” scenario and the vanishing gradient problem has been explored in other contexts. Discussing these connections would strengthen the paper's contribution.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** Some of the proofs are sketchily presented. The leap to a Cauchy distribution feels particularly unsupported. More detailed derivations and explanations are needed.
*   **Figure Quality:** Improve the quality of the figures. Axis labels, clear descriptions, and comparison with baseline methods are necessary.



I have several questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide empirical evidence supporting the claim that the supports of Pr and Pg often have disjoint properties or lie on low-dimensional manifolds in typical GAN training scenarios?
*   What is a reasonable justification for the Gaussian noise assumption in the proposed solution? How sensitive are the results to the choice of noise distribution?
*   Can you provide a detailed explanation of the computational cost of using the Wasserstein metric compared to the standard GAN loss functions?
*   What specific architectural choices or hyperparameters were used in the experiments shown in Figures 2 and 3? What baseline methods were used for comparison?
*   Can you elaborate on the connection between the "perfect discriminator" and the known issues of mode collapse in GANs?
