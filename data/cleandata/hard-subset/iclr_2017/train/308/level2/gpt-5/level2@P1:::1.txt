Summary of contributions
- The paper analyzes why standard GAN training is unstable and why generator gradients either vanish or explode as the discriminator improves. The authors formalize a commonly observed but insufficiently explained phenomenon: when the real and model distributions live on low-dimensional manifolds with negligible overlap (the typical case for neural generators), there exist smooth, perfect discriminators whose gradients are zero almost everywhere on the supports. They provide rigorous statements via manifold arguments (Lemmas 1–3, Theorems 2.1–2.3).
- For the original GAN generator update (minimize log(1−D)), they prove vanishing gradients as D approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4). For the commonly used “non-saturating” update (maximize log D), they show the update is not aligned with maximum likelihood; at a fixed D*, the gradient equals ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)] at θ0 (Theorem 2.5), shedding light on mode dropping. Furthermore, under a simple noise model for discriminator approximation error, the generator update exhibits heavy-tailed instability (Cauchy coordinates; Theorem 2.6).
- To address these issues, they propose convolving both the data and model distributions with noise (instance noise), thereby making them absolutely continuous and restoring informative gradients. They derive the resulting gradients (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2), argue that training with noise approximates minimizing JSD between smoothed distributions, and prove a bound relating the Wasserstein distance between the original (unsmoothed) distributions to the noise variance and JSD of the smoothed distributions (Theorem 3.3). This gives a principled annealing scheme: train with noise to stabilize gradients; reduce noise to drive the true distributions closer.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, rigorous analysis that explains widely observed GAN pathologies and distinguishes the behavior of the two standard generator objectives; (2) A principled, theoretically grounded remedy (instance noise + annealing) with supporting analysis and useful connections to Wasserstein distance.

Supporting arguments
- Importance and clarity of the problem: Understanding GAN instability and mode dropping was a central open issue. The paper squarely targets this and frames it in terms of the geometry of supports, which is realistic for neural generators and real data.
- Rigor and placement in literature: The manifold arguments (Lemma 1; use of transversality and Sard’s lemma) make the “supports on low-dimensional sets” claim precise for common network classes. The instability/vanishing gradient results formalize anecdotes reported in Radford et al., Salimans et al., and community practice. The link to divergences (KL directions and JSD) engages prior discussions (Huszár, Theis et al.).
- Novel insights on objectives: Theorem 2.5 gives a concrete characterization of what the non-saturating update is doing near a fixed D*, explaining mode dropping tendencies by tying it to KL(Pg||Pr) minus a term pushing away via JSD.
- Remedy and theory: Convolution with noise to ensure overlap is simple and practical. The gradient form in Theorem 3.2/Corollary 3.2 provides an interpretable “attract-toward-data, repel-from-model” force, and the Wasserstein upper bound (Theorem 3.3) gives a principled reason why annealing the noise can reduce the true distance. This is a useful, theoretically motivated training heuristic.
- Empirical alignment: Although experiments are only briefly mentioned, the reported trends (vanishing gradients as D improves for the saturating loss; variance/explosion for non-saturating updates) are consistent with broad practitioner experience.

Caveats
- Some assumptions are strong. The white-noise, independent error model for D and ∇D in Theorem 2.6 is idealized; correlations across x are expected in practice. The authors acknowledge this and provide qualitative evidence, but a more realistic model would strengthen the result.
- The theory covers common fully connected and piecewise-linear networks via Lemma 1; it would help to explicitly state applicability to CNN-based generators (which remain piecewise affine), and any caveats for architectures with stochastic layers or skip connections.
- Empirical validation appears limited in scope. The paper claims “targeted experiments,” but it would be valuable to show a systematic study of the proposed instance-noise training on standard benchmarks, including ablations on noise level and annealing schedules and metrics for mode coverage and sample quality.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Clarify notation to avoid confusion between Euclidean norm and its square. Earlier you use ‖·‖2 for Euclidean norm and ‖·‖22 for its square; keep this consistent in Definition 3.1 and Lemma 4.
- The optimal discriminator expression D*(x) should be checked for typos in Section 2.5 and elsewhere. Ensure all fractions are rendered correctly to avoid ambiguity.
- Theorem 2.5: Provide an intuitive derivation in the main text (perhaps in a short appendix) and a brief discussion of when each term dominates; concrete toy examples could illustrate the mode-dropping behavior predicted by the KL(Pg||Pr) direction.
- Theorem 2.6: Since the independence assumption is strong, consider stating a version under mild correlation structures (e.g., Gaussian processes with stationary kernels) to show that heavy tails persist under broader conditions, or at least provide a robustness discussion.
- Experiments: Add a thorough empirical section demonstrating the instance-noise method with annealing on MNIST/CIFAR-10/CelebA. Include comparisons to standard GAN training, report inception-type metrics (or other quantitative proxies available), visualize how gradient norms and discriminator accuracy evolve with and without noise, and show the effect on mode coverage (e.g., class-conditional counts on CIFAR-10).
- Relation to contemporary work: Discuss connections and contrasts to Wasserstein GAN (if concurrent) and f-GANs. Your method stabilizes JSD on convolved distributions and provides an upper bound to Wasserstein distance; it would be instructive to compare the optimization behavior and robustness of both approaches.

Questions for the authors
1. Empirical evidence: Can you provide quantitative results showing that instance noise with annealing improves stability and mode coverage across datasets and architectures, including ablations over noise variance schedules?
2. Robustness of Theorem 2.6: How sensitive is the heavy-tailed gradient claim to relaxing the white-noise, independence assumptions? Do you observe similar heavy tails when D-approximation errors are correlated in space?
3. Practical implementation: Do you add the same Gaussian noise to both real and generated samples during discriminator training and to generated samples during generator updates (as suggested by Corollary 3.2)? What annealing schedule do you recommend, and how sensitive are results to σ initialization and decay?
4. Scope of Lemma 1: Does the “countable union of manifolds” result extend directly to convolutional and residual architectures used in DCGAN-like generators? Any exceptions (e.g., non-pointwise nonlinearities, stochastic layers)?
5. On Theorem 3.3: Can you tighten the constants or provide empirical evidence that minimizing JSD between smoothed distributions indeed tracks reductions in an empirical approximation to W1 between the original distributions during training?
6. Non-saturating update analysis: In Theorem 2.5, does the negative JSD term practically dominate near typical training configurations, or is the KL(Pg||Pr) term dominant? Any experiments isolating their relative contributions?

Overall, this paper offers timely and useful theory explaining GAN pathologies and proposes an elegant, practical stabilization strategy grounded in analysis. With strengthened empirical validation and minor clarifications, it would make a solid contribution.
