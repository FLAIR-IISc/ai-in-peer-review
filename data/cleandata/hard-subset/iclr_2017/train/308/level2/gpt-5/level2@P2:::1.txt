1. Summary and contributions
The paper provides a theoretical analysis of why GAN training is unstable and why gradients can vanish or explode as the discriminator improves. The core contributions are:
- Formalizing that under realistic assumptions (data and model distributions supported on low-dimensional manifolds that do not perfectly align), the optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant (zero gradient) almost everywhere on both supports. This explains vanishing gradients for the generator under the original GAN objective.
- Showing that the commonly used “non-saturating” generator update −log D corresponds locally to optimizing KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr), which biases toward plausible samples and tolerates mode dropping, and can yield heavy-tailed (Cauchy) gradient noise under a noisy discriminator approximation, explaining empirical instability.
- Proposing “instance noise” (adding continuous noise to inputs of both discriminator and generator during backprop) to regularize the problem, ensuring overlapping supports and smooth gradients. The paper derives explicit generator gradients that attract model samples toward the data manifold and repel them from high-density model regions, and argues this stabilizes training.
- Establishing a bound that connects the Wasserstein distance between the true (noiseless) distributions and the Jensen–Shannon divergence between their noisy counterparts plus a noise-variance term, motivating annealing the noise and training a discriminator on the noisy distributions.

Overall, the paper aims to provide principled explanations for known GAN pathologies and proposes a theoretically grounded remedy with targeted empirical illustrations.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper addresses an important and timely problem with insightful, mostly sound analysis that clarifies widely observed GAN behaviors (vanishing gradients, instability, mode dropping) and proposes a simple, practical regularization (instance noise) with a clear theoretical rationale.
- However, there are nontrivial issues in the Wasserstein section (definition/inequalities) and some strong assumptions (e.g., white-noise discriminator error independence) that limit the generality or correctness of certain claims. These appear fixable and do not undermine the main qualitative message.

3. Supporting arguments
Strengths:
- Clear identification of the manifold-support mismatch as a primary source of perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients, backed by Theorems 2.1–2.4. The formal geometric setup (transversality, Sard’s lemma) is appropriate and insightful.
- The local equivalence derived in Theorem 2.5 for the −log D generator update provides a solid theoretical explanation for mode dropping and for the empirical observation that stabilized GANs tend to prioritize sample plausibility over coverage.
- The Cauchy-noise result (Theorem 2.6), while relying on strong independence assumptions, offers a compelling stylized model for unstable gradients as the discriminator becomes near-optimal.
- The instance-noise proposal is well motivated: adding noise yields absolutely continuous, overlapping distributions; the derived gradient expressions (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2) are intuitive (attraction to data manifold, repulsion from model manifold) and align with empirical regularization practices. The idea of annealing the noise is principled and practical.

Concerns/limitations:
- Wasserstein section: The definition of W(P,Q) uses ∫‖x−y‖^2 dγ without a square root, but the subsequent properties (triangle inequality, Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality link to total variation) are for W1 (with cost ‖x−y‖), not the squared-cost version. This leads to inconsistencies in Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 (both constants and which metric is being used). This needs correction (either redefine W as W1 or use W2 with appropriate square roots and adjusted inequalities).
- Pinsker’s inequality constants are imprecise (δ ≤ sqrt(KL/2)), and the JSD definition earlier also contains coefficient typos. While constants do not change the qualitative conclusion, the presentation should be precise.
- Theorem 2.6 assumes independent white noise for D and its gradient across x and independence between numerator and denominator; in practice, discriminator errors are structured and correlated. The result is best viewed as an illustrative extreme; the paper acknowledges this but should better qualify the scope of the claim and (ideally) provide empirical evidence of heavy tails in gradient updates.
- Some assumptions (compactness of supports, smoothness, “continuity on the manifold”) and technical lemmas (e.g., Urysohn’s smooth lemma application) should be stated with more care; data manifolds need not be compact.
- Empirical validation is limited and mostly diagnostic. While the figures reportedly illustrate vanishing gradients and gradient-norm blow-up, there is no comprehensive experimental demonstration that instance noise + annealing materially improves training across standard benchmarks, nor comparisons to prevailing stabilization techniques (e.g., feature matching, minibatch discrimination). This weakens the practical significance.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Fix the Wasserstein section: choose W1 (earth mover’s) or W2 consistently. If using W1, redefine W(P,Q)=infγ ∫‖x−y‖ dγ, then Lemma 4 and the triangle inequality follow naturally; the bound to total variation with diameter C is standard. If using W2, introduce square roots and adjust bounds and constants accordingly.
- Make constants precise: correct JSD coefficients and Pinsker constants; isolate constants to a single symbol if brevity is desired.
- Better qualify Theorem 2.6: clarify that it models the limiting behavior with unstructured noise and discuss how correlation would alter the conclusion. Empirically test gradient distributions (e.g., tail index estimates) as the discriminator approaches optimality.
- Expand empirical section: demonstrate that instance noise + annealing stabilizes training and improves metrics (e.g., Inception Score) on CIFAR-10/LSUN compared to vanilla GAN and common stabilization baselines; include ablations on noise variance schedules.
- Clarify assumptions in Theorems 2.1–2.2 (compactness, boundary conditions) and discuss practical implications when supports are not compact. Ensure all referenced lemmas are properly cited and standard conditions are met.
- Editing: fix typos (e.g., “symetrical,” “aboslutely”), improve reference formatting, and ensure all citations (e.g., Huszár notes) are complete.

Questions for the authors
- Can you resolve the metric inconsistency and restate Theorem 3.3 with the correct Wasserstein definition and constants? Does the qualitative conclusion (anneal noise + minimize JSD of noisy distributions reduces W(Pr,Pg)) still hold with the corrected metric?
- How sensitive is the proposed instance-noise scheme to the choice of noise distribution and annealing schedule? Can you provide a practical recipe (initial σ, decay schedule) and demonstrate robustness across datasets and architectures?
- Do you observe heavy-tailed generator gradient updates empirically as D approaches optimality? If so, can you quantify tail behavior or report variance/quantile trends?
- How does training with instance noise compare empirically to other stabilization techniques (feature matching, historical averaging, gradient penalties) in terms of stability and sample quality?
- Can the analysis extend to f-GANs or Wasserstein GAN variants where the critic has Lipschitz constraints? How do your conclusions about vanishing gradients and instability translate under such constraints?
