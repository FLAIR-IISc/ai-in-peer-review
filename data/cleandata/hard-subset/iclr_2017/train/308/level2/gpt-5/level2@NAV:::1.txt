Summary and contributions: The paper investigates why GAN training is unstable and provides a principled analysis of generator gradients under common objectives. The central claims are: (1) when real and model distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds with non-overlapping support (the generic case), optimal discriminators are perfect and locally flat almost everywhere on the supports, which yields vanishing generator gradients for the original GAN loss; (2) using the alternative −log D generator update avoids vanishing but becomes intrinsically unstable under realistic discriminator approximation noise; (3) injecting continuous noise (instance noise) to both data and generator outputs smooths the distributions, yields well-behaved gradients proportional to ∇ JSD of the smoothed distributions, and admits a bound relating the true Wasserstein distance to the JSD between the noisy distributions plus a term controlled by noise variance. The paper provides rigorous theorems and targeted experiments to illustrate these phenomena and introduces tools (manifold-based discrimination results, gradient characterizations, and Wasserstein/JSD connections) to study GAN dynamics.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (i) Strong, clear theoretical advances that directly address widely observed GAN pathologies; (ii) A principled and practical training remedy (instance noise with annealing) supported by analysis and preliminary evidence.

Supporting arguments: The “perfect discrimination” theorems (2.1–2.3) are technically sound and make explicit the measure-theoretic pitfalls of divergences on manifolds. Theorem 2.4 formalizes vanishing gradients for the original loss as D→D*, and Theorem 2.6 convincingly explains the instability of −log D under realistic noisy discriminator estimates (heavy-tailed updates). Section 3 gives a compelling alternative: smoothing restores absolute continuity, leading to stable gradients (Cor. 3.2) and a useful bound (Thm 3.3) linking the optimization of a computable quantity (JSD of smoothed distributions) to the true metric of interest (Wasserstein), with an annealing path that controls the bias. The work is well-situated in the literature and uses standard tools (Sard’s lemma, Pinsker, KR duality).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper): 
- Expand empirical validation: ablate noise type and variance schedules, compare against strong baselines, and report quantitative metrics and stability diagnostics across datasets. 
- Discuss the realism of the “white-noise, independent across x” assumption and explore correlated/noise-in-gradient settings. 
- Clarify applicability with finite datasets and discrete pixelization. 
- Improve reference formatting and ensure proofs address nonsmooth activations (e.g., ReLU corners) explicitly. 
- Provide implementation details for reproducibility (noise annealing schedules, architecture, optimization settings).

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive are results to the precise noise distribution/covariance, and how should practitioners choose and anneal it? 
- Do the instability results persist under more realistic discriminator error models (e.g., correlated or structured noise)? 
- Can you quantify trade-offs between bias from noise and variance/stability in practice? 
- How does your approach compare empirically to Wasserstein GAN variants and gradient penalties on identical setups?
