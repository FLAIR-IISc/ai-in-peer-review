Summary and contributions
The paper analyzes why training GANs is unstable and proposes a principled remedy. The main contributions are:

- Formalization of the “manifolds with disjoint/low-dimensional support” setting for data and generator distributions. Under mild assumptions on neural network generators (piecewise linear or smooth monotone activations), the generated distribution lies on a countable union of manifolds with dimension at most that of the latent space (Lemma 1).
- Perfect discrimination results: when the supports of data and generator lie on disjoint or non-perfectly-aligned low-dimensional manifolds, an optimal discriminator exists that achieves accuracy 1 and is locally constant (zero gradient) almost everywhere on the supports (Theorems 2.1–2.2). Consequently, JSD is maxed out and both KL divergences diverge (Theorem 2.3).
- Generator gradient analysis: (i) for the original GAN generator objective log(1−D(gθ(z))), the gradient vanishes as D approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4 and corollary), and (ii) for the commonly used −log D(gθ(z)) alternative, when D is a noisy approximation to the optimal discriminator, the update becomes a centered Cauchy with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6), explaining empirically observed instability.
- A principled stabilization via instance noise: adding continuous noise to inputs smooths the distributions so that supports overlap; the resulting gradients toward the data manifold are derived explicitly (Theorem 3.2) and, when adding noise to both real and generated inputs and backpropagating through generator noise, the update equals the gradient of JSD between the noisy distributions (Corollary 3.2), yielding stable, meaningful signal.
- A link to transport metrics: a bound that relates the Wasserstein distance between the true (noiseless) distributions and the JSD between their noisy counterparts plus the noise variance (Theorem 3.3), justifying annealing noise while minimizing a tractable divergence.
- Targeted experiments show vanishing gradients for the original objective and exploding, high-variance gradients for the −log D objective as D improves, supporting the theoretical claims.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Rigorous and insightful analysis that explains widely reported GAN pathologies through precise geometric and measure-theoretic arguments; (2) A practical, theoretically grounded stabilization strategy (instance noise) with a clear optimization target and a provable relationship to the desired noiseless objective.

Supporting arguments
- Significance and clarity of the question: The work squarely tackles core issues in GAN training (saturation, vanishing/unstable gradients, mode dropping) that have hindered progress and have often been addressed heuristically.
- Technical correctness and novelty: The manifold-support and perfect discrimination results are carefully stated with appropriate regularity assumptions; the vanishing gradient result for the original generator objective is clean and aligns with practice. The identification of the −log D update as a combination of ∇ KL(Pg||Pr) and −∇ JSD at θ0, and the Cauchy update result under white-noise discriminator errors, provide a compelling explanation for instability. The instance-noise derivations, including the explicit gradient form and the JSD-of-noisy-distributions interpretation, are insightful. The Wasserstein bound is a useful tool to connect noisy training to a metric on the original distributions.
- Placement in literature: The paper relates GAN training to JSD/KL behavior and motivates alternative distances (Wasserstein). The perspective complements existing empirical stabilization techniques and connects to broader generative modeling discussions about f-divergences and IPMs. Citations to prior empirical work and divergence discussions are present; expanding related work would strengthen the positioning (see feedback).
- Empirical support: While limited to targeted measurements (e.g., gradient norms vs discriminator optimality), the experiments are appropriate to validate the theoretical phenomena, and the trends reported are consistent with the analysis.

Feedback to improve the paper (to help, not required for the decision)
Essential:
- Clarify assumptions and scope:
  - State explicitly the architectural conditions under which Lemma 1 holds (e.g., inclusion of biases, batch normalization effects, stochastic generators) and whether results extend to generators with injected noise at intermediate layers (non-degenerate mixtures may yield absolutely continuous Pg).
  - In Theorems 2.1–2.2, discuss practical implications when supports are not strictly compact or when distributions have small absolutely continuous components; indicate robustness of conclusions under approximate conditions.
- Strengthen the discriminator error model: Theorem 2.6 assumes independent white noise for D and ∇D. Provide an alternative argument or empirical evidence for more realistic, correlated approximation errors (e.g., bounded but heteroscedastic errors across X). A weaker assumption still implying large variance or sign-incoherent updates would further substantiate instability claims for −log D.
- Empirical validation of instance noise:
  - Include experiments demonstrating that adding/annealing input noise stabilizes training and improves diversity/quality across at least one image dataset and architecture, with comparisons to a no-noise baseline. Report gradient statistics (mean/variance), training curves, and sample quality/diversity proxies.
  - Provide practical guidance on selecting the noise distribution and annealing schedule, and an ablation on sensitivity to σ and to adding noise on real only vs both real and generated samples.

Nice-to-have:
- Discuss relations to f-divergence GANs and IPM-based objectives (e.g., how instance noise compares to alternatives that directly optimize metrics with weaker topology, and whether the analysis predicts similar benefits).
- Expand on the adversarial example connection in Section 3: quantify how adding generator noise mitigates measure-zero adversarial directions seen by D when evaluated on g(Z).
- Tightness and constants in Theorem 3.3: comment on regimes where the bound is informative, and whether alternative couplings yield improved constants.

Questions for rebuttal/clarification
- How should the noise level be chosen and annealed in practice? Is there an adaptive criterion (e.g., discriminator accuracy plateau, estimated JSD-noisy, or gradient variance) to schedule σ?
- Do the conclusions change if the generator includes sufficient internal noise to render Pg absolutely continuous (e.g., additive Gaussian at each layer)? Under what conditions would vanishing/instability persist or disappear?
- Can Theorem 2.6 be extended to a bounded, correlated error model for D and ∇D, and if so, what stability properties can be guaranteed?
- How sensitive is the proposed method to the choice of noise distribution (Gaussian vs heavy-tailed) and to anisotropy (Σ ≠ σ2I)? Are there benefits to learned or data-adaptive noise covariances?
- In practice, does minimizing JSD between noisy distributions correlate with reduced Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions, as suggested by Theorem 3.3? Any empirical measurement of this surrogate relationship on toy or image domains?

Overall, the work provides a clear, rigorous explanation of core GAN issues and a principled stabilization mechanism with actionable guidance. The theoretical development and targeted experiments support acceptance. Further empirical demonstrations and clarification of assumptions would increase impact.
