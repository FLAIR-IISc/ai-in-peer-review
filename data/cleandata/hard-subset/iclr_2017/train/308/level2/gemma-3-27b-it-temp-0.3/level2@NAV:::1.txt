## Review of "Towards Understanding the Training Dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks"

This paper tackles the fundamental problem of understanding the notoriously unstable training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). Rather than proposing a new algorithm, the authors aim to provide a theoretical foundation for these instabilities and suggest principled directions for improvement. They rigorously analyze the issues of instability and saturation, and introduce new tools for studying them, ultimately proposing a noise-based approach to stabilize training.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper in its current form. While the identified problem is crucial and the ambition of a theoretical understanding is commendable, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and empirical validation to convincingly support its claims. Specifically, the theoretical analysis, while detailed, feels disconnected from practical GAN training and the proposed solution, while intuitively appealing, isn’t adequately demonstrated to address the identified issues.

The core argument revolves around perfect discrimination occurring when generator and discriminator supports are low-dimensional or disjoint. The theorems establishing this are mathematically rigorous, but their practical relevance isn't fully explored. The subsequent analysis of cost function behavior (original GAN loss vs. the alternative) is insightful, but the jump to the noise-based solution feels abrupt. The authors claim the noise prevents perfect discrimination and stabilizes training, but this is largely asserted rather than demonstrated with compelling experiments. Figure 1 and 2 are mentioned but not included in the submission, hindering evaluation. The theoretical justification for the effectiveness of adding noise, while presented, is complex and requires more accessible explanation.

The paper acknowledges limitations, specifically the reliance on theoretical assumptions. However, it doesn’t sufficiently address how these assumptions hold in real-world GAN training scenarios. Further, the connection between the theoretical analysis and the practical implications for architecture design or hyperparameter tuning is weak. 

To improve this paper, I suggest: 1) Including the mentioned figures and providing more comprehensive experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed noise injection technique across various GAN architectures and datasets. 2) Providing a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results, perhaps with simplified examples. 3) Clearly articulating the limitations of the analysis and discussing potential avenues for future research. 4) Addressing the potential drawbacks of adding noise, such as its impact on sample quality or training speed.

I encourage the authors to revise and resubmit. The core idea of a theoretical understanding of GAN training is valuable, but the current manuscript requires significant refinement and empirical support to be considered for acceptance.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Can you elaborate on the choice of noise distribution (Gaussian) and its impact on the results?
*   How does the proposed noise injection technique compare to existing GAN stabilization methods (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) in terms of performance and computational cost?
*   What specific architectural choices or hyperparameters are most sensitive to the noise level, and how can these be tuned for optimal performance?
*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the Wasserstein distance relates to the practical evaluation of GANs, and how it can be used to guide training?
