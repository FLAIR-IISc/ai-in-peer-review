

# Review

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental challenges in training generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors tackle the critical problem of why GANs are notoriously difficult to train, with unstable behavior and saturation issues, by providing a rigorous theoretical analysis of the underlying mathematical causes. They demonstrate that the instability arises because the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, leading to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients on the generator's manifold. The paper introduces novel theoretical tools to analyze these phenomena and proposes a principled solution involving adding continuous noise to create meaningful divergences between distributions.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this recommendation are: (1) the paper provides a clear, theoretically grounded explanation for the long-observed instability in GAN training that has been lacking in the literature, and (2) the proposed solution involving noise injection is theoretically well-motivated and has practical implications for future GAN research.

The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and well-executed. The authors prove that when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (as is typical in GANs), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect with zero gradient on the manifolds, explaining both vanishing gradients and instability. Theorem 2.4 and Corollary 2.1 rigorously demonstrate the vanishing gradient phenomenon as the discriminator approaches optimality, while Theorem 2.6 shows that the alternative cost function leads to unstable updates with infinite variance. These results are supported by detailed proofs and careful handling of technical conditions. The authors also make a valuable contribution by introducing the concept of "softer metrics" through noise injection, proving that this approach creates continuous distributions for which meaningful divergences can be computed, and establishing the connection to Wasserstein distance in Theorem 3.3.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building upon and extending previous work on GAN theory (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Theis et al., 2016) while addressing a critical gap in our understanding of GAN training dynamics. The authors clearly situate their work within the existing literature and demonstrate how their theoretical framework resolves longstanding questions about GAN behavior.

For additional improvement, the authors could consider:
1. Providing more intuition for the technical proofs, particularly for Theorems 2.1 and 2.2. While the proofs are rigorous, adding a few sentences explaining the geometric intuition behind why manifolds don't perfectly align under perturbations would help readers grasp the key insights more quickly.
2. Including a brief empirical demonstration of their noise injection approach in practice. While the paper mentions "targeted experiments," a single simple experiment showing that adding noise to the discriminator inputs stabilizes training would strengthen the connection between theory and practice.
3. Clarifying the relationship between their noise-based approach and other recent stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty). This would help situate their contribution within the broader landscape of GAN training improvements.

I have a few questions to clarify the paper's claims:

1. In Theorem 2.6, the authors assume that the noise in the discriminator approximation is a centered Gaussian process. How would the results change if the noise had a different distribution (e.g., Laplace or uniform)? Would the Cauchy distribution result generalize?

2. The paper states that "the generator's backprop term will be through samples on a set of positive measure that the discriminator will care about." Could the authors elaborate on why this is sufficient to prevent adversarial examples, particularly in cases where the generator might learn to produce samples that are very close to the data manifold but still fall outside it?

3. In the discussion of Lemma 1, the authors state that "having an n-dimensional parameterization does absolutely not imply that the image will lie on an n-dimensional manifold." Could the authors provide a simple counterexample to illustrate this point?

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the GAN literature. The authors successfully identify and rigorously analyze the core mathematical reasons behind GAN training instability, and their proposed solution is both theoretically sound and practically promising. The paper's clarity, rigor, and significance to the field make it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR 2017.
