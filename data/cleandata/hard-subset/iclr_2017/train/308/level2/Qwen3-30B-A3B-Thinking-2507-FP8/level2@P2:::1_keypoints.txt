- Paper studies why GAN training is unstable, attributing instability to low-dimensional data manifolds causing perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients.  
- Proves that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds the optimal discriminator is perfect and its gradient vanishes almost everywhere.  
- Shows the commonly used alternative GAN cost optimizes a problematic divergence (inverted KL minus two JSD), explaining mode dropping.  
- Proposes adding noise to inputs to create continuous distributions that yield meaningful gradients and proves theoretical links to the Wasserstein distance.  
- Paper is well-motivated and situates itself clearly within the GAN and generative-modeling literature.  
- Theoretical results are rigorous with solid proofs for the main theorems.  
- Empirical validation is minimal (Figures 1–3) and lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art stabilization methods.  
- Practical significance is unclear because the proposed solution isn’t convincingly demonstrated empirically.  
- Some technical language is imprecise (e.g., conflating continuity with absolute continuity); clarification appears only in the appendix.  
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its noise-based solution from prior practical approaches (e.g., spectral normalization).  
- Connection to Wasserstein distance is interesting but does not provide a clear implementation path to outperform current methods.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Key reasons for rejection: insufficient empirical validation and limited novelty/practical distinction of the proposed solution.  
- Suggested improvements: add thorough empirical comparisons to SOTA GAN stabilization techniques on standard benchmarks.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify the distinction between “continuous” and “absolutely continuous” earlier in the paper.  
- Suggested improvements: provide concrete implementation details for noise addition (noise type, variance schedule).  
- Suggested improvements: explicitly acknowledge and differentiate related prior work that uses input noise or similar stabilization.  
- Questions for authors: how to implement the noise addition in practice, including recommended noise type and variance schedule?  
- Questions for authors: how does the method compare to other stabilization techniques (spectral normalization, gradient penalty) theoretically and empirically?  
- Questions for authors: how should success be measured for this theoretically grounded approach compared to alternative methods?
