

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental instability issues in GAN training. The authors identify that the core problem stems from generator distributions lying on low-dimensional manifolds when the latent space dimension is less than the data space dimension, leading to perfect discriminators with vanishing or unstable gradients. The paper rigorously proves that when data and generator distributions have disjoint supports or lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (with accuracy 1) and has zero gradients almost everywhere on the manifolds. The authors further demonstrate that the original cost function leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves, while the alternative cost function (-log D) produces unstable updates with infinite variance due to the Cauchy distribution of gradients. The paper then proposes a theoretically grounded solution by adding noise to inputs, creating a "softer" metric that avoids these issues. The authors prove that this approach leads to meaningful gradients and connects to the Wasserstein distance, providing a theoretically sound path toward stable GAN training.

The paper should be accepted. The theoretical insights are novel, rigorously proven, and directly address a fundamental question in GAN research. The key reasons for acceptance are the paper's clear identification of the theoretical root cause of GAN instability and the well-motivated, mathematically sound solution that provides practical guidance for future work.

The paper's theoretical contributions are strong and well-supported. The proof that generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1) is particularly important and correctly identifies a crucial property of GANs often overlooked in practice. The analysis of gradient behavior for both cost functions (Theorems 2.4 and 2.6) provides a clear theoretical explanation for the observed instability in GAN training. The connection to Wasserstein distance in Theorem 3.3 is a significant contribution that bridges theoretical understanding with practical implementation. The paper's motivation is excellent, building carefully on prior work in GANs and divergence measures, and it properly addresses the gap in theoretical understanding that has plagued the field.

The paper could be strengthened by including more empirical validation of the proposed noise-based approach. While Figures 2 and 3 provide some experimental support for the theoretical claims, a more comprehensive comparison with existing GAN variants (such as DCGAN, WGAN, or spectral normalization) would better demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method. The authors should also clarify how the noise parameter should be chosen and annealed in practice, as this is crucial for implementation.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the proposed noise-based approach and existing stabilization techniques. While the theoretical justification is strong, a comparison with methods like gradient penalty or spectral normalization would help contextualize the contribution. Additionally, the paper should address whether the noise-based approach preserves the mode coverage properties of GANs, which is a key advantage over maximum likelihood approaches.

Questions for the authors:
1. How does the proposed noise-based approach compare to existing stabilization techniques (e.g., gradient penalty, spectral normalization) in terms of training stability and sample quality across different datasets?
2. What is the recommended schedule for annealing the noise parameter during training, and how does this affect the final model quality?
3. Can the theoretical guarantees provided in Theorem 3.3 be extended to the case where the noise is added to both the generator and discriminator, as suggested in the paper?

The paper provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for understanding GAN training dynamics, and its insights will likely guide future research in the field. The theoretical results are sound, well-motivated, and directly address a fundamental problem in GANs that has been largely empirical up to this point. The proposed solution is both theoretically grounded and practically implementable, making this a valuable contribution to the field.
