

**Review**

This paper addresses a fundamental question in GAN research: why GAN training is unstable and prone to saturation, despite their empirical success in generating realistic images. The authors present a comprehensive theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics, identifying that the instability arises from the low-dimensional manifold structure of data distributions, leading to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients. They prove that under these conditions, both the original and alternative cost functions exhibit problematic behavior, and propose a solution involving adding noise to inputs to create "softer" metrics that enable stable training.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it rigorously establishes that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (a common scenario), the optimal discriminator is perfect and its gradient vanishes almost everywhere, explaining the instability observed in practice. Second, it proves that the alternative cost function (used in most stable GAN implementations) actually optimizes a problematic divergence (inverted KL minus two JSD), explaining why GANs suffer from mode dropping. Finally, it proposes a principled solution by adding noise to inputs, creating continuous distributions that allow for meaningful gradient updates, and proves theoretical connections to Wasserstein distance.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. It begins with a clear explanation of the problem, correctly situating its work within the broader context of generative modeling and GAN literature. The authors cite relevant prior work and clearly identify the gap their paper fills: a lack of theoretical understanding of GAN instability.

However, the paper falls short on empirical validation. The experiments (Figures 1-3) are minimal and lack proper comparison to state-of-the-art stabilization techniques. The paper claims to "perform targeted experiments to verify our assumptions," but the experiments don't convincingly demonstrate the proposed solution's effectiveness or compare it to alternatives. Without more substantial empirical evidence, the practical significance of the theoretical findings remains unclear.

The theoretical contributions are strong, with rigorous proofs for the main theorems. However, some claims could be more nuanced. For instance, the assertion that "the distributions are not continuous" (p. 4) conflates continuity with absolute continuity, which is clarified only in Appendix B. The paper would benefit from more precise language throughout to avoid potential confusion.

The paper's main limitation is the lack of practical demonstration. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, the proposed solution (adding noise to inputs) has been explored in other works (e.g., "Spectral Normalization" by Miyato et al., 2018), and the paper doesn't adequately distinguish its theoretical justification from existing approaches. The connection to Wasserstein distance is interesting but doesn't provide a clear path to implementation that would outperform current methods.

**Decision:** Reject

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. The empirical validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims about the practical effectiveness of the proposed solution.
2. The paper's main contribution (theoretical explanation of GAN instability) is valuable, but the proposed solution lacks novelty in its practical implementation and isn't convincingly validated.

**Additional feedback:**
- The paper would significantly benefit from more thorough empirical validation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art GAN stabilization techniques on standard benchmarks.
- Consider clarifying the distinction between "continuous" and "absolutely continuous" random variables earlier in the paper, as this is a critical technical point that could confuse readers.
- The connection between the theoretical findings and practical implementation could be strengthened by providing specific details about how the noise addition should be implemented in practice (e.g., noise type, variance schedule).
- The paper should more explicitly acknowledge related work that has explored similar ideas (e.g., adding noise to inputs in GANs), while clearly differentiating the theoretical justification provided here.

**Questions for authors:**
1. How would you implement the noise addition in practice? What noise type and variance schedule would you recommend, and how would these choices affect the final results?
2. How does your proposed method compare to other stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance?
3. Given that the paper claims to provide a "theoretically grounded direction" toward solving GAN instability, how would you measure the success of this approach compared to other methods?
