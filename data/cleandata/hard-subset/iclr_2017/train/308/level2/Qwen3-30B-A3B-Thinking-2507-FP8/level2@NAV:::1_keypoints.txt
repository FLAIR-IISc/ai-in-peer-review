- Main contribution: Rigorous proof that when generator and data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect and constant almost everywhere on those manifolds.  
- Consequence: This explains why the original GAN loss yields vanishing gradients and why the -log D alternative causes unstable updates.  
- Solution proposed: A noise-based method that creates “softer” metrics to avoid vanishing/instability, with theoretical guarantees.  
- Empirical validation: Experiments (Figures 1–3) support the theoretical claims about gradient behavior during training.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: Comprehensive theoretical framework, rigorous mathematical proofs, strong experimental verification, and solid positioning within related work.  
- Supporting arguments: Exceptionally thorough analysis, correct identification of low-dimensional manifold as the core issue, and a principled, theoretically sound mitigation strategy.  
- Additional feedback: Paper needs more discussion of practical implementation—how to choose noise parameters and trade-offs between noise variance and sample quality.  
- Questions for authors: How does the noise-based approach compare to gradient-penalty and other recent stability techniques, and how should optimal noise variance be chosen across architectures and datasets?
