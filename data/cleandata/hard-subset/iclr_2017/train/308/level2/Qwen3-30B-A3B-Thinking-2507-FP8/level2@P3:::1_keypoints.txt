- Paper identifies GAN instability arising from generator distributions on low-dimensional manifolds when latent dim < data dim.  
- Proves that disjoint supports or low-dimensional manifolds yield an optimal discriminator with perfect accuracy and zero gradients almost everywhere.  
- Shows the original GAN cost leads to vanishing generator gradients as the discriminator improves.  
- Demonstrates the alternative cost (-log D) gives unstable updates with infinite-variance (Cauchy) gradient distributions.  
- Proposes adding input noise to soften the metric, yielding meaningful gradients and avoiding the pathological behaviors.  
- Proves the noise-based approach connects to the Wasserstein distance, providing theoretical grounding for stability.  
- Strong recommendation to accept based on novel, rigorous theoretical contributions addressing a fundamental GAN problem.  
- Highlights key technical strengths: Lemma 1 (generators on manifolds), Theorems on gradient behavior, and Theorem 3.3 linking to Wasserstein.  
- Motivation and literature grounding are well-built, filling a notable theoretical gap in GAN research.  
- Criticism: empirical validation of the noise-based method is limited; Figures 2–3 are insufficient.  
- Suggests more comprehensive comparisons to existing GAN variants (e.g., DCGAN, WGAN, spectral normalization).  
- Requests clarification on practical choice and annealing schedule for the noise parameter.  
- Recommends discussing relationships to other stabilization methods (gradient penalty, spectral norm) and effects on mode coverage.  
- Asks three concrete questions for authors: comparison with existing stabilizers, recommended noise annealing schedule and its impact, and extension of Theorem 3.3 when noise is added to both generator and discriminator.
