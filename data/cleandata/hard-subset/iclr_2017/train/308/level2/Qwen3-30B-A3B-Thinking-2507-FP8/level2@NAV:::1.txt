

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental causes of instability in GAN training. The authors rigorously prove that when generator and data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical for GANs), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and constant almost everywhere on these manifolds. This theoretical insight explains why vanishing gradients occur with the original cost function and why unstable updates arise with the -log D alternative. The authors then propose a noise-based solution that creates "softer" metrics to avoid these issues, with theoretical guarantees and experimental validation.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons: The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework that addresses a fundamental gap in GAN understanding, and the claims are strongly supported by rigorous mathematical proofs and experimental verification. The work is well-motivated and properly situated within the existing literature.

Supporting arguments: The theoretical analysis is exceptionally thorough, with detailed proofs for all key claims. The authors correctly identify the core issue as the low-dimensional manifold structure of both data and generator distributions, which leads to the problematic behavior of discriminators. The noise-based solution they propose is theoretically sound and provides a principled direction for future work. The experimental results (Figures 1-3) effectively validate the theoretical claims about gradient behavior during training.

Additional feedback: While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on how to select optimal noise parameters for different architectures and datasets, and provide more guidance on the trade-offs between noise variance and sample quality. A comparison with recent stability techniques (such as gradient penalty methods) would also strengthen the paper's position in the current landscape.

Questions for authors: How would your noise-based approach compare to recent GAN stability techniques like gradient penalty methods? What are the practical considerations for choosing the optimal noise variance in different applications, and how might these considerations vary across different datasets and architectures?
