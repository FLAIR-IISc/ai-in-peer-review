- Significant theoretical contribution explaining why GANs are difficult to train.  
- Core problem: data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional, disjoint manifolds leading to perfect discriminators and vanishing gradients.  
- Authors introduce novel theoretical tools and propose adding continuous noise to generate meaningful divergences.  
- Recommendation: accept — reasons: (1) clear, theoretically grounded explanation for GAN instability, (2) noise-injection solution is well-motivated and practically relevant.  
- Theorem 2.4 and Corollary 2.1: prove vanishing generator gradients as discriminator approaches optimality.  
- Theorem 2.6: shows the alternative cost leads to unstable updates with infinite variance.  
- Noise injection / “softer metrics”: produces continuous distributions, enabling meaningful divergences and a connection to Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3).  
- Paper is well-placed in literature, extending prior GAN theory and filling a gap on training dynamics.  
- Suggestion: add more geometric intuition for technical proofs, especially Theorems 2.1 and 2.2.  
- Suggestion: include a brief empirical demonstration that noise injection stabilizes training.  
- Suggestion: clarify relationship between the noise-based approach and other stabilization methods (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty).  
- Question: does Theorem 2.6 generalize if discriminator noise is non-Gaussian (e.g., Laplace or uniform), and does the Cauchy result hold?  
- Question: why is backprop through samples on a positive-measure set sufficient to prevent adversarial examples when generator samples remain near but outside the data manifold?  
- Question: provide a simple counterexample showing an n-dimensional parameterization need not have an n-dimensional image manifold.  
- Overall: rigorous, significant, and practically promising work; strong candidate for ICLR 2017 acceptance.
