- Identifies GAN instability and saturation as arising from data and generator distributions lying on low-dimensional manifolds, producing perfect discriminators and problematic gradients.
- Provides rigorous analysis of why the original GAN loss leads to vanishing gradients and why the -log D alternative yields unstable updates.
- Proposes adding noise to inputs to create a "softer" metric that avoids the problematic behavior.
- Proves that with input noise, gradient updates become stable and meaningful, and connects this approach to the Wasserstein distance.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main rejection reason 1: Lack of sufficient empirical validation—claimed "targeted experiments" are not presented and referenced figures are missing.
- Main rejection reason 2: Theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions (e.g., low-dimensional supports, Lemma 1 about neural nets) that may not hold in practice and aren’t adequately addressed.
- Supporting argument: No comparative experiments showing the noise-based method outperforms standard GAN variants (DCGAN, WGAN) in sample quality, stability, or convergence speed.
- Supporting argument: The claim that data and network outputs lie on low-dimensional manifolds is asserted without empirical evidence.
- Supporting argument: Missing figures (Figure 1–3) referenced in the abstract undermine claimed empirical support.
- Suggested improvement: Add comprehensive empirical results on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) comparing against baseline GAN methods.
- Suggested improvement: Provide practical implementation details for the noise approach (choice of noise distribution, variance, annealing schedule).
- Suggested improvement: Discuss the computational cost and scalability of the noise method relative to standard GAN training.
- Suggested improvement: More thoroughly discuss limitations of the theoretical approach and the realism of its assumptions.
- Question: Provide details of the "targeted experiments"—datasets, architectures, noise parameters, and quantitative results.
- Question: Supply empirical evidence that generator networks map to low-dimensional manifolds for common architectures (DCGAN, ResNet GANs).
- Question: How does adding noise to discriminator inputs scale to high-dimensional image data and what is the computational overhead?
- Question: Provide evidence that the proposed approach eliminates the need for careful training schedules and is robust to hyperparameter choices.
- Question: How would one practically compute or estimate the Wasserstein distance between high-dimensional distributions in real-world GAN applications?
