- Main contribution: rigorous theoretical analysis explaining GAN instability and saturation when data/generator supports are disjoint or lie on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- Key theoretical finding: optimal discriminator becomes perfect (accuracy 1) with zero gradient almost everywhere, causing vanishing gradients for the generator.  
- Analysis of alternative cost (-log D): shows updates can have infinite variance (centered Cauchy), explaining observed unstable training.  
- Proposed solution: add noise to discriminator inputs to create a softer metric that avoids vanishing gradients and instability.  
- Connection to Wasserstein: theoretical link showing noise yields a meaningful distance approximation (Theorem 3.3).  
- Empirical support: experiments (Figures 1–3) illustrate and validate the theoretical claims.  
- Decision: accept with minor revisions due to strong theoretical and experimental contributions.  
- Requested improvements: provide practical guidance on noise choice and annealing schedule.  
- Requested improvements: include empirical comparisons to state-of-the-art stabilization methods (WGAN, spectral normalization, gradient penalties).  
- Requested improvements: expand and clarify the connection to the Wasserstein metric.  
- Requested improvements: include a more detailed comparison with related theoretical work on GAN stability.  
- Questions for authors: how does the noise approach compare theoretically and empirically to other stabilization methods?  
- Questions for authors: what is the effect of the noise parameter σ on sample quality and stability, and can σ be chosen theoretically?  
- Questions for authors: how does the method perform when the real data distribution has low-dimensional structure (e.g., images)?  
- Questions for authors: how does discriminator training behave when the optimal discriminator has zero gradients almost everywhere?
