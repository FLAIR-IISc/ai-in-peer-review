

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental instability issues in GAN training. Rather than proposing new architectures or heuristics, the authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for why GANs are difficult to train, identifying the role of low-dimensional manifolds in the data and generator distributions. They prove theorems demonstrating why gradients vanish and why updates become unstable, and they propose a theoretically grounded solution involving noise injection to create a softer metric for comparison between distributions.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons: The paper provides a well-motivated, theoretically sound explanation for GAN training instability that addresses a fundamental gap in the literature. The theoretical contributions are rigorous, well-supported with proofs, and validated through targeted experiments.

The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and well-executed. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 establish the conditions under which perfect discrimination occurs, while Theorems 2.4-2.6 rigorously explain the vanishing gradients and instability issues observed in practice. The authors successfully connect these theoretical insights to practical solutions through Theorem 3.3 and Corollary 3.2, showing how noise injection creates a continuous metric that enables stable training. The proofs are detailed and appear correct, with Appendix A providing sufficient mathematical rigor. The experimental results (Figures 1-3) effectively support the theoretical claims about gradient behavior and instability.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of the proposed noise injection approach versus standard GAN training. A brief discussion of practical implementation challenges would also strengthen the presentation.

Questions for authors: How does the proposed noise injection approach scale to high-dimensional data (e.g., 256x256 images) compared to standard GAN training? Could you provide more intuition about why the Cauchy distribution appears in Theorem 2.6 and how this relates to practical observations of gradient explosion in GAN training?
