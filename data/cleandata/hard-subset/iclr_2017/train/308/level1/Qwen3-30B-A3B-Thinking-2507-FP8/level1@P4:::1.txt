

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper makes significant theoretical contributions to understanding the instability issues in generative adversarial network (GAN) training. The authors systematically analyze why GAN training is unstable, showing that the supports of both the data and generator distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discriminators that cause vanishing gradients or unstable updates. They prove rigorous theoretical results demonstrating that as the discriminator approaches optimality, the generator gradients either vanish (for the original cost function) or become unstable (for the alternative cost function). The paper then proposes a solution by adding noise to the discriminator inputs, which smooths the distributions and enables stable training with interpretable gradients. The authors establish a relationship between the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions, providing a theoretically grounded approach to GAN training.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

Key reasons: (1) The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability, addressing a fundamental gap in the literature. (2) The theoretical results are well-supported with detailed proofs and experimental verification.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses the core question of why GAN training is unstable. The authors prove that when the supports of the data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical in GANs), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and constant almost everywhere on the supports. This leads to vanishing gradients for the generator in the original cost function (Theorem 2.4), and unstable updates for the alternative cost function (Theorem 2.6). These theoretical results are well-motivated and build upon the existing literature, with clear connections to prior work by Goodfellow et al. and Theis et al.

The paper's theoretical contributions are particularly strong due to the careful mathematical treatment of the problem. The authors provide rigorous proofs for all major claims, with detailed arguments in the appendix. The paper's structure is logical, moving from problem statement to theoretical analysis to proposed solution. The experimental results in Figures 2 and 3 provide empirical validation of the theoretical claims about vanishing and unstable gradients.

The proposed solution of adding noise to the discriminator inputs is theoretically grounded and addresses the core problem identified in the analysis. The authors prove that this approach leads to gradients that move generator samples toward the data manifold (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2), and they establish a relationship between the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3). This provides a principled way to train GANs without the instability issues.

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, the theoretical approach, and the proposed solution. The mathematical arguments are sound, and the paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GAN training dynamics.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. While the paper discusses the relationship between the proposed method and existing stabilization techniques, a more detailed comparison with specific methods (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

2. The paper mentions "a series of open questions and problems" but doesn't explicitly list them. A dedicated section outlining these open questions would help frame the paper's contribution within the broader research landscape.

3. The paper uses a specific norm for the discriminator (‖D‖ = sup_x |D(x)| + ‖∇xD(x)‖_2), which is motivated for simplicity. A brief discussion of how this choice affects the generality of the results would be helpful.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of adding noise to the discriminator inputs, particularly for high-dimensional data like images.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does the computational cost of the proposed noise-based approach scale with image resolution (e.g., from 32x32 to 256x256 images)? Would the noise addition significantly increase training time compared to standard GAN training?

2. In Theorem 3.3, the authors establish a bound involving the Wasserstein distance. How does this bound compare to existing bounds on the Wasserstein distance in the GAN literature, particularly those related to the use of gradient penalties?

3. The paper shows that adding noise leads to a gradient that moves samples toward the data manifold. How does this relate to the concept of mode collapse in GANs, and does the proposed approach help mitigate mode collapse?
