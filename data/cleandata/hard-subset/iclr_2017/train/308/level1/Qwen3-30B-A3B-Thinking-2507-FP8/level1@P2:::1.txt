

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental challenges in GAN training. The authors identify that the instability and saturation problems in GAN training arise from the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discriminators that cause problematic gradient behavior for the generator. The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis to explain why the original GAN cost function leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves, and why the alternative cost function (using -log D) leads to unstable gradient updates. Most importantly, the authors propose a theoretically grounded solution involving adding noise to the inputs, which creates a "softer" metric that avoids the problematic behavior. They prove that with this noise, the gradient updates become stable and meaningful, and they establish connections to the Wasserstein distance as a more appropriate metric for comparing distributions with disjoint supports.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

Key reasons for rejection:
1. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its theoretical claims. While the authors mention "targeted experiments," the paper itself does not present comprehensive empirical results demonstrating that their proposed noise-based approach solves the problems they identify.
2. The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, makes several strong assumptions about the nature of GANs and their distributions that may not hold in practice, and the paper doesn't adequately address these limitations.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's theoretical contributions are mathematically rigorous and well-motivated, but the lack of empirical validation is a critical flaw for a machine learning conference like ICLR. The authors claim to "perform targeted experiments to verify our assumptions, illustrate our claims, and quantify the phenomena," but the paper itself contains no detailed experimental results to support these claims. The abstract mentions "Figure 1," "Figure 2," and "Figure 3" without providing the actual figures or results, which is unusual for a paper claiming to have empirical validation.

The theoretical analysis is strong in its own right, but it makes assumptions that may not hold in practical GAN training scenarios. For example, the paper assumes that the data distribution Pr and generator distribution Pg lie on low-dimensional manifolds, but it doesn't provide evidence that this is universally true for all practical GAN applications. The paper also assumes that neural networks map to low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1), but this is a non-trivial claim that needs empirical validation, not just theoretical justification.

Furthermore, while the authors propose a noise-based solution, they don't provide any comparative experiments showing that this approach outperforms existing GAN training methods (like DCGAN, WGAN, etc.) in terms of sample quality, training stability, or convergence speed. Without empirical evidence, the practical value of their theoretical insights remains speculative.

The paper's theoretical contributions are valuable, but for a conference like ICLR, which values both theoretical and empirical contributions, the lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness that prevents acceptance.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. Adding comprehensive empirical results that validate the theoretical claims. This should include training GANs with the proposed noise-based approach and comparing them against standard GAN training methods on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet).

2. Providing more details about the practical implementation of the noise-based approach, including how to choose the noise parameters (variance, distribution) and how to anneal the noise as training progresses.

3. Discussing the computational cost of the proposed method compared to standard GAN training, as this is an important practical consideration.

4. Including a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the theoretical approach, particularly regarding the assumptions about the data distribution and the neural network mappings.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims to have performed "targeted experiments" but doesn't present any empirical results in the main text. Could you provide specific details about these experiments, including the datasets used, the architectures, the noise parameters, and the quantitative results that support your theoretical claims?

2. The paper assumes that neural networks map to low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1), but this is a strong assumption. Could you provide empirical evidence that this is generally true for GAN generator networks, particularly for the architectures commonly used in practice (e.g., DCGAN, ResNet-based GANs)?

3. The proposed noise-based solution involves adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. How would this approach scale to high-dimensional image data, and what is the computational cost compared to standard GAN training? Would the noise need to be adapted for different image resolutions or datasets?

4. The paper claims that "we no longer have to worry about training schedules" with the proposed approach. However, the paper doesn't provide any evidence of this claim. Could you provide results showing that the proposed method is robust to different training schedules or hyperparameter choices?

5. The paper states that "the Wasserstein metric incorporates the notion of distance between the elements in the support of P and the ones in the support of Q." However, the authors don't explain how this metric can be practically computed or estimated for real-world GAN applications. How would you estimate the Wasserstein distance between two high-dimensional distributions in practice?
