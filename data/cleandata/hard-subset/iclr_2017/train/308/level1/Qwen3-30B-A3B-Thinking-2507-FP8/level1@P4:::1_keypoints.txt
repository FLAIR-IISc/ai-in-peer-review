1. Paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis explaining GAN instability due to data and generator supports lying on low-dimensional manifolds.  
2. Shows optimal discriminator becomes perfect/constant on supports, causing vanishing gradients (original loss) or unstable updates (alternative loss).  
3. Proposes adding noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and produce stable, interpretable generator gradients.  
4. Proves gradients from noisy discriminator move generator samples toward the data manifold (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2).  
5. Establishes a relationship between Wasserstein distance of original distributions and Jensen–Shannon divergence of noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3).  
6. Provides detailed proofs and experimental validation supporting the theoretical claims (Figures 2 and 3).  
7. Paper is well-written, logically structured, and makes a significant theoretical contribution to GAN training dynamics.  
8. Recommendation: Accept — main reasons are rigorous theory and supporting experiments.  
9. Suggest more detailed empirical comparison with other stabilization methods (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty).  
10. Recommend explicitly listing the "open questions and problems" referenced in the paper.  
11. Ask for discussion on how the chosen discriminator norm (sup |D(x)| + ||∇xD(x)||2) affects the generality of results.  
12. Advise addressing computational cost of adding noise to discriminator inputs, especially for high-dimensional image data.  
13. Question: How does the computational cost scale with image resolution (e.g., 32x32 → 256x256) and affect training time?  
14. Question: How does the Theorem 3.3 Wasserstein bound compare to existing Wasserstein bounds, particularly those using gradient penalties?  
15. Question: Does adding noise and the resulting gradients help mitigate mode collapse, and how does this relate to mode collapse mechanisms?
