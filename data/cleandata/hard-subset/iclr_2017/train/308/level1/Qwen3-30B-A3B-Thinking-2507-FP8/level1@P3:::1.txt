

# ICLR 2017 Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics. The authors identify that the core instability in GAN training stems from the fact that both data and generated distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds, making their supports disjoint or nearly disjoint. They prove that under these conditions, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (100% accurate) and its gradient vanishes almost everywhere on the support of the generated distribution. This explains why gradient updates to the generator vanish as the discriminator improves. The paper further demonstrates that the commonly used alternative cost function (-log D) suffers from instability due to the Cauchy-distributed gradients when the discriminator is approximated with noise. Finally, they propose a theoretically grounded solution involving adding noise to inputs to create smoother distributions, and prove that this approach leads to well-behaved gradients that can be optimized using standard GAN training.

## 2. Decision
**Reject**. While the theoretical analysis is strong and insightful, the paper fails to adequately address the relevance of its claims to practical GAN training, and the proposed solution lacks empirical validation against established methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical contributions are novel and well-executed, particularly the proof that when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the Jensen-Shannon divergence is maximized (Theorem 2.3) and that gradients vanish as the discriminator approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4). The connection between low-dimensional manifolds and GAN instability is compelling and fills an important theoretical gap.

However, the paper's critical weakness is its lack of empirical validation against contemporary methods. The paper was submitted in 2016, but by 2017, the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) paper was already published (Arjovsky et al., 2017), which directly addresses the instability issues the authors describe. The paper makes no mention of WGAN or other solutions to GAN instability, which significantly weakens the claim that their approach represents a "new direction" toward solving these problems. Without comparing their noise-based approach to WGAN or other methods, the practical relevance of their theoretical contribution remains unclear.

Furthermore, the paper's claim that "the Jensen-Shannon divergence is maximized" (Theorem 2.3) is technically correct but doesn't fully address the practical optimization challenge. The authors show that the divergence is maximized, but they don't explain why this is a problem for gradient descent optimization in practice. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the divergence being maximized affects practical training.

The experimental validation (Figures 1-3) is insufficient. The paper claims to "perform targeted experiments to verify our assumptions," but the figures lack proper error bars, statistical significance testing, and comparison to baseline methods. The experiments don't demonstrate that the proposed noise-based solution actually improves GAN training quality or stability compared to existing approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would be significantly strengthened by a thorough comparison to existing approaches for stabilizing GAN training, particularly WGAN, which was published in 2017 (just a few months before the ICLR deadline). The authors should explicitly discuss how their noise-based approach relates to WGAN and whether it offers advantages or disadvantages.
- The theoretical analysis assumes the discriminator is trained to optimality, which is not the case in practice. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how the theoretical results translate to practical training scenarios where the discriminator is only approximately optimal.
- The paper claims that "the gradient passed to the generator has the form" (Equation 4), but doesn't provide intuition for why this form is beneficial for training. A more intuitive explanation would help readers understand the practical value of the theoretical results.
- The experimental section should be expanded to include quantitative comparisons of sample quality (e.g., FID scores) between the noise-based approach and standard GAN training.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How does your noise-based approach compare to the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) method in terms of training stability, sample quality, and computational cost? Why would a practitioner choose your method over WGAN?
2. The paper claims that "the Jensen-Shannon divergence is maximized" when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Theorem 2.3), but this is a theoretical property. How does this theoretical property manifest in practical training behavior, and how does it specifically cause the instability you describe?
3. Your proposed solution involves adding noise to inputs, but the paper doesn't specify how much noise should be added or how to anneal it during training. What are the practical considerations for implementing this approach, and how sensitive is it to the noise parameters?
4. The paper makes no mention of the recent WGAN paper (Arjovsky et al., 2017), which addresses the exact instability issues you describe. How does your theoretical analysis relate to the WGAN approach, and what are the key differences between your method and WGAN?
