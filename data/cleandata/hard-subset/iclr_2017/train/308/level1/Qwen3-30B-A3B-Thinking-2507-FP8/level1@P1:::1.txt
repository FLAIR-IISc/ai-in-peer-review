

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental challenges in GAN training dynamics. The authors rigorously analyze why GAN training is unstable and prone to saturation, demonstrating that when data and generator distributions have disjoint supports or lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typically the case for neural network-based generators), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (accuracy 1) with zero gradient almost everywhere. This explains both vanishing gradients in the original GAN formulation and unstable updates with infinite variance in the alternative cost function (-logD). The authors then propose a theoretically grounded solution involving adding noise to discriminator inputs, creating a softer metric that avoids these issues. The paper is well-motivated, theoretically rigorous, and supported by carefully designed experiments that illustrate the key phenomena. The work provides valuable theoretical tools for analyzing GAN training dynamics and opens new research directions for more stable GAN training.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

Key reasons: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a fundamental problem in GAN training that has been widely observed but not well understood. The theoretical results are well-supported with proofs and experimental validation, making a strong contribution to the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial and well-executed. The authors prove that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (as is almost always the case for neural network-based generators), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere. This explains the vanishing gradients phenomenon observed in practice when the discriminator gets too good. The proof of Theorem 2.4 is particularly compelling, showing that as the discriminator approximation gets better, the generator gradient vanishes.

The paper also provides a clear analysis of the alternative cost function (-logD), demonstrating that it leads to unstable updates with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6). This explains a well-known practical issue that has been observed in GAN training but not theoretically justified. The proof of Theorem 2.6 is technically sound, showing that the gradient updates become centered Cauchy random variables with infinite variance.

The proposed solution of adding noise to the discriminator inputs is theoretically sound and well-motivated. The authors show that this noise creates a softer metric that avoids the issues of vanishing gradients and instability. The connection to the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3) is particularly valuable, as it provides a theoretical foundation for using noise to approximate meaningful distances between distributions.

The paper's experimental validation (Figures 1-3) is well-designed to illustrate the key theoretical claims, providing concrete evidence that supports the theoretical analysis. The authors have carefully situated their work within the broader literature on GANs, variational methods, and generative modeling.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations, particularly regarding the choice of noise distribution and the annealing schedule for the noise parameter. While the paper mentions annealing, it would be helpful to provide more concrete guidance for practitioners.

- The authors should provide more empirical evidence comparing the noise-based approach to state-of-the-art GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN, spectral normalization) to demonstrate its practical value beyond the theoretical analysis.

- The connection to the Wasserstein metric could be expanded. While the paper mentions the relationship, it would strengthen the argument to explicitly show how the noise-based approach approximates the Wasserstein distance.

- A more detailed comparison with related theoretical work on GAN stability would help situate the contribution more precisely within the literature.

## 5. Questions for the Authors
1. How does the noise-based approach compare to other recent methods for stabilizing GAN training (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty, etc.) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance?

2. What is the impact of the noise parameter σ on the quality of generated samples and the stability of training? Is there a theoretical way to determine the optimal σ, or does it require empirical tuning?

3. The paper focuses on the case where the generator's support lies on a low-dimensional manifold, but how does the approach handle cases where the data distribution itself has a low-dimensional structure (e.g., natural images)? Does it still provide meaningful gradients in these cases?

4. The paper mentions that the optimal discriminator has zero gradient almost everywhere, but doesn't discuss how this affects the training dynamics of the discriminator itself. How does the discriminator training behave when the optimal discriminator has zero gradients almost everywhere?
