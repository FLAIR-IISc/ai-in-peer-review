- Paper aims to theoretically explain GAN training instability and saturation, attributing problems to disjoint or low-dimensional supports of generator/discriminator distributions.  
- Identifies that perfect discrimination by the discriminator yields vanishing gradients for the generator.  
- Proposes using noisy distributions (Gaussian noise) to mitigate these issues and introduces a Wasserstein-based metric for evaluation.  
- Overall recommendation: Reject — theoretical claims insufficiently substantiated and proposed solution lacks empirical support; writing is dense and hard to follow.  
- Theorems on perfect discrimination are insightful but the jump to noise injection is underdeveloped and poorly justified.  
- Justification for Gaussian noise and the detailed gradient analysis (Theorems 3.2/3.3) are complex and need simplification and clearer explanation.  
- Analysis of original vs. alternative cost functions is valuable; connection to Wasserstein distance is promising but practical benefits over WGAN are not demonstrated.  
- Provided figures give limited evidence and lack comparisons to other training scenarios or regularization techniques.  
- Major empirical shortcoming: no comprehensive experiments on standard GAN benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) or comparisons to established methods.  
- Paper structure issues: overly long introduction, dense proofs requiring measure-theory/differential-geometry background, and excessive technical jargon.  
- Recommended improvements: rewrite for clarity, add extensive benchmark experiments and comparisons, discuss limitations (cost, noise sensitivity), simplify theory/notation, and relate to existing stabilization techniques.  
- Key questions for authors: advantages over WGAN; sensitivity to noise type/variance and parameter selection; intuitive explanation of Theorem 3.2; evidence that noise injection mitigates adversarial examples; and clearer, more accessible proofs.
