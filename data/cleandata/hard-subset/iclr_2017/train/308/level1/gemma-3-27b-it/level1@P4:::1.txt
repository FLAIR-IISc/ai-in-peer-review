This paper attempts to provide a theoretical understanding of the difficulties in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), focusing on instability and saturation issues. The authors identify the problem with disjoint or low-dimensional supports of the generator and discriminator distributions, leading to perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients. They then propose a novel approach using noisy distributions to mitigate these problems and introduce a new metric based on Wasserstein distance for evaluating GAN performance.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies important issues in GAN training and attempts to provide a theoretical grounding, the theoretical claims are not fully substantiated and the proposed solution lacks sufficient empirical evidence. The writing is also dense and difficult to follow, hindering a clear understanding of the contributions.

The paper correctly points out the limitations of directly minimizing divergences like KL or JSD in the context of GANs due to potential mode dropping and the impact of disjoint supports. The theorems concerning perfect discrimination are insightful, demonstrating how an optimal discriminator can lead to vanishing gradients. However, the leap from these theoretical observations to the proposed solution involving adding noise feels underdeveloped. The justification for the choice of Gaussian noise and the detailed analysis of the resulting gradient behavior (Theorems 3.2 and 3.3) are complex and require more simplification and explanation. 

The analysis of the original and alternative cost functions is valuable, clearly demonstrating the instability issues arising from the latter. The connection drawn to Wasserstein distance is promising, offering a potential path for more stable training. However, the practical implications and advantages of this approach over existing methods (e.g., Wasserstein GAN) are not sufficiently demonstrated. The provided figures (1, 2, 3) offer limited evidence supporting the claims. Figure 1 simply illustrates the discriminator error going to zero, which is already known. Figure 2 shows vanishing gradients, but lacks a clear comparison to other training scenarios or regularization techniques. Figure 3 demonstrates the growth of the gradient norm, but doesn’t definitively link it to the theoretical analysis presented. The lack of comprehensive experimental results on standard GAN benchmarks is a significant drawback. 

The paper’s structure is also problematic. The introduction is excessively lengthy and lacks a concise statement of the core contributions. The proofs, while mathematically detailed, are difficult to follow without a strong background in measure theory and differential geometry. The extensive use of technical jargon without adequate explanation further complicates the reading experience.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity & Structure:** The paper needs a significant rewrite to improve clarity and organization. The introduction should be more focused, and the theoretical sections should be broken down into smaller, more digestible parts.
*   **Empirical Validation:** More extensive experiments on standard GAN datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) are crucial to validate the proposed approach. Comparisons with existing GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN, improved training techniques) are necessary to demonstrate its effectiveness.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The authors should address the limitations of their approach, such as the computational cost of using noisy distributions and the sensitivity to the choice of noise level.
*   **Simplification of Theory:** The theoretical sections could be made more accessible by providing more intuition and simplifying the mathematical notation.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** Explicitly discuss the relationship between the proposed approach and existing literature on GAN training stabilization, such as gradient clipping, spectral normalization, and regularization techniques.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific advantages does the proposed approach offer over existing GAN training techniques like WGAN, which also utilizes a Wasserstein distance-based objective?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the proposed method to the choice of the noise distribution and its variance? What guidelines can you provide for selecting appropriate parameters?
3.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 3.2, clarifying how the proposed gradient update addresses the instability issues identified in the earlier sections?
4.  The paper mentions the potential for adversarial examples. Could you elaborate on how the proposed noise injection mitigates this risk and provide experimental evidence to support this claim?
5.  The proofs are complex and rely heavily on measure theory. Could you provide more intermediate steps or explanations to make them more accessible to a wider audience?
