This paper tackles the challenging problem of understanding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). It aims to provide a theoretical foundation for the instability and saturation issues commonly observed during GAN training, and proposes a new direction towards more stable training based on these insights. The paper is ambitious and attempts to go beyond heuristic approaches by offering rigorous mathematical analysis supported by targeted experiments.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical exploration of GAN training, its lack of clarity, the absence of concrete experimental validation beyond simple figures, and the highly theoretical nature of the proposed solutions make it unsuitable for ICLR 2017. The claims are difficult to fully assess due to the complexity of the mathematics and the limited empirical evidence.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Empirical Rigor:** The experiments presented are minimal and lack the depth required to convincingly support the theoretical claims. Figure 1 shows discriminator error converging to 0, but lacks details on the specific architectures, datasets, or hyperparameters used. Figure 2 demonstrates vanishing gradients but feels more like an observation than a rigorous test of the presented theorems. Figure 3 shows gradient norm growth, but lacks any quantitative analysis or baseline comparisons. The experimental section feels incomplete and fails to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach. It’s unclear whether the proposed modifications lead to improved sample quality or training stability in any meaningful way.

2. **Mathematical Complexity & Clarity:** The paper is densely packed with mathematical notation and theoretical arguments. While rigor is appreciated, the presentation often lacks clarity, making it difficult for the reader to follow the reasoning and understand the implications of the results. Key definitions (e.g., transversality, perfect alignment) are introduced but not always explained in an intuitive manner. The numerous theorems and lemmas, while potentially insightful, are presented without sufficient motivation or discussion of their relevance to the broader problem of GAN training. The leap from theoretical analysis to the proposed solution feels abrupt.

3. **Unclear Practical Implications:** The proposed solution involving adding continuous noise and utilizing the Wasserstein metric, while theoretically grounded, lacks a clear implementation guide or practical considerations. The paper doesn’t address how the variance of the noise should be chosen, how the Wasserstein distance is estimated in practice, or how this approach compares to existing GAN training techniques. It is unclear how a practitioner would implement these ideas in a real-world setting.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Strengthen Empirical Validation:** Conduct more comprehensive experiments with diverse datasets, architectures, and hyperparameters. Include quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) to evaluate the quality of generated samples. Compare the proposed approach to state-of-the-art GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN, Spectral Normalization).
*   **Improve Clarity and Motivation:** Re-write sections with a focus on clarity and intuitive explanation. Provide more motivation for each theorem and lemma, and explain its connection to the core problem of GAN training. Use simpler notation where possible and provide illustrative examples.
*   **Provide Implementation Details:** Offer a practical guide for implementing the proposed solution, including recommendations for choosing hyperparameters and estimating the Wasserstein distance. Discuss the computational cost and scalability of the approach.
*   **Address Limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the theoretical analysis and the assumptions made. Discuss potential areas for future research.
*   **Clarify the connection to existing work:** Expand on the discussion of related work, particularly in the context of alternative divergence metrics and regularization techniques.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the variance of the added noise should be chosen and how it impacts the performance of the proposed method?
2.  What are the computational challenges associated with estimating the Wasserstein distance in high-dimensional spaces, and how can these challenges be addressed in practice?
3.  Can you elaborate on how the proposed approach compares to existing techniques like WGAN in terms of stability, sample quality, and computational cost?
4.  The theoretical proofs rely on assumptions about the smoothness and continuity of the distributions. How sensitive are the results to violations of these assumptions in real-world GAN training scenarios?
5.  In Theorem 2.6, the noise is modeled as a Gaussian process. How does the performance change if other noise distributions are considered?
