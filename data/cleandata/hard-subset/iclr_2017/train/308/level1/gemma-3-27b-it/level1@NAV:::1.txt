## Review of "Towards Understanding the Training Dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks"

This paper tackles a fundamental problem in the field of GANs: the lack of theoretical understanding behind their notoriously unstable training dynamics. The authors aim to move beyond heuristic stabilization techniques by providing a rigorous theoretical analysis of the issues of instability and saturation, and proposing a theoretically grounded direction for improvement. This is a very ambitious and important goal, as progress in GANs is often hampered by empirical trial-and-error.

I **reject** the paper in its current form. While the problem addressed is crucial and the attempt to provide a theoretical foundation is commendable, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and empirical validation to support its complex theoretical claims. Specifically, the theoretical arguments, while mathematically presented, are difficult to follow and their connection to practical GAN training is not convincingly demonstrated. 

The paper meticulously dissects the potential sources of instability by analyzing the behavior of the discriminator and generator gradients under different conditions. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2, establishing the existence of "perfect discriminators" when distributions have low-dimensional supports, are interesting starting points. However, the subsequent claims regarding vanishing gradients and unstable updates, while intuitively plausible, rely heavily on assumptions (like the noise being a centered Gaussian process) that are not thoroughly justified in the context of real-world GAN training. The connection between these theoretical results and the observed instability in practice is asserted rather than convincingly demonstrated. The proposed solution using noise addition and the Wasserstein metric appears promising, but its practical benefits are not shown with sufficiently compelling experiments. Figure 1 and 2 are not enough to demonstrate the points made in the paper, given the complexity of the theory. 

To improve this paper, I recommend the following:

*   **Simplify the Theoretical Presentation:** The mathematical notation is dense and the flow of arguments is often challenging. Providing more intuitive explanations and concrete examples would greatly enhance readability.
*   **Strengthen Empirical Validation:** The authors claim the theory explains observed phenomena. Robust experiments, including tests with various GAN architectures and datasets, are needed to validate these claims. Quantify the gradient behavior and stability with and without the proposed noise addition.
*   **Address the Assumption of Gaussian Noise:** The reliance on a Gaussian noise process for the discriminator seems strong. Investigate the robustness of the results to different noise distributions and potential violations of this assumption.
*   **Clarify the Practical Implications:** How should practitioners implement the proposed approach (noise addition, Wasserstein metric)? What guidelines can be given for choosing the appropriate level of noise?

**Questions for the authors:**

*   The paper repeatedly refers to "measure 0" sets. Could you provide a more accessible explanation of what this means in the context of GAN training, and why it’s problematic?
*   How sensitive are the theoretical results to deviations from the idealized conditions assumed (e.g., perfect discriminator, Gaussian noise)?
*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for choosing the Gaussian noise distribution? What are the implications of using other noise distributions?
*   What specific implementations of the proposed Wasserstein-based approach were tested, and what were the corresponding hyperparameter settings?
