- Paper provides a theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics, focusing on vanishing gradients, instability, and discriminator behavior.
- Strength: rigorous mathematical exploration and detailed proofs, notably the “perfect discrimination” theorem.
- Main conclusion: optimal discriminator quickly becomes too good, causing vanishing gradients or unstable updates.
- Proposed remedy: inject noise and use the Wasserstein metric to stabilize training.
- Overall assessment: Reject — analysis impressive but paper feels incomplete and overly theoretical.
- Weak empirical support: only initial motivating figures; lacks experiments validating the proposed method.
- Implementation gaps: no concrete details on how to inject noise or compute/use the Wasserstein metric in practice.
- Comparative evaluation missing: no comparisons against existing stabilization techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, SN-GAN, feature matching).
- Abrupt theory-to-solution transition: the jump from proofs to the proposed fix is insufficiently justified.
- Questionable assumptions: reliance on perfectly aligned manifolds and continuous densities seems unrealistic in high-dimensional or discrete-data settings.
- Heavy dependence on Lemma 2 (perturbations lead to transversal intersections) is a strong claim that needs more nuance.
- Role of noise unclear: not framed explicitly as regularization nor mechanistically explained how it prevents perfect discrimination.
- Gaussian noise assumption: the focus on Gaussian noise lacks analysis of other noise distributions or sensitivity to noise choice.
- Practical considerations missing: behavior with limited or noisy real data and computational costs of Wasserstein calculations are not discussed.
- Literature connection weak: needs clearer positioning relative to prior theoretical GAN work (non-convexity, curse of dimensionality).
- Suggested improvement: add extensive empirical validation on standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10, CelebA) and ablations.
- Suggested improvement: provide implementation details (noise type, variance selection, computational overhead).
- Suggested improvement: compare empirically to established stabilization methods and regularizers.
- Question for authors: can you provide empirical results on benchmarks comparing your method to existing techniques?
- Question for authors: which noise type and variance work best, and how are they chosen in practice?
- Question for authors: what are the computational costs of calculating or approximating the Wasserstein distance?
- Question for authors: how does the method perform with limited or noisy data?
- Question for authors: how does the analysis change for discrete data distributions?
- Question for authors: can you quantify the likelihood of manifold alignments in high-dimensional GAN settings?
- Question for authors: how does input noise differ from or relate to other GAN regularization techniques?
