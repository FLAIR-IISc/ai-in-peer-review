- Paper addresses a fundamental GAN problem and aims for rigorous theoretical analysis of instability and saturation.  
- Reviewer recommends rejection in current form due to insufficient clarity and empirical support.  
- Theoretical arguments are mathematically dense and hard to follow, with weak connection to practical GAN training.  
- Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 (existence of “perfect discriminators” for low-dimensional supports) are interesting but insufficient.  
- Claims about vanishing gradients and unstable updates rely on strong assumptions (e.g., centered Gaussian noise) that aren’t well-justified.  
- The link between the theoretical results and observed practical instability is asserted rather than convincingly demonstrated.  
- Proposed remedy (noise addition + Wasserstein metric) is promising but lacks compelling experimental validation.  
- Figures 1 and 2 are inadequate to substantiate the complex theoretical claims.  
- Recommendation: simplify theoretical presentation with more intuition and concrete examples.  
- Recommendation: strengthen empirical validation across architectures/datasets and quantify gradient behavior with/without noise.  
- Recommendation: investigate robustness to different noise distributions and relax the Gaussian assumption.  
- Recommendation: clarify practical implementation guidance (how to add noise, choose noise level, use Wasserstein metric).  
- Questions for authors: explain “measure 0” in accessible terms and why it’s problematic for GAN training.  
- Questions for authors: how sensitive are results to deviations from idealized conditions (perfect discriminator, Gaussian noise)?  
- Questions for authors: justify Gaussian noise choice and implications of other noise types.  
- Questions for authors: specify which Wasserstein implementations and hyperparameter settings were tested.
