- Well-motivated work with clear placement in and review of related literature.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow with useful intuitions.  
- Main theoretical result: when real and generated supports are disjoint or lie on low-dimensional manifolds, a perfect discriminator exists with accuracy 1.  
- Consequence: the perfect discriminator has gradients zero almost everywhere, causing vanishing gradients and unstable generator updates.  
- Proposed solution: add continuous noise to discriminator inputs to stabilize training and produce smooth gradients.  
- Analysis includes use of the Wasserstein distance as a tool to evaluate real vs. generated distributions.  
- Paper provides significant theoretical insights that guide GAN architecture and training method development.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance based on the clarity, significance, and novelty of contributions.  
- Minor suggestion: provide more experimental evaluation details and empirical evidence for the proposed approach.  
- Minor suggestion: discuss limitations, potential drawbacks, and risks of adding noise to discriminator inputs and how to mitigate them.  
- Questions for authors: provide experimental setup/results, detail limitations and mitigation strategies for input noise, and clarify the relationship between Wasserstein distance and GAN performance.
