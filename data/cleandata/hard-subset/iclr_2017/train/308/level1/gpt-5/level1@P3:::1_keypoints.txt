- Provides a precise explanation for “perfect discrimination” when real and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, including when the discriminator is constant a.e. and gives no reliable gradient.  
- Proves a vanishing-gradient result for the original GAN generator loss when the discriminator approximates optimality on manifold-like supports (Theorem 2.4).  
- Shows instability for the non-saturating loss: noisy discriminator approximations induce heavy-tailed (Cauchy) generator updates with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6).  
- Derives that the non-saturating generator update with an optimal discriminator equals (locally) the gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr), explaining good sample quality but mode collapse (Theorem 2.5).  
- Proposes instance noise (injecting noise into real and generated samples) to make convolved distributions absolutely continuous and stabilise training.  
- Shows with Gaussian noise the generator gradient becomes a smooth, distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from the model manifold (Theorem 3.2).  
- Proves backpropagating through noisy samples aligns with minimizing JSD between smoothed distributions (Corollary 3.2).  
- Gives a bound linking Wasserstein distance between original distributions and JSD of their smoothed versions plus a noise-variance term, justifying annealing noise and training D to optimality (Theorem 3.3).  
- Overall: coherent theoretical narrative plus a simple, theoretically grounded stabilization technique (instance noise with annealing).  
- Decision: clear accept — strong theoretical analysis and a practical, principled method.  
- Problem significance: advances understanding of GAN instability and saturation by proving perfect discrimination on manifolds and linking support mismatch to JSD saturation and gradient pathologies.  
- Rigor and correctness: manifold results use standard tools and are appropriate; vanishing-gradient and noise-smoothing results are clean and well motivated.  
- Practical implications: justifies training D near optimality after adding input noise and provides an intuitive attraction–repulsion gradient structure.  
- Novelty: provides theoretical basis for input noise, gradient-structure analysis, Wasserstein-bound convergence argument, and a heavy-tail insight for non-saturating loss.  
- Suggest relaxing or empirically justifying strong white-noise assumptions in Theorem 2.6 (correlated Gaussian processes or evidence of heavy tails).  
- Clarify scope of Theorem 2.5: explain effects when D is approximate and co-evolving with θ in practice.  
- Comment on Lemma 1’s “countable union of manifolds” claim for modern architectural components (max-pooling, batch norm, strided convs).  
- Empirics needed: toy 2D experiments showing vanishing gradients, unstable non-saturating updates, and stabilization via instance noise with annealing.  
- Empirics needed: image experiments comparing stability and mode coverage to baselines using metrics (e.g., Inception Score, coverage on synthetic mixtures).  
- Empirics needed: ablation on noise schedule and variance selection with practical annealing guidance.  
- Related work: situate instance-noise relative to f-GANs, MMD/IPM GANs, WGAN/gradient penalty, and earlier input-noise uses; clarify similarities/differences given the Wasserstein bound.

- Question: does adding noise harm sample sharpness and is there a trade-off between early stability and final fidelity mitigated by annealing?
