- Formalized that when Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional, misaligned manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant on both supports.
- Proved that this constant optimal discriminator yields vanishing gradients for the original saturating generator loss as D approaches optimality.
- Showed the non-saturating loss at the true D corresponds to gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) minus 2·JSD(Pr‖Pg), explaining mode dropping and instability.
- Demonstrated that noisy approximations to D can produce Cauchy-like heavy-tailed generator gradients (under independence assumptions), aligning with observed instability.
- Proposed injecting noise into inputs/outputs so smoothed distributions are absolutely continuous, making D’s gradients informative and stable.
- Proved these smoothed gradients push generated samples toward the data manifold while repelling overly likely generator regions.
- Related smoothing to Wasserstein distance with a bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+,Pg+), motivating annealed-noise training.
- Introduced useful analytical tools (manifold arguments, transversality, optimal transport links) for future GAN analysis.
- Decision: accept.
- Main reason: provides rigorous, impactful explanations of GAN pathologies tied to geometric and measure-theoretic properties.
- Main reason: offers a principled remedy (smoothing via noise) with a provable link to Wasserstein distance and practical guidance beyond ad hoc tricks.
- Problem significance: unifies explanations for mode collapse, vanishing gradients, and training fragility in a central GAN question.
- Soundness: Theorems 2.1–2.5 rigorously capture the “perfect discriminator on manifolds” intuition and explain non-saturating loss behavior.
- Instability analysis: Theorem 2.6 predicts heavy-tailed, high-variance updates but relies on strong independence assumptions, acknowledged by authors.
- Smoothing argument: shows noise makes distributions absolutely continuous and gradients measure distance-to-manifold weighted by probability; backprop through generator noise optimizes JSD of smoothed distributions.
- Wasserstein link: the presented bound, despite loose constants and strong assumptions, gives principled justification for annealed smoothing.
- Literature positioning: well-situated relative to Goodfellow, Theis, and optimal transport work and anticipates Wasserstein-based shifts.
