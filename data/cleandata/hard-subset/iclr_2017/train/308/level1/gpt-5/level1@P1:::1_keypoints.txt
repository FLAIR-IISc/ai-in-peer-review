- Paper formalizes that real and generated data lie on low-dimensional manifolds and proves generator outputs have measure zero in data space (Lemma 1).  
- When supports are disjoint or on different low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect and flat, making the JS game degenerate (Theorems 2.1–2.3).  
- With an approximately optimal discriminator, the generator gradient vanishes for the saturating GAN loss (Theorem 2.4).  
- The commonly used non‑saturating loss (−log D) can produce highly unstable updates with heavy (Cauchy) tails and infinite variance under a stylized noise model (Theorem 2.6).  
- Proposed remedy: add continuous instance noise to real and generated samples and backpropagate through noise on the generator to smooth distributions and obtain meaningful gradients (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2).  
- Smoothing via noise makes Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous, prevents perfect discrimination, and yields interpretable attraction/repulsion gradient terms.  
- The paper relates smoothing to Wasserstein distance with a bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2 Var(ε)1/2 + 2C sqrt(JSD(Pr+‖Pg+)), motivating annealing noise while optimizing JSD on smoothed distributions (Theorem 3.3).  
- Targeted experiments support vanishing gradients and rising variance as the discriminator improves.  
- Overall contribution: clarifies GAN pathologies and offers a simple, theoretically justified stabilization (add+anneal noise; train D near-optimal; backprop through noise).  
- Decision: weak accept — strong theory and practical prescription, but some idealized assumptions and limited empirical validation.  
- Paper addresses three central questions: vanishing gradients as D improves, instability of −log D, and which divergence is being optimized.  
- Manifold/measure-zero results are plausible and technically careful, using transversality and Sard’s lemma.  
- Vanishing gradient bound is reasonable under stated norms and bounded Jacobian assumptions.  
- Identification of −log D update as gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr) is insightful and explains mode‑dropping bias.  
- Instability result highlights heavy-tailed updates but depends on strong white‑noise independence assumptions that are unrealistic.  
- Smoothing analysis is clear and yields interpretable local gradient behavior modulated by Pr+/Pg+.  
- Wasserstein connection is neat but relies on restrictive bounded-support assumptions and constants.  
- Empirical support is limited; more extensive experiments would strengthen claims though not strictly required for a theory paper.  
- Suggestion: relax Theorem 2.6 to more realistic correlated/bounded‑variance error models showing high variance or bias.  
- Suggestion: include experiments adding and annealing instance noise on standard image datasets and report Inception/FID/mode coverage.  
- Suggestion: explicitly position “add noise” relative to prior instance-noise/input-noise work and compare to WGAN and f‑GANs.  
- Suggestion: provide practical guidance for choosing initial noise scale, annealing schedule, and covariance structure.  
- Suggestion: clarify scope of manifold and compactness assumptions and when theorems may fail.  
- Suggestion: fix notation, include all referenced figures, and define constants/norms at first use.  
- Questions for authors: can Theorem 2.6 be relaxed to correlated error models and do heavy tails persist?  
- Questions for authors: how to set/anneal noise variance in practice; any principled schedules?  
- Questions for authors: does adding noise consistently reduce mode dropping (quantitative evidence)?  
- Questions for authors: how does smoothing compare to WGAN in stability and diversity; can they be combined?  
- Questions for authors: can backprop through generator noise cause collapse or blur, and how to avoid it?  
- Questions for authors: can the Wasserstein bound be tightened or extended beyond bounded-support assumptions?
