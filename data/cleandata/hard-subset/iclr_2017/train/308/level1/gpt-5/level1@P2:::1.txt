1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper tackles a central open question in GANs: why discriminator improvements often degrade generator updates, leading to vanishing gradients or instability, and what principled objectives can avoid these pathologies. The authors (a) formalize that when Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional manifolds that don’t perfectly align (typical in GANs with lower-dimensional latent codes), the optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant on both supports; (b) show rigorously that this produces vanishing gradients for the original saturating generator loss as D approaches optimality; (c) identify that the popular “non-saturating” loss corresponds (at the true D) to the gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) minus 2·JSD(Pr‖Pg), explaining mode dropping and instability; (d) show that noisy approximations to D can yield generator gradients with Cauchy-like heavy tails (under independence assumptions), aligning with observed instability; (e) propose injecting noise into inputs (and generator outputs) so that the smoothed distributions become absolutely continuous, making D’s gradients informative and stable; (f) show these gradients move generated samples toward the data manifold while repelling overly likely generator regions; and (g) connect this to the Wasserstein distance by bounding W(Pr,Pg) via the variance of the injected noise and the JSD between the smoothed distributions, suggesting an annealed-noise training strategy that is theoretically grounded. The paper also introduces useful tools (manifold arguments, transversality, and links to optimal transport) that structure future analysis.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Theoretical clarity and impact: The paper gives a compelling, rigorous account of multiple well-known but poorly understood GAN pathologies and ties them to precise geometric and measure-theoretic properties.
- Principled remedy and direction: Smoothing via noise, with a provable link to Wasserstein distance, offers a clear research path beyond ad hoc stabilization tricks and explains why distance-like objectives are advantageous.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Understanding GAN training dynamics and instability is central to the field; the arguments here explain common empirical observations (mode collapse, vanishing gradients, training fragility) in a unified framework.
- Soundness of key results: Theorems 2.1–2.4 make precise the “perfect discriminator on manifolds” intuition and its implication (vanishing generator gradients) for the saturating loss. The analysis of the non-saturating loss (Theorem 2.5) is insightful: it exposes an implicit objective that penalizes “fake-looking” samples more than mode dropping, consistent with empirical behavior.
- Instability analysis: The Cauchy-distributed updates (Theorem 2.6) rely on strong independence assumptions (white noise and independence between numerator and denominator), which are not realistic. Still, the conclusion—heavy-tailed, high-variance, poorly directed updates—matches practice, and the paper is transparent about these assumptions and complements them with empirical evidence.
- Smoothing argument: The smoothing theorems clarify how adding noise yields absolutely continuous smoothed distributions, and how gradients then become distances-to-manifold weighted by probability. The corollary showing that backprop through generator noise leads to optimizing JSD of smoothed distributions is important for avoiding measure-zero adversarial effects.
- Wasserstein link: The bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) is valuable. It justifies minimizing JSD between smoothed distributions while annealing noise to control Wasserstein distance between the original distributions. Although constants are loose and the ball-diameter assumption is strong, the direction is principled and prescient.
- Positioning in literature: The work is well placed relative to Goodfellow et al., Theis et al., and foundational optimal transport references. It also anticipates the shift toward Wasserstein-based objectives.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect my decision)
- Assumptions and generality:
  - Clarify the scope of Lemma 1 for common architectures (e.g., inclusion of batchnorm, residual connections) and typical latent dimensionalities; discuss exceptions (e.g., injective generators with dim Z ≥ dim X).
  - The independence assumptions in Theorem 2.6 are strong; consider relaxing them (e.g., to correlated Gaussian processes with Lipschitz structure) or provide empirical ablations showing that heavy tails persist under more realistic approximation errors.
  - State compactness/boundedness assumptions explicitly when used (e.g., Theorem 2.1, Theorem 3.3 with diameter C).
- Empirical validation:
  - The targeted experiments are helpful but limited; more comprehensive studies comparing the proposed noisy training (and annealing schedules) against common baselines (DCGAN tricks, feature matching, WGAN) on standard datasets would sharpen the practical value.
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for the noise distribution (Gaussian vs. other heavy/light tails), magnitude, and annealing schedule; relate these to the constants in the Wasserstein bound.
- Objective insight:
  - For Theorem 2.5, clarify the local nature of the identity (gradient at θ0 with fixed D*) and discuss implications when D is learned jointly and imperfectly.
  - Discuss how smoothing interacts with discriminator capacity; e.g., does smoothing reduce the need for very high-capacity D?
- Presentation:
  - Fix numerous typos and improve notation consistency; some footnote markers and references are garbled.
  - Briefly connect to contemporaneous work on Wasserstein GANs to situate similarities/differences in objectives and guarantees.

Questions for the authors
- How robust are the smoothing-based updates to realistic discriminator approximation errors (non-white, correlated, finite-capacity)? Can you provide empirical evidence of reduced gradient variance/heavy tails under smoothing?
- What annealing schedules for noise variance work best in practice, and how sensitive are results to schedule choices? Can you relate empirical convergence to the √V and √JSD terms in Theorem 3.3?
- Does smoothing the generator inputs/outputs introduce bias that slows convergence or hurts sample quality at low noise? How do you trade off bias vs. variance across training?
- Can you provide experiments or theory for discrete data (text) where smoothing may be less straightforward?
- How does your approach compare empirically and conceptually to enforcing Lipschitz constraints (e.g., gradient penalties) that are motivated by Wasserstein objectives?
- In Theorem 2.5, have you observed empirically the predicted push toward KL(Pg‖Pr) behavior (e.g., pronounced mode dropping) when using the non-saturating loss with strong discriminators? Can you quantify it?
