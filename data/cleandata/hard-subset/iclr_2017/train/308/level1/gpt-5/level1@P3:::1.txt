1. Summary and contributions
The paper tackles fundamental questions about why GAN training is unstable and what principled remedies might look like. The main claims and contributions are:
- A precise explanation for “perfect discrimination” when the real and generator distributions live on low-dimensional manifolds (or have disjoint support), including conditions under which the optimal discriminator is constant almost everywhere on the supports and thus provides either no or highly unreliable gradient signal to the generator.
- A vanishing-gradient result for the original GAN generator loss when the discriminator approximates the optimal one and supports are manifold-like (Theorem 2.4), and a complementary instability result for the non-saturating loss showing that noisy discriminator approximations induce heavy-tailed (Cauchy) updates with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6).
- A derivation that the non-saturating generator update with an optimal discriminator corresponds (locally at θ0) to the gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr) (Theorem 2.5), helping to rationalize good sample quality but mode dropping.
- A principled remedy: inject noise to both real and generated samples (instance noise), making the convolved distributions absolutely continuous. The paper shows that with Gaussian noise, the generator gradient becomes a smooth, distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from the model manifold (Theorem 3.2), and that backpropagating through noisy samples aligns with minimizing JSD between the smoothed distributions (Corollary 3.2).
- A new bound connecting the Wasserstein distance between the original (unsmoothed) distributions and the JSD between their smoothed versions plus a term depending on the noise variance (Theorem 3.3), thereby justifying annealing the noise and training a discriminator to optimality without destabilizing the generator.

Overall, the work offers a coherent theoretical narrative, a simple, theoretically grounded stabilization technique, and tools to reason about divergences and distances under support mismatch.

2. Decision
Accept (clear accept). Key reasons:
- Strong and timely theoretical analysis that addresses central open questions in GAN training with proofs that, while technical, are largely sound and illuminating.
- A practical, simple method (instance noise with annealing) that is theoretically motivated via JSD of smoothed distributions and a provable connection to Wasserstein distance, offering a principled alternative to ad-hoc heuristics.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and placement: The instability and saturation of GAN training were pressing issues in 2016–2017; prior work (Goodfellow et al.; Theis et al.; Huszár) discussed divergences qualitatively. This paper advances the state of understanding by proving perfect discrimination on manifolds under mild non-alignment assumptions, tightly linking support mismatch to JSD saturation and to gradient pathologies.
- Rigor and correctness: The manifold results (Theorems 2.1–2.3) rely on standard tools (Urysohn’s lemma, transversality, Sard’s lemma) and are appropriate for the neural-network architectures considered (Lemma 1). The vanishing gradient bound formalizes a widely observed phenomenon. The identification of the non-saturating update with KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD near θ0 is carefully stated. The noise-smoothing results are clean, including the convolution identities and the gradient structure, and the Wasserstein–JSD bound is well motivated via coupling, total variation, Pinsker, and mixture distributions.
- Practical implications: The analysis justifies training the discriminator close to optimality once input noise is added, avoiding the unstable scheduling heuristics. The attraction–repulsion structure in Theorem 3.2 is intuitive and actionable.
- Novelty: While adding noise had appeared heuristically in some settings, the paper provides the theoretical basis, the gradient structure, and a convergence argument via Wasserstein bounds. The heavy-tail argument for non-saturating loss under noisy D is also insightful, even if based on simplifying assumptions.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Assumptions in Theorem 2.6: Modeling D*−D and its gradient as white noise, independent across x and between value and gradient, is strong and unrealistic for finite networks. Please either (a) relax these assumptions (e.g., correlated Gaussian processes with mild regularity) or (b) provide empirical evidence that gradient updates exhibit heavy tails or diverging variance as D improves.
- Scope of Theorem 2.5: The equality holds at θ0 with optimal D*. Please clarify the implications when D is only approximate and changing simultaneously with θ. A short discussion of how this affects the practical gradient field would help.
- Lemma 1 and smoothness: The “countable union of manifolds of dimension at most dim Z” argument is elegant but assumes standard activations. Please comment on modern architectural components (e.g., max-pooling, batch norm, strided convolutions) and whether the manifold conclusions still hold generically.
- Empirics: The paper would be strengthened by:
  - Controlled toy experiments (2D mixtures) demonstrating vanishing gradient, unstable non-saturating updates, and stabilization via instance noise with annealing.
  - Image experiments showing improved stability and mode coverage compared to baselines, with metrics (e.g., Inception Score, coverage on synthetic mixtures).
  - An ablation on noise schedule and variance selection; practical guidance for annealing.
- Related work: Situate the instance-noise idea relative to concurrent stabilization methods (f-GANs, MMD/IPM-based GANs, WGAN/gradient penalty) and earlier uses of input noise in adversarial training. Clarify similarities and differences, especially given your Wasserstein bound.
- Notational/clarity issues: Several typos (e.g., “arize”, “aboslutely”, “transversallity”) and minor inconsistencies (norm definition, constants in Theorem 2.4). A careful proofread would improve readability. Also, specify measurability conditions where you note technicalities.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is the method to the noise distribution and covariance? Do anisotropic covariances aligned with data manifolds help, and how would one choose them in practice?
- Can you provide empirical evidence that the gradient distribution under the non-saturating loss becomes heavy-tailed as D approaches optimality? For instance, QQ-plots or tail-index estimates over training.
- How do you recommend selecting and annealing σ in practice? Any adaptive criterion tied to D’s training loss or gradient norms?
- Does adding noise to generator outputs harm sample sharpness? Do you observe a trade-off between early stability and final fidelity, and how does annealing mitigate it?
- Can you report JSD estimates between smoothed distributions during training to validate Corollary 3.2 empirically?
- How does instance noise compare to WGAN-style IPMs or gradient penalties on the same tasks with identical architectures and compute?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, insightful paper that advances theoretical understanding and offers a simple, principled stabilization technique. I recommend acceptance.
