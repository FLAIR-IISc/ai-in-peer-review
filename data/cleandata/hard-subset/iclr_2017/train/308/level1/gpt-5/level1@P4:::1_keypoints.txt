- Proves that when real and model distributions lie on low-dimensional, disjoint/near-disjoint manifolds, a (near-)perfect discriminator is locally constant a.e., explaining vanishing generator gradients with the original GAN loss.
- Shows the non-saturating generator loss (−log D) corresponds locally to the gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2·JSD(Pg‖Pr), which favors sharp samples and can encourage mode dropping.
- Demonstrates that simple noisy approximations to the optimal discriminator can induce heavy-tailed (Cauchy) gradient noise for the generator, offering a mechanism for instability.
- Proposes adding continuous noise to discriminator and generator inputs to make distributions absolutely continuous and derives Gaussian-noise generator gradients that pull samples toward data-density and push from model-density regions.
- Motivates Wasserstein distance for disjoint supports and gives a bound relating W(Pr,Pg) to injected-noise variance and JSD of noisy distributions, recommending training the discriminator on noisy samples with annealed noise.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main flaw 1: Technical inconsistencies and gaps in the metric section (Wasserstein vs. TV/KR inequalities and constants) undermine Theorem 3.3.
- Main flaw 2: Insufficient empirical validation—no quantitative experiments showing the proposed noise-based method improves stability or sample quality on standard benchmarks.
- Clarity: Motivation and manifold/JSD intuition are well-placed and largely clear, but the exposition has numerous typos and imprecisions.
- Theorem 2.4 (vanishing-gradient) is plausible given discriminator local constancy and Sobolev-type approximation control.
- The KL−2·JSD identification for the non-saturating update aligns with prior work and supports claims about sharpness/mode dropping.
- The Cauchy-gradient instability result uses a strong, unrealistic per-x white-noise error model; it illustrates a failure mode but needs a more realistic or correlated error model for practical relevance.
- Noise-injection analyses (Thms 3.1, 3.2, Cor 3.2) correctly use convolution to get densities and yield interpretable “pull toward data / push from model” gradient expressions.
- Central metric bound (Thm 3.3) appears inconsistent: the paper mixes W1/W2/squared-cost definitions, misapplies KR duality, and confuses constants (C vs C^2), calling the bound into question.
- Some proofs invoke advanced theorems (Sard, transversality, Urysohn) informally; several regularity, measurability, and boundedness assumptions need explicit statement and justification.
- Empirical reporting is weak: referenced figures lack dataset/setup/metrics, no FID/Inception or mode-coverage comparisons, and no ablations of noise choices or schedules.
- Recommendations: fix and consistently define the Wasserstein metric and constants; re-derive Thm 3.3 under a clear metric; provide full rigorous proofs and assumptions; replace or qualify the white-noise error model; and substantially strengthen experiments (benchmarks, FID, ablations, noise schedules).
- Key questions for authors: which Wasserstein metric is used and how are KR/TV inequalities applied? what are precise constants in Thm 3.3? how is denominator positivity handled in Thm 2.4? how sensitive are results to relaxing transversality/independence assumptions? can you provide concrete experimental evidence for noise+anneal? how does this compare conceptually/empirically to WGAN/gradient-penalty? does noisy training measurably reduce heavy-tailed gradient statistics?
