1. Summary of contributions
The paper offers a theoretical analysis of why GAN training is unstable and often saturates, and develops a principled remedy. The main claims and contributions are:
- It formalizes that both real data and GAN-generated data typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds, and proves that the generator’s image under common neural architectures has measure zero in data space (Lemma 1). This implies that when supports are disjoint or lie on different low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect and flat (Theorems 2.1–2.3), making the classical Jensen–Shannon (JS) game degenerate (JSD = log 2; both KLs infinite).
- It proves that with an approximately optimal discriminator, the generator gradient vanishes for the “saturating” loss (Theorem 2.4), and that for the commonly used “non-saturating” loss −log D, the gradient can become highly unstable: in a stylized noise model, updates have Cauchy tails with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6), aligning with empirical observations of exploding variance and instability.
- It proposes and analyzes a theoretically grounded stabilization: add continuous noise (“instance noise”) to both real and generated samples before feeding the discriminator, and backpropagate through the noise on the generator side. This makes the smoothed distributions Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous, avoids perfect discrimination, and yields meaningful, smooth gradients (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2), corresponding to descending JSD(Pr+‖Pg+).
- It connects this smoothing approach to the Wasserstein distance: it proves that the earth-mover distance between the original unsmoothed distributions satisfies W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2 Var(ε)1/2 + 2C sqrt(JSD(Pr+‖Pg+)) when supports are bounded (Theorem 3.3). This provides a quantitative rationale for annealing the noise while optimizing a computable divergence on the smoothed distributions.
- The paper includes targeted experiments (figures referenced) to validate vanishing gradients and rising variance in practice as the discriminator improves.

Overall, the paper advances understanding of GAN pathologies and offers a conceptually simple, theoretically justified fix with clear prescriptions (add and anneal noise; train D near-optimal; backprop through noise).

2. Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated theoretical analysis linking manifold support, perfect discrimination, and generator gradient pathologies; the smoothing remedy is principled and practically actionable.
- Some assumptions are idealized (e.g., white-noise discriminator errors, compact supports), and empirical validation appears limited; connections to contemporaneous literature (e.g., instance noise, Wasserstein GAN) could be strengthened.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: The paper tackles three central questions: why gradients vanish as D improves; why the −log D trick can be unstable; and whether a divergence is being optimized and with what properties.
- Motivation and placement: The analysis is well positioned within GAN literature, referencing JS/KL trade-offs, empirical stability heuristics, and theoretical expectations. The manifold support perspective is compelling and consistent with common intuitions. The proposal to smooth distributions is natural and echoes known regularization ideas, but here is derived and quantified precisely.
- Correctness and rigor:
  - The manifold/measure-zero results (Lemma 1; Theorems 2.1–2.3) are plausible and technically careful; the use of transversality and Sard’s lemma is appropriate. The conclusion that optimal D is flat almost everywhere on supports explains gradient vanishing cleanly.
  - The vanishing gradient bound (Theorem 2.4) is reasonable under the stated approximation norm and bounded Jacobian assumption.
  - The identification of the −log D update as the gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr) at θ0 (Theorem 2.5) is insightful and explains mode-dropping bias. The derivation appears correct.
  - The instability result (Theorem 2.6) uses a strong white-noise independence assumption between D and ∇D errors; while idealized, it highlights heavy-tailed updates and rising variance that match practice. The authors acknowledge technical measurability caveats.
  - The smoothing analysis (Theorem 3.1; Corollary 3.1; Theorem 3.2/Corollary 3.2) is clear and yields interpretable gradient structure: attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from generator’s own mass, with strength modulated by local Pr+/Pg+. Backpropagating through generator noise addresses measure-zero adversarial directions.
  - The Wasserstein bound (Lemma 4; Theorem 3.3) provides a neat bridge: optimizing JSD on smoothed distributions while annealing noise controls W(Pr,Pg). The assumptions (bounded support, known diameter C) are restrictive but the qualitative message is valuable.
- Empirical support: The paper cites targeted experiments (not fully shown here) demonstrating vanishing gradients and increased variance as D improves, and notes degradation when using −log D as D nears optimality. More extensive empirical evidence of the proposed smoothing+annealing scheme would strengthen the claims but is not strictly required for a theory paper.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not decisive for acceptance)
- Clarify and temper Theorem 2.6: the white-noise independence across x and between D and ∇D is unrealistic. Can you provide a version under milder, more realistic correlated-noise models (e.g., smooth approximation errors, bounded bias), still showing heavy tails or high variance?
- Empirical validation: Include concrete experiments where instance noise is added and annealed on standard image datasets (CIFAR-10, LSUN), comparing against standard GAN baselines. Report metrics such as Inception score and qualitative mode coverage (e.g., Gaussian mixture toy data) to substantiate the predicted stabilization and reduced mode dropping.
- Prior art: Explicitly position the “add noise” strategy relative to prior “instance noise”/input noise regularization (e.g., Sønderby et al., 2016), and discuss differences (notably backprop through generator noise and theoretical guarantees). Also discuss relationships to WGAN (contemporaneous work leveraging Wasserstein distance) and f-GANs; explain when smoothing JSD versus optimizing Wasserstein is preferable.
- Practical guidance: Provide actionable prescriptions for choosing the initial noise scale, the annealing schedule, and covariance structure (isotropic vs. learned Σ). Discuss trade-offs between blurring and stability, and how to detect when to reduce noise.
- Assumptions: State more clearly the scope of manifold assumptions (e.g., piecewise-linear nets with ReLU/leaky ReLU; smooth monotone activations), the compactness conditions, and when the theorems may fail (e.g., high-dimensional Z same as X).
- Notation and figures: Ensure all referenced figures are included and legible; define all norms and constants at first use; clean up minor typos.

Questions for the authors
- Can you relax Theorem 2.6 to a correlated, bounded-variance approximation error model and still prove high variance or bias in −log D updates? Empirically, how sensitive are the heavy tails to error correlations?
- In practice, how do you set and anneal the noise variance? Do you have a principled criterion (e.g., based on D’s accuracy or JSD(Pr+‖Pg+)) to schedule annealing?
- Does adding noise to both D inputs and generator outputs consistently reduce mode dropping on standard benchmarks? Can you provide quantitative evidence (e.g., number of modes recovered in synthetic data; Inception score/FID on images)?
- How does the proposed smoothing compare experimentally to WGAN training in terms of stability and sample diversity? Could the two be combined?
- Does backpropagating through generator noise ever encourage trivial solutions (e.g., collapsing to match the noise distribution) or exacerbate blur? How do you prevent this?
- Can your Wasserstein bound be tightened (e.g., removing the bounded support assumption or improving constants), or extended to other f-divergences on smoothed distributions?
