Summary of contributions
- The paper analyzes why GAN training often suffers from vanishing gradients and instability. It formalizes the setting in which the real and model distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint or near-disjoint supports and proves that, under mild conditions, there exists a (near-)perfect discriminator that is locally constant almost everywhere on those supports, explaining vanishing gradients for the generator with the original GAN loss.
- It further analyzes the commonly used non-saturating generator loss (−log D), showing that the associated update corresponds (locally) to the gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr), thereby favoring sharp samples and potentially encouraging mode dropping. It also shows that noisy approximations to the optimal discriminator can induce heavy-tailed (Cauchy) gradient noise for the generator under a simple noise model, offering a mechanism for instability.
- To mitigate these issues, the paper proposes adding continuous noise to inputs of both discriminator and generator, making the “noisy” distributions absolutely continuous. It derives the form of the generator gradient with Gaussian noise and argues that the resulting updates move samples toward high-density regions of the data manifold while repelling them from high-density model regions, stabilizing training.
- Finally, it motivates Wasserstein distance as a softer metric for comparing distributions with disjoint supports and provides a bound relating W(Pr,Pg) to the variance of the injected noise and the JSD between the corresponding noisy distributions, suggesting a practical procedure: train the discriminator on noisy samples (optimize JSD of noisy distributions) while annealing the noise to reduce the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Technical inconsistencies and gaps in the metric section (definition and bounds involving the Wasserstein distance and total variation) undermine a central theoretical claim (Theorem 3.3).
2) Insufficient empirical validation: the paper claims targeted experiments but provides no quantitative results or thorough evaluation demonstrating that the proposed noise-based approach improves stability or performance across standard benchmarks.

Supporting arguments
- Clarity and placement in literature:
  - The motivation (manifold mismatch, JSD saturation, discriminator optimality leading to vanishing gradients) is well-placed, aligns with prior observations, and is explained clearly with formal tools. The connection to existing divergences and their behavior (KL asymmetries, JSD saturation) is accurate and helpful for practitioners.
  - The discussion of “perfect discrimination” on low-dimensional supports is rigorous in spirit and provides useful intuition for why standard GAN gradients vanish as D improves. Assumptions (transversality after small perturbations) are standard.
  - However, the exposition suffers from numerous typographical errors and some imprecisions, especially in the metric section.

- Technical soundness:
  - The vanishing-gradient result (Theorem 2.4) is plausible given D*’s local constancy on Pg’s support and a discriminator approximation controlled in a Sobolev-type norm; the bound structure matches the intuition.
  - The identification of the non-saturating update with ∇θ[KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD] at θ0 is consistent with known derivations (e.g., Huszár’s note). This supports the claim about tendencies toward sharpness and mode dropping.
  - The instability result (Cauchy gradients) uses a strong “white-noise per x” assumption for discriminator error and its gradient; while this is not realistic, it illustrates heavy-tailed behavior under ratio-of-Gaussians noise. As a formal theorem, it clarifies a failure mode but its practical import would benefit from a weaker/noisier but correlated error model.
  - The noise-injection analysis (Theorems 3.1, 3.2, Corollary 3.2) correctly leverages convolution to obtain absolutely continuous densities and provides interpretable gradient expressions with Gaussian kernels that align with “pull toward data, push from model” forces.
  - The central metric bound (Theorem 3.3) appears inconsistent: the paper defines W(P,Q) as inf ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ (squared cost) but then derives bounds that match W2 (square root of expected squared distance) and invokes inequalities (Kantorovich–Rubinstein, W ≤ C·TV) that apply to W1 with bounded cost. Constants and metric orders are conflated (C vs C^2; W1 vs W2), casting doubt on the bound as stated. This is a major issue because the bound underpins the claim that minimizing JSD of noisy distributions while annealing the noise reduces the actual distance between original distributions.
  - Several proofs rely on advanced results (Sard, transversality, Urysohn’s lemma) in ways that seem reasonable but require care regarding measurability and regularity; some steps are deferred or stated informally.

- Empirical support:
  - The text references figures demonstrating discriminator loss going to zero, gradient norms increasing with discriminator strength, and vanishing gradients, but provides no detailed experimental setup, datasets, metrics, or comparisons. There is no demonstration that the noise-based procedure yields better FID/Inception scores, reduces mode dropping, or improves training stability relative to baselines. Given the strong practical motivation, the lack of robust empirical evaluation is a significant weakness.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Fix the Wasserstein metric section:
  - Clearly specify whether you use W1, W2, or the squared-cost transport. Ensure consistency in definitions, constants, and the inequalities used (e.g., Pinsker’s inequality connects TV and KL; KR duality is for W1; for bounded supports, W1 ≤ C·TV, while W2 (or squared-cost) requires different constants).
  - Re-derive Theorem 3.3 under a consistent metric choice and check constants carefully. If using W2, consider Talagrand-type inequalities or relate W2 to JSD via intermediate TV/KL with justified constants and support assumptions.
- Provide complete, rigorous proofs or precise statements of assumptions (regularity of manifolds, measurability of processes, norms used for discriminator approximation, boundedness assumptions needed for denominator manipulations in Theorem 2.4).
- Replace the white-noise discriminator model with a more realistic error model (e.g., correlated noise, function-class approximation bounds) or present it as an illustrative toy with caveats, and complement with empirical evidence of heavy-tailed gradient statistics in practice.
- Strengthen experiments:
  - Evaluate the proposed noise-injection and annealing scheme on standard image datasets, compare to baseline GAN training (with and without tricks), and report stability metrics, sample quality (e.g., FID), and mode coverage.
  - Ablate noise distributions, variance schedules, and their impact on training dynamics and performance.
  - Show that training the discriminator to near-optimality with noise avoids vanishing gradients and improves convergence.

Questions for the authors
1) Which Wasserstein metric do you intend to use in Section 3 (W1, W2, or squared-cost)? Please clarify the definition and revise Theorem 3.3 accordingly. How do you justify inequalities mixing KR duality (W1) and squared-cost definitions?
2) In Theorem 3.3, what are the precise constants when supports are bounded? Should the factor be C or C^2 (or involve √C) given your chosen metric?
3) For Theorem 2.4, what conditions ensure |1 − D(gθ(z))| − ε remains positive, and how is ε chosen relative to the discriminator norms to avoid division by a small denominator?
4) How sensitive are the main conclusions (vanishing gradients and instability) to relaxing the manifold transversality assumptions and the independence assumptions in Theorem 2.6?
5) Can you provide concrete experimental results demonstrating that adding noise to both generator and discriminator inputs, with annealing, improves stability and sample quality across datasets? What noise schedules work best?
6) How does your proposed approach compare to Wasserstein GANs and gradient penalty methods introduced contemporaneously, both conceptually and empirically?
7) Does the noisy training induce measurable reductions in heavy-tailed gradient statistics (e.g., kurtosis) for the generator updates?
