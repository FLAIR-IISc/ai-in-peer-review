Summary and contributions
The paper aims to provide a principled theoretical account of why GAN training is unstable and why gradients either vanish or explode as the discriminator improves. It proves: (i) “perfect discrimination” theorems showing that when real and model distributions lie on low-dimensional, non-aligned manifolds, the optimal discriminator is constant almost everywhere on the supports, implying zero generator gradients; (ii) a vanishing-gradient result for the original GAN loss as D→D*, and (iii) an instability result for the −log D update under small discriminator noise, yielding Cauchy-distributed updates with infinite variance. To remedy this, the paper proposes adding noise (“instance noise”) to both real and generated samples, showing that this smooths distributions, yields well-behaved gradients proportional to JSD between the smoothed distributions, and provides a bound linking the true Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg to the JSD of their noisy counterparts plus a term controlled by the noise variance. The analysis is rigorous and accompanied by targeted empirical checks.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated theoretical explanation of core GAN pathologies (vanishing gradients and instability) with clear, novel proofs; (2) A principled, practical training remedy (input noise with annealing) supported by theory and linked to Wasserstein geometry.

Supporting arguments
- The manifold-support perspective and transversality arguments convincingly explain why divergences saturate even when samples look good, matching empirical observations. The Urysohn-based construction of perfect discriminators and the resulting gradient analyses are technically sound and insightful.
- The instability result for −log D, while using stylized white-noise assumptions, captures the heavy-tailed gradient behavior practitioners observe.
- The smoothing approach is theoretically grounded: it restores continuity, yields tractable JSD optimization, and the Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) connects the smoothed training objective to the true distance of interest as noise is annealed.

Additional feedback (to help improve, not central to the decision)
- Clarify and, if possible, relax the independence/white-noise assumptions in Theorem 2.6; a version with correlated noise would strengthen the claim.
- Provide more comprehensive empirical validation: compare instance noise against popular stabilization techniques (feature matching, gradient penalty, spectral normalization) across datasets using precision/recall and FID; ablate noise schedules and variances.
- Discuss applicability to discrete domains (text) and to cases where dim Z ≈ dim X.
- Minor: tighten exposition (define absolute continuity early, streamline measure-theoretic caveats), fix typos, and include the referenced figures.

Questions for the authors
- Can you extend Theorem 2.6 to more realistic discriminator errors (correlated, heteroskedastic, or bounded) and still obtain heavy tails or instability?
- How sensitive is performance to the noise distribution and annealing schedule? Any principled schedule from the theory?
- Do you observe reduced mode dropping empirically under instance noise, relative to strong baselines?
- Can your bounds be adapted to alternative metrics (e.g., IPM with Lipschitz constraints) without input noise?
- What happens when the generator’s image is not strictly lower-dimensional (e.g., expressive flows) or when latent dimension equals data dimension?
