Identifies GAN instability’s root cause: Pg from neural generators lies on a low-dimensional manifold so Pr and Pg supports are almost surely disjoint.  
Shows vanishing gradients: a perfect, smooth discriminator exists with zero gradients almost everywhere on the data/generator manifolds, explaining log(1-D) failures.  
Analyzes the -log D generator objective: formally derives it as KL(Pg||Pr)+JSD, explaining sharp samples but mode dropping and showing noisy near-optimal discriminators give infinite-variance (Cauchy) gradients.  
Proposes noise injection into discriminator inputs to make distributions continuous, yield meaningful gradients, and links minimizing JSD of noisy distributions to upper-bounding the Wasserstein distance between original distributions.  
Decision: Accept.  
Key reason — fundamental insight: provides a novel, profound theoretical explanation for longstanding GAN training failures.  
Key reason — rigor and solution: delivers rigorous proofs and a principled, theoretically-grounded stabilization strategy (noise + Wasserstein perspective).  
Lemma 1 significance: proves neural-network generators’ outputs concentrate on low-dim manifolds, underpinning the paper’s arguments.  
Theorems 2.1/2.2 significance: rigorously establish existence of a perfect discriminator and resulting vanishing gradients.  
Theorem 2.5 significance: formalizes why -log D yields high-quality samples but encourages mode collapse.  
Theorem 2.6 significance: shows noisy near-optimal discriminators produce generator gradients with infinite variance, explaining practical instability.  
Theorems 3.2/Corollary 3.2 significance: derive well-behaved, interpretable gradients under input noise and show they push samples toward the data manifold.  
Theorem 3.3 significance: connects JSD minimization on noisy distributions to an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance, providing a meaningful evaluation/training objective.  
Feedback — clarify noise assumptions: discuss generalization of the white-noise Cauchy result to correlated or other noise types.  
Feedback — practical guidance: add pseudo-code/algorithm and discuss choices for noise type, variance, and annealing schedules.  
Feedback — experiments: include small-scale comparisons (e.g., DCGAN vs. noise-injected GAN) showing stability metrics like gradient norms.  
Question — bound tightness: how tight is the Wasserstein upper bound in practice and could large noise variance loosen it?  
Question — annealing schedule: any practical intuition or sensitivity analysis on how quickly to reduce noise for stability?  
Question — -log D in noisy setting: would using -log D with noise help or suffer similar instabilities?  
Question — direct Wasserstein loss: can W(Pr,Pg), via its dual, be used directly as a GAN loss instead of optimizing the upper bound?
