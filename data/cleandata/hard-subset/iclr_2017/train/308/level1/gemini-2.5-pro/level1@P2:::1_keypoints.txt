- Proves that when real and generated distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, a perfect discriminator exists that separates them with 100% accuracy.
- Shows a perfect discriminator gives the generator zero gradient almost everywhere, causing vanishing gradients with the original log(1-D) loss.
- Analyzes the -log D objective, proving it optimizes a mixture of KL and JSD and that it induces unstable, high-variance updates as the discriminator approaches optimality.
- Proposes adding continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions, prevent perfect discrimination, and provide informative gradients to the generator.
- Introduces the Wasserstein distance as a more appropriate metric for manifold-supported distributions and proves minimizing JSD of noisy distributions upper-bounds the Wasserstein distance between original distributions.
- Recommendation: Accept — the paper provides a rigorous, clarifying theoretical foundation for GAN instability and a principled, practical remedy.
- Key theoretical insight: modeling Pr and Pg as concentrated on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1) is the central idea enabling the analysis.
- Theorems 2.1–2.4 formalize perfect discrimination and directly explain vanishing gradients (saturation) observed in original GAN training.
- Theorem 2.5 explains why -log D produces sharp samples but encourages mode collapse; Theorem 2.6 shows discriminator errors can lead to infinite-variance gradients.
- Section 3 and Theorem 3.3 unify the noise-based fix and the Wasserstein metric, turning an ill-posed manifold comparison into a well-posed continuous one.
- Readability concern: manifold transversality and dense technical sections need more high-level intuition to aid accessibility.
- Framing suggestion: emphasize the Wasserstein contribution more strongly and consider renaming Section 3 to reflect its centrality.
- Practical guidance lacking: more discussion on choosing/annealing noise variance V and the trade-offs between optimization ease and tightness of the Wasserstein bound.
- Question: Have you considered optimizing approximations of the Wasserstein distance directly (e.g., via Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality) and how would that compare to instance noise?
- Question: Does the physical interpretation of gradients as forces suggest alternative noise choices (e.g., heavy-tailed noise) that might yield different/better behavior?
- Question: How sensitive is the instability result (Theorem 2.6) to the white-noise error model—would structured or correlated discriminator errors change the conclusion?
