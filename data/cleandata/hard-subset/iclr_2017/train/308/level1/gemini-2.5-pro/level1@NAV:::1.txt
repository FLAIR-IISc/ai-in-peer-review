**Review of "Towards Principled Methods for Training Generative Adversarial Networks"**

This paper provides a much-needed and rigorous theoretical analysis of the training instabilities that plague Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors' core contribution is to move beyond heuristic fixes and establish a foundational understanding of why GAN training often fails. They posit that the data distribution and the generator distribution lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which allows for the existence of a perfect discriminator. The paper compellingly demonstrates how such a discriminator leads to vanishing gradients for the generator under the original GAN cost function and unstable, high-variance gradients under the commonly used alternative `-logD` cost function. As a solution, the authors propose adding noise to the discriminator's inputs, which they prove results in smooth, meaningful gradients. Crucially, they connect this approach to a more stable metric, the Wasserstein distance, providing a principled path towards stable GAN training.

I strongly recommend **Accepting** this paper. My decision is based on two key points: 1) The paper offers a groundbreaking theoretical explanation for the widely observed but poorly understood instabilities in GAN training, replacing empirical guesswork with mathematical rigor. 2) It introduces a theoretically sound and practical solution that fundamentally shifts the GAN objective towards optimizing a more sensible distance metric between distributions.

The supporting arguments for this decision are strong. The theorems proving the existence of a perfect discriminator (Theorems 2.1, 2.2) and its consequences—vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) or infinite-variance updates (Theorem 2.6)—are insightful and directly explain common empirical frustrations. The derivation showing the `-logD` objective relates to `KL(Pg||Pr)` (Theorem 2.5) provides a clear theoretical justification for GANs’ tendency towards mode collapse. The paper's primary novelty lies in Section 3, where adding noise is shown not merely as a heuristic but as a method to optimize a bound on the Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3). This recasts the problem in a way that avoids the pitfalls of divergence maximization and provides a clear, actionable research direction.

For the authors, here is some additional feedback aimed at improving the paper:
*   The connection between the theoretical noise models (e.g., the white noise process in Theorem 2.6) and the practical Gaussian noise added in Section 3 could be made more explicit.
*   A brief discussion on the practical implications of annealing the noise variance `V` would be valuable. Does a specific schedule emerge from the theory, or is it still an empirical choice?

I would also appreciate clarification on the following questions:
*   The instability proof in Theorem 2.6 relies on a specific Gaussian noise model for the discriminator's error. How sensitive are these conclusions to other forms of approximation error?
*   The bound in Theorem 3.3 depends on the support's diameter `C`. How does the potentially large or unknown value of `C` affect the practical utility of minimizing the JSD term as a surrogate for the Wasserstein distance?
