- Paper gives a rigorous explanation that GAN training difficulties stem from real and generated data lying on low-dimensional, almost surely disjoint manifolds.
- Proves that with disjoint supports an optimal discriminator perfectly separates distributions, causing vanishing gradients with the original log(1-D) loss.
- Analyzes the -log(D) objective and shows it equals KL(Pg||Pr) - 2·JSD(Pg||Pr), explaining mode-dropping tendencies.
- Shows generator gradients under -log(D) can have Cauchy-like tails (infinite variance) under a white-noise assumption, causing extreme instability.
- Proposes adding noise to discriminator inputs (instance noise) to make distributions absolutely continuous and overlapping, restoring smooth, informative gradients.
- Introduces the Wasserstein distance as an appropriate metric on manifolds and proves the noise-based scheme minimizes an upper bound on this distance for the noiseless distributions.
- Decision: Accept — judged a strong paper because of its deep theoretical contributions to GAN understanding.
- Key reasons for acceptance: (1) a novel, rigorous diagnosis of GAN instability; (2) a principled path toward stable training linking noise, JSD, and Wasserstein distance.
- Supporting strengths: Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1/2.2 establish low-dimensional generator manifolds and existence of perfect discriminators causing gradient saturation.
- Supporting strengths: Theorems 2.5/2.6 provide a compelling characterization of -log(D) and a mathematical model for gradient chaos.
- Supporting strengths: Theorem 3.2 explains the generator dynamics under input noise; Theorem 3.3 connects the method to minimizing Wasserstein distance.
- Main weakness: insufficient empirical validation—more experiments are needed to demonstrate practical stability and sample quality gains.
- Suggests comparative experiments against standard GANs and stabilization methods on benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) with metrics like loss curves and Inception Scores.
- Recommends discussion of the white-noise assumption’s limitations and how conclusions might change with structured noise.
- Requests practical guidance or examples for annealing the added noise and how to choose schedules in practice.
- Question: How sensitive are the gradient-instability results to the assumption of independent, centered Gaussian noise and to correlations between noise in D and ∇D?
- Question: Have the authors measured generator gradient variance empirically and shown it decreases with input noise as claimed?
- Question: How tight is the Wasserstein upper bound in practice, and how does the choice of noise distribution (Gaussian vs. uniform) affect bound tightness and training dynamics?
