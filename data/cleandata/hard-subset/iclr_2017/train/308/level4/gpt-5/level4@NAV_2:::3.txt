1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles fundamental questions about why GAN training is unstable and why generator updates degrade as the discriminator improves. It rigorously analyzes the geometry of supports for Pr and Pg, proving that when these lie on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds, an (almost everywhere) constant optimal discriminator exists, implying vanishing or uninformative gradients for the generator under the original loss. It further shows that the commonly used non-saturating generator update corresponds, at the expansion point, to optimizing KL(Pg‖Pr) minus 2·JSD, explaining sharp samples and mode dropping, and that noisy approximations to the optimal discriminator can yield heavy-tailed, high-variance updates. To mitigate these issues, the paper proposes adding noise to smooth the distributions, derives the resulting well-behaved gradients, and connects the approach to Wasserstein distance via bounds that relate W(Pr,Pg) to the noise variance and JSD between the smoothed distributions. The work combines tools from analysis and differential topology with targeted experiments to verify assumptions and illustrate phenomena, and it outlines principled research directions to address instability in GANs.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- The paper offers clear theoretical explanations for widely observed GAN pathologies (vanishing gradients and instability) and ties them to precise geometric conditions, together with well-justified remedies (noise smoothing and Wasserstein-inspired analysis).
- The results are well placed in the literature and are likely to influence both theory and practice, providing an actionable, theoretically grounded direction to stabilize training.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper studies the failure modes of GAN training—instability, saturation, and degradation of generator updates as the discriminator improves—and seeks metrics and procedures that yield stable, informative gradients even when supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds or are nearly disjoint.
- Is the approach well motivated and well situated in prior work? Yes. The analysis builds on the standard GAN framework (Goodfellow et al.), contrasts divergences (Theis et al.; Huszár), and leverages classical tools (e.g., transversality, Sard’s lemma, Urysohn’s lemma, Pinsker, Wasserstein/optimal transport). The choice to consider smoothing and to relate it to Wasserstein distance is well motivated and addresses known issues with KL/JSD when supports do not overlap.
- Are the claims supported and rigorous? Lemmata and theorems precisely characterize perfect discrimination on disjoint/low-dimensional supports (Theorems 2.1–2.3), vanishing gradients under the original generator loss as the discriminator approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4), the objective implicitly optimized by the non-saturating update (Theorem 2.5), and instability under noisy discriminator approximations (Theorem 2.6). The smoothing results (Theorem 3.1, Corollary 3.1, Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2) and the bound relating W(Pr,Pg) to JSD of smoothed distributions and noise variance (Theorem 3.3) are coherent and technically sound. Empirical checks (as described in the text) substantiate the theoretical predictions about gradient norms and behavior as the discriminator improves. While some proofs rely on standard analysis/topology facts, the synthesis and their implications for GAN training are novel and valuable.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not essential to the acceptance decision)
- Streamlining exposition:
  - Section 2.1 could be shortened by moving proofs to the appendix and referencing standard results where appropriate; this would improve readability without sacrificing rigor.
  - The numbering of theorems/lemmas is occasionally confusing; a pass to ensure consistent numbering and cross-references would help.
- Clarifications and precision:
  - In Theorem 2.4, briefly restate the distributional assumption on p(z) for reader convenience.
  - When introducing random variables in Section 2.6 and related places, specify their domains and regularity assumptions.
  - Theorem 2.2: clarify whether the “closed manifolds” are assumed to have boundary or not (and ensure definitions and lemmas consistently handle boundary cases).
  - Lemma 1 likely holds under more general activation classes; consider stating the maximal class assumed or adding a remark on generality.
  - The “white noise” independence assumption for D and ∇D in Theorem 2.6 is idealized; a short discussion of more realistic correlated/noisy approximations and how the instability argument degrades would strengthen the practical relevance.
- Literature and attributions:
  - Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 rely on standard analysis/topology (e.g., Urysohn’s lemma, general position/transversality, Sard’s lemma). Adding references could replace some proofs and guide readers.
- Wording, typos, and minor edits:
  - Page 2, second bullet: it also implies that Pg must be smaller than Pr at some x elsewhere, which ensures KL divergence does not vanish.
  - Replace “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” with “for generating implausible-looking pictures.”
  - “relly” -> “rely.”
  - Theorem 2.5: “Therefore” -> “Then” (or “Thus”) for smoother flow.
  - Theorem 2.6: “Is a” -> “is a.”
  - Corollary 2.1 references “Theorem 1.3,” which seems missing; please correct the reference.
  - Consider reminding the reader that p(z) denotes the prior over Z when it first appears in a theorem or corollary.

Questions for the authors
- Assumptions on manifolds:
  - In Theorem 2.2, are the “closed manifolds” allowed to have boundary? If so, can you clarify how boundary points are treated in the transversality and “perfect alignment” definitions, and ensure all proofs explicitly cover these cases?
  - How sensitive are the “perfect discrimination” conclusions to mild violations of the manifold assumptions (e.g., supports with small absolutely continuous components)?
- Noise smoothing details:
  - What annealing schedule for the noise variance do you recommend in practice to balance the V1/2 term and the JSD term in Theorem 3.3? Any empirical guidance on when to reduce σ without destabilizing training?
  - How sensitive are results to the choice of noise family and covariance (e.g., isotropic vs. anisotropic Gaussian, heavy-tailed noise as in Corollary 3.1)? Do you observe practical differences in stability or sample quality?
  - You note that backpropagating through noisy generator outputs mitigates measure-zero adversarial directions. Could you quantify this effect empirically (e.g., by measuring adversarial susceptibility as σ varies)?
- Gradient stability under non-ideal discriminators:
  - Theorem 2.6 uses independent white-noise errors in D and ∇D. Can you relax this to correlated, bounded-variance approximation errors that better model finite-capacity discriminators? Do heavy tails or large variance persist under weaker assumptions?
- Extensions and scope:
  - Lemma 1 is stated for common activations; can the result extend to broader function classes (e.g., residual connections with non-monotone activations) without changing the main conclusions?
  - Do your results suggest concrete architectural or regularization choices (beyond noise) that increase overlap of supports early in training (e.g., explicit smoothing layers, data augmentation, or generator priors that increase support dimension)?
- Empirical validation:
  - You reference figures demonstrating vanishing gradients and increased gradient variance as D improves. Could you add quantitative metrics (e.g., gradient norm statistics vs. discriminator accuracy) and ablations across architectures/datasets to bolster the empirical side?
