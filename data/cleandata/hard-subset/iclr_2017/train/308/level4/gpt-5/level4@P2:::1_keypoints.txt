- Rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics focusing on instability and gradient saturation.  
- Shows when real and generated distributions lie on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant on the supports.  
- Proves gradients to the generator vanish under the original GAN loss as the discriminator approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4).  
- Derives that the −log D generator update effectively optimizes KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr), explaining why it tolerates mode dropping (Theorem 2.5).  
- Shows noisy approximations to the optimal discriminator can produce generator updates with Cauchy-distributed components (infinite variance), theoretically grounding observed instability of −log D (Theorem 2.6).  
- Proposes adding continuous noise to inputs and generator outputs to obtain smoothed distributions with stable, well-behaved gradients (Section 3; Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2).  
- Proves a bound linking the Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions to the JSD between their noisy versions plus a variance term, justifying noise annealing (Theorem 3.3).  
- Provides targeted experiments that empirically illustrate predicted vanishing/unstable gradients and improved behavior with noise.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: novel, rigorous theorems that explain empirical GAN failures (instability, saturation, mode dropping) and propose a principled noise-smoothing remedy.  
- Contribution: introduces analytical tools (perfect discrimination, vanishing/unstable gradient theorems, Wasserstein–JSD bound) likely to influence research and practice.
