- Clear analysis of GAN training dynamics explaining why gradients vanish under the original objective and become unstable under common variants.  
- Proposes a simple perturbation-based technique to mitigate vanishing and unstable gradients.  
- Theoretical insight: gradient-behavior analysis is well-motivated, intuitive, and technically grounded.  
- Practical relevance: the perturbation mechanism is simple and potentially widely applicable without major architectural changes.  
- Clarity of contribution: combines a diagnostic (gradient pathologies) with a pragmatic remedy (perturbation).  
- Scope/assumptions need clarification (discriminator class, support mismatch, smoothness/regularity conditions).  
- Identify regimes where the analysis or perturbation may not apply or could degrade performance.  
- Specify the nature and placement of perturbation (inputs, features, logits, parameters, or gradients; generator, discriminator, or both; additive/multiplicative/masking).  
- Clarify connection to existing techniques like noise injection and dropout, and how perturbation affects bias, Lipschitz properties, or support mismatch.  
- Address hyperparameter sensitivity: magnitude, distribution, schedule, and whether annealing helps.  
- Empirical validation is insufficient: show diagnostic plots for the perturbed setup (gradient norms, stability indicators, divergence proxies).  
- Revision: precisely define perturbation distributions and exactly where/when they are applied during training.  
- Revision: discuss theoretical implications—does perturbation change the effective objective or smooth the discriminator landscape?  
- Empirical additions: replicate Figures 2 and 3 for perturbed GANs, include ablations on type/magnitude/schedule, show training curves, qualitative samples, and standard metrics.  
- Compare against simple stabilization baselines (e.g., label smoothing, feature matching) and report computational overhead and convergence speed changes.  
- Clarify generality across architectures/datasets, interactions with components like batch normalization, and potential failure cases (e.g., excessive noise harming the generator).  
- Relate explicitly to dropout and noise-injection strategies and, if possible, include experiments comparing Bernoulli vs Gaussian perturbations.  
- Overall: valuable and timely contribution, but needs clearer perturbation definition, positioning relative to prior work, and stronger empirical evidence to be fully convincing.
