Summary of contributions
- The paper offers a rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics, focusing on instability, saturation, and the behavior of gradients as the discriminator approaches optimality. It explains why the original GAN objective leads to vanishing gradients for the generator when the discriminator is strong (Theorem 2.4) and why the commonly used alternative generator update based on −log D can yield unstable, high-variance updates (Theorem 2.6). Theorem 2.5 clarifies what objective the −log D update actually optimizes, providing insight into mode-dropping behavior.
- The work formalizes the “manifold support” viewpoint: both Pr and Pg typically concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discrimination and saturated divergences (Theorems 2.1–2.3). This is a compelling explanation for observed training pathologies.
- The paper proposes a principled remedy: inject continuous noise so that the discriminator sees smoothed (absolutely continuous) distributions, which yields usable gradients and avoids measure-zero issues (Theorem 3.1 and Corollary 3.2). It further connects this strategy to the Wasserstein distance by bounding W(Pr,Pg) in terms of the noise variance and the JSD between the smoothed distributions (Theorem 3.3). This gives a tractable, theoretically grounded direction to stabilize training and reduce dependence on delicate training schedules.
- The theorems (notably 2.4–2.6 and 3.1–3.3) are, to my knowledge, novel in this context and employ interesting mathematical tools. The targeted experiments help illustrate the phenomena (e.g., vanishing gradients and exploding gradient variance as D improves).

Decision: Accept
- Primary reasons: (1) strong and timely theoretical contributions that directly address core open questions about GAN stability; (2) clear, well-motivated connection between theory and practice, including a concrete stabilization strategy (noise injection) with provable properties.

Supporting arguments
- Importance of the problem: Understanding and stabilizing GAN training is crucial, given their impact across image generation, semi-supervised learning, and sequence modeling. The paper addresses exactly the failure modes practitioners have observed.
- Positioning and motivation: The work is well placed in the literature and articulates how the classical divergences (KL and JSD) behave on manifold-supported distributions, explaining why naive divergence minimization can fail. It also clarifies the practical consequences of the choice of generator objective.
- Rigor and correctness: The mathematical development is careful and, in my view, correct. The manifold-support arguments (Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1–2.3) are persuasive and align with common architectural choices (pointwise nonlinearities and affine layers). The gradient analyses (Theorems 2.4–2.6) capture the observed trade-off between discriminator optimality and generator learning signal. The smoothing results and Wasserstein bound (Theorems 3.1–3.3) are both elegant and practical.
- Empirical support: Although not the focus, the experiments are targeted and appropriate to validate the theoretical claims (e.g., demonstrating gradient vanishing and increased gradient variance).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not central to my decision)
- Clarify assumptions in Theorem 2.6: The white-noise assumption and independence between the value and gradient noise are strong. You do note this, but a short discussion of how correlated or structured approximation errors affect the conclusion (even qualitatively) would strengthen the result.
- Theorem 2.5 derivation: Provide a more explicit step-by-step derivation connecting the −log D update with KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr), including all regularity assumptions (existence of densities, differentiability under the integral sign, and conditions for exchanging gradients and expectations).
- Practical guidance: The noise-injection strategy is compelling. A brief set of recommendations on the noise schedule (initial variance, annealing rate), the choice of covariance structure, and how to balance discriminator/generator updates would increase practical impact.
- Scope of Lemma 1: It would help to explicitly state that common convolutional architectures fit the “affine + pointwise nonlinearity” framework, to reassure readers that the manifold-support conclusion applies to standard GANs.
- Experimental details: Include more specifics for the figures (architectures, datasets, optimization hyperparameters) and, if space allows, an ablation on the noise injection (variance and covariance) to demonstrate robustness.

Questions for the authors
1. Theorem 2.5: Could you provide a fully explicit proof, including conditions under which gradients can pass through the optimal discriminator D* (e.g., dominated convergence or other justification for exchanging ∇θ and expectations)? Are there edge cases where the identity fails (e.g., non-smooth generators or non-absolutely continuous Pg)?
2. Theorem 2.6: How sensitive are the instability conclusions to relaxing the independence/white-noise assumption? Do you have empirical evidence or theoretical intuition for the case where approximation errors in D and ∇D are correlated or heteroscedastic?
3. Noise injection: What annealing schedules worked best in practice? How sensitive are results to the initial variance and the choice of isotropic vs. anisotropic noise? Does injecting noise into both the discriminator input and the generator output materially outperform injecting noise only into D?
4. Metrics: In Theorem 3.3, the bound depends on the diameter C of the support of the smoothed distributions. Can you comment on how conservative this bound is in practice, and whether tighter problem-dependent constants are possible?
5. Manifold assumptions: Do your results change if the generator’s support is a union of manifolds with varying dimensions (as often happens with ReLU networks), or if the data manifold has boundaries? Are there practical diagnostics to check the “no perfect alignment” condition?
6. Empirical validation: Beyond gradient norms, do you observe improved sample quality or reduced mode dropping with the proposed noise-injection strategy across multiple datasets/architectures?

Overall, this is a strong, insightful paper that advances our theoretical understanding of GAN training and offers a principled path to more stable optimization. I recommend acceptance.
