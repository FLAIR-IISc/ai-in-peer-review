Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles a fundamental problem: understanding why GAN training is unstable and why generator updates degrade as the discriminator improves. It analyzes training dynamics when data and generator supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds, showing that the discriminator can be perfect and locally constant, which leads to vanishing gradients for the original generator loss and instability for the −log D alternative.
- It provides rigorous results: the existence of smooth perfect discriminators with zero gradients almost everywhere on the supports; vanishing gradients for the original loss as D→D*; identification of the generator update under −log D as optimizing KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD, which explains mode dropping; and a formal instability result showing Cauchy-distributed updates under noisy discriminator approximations.
- It proposes a principled fix: add continuous noise to both real and generated samples to make the distributions absolutely continuous, derive smooth, interpretable gradients, and relate optimization of JSD between the noisy distributions to an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions. This yields a stable training direction and removes the need for delicate discriminator/generator scheduling.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) strong, clear theoretical insights that explain widely observed GAN pathologies and link them to divergence choices and manifold geometry; (2) a principled, simple remedy (noise smoothing) with rigorous analysis and practical implications.

Supporting arguments
- Problem relevance (Q1): The paper targets central, unsolved questions in GAN training: why updates worsen with a better discriminator, the roots of instability, and what divergences are actually optimized in practice. This is timely and important.
- Motivation and placement (Q2): The analysis builds on known GAN objectives (Goodfellow et al.), connects to KL/JSD behavior (Theis et al., Huszár), and leverages tools from analysis and differential topology. The direction toward Wasserstein-based reasoning is well motivated and conceptually bridges manifold distance with trainable divergences via noise.
- Support for claims (Q3): Theoretical results are clearly stated and, where provided, rigorous. Theorems about perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients, and instability under noisy D are compelling and align with empirical observations. The smoothing approach is justified by explicit gradient expressions and a bound relating JSD of smoothed distributions to the Wasserstein distance of the originals. Targeted experiments (e.g., gradient norms vs. discriminator optimality) substantiate the theory qualitatively.

Caveats that do not overturn the main conclusion:
- Some proofs are standard and could be shortened or referenced, and a few statements would benefit from clarifications (e.g., boundary conditions on manifolds, domains of random variables). The manuscript is long and technical in places, but the core ideas are clear and impactful.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all are decision-critical)
- Exposition and organization
  - Section 2.1: Consider shortening and moving full proofs to the appendix; keep the main text focused on intuition and implications.
  - The numbering of theorems/lemmas is confusing at points; ensure consistency and correct cross-references.
  - In Theorem 2.4, briefly remind the reader of the prior p(z) and assumptions (e.g., bounded Jacobian norm conditions).
  - Specify the domains of all random variables and processes (e.g., where x, z, η, η′ live; what “white noise” means operationally for D and ∇D).
  - Clarify in Theorem 2.2 whether the closed manifolds have boundary or not; align with the definitions used in Lemmas 2–3.
  - Corollary 2.1 references “Theorem 1.3”; correct this reference.
  - Theorem 2.5: “Therefore” → “Then” would read more naturally.
  - Theorem 2.6: “Is a…” → “is a…”.
- Technical/content clarifications
  - Page 2, second bullet: Note explicitly that if Pr(x) < Pg(x) for some x, then necessarily Pg is smaller than Pr elsewhere, making KL(Pr‖Pg) nonzero overall.
  - Page 2 wording: “for generating not plausibly looking pictures” is preferable to “for not generating plausibly looking pictures.”
  - Lemma 1 likely holds in greater generality; consider stating the most general sufficient conditions (beyond common neural-network nonlinearities) or add a reference.
  - Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 are standard analysis/topology; cite standard references to shorten proofs in the main text.
  - Fix typos: “relly” → “rely”, “aboslutely” → “absolutely”, “symetrical” → “symmetrical”, “arize/arizing” → “arise/arising”, etc.
- Results and implications
  - Emphasize that adding noise to both discriminator inputs and generator outputs is crucial; backpropagating only through noiseless g(Z) risks measure-zero adversarial directions.
  - Discuss the practical choice of noise distributions and annealing schedules; provide guidance on covariance selection and how it affects the induced notion of distance.
  - When introducing the Wasserstein bound, comment on how to estimate the constants (e.g., C) in practice and whether the bound is tight enough to guide training.

Questions for the authors
- Experiments: Beyond the reported figures, can you quantify how the variance and bias of generator gradients change as D approaches optimality under both losses? Do you observe the predicted heavy tails?
- Noise design: How sensitive are results to the choice of noise distribution and covariance structure? Any empirical guidance for σ-annealing schedules that balance smooth gradients with faithfulness to the original problem?
- Practical training: Do you recommend always adding noise to both real and generated samples, and to generator outputs during backprop? How does this interact with batch normalization or other stabilizing heuristics?
- Relation to WGAN: Your analysis motivates Wasserstein-type reasoning. How do your smoothing-based updates compare empirically and theoretically to enforcing Lipschitz constraints (e.g., via clipping or penalties)?
- Assumptions: In realistic image models, supports are not exactly manifolds. Which conclusions (e.g., perfect discrimination, maxed-out JSD) still hold approximately, and which require exact manifold assumptions?
- Bound constants: In Theorem 3.3, how should practitioners estimate or upper-bound C and V during training to monitor or control W(Pr,Pg)?
- Evaluation: Do you propose JSD between smoothed distributions as a practical metric? If so, how should σ be chosen for fair comparison across models and datasets?

Overall, the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution, explains key empirical phenomena, and proposes a principled stabilization approach. I recommend acceptance.
