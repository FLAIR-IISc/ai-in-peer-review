This paper makes a valuable contribution by clarifying the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs) and their variants. Building on new insights into these dynamics, the authors explain why gradients tend to vanish in the original GAN and become unstable in certain variants. More importantly, they propose introducing perturbations to mitigate these issues. I expect this work to inspire more principled research in this direction.

I am particularly interested in the proposed perturbation technique for avoiding gradient vanishing and instability. This approach appears closely related to dropout, where the perturbation can be viewed as sampling from a Bernoulli distribution. It would strengthen the paper to explicitly discuss this connection: in what sense is the proposed perturbation equivalent to, a generalization of, or fundamentally different from dropout? For example, does it act on inputs, features, parameters, or gradients, and how does that choice affect the training dynamics?

In addition to the theoretical analysis, is there empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of this perturbation technique? I would appreciate experiments analogous to Figs. 2 and 3 that directly compare the proposed perturbed GAN against the original methods under the same setups. Such results would help validate the theoretical claims, illustrate practical impact, and clarify any trade-offs (e.g., convergence speed, stability across architectures or datasets, and sensitivity to the magnitude or distribution of the perturbation).

Overall, the paper provides useful theoretical insight into why gradients vanish in the original GAN and become unstable in its variants, and offers a principled perturbation-based remedy. Clarifying the relationship to dropout and adding empirical comparisons (e.g., versions of Figs. 2 and 3 for the perturbed GAN) would substantially strengthen the paper.
