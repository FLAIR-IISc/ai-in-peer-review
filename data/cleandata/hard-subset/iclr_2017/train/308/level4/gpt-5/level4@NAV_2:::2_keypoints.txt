- Paper gives a clear theoretical account that GAN training is difficult because data and generator on low-dimensional/disjoint manifolds make optimal discriminators perfect and locally flat, causing generator gradients to vanish or become unstable.
- Identifies two failure modes: vanishing gradients under the original GAN objective and heavy-tailed, high-variance updates under the non-saturating (−log D) objective.
- Proposes injecting continuous noise (smoothing/convolution of distributions) so smoothed distributions overlap, producing well-behaved divergences and stable, informative gradients.
- Develops analytic tools and bounds (e.g., relating Wasserstein distance to JSD of smoothed distributions plus noise-variance terms) to justify the perturbation approach and annealing.
- Uses targeted experiments to validate assumptions and illustrate the theorized phenomena.
- Decision: Accept — the paper offers a compelling theoretical explanation of core GAN pathologies and a principled perturbation-based mitigation likely to spur further work.
- Supporting argument: Optimal discriminators being perfect and flat explains why improving discriminators can worsen generator updates (vanishing gradients).
- Supporting argument: Noisy discriminator approximations induce heavy-tailed (Cauchy) gradient components with infinite variance, explaining observed instability even with good discriminators.
- Supporting argument: Smoothing makes the discriminator objective meaningful again, yields stable directional gradients, and allows near-optimal discriminator training without harming generator updates.
- Supporting argument: The provided bounds justify annealing noise while still targeting the true unsmoothed distributions.
- Additional feedback: Discuss relationship to dropout/Bernoulli-style noise and compare effects of different noise types/locations on implied divergences and gradient fields.
- Additional feedback: Add empirical validation of the perturbation trick (e.g., gradient norms, variance, and sample quality as discriminator improves; ablations over noise scale and distribution).
- Additional feedback: Provide implementation specifics for reproducibility — where to inject noise, whether to perturb real and fake samples, annealing schedules, and sensitivity to noise covariance.
- Questions: Do you add noise to real and generated samples and to discriminator and/or generator pathways — which configuration yields the claimed smooth gradient in practice?
- Questions: Which noise distributions and covariance structures work best empirically, and how should noise variance be set and annealed?
- Questions: Can you provide experiments mirroring Fig.2/3 under perturbation to show reduced gradient vanishing/instability and improved sample quality?
- Questions: Does the perturbation strategy measurably reduce mode collapse in practice, and can this be quantified on standard benchmarks?
