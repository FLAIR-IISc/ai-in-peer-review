Summary of contributions
The paper develops a rigorous theoretical account of why standard GAN training is fragile and often fails. The authors prove that, under realistic assumptions, (i) the generator and data distributions concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint or nearly disjoint support, (ii) an optimal discriminator is essentially perfect and locally flat on these manifolds, and hence (iii) generator gradients either vanish (with the original saturating loss) or become highly unstable (with the non-saturating −log D loss). They formalize the latter effect by showing heavy-tailed, ill-conditioned gradient estimates under small discriminator noise. To address these issues, the paper proposes injecting noise to both real and generated samples (“instance noise”) so that the convolved distributions become absolutely continuous and overlap, yielding smooth gradients and a meaningful Jensen–Shannon signal. The authors further relate the JSD between noisy distributions to the Wasserstein distance between the original ones, and provide a training-time–annealing perspective with accompanying bounds. The work offers clear mathematical tools (manifold arguments, smooth separation via Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, and Wasserstein–JSD bounds) to analyze GAN training dynamics and suggests a principled stabilization mechanism.

Decision
Accept. The paper offers a compelling, well-motivated theoretical explanation for two core failure modes of GANs and proposes a simple, principled remedy (additive noise with annealing) supported by clean analysis and targeted diagnostics.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and clarity: The paper tackles a fundamental open question—why discriminator improvement can degrade generator updates. The manifold-support perspective and perfect discrimination results precisely explain saturation and instability, matching widespread empirical observations.

- Soundness and novelty: Theorems 2.1–2.4 formalize perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients for the saturating loss; Theorems 2.5–2.6 identify the effective divergence optimized by the non-saturating loss and demonstrate heavy-tailed, unstable updates in the presence of small discriminator noise. Section 3 shows that instance noise yields well-behaved gradients and connects JSD of convolved distributions to Wasserstein distance of the original ones (Theorem 3.3). These are insightful and, to my knowledge, novel combinations of ideas with careful proofs.

- Position in the literature: The work is well situated with respect to GANs, likelihood-based models, and divergences (KL, JSD). The connection to Earth Mover/Wasserstein distance is timely and illuminates why “softer” metrics can behave better when supports do not overlap. The analysis complements prior empirical heuristics by providing a principled foundation.

- Empirical support: The targeted experiments substantiating vanishing gradients and gradient explosion (Figures 2 and 3) appropriately validate key assumptions and claims. While limited, they are well aligned with the theoretical narrative.

Areas for improvement (non-blocking, intended to help strengthen the paper)
- Empirical validation of instance noise: The proposed stabilization via additive noise is theoretically compelling. However, the paper would benefit from experiments demonstrating that (a) training with instance noise (and annealing) stabilizes optimization without degrading sample quality, and (b) ablations on the noise schedule and magnitude. Ideally, include plots analogous to Figures 2 and 3 for the noisy setting.

- Clarify assumptions and scope:
  • The independence/white-noise model in Theorem 2.6 is strong. Discuss whether the heavy-tailed conclusion persists under more realistic, correlated or bounded-variance discriminator errors.
  • Explicitly state compactness/smoothness assumptions and the ambient space for Urysohn’s lemma (e.g., Rd) early in Section 2 for readability.
  • In Lemma 1, briefly summarize the key regularity used from the network architecture (piecewise-linear or smooth monotone activations) and note common exceptions, if any.

- Notation and minor correctness:
  • In Theorem 2.5, double-check the expression of D* and the sign/interpretation of the derived objective; a concise derivation in the main text would aid readers.
  • Define the norm ‖D‖ precisely when first introduced, and ensure consistent use of subscripts/superscripts (e.g., gradients with respect to x vs. θ).
  • Fix typographical errors (e.g., “arize,” “symetrical,” “aboslutely”) and tidy the references.

- Practical guidance:
  • Provide a simple recipe for instance-noise training (choice of noise distribution, variance schedule, and when to stop annealing).
  • Discuss the trade-off between bias (from noise) and variance/stability, and relate it directly to the bound in Theorem 3.3.

- Relation to related regularizers: Briefly discuss connections to dropout or other perturbation-based regularizers (e.g., Gaussian input noise, label smoothing). Highlight similarities and key differences in objectives and effects on the discriminator landscape.

Questions for the authors
- Instance noise experiments: Can you include empirical results showing that adding Gaussian noise to inputs of both D and G (with annealing) stabilizes training and improves sample quality and mode coverage? Plots mirroring Figures 2 and 3 for the noisy vs. noiseless cases would be very helpful.

- Noise schedule: How sensitive is performance to the initial variance and annealing rate? Do you have a heuristic or theory-guided rule (e.g., aligning the first term in Theorem 3.3 with the decreasing JSD term)?

- Practical implementation: Should noise be added to both real and generated samples at all times? Is it preferable to add noise inside the network (feature space) or strictly at the input? Any observations on covariance structure (isotropic vs. anisotropic) and its effect on convergence?

- Robustness of Theorem 2.6: Can you relax the independence/white-noise assumptions and still obtain heavy-tailed (or at least high-variance) update behavior? Even a sketch or simulation under correlated noise would strengthen the claim.

- Objective in Theorem 2.5: The derived gradient corresponds to KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr). Could you clarify the implications for mode dropping in a small example, and reconcile with the empirical success of non-saturating loss in practice?

- Relation to dropout: Since dropout can be viewed as a Bernoulli perturbation, do you expect analogous benefits to instance noise? Are there pitfalls (e.g., discrete noise failing to ensure absolute continuity) that make Gaussian noise preferable?

- Beyond Gaussian noise: Do other noise distributions (e.g., Laplace, Cauchy, or data-dependent covariances) offer advantages? Any empirical comparisons?

Overall, this is a strong and insightful paper that advances our theoretical understanding of GANs and suggests a principled stabilization mechanism. Strengthening the empirical section around instance-noise training would significantly increase the paper’s impact.
