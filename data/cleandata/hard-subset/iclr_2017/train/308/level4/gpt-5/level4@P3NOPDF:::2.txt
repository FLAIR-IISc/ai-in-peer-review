Summary
This paper provides a clear and useful analysis of the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs). It explains why gradients tend to vanish under the original GAN objective and why they can become unstable under certain commonly used variants. Building on this insight, the authors propose a perturbation-based technique to mitigate vanishing and unstable gradients. The work has the potential to inspire more principled approaches to stabilizing GAN training.

Strengths
- Theoretical insight: The analysis of gradient behavior across different GAN objectives is well motivated and addresses a core challenge in GAN training. The explanation for when and why gradients vanish or become unstable is both intuitive and technically grounded.
- Practical relevance: The proposed perturbation mechanism is simple and potentially widely applicable, providing a concrete avenue to improve training stability without major architectural changes.
- Clarity of contribution: The paper makes a distinct conceptual contribution (diagnosing gradient pathologies) and couples it with a pragmatic remedy (perturbation), which strengthens its impact.

Concerns and Questions
- Scope and assumptions: It would help to more clearly state the assumptions under which the theoretical results hold (e.g., properties of the discriminator class, distributional support mismatch, smoothness/regularity conditions). Are there regimes where the analysis does not apply or where perturbation could degrade performance?
- Nature of perturbation: The paper would benefit from a more explicit description of the perturbation mechanism. Is the perturbation applied to inputs, intermediate features, logits, parameters, or gradients? To the generator, discriminator, or both? Is the perturbation additive, multiplicative, or masking-based?
- Connection to existing techniques: The proposed method appears closely related to noise injection and dropout-style regularization. In particular, a Bernoulli perturbation resembles dropout, while additive Gaussian noise is a common regularizer. Clarifying similarities and differences—especially in terms of biasing the objective, affecting the discriminator’s Lipschitz properties, or alleviating support mismatch—would situate the contribution more clearly within prior work.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the magnitude, distribution, and schedule of the perturbation? Is an annealing strategy beneficial? Providing guidance here would increase practical utility.
- Empirical validation: While the theoretical analysis is compelling, the practical value of the perturbation trick should be substantiated empirically. In particular, it would be helpful to see the same diagnostic plots as in Figures 2 and 3 replicated for the perturbed setup, allowing an apples-to-apples comparison of gradient norms, stability indicators, or divergence proxies.

Suggestions for Revision
- Expand the description of the perturbation mechanism:
  - Precisely define the perturbation distribution(s) considered (e.g., Bernoulli/dropout, Gaussian, uniform).
  - Specify where and when perturbations are applied in the training loop and whether they differ for generator and discriminator.
  - Discuss theoretical implications: does perturbation change the effective objective, and if so, in what way (e.g., smoothing the discriminator landscape, addressing support mismatch)?
- Empirical section:
  - Add experiments that mirror Figures 2 and 3 for the perturbed GAN to directly demonstrate improvements in gradient magnitude and stability.
  - Include ablations on perturbation type, magnitude, and scheduling. Show training curves (losses, gradient norms) and qualitative samples; if applicable, include standard metrics used in the paper.
  - Compare against simple stabilization baselines (e.g., label smoothing or feature matching, if those are in scope) to contextualize gains.
  - Report computational overhead and any changes in convergence speed.
- Clarify generality:
  - Indicate whether the method applies across architectures and datasets and whether it interacts with common components (e.g., batch normalization) in nontrivial ways.
  - Discuss potential failure cases where perturbation may be detrimental (e.g., excessive noise harming generator signal).
- Relate to dropout and noise injection:
  - Add a discussion that frames the perturbation trick relative to dropout (Bernoulli noise) and other noise-injection strategies. If feasible, include a small experiment comparing Bernoulli vs Gaussian perturbations to test whether the effect is distribution-agnostic or specific.

Overall Evaluation
This paper offers a valuable and timely contribution by diagnosing fundamental issues in GAN training and proposing a simple, principled mitigation via perturbation. The theoretical insights are compelling, and the proposed remedy is attractive in its simplicity. To strengthen the paper, I encourage the authors to more explicitly define the perturbation mechanism, situate it relative to dropout/noise-injection methods, and provide empirical evidence—ideally reproducing key diagnostic figures for the perturbed case and adding targeted ablations. With these additions, the work would present a well-rounded and convincing case for the utility of the perturbation trick and its broader implications for stable GAN training.
