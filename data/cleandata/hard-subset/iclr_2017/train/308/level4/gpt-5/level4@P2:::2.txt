Summary
This paper offers a principled theoretical analysis of why GAN training is often unstable. The authors formalize the observation that, when real and model distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds with (nearly) disjoint support, an optimal discriminator exists that is almost everywhere constant on each manifold. They show this leads to vanishing gradients for the original GAN loss, and unstable, high-variance updates for the non-saturating −log D generator loss under realistic discriminator approximation noise. They then propose a theoretically grounded remedy: inject continuous noise (perturbations) into both discriminator and generator inputs, which “thickens” the supports and yields smooth, meaningful gradients. They derive gradient forms that move samples toward high-density regions of the data manifold while repelling overrepresented model regions, and they connect the Jensen–Shannon divergence between the noisy distributions to the Wasserstein distance between the original (noiseless) distributions, providing a bound and an annealing strategy. Targeted experiments diagnose the vanishing/instability phenomena and support the analysis.

Decision: Accept
Reasons:
- Strong, clear theoretical diagnosis of GAN training pathologies (vanishing gradients and instability), with novel and rigorous results that align with empirical observations.
- A principled, simple remedy (instance-like noise) with theoretical guarantees, including an interpretable gradient form and a bound linking noisy JSD minimization to the true Wasserstein distance between the original distributions.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles why GAN training deteriorates as the discriminator improves and why the standard loss formulations fail. This is a central, timely question, well positioned in the literature and of direct practical importance.
- Theoretical contributions: The “perfect discrimination” theorems under manifold support and non-alignment are compelling. The results on vanishing gradients for the original loss and the Cauchy-distributed, infinite-variance updates for the −log D variant (under noisy discriminator approximation) are insightful and technically novel. The noisy-input analysis is well motivated, and the bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) offers a clean link between a trainable proxy objective and a meaningful distance between original distributions.
- Empirical support: The figures diagnosing gradient vanishing/explosion trends substantiate the theoretical claims. Although the empirical evaluation is intentionally targeted rather than extensive, it is appropriate to the paper’s goals.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not required for my acceptance decision)
- Empirical validation of the perturbation approach: Please add experiments demonstrating that training with noise on both discriminator and generator stabilizes learning and improves sample quality and mode coverage. Suggested comparisons: baseline GAN vs. perturbed GAN; metrics such as sample fidelity and mode diversity; plots of gradient norms/variance over training; and ablations over noise variance and annealing schedules.
- Noise design and schedules: Elaborate on how to choose the noise distribution (Gaussian vs. other decays), covariance structure, and annealing schedule. Empirically explore sensitivity to these choices and provide practical guidelines (e.g., start/stop variances, schedule shape).
- Relation to dropout: The proposed perturbation resembles dropout conceptually (Bernoulli-type noise). Please discuss connections and differences: does Bernoulli/multiplicative noise satisfy the assumptions and yield similar smoothing? Are there theoretical or empirical reasons to prefer additive Gaussian noise?
- Where to inject noise: You argue for adding noise to both discriminator and generator pathways. Please quantify the effect of (i) noise on D only, (ii) noise on G only, and (iii) both, to verify the necessity of each component and to illustrate the adversarial-example argument.
- Practical considerations: Clarify computational overhead and interactions with common architectural choices (e.g., batch normalization, convolutional layers). Provide guidance for finite-capacity discriminators and the approximation norm assumptions used in proofs.
- Notation and presentation: The paper is generally clear, but a minor pass to consolidate notation (e.g., consistently denote noisy variables) and to fix typos would improve readability. The reference list needs formatting cleanup.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide empirical results for the perturbation method analogous to Figures 2 and 3, showing stabilized gradient magnitudes/variance and improved sample quality?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of noise distribution (Gaussian vs. heavy-tailed) and covariance? Does anisotropic noise help in high-dimensional image spaces?
- What annealing schedules work best in practice? Can you propose an adaptive schedule based on discriminator accuracy or an estimate of JSD(Pr+ || Pg+)?
- Do the guarantees require noise on both G and D, or is D-only noise sufficient in practice? How does each choice affect susceptibility to adversarial directions?
- The bound involves the support diameter C. How restrictive is this assumption in common image datasets, and how should one interpret or estimate C in practice?
- Can you report quantitative metrics (e.g., precision/recall for generative models) to assess both fidelity and mode coverage under your perturbation scheme?

Overall, the paper provides valuable theoretical clarity and a practical path toward stabilizing GANs. Adding empirical validation of the perturbation approach would make the contribution even stronger.
