- Paper proves GAN failure modes: generator/data concentrate on low-dimensional, (nearly) disjoint manifolds, causing perfect discriminators and broken gradients.
- Shows saturating loss leads to vanishing gradients and non-saturating loss yields highly unstable, heavy-tailed gradient estimates under small discriminator noise.
- Formalizes instability via heavy-tailed, ill-conditioned gradient estimates and uses manifold and measure-theoretic tools in the analysis.
- Proposes instance noise (additive noise to real and generated samples) so convolved distributions overlap, producing smooth gradients and meaningful JSD signals.
- Relates JSD of noisy distributions to the Wasserstein distance of original distributions and gives an annealing-based training perspective with bounds.
- Provides rigorous mathematical tools (Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, manifold arguments, Wasserstein–JSD bounds) to analyze GAN dynamics.
- Decision: Accept — compelling theoretical explanation and a principled, simple remedy with clean analysis and diagnostics.
- Significance: Addresses why discriminator improvement can harm generator updates with a clear manifold-support perspective matching empirical observations.
- Soundness/novelty: Theorems 2.1–2.6 and Section 3 offer novel, carefully proved insights on perfect discrimination, vanishing/unstable gradients, and benefits of instance noise.
- Positioning: Well situated relative to GAN/divergence literature; timely connection to Earth Mover/Wasserstein distances.
- Empirical support: Targeted experiments validate key claims about vanishing gradients and gradient explosion, though limited in scope.
- Improvement — empirical validation: Add experiments showing instance-noise training stabilizes optimization and preserves/improves sample quality and mode coverage.
- Improvement — ablations: Include ablations on noise schedule and magnitude and plots analogous to Figures 2 and 3 for noisy settings.
- Improvement — clarify assumptions: Discuss strength of independence/white-noise in Theorem 2.6 and whether conclusions hold under correlated or bounded-variance errors.
- Improvement — explicit assumptions: State compactness/smoothness and ambient space (e.g., R^d) early for readability and provenance of Urysohn’s lemma.
- Improvement — network regularity: Summarize regularity requirements from network architecture in Lemma 1 and note common exceptions.
- Improvement — notation/corrections: Check D* expression in Theorem 2.5, define norm ‖D‖, ensure consistent subscripts/superscripts, and fix typographical errors.
- Improvement — practical guidance: Provide a concrete recipe for instance-noise training (noise distribution, variance schedule, stopping criterion) and discuss bias–variance trade-off relative to Theorem 3.3.
- Improvement — relate to regularizers: Discuss connections/differences to dropout and other perturbation-based regularizers (Gaussian input noise, label smoothing).
- Question — instance noise experiments: Can authors include Gaussian-noise training results (with annealing) showing improved stability, quality, and mode coverage?
- Question — noise schedule sensitivity: How sensitive are results to initial variance and annealing rate; any heuristic or theoretical rule?
- Question — practical implementation: Should noise be added to both real and generated samples always; input vs. feature-space injection; isotropic vs. anisotropic covariance effects?
- Question — robustness of Theorem 2.6: Can independence/white-noise assumptions be relaxed and do heavy-tailed/high-variance updates persist under correlated noise?
- Question — Theorem 2.5 implications: Clarify the derived objective KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr) and implications for mode dropping with small illustrative examples.
- Question — relation to dropout and discrete noise: Would Bernoulli/dropout-like perturbations yield similar benefits, or is absolute continuity via Gaussian noise necessary?
- Question — other noise distributions: Do Laplace, Cauchy, or data-dependent covariances offer advantages; any empirical comparisons?
