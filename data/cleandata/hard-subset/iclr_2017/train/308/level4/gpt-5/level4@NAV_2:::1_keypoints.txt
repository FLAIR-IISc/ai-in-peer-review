- Rigorous theoretical study explaining GAN instability and generator gradient vanishing/exploding due to low-dimensional supports.
- Shows optimal discriminators become perfect but locally flat on supports, which undermines gradient-based generator learning.
- Proves vanishing generator gradients for the original GAN objective (Theorem 2.4).
- Characterizes the implicit objective optimized by −log D and explains its tendency toward good-looking samples with mode dropping (Theorem 2.5).
- Demonstrates that −log D induces heavy-tailed, high-variance update noise causing intrinsic instability (Theorem 2.6).
- Proposes adding input noise to produce absolutely continuous (“smoothed”) distributions and derives the resulting generator gradients (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2).
- Provides a Wasserstein–JSD bound linking original distributions to their noisy counterparts, enabling geometry-aware training and an annealing path (Lemma 4, Theorem 3.3).
- Targeted experiments qualitatively corroborate theoretical phenomena (discriminator saturation, gradient norms and variability).
- Decision: Accept — paper offers novel, rigorous insights and a principled remedy via noise with concrete guarantees.
- Strong motivation and placement relative to literature on divergences, supports, and GAN training pathologies.
- Correctness and rigor: key results on perfect discrimination on manifolds and consequences are established under realistic assumptions (Theorems 2.1–2.3).
- Theorem-suite cohesively explains practical behaviors: saturation, vanishing gradients, implicit −log D objective, and heavy-tailed updates.
- Remedy in Section 3 yields stable, geometry-aware gradients and a principled annealing strategy to bridge smoothed training to original distributions.
- Empirical alignment: experiments illustrate predicted behaviors though are targeted rather than broad.
- Novelty and impact: among the first comprehensive theoretical treatments of GAN stability with useful mathematical techniques.
- Suggestion: relax or discuss the strong independence assumption in Theorem 2.6 (value vs. gradient errors) and robustness to correlated/structured noise.
- Suggestion: provide practical guidance and sensitivity analyses for selecting noise distributions and annealing schedules (σ, decay, isotropic vs. anisotropic).
- Suggestion: expand experiments to diverse architectures/datasets and demonstrate end-to-end training following the noisy-D + anneal recipe.
- Suggestion: discuss realism and sensitivity of manifold assumptions (e.g., “no perfect alignment”, boundary-free) in common data domains.
- Suggestion: highlight bounded-support assumption and discuss extensions to sub-Gaussian/unbounded tails and tightness of constants in Theorem 3.3.
- Suggestion: improve exposition and unify notation (densities vs. measures, “continuous” vs. “absolutely continuous”), and consolidate a(z), b(z) intuition.
- Question: clarify regularity conditions for exchanging differentiation and integration in Theorem 2.5 and assumptions on gθ (Lipschitz, bounded Jacobian).
- Question: how sensitive is the Cauchy/heavy-tail conclusion to the independence assumption; any empirical tail-index measurements under realistic noise?
- Question: practical criteria for choosing σ and annealing schedule; comparisons of Gaussian vs. other kernels (Laplace, Cauchy) for stability and quality?
- Question: does adding generator noise improve discriminator robustness to generator-induced adversarial examples; any empirical diagnostics?
- Question: can Theorem 3.3 be adapted to unbounded supports via tail conditions (e.g., sub-Gaussian) and how tight is the √JSD dependence in practice?
- Question: provide plots/intuitions for a(z), b(z) showing how they modulate attraction/repulsion as Pr+ and Pg+ imbalance and how this stabilizes equilibrium.
- Question: have you tested the full “train D to optimality under noise + anneal” recipe on modern conv architectures with quantitative metrics (e.g., FID)?
