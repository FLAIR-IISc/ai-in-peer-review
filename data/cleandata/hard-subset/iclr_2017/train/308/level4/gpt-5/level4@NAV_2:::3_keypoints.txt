- Proves that when Pr and Pg lie on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is (almost everywhere) constant, causing vanishing or uninformative generator gradients under the original GAN loss.
- Shows the non-saturating generator update asymptotically optimizes KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2·JSD, explaining sharp samples and mode collapse.
- Demonstrates that noisy approximations to the optimal discriminator can produce heavy-tailed, high-variance generator updates causing instability.
- Proposes smoothing distributions by adding noise, derives well-behaved gradients for the generator, and connects this smoothing to Wasserstein distance via bounds relating W(Pr,Pg) to noise variance and JSD of smoothed distributions.
- Combines analysis and differential topology tools with experiments to verify assumptions and illustrate predicted phenomena.
- Decision: Accept — paper gives clear theoretical explanations for GAN pathologies and well-justified remedies likely to influence theory and practice.
- Tackles failure modes of GAN training (instability, saturation, degradation of generator updates) and seeks metrics/procedures for stable, informative gradients on low-dimensional or nearly disjoint supports.
- Approach is well motivated and grounded in prior work, relating divergences, smoothing, and optimal transport in a coherent framework.
- Claims are supported by precise theorems (Theorems 2.1–2.6, 3.1–3.3 and corollaries) and empirical checks that substantiate theoretical predictions about gradient behavior.
- Suggests streamlining exposition: move some proofs (e.g., Section 2.1) to appendix and improve theorem/lemma numbering and cross-references.
- Clarify assumptions: restate p(z) in Theorem 2.4, specify domains/regularity for random variables, and clarify manifold boundary assumptions in Theorem 2.2.
- Note Lemma 1 may hold for broader activation classes—state the maximal class assumed or add a remark on generality.
- Point out the white-noise independence assumption in Theorem 2.6 is idealized; discuss more realistic correlated/noisy discriminator approximations and their effect on instability.
- Recommend adding references for standard analysis/topology results (Urysohn, transversality, Sard’s lemma) to replace or shorten some proofs.
- Minor wording and typo fixes suggested (several specific phrase and reference corrections).
- Question: Are “closed manifolds” allowed to have boundary in Theorem 2.2, and how are boundary points treated in transversality/perfect alignment arguments?
- Question: How sensitive are perfect-discrimination conclusions to mild violations of manifold assumptions (e.g., small absolutely continuous components)?
- Question: What practical annealing schedule for noise variance σ is recommended to balance the V1/2 term and JSD term in Theorem 3.3, and when to reduce σ safely?
- Question: How sensitive are results to the noise family/covariance choice (isotropic vs anisotropic Gaussian, heavy-tailed noise) and are there observed practical differences?
- Question: Can the mitigation from backpropagating through noisy generator outputs be quantified empirically (e.g., adversarial susceptibility vs σ)?
- Question: Can Theorem 2.6’s white-noise error model be relaxed to correlated, bounded-variance approximation errors to better model finite-capacity discriminators?
- Question: Can Lemma 1 extend to broader function classes (e.g., residual connections, non-monotone activations) without changing conclusions?
- Question: Do results suggest concrete architectural or regularization choices (beyond noise) to increase support overlap early in training (smoothing layers, data augmentation, generator priors)?
- Request: Add quantitative empirical metrics (gradient norm vs discriminator accuracy) and ablation studies across architectures/datasets to strengthen empirical validation.
