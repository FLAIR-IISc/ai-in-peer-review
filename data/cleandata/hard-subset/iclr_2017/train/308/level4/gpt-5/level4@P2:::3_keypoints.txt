- Shows that when Pr and Pg lie on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds the optimal discriminator is (almost everywhere) constant on supports, causing saturated divergences (JSD = log 2; KLs infinite) and vanishing generator gradients under the original GAN objective.  
- Proves that the −log D generator update, with discriminator noise, yields heavy-tailed (Cauchy) update distributions, explaining observed instability and variance spikes.  
- Proposes adding noise to both real and generated samples to make smoothed distributions absolutely continuous, producing stable, informative gradients.  
- Derives bounds connecting JSD on smoothed distributions to the Wasserstein distance between original distributions, motivating annealing the noise during training.  
- Uses analysis and differential topology to formalize why perfect discrimination emerges and to explain the geometric origin of GAN pathologies.  
- Provides targeted experiments that match and support the theoretical predictions (gradient norms, vanishing gradients as discriminators improve).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Strong, well-motivated theoretical analysis identifying concrete failure modes of standard GAN training and explaining common empirical observations.  
- Key reason 2: Principled and practical mitigation (smoothing + annealing) backed by theory and experiments.  
- Problem significance: Tackles core GAN instabilities important for generative modeling progress.  
- Placement and motivation: Well-situated in literature, contrasting KL-based ML, original GAN (JSD), and −log D, and explaining failures when supports are low-dimensional.  
- Correctness and rigor: Theorems on perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients, and noisy discriminator instability follow from clear assumptions; smoothing analysis and Wasserstein bound are solid.  
- Empirical support: Experiments align with theoretical claims, increasing confidence despite being secondary to theory.
