- Rigorous theory showing original GAN objective yields vanishing generator gradients when the discriminator is strong (Theorem 2.4).  
- Analysis showing the −log D generator update can produce unstable, high-variance updates (Theorem 2.6).  
- Clarification of what the −log D update actually optimizes, explaining mode-dropping behavior (Theorem 2.5).  
- Formalization of the manifold-support viewpoint: Pr and Pg on low-dimensional manifolds cause perfect discrimination and divergence saturation (Theorems 2.1–2.3).  
- Proposal to inject continuous noise so the discriminator sees smoothed, absolutely continuous distributions, restoring usable gradients (Theorem 3.1, Corollary 3.2).  
- Connection between smoothing and Wasserstein distance: bound on W(Pr,Pg) in terms of noise variance and JSD of smoothed distributions (Theorem 3.3).  
- Theorems (notably 2.4–2.6 and 3.1–3.3) are novel and employ interesting mathematical tools.  
- Targeted experiments effectively illustrate vanishing gradients and exploding gradient variance as discriminator quality improves.  
- Decision: Accept — motivated by strong, timely theoretical contributions and a provable, practical stabilization strategy.  
- Importance: Addresses core GAN instability issues relevant to image generation, semi-supervised learning, and sequence modeling.  
- Positioning: Well situated in literature, explaining failures of KL/JSD minimization on manifold-supported distributions and practical implications of generator objective choice.  
- Rigor and correctness: Mathematical development is careful and persuasive; manifold-support and gradient analyses align with common architectures.  
- Empirical support: Experiments are appropriate and validate the main theoretical claims.  
- Suggestion: Clarify strong assumptions in Theorem 2.6 (white-noise and independence) and discuss effects of correlated/structured approximation errors.  
- Suggestion: Provide a more explicit, step-by-step derivation for Theorem 2.5 including regularity assumptions for interchanging gradients and expectations.  
- Suggestion: Add practical guidance on noise schedules (initial variance, annealing), covariance structure, and update balancing for practitioners.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly state that common convolutional architectures fit the “affine + pointwise nonlinearity” framework to justify Lemma 1’s scope.  
- Suggestion: Include more experimental details (architectures, datasets, hyperparameters) and ablations on noise variance/covariance.  
- Question: Provide full proof for Theorem 2.5 and conditions allowing gradients to pass through the optimal discriminator D*.  
- Question: How sensitive are Theorem 2.6’s instability conclusions to relaxing independence/white-noise assumptions; any empirical/theoretical evidence for correlated errors?  
- Question: Which noise annealing schedules and isotropic vs. anisotropic choices worked best, and does injecting noise into both D and G outperform injecting only into D?  
- Question: How conservative is Theorem 3.3’s bound depending on support diameter C, and can tighter problem-dependent constants be derived?  
- Question: Do results change if generator support is a union of manifolds with varying dimensions or with boundaries, and are there diagnostics for the “no perfect alignment” condition?  
- Question: Beyond gradient norms, does noise injection improve sample quality or reduce mode dropping across datasets and architectures?
