Summary
This paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs), focusing on instability and gradient saturation. The authors show that when the real and generated distributions lie on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds (a realistic setting for modern generators), the optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant almost everywhere on the supports. They prove that, under these conditions, gradients passed to the generator either vanish for the original GAN loss or become highly unstable when using the common −log D surrogate. They then propose adding continuous noise to both inputs and generator outputs to obtain well-behaved, continuous distributions whose Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD) can be optimized with stable gradients. Importantly, they connect this noisy JSD to the Wasserstein distance between the original (noiseless) distributions, providing a bound that justifies annealing the noise. The theorems are complemented by targeted experiments that empirically illustrate the predicted behaviors. Overall, the paper provides valuable tools and insights for understanding and stabilizing GAN training.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper offers novel, rigorous theoretical results that explain well-known empirical issues in GAN training (instability, saturation, and mode dropping), and it proposes a principled remedy via noise smoothing with a clear optimization objective.
- The analysis introduces tools (e.g., theorems on perfect discrimination, vanishing/unstable gradients, and a Wasserstein bound) that advance our understanding of GAN dynamics and are likely to influence subsequent research and practice.

Supporting arguments
- Theorems 2.1–2.3 establish that, when supports are disjoint or lie on non-aligned low-dimensional manifolds, a smooth discriminator can achieve perfect accuracy while being locally constant almost everywhere on the supports, which implies saturated signals for the generator. This formalizes a common but previously informal intuition.
- Theorem 2.4 shows that the generator’s gradient vanishes as the discriminator approaches optimality under the original GAN objective, explaining why training can stall when the discriminator is strong.
- Theorem 2.5 identifies the effective objective optimized by the −log D generator update: KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr), clarifying why this update encourages realistic samples while tolerating mode dropping. This is a notable contribution to understanding the empirical behavior of stabilized GANs.
- Theorem 2.6 demonstrates that noisy approximations of the optimal discriminator can induce generator updates with Cauchy-distributed components (infinite variance), theoretically grounding the observed instability of the −log D update when the discriminator is near-optimal but imperfect.
- Section 3 introduces noise injection to obtain continuous smoothed distributions (Pr+ and Pg+), leading to stable gradients (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2) that move samples toward the data manifold while discouraging overly likely generated modes, with a clear interpretation akin to contrastive forces.
- Theorem 3.3 provides a valuable bound linking the Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions to the JSD between their noisy versions plus a variance term, justifying an annealing strategy and enabling principled training and evaluation.
- The results are well-motivated, well-placed in the literature, and empirically supported by targeted experiments that illustrate vanishing/unstable gradients and improved behavior with noise.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not central to the decision)
- Assumptions and scope:
  - Clarify regularity assumptions in Lemma 1 (e.g., handling of non-strictly increasing activations like ReLU at zero, and architectures with skip connections or normalization layers).
  - In Theorem 2.1, discuss the role of compactness and whether the result extends under weaker conditions (e.g., closed sets with appropriate separation).
  - Explicitly distinguish continuity vs absolute continuity, and restate where each is required.
- Approximation and noise modeling:
  - The independence/whiteness assumptions for D∗−D and ∇xD∗−∇xD in Theorem 2.6 are strong; discuss more realistic correlated error models and whether similar heavy-tailed behavior persists.
  - Provide practical guidance on noise choice (Gaussian vs other tails), covariance shaping, and annealing schedules, including ablations demonstrating sensitivity.
- Empirical support:
  - Expand experimental results to compare: original loss vs −log D vs noisy variants (noise on discriminator only vs both discriminator and generator), and report metrics tied to the theory (e.g., proxy JSD between noisy distributions, Wasserstein estimates).
  - Include sensitivity to latent dimensionality, data manifold complexity, and discriminator capacity.
- Presentation:
  - Improve notational consistency (e.g., consistent use of densities vs measures; clear definitions for norms and spaces).
  - Fix minor typos and streamline proofs where possible; add more intuition around key steps (e.g., transversality and its practical implications).
  - Briefly discuss connections to evaluation metrics for generative models and how the proposed bounds could inform them.

Questions for the authors
- Theorem 2.5:
  - Could you provide a more detailed derivation, especially around the sign and the role of the mixture distribution? A step-by-step proof or an appendix expansion would help, as this result underpins the interpretation of −log D.
- Theorem 2.6:
  - How sensitive is the Cauchy behavior to the independence/whiteness assumptions? Do similar conclusions hold under correlated noise or finite-precision errors typical in practice?
- Lemma 1:
  - How robust is the manifold image result to common architectural elements (e.g., batch normalization, residual connections) and to piecewise-linear activations at nondifferentiable points?
- Noise design and annealing:
  - What practical criteria guide the initial noise scale and annealing rate? Do you recommend estimating the JSD between Pr+ and Pg+ during training to adapt noise?
  - Beyond Gaussian noise, which heavy-tailed or anisotropic choices have you found beneficial, and how do they affect the induced “distance” weighting in Theorem 3.2?
- Practical optimization:
  - When noise is added to both the discriminator inputs and generator outputs (Corollary 3.2), do you use a reparameterization trick for unbiased gradient estimates? Any variance-reduction techniques recommended?
- Bounds and constants:
  - In Theorem 3.3, can C be tightened or estimated in practice? Are there data-dependent bounds that could make the inequality more actionable?

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution that clarifies fundamental issues in GAN training and proposes a principled path to stabilizing it. I recommend acceptance.
