- Core theoretical insight: when real and model distributions lie on low-dimensional, non-overlapping manifolds, an optimal discriminator is essentially perfect and flat (zero gradient) almost everywhere.  
- Theorems 2.1–2.2: perfect discrimination holds under disjoint or non-transverse manifold intersections.  
- Theorem 2.3: divergences such as JSD saturate at log 2 and KLs become infinite in these settings.  
- Theorem 2.4: original GAN generator loss has vanishing gradients as the discriminator approaches optimality, explaining stalled training.  
- Theorem 2.5: for the −log D generator loss, the gradient equals the derivative of KL(Pg||Pr) − 2·JSD(Pg||Pr), explaining preference for sharp samples and mode dropping.  
- Theorem 2.6: noisy discriminators induce heavy-tailed (coordinate-wise Cauchy) generator updates with infinite variance, explaining instability.  
- Proposed remedy: add continuous "instance noise" to real and generated inputs to smooth distributions and create overlapping supports.  
- Theorem 3.2: with noise, explicit generator gradients move samples toward the data manifold while repelling overly likely model regions.  
- Corollary 3.2: noisy updates correspond to optimizing JSD between the noisy (smoothed) distributions.  
- Theorem 3.3: Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions is bounded by the JSD between noisy distributions plus a term controlled by noise variance, motivating annealed noise schedules.  
- Empirical contribution: experiments illustrate vanishing gradients, heavy tails, and the stabilizing effect of instance noise.  
- Overall claim: coherent theoretical framework linking geometric causes of GAN pathologies to a principled, practical mitigation (instance noise).  
- Decision: Accept — novel, rigorous explanations of GAN instability and a principled remedy with theoretical guarantees.
