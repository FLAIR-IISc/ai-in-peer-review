Summary
This paper tackles important questions about the challenges of training generative adversarial networks (GANs). It examines the consequences of using asymmetric divergences and identifies sources of instability during training, then proposes a smoothing-based alternative to mitigate these issues. The analysis blends tools from analysis and differential topology and offers both theoretical insights and practical guidance.

Strengths
- Strong theoretical framing: the paper poses clear, relevant questions and provides principled, well-motivated answers.
- Innovative use of concepts from analysis and differential topology to study GAN behavior.
- Proposes concrete avenues to reduce instability in GAN training, with Section 3 offering a particularly intuitive and simple solution.

Weaknesses
- The manuscript is on the long and technical side; several arguments could be streamlined and moved to an appendix without loss of clarity.
- Some results appear to be standard in analysis; citing appropriate references could replace full proofs.
- Certain implications and practical consequences would benefit from further development, which is reasonable to leave for future work.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Section 2.1: Consider shortening this section and moving proofs to the appendix to improve readability.
- Section 3: This section presents a clear, intuitive, and simple solution; it effectively connects theory to practice.
- Page 2, second bullet: This also implies that p_g(x) < p_data(x) for some x, which in turn makes the KL divergence nonzero.
- Page 2 wording: Replace “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” with “for generating implausible-looking pictures.”
- Lemma 1: The statement appears to hold under more general conditions; please clarify the minimal assumptions under which it remains valid.
- Theorem 2.1: This seems to be a standard result in analysis; a citation may suffice instead of a full proof.
- Theorem 2.4: Please remind the reader of the definition and assumptions on p(z) at the point of use.
- Lemma 2: Also appears to be standard; consider replacing the proof with a reference.
- Throughout: Specify the domains of all random variables to avoid ambiguity.
- Typo: “relly” -> “rely.”
- Theorem 2.2: Clarify whether the closed manifolds under consideration have boundaries. If “closed” is intended to mean compact and without boundary, please state this explicitly.
- Corollary 2.1: It refers to “assumptions of Theorem 1.3,” which I could not find; please correct the reference.
- Theorem 2.5: Consider replacing “Therefore” with “Then” to improve logical flow if the statement is a direct consequence rather than a concluding remark.
- Theorem 2.6: Correct “Is a...” to “is a...”
- Numbering: The theorem numbering is confusing; please revise for consistency and ease of cross-referencing.
- General clarity: Before major theorems, restate key assumptions and notation (e.g., properties of the generator distribution p_g, the data distribution, and the latent prior p(z)). A short summary box at the start of each subsection listing assumptions could help readers track the logic.
- Practical angle: The smoothing approach is promising; if space permits, adding a brief discussion of how smoothing interacts with commonly used GAN objectives and architectures would strengthen the practical relevance.

Overall assessment
This is a thoughtful and well-motivated paper that addresses foundational issues in GAN training. The theoretical analysis is sound and insightful, and the proposed smoothing approach provides a constructive path to improved stability. Streamlining exposition, clarifying assumptions and references, and tightening the presentation (particularly in Sections 2.x) would significantly enhance readability without diminishing the technical contribution. I view the paper positively and believe it will be of interest to the community.
