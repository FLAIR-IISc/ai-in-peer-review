This paper offers a clear and valuable analysis of the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs). Building on this perspective, the authors explain why the original GAN can suffer from vanishing gradients and why certain variants exhibit gradient instability. More importantly, they propose a perturbation-based remedy that addresses both issues. I expect these insights to stimulate more principled research on GAN optimization.

I am particularly interested in the proposed perturbation mechanism for mitigating gradient vanishing and instability. The idea appears closely related to dropout; one can view the perturbation as sampling from a Bernoulli distribution. Please elaborate on this connection—both conceptually and, if possible, formally—clarifying similarities and differences, such as where the noise is injected, its effect on the objective and gradients, and its impact on training dynamics relative to standard dropout.

Beyond the theoretical analysis, is there empirical evidence demonstrating that the perturbation improves stability and performance? It would significantly strengthen the paper to include experiments evaluating the perturbed GAN alongside the baselines, ideally with plots analogous to Figures 2 and 3. Such comparisons would help quantify practical benefits, sensitivity to the perturbation magnitude and distribution, and any trade-offs in sample quality, convergence speed, or mode coverage.

Overall, the paper delivers useful insights into why GAN training can fail and proposes a promising, principled fix. With a clearer discussion of the relationship to dropout and empirical validation of the perturbation strategy, the contribution would be even more compelling.
