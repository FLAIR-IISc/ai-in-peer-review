- Addresses why GAN training is unstable: vanishing gradients (original loss) and erratic/high-variance gradients (−log D), via geometric analysis of data and model distributions.
- Proves that when Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional manifolds or have disjoint supports, an optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant almost everywhere, causing vanishing generator gradients under the original loss.
- Shows that with the −log D update, noisy discriminator approximations produce Cauchy-distributed gradient coordinates, yielding high variance and instability.
- Proposes smoothing distributions by adding noise, deriving generator gradients and showing backpropagation through noise yields an objective equivalent to JSD between noisy distributions.
- Connects smoothing to Wasserstein distance with a bound: W(Pr,Pg) ≤ noise variance + JSD(Pr+noise, Pg+noise), motivating an annealing strategy.
- Provides targeted experiments (e.g., Figures 2 and 3) validating vanishing gradients and instability as the discriminator approaches optimality.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reasons for accept: clear, rigorous explanations of GAN pathologies under realistic manifold assumptions and a principled, analyzable noise+annealing direction supported by new theoretical tools and bounds.
- Problem significance: tackles a central obstacle in GAN research and is well situated in prior literature; manifold assumptions align with data geometry intuition.
- Theoretical rigor: coherent sequence of results including perfect discrimination theorems, divergence saturation, vanishing gradient bound, Cauchy-instability argument, smoothing to absolute continuity, and the Wasserstein–JSD bound.
- Practical relevance: explains brittleness of discriminator tuning and proposes a perturbation-and-annealing approach that keeps meaningful gradients and bounds true distribution distance.
- Empirical support: demonstrates core phenomena, but lacks direct empirical validation of the perturbation-annealing strategy itself.
- Suggestion: add experiments that evaluate additive noise to both D and G with annealing, comparing to standard GAN and −log D baselines.
- Suggestion: evaluate metrics like gradient norms, loss dynamics, sample quality, diversity (mode dropping), and empirical proxies for Wasserstein/JSD; include visuals analogous to Figs 2/3 under perturbation.
- Suggestion: provide practical guidance on noise type (Gaussian vs others), covariance choices, annealing schedules, and sensitivity to noise scale.
- Suggestion: clarify relation to dropout/Bernoulli perturbation and whether continuous noise is necessary for absolute continuity results.
- Suggestion: discuss robustness of assumptions (e.g., independence in Theorem 2.6), correlated D and ∇D errors, partial support overlap, and approximate manifold settings.

- Question: does Bernoulli/dropout-style perturbation yield similar smoothing benefits, or is continuous noise required for the theoretical guarantees?
