Summary of contributions
- The paper offers a rigorous analysis of why GAN training is difficult. It shows that when Pr and Pg lie on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is (almost everywhere) constant on the supports, causing saturated divergences (JSD at log 2; both KLs infinite) and vanishing gradients for the generator under the original GAN objective. 
- It analyzes the alternative generator update using −log D and proves that, with discriminator noise, the update distribution becomes heavy-tailed (Cauchy), explaining the observed instability and variance spikes.
- To mitigate these issues, the paper proposes adding noise to both real and generated samples, which makes the smoothed distributions absolutely continuous and yields stable, informative gradients. It derives connections between JSD on the smoothed distributions and the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions, providing bounds that motivate annealing the noise while training.
- The work uses tools from analysis and differential topology to formalize why perfect discrimination emerges and to clarify the geometric origin of GAN pathologies. It is accompanied by targeted experiments that match the theoretical predictions.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Strong, well-motivated theoretical analysis that identifies concrete failure modes of standard GAN training and explains common empirical observations (saturation, vanishing gradients, instability under −log D).
2) A principled, practical direction—smoothing via noise and annealing—backed by theory (including bounds relating JSD of smoothed distributions to Wasserstein distance) and supporting experiments.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Understanding GAN instabilities is central to progress in generative modeling; the paper tackles this head-on and clarifies core phenomena that have hindered practical use.
- Placement and motivation: The work is well-situated within the literature, contrasting maximum likelihood (KL directions), original GAN (JSD), and the −log D trick, and articulates why each can fail when supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds.
- Correctness and rigor: The results are carefully stated; theorems on perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients, and instability under noisy discriminator updates follow from clear assumptions. The smoothing analysis is elegant and practically actionable. The Wasserstein bound tying back to the original (unsmoothed) distributions is especially useful.
- Empirical support: While not the focus, the experiments shown align with the theoretical claims (e.g., gradient norms, vanishing gradients as discriminators improve), increasing confidence in the conclusions.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not required for acceptance)
- Streamline Section 2.1 by moving full proofs to the appendix; several results (e.g., Urysohn-type separations, transversality/genericity) could be replaced with references to standard texts, with succinct statements in the main body.
- Clarify and standardize theorem numbering; there are cross-references pointing to non-existent items (e.g., “Theorem 1.3” in Corollary 2.1).
- Tighten exposition:
  - In Theorem 2.4, briefly remind the reader of p(z) and assumptions on its support/tails.
  - Specify domains of random variables and manifolds when introduced.
  - State explicitly whether “closed manifolds” in Theorem 2.2 have boundary or not; reconcile with later uses of interior/boundary.
  - Indicate regularity assumptions on gθ and the discriminator (e.g., differentiability classes) where needed.
- Editorial/typo fixes:
  - Page 2, second bullet: rephrase to “for generating implausible (not plausibly looking) pictures.”
  - “relly” → “rely.”
  - Theorem 2.5: “Therefore” → “Then” (for flow).
  - Theorem 2.6: “Is a…” → “is a…”
  - Consider noting that Lemma 1 holds under more general nonlinearities (beyond the listed classes) or delimit the exact scope.
  - Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 resemble standard analysis/topology facts; citations could suffice, with proofs relegated to the appendix.
- Section 3 is strong and intuitive; consider foregrounding the practical training recipe (noise on both data and generator paths, annealing schedule) and summarizing default choices (e.g., Gaussian noise, covariance selection).

Questions for the authors
- Noise design and schedule: What default variance/covariance and annealing schedules do you recommend? How sensitive are results to these choices across datasets and architectures?
- Scope of Lemma 1: Can you characterize more general activation classes under which the “countable union of manifolds of dim ≤ dim Z” conclusion holds?
- Discriminator noise model: The instability result (Cauchy updates) assumes independent white noise on D and ∇D. How robust are the conclusions if the noise is correlated or state-dependent, as in realistic finite-precision/optimization noise?
- Overlapping supports: How do the main conclusions change when Pr and Pg already overlap with non-negligible measure? Does smoothing still materially help?
- Empirical validation of smoothing: Do you have controlled comparisons showing improved stability and sample quality when adding noise to both real and generated inputs, with annealing, versus standard training?
- Choice of noise distribution: Beyond isotropic Gaussian, how do different covariances or heavy-tailed noise affect optimization and the induced “distance” weighting in the gradients?
- Discrete or sequence data: Can the smoothing framework extend to discrete domains (e.g., text) where additive noise is less natural? What are viable analogs?
