Summary and contributions
This paper presents a principled theoretical analysis of why GAN training is difficult and how to mitigate these issues. The core insight is geometric: when the real data and model distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds with negligible overlap (the typical case for neural generators with low-dimensional latents), an optimal discriminator exists that is essentially perfect and flat (zero gradient) almost everywhere on both supports. The authors prove:
- Perfect discrimination under disjoint or non-transversally intersecting manifolds (Theorems 2.1–2.2), which immediately implies divergences such as JSD saturate at log 2 and both KLs are infinite (Theorem 2.3).
- Vanishing generator gradients for the original generator loss as the discriminator approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4), explaining stalled training.
- For the alternative −log D generator loss, the gradient at θ0 equals the derivative of KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr) (Theorem 2.5), clarifying why GANs favor sharp, plausible samples and are prone to mode dropping.
- When the discriminator is noisy, −log D updates become heavy-tailed (coordinate-wise Cauchy) with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6), explaining instability in practice.
To address these issues, the paper proposes adding continuous noise (“instance noise”) to both real and generated inputs. This smooths distributions so that supports overlap, yielding stable, informative gradients. The authors:
- Derive explicit generator gradients under noise that move samples toward the data manifold while repelling overly likely model regions (Theorem 3.2) and show the resulting update corresponds to optimizing JSD between noisy distributions (Corollary 3.2).
- Relate the Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions to the JSD between their noisy counterparts plus a term controlled by the noise variance (Theorem 3.3), motivating annealed noise schedules.
Overall, the paper offers a coherent theoretical framework, experimentally illustrates key phenomena, and introduces practical tools (instance noise) grounded in the analysis.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The paper provides clear, novel theoretical explanations for instability and saturation in GANs, addressing a major open problem with rigorous arguments and insightful geometry.
2) It proposes a principled, practical remedy (adding noise and annealing) with theoretical guarantees linking training signals to meaningful distance measures.

Supporting arguments
- Specific problem: The work directly tackles why GANs destabilize: perfect discriminators on disjoint/low-dimensional supports cause both vanishing gradients (original loss) and highly unstable updates (−log D) as D improves. The assumptions (neural generator from low-dim latent; real data concentrated near a manifold) are realistic and well-motivated.
- Placement in literature: The paper situates divergences (KL directions, JSD) and their empirical behavior (Theis et al., Huszár) and draws on classical results in geometry and optimal transport (Villani). It complements heuristic stabilization work (DCGAN, improved GANs) by providing first-principles analysis.
- Scientific rigor: The theoretical claims are supported by formal theorems and clearly stated assumptions. While some proofs invoke idealizations (e.g., independence of discriminator value and gradient noise in Theorem 2.6), the resulting qualitative predictions match observed training pathologies (vanishing gradient, exploding variance, mode dropping). The noise-based remedy is justified both analytically (smooth gradients; JSD of noisy distributions; Wasserstein bound) and by targeted experiments.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the accept decision)
- Assumption clarity and generality:
  - Theorem 2.6 assumes independent white-noise perturbations for D and ∇D. Please discuss how results change with correlated or structured approximation errors (e.g., finite-precision or mini-batch noise). A weaker assumption (e.g., nonzero probability mass near zero for denominator noise) might still yield heavy tails.
  - Explicitly state regularity conditions enabling differentiation under the integral and interchange of limits in Theorems 2.4–2.5 (e.g., bounded Jacobians, dominated convergence).
  - In Theorem 2.5, highlight precisely where D* depends on θ, why evaluation at θ0 is legitimate, and the role of absolute continuity for Pr and Pg.
- Practical guidance:
  - Provide an algorithmic summary of instance noise (how to inject noise into both data and generator samples; how to anneal variance; typical schedules and magnitudes).
  - Offer empirical ablations illustrating sensitivity to noise level and annealing rate across datasets/architectures, and compare to common stabilization heuristics (feature matching, label smoothing). Even small-scale studies would strengthen the practical message.
  - Discuss discrete data (e.g., text): does adding continuous noise approximate a sensible objective, or is a different smoothing needed?
- Relations to distances and evaluation:
  - The Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) is a particularly nice bridge. Clarify constants and conditions (support contained in a ball of diameter C). Comment on whether a data-dependent C or tighter bounds are possible.
  - Suggest how one might monitor JSD(Pr+||Pg+) during training as a proxy for progress and how to set/anneal noise to balance the V1/2 term and the JSD term in the bound.
- Exposition and polish:
  - Fix typos and minor grammatical errors (e.g., “arize” → “arise”, “symetrical” → “symmetrical”), standardize notation (D*, norms), and clean up references and citations.
  - Consider moving some geometric preliminaries (transversality, Sard’s lemma, Urysohn) to a short background subsection to aid readability.

Questions for the authors
1) Theorem 2.5: Could you provide a more detailed derivation, including the steps justifying differentiation through the expectation and clarifying the dependence of D* on θ? Under what minimal conditions on Pgθ and Pr does the identity hold?
2) Theorem 2.6: How sensitive is the Cauchy instability to the independence/white-noise assumption? Do you observe heavy-tailed gradient coordinate distributions empirically as D improves? Any quantitative evidence (e.g., tail index estimates)?
3) Instance noise design: What annealing schedules work best in practice? How do you set the initial noise scale relative to data variability and generator capacity? Does annealing too quickly negate the benefits?
4) Discrete or mixed data types: For domains like text or graphs, how would you adapt smoothing so that the theoretical guarantees still provide overlapping supports and stable gradients?
5) Capacity and finite-sample effects: Many arguments consider an optimal or near-optimal discriminator. How do your conclusions change with capacity-limited discriminators and finite data? Are there failure modes where instance noise is insufficient?
6) Relation to alternative distances: Given Theorem 3.3, do you foresee training objectives that directly approximate Wasserstein (beyond the scope here) or hybridize with instance noise? How does your analysis inform such designs?

Overall, this is a timely and insightful paper. It offers a compelling theoretical account of GAN training pathologies and a principled, practically useful remedy. I recommend acceptance.
