This is a strong and timely submission on the theory of generative adversarial networks (GANs). The paper develops a rigorous analysis of convergence and provides a careful discussion of stability—two questions that are central to understanding and improving GAN training. To my knowledge, this is among the earliest works to offer substantive theoretical insight into GANs, going beyond empirical findings to formal results. I maintain my original, positive evaluation and hope the paper will be accepted.

Strengths:
- Significance: Convergence and stability are core challenges for GANs. Early GAN variants were notoriously unstable, and theoretical guidance is much needed; this paper addresses that gap directly.
- Novelty and rigor: Theorems 2.4–2.6 appear novel and are accompanied by interesting mathematical techniques. The results are nontrivial and, if correct under the stated assumptions, represent a meaningful advance in our understanding of adversarial training.
- Clarity of contributions: The paper draws a clear line between conditions that ensure convergence and those that promote stability, helping disentangle two concepts that are often conflated in practice.
- Potential impact: The analysis has practical implications for algorithm design, hyperparameter choices, and architecture constraints that promote stable training.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification (minor):
- Theorem 2.5 proof details: I have a few technical questions that, while minor, would benefit from clarification in the camera-ready version.
  - Assumptions: Please state explicitly which regularity assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz continuity, smoothness class, compactness of parameter spaces) are essential for the key steps. It would help to separate assumptions used for existence/uniqueness from those used for rates.
  - Interchange of limits: There is a step that appears to rely on exchanging limits (or expectations) with gradients. Please justify the interchange with a precise dominated convergence or uniform convergence argument, or cite a supporting lemma with conditions verified.
  - Error bounds and constants: The dependence of the bounds in Theorem 2.5 on key problem parameters (e.g., discriminator capacity, step sizes, noise level) is not fully transparent. A brief discussion of the scaling of constants would aid interpretation.
  - Stability notion: It would be helpful to clarify the exact stability notion used (e.g., Lyapunov stability, asymptotic stability, or stability in probability) and how it aligns with the practical training behavior observed in GANs.
  - Stochasticity: If the proof relies on an ODE or stochastic approximation view, please make explicit which stochastic approximation theorem is being invoked, and check that the martingale difference and step-size conditions are fully verified.
- Scope and generality: A short discussion on how sensitive the results are to the choice of divergence (e.g., JS, f-divergences, Wasserstein) and discriminator parameterization would broaden the paper’s applicability.
- Connection to practice: While the theoretical results stand on their own, a brief paragraph interpreting how these insights translate to practical heuristics (e.g., regularization, learning-rate schedules, gradient penalties) would enhance the paper’s relevance.

Minor comments and suggestions:
- Ensure notational consistency across theorems and proofs (e.g., norms, distance measures, and parameter domains).
- Where possible, provide intuition following each main theorem to guide readers less familiar with the technical machinery.
- If space permits, include a concise summary table of assumptions vs. guarantees for Theorems 2.4–2.6.

Overall evaluation: Accept. This paper tackles foundational questions about GAN convergence and stability with novel and rigorous analysis. The contributions are important, timely, and likely to influence both theory and practice. My remaining concerns are minor and primarily relate to clarifying certain technical steps in the proof of Theorem 2.5.
