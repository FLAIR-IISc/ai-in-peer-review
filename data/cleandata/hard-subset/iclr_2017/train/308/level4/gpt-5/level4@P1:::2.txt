Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles a fundamental question: why GAN training is unstable and why generator gradients either vanish (original GAN loss) or become erratic and unstable (the −log D variant). The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis grounded in the geometry of the data and model distributions.
- They prove that when Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional manifolds or have disjoint supports, an optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant almost everywhere on the supports, which implies vanishing gradients for the generator under the original loss. They also show that with the −log D update, noisy discriminator approximations yield generator gradients with Cauchy-distributed coordinates, leading to high variance and instability.
- To address this, they propose adding noise to smooth the distributions, derive the resulting generator gradients, and show that backpropagating through noise in both D and G yields a well-behaved objective equivalent to JSD between noisy distributions. Finally, they connect this to the Wasserstein distance, bounding W(Pr,Pg) by the noise variance plus JSD(Pr+‖Pg+), which motivates a practical annealing strategy.
- The theoretical results are complemented by targeted experiments (e.g., Figures 2 and 3) validating vanishing gradients and instability as the discriminator approaches optimality.

Decision: Accept
- Key reasons: (1) The paper delivers clear, rigorous explanations for widely observed GAN pathologies (vanishing gradients and instability) under realistic manifold assumptions; (2) It proposes a principled and analyzable direction—noise perturbation with annealing—supported by new theoretical tools and bounds that can guide practice.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and placement: The work addresses a central obstacle in GAN research—unstable training—and is well situated within the literature (Goodfellow et al., Theis et al., Huszár). The manifold-based analysis matches common intuitions about data geometry and generator supports.
- Theoretical rigor: The sequence of results is coherent and technically sound: perfect discrimination theorems under manifold assumptions; divergence saturation (JSD at log 2, KLs infinite) even when supports are close; vanishing gradient bound for the original objective; instability of −log D updates via a Cauchy argument with approximate discriminators; smoothing via additive noise leading to absolute continuity, interpretable gradients toward the data manifold and away from the generator manifold; and a clean bound linking Wasserstein distance to JSD of noisy distributions plus noise variance.
- Practical relevance: The analysis explains why tuning discriminator strength is so brittle and why gradient quality deteriorates as D improves. The perturbation-and-annealing proposal has an appealing practical interpretation: it justifies training D near-optimality on smoothed distributions while maintaining meaningful gradients and a bound on the true distance between Pr and Pg.
- Empirical support: The paper provides targeted evidence for the core phenomena (e.g., Figures 2 and 3). However, empirical validation of the proposed perturbation strategy itself is currently limited or absent, which is the main remaining gap.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Empirical validation of perturbation: Please include experiments that directly evaluate training with additive noise to both discriminator inputs and generator outputs, with an annealing schedule. Suggested comparisons:
  - Baselines: standard GAN loss, −log D variant, and your perturbed GAN.
  - Metrics: training stability (gradient norms, loss dynamics), sample quality, and diversity (to probe mode dropping), and, if feasible, an empirical proxy for the Wasserstein distance or JSD between noisy distributions.
  - Visuals: Analogues of Figures 2 and 3 under the perturbation scheme to demonstrate stabilized gradient magnitudes and reduced variance.
- Practical guidance: Provide recommendations on noise type (e.g., Gaussian vs. other tails), covariance choice, and annealing schedules. Discuss sensitivity to noise scale and how to tune it in practice.
- Relation to dropout: The perturbation idea resembles dropout in spirit. Could you clarify whether Bernoulli (dropout-style) noise preserves the desired properties (e.g., absolute continuity of the smoothed distributions)? My intuition is that continuous noise (e.g., Gaussian) is essential for the absolute continuity results underpinning your theorems; a brief discussion would help practitioners avoid pitfalls.
- Assumptions and robustness: Comment on the realism of the independence assumptions in Theorem 2.6 and the extent to which similar instability arises when D and ∇D errors are correlated. Also, discuss how the results change if Pr and Pg have partial overlap or if supports are only approximately manifold-like.
- Connection to practice: The gradient form in Theorem 3.2 resembles contrastive divergence. A short, intuitive comparison would be useful. Likewise, since the Wasserstein metric plays a central role in your bound, a brief discussion of how your approach relates to objectives that directly approximate Wasserstein distance would contextualize the contribution.
- Presentation: There are several typos and minor grammatical errors (e.g., “arize,” “aboslutely,” “transversallity”). A careful proofreading pass will improve readability.

Questions for the authors
- Could you provide empirical results demonstrating that additive noise to both D and G, with annealing, leads to more stable optimization and improved sample quality/diversity across standard image benchmarks?
- How should practitioners choose the initial noise level and annealing schedule? Are there diagnostics to adaptively control the noise variance during training?
- Does Bernoulli/dropout-style perturbation achieve similar smoothing benefits, or is continuous noise necessary for the absolute continuity and JSD/Wasserstein arguments?
- How sensitive is the method to discriminator capacity and training to near-optimality? Does the perturbation approach remove the need for carefully balancing the number of D vs. G updates?
- In practice, does your method mitigate mode dropping, as suggested by the gradient analysis and the bound to W(Pr,Pg)? If you have preliminary evidence, please share it.

Overall, this work offers a compelling theoretical account of GAN training pathologies and a principled path forward. Adding empirical validation of the perturbation-annealing strategy would further strengthen the paper and its practical impact.
