1. Summary of claimed contributions
The paper offers a rigorous theoretical study of GAN training dynamics, focusing on why training often becomes unstable and why generator gradients can vanish or explode as the discriminator improves. It formalizes the role of low-dimensional supports for Pr and Pg and shows that, under mild and realistic assumptions, optimal discriminators become perfect yet locally flat on the relevant supports, which undermines gradient-based learning for the generator. It then analyzes both the original GAN generator objective and the popular −log D alternative, proving vanishing gradients in the former (Theorem 2.4) and an intrinsic instability in the latter due to the emergence of heavy-tailed update noise (Theorem 2.6), while also characterizing the implicit objective optimized by −log D (Theorem 2.5). To mitigate these issues, the paper proposes adding noise to induce absolutely continuous “smoothed” distributions, derives the form of the resulting generator gradients (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2), and connects this to controlling a meaningful distance between Pr and Pg via a bound relating the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions to the Jensen–Shannon divergence between their noisy counterparts (Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3). The analysis is complemented by targeted experiments that qualitatively corroborate the theoretical phenomena. Overall, this is a timely, thoughtful, and technically deep contribution on an important topic.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) novel and rigorous theoretical insights into GAN instability and generator gradient behavior (Theorems 2.4–2.6) that address widely observed practical issues; (ii) a principled, theoretically grounded remedy via input noise with clear gradient characterizations and distance-based guarantees (Section 3, Theorem 3.3), offering concrete tools and research directions.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and motivation: The paper tackles fundamental questions about why training GANs is hard, why discriminator improvements can degrade generator updates, and how objective choices affect stability. The motivation is well-placed relative to the literature and long-standing empirical observations.
- Placement in the literature: The work contrasts classical likelihood/KL approaches with GANs’ JSD objective, articulating how divergences behave when supports are low-dimensional or disjoint.
- Correctness and rigor of claims:
  - Perfect discrimination on manifolds and its consequences are established under realistic assumptions (Theorems 2.1–2.3), explaining saturation and the lack of informative generator gradients.
  - Theorem 2.4 shows vanishing generator gradients for the original objective as the discriminator approaches optimality, matching practice.
  - Theorem 2.5 precisely identifies the objective underlying −log D updates, explaining the tendency toward good-looking samples alongside mode dropping.
  - Theorem 2.6 analyzes noisy discriminator approximations and shows heavy-tailed, high-variance updates for −log D, aligning with the observed instability.
  - Section 3 presents a coherent remedy: smoothing both data and model distributions yields stable, geometry-aware gradients (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2). The Wasserstein–JSD relation (Lemma 4, Theorem 3.3) provides a compelling bridge from trainable, smoothed divergences to a meaningful metric on the original distributions, with an annealing path that avoids precarious scheduling.
- Empirical alignment: The provided experiments, though targeted, illustrate the predicted phenomena (e.g., discriminator saturation, gradient norms and variability) and support the theoretical narrative.
- Novelty and impact: To my knowledge, this is among the first comprehensive theoretical treatments of GAN stability, and the mathematical techniques around manifold supports, noise-induced smoothing, and the derived bounds are both interesting and useful.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not central to my decision)
- Assumption clarity: In Theorem 2.6, the independence of the discriminator value and gradient errors is strong; it would help to discuss robustness when they are correlated or structured (e.g., finite-precision or optimization-induced noise). Even qualitative extensions would strengthen the practical relevance.
- Practical guidance: Provide more details on selecting noise distributions and annealing schedules (initial σ, decay strategy, and stopping criteria), and sensitivity analyses (e.g., isotropic vs. anisotropic noise, heavy-tailed noise variants) to help practitioners adopt the approach.
- Scope of experiments: Expanding beyond the current setups to diverse architectures/datasets would bolster the empirical case and illustrate the suggested training procedure end-to-end (including discriminator-to-optimal training under noise).
- Manifold assumptions: Briefly discuss the realism of “no perfect alignment” and boundary-free assumptions in common data domains, and how sensitive the conclusions are to violations.
- Theorem 3.3 constants and conditions: The bounded support (diameter C) assumption could be highlighted earlier, and comments on extending to sub-Gaussian tails or unbounded supports would be valuable. Some intuition on the tightness of the bound would also help.
- Exposition and notation: A pass to unify notation (e.g., consistent use of densities vs. measures, careful use of “continuous” vs. “absolutely continuous”) and to fix minor typos would improve readability. Consolidating the definitions of a(z), b(z) and their behavior in Theorem 3.2 in the main text could aid intuition.

Questions for the authors
- Theorem 2.5: Could you clarify the regularity conditions justifying the exchange of differentiation and integration, and the steps involving reparameterization of Pgθ when taking θ-derivatives at θ0? Are there assumptions on gθ (e.g., Lipschitzness, bounded Jacobian) and on support overlap needed for the derivation?
- Theorem 2.6: How sensitive is the Cauchy-behavior conclusion to the independence assumption between value and gradient noise? Do similar heavy-tailed updates persist for correlated Gaussian perturbations or bounded but biased approximations? Any empirical evidence quantifying tail index under realistic training noise?
- Noise design and annealing (Section 3): What practical criteria do you recommend for choosing σ and its decay schedule? How do you detect when to reduce noise, and how does this interact with discriminator capacity? Have you compared Gaussian vs. other kernels (e.g., Laplace, Cauchy) in terms of stability and sample quality?
- Adversarial directions: You note that backpropagating only through clean g(Z) invites measure-zero adversarial artifacts. When adding generator noise, do you observe improved robustness of D to adversarial examples induced by the generator? Any empirical diagnostics validating this mechanism?
- Theorem 3.3: In practice, supports are often unbounded. Can your bound be adapted using tail conditions (e.g., sub-Gaussian with known variance proxy) to replace C by a high-probability radius? How tight is the dependence on √JSD in realistic settings?
- Gradient structure in Theorem 3.2: a(z) and b(z) modulate attraction toward data mass and repulsion from model mass. Can you provide plots or further intuition showing how these weights vary as Pr+ and Pg+ become locally imbalanced, and how this stabilizes training near equilibrium?
- Empirical scope: Beyond the reported figures, have you tested the full “train D to optimality under noise + anneal noise” recipe on modern convolutional architectures, and can you share quantitative metrics (e.g., FID-like proxies or classifier two-sample tests) alongside qualitative samples?

Overall, this is a compelling, carefully argued paper. My questions on Theorem 2.5 are minor clarifications and do not affect my positive assessment.
