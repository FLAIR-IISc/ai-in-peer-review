- Paper offers a principled theoretical analysis explaining GAN training instability when real and model distributions lie on low-dimensional, nearly disjoint manifolds.
- Shows an optimal discriminator becomes almost constant on each manifold, causing vanishing gradients for the original GAN loss.
- Demonstrates the −log D generator loss can produce unstable, infinite-variance (Cauchy-like) updates under realistic discriminator approximation noise.
- Proposes injecting continuous noise into discriminator and generator inputs to “thicken” supports and produce smooth, meaningful gradients.
- Derives gradient forms that push samples toward high-density data regions and repel overrepresented model regions.
- Provides a bound linking noisy Jensen–Shannon divergence to Wasserstein distance between the original distributions and suggests an annealing strategy.
- Presents targeted experiments diagnosing gradient vanishing/explosion that support the theoretical claims.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reasons: strong, clear theoretical diagnosis of GAN pathologies with novel rigorous results that match empirical observations.
- Main reasons: principled, simple remedy (instance-like noise) with theoretical guarantees and interpretable gradients.
- Theoretical contributions include “perfect discrimination” theorems, vanishing-gradient proofs, and analysis of infinite-variance updates under noise.
- The noisy-input analysis yields the bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+ || Pg+), connecting a trainable proxy to a meaningful distance.
- Empirical support is appropriate and targeted, though not extensive.
- Suggested experiment additions: show training stabilization, improved sample quality and mode coverage, compare baseline vs perturbed GAN, and plot gradient norms/variance and ablations over noise variance and annealing.
- Ask authors to elaborate on noise design: distribution choice (Gaussian vs heavy-tailed), covariance, annealing schedules, and practical guidelines.
- Request discussion on relation to dropout and whether multiplicative/Bernoulli noise satisfies assumptions or is preferable.
- Ask for quantification of effects of injecting noise into D only, G only, and both, to verify necessity and adversarial-example arguments.
- Request clarification of practical considerations: computational overhead, interactions with batch norm/convolutions, finite-capacity discriminators, and approximation norm assumptions.
- Recommend minor presentation fixes: notation consolidation, typos, and reference formatting.
- Specific questions: provide empirical perturbation results analogous to Figures 2–3; sensitivity to noise distribution and covariance; best annealing schedules or adaptive rules; whether guarantees require noise on both G and D; interpretation/estimation of support diameter C; and quantitative fidelity/diversity metrics under perturbation.
