1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper delivers a clear theoretical account of why GAN training is difficult. It explains, with rigorous analysis, that when the data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (or have disjoint supports), optimal discriminators become perfect and locally flat, which makes generator gradients either vanish (for the original GAN objective) or become highly unstable (for the −log D variant).
- It identifies and formalizes the mechanisms behind these two failure modes: vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves under the original objective, and heavy-tailed, high-variance updates under the non-saturating objective.
- It proposes a principled remedy: injecting continuous noise (perturbations) so that the convolved (“smoothed”) distributions overlap, yielding well-behaved divergences and stable, informative gradients. The paper also develops tools (e.g., bounds relating Wasserstein distance to the JSD of smoothed distributions and noise variance) that ground this approach.
- Targeted experiments are used to validate assumptions and illustrate the phenomena predicted by the theory.

2. Decision
Accept. The paper provides (i) a compelling theoretical explanation of core GAN pathologies (vanishing vs unstable gradients) and (ii) a principled, analyzable perturbation-based direction to mitigate them, which I expect will catalyze further methodical progress.

3. Supporting arguments
- The analysis that optimal discriminators are perfect and flat almost everywhere on the data/generator manifolds directly explains why generator gradients vanish under the original objective as the discriminator improves. This clarifies why better discriminators paradoxically yield worse generator updates.
- For the −log D update, the demonstration that noisy discriminator approximations induce heavy-tailed (Cauchy) gradient components with infinite variance gives a precise account of the widely observed instability, even when the discriminator is “good.”
- The perturbation strategy is theoretically grounded: smoothing the distributions makes the discriminator’s objective meaningful again, produces stable and directionally useful gradients toward/away from the respective manifolds, and enables training the discriminator to (near) optimality without harming generator updates.
- The bounds tying Wasserstein distance between the original (unsmoothed) distributions to the JSD between their smoothed versions plus a term depending on noise variance provide a clean justification for annealing noise while still targeting the true distributions of interest.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not strictly part of the assessment)
- Please discuss the relationship between your perturbation scheme and dropout-style noise (Bernoulli perturbations). The conceptual link is appealing: both inject stochasticity to improve optimization and generalization, but their effects on the induced divergences and gradient fields differ. A short comparison (e.g., Bernoulli vs Gaussian noise; where injected; impact on the implied metric) would strengthen the presentation.
- Empirical validation of the perturbation trick would substantially reinforce the theory. Concretely, adding experiments analogous to your Fig. 2 and Fig. 3 for the perturbed setting (e.g., gradient norms, variance, and sample quality as the discriminator improves; with/without noise; ablations over noise scale and distribution) would be very informative.
- Implementation specifics would aid reproducibility and adoption: where to inject noise (discriminator inputs only vs both generator and discriminator), whether to perturb both real and generated samples, the annealing schedule for noise variance, and sensitivity to the choice of noise distribution and covariance.

Questions for the authors
- Noise injection details: Do you add noise to both real and generated samples and to both the discriminator and generator pathways, or only to discriminator inputs? Which configuration is required to obtain the smooth gradient result you claim in practice?
- Noise distribution and scheduling: Which distribution(s) work best empirically (e.g., Gaussian vs Bernoulli/dropout-like), and how sensitive are results to the covariance structure? How do you set and anneal the noise variance in practice?
- Empirical evidence: Can you provide experiments mirroring Fig. 2 and Fig. 3 under the perturbation approach to show reduced gradient vanishing/instability and improved sample quality/stability?
- Mode dropping: Does the perturbation strategy measurably reduce mode collapse in practice, consistent with your theoretical perspective on the induced objective? If so, please quantify on standard benchmarks.
