This paper makes a valuable contribution to provide a more clear understanding of generative adversarial network (GAN) training procedures. With the new insight of the training dynamics of GAN, as well as its variant, the authors reveal the reason why the gradient is either vanishing in the original GAN or unstable in its variant. More importantly, they also provide a way to avoid such difficulties by introducing perturbation.

I believe this paper will inspire more principled research in this direction. I am very interested in the perturbation trick to avoid the gradient instability and vanishment. In fact, this is quite related to the dropout trick, where the perturbation can be viewed as a Bernoulli distribution. It would be great if the connection could be discussed.

Besides the theoretical analysis, it would be beneficial to include an empirical study to justify this trick. The authors could add some experiments, similar to Figures 2 and 3, for the perturbated GAN for comparison. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.

Accept.

The main reasons for this choice are:

1. The paper provides a clear and thorough analysis of the GAN training procedure, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The authors propose a novel solution to the gradient instability and vanishment issues, which is supported by theoretical analysis and has the potential to inspire further research.

Supporting arguments for the reasons for the decision:

1. The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear introduction, literature review, and methodology section. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the GAN training procedure, including the theoretical background and the proposed solution.
2. The authors' proposal to introduce perturbation to avoid gradient instability and vanishment is innovative and has the potential to improve the performance of GANs. The theoretical analysis provided in the paper supports this proposal and provides a clear understanding of the underlying mechanisms.

Additional feedback:

1. The authors could consider including more empirical studies to support their theoretical analysis. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method and its potential applications.
2. The connection between the perturbation trick and the dropout trick could be further explored. This could provide a more nuanced understanding of the proposed method and its relationship to existing techniques.

Questions for the authors:

1. Could you provide more details on the empirical studies that support the proposed method? How do the results of these studies compare to existing methods?

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of GANs and has the potential to inspire further research. With some additional empirical studies and a more detailed analysis of the connection to existing techniques, this paper could be even stronger.
