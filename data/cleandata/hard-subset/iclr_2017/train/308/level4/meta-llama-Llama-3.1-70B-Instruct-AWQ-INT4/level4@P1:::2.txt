This paper makes a valuable contribution to providing a more clear understanding of the generative adversarial network (GAN) training procedure. With the new insight into the training dynamics of GANs, as well as their variants, the authors reveal the reason why the gradient is either vanishing in the original GAN or unstable in its variant. More importantly, they also provide a way to avoid such difficulties by introducing perturbation. I believe this paper will inspire more principled research in this direction.

I am very interested in the perturbation trick to avoid gradient instability and vanishment. In fact, this is quite related to the dropout trick, where the perturbation can be viewed as a Bernoulli distribution. It would be great if the connection could be discussed. Besides the theoretical analysis, are there any empirical studies to justify this trick? Could you please add some experiments, similar to Figures 2 and 3, for the perturbed GAN for comparison?

Accept (Preliminary Decision)

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the training dynamics of GANs, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors' insights into the vanishing and unstable gradients in GANs and their variants are valuable, and their proposed solution using perturbation is promising. However, I would like to see more empirical evidence to support their claims, particularly in comparison to existing methods.

Major Comments:

1. The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. However, some of the proofs and derivations could be moved to the appendices to make the main text more concise.
2. The authors should provide more context and background information on GANs and their applications to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
3. The connection between the perturbation trick and the dropout technique is intriguing and deserves more discussion.
4. The empirical studies presented in the paper are limited, and more experiments are needed to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Minor Comments:

1. There are some minor typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper. A thorough proofread is necessary to correct these errors.
2. The references could be formatted more consistently, and some of the citations are missing.
3. The figures and tables could be improved for better clarity and readability.

Questions for the Authors:

1. Can you provide more intuition behind the perturbation trick and how it relates to the dropout technique?
2. How do you plan to extend your work to more complex GAN architectures and applications?
3. Are there any potential limitations or drawbacks to using the perturbation trick in GAN training?
4. Can you provide more details on the experimental setup and hyperparameter tuning for the perturbed GAN?
