This paper addresses important questions about the difficulties in training generative adversarial networks (GANs). It discusses the consequences of using an asymmetric divergence function and sources of instability in training GANs, providing a thorough theoretical analysis of these issues. The authors then propose an alternative approach using a smoothening method to alleviate these problems.

The paper's strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical framework, which leverages concepts from analysis and differential topology to provide insightful answers to the questions posed. The authors' use of these concepts is impressive and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying mathematics. Additionally, the paper proposes avenues to avoid instability in GANs, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the paper is a bit too long and technical, which may make it challenging for non-experts to follow. Some parts of the paper, particularly the consequences of the theoretical results, still need to be further developed, which is perfectly fine for future work.

In terms of minor comments, the authors may consider shortening Section 2.1 by moving the proofs to the appendix. Section 3 provides a nice, intuitive, and simple solution to the problems discussed in the paper. There are a few typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper, such as "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" instead of "for generating not plausibly looking pictures" on page 2.

The authors may also consider providing more context and background information on some of the technical concepts, such as Lemma 1 and Theorem 2.1, which seem to be basic analysis results. Additionally, the domain of the random variables should be specified, and the numbering of the theorems could be improved for clarity.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the understanding and training of GANs. With some revisions to address the minor comments, it has the potential to be a seminal work in the field.
