This paper makes a valuable contribution to providing a clearer understanding of the generative adversarial network (GAN) training procedure.

The authors tackle the specific question of understanding the training dynamics of GANs, shedding light on the instability and saturation issues that arise during training. They provide a rigorous analysis of these problems, introducing new tools to study them and proposing a direction to address these challenges.

The approach is well-motivated, building upon existing literature on GANs and providing a solid foundation for their theoretical analysis. The authors' use of mathematical proofs and theorems to support their claims adds to the paper's rigor and scientific value.

The paper's main contribution lies in its ability to explain the vanishing gradients and unstable behavior observed in GAN training. The authors' introduction of perturbation as a means to avoid these difficulties is a significant insight, and their theoretical analysis provides a clear understanding of why this approach is effective.

I am very interested in the perturbation trick to avoid gradient instability and vanishment. In fact, this is quite related to the dropout trick, where the perturbation can be viewed as a Bernoulli distribution. It would be great if the connection could be discussed.

Besides the theoretical analysis, I would like to see more empirical studies to justify this trick. Could you please add some experiments, similar to Fig 2 and 3, for the perturbated GAN for comparison?

Additionally, I would appreciate it if the authors could provide more context on how their work relates to existing research on GANs, such as the use of different architectures or training procedures.

Overall, I believe this paper has the potential to inspire more principled research in this direction and contribute significantly to the field of GANs.

Rating: Accept

Reasons for acceptance:

1. The paper provides a clear and rigorous analysis of the training dynamics of GANs, addressing a significant challenge in the field.
2. The authors introduce a novel approach to avoiding gradient instability and vanishment, which is supported by theoretical analysis and has the potential to inspire further research.

Questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more context on how your work relates to existing research on GANs, such as the use of different architectures or training procedures?
2. How do you plan to extend your work to more complex GAN architectures or applications?
3. Can you provide more empirical studies to justify the perturbation trick, including experiments on different datasets or tasks?
