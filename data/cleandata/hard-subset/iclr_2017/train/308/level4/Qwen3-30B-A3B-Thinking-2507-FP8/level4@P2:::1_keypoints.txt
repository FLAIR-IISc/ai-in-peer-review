- The paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework explaining GAN training instability due to real and generator distributions lying on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- It proves that when supports are disjoint or on low-dim manifolds, the optimal discriminator is constant on those manifolds, causing vanishing gradients for the generator.  
- The authors propose adding noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and restore meaningful gradient updates.  
- The paper establishes connections to the Wasserstein metric, offering a more appropriate similarity measure for distributions on different manifolds.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Novelty: one of the first rigorous theoretical analyses of GAN training dynamics, with novel results in Theorems 2.4–2.6.  
- The work introduces new mathematical tools that provide a principled foundation for understanding and addressing GAN instability.  
- Theoretical analysis is well-motivated, rigorous, and accessible to both practitioners and experts.  
- Suggestion: clarify practical implementation details and computational costs of the noise-based approach.  
- Suggestion: provide a detailed comparison between the noise approach and existing stabilization methods (spectral normalization, gradient penalties, adaptive learning rates).  
- Suggestion: discuss limitations, e.g., how required noise level scales with manifold dimensionality and complex tasks.  
- Question: ask for intuition why the gradient step corresponds to KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2·JSD(Pg‖Pr) and how that causes mode dropping.  
- Question: request clarification on the gradient form in Theorem 3.2 and how it differs from standard GAN gradients with respect to mode dropping.  
- Question: ask for an intuitive explanation of how the Wasserstein metric differs from Jensen-Shannon divergence when supports lie on different manifolds.  
- Overall: an outstanding, impactful theoretical contribution that advances understanding of GAN training and guides future work.
