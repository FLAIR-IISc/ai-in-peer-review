- The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis explaining GAN training instabilities via differential topology and measure theory.  
- Key insight: data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional, disjoint manifolds, causing optimal discriminators to be perfect with zero gradients almost everywhere.  
- Vanishing or unstable generator gradients are explained through formal theorems (notably Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4–2.6).  
- Proposed solution: add continuous noise to inputs to smooth distributions and enable meaningful gradient flow.  
- The work connects the noise-smoothed approach to the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), giving a theoretical basis for more suitable distribution comparisons.  
- Decision: recommend accept — substantial theoretical contribution with practical implications for GAN training.  
- Strength: rigorous, well-motivated proofs that attribute instability to geometric distribution properties rather than optimization artifacts.  
- Improvement: move elementary proofs (e.g., Theorems 2.1, Lemma 2) to the appendix to improve readability.  
- Improvement: clarify the distinction between "continuous" and "absolutely continuous" random variables in the main text.  
- Improvement: fix inconsistent theorem/corollary numbering (e.g., Corollary 2.1 referencing "Theorem 1.3").  
- Improvement: rephrase claim about KL divergence to specify it applies to the original distributions, since noise-smoothed distributions have finite KL.  
- Minor copy edits: fix typos such as "relly"→"rely", grammar in "for not generating", and capitalization in "Is a..."  
- Question: do the "closed manifolds" in Theorem 2.2 have boundaries, and how would boundaries affect the results?  
- Question: how does the intuition that noise makes Pr+ and Pg+ almost overlap reconcile with Theorem 3.3’s bound showing Wasserstein distance depends on both noise variance and JSD of the smoothed distributions?
