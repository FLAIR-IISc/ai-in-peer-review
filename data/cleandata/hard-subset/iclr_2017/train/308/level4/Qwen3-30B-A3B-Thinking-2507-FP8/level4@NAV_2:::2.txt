

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of generative adversarial network (GAN) training dynamics. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the fundamental challenges in GAN training, including instability and saturation, and establish the theoretical foundations for these phenomena. They demonstrate that the perfect discrimination between data and generator distributions on low-dimensional manifolds leads to vanishing gradients in the original GAN formulation and unstable updates in its variants. Most importantly, the authors introduce a principled solution through the use of perturbation to create smoother training dynamics, while establishing a connection to more robust metrics for evaluating generative models.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in GAN research, and the proposed perturbation approach offers a principled direction for future work.

The paper's theoretical framework is exceptionally well-developed, with clear theorems establishing why GAN training becomes unstable and why the proposed perturbation approach resolves these issues. The authors successfully connect their theoretical findings to practical implementation considerations, demonstrating that the perturbed approach avoids the problematic properties of the original GAN formulation. The proof techniques are sound and well-justified, and the paper provides valuable new tools for analyzing GAN training dynamics.

For further improvement, it would be helpful to more explicitly connect the perturbation approach to existing techniques like dropout, which also introduce noise to improve generalization. The relationship between the Gaussian perturbation used in the paper and Bernoulli noise in dropout deserves clarification, as this could strengthen the paper's theoretical framework and provide additional context for practitioners.

Additionally, while the paper provides theoretical justification for the perturbation approach, including empirical validation would significantly strengthen the contribution. Could the authors please provide experiments comparing standard GANs with their perturbed variant, particularly showing how the perturbation affects gradient behavior and sample quality as illustrated in Figures 2 and 3 for the standard GANs? This would help demonstrate the practical impact of their theoretical insights.
