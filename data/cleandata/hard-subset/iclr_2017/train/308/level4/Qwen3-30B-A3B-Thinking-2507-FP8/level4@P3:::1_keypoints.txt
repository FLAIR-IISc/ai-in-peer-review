- Significant theoretical contribution explaining GAN training instability and saturation through distributional and manifold analysis.  
- Paper is well-structured and connects problem identification, theory, and a practical noise-based stabilization solution.  
- Decision: Accept — strong theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs and empirical validation.  
- Tackles a central GAN question: why training is unstable and why saturation occurs.  
- Correctly attributes instability to generator and data distributions having disjoint or low-dimensional supports, leading to perfect discrimination.  
- Provides multiple theorems (2.1–2.6) characterizing perfect discrimination and resulting gradient behavior (vanishing gradients and instability).  
- Shows original cost leads to vanishing gradients while the -log D alternative avoids vanishing gradients but causes unstable, high-variance updates.  
- Proposes adding noise to inputs to produce continuous distributions (Pr+ and Pg+), yielding meaningful JS divergence and smooth gradients.  
- Connects the noise-based approach to Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3), offering a way to measure distance between original distributions.  
- Suggests clearer, more intuitive explanations of manifold theory concepts for non-expert readers.  
- Recommends expanding empirical validation to compare with recent stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty).  
- Advises strengthening related-work discussion to explicitly link theoretical insights to practical stabilization methods.  
- Asks how the noise-based approach compares practically and theoretically to spectral normalization/gradient penalty.  
- Asks how to choose noise variance in practice and the trade-offs between noise level and stability.  
- Asks how conclusions change when the discriminator is only approximately optimal, as in practical training.
