

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**Summary of Contributions**  
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors address fundamental questions about GAN instability and saturation by developing rigorous mathematical analysis of the underlying mechanisms. They prove that the support of generator distributions typically lies on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discrimination between real and generated data, which explains the vanishing gradients and instability observed in practice. The paper further introduces a novel theoretical framework for analyzing GAN training dynamics, including a softened metric approach that addresses these challenges. This work is among the first to provide deep theoretical insights into GAN training behavior, moving beyond heuristic approaches that have dominated the field.

**Decision**  
Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that fundamentally advances our understanding of GAN training dynamics, with clear implications for future research and practical implementations.

**Key Reasons for Acceptance**  
1. The paper successfully answers critical questions about GAN instability through novel theoretical analysis. Theorems 2.4-2.6 provide the first rigorous explanation for vanishing gradients and instability in GAN training, demonstrating how the geometry of data distributions leads to these phenomena. This addresses a long-standing challenge in the field that has previously only been handled through empirical heuristics.

2. The paper introduces a theoretically grounded approach to addressing GAN instability through noise-regularized distributions and the Wasserstein metric. The authors demonstrate how this approach resolves the fundamental issues identified in their analysis, providing a principled path forward for more stable GAN training.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's theoretical analysis is exceptionally strong. Lemma 1 provides a rigorous foundation for understanding why generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which is crucial for the subsequent analysis. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 establish that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which they typically do), perfect discriminators exist that are constant almost everywhere on these manifolds. This explains why gradients vanish as the discriminator improves—a phenomenon previously only observed empirically.

The analysis of the two cost functions (Theorems 2.4-2.6) is particularly compelling. The authors demonstrate that the original cost function suffers from vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves, while the alternative cost function (−log D) causes unstable updates with infinite variance due to the noisy approximation of the optimal discriminator. This explains both the observed training challenges and the need for careful training schedules.

The proposed solution (Section 3) is theoretically elegant and practical. By introducing noise to the discriminator inputs, the authors create continuous distributions that allow for meaningful gradient updates. Theorem 3.3 establishes a crucial relationship between the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions, providing a theoretical foundation for their approach.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
While the paper is strong, a few refinements would enhance its impact:

1. The discussion of "continuous" versus "absolutely continuous" random variables (Appendix B) is important but could be integrated more smoothly into the main text to avoid disrupting the flow for readers unfamiliar with these technical distinctions.

2. The connection between the proposed noise-regularized approach and existing practical methods (such as label smoothing or spectral normalization) could be more explicitly developed, as this would help bridge theory and practice.

3. A brief empirical validation of the proposed theoretical framework (even if limited) would strengthen the paper's contribution by demonstrating how these theoretical insights translate to practical improvements in GAN training.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. Theorem 2.5 shows that the gradient update corresponds to optimizing KL(Pg‖Pr) - 2JSD(Pg‖Pr). Could you elaborate on how this specific combination of divergences relates to the observed mode collapse in GANs, and whether this formulation suggests additional modifications to the training procedure?

2. The paper establishes that the noise-regularized approach leads to smooth gradients, but doesn't explicitly address how the noise level should be chosen in practice. What guidance would you provide for selecting the noise variance σ² for different datasets or architectures?

3. In Corollary 3.2, the authors show that backpropagating through noisy samples yields a gradient proportional to ∇θJSD(Pr+‖Pg+). How does this relate to the recently proposed Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) approach, and do you see these methods as complementary or competing?
