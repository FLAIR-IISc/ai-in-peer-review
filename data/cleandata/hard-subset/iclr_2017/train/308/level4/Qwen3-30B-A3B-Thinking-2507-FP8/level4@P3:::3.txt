

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**Summary**  
This paper provides a theoretically rigorous analysis of the fundamental challenges in training generative adversarial networks (GANs), addressing instability and saturation issues that have plagued practical implementations. The authors make a significant contribution by formalizing the problem through the lens of differential topology and measure theory, demonstrating that GAN training difficulties arise from the fact that both the data and generator distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. The paper's key insight is that this geometric property causes optimal discriminators to be perfect (with zero gradient almost everywhere), leading to vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator. The authors then propose a principled solution by adding continuous noise to inputs, which smooths the distributions and enables meaningful gradient flow, ultimately connecting to the Wasserstein metric as a more suitable measure for comparing distributions.

**Decision**  
I recommend *accept* the paper. The theoretical contributions are substantial and address a critical gap in GAN theory, with implications for both understanding and improving generative models.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's core contributions are well-motivated and rigorously proven. The authors successfully establish that the instability in GAN training stems from the geometric properties of the data and generator distributions, rather than being an artifact of the specific optimization procedure. Their Theorems 2.1 and 2.2—proving the existence of perfect discriminators with zero gradients when distributions lie on disjoint manifolds—are particularly compelling, as they directly explain the practical difficulties observed in GAN training. The subsequent analysis of gradient behavior under both original and alternative cost functions (Theorems 2.4, 2.5, and 2.6) provides a satisfying theoretical explanation for why vanishing gradients and instability occur, resolving long-standing questions about GAN behavior.

The proposed solution in Section 3 is elegant and well-grounded: adding noise to inputs creates smoothed distributions for which the Jensen-Shannon divergence becomes meaningful, enabling stable gradient updates. The connection to the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3) is particularly valuable, as it provides a theoretical foundation for using noise to bridge the gap between discrete and continuous distributions. The paper's approach is not merely heuristic but builds on deep mathematical insights, making it a significant contribution to the field.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
While the paper makes a strong theoretical contribution, a few refinements would enhance its clarity and accessibility:

1. **Proofs and Technical Details**: Several proofs (e.g., Theorems 2.1, Lemma 2) are elementary and could be referenced rather than re-proven. Moving these to the appendix would improve readability without compromising rigor.

2. **Terminology**: The distinction between "continuous" and "absolutely continuous" random variables (Appendix B) is crucial but currently buried. A brief clarification in the main text would prevent confusion for readers unfamiliar with measure-theoretic distinctions.

3. **Theorem Numbering**: The current theorem numbering is inconsistent (e.g., Corollary 2.1 references "Theorem 1.3"). A careful review of the numbering would improve the paper's organization.

4. **Clarity of Key Claims**: The paper states that "the KL divergence will be infinity" (Theorem 2.3), but this should be clarified as "the KL divergence between the original distributions is infinite," since the noise-smoothed distributions have finite KL divergence.

5. **Minor Typos**: "relly" should be "rely," "for not generating" should be "for generating not," and "Is a..." should be "is a."

**Questions for Authors**  
To help me better assess the paper, I would appreciate clarification on two points:

1. In Theorem 2.2, the paper states "closed manifolds" but doesn't specify whether they have boundaries. Could you clarify the boundary conditions for the manifolds and how they affect the results?

2. The paper suggests that "the noise term will make the noisy distributions Pr+ and Pg+ almost overlap." How does this intuition align with the bound in Theorem 3.3, which shows that the Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg depends on both the noise variance and the JSD of the smoothed distributions?

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to understanding GANs and provides a principled path toward more stable training. With minor revisions to improve clarity and presentation, it would be an excellent addition to the ICLR 2017 program.
