

This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors rigorously analyze the fundamental causes of instability and saturation in GAN training, demonstrating that these issues arise when the data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. They prove that under these conditions, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and its gradient vanishes almost everywhere, explaining why standard GAN training suffers from vanishing gradients in the original formulation and unstable updates in the modified cost function. Most importantly, the authors propose a principled solution by introducing a perturbation-based approach that smooths the distributions, enabling stable gradient updates and providing a new theoretical framework for analyzing GAN training.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work represents a major theoretical advance in the field, addressing a fundamental challenge in GAN training with mathematical rigor. The key reason for acceptance is that the paper provides a comprehensive theoretical explanation for why GAN training is unstable, which has been a persistent issue in the field without a solid theoretical foundation. The second key reason is that the authors introduce a principled solution that resolves these instability issues while maintaining theoretical grounding, opening new research directions for GAN development.

The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and well-justified. The authors prove several important theorems establishing that when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the Jensen-Shannon divergence is maximized (with KL divergences being infinite), explaining why traditional divergence-based approaches fail. They then demonstrate how the optimal discriminator becomes constant almost everywhere on the support of the generator distribution, leading to vanishing gradients. For the modified cost function, they prove that while it avoids vanishing gradients, it introduces instability due to the Cauchy-distributed nature of the gradient updates. These mathematical arguments are carefully developed and rigorously proven, with clear connections to practical observations in GAN training.

The proposed solution—adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator)—is theoretically sound and elegantly derived from the paper's analysis. The authors demonstrate that this perturbation creates continuous distributions that allow for meaningful gradient updates, and they establish a connection between the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the perturbed distributions. This connection provides a principled way to minimize the distance between the data and generator distributions while avoiding the instability issues identified in the paper.

I would like the authors to address a few points to strengthen the paper further. First, the connection between their perturbation approach and dropout (which also introduces noise) is intriguing but not explored in the paper. It would be valuable to discuss whether their perturbation technique can be viewed as a continuous analog of dropout, and how the different noise distributions might affect performance. Second, while the theoretical analysis is compelling, the paper would benefit from additional empirical validation of the perturbation approach. Including experiments that directly compare standard GAN training with the perturbed approach (similar to Figures 2 and 3 in the paper) would provide stronger evidence for the practical utility of their theoretical framework. Finally, the paper could briefly discuss how the optimal noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian vs. other distributions) might impact the performance of the perturbed GAN.

The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear theorems and proofs. The mathematical arguments are thorough and accessible to researchers with a solid background in machine learning theory. The paper's contributions are significant and will likely influence future research on GANs by providing a theoretical foundation for understanding training dynamics and a principled approach to addressing instability issues. I believe this work represents an important step toward more reliable and theoretically grounded GAN training methods.
