

# Review of "Theoretical Analysis of Generative Adversarial Networks"

## Summary
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental challenges in training generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors rigorously analyze why GANs exhibit instability and saturation during training, providing a comprehensive theoretical framework that explains these phenomena through the lens of distributional properties and manifold theory. The paper is well-structured, moving logically from problem identification to theoretical analysis and finally to a proposed solution involving "softer" metrics. The authors successfully bridge the gap between theoretical understanding and practical implementation by introducing a noise-based approach to stabilize training while maintaining theoretical guarantees. This work represents a major step toward principled research directions in GANs rather than heuristic engineering approaches.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in GAN training, and the claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs and empirical validation.

## Key Reasons for Acceptance
1. The paper tackles a critical question in GAN research: why training is unstable and what causes saturation. This question has been central to GAN development since their introduction, yet there has been little theoretical understanding of the underlying mechanisms.

2. The approach is exceptionally well-motivated and placed within the literature. The authors correctly identify that the instability stems from the fact that generator and data distributions typically have disjoint or low-dimensional supports, which leads to perfect discrimination and problematic gradient behavior. This is a natural extension of prior work on GAN divergences (e.g., Theis et al., 2016) and provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for understanding GAN behavior.

3. The paper rigorously supports its claims through multiple theorems (2.1-2.3) that characterize the perfect discrimination problem, followed by analysis of gradient behavior (Theorems 2.4-2.6) that explains both vanishing gradients and instability. The theoretical framework is sound, with proofs that are detailed and well-constructed.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical contributions are significant because they explain long-observed practical phenomena with mathematical precision. The authors correctly identify that when generator and data distributions have disjoint supports (which is typical in practice), the optimal discriminator achieves perfect accuracy and has zero gradients almost everywhere on the supports of both distributions. This explains why standard GAN training suffers from vanishing gradients and instability.

The analysis of the two cost functions (original vs. -logD alternative) is particularly compelling. The authors prove that the original cost function leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves, while the alternative cost function, though avoiding vanishing gradients, produces unstable updates with infinite variance. This provides a rigorous explanation for why both cost functions have limitations in practice.

The proposed solution—adding noise to inputs before the discriminator—provides a theoretically grounded approach to stabilization. The authors show that this creates continuous distributions (Pr+ and Pg+), making the Jensen-Shannon divergence meaningful and providing smooth gradients. The connection to Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is insightful and demonstrates how the noise-based approach can be used to measure the distance between the original distributions.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its clarity and impact:
- The paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the manifold theory concepts for readers less familiar with differential geometry. A brief illustrative example might help convey the key insights without adding significant length.
- The empirical validation section could be expanded to include comparisons with more recent stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) to better contextualize the proposed approach.
- The discussion of related work could be strengthened by explicitly connecting the theoretical insights to existing practical stabilization methods.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the proposed noise-based approach compare to other recent stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) in terms of practical performance and theoretical guarantees? 

2. The paper shows that adding noise creates continuous distributions Pr+ and Pg+, but the noise variance needs to be carefully chosen. How should this variance be selected in practice, and what are the trade-offs between noise level and training stability?

3. The authors note that the theoretical results assume the discriminator is trained to optimality. In practice, discriminators are not trained to full optimality. How do the theoretical insights change when the discriminator is only approximately optimal, as is typical in practice?

The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to GAN research that explains long-observed practical phenomena with mathematical rigor. The proposed solution is both theoretically sound and practically implementable, making this an important step toward principled GAN development. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR 2017 program.
