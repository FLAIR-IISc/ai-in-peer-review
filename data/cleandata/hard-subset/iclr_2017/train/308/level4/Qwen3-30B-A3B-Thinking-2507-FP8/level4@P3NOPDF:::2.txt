

This paper makes a valuable contribution toward clarifying the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs) and their variants. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis that elucidates the underlying reasons for two critical challenges: gradient vanishing in standard GANs and instability in their common variants. Crucially, they propose a simple yet effective perturbation-based mechanism to mitigate these issues, offering a principled approach to stabilize training. This work significantly advances our understanding of GAN dynamics and will likely inspire more rigorous, theoretically grounded research in the field.

The core insight—that perturbation can regularize the discriminator’s gradient flow—deserves particular attention. The connection to dropout, where Bernoulli noise inherently introduces perturbations, is highly relevant and merits deeper discussion. The authors should explicitly explore this analogy: how does the proposed perturbation mechanism relate to dropout’s stochastic regularization? Does it share similar convergence properties or generalization benefits? A comparative analysis here would strengthen the theoretical narrative and contextualize the method within established deep learning practices.

While the theoretical framework is sound, the empirical validation requires strengthening. The paper currently lacks direct evidence demonstrating the perturbation’s efficacy in practical settings. To substantiate their claims, the authors should include comparative experiments showing the impact of perturbation on key metrics (e.g., FID scores, training stability, mode coverage) across standard benchmarks like CIFAR-10 or STL-10. Specifically, adding results analogous to Figures 2–3 (e.g., training curves with/without perturbation, qualitative samples, or gradient magnitude analyses) would provide concrete justification for the proposed method. Without such empirical support, the practical significance of the theoretical contribution remains speculative.

Additionally, the paper should address potential limitations. How sensitive is the perturbation to hyperparameters (e.g., noise magnitude, distribution)? Does it generalize to more complex architectures (e.g., progressive growing, StyleGAN) or loss functions beyond Wasserstein GANs? A brief ablation study exploring these aspects would enhance the paper’s robustness and applicability.

The writing is generally clear but would benefit from tighter phrasing. For instance, "reveal the reason that why the gradient is either vanishing... or unstable" should be revised to "explain why gradients vanish in standard GANs or become unstable in variants." Minor grammatical improvements (e.g., "more clear" → "clearer," "vanishment" → "vanishing") would elevate the professionalism. The title and abstract should explicitly highlight the perturbation mechanism as the key innovation to avoid ambiguity.

Overall, this is a promising contribution with strong theoretical motivation. The perturbation technique is novel and potentially impactful, but its value hinges on empirical validation. Addressing the gaps in experimentation and the dropout analogy would transform this into a highly compelling paper. The authors are encouraged to incorporate these elements to solidify the work’s significance for the GAN community. The revised version would make a strong case for acceptance at ICLR 2017.
