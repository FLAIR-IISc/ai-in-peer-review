- Significant theoretical contribution explaining GAN training instability and proposing a stabilization approach.  
- Proves instability stems from data and generator distributions lying on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- Shows the optimal discriminator becomes perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere on the generator support, explaining vanishing gradients (Theorems 2.4–2.6).  
- Analyzes the alternative cost function and explains why it remains unstable despite avoiding vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.5).  
- Demonstrates that adding noise to discriminator inputs smooths distributions and yields meaningful gradients for the generator (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2).  
- Provides the first rigorous theoretical framework for GAN behavior, moving beyond heuristic stabilization methods.  
- Recommends acceptance based on the paper’s theoretical thoroughness and clarity.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and makes a major contribution to the field.  
- Suggests adding a detailed comparison of the noise-based approach to practical stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties).  
- Suggests discussing computational considerations for different noise distributions for practitioners.  
- Requests clarification on sensitivity of the Wasserstein–JSD bound to the choice of noise distribution (Theorem 3.3).  
- Requests elaboration on the types of adversarial examples arising without noisy generator samples and how Corollary 3.2 mitigates them.  
- Requests intuition for why the gradient update being "inverted KL minus two JSD" would cause mode dropping in GANs (Theorem 2.5).
