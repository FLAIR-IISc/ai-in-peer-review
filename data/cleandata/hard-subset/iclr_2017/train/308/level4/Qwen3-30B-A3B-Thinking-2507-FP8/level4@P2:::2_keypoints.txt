1) Significant theoretical contribution explaining fundamental GAN training challenges.  
2) Rigorous analysis identifies instability and vanishing gradients in traditional GANs.  
3) Shows that when data and generator lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect and constant almost everywhere.  
4) Explains that this discriminator behavior causes vanishing gradients for the original GAN loss and unstable updates for the alternative cost.  
5) Proposes adding continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and restore meaningful gradients.  
6) Introduces new theoretical tools and connections to Wasserstein metrics for analyzing GANs.  
7) Recommends acceptance with minor revisions due to strong theory and relevance.  
8) Proves JS divergence is maximized for distributions on disjoint/low-dim manifolds, explaining failure of standard divergences.  
9) Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2 theoretically guarantee that input noise yields meaningful gradient updates.  
10) Suggests adding empirical validation comparing standard GAN training to the proposed perturbation method (e.g., experiments like Figures 2–3).  
11) Notes intriguing connection to dropout and calls for deeper exploration of differences between continuous noise and Bernoulli dropout.  
12) Requests clarification of practical implementation details: optimal noise distribution, variance, annealing schedule, and computational overhead.  
13) Asks how the noise-based approach compares to other GAN regularizers (spectral normalization, gradient penalty, label smoothing).  
14) Seeks guidance on how choice of noise distribution (Gaussian vs. others) affects practical GAN performance.  
15) Wants recommended annealing schedules for noise variance and their effect on final sample quality.
