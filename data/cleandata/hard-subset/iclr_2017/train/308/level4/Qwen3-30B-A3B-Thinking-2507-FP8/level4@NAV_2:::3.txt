

1. Summary  
This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the fundamental challenges in training generative adversarial networks (GANs), addressing the core question of why GANs exhibit instability and saturation during training. The authors successfully demonstrate that these issues arise from the low-dimensional manifold structure of both the data and generator distributions, which leads to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients. The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a theoretically grounded approach to stabilize GAN training through the addition of noise to create smoother distributions, enabling meaningful gradient updates. This work not only explains existing phenomena but also establishes new tools for analyzing and improving generative models.

2. Decision  
Accept. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics, and the proposed smoothing approach offers a principled solution to the identified problems.

3. Supporting arguments  
The paper successfully addresses all three key questions: (1) It clearly identifies the problem of instability and saturation in GAN training as stemming from the low-dimensional manifold structure of distributions, (2) The approach is well-motivated through a thorough literature review and rigorous mathematical analysis that places it in context with existing work on divergences and GAN theory, and (3) The claims are strongly supported through formal proofs (Theorems 2.1-2.6) and experimental verification (Figures 1-3). The theoretical framework provides a comprehensive explanation for observed GAN behaviors, and the proposed smoothing approach is both theoretically sound and practically promising, with Corollary 3.2 showing how it enables stable gradient updates.

4. Additional feedback  
The paper is technically strong, but could benefit from minor improvements to enhance accessibility and clarity. Consider moving the detailed proofs from Section 2.1 to an appendix to improve the flow of the main argument. The connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation could be strengthened with more explicit discussion of how the smoothing approach translates to specific GAN architectures. The discussion of the Wasserstein metric in Section 3 would benefit from a clearer explanation of its relationship to the proposed solution and how it differs from the JSD-based approach.

5. Questions for authors  
- How does the optimal noise variance scale with the dimensionality of the data manifold, and how might this impact the practical implementation of your smoothing approach?  
- Could you provide more specific guidance on when the proposed smoothing method would be most beneficial compared to other stabilization techniques (such as spectral normalization or gradient penalty)?  
- The paper mentions that the smoothing approach "protects the discriminator against measure 0 adversarial examples" - could you elaborate on the specific vulnerability this addresses and how it differs from other defenses against adversarial examples?
