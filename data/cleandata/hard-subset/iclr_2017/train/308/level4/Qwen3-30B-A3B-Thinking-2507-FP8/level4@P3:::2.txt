

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental challenges in training generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors systematically analyze why GAN training exhibits instability and saturation, demonstrating that these issues arise because both the real data distribution and the generator's distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds. This leads to perfect discriminators with zero gradients on the generator's support, causing vanishing gradients in the original GAN formulation and unstable updates in the modified version. The authors then propose a principled solution using distributional perturbation to smooth the training dynamics, providing a clear theoretical foundation for understanding GAN behavior.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons for acceptance: The paper successfully answers three critical questions about GAN training dynamics—why gradients vanish in the original formulation, why updates become unstable in the modified version, and how to address these issues—through rigorous theoretical proofs. The proposed perturbation technique is theoretically sound and directly addresses a fundamental limitation in GAN training.

Supporting arguments: The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and properly situated within the GAN literature. The authors build upon and extend existing theoretical work on generative modeling and GANs, providing a clear mathematical framework that explains long-observed empirical phenomena. Their analysis of the optimal discriminator's behavior when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds is particularly compelling, as it explains why traditional approaches to analyzing GAN training fail. The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and fill a significant gap in the literature, providing a much-needed principled understanding of GAN training dynamics. The connection between the theoretical analysis and the proposed perturbation technique is especially strong, offering a concrete path forward for more stable GAN training.

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation of the perturbation technique. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on how to choose the perturbation parameter (σ) in practice and how it affects the trade-off between training stability and sample quality. The connection to dropout regularization, as mentioned in the draft review, is intriguing and could be expanded upon to demonstrate how the perturbation technique relates to existing regularization methods in deep learning.

Questions for authors:
1. In Figure 3, the paper shows gradient norms increase as the discriminator approaches optimality. Could you provide more details about the specific GAN architecture and training procedure used? How sensitive are these results to different network architectures and hyperparameters?
2. The paper proposes using perturbation to smooth distributions. What is the theoretical relationship between the perturbation parameter σ and the expected quality of generated samples? How does the perturbation affect convergence rates compared to standard training procedures?
3. The authors state that their perturbation technique eliminates the need for careful scheduling of discriminator training. Could you provide a more direct comparison with standard training approaches (e.g., alternating steps) in terms of both sample quality and computational efficiency?

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of GAN training dynamics. The authors have provided a rigorous foundation for understanding why GANs behave as they do, and their proposed solution offers a principled approach to addressing fundamental instability issues. The theoretical analysis is sound, well-motivated, and directly addresses a critical gap in the literature, making this a valuable contribution to the field.
