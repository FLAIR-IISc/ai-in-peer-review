

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs). Rather than introducing a specific algorithm, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical framework that explains why GAN training is unstable and prone to saturation. They identify the fundamental issue as arising from the fact that both the real data distribution and the generator's distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds, making them impossible to distinguish with a perfect discriminator. The paper proves that when distributions have disjoint supports or lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator becomes constant on these manifolds, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator. The authors then introduce a theoretically grounded approach to address these issues by adding noise to the discriminator inputs, which smooths the distributions and enables meaningful gradient updates. The paper also establishes important connections to the Wasserstein metric, providing a more meaningful way to measure similarity between distributions that lie on different manifolds.

## 2. Decision
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
This paper stands out as one of the first theoretical works to rigorously analyze GAN training dynamics, addressing a fundamental problem that has plagued practical implementations. The authors successfully answer key questions about GAN instability that have been discussed in the literature but never properly formalized. The theoretical results (particularly Theorems 2.4-2.6) are novel and provide a solid foundation for understanding why standard GAN training is unstable. 

The paper's main contribution is not just the theoretical analysis itself, but the new mathematical tools it develops to study GANs. These tools allow for a deeper understanding of the fundamental challenges in GAN training and provide a principled direction for future research. The connection to the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3) is particularly significant, as it offers a meaningful way to measure similarity between distributions that lie on different manifolds, which is crucial for evaluating generative models. 

The paper's theoretical analysis is exceptionally well-motivated and places the work firmly within the existing literature on GANs and generative modeling. The authors carefully build their argument from first principles, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with basic machine learning concepts while providing substantial depth for experts.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
The paper is exceptionally well-written and theoretically sound. For further improvement, I suggest:

1. Clarify the practical implications of the noise-based approach in Section 3. While the theoretical results are compelling, a brief discussion of how this approach might be implemented in practice, including potential computational costs, would strengthen the paper's impact.

2. Provide a more detailed comparison between the proposed noise-based approach and existing stabilization techniques (such as spectral normalization, gradient penalties, or adaptive learning rates). This would help position the work more clearly within the current landscape of GAN stabilization methods.

3. Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the required noise level depend on the dimensionality of the data manifold, and how might this scale with more complex generative tasks?

## 5. Questions for Authors
To help clarify my understanding and ensure the theoretical results are fully supported, I would appreciate clarification on:

1. In Theorem 2.5, the authors claim that the gradient step ∇θEz∼p(z)[-logD(gθ(z))] corresponds to optimizing "KL(Pgθ‖Pr) - 2JSD(Pgθ‖Pr)". Could the authors provide more intuition about why this particular combination of divergences appears, and why it leads to the observed mode dropping behavior?

2. In Theorem 3.2, the authors state that the gradient has the form involving integrals over the data manifold and generator manifold. Could they elaborate on how this gradient differs from standard GAN training, particularly in terms of how it addresses the mode dropping issue?

3. The paper mentions that "the Wasserstein metric incorporates the notion of distance between the elements in the support of P and the ones in the support of Q." Could the authors provide a brief intuitive explanation of how this differs from the Jensen-Shannon divergence in practical terms when comparing two distributions on different manifolds?

Overall, this is an outstanding theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of GAN training dynamics. The paper is well-written, rigorously argued, and makes a clear case for its importance in the field. I believe it will be of substantial interest to the machine learning community and provide a solid foundation for future work on GANs.
