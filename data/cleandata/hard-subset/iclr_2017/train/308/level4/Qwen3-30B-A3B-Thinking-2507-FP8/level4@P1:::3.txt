

SUMMARY
This paper provides a theoretically rigorous analysis of the instability and saturation problems in GAN training. It identifies the fundamental reasons behind these issues by showing that when the data and generator distributions have disjoint supports (which is common as both typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and the generator gradients vanish. The paper then analyzes the alternative cost function used in practice, showing it leads to unstable updates. Finally, it proposes a solution by adding noise to the inputs to smooth the distributions, creating a more stable training process.

DECISION
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics, which is a fundamental problem in generative modeling. The key reason for acceptance is that it provides a clear theoretical explanation for the instability issues that have plagued GAN training for years, along with a principled solution that avoids the need for heuristic tuning.

SUPPORTING ARGUMENTS
The paper effectively addresses all three key questions:

1. The paper clearly identifies the specific problem: the instability and saturation issues in GAN training, which have been widely recognized but poorly understood.

2. The approach is well motivated and properly situated within the literature. The paper thoroughly reviews relevant divergence measures (KL, JSD) and establishes how the geometric properties of the data and generator distributions lead to the observed training challenges. The theoretical framework builds logically on previous work while extending it in meaningful ways.

3. The paper strongly supports its claims with rigorous theoretical proofs and experimental verification. The key theorems (2.1-2.6) are carefully proven, and Figures 2 and 3 provide empirical confirmation of the theoretical predictions about gradient vanishing and instability. The paper's core contributions—explaining why GAN training is unstable and proposing a theoretically grounded solution—are well substantiated.

The paper's main strength is its clear theoretical explanation for the persistent instability in GAN training, which has been an open question in the field. It also provides a principled approach to addressing this issue that avoids reliance on heuristic engineering.

ADDITIONAL FEEDBACK
The paper is technically strong but could benefit from minor improvements:

- Consider moving some of the detailed proofs from Section 2 to the appendix to improve readability while maintaining technical rigor.

- The relationship between the proposed noise-based solution and existing approaches (e.g., adding noise to the generator) could be better contextualized.

- The discussion of the Wasserstein metric in Section 3 could be slightly expanded to better connect it to the main theoretical contributions.

- Several minor grammatical and typographical errors need correction (e.g., "arize" should be "arise," "symetrical" should be "symmetrical," "reallistically" should be "realistically").

QUESTIONS FOR AUTHORS
