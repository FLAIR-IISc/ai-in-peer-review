

Summary: This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs). It rigorously analyzes the instability and saturation issues that plague GAN training, provides a novel theoretical foundation explaining these phenomena, and proposes a practical smoothing approach to address them. The paper's key insight is that the optimal discriminator becomes constant on the support of the generator distribution, leading to vanishing gradients, and demonstrates how adding noise to inputs can create a more stable training process.

Decision: Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to a core problem in deep generative modeling, and the proposed smoothing approach offers a principled solution to GAN training instability.

Supporting arguments: The paper successfully answers the fundamental questions about GAN training dynamics that it poses. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses a long-standing gap in the literature. The proofs of Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4, and 2.6 are particularly compelling, as they explain why traditional GAN training suffers from vanishing gradients and instability. The paper's key contribution—showing that adding noise to inputs creates continuous distributions whose Jensen-Shannon divergence can be meaningfully minimized—provides a theoretically grounded solution to a major practical problem. The connection to the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3) further strengthens the theoretical foundation. The paper's approach is both elegant and practical, with clear implications for future GAN research.

Additional feedback: The paper is technically strong but could benefit from improved organization to enhance readability. I recommend moving all proofs of Theorems 2.1, 2.2, and 2.4 to the appendix, as they are largely technical and don't significantly contribute to the main narrative. The discussion of manifolds (Section 2.1) is important but could be more concise to focus on the key insights. The authors should clarify the domain of random variables throughout, especially in Theorems 2.4 and 2.6. In Corollary 2.1, the reference to "Theorem 1.3" appears to be a typo. The paper would benefit from a more explicit connection between the theoretical results and their practical implementation in Section 3. The authors should also more clearly distinguish between the "noiseless" and "noisy" distributions throughout the paper.

Clarifying questions:
1. Theorem 2.2 mentions "closed manifolds" but doesn't specify whether they have boundaries. Could you clarify if this applies to manifolds with or without boundaries, and how boundary cases affect the analysis?
2. The paper states that "the support of Pg has to be contained in g(Z)" (Section 2). Given that Z typically has lower dimension than X, how does this affect the practical implementation of the smoothing approach, especially in terms of the required noise variance?
3. Theorem 3.3 provides a bound on the Wasserstein distance, but the authors don't discuss how to choose the optimal noise level σ. How should practitioners determine the appropriate noise variance in practice to balance between stability and fidelity?
4. In Corollary 3.2, the gradient is proportional to 2∇θJSD(Pr+‖Pg+). How does this differ from directly minimizing JSD(Pr+‖Pg+), and what are the practical implications for training?

The paper's contributions are significant and well-motivated, and it makes an important step toward theoretically understanding GAN training dynamics. The proposed smoothing approach offers a principled direction for future research and could have immediate practical impact on GAN training stability. While some technical details could be clarified, the paper's theoretical insights are substantial and well-supported.
