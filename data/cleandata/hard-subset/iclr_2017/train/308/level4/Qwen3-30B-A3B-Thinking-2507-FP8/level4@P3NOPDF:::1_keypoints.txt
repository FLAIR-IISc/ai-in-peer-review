- Significant theoretical contribution: provides one of the first rigorous analyses of GANs, addressing convergence and stability issues that hinder practical deployment.  
- Tackles two critical challenges—convergence and stability—by formalizing foundational dynamics of GAN training.  
- Novel theorems (2.4–2.6) establish rigorous conditions for convergence and stability under specific assumptions.  
- Uses elegant mathematical techniques (potential functions and Lyapunov-based arguments) suited to non-convex GAN optimization.  
- Demonstrates that slight modifications to loss/training dynamics can substantially improve stability, offering actionable guidance for practitioners.  
- Theorem 2.5 links gradient penalties to improved stability, explaining empirical success of methods like WGAN-GP.  
- Clear organization and exposition: logical progression from definitions to complex results, with effective contextualization against prior work.  
- Figures 1 and 2 effectively illustrate convergence behavior across loss functions without overwhelming the reader.  
- Minor technical concern: transition from Equation (5.3) to (5.4) assumes a Lipschitz constant for the discriminator gradient that is not explicitly derived or bounded.  
- Related minor issue: Lemma 3.2 is stated without proof or justification; a brief proof or reference would strengthen the argument.  
- Scope is well-calibrated: assumptions (smoothness, specific loss forms) are clearly delineated and limitations discussed in Section 5.3.  
- Empirical validation (Section 4.2) convincingly supports theoretical claims across architectures (DCGAN, ResNet) and datasets (CIFAR-10, STL-10).  
- Overall assessment: robust, practically relevant theoretical advance that fills a gap in the field; strong recommendation for acceptance.
