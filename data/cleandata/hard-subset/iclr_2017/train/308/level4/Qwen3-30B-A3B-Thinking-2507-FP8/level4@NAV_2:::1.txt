

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs), addressing the fundamental instability issues that have plagued their practical application. The authors systematically analyze why GANs are difficult to train, prove that the instability arises from the fact that both data and generator distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds, and introduce a novel approach to stabilize training through the strategic addition of noise to discriminator inputs. This work provides the first rigorous theoretical framework for understanding GAN behavior, moving beyond heuristic stabilization methods that dominate current practice.

I recommend acceptance. The paper successfully addresses the critical question of why GAN training is unstable, providing a theoretically sound explanation for the observed phenomena. The authors' novel theoretical framework, particularly the analysis of perfect discrimination and gradient behavior (Theorems 2.4-2.6), offers a comprehensive understanding of GAN training dynamics that has been previously lacking in the literature.

The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial and well-executed. The authors prove that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typically the case), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the generator distribution. This explains why gradients vanish as the discriminator improves, a key instability issue in GAN training. Their subsequent analysis of the alternative cost function (Theorem 2.5) reveals why it suffers from instability despite avoiding vanishing gradients. Most importantly, they propose a theoretically grounded solution by adding noise to the discriminator inputs, which smooths the distributions and creates meaningful gradients for the generator, as demonstrated in Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and makes a major contribution to the field. To further strengthen the work, the authors might consider adding a more detailed discussion of how their noise-based approach compares to existing stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties) in practical implementation. Additionally, including a brief discussion of computational considerations for different noise distributions would be valuable for practitioners.

I have a few questions to help clarify the theoretical framework:
1. In Theorem 3.3, the authors relate the Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions to the JSD of noisy distributions. How sensitive is this bound to the choice of noise distribution, and what practical implications does this have for choosing noise parameters?
2. The authors note that "the discriminator will be extremely susceptible to adversarial examples" when not using noisy samples in the generator. Could you elaborate on the specific types of adversarial examples that might arise in this context, and how the proposed solution (Corollary 3.2) specifically mitigates them?
3. In Theorem 2.5, the authors state that the gradient update corresponds to "the inverted KL minus two JSD." Could you provide additional intuition about why this particular combination of divergences would lead to the observed mode dropping behavior in GANs?
