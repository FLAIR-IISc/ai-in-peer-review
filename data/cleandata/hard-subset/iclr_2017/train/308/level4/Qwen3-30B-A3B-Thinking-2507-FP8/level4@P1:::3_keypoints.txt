- The paper analyzes GAN instability and saturation, showing disjoint supports lead to a perfect discriminator and vanishing generator gradients.  
- It shows the commonly used alternative cost leads to unstable updates in practice.  
- It proposes adding noise to inputs to smooth distributions and stabilize training.  
- Recommendation: accept — substantial theoretical contribution with a principled solution avoiding heuristics.  
- The paper clearly identifies the instability/saturation problem and why it occurs.  
- It situates the work within prior literature, reviewing divergences (KL, JSD) and geometric causes of issues.  
- Theoretical claims are supported by rigorous proofs (Theorems 2.1–2.6) and experiments (Figures 2–3).  
- Main strength: provides a clear theoretical explanation for persistent GAN instability and a principled fix.  
- Suggestion: move some detailed proofs from Section 2 to the appendix to improve readability.  
- Suggestion: better contextualize the proposed noise-based solution relative to existing noise-adding approaches.  
- Suggestion: expand the Wasserstein metric discussion to better connect it to the main theoretical contributions.  
- Minor issues: several typographical and grammatical errors need correction.  
- Question: clarify whether "support of Pg has to be contained in g(Z)" holds for all network architectures or only specific ones/activations.  
- Question: elaborate how the bound in Theorem 2.4 (Ez[‖Jθgθ(z)‖22] ≤ M2) behaves in practice for common GAN architectures.  
- Question: compare the proposed input-noise smoothing to other noise-based methods (e.g., noise on generator output) in practical effectiveness and computational cost.
