

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental challenges in training generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors rigorously analyze the training dynamics of GANs, identifying why traditional implementations suffer from instability and vanishing gradients. They demonstrate that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (a common scenario in GANs), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and constant almost everywhere on these manifolds, leading to vanishing gradients in the original formulation and unstable updates in the alternative cost function. The paper's key contribution is a theoretically grounded solution: introducing continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator, which smooths the distributions and enables stable training with meaningful gradients. The authors also introduce new theoretical tools to analyze GANs, including connections to Wasserstein metrics, and provide a principled direction for future research.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical analysis is thorough and well-motivated, addressing a critical gap in GAN theory. The proposed solution is both theoretically sound and practically relevant, directly tackling the instability problems that have plagued GAN applications. The paper successfully answers the three key questions: it clearly identifies the problem (GAN training instability), properly situates itself in the literature (providing the first rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN dynamics), and provides scientifically rigorous evidence to support its claims through both theoretical proofs and empirical validation.

The theoretical arguments are particularly strong. The authors prove that when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical in GANs), the Jensen-Shannon divergence between them is maximized regardless of how close the manifolds are. This explains why standard divergences fail to measure similarity between GAN distributions. Their analysis of gradient behavior under different cost functions is particularly compelling, showing why the original GAN formulation suffers from vanishing gradients and why the alternative cost function leads to unstable updates. The proposed solution—adding continuous noise to inputs—creates smoother distributions that avoid these theoretical pitfalls, and the authors prove this leads to meaningful gradient updates through Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2.

For improvement, I recommend adding empirical validation of the perturbation approach. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including experiments that compare standard GAN training with the proposed perturbation method. Specifically, experiments similar to Figures 2 and 3 in the paper would help demonstrate the practical benefits of the noise-based approach, showing how it prevents the instability observed in standard GAN training.

The connection to dropout regularization is intriguing and deserves more exploration. The authors note that the perturbation technique resembles dropout, but this relationship could be developed more thoroughly. A discussion of how the continuous noise approach differs from Bernoulli dropout (and why continuous noise might be more effective for GANs) would provide valuable context for practitioners.

I also recommend clarifying the practical implementation details of the noise-based approach. While the paper provides a theoretical foundation, it would be helpful to specify how the noise should be added in practice (e.g., the optimal noise distribution, variance, and when to anneal it) and to discuss computational overhead.

To clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I have a few specific questions:
1. How does the proposed noise-based approach compare to other regularization techniques commonly used in GANs (such as spectral normalization, gradient penalty, or label smoothing)?
2. The paper demonstrates that the noisy distributions Pr+ and Pg+ have meaningful divergences, but how does the choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian vs. other continuous distributions) affect the practical performance of the GAN?
3. In the practical implementation of the noise-based approach, what is the recommended schedule for annealing the noise variance, and how does this affect the final quality of generated samples?

The paper's theoretical insights are profound and will likely guide future research on GANs. By connecting the theoretical analysis to a practical solution, the authors provide a much-needed principled foundation for GAN training. The work represents a significant step toward understanding and improving generative modeling, and I believe it will be highly influential in the field. The minor empirical additions suggested would further solidify the paper's contribution.
