This paper addresses fundamental questions regarding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), a topic of significant current interest. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of instability and saturation issues encountered during GAN training, linking these to the choice of divergence function and the geometric properties of the generated and real data distributions. They then propose a smoothing-based approach to mitigate these problems, offering a principled direction for future research.

**Accept with Minor Revisions**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the notoriously difficult training process of GANs. The work is theoretically sound, raises important questions, and provides insightful answers, backed by a rigorous mathematical treatment. The proposed smoothing technique offers a promising avenue for improving GAN training stability.

The paper's strengths lie in its strong theoretical foundation and its attempt to bridge the gap between theoretical analysis and practical considerations. The use of tools from analysis and differential topology is particularly noteworthy. The identified sources of instability are clearly explained, and the proposed solution is intuitive and well-motivated. The authors successfully highlight the limitations of existing approaches and propose a novel direction for future research. 

However, the paper is quite lengthy and technical, which might limit its accessibility to a broader audience. While the detailed derivations are valuable, some parts could benefit from conciseness. Specifically, proofs that rely on standard results could be moved to the appendix or accompanied by relevant citations. The numbering of theorems also seems inconsistent and should be checked. 

Here are some specific suggestions for improvement:

*   **Section 2.1:** Consider shortening this section by moving proofs to the appendix.
*   **Page 2, second bullet:** Rephrase to: "This also means that P<sub>g</sub> is smaller than the data distribution in some regions, which in turn will make the KL divergence non-zero."
*   **Page 2:** Correct "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" to "for generating not plausibly looking pictures."
*   **Lemma 1:** Note that the lemma's generality could be broadened.
*   **Theorem 2.1:**  This result appears to be a standard result in analysis; a citation would be helpful.
*   **Theorem 2.4:** Briefly remind the reader about the prior distribution p(z).
*   **Lemma 2:** Similar to Theorem 2.1, this appears to be a standard result and could benefit from a citation. Clarify the domain of the random variables involved.
*   **Page 6:** Correct "relly" to "rely."
*   **Theorem 2.2:** Clarify whether the closed manifolds are assumed to have a boundary or not.
*   **Corollary 2.1:** Correct the reference to "Theorem 1.3" to the correct theorem number.
*   **Theorem 2.5:** Change "Therefore" to "Then."
*   **Theorem 2.6:** Change "Is a..." to "is a..."
*   **Theorem numbering:** Ensure consistent theorem numbering throughout the paper.

I have several clarifying questions for the authors:

*   **Practical Implementation:** While the theory is compelling, what are the practical considerations for implementing the proposed smoothing approach? Specifically, how should the noise level be chosen, and how does this affect performance?
*   **Computational Cost:** What is the computational overhead associated with the smoothing approach, and how does it compare to existing GAN training techniques?
*   **Empirical Validation:** While the paper mentions targeted experiments, could the authors provide more detailed results demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach on standard benchmark datasets?
*   **Generality of Lemma 1:** Could you elaborate on the extent to which Lemma 1 generalizes to more complex network architectures?
*   **Relation to Existing Work:** How does your approach relate to existing methods for stabilizing GAN training, such as Wasserstein GANs or spectral normalization?
