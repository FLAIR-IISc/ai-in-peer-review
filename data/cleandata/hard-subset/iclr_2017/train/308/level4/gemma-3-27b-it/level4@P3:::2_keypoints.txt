- Overall contribution: Rigorous theoretical investigation into GAN training dynamics explaining instability and saturation.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions; strong theory but needs stronger empirical validation.  
- Core strength: Well-structured proofs linking failure modes to geometry of data and generator distributions.  
- Key insight: Identification of perfect discrimination on low-dimensional supports as a root cause of vanishing/unstable gradients.  
- Proposed solution: Injecting noise to smooth distributions and avoid perfect discrimination.  
- Theoretical development: Analysis of noise-injected training, connection to Wasserstein distance and soft metrics.  
- Empirical shortcoming: Preliminary experiments are limited and insufficient given the theoretical depth.  
- Suggested experiments: Compare standard GAN, noise-injected GAN, and dropout across benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) with quantitative metrics (Inception Score, FID).  
- Suggested analyses: Show gradient behavior during training with/without noise (plots like Figures 2 & 3).  
- Dropout connection: Expand and clarify similarities/differences between the proposed noise injection and dropout, and consider framing as data augmentation.  
- Noise choices: Explore noise distributions beyond Gaussian (e.g., uniform, Laplacian) and report effects on stability and sample quality.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Discuss sensitivity to noise level (standard deviation) and practical tuning guidance.  
- Clarification requested: Provide an intuitive explanation of why Wasserstein distance is suitable (Theorem 3.3) for readers unfamiliar with optimal transport.  
- Questions for authors: Implementation challenges and dataset/architecture-specific performance; practical implications and tightness of Theorem 3.3 bounds relative to noise choice; potential drawbacks of viewing noise injection as data augmentation; and whether perturbation prevents mode collapse and why.
