This paper provides a valuable contribution toward a more thorough understanding of generative adversarial network (GAN) training dynamics. Through novel theoretical analysis, the authors illuminate the causes of gradient vanishing in the original GAN formulation and instability in a common variant. Critically, they propose a perturbation-based approach to mitigate these issues, offering a principled direction for future research. I recommend acceptance based on the significance of the theoretical insights and the potential impact of the proposed solution.

The paper rigorously analyzes the challenges inherent in GAN training, demonstrating why updates can degrade as the discriminator improves, and explaining the instability that plagues many GAN implementations. The authors convincingly link these problems to the characteristics of the distributions being modeled and the limitations of the commonly used cost functions. The introduction of a perturbation technique, grounded in theory, offers a compelling path toward more stable and reliable GAN training.

However, while the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would benefit from further empirical validation. The connection between the proposed perturbation method and existing techniques like dropout is intriguing but not fully explored. A discussion of this relationship would strengthen the paper. Moreover, supporting the theoretical claims with experimental results – analogous to Figures 2 and 3 – demonstrating the impact of the perturbation on training stability and sample quality would significantly enhance the impact of the work. Specifically, visualizing the gradient behavior and generated samples with and without the perturbation would be extremely valuable. It would also be beneficial to explore the sensitivity of the method to the choice of perturbation distribution and magnitude.

To further clarify my understanding and increase my confidence in the assessment, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The perturbation technique bears a resemblance to dropout. Could the authors elaborate on the connection, and discuss potential benefits or drawbacks of viewing the perturbation as a stochastic regularization method akin to dropout?
2.  Beyond the theoretical analysis, is there empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed perturbation technique? If so, could the authors include experimental results—similar to Figures 2 and 3—that demonstrate its impact on training stability and the quality of generated samples?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the perturbed GAN to the choice of perturbation distribution (e.g., Gaussian, uniform) and its scale (e.g., the standard deviation of the Gaussian)? Are there guidelines for selecting appropriate parameters for the perturbation?
