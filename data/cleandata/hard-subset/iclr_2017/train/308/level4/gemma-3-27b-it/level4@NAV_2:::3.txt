SUMMARY
This paper tackles fundamental questions regarding the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs), addressing the instability issues that plague their optimization. It provides a theoretical analysis of these problems, connecting them to the choice of divergence functions and the geometric properties of the distributions involved. The authors propose a novel approach based on smoothing to mitigate these instabilities and introduce tools for further study in this area.

DECISION
I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form. While the theoretical contributions are potentially valuable and the problem addressed is highly relevant, the presentation is excessively dense and lacks sufficient clarity to fully appreciate the significance of the results. The extensive use of mathematical formalism, without sufficient contextualization, hinders accessibility and makes it difficult to assess the practical implications of the proposed approach.

SUPPORTING ARGUMENTS
The paper identifies critical issues in GAN training, specifically linking instability to the properties of the Jensen-Shannon divergence and the potential for perfect discrimination when generator and data distributions have low-dimensional supports. The proposed smoothing technique, building upon the Wasserstein metric and noise injection, offers a promising direction for stabilizing training. However, the sheer volume of technical detail, including numerous theorems and proofs, overshadows the core ideas. Many results, while potentially correct, feel unnecessarily generalized or lack sufficient motivation regarding their relevance to practical GAN training. The numerous theorems and lemmas require substantial effort to verify, and some appear to rely on well-established results without proper citation (e.g., Theorem 2.1, Lemma 2). The connections between the theoretical analysis and potential practical improvements aren't always clear. The final section on softer metrics is particularly promising but feels underdeveloped.

ADDITIONAL FEEDBACK
This work has the potential to make a significant contribution to the theory of GAN training. To that end, I suggest the following improvements:

*   **Streamline the presentation:** Reduce the level of mathematical detail in the main text. Move proofs to the appendix and focus on conveying the intuition behind the results.
*   **Provide more context:** Clearly articulate the practical implications of the theoretical findings. How do these results translate to improved training procedures or architectures for GANs?
*   **Connect to existing work:** More explicitly relate the contributions to the existing literature on GAN training and optimal transport.
*   **Clarify notation and terminology:** Ensure consistency in notation and provide clear definitions for all terms used.
*   **Expand on the smoothing approach:** Provide more details on how the proposed smoothing technique can be implemented in practice and evaluate its performance on standard GAN benchmarks.
*   **Address minor errors:** Correct the typographical errors identified (e.g., "relly" to "rely," "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" to "for generating not plausibly looking pictures"). Verify theorem numbers for consistency (e.g. Theorem 1.3).

QUESTIONS FOR THE AUTHORS
1.  Could you elaborate on the specific choice of noise distribution used in the smoothing approach and justify its advantages over other possible distributions?
2.  How does the computational cost of the proposed smoothing technique compare to existing GAN training methods?
3.  Have you conducted any preliminary experiments to validate the effectiveness of the smoothing approach on standard GAN benchmarks? If so, could you share the results?
4.  What are the limitations of the theoretical analysis presented in the paper? Are there any assumptions that may not hold in practice?
5.  The paper frequently refers to manifolds. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the dimensionality of these manifolds is crucial for understanding GAN training instability?
