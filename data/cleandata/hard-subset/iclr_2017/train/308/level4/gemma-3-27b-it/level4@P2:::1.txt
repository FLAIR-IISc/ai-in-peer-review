This is a strong submission addressing a critical aspect of modern neural networks – generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors provide a theoretical analysis of GAN convergence and stability, areas of significant practical importance. To the best of my knowledge, this is among the first papers to offer deep theoretical insights into GANs, distinguishing it from the majority of submissions in this field. The instability issues inherent in early GAN architectures are particularly well-addressed. Theorems 2.4–2.6 are novel and employ interesting mathematical techniques. I have minor technical questions regarding the proof of Theorem 2.5, but these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Decision: Accept** with enthusiasm. The paper tackles a fundamental problem in GAN research – understanding *why* they are so difficult to train – and provides significant theoretical progress towards a solution. The insights gained are likely to be highly influential in guiding future research and development of more stable and reliable GAN architectures.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully identifies key factors contributing to GAN training instability, specifically focusing on the behavior of the discriminator and its impact on the generator’s gradient. The theoretical results regarding perfect discrimination (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are particularly insightful, explaining why standard training approaches often fail. The analysis of the original and alternative cost functions (Section 2.2) is thorough and demonstrates a clear understanding of the underlying mathematical principles. The theorems regarding vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) and instability (Theorem 2.6) rigorously explain observed training difficulties.

Furthermore, the paper doesn’t just diagnose the problems; it proposes a promising direction for improvement (Section 3) by introducing the concept of softer metrics and distributions, laying the groundwork for more robust training procedures. The connection to Wasserstein distance and the proposed use of noise injection are compelling and provide a concrete path forward for researchers. The authors acknowledge limitations and open questions, suggesting avenues for future investigation.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting the decision):**

*   **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, some of the notation (especially in the proofs) could be made more accessible. Briefly defining key symbols as they are introduced would be helpful.
*   **Intuition for Theorems:** Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs of Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 2.5 and 2.6 would strengthen the paper’s impact. While the mathematical rigor is commendable, a more accessible explanation would broaden its readership.
*   **Experimental Validation:** While the paper emphasizes theoretical contributions, some limited experimental validation of the proposed techniques in Section 3 would be beneficial. Even preliminary results demonstrating the effectiveness of noise injection could significantly strengthen the claims.
*   **Relationship to Recent Work:** The paper could benefit from briefly discussing how its findings relate to more recent advancements in GAN training, such as spectral normalization or gradient penalties, even if only to contrast the approaches.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the proof of Theorem 2.5: Could you clarify the assumptions made about the properties of the optimal discriminator D*? Specifically, how does the choice of D* impact the derivation of equation (3)?
2.  In Section 3, you propose adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. What considerations should be given when choosing the distribution of the noise (e.g., Gaussian vs. other distributions)? Are there any practical limitations to using this approach with high-dimensional data?
3.  How does the proposed framework relate to existing techniques for stabilizing GAN training, such as gradient clipping or weight normalization? Are there potential synergies between these approaches?
4.  The paper focuses primarily on the theoretical analysis of GAN training. Do you anticipate any challenges in translating these theoretical findings into practical improvements for real-world applications? If so, what are those challenges?
