- Decision: Reject — important theoretical questions but analysis is overly complex/long and empirical support is insufficient.  
- Topic: Studies GAN training instability, focusing on asymmetric divergences (KL) and mode dropping.  
- Theoretical strength: Section 2 formalizes instability, identifies “perfect discriminators,” and analyzes resulting gradient behavior.  
- Theoretical weakness: Proofs are dense, rely on advanced math, and lack sufficient intuition or motivation for readers.  
- Proposed fix: Use noise smoothing to move distributions toward Wasserstein-like behavior; connections to contrastive divergence and adversarial-example mitigation are noted.  
- Proposal critique: Presentation of the smoothing approach is incomplete and lacks concrete practical examples.  
- Empirical gap: Insufficient experimental evaluation — needs thorough comparisons to existing GAN training methods on benchmarks.  
- Presentation issue: Paper is too long and disorganized; many proofs should be moved to an appendix and the narrative streamlined.  
- Requested revisions: Improve clarity and intuition, define key terms, and strengthen empirical validation with metrics and benchmark results.  
- Minor/copy-edit issues: Multiple typos, inconsistent theorem numbering, missing citations, ambiguous lemma/theorem statements, and suggestions for specific wording fixes.  
- Technical clarifications needed: State prior p(z), specify domains and manifold boundary assumptions, and note when lemmas/theorems are standard results with citations.  
- Questions for authors: Explain intuitively how smoothing avoids perfect discrimination; report computational cost vs. standard GANs.  
- Questions for authors: Describe which noise distributions were tried (e.g., Gaussian covariances) and their effects.  
- Questions for authors: Provide planned/actual experimental details and evaluation metrics to validate the method.
