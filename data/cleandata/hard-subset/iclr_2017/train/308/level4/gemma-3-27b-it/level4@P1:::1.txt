This is a strong submission regarding one of the most important and recently introduced methods in neural networks – generative adversarial networks. The authors analyze theoretically the convergence of GANs and discuss their stability. Both are very important topics. To the best of my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to provide a theoretical analysis of GANs, and, unlike many submissions in the field, it offers deep theoretical insight into this architecture. The stability issues surrounding GANs are extremely important, as the initial proposed GAN architectures were often unstable and difficult to train in practice. Theorems 2.4–2.6 are novel, and the mathematical techniques employed are interesting. I have some technical questions regarding the proof of Theorem 2.5, but these are relatively minor.

**Decision: Accept** with minor revisions. The paper tackles a crucial problem in the field – understanding the training dynamics of GANs – and provides valuable theoretical insights into their instability. The proposed analysis is rigorous and the theorems presented are novel contributions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper addresses a fundamental question in the field: *Why are GANs so difficult to train?* The authors go beyond empirical observations and offer a theoretical framework to explain the instability and saturation issues commonly encountered in GAN training. The identified problems with both the original and alternative cost functions (Theorem 2.5, 2.6) are particularly insightful and explain many of the observed practical difficulties. The theorems concerning perfect discrimination (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) provide a clear understanding of the conditions under which training fails. The introduction of softer metrics (Section 3) and the accompanying theoretical analysis offer a promising direction for addressing these challenges. The connection to Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is particularly compelling. The rigorous mathematical treatment is a significant strength, setting this work apart from many other papers in the GAN literature.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas where clarification or further elaboration would be beneficial.

*   **Theorem 2.5:** The proof of Theorem 2.5 would benefit from a slightly more detailed explanation of the steps involved.
*   **Practical Implications:** While the theoretical results are compelling, a more explicit discussion of the practical implications of the proposed approach would be helpful. For instance, how would one implement the proposed noise injection strategy in practice? What specific types of noise distributions are most suitable, and how would one choose the appropriate noise level?
*   **Experimental Validation:** While the authors mention targeted experiments to verify assumptions, more detailed experimental results illustrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach would strengthen the paper. It would be valuable to see empirical evidence demonstrating the improved stability and convergence achieved with the proposed noise injection strategy.
* **Clarification on notation:** The notation can be slightly dense in places. While mathematically precise, some readers may benefit from more descriptive variable names or more frequent reminders of the definitions used.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the implementation of the noise injection strategy proposed in Section 3? Specifically, what type of noise distribution would you recommend, and how would you select the appropriate noise level (σ)?
2.  The paper focuses on the theoretical aspects of GAN training. Do you have any preliminary experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in improving GAN stability and convergence? If so, could you please share some details?
3.  In Theorem 2.6, you state that the gradient updates have a Cauchy distribution. Could you elaborate on the practical consequences of this finding? Does this suggest that alternative optimization algorithms might be more suitable for training GANs?
4. The discussion of the Wasserstein distance is very interesting. How does this approach compare to recent work on Wasserstein GANs (WGANs)? What are the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method relative to WGANs?
