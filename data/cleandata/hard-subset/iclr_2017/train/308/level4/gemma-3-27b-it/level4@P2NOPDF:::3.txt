## Review of Generative Adversarial Networks

**SUMMARY**

This paper investigates key challenges in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), specifically addressing the consequences of employing an asymmetric divergence function and the resulting instabilities during training. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of these issues and propose a smoothing-based approach as a potential remedy.

**PROS**

The paper presents a strong theoretical foundation for understanding GAN training dynamics. It raises important questions about existing methodologies and offers insightful answers. Notably, the work leverages concepts from analysis and differential topology in a compelling manner to analyze GAN behavior. The proposed approach offers promising avenues for mitigating instability, a significant obstacle in practical GAN training.

**CONS**

The paper is somewhat lengthy and relies on a high level of technical detail, which may limit its accessibility. While some aspects of the analysis and their consequences could benefit from further development – a perfectly acceptable direction for future work – the current presentation feels dense. 

**OVERALL ASSESSMENT & RECOMMENDATION**

This is a theoretically sound paper that tackles an important problem in the GAN research area. The theoretical contributions are valuable, and the proposed smoothing technique appears promising. While the technical depth and length might present a barrier to some readers, the insights gained from this work are worthwhile. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the minor comments detailed below.

**MINOR COMMENTS**

* **Section 2.1:** Consider streamlining this section to improve readability. Moving the detailed proofs to an appendix would make the main text more concise and focused.

* **Section 3:** The proposed solution in Section 3 is commendably intuitive and straightforward. 

* **Page 2, second bullet point:** The phrasing “P_g is smaller than the data distribution in some other x” could be clarified. Rephrasing to emphasize that the support of P_g may not fully cover the data distribution would improve understanding. The connection to a non-zero KL divergence should be explicitly stated.

* **Page 2:** Correct the grammatical error: change “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” to “for generating not plausibly looking pictures.”

* **Lemma 1:** The lemma’s generality could be broadened. Exploring this could strengthen the result.

* **Theorem 2.1:** The proof appears to rely on standard results from analysis. Citing a relevant reference could reduce redundancy.

* **Theorem 2.4:** Briefly remind the reader of the probability distribution *p(z)* to enhance clarity and self-containment.

* **Lemma 2:** Similar to Theorem 2.1, this lemma appears to be a standard analytical result. A citation could suffice.  Furthermore, please explicitly state the domain of the random variables involved.

* **Typo:** Correct “relly” to “rely.”

* **Theorem 2.2:** Clarify whether the closed manifolds are assumed to have a boundary. This point was raised in the questions accompanying the submission and should be addressed.

* **Corollary 2.1:** The reference to “assumptions of Theorem 1.3” is inaccurate. Theorem 1.3 was not found in the current manuscript. Please verify and correct the theorem number.

* **Theorem 2.5:** Replace “Therefore” with “Then” for smoother flow.

* **Theorem 2.6:** Change “Is a...” to “is a.”

* **Theorem Numbering:** The numbering of theorems appears inconsistent and potentially confusing. Please ensure a clear and logical numbering scheme throughout the paper.
