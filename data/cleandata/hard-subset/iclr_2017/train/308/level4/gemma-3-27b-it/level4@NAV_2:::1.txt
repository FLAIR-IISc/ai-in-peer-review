This is a strong submission regarding one of the most important and recently introduced methods in neural networks – generative adversarial networks. The authors analyze theoretically the training dynamics of GANs and discuss the sources of instability. To the best of my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training, offering deep insight into this architecture, unlike many empirical submissions in the field. The stability issues surrounding GANs are critical, given the initial difficulties in achieving stable training with the original architectures. Theorems 2.1 through 2.6 are novel and employ interesting mathematical techniques. I have some technical questions regarding the derivation within Theorem 2.5, but these are relatively minor.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a significant theoretical contribution, the core arguments regarding instability and vanishing gradients rely on assumptions about the structure of the distributions (low-dimensional manifolds, disjoint supports) that, while plausible, are not sufficiently justified or empirically validated within the paper. The link between the theoretical findings and practical GAN training remains somewhat tenuous.

The paper convincingly identifies potential sources of instability in GAN training. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 demonstrate that perfect discriminators can emerge when the generator and data distributions have limited support, leading to vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) or unstable updates (Theorem 2.6). The analysis of the original and alternative cost functions (Section 2.2) is insightful, highlighting the potential for mode dropping and the lack of guaranteed gradient flow. The introduction of a noise-based approach (Section 3) and the associated theoretical results (Theorems 3.2 and 3.3) offer a promising direction for addressing these issues by softening the divergences and providing a more stable training signal. However, the leap from these theoretical results to a practical solution for general GAN training feels underdeveloped. The reliance on strong assumptions regarding the data and generator manifolds limits the applicability of the findings.

I have several questions that would strengthen the paper:

1.  The paper frequently assumes distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds. What empirical evidence supports this assumption for real-world datasets commonly used in GAN training (e.g., ImageNet, CelebA)? Could the authors provide some initial experiments to assess the dimensionality of the data manifold?
2.  Theorem 2.5 states that the gradient update with the alternative cost function optimizes a specific combination of KL and JSD. The authors acknowledge this may lead to mode dropping. How can this be mitigated in practice? Are there any modifications to the training procedure or network architecture that could address this issue?
3.  The proposed noise injection (Section 3) relies on the choice of noise distribution. How sensitive are the results to the variance of the noise? Is there an optimal range for the noise level, and how can this be determined in practice?
4.  The Wasserstein distance (Section 3) is introduced as a potentially useful metric. How does the computational cost of evaluating and minimizing this metric compare to the standard GAN cost functions? Are there practical challenges associated with using the Wasserstein distance for large-scale image generation?
5. Could the authors provide more intuition behind the mathematical formalism of the perfect discrimination theorems? A geometric interpretation of why and how perfect discrimination occurs, perhaps with illustrative examples, could greatly improve clarity.
