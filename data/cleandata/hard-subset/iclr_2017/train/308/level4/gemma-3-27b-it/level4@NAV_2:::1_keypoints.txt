- Strong, novel theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics with rigorous theorems (2.1–2.6) offering deep insight.  
- Reviewer recommends rejection despite contributions because core arguments depend on strong, under-validated assumptions about distribution structure.  
- Main theoretical finding: perfect discriminators can emerge when generator/data distributions have limited or disjoint support.  
- Perfect discrimination leads to vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) and unstable updates (Theorem 2.6).  
- Analysis of original vs. alternative GAN cost functions (Section 2.2) shows risk of mode dropping and lack of guaranteed gradient flow.  
- Proposed noise-injection approach (Section 3) and Theorems 3.2–3.3 suggest softening divergences and providing more stable training signals.  
- Practical relevance is unclear: theoretical fixes (noise/Wasserstein) are promising but not sufficiently developed or empirically validated.  
- Key weakness: heavy reliance on low-dimensional manifold and disjoint-support assumptions limits applicability to real datasets.  
- Requested experiment: provide empirical evidence or experiments assessing data manifold dimensionality on datasets like ImageNet/CelebA.  
- Technical question: Theorem 2.5’s claim that the alternative cost optimizes a KL+JSD combination may induce mode dropping—how to mitigate in practice?  
- Question on noise injection: sensitivity to noise distribution/variance and how to choose optimal noise level in practice.  
- Question on Wasserstein distance: computational cost and practical challenges for large-scale image generation compared to standard GAN losses.  
- Suggestion: add geometric intuition and illustrative examples to clarify the perfect-discrimination theorems.
