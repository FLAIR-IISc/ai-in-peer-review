This paper makes a valuable contribution to providing a clearer understanding of generative adversarial network (GAN) training procedures. With new insights into the training dynamics of GANs, and their variants, the authors reveal the reasons for vanishing gradients in the original GAN and instability in its variant. More importantly, they provide a means to avoid these difficulties by introducing perturbation. I believe this paper will inspire more principled research in this direction.

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper due to its strong theoretical foundation and potential impact on the field. The analysis provides a compelling explanation for observed training difficulties and proposes a promising direction for improvement.

The paper rigorously analyzes the challenges in GAN training, specifically focusing on the issues of instability and saturation. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 establish clear conditions under which perfect discrimination occurs, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates as detailed in Theorems 2.4 and 2.6. The introduction of noise as a means to mitigate these issues, and the subsequent development of Theorems 3.2 and 3.3, provides a theoretically sound approach. The proofs are detailed and the arguments are logically presented. The connection drawn to existing divergences like KL and JSD strengthens the paper’s position within the broader landscape of generative modeling.

Several avenues for future work are highlighted, indicating the authors’ awareness of the ongoing challenges and potential extensions of this research. To further enhance the paper, it would be beneficial to discuss the relationship between the proposed perturbation technique and existing regularization methods like dropout. While the paper focuses on theoretical underpinnings, including empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the perturbation method would significantly strengthen the claims. Specifically, figures comparable to Figures 2 and 3 illustrating the impact of the perturbation on gradient behavior and sample quality would be valuable. Additionally, exploring the sensitivity of the approach to the choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian versus other distributions) could provide further insights.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions that the perturbations aim to avoid measure 0 adversarial examples. Could you elaborate on how the choice of noise distribution (e.g., variance in the Gaussian case) influences the robustness against adversarial attacks beyond simply avoiding perfect discrimination?
2.  Theorem 3.3 relates the Wasserstein distance to the JSD of perturbed distributions. How does the choice of perturbation scale (σ) influence the trade-off between approximating the true Wasserstein distance and maintaining computational feasibility?
3.  In the discussion of Theorem 2.5, you state that the gradient updates tend to promote mode dropping. Can you explain how the proposed perturbation technique specifically addresses and mitigates this issue in practice? Is there a quantitative measure of mode coverage that you could track to demonstrate improvement?
