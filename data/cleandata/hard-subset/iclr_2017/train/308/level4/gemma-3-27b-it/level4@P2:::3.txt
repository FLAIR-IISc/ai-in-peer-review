This paper tackles fundamental questions regarding the challenging training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs). It investigates the theoretical underpinnings of instability and mode collapse, stemming from the asymmetric nature of the divergences commonly used in GAN training. The authors then propose a novel approach based on smoothing the distributions to mitigate these issues and provide a more principled framework for analysis.

**Accept with minor revisions.** The paper offers a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training, identifying key sources of instability, and proposing a potential path towards more robust training procedures. While the paper is technically dense, the insights gained are significant and the proposed approach appears promising.

The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous mathematical treatment of GAN training. By leveraging concepts from analysis and differential topology, the authors provide a deeper understanding of why GANs are difficult to train and offer a solid theoretical justification for their proposed solution. The analysis of the original and alternative cost functions is particularly insightful, clearly explaining the issues of vanishing gradients and instability. The introduction of a smoothing approach, along with the associated theoretical analysis and the connection to Wasserstein distance, presents a compelling direction for future research. 

However, the paper’s technical depth could be a barrier to accessibility for some readers. Certain sections, particularly those containing proofs, could be streamlined or moved to the appendix. The frequent referencing of theorems without immediate context (e.g., Corollary 2.1 referencing “Theorem 1.3,” which does not exist) is also somewhat distracting. Furthermore, while the proposed smoothing approach is theoretically sound, empirical validation beyond the figures included would strengthen the paper. 

Here are some specific suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Streamline technical details:** Consider moving proofs of established results (e.g., Theorem 2.1, Lemma 2) to the appendix. Focus the main text on conveying the core ideas and insights.
*   **Clarify notation and references:** Ensure consistent and accurate referencing of theorems and lemmas throughout the paper.
*   **Expand empirical evaluation:** Include additional experimental results to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed smoothing approach on more diverse datasets and network architectures.
*   **Address reviewer comments:**
    *   Page 2, second bullet: Change "This also means that P_g is smaller than the data distribution in some other x" to “This implies that P_g assigns lower probability to some regions than the data distribution.”
    *   Page 2: Change “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” to “for generating not plausibly looking pictures.”
    *   Lemma 1: Acknowledge that the lemma’s generality could be expanded.
    *   Theorem 2.4: Briefly remind the reader about the prior distribution p(z).
    *   Theorem 2.2: Explicitly state whether the closed manifolds have a boundary or not.
    *   Theorem 2.5: Change “Therefore” to “Then”.
    *   Theorem 2.6: Change “Is a…” to “is a”.
    *   Correct the typographical errors: "relly" to "rely".

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with implementing the proposed smoothing approach, and how it compares to existing GAN training techniques?
2.  The paper mentions that the smoothing approach connects to the Wasserstein distance. Could you discuss potential limitations or challenges when applying this connection in practice, especially for high-dimensional data?
3.  The analysis primarily focuses on the generator's perspective. How does the proposed approach impact the training dynamics of the discriminator? Are there any specific considerations for optimizing the discriminator alongside the smoothed generator?
4.  What are the implications of the findings regarding the Jensen-Shannon divergence for other generative models that rely on similar divergence-based training objectives (e.g., VAEs)?
