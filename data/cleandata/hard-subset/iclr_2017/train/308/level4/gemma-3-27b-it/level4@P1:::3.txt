This paper addresses important questions about the difficulties in training generative adversarial networks. It discusses the consequences of using an asymmetric divergence function and sources of instability in training GANs, and then proposes an alternative approach using a smoothing technique.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper raises important theoretical questions about GAN training and attempts to provide answers, the complexity of the analysis and the length of the presentation hinder clear understanding and impact. The theoretical contributions, while potentially valuable, are not sufficiently supported by compelling empirical evidence to warrant acceptance at this time.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The central issue the paper tackles is understanding the unstable behavior of GAN training. The authors correctly identify the asymmetry of the KL divergence and the potential for mode dropping as key problems. The theoretical analysis, particularly in Section 2, is a significant attempt to formalize these issues, linking them to the geometry of the data and generator distributions. The identification of “perfect discriminators” and the subsequent analysis of gradient behavior are insightful. However, the proofs are dense and often rely on advanced mathematical concepts without sufficient intuition or motivation for the reader.

The proposed solution in Section 3, using noise to smooth the distributions and move towards Wasserstein distance, is promising. The connection to contrastive divergence and the mitigation of adversarial examples are well articulated. However, the presentation feels incomplete. While the authors state the theoretical benefits, a more thorough empirical evaluation comparing performance with existing GAN training techniques is needed to validate the approach. The discussion lacks concrete examples demonstrating the practical impact of the proposed method.

The paper’s length is a significant drawback. Much of the theoretical development, particularly the detailed proofs, could be moved to an appendix to improve readability. The organization sometimes feels disjointed, jumping between different theoretical frameworks without a clear narrative flow. 

**Additional Feedback:**

This paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GANs. However, several improvements are needed.

*   **Streamline the presentation:** Reduce the length by moving proofs to an appendix and focusing on the key insights and their implications.
*   **Improve clarity:** Add more intuition and explanations for the mathematical concepts used. Define key terms more clearly.
*   **Strengthen empirical validation:** Include a more extensive experimental evaluation comparing the proposed method to existing GAN training techniques. Demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach on standard benchmark datasets.
*   **Address minor issues:**
    *   On page 2, second bullet: “This also means that Pg is smaller than the data distribution in some other x, which in turn will make the KL divergence non zero.” -> “This means that Pg(x) can be higher than Pr(x) in other regions, leading to a non-zero KL divergence.”
    *   On page 2, “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” should be “for generating not plausibly looking pictures”.
    *   Lemma 1 could hold in more generality; consider mentioning this.
    *   Theorem 2.1 relies on well-known analytical results; a citation would be helpful.
    *   In Theorem 2.4, remind the reader about the prior distribution p(z).
    *   Lemma 2 appears to be a standard result in analysis; a citation would be sufficient.
    *   Specify the domain of the random variables in Lemma 2.
    *   Correct “relly” to “rely”.
    *   In Theorem 2.2, clarify whether the closed manifolds have a boundary.
    *   In Corollary 2.1, change "assumptions of Theorem 1.3" to "assumptions of Theorem 2.4".
    *   In Theorem 2.5, replace "Therefore" with "Then".
    *   In Theorem 2.6, replace "Is a…" with "is a…".
    *   The numbering of theorems seems inconsistent.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the proposed smoothing approach (adding noise) helps to avoid the issues identified in Section 2? Specifically, how does this approach mitigate the problem of perfect discrimination?
2.  What is the computational cost associated with the proposed method compared to standard GAN training techniques?
3.  What specific choices of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian with different covariance matrices) have you explored, and how do these choices affect the performance of the method?
4.  Can you provide more details about the experiments you have conducted (or plan to conduct) to validate the proposed approach? What metrics will you use to evaluate the performance of the GANs trained using your method?
