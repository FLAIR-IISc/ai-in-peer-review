This paper makes a valuable contribution to providing a clearer understanding of generative adversarial network (GAN) training. With new insights into the training dynamics of GANs, and their variants, the authors reveal the reasons for vanishing gradients in the original GAN and instability in its variant. More importantly, they provide a way to avoid these difficulties by introducing perturbation. I believe this paper will inspire more principled research in this direction.

I **accept** the paper. The core theoretical arguments are convincing and address a fundamental open question in the field – why GAN training is so unstable. The proposed approach, leveraging noise to smooth the optimization landscape, is well-motivated and offers a promising direction for future work.

The paper rigorously analyzes the training dynamics of GANs, identifying the issues of perfect discrimination and vanishing/unstable gradients. The authors demonstrate that, under certain conditions, the optimal discriminator becomes too “perfect,” leading to uninformative gradients for the generator. They then show how the commonly used alternative cost function suffers from instability issues. The proposed solution, adding noise to smooth the distributions, is theoretically grounded and appears to be a significant step toward more stable GAN training. The inclusion of proofs and detailed explanations adds to the paper’s strength.

I am very interested in the perturbation trick to avoid gradient instability and vanishing gradients. In fact, this is quite related to the dropout trick, where the perturbation can be viewed as a Bernoulli distribution. It would be great if the connection could be discussed. Besides the theoretical analysis, is there any empirical study to justify this trick? Could you please add experiments similar to Figures 2 and 3 for the perturbed GAN to enable comparison?

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper, not part of the decision):**

*   **Connection to Dropout:** Exploring the relationship between the proposed perturbation method and dropout would strengthen the paper. Discussing whether similar theoretical benefits can be expected from dropout in GANs would be insightful.
*   **Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical analysis is compelling, including empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method would be crucial. Adding experiments similar to Figures 2 and 3, showing the gradient behavior and sample quality of a GAN trained with the proposed noise injection, would substantially strengthen the paper.
*   **Wasserstein Distance and Practical Implementation:** The introduction of the Wasserstein distance as a metric for comparing distributions is a valuable addition. However, a brief discussion of the practical challenges in estimating and optimizing the Wasserstein distance could be beneficial.
*   **Clarify Notation:** While generally clear, some of the notation (e.g., the use of different norms) could benefit from more explicit definitions and explanations.
*   **Discussion of Hyperparameters:** The sensitivity of the proposed method to the variance of the noise could be discussed. Providing guidance on how to choose this hyperparameter would be helpful for practitioners.
