- Presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics, targeting instability and saturation rather than a new architecture.  
- Shows real and generated data supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds and are likely disjoint or intersect on a measure-zero set (Theorems 2.1, 2.2).  
- Explains that disjoint supports make the optimal discriminator perfect, causing vanishing gradients in the original GAN objective (Theorem 2.4).  
- Analyzes the alternative -log(D) cost and proves it optimizes a different objective that can induce mode dropping (Theorem 2.5).  
- Demonstrates that gradients from -log(D) near an optimal discriminator follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance, explaining extreme instability (Theorem 2.6).  
- Proposes adding instance noise to inputs to smooth distributions, produce overlapping supports, and yield stable gradients (Section 3).  
- Connects the noise-injection approach to the Wasserstein distance, offering a new perspective for evaluating/optimizing GANs (Theorem 3.3).  
- Strongly recommends acceptance, citing two main contributions: principled theoretical explanation for GAN instabilities and a practical, theory-motivated mitigation.  
- Highlights the paper’s core strength: methodical, rigorous theoretical development using manifold theory to ground the analysis.  
- Suggests adding empirical experiments validating that noise injection stabilizes gradient norms and training behavior.  
- Recommends briefly discussing connections between continuous input noise and other noise-based regularizers (e.g., dropout) to contextualize the contribution.
