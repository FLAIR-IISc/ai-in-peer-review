- Rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training instabilities rather than a new architecture.  
- Shows disjoint supports or low-dimensional data/support manifolds let the optimal discriminator become perfect, causing uninformative gradients.  
- Formally proves vanishing gradients for the original GAN loss and unstable, high-variance updates for the alternative -log D loss.  
- Theorem 2.5 links the -log D objective to a mix of KL and JS divergences, providing a formal explanation for mode dropping.  
- Theorem 2.6 demonstrates that a noisy but near-optimal discriminator can induce updates with infinite variance (Cauchy-like behavior).  
- Proposes adding noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and enable stable gradient flow.  
- Connects the noise-based stabilization to the Wasserstein distance, offering a new perspective on distribution similarity.  
- Decision: Accept — considered a pioneering, much-needed theoretical contribution moving beyond heuristics.  
- The analysis and theorems are novel, sound, and effectively explain widely observed empirical GAN failure modes.  
- Paper is well-written and clear; suggestion to expand derivation steps in Theorem 2.5 for clarity.  
- Raises a question about the white-noise assumption in Theorem 2.6 and whether infinite-variance results hold under more realistic correlated noise models.
