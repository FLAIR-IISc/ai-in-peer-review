- Paper gives a rigorous theoretical explanation for GAN instability: real and generated data lie on low-dimensional (often disjoint) manifolds, enabling a perfect discriminator with uninformative gradients.  
- The authors prove that when discriminator approaches optimality, the generator's gradients vanish for the original GAN objective.  
- They show the -log D objective corresponds to optimizing a mix of KL and JS divergences, explaining mode dropping.  
- Noisy approximations to a perfect discriminator can produce updates with infinite variance (Cauchy-like), explaining severe training instability.  
- Proposed fix: add continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions, overlap supports, and restore meaningful gradients.  
- Corollary: noise injection yields smooth, interpretable generator gradients that push samples toward the data manifold and away from over-dense generator regions.  
- Novel theoretical link: bound relating Wasserstein distance of original distributions to JS divergence of smoothed distributions, justifying noise-based training and suggesting principled evaluation.  
- Decision: Accept — paper is strong and recommended for acceptance.  
- Reason 1 for accept: provides principled, rigorous analysis moving GAN understanding beyond heuristics.  
- Reason 2 for accept: proposed noise-based solution is simple, practical, theoretically grounded, and connects to Wasserstein distance.  
- Strength: thorough theoretical development (Lemma 1 shows generator support is low-dimensional manifold; Theorems 2.1–2.6 formalize discriminator/gradient pathologies).  
- Strength: shows noisy smoothing prevents perfect discrimination and yields stable dynamics similar to contrastive divergence.  
- Suggestion: move standard technical proofs (e.g., Urysohn’s Lemma, transversality) to appendix and cite results for readability.  
- Minor edits: fix typos/awkward phrasings, correct incorrect theorem reference (Corollary 2.1 → Theorem 2.4), and check theorem numbering.  
- Assumptions: clarify p(z) in Theorem 2.4 and emphasize generality of Lemma 1 for broader activation functions.  
- Question: clarify whether manifolds in Theorem 2.2 are assumed with or without boundary.  
- Question: practical guidance on choosing or annealing noise variance for training.  
- Question: can the Wasserstein-bound be used directly as a training objective or evaluation metric in practice?
