### **Summary**

This paper addresses important and timely questions regarding the difficulties encountered in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the consequences of using asymmetric divergence functions and identify key sources of training instability. Specifically, they argue that when the supports of the data and generator distributions have negligible intersection, the generator's gradient can vanish, hindering learning. To address this, the paper proposes an alternative approach based on adding noise to the inputs, which effectively smooths the distributions and encourages their supports to overlap, thereby stabilizing the training process.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its rigorous theoretical approach to understanding the challenges in GAN training. It poses important questions about the underlying mechanics of GANs and provides well-reasoned answers.
2.  **Novel Application of Mathematical Concepts:** The paper makes interesting and effective use of concepts from mathematical analysis and differential topology to formalize intuitions about why GANs can be unstable. This provides the community with a more solid theoretical foundation for discussing and addressing these issues.
3.  **Practical Implications:** The theoretical analysis leads directly to concrete suggestions for avoiding instability in GAN training. The proposed solution in Section 3 is intuitive, simple, and offers a practical path toward more stable and reliable GAN models.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Density and Accessibility:** The paper is quite dense and technical, which may limit its accessibility to a broader audience. Certain sections, particularly the theoretical development in Section 2, could benefit from being streamlined to improve readability for those less familiar with the specific mathematical machinery being used.
2.  **Scope for Further Development:** While the theoretical analysis is a major contribution, the practical consequences and empirical validation of some of the theoretical claims could be further developed. This is not a major flaw and is perfectly acceptable as a direction for future work, but the current paper is more heavily weighted toward theory than empirical results.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

The following are more specific points of feedback and questions for clarification:

*   **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   Section 2.1 is quite dense with proofs. To improve the flow of the main text, I suggest moving the proofs of Lemma 1, Theorem 2.1, and Lemma 2 to the appendix. These results appear to be applications of standard analysis, and for some, a citation to a relevant textbook might suffice in the main paper.
    *   The numbering of theorems and corollaries is confusing and appears to contain errors. For instance, Corollary 2.1 refers to the "assumptions of Theorem 1.3," but I could not locate a Theorem 1.3 in the manuscript. Please review the numbering throughout the paper for consistency.

*   **Technical Points and Clarifications:**
    *   On page 2, in the second bullet point, you argue that the discriminator can achieve perfect separation if P_g(x) is high where P_data(x) is low. It is worth explicitly noting the converse: for the distributions to integrate to 1, this also implies that P_g(x) must be lower than P_data(x) in other regions, which also contributes to a non-zero (and potentially infinite) KL divergence.
    *   Regarding Theorem 2.2: Could you please clarify whether the closed manifolds are assumed to have a boundary or not? This is an important detail for the scope of the theorem.
    *   In Theorem 2.4, it would be helpful to briefly remind the reader of the definition of p(z).
    *   It seems that Lemma 1 might also hold under more general conditions than stated. Have you considered the full generality of this result?
    *   Please specify the domain of the random variables used throughout the analysis to enhance clarity.

*   **Minor Wording and Typos:**
    *   On page 2, the phrase "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" should likely be "for generating not plausibly looking pictures."
    *   Typo: "relly" should be corrected to "rely."
    *   In Theorem 2.5, consider replacing "Therefore" with "Then" for better logical flow.
    *   In Theorem 2.6, the sentence beginning "Is a..." should be corrected to start with a lowercase "is a...".
