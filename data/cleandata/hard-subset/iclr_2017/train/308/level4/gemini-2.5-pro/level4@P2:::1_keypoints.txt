- Paper shows GAN training problems stem from real and generated data lying on low-dimensional manifolds, enabling a perfect discriminator.  
- A perfect discriminator yields vanishing or uninformative gradients for the generator, explaining instability and saturation.  
- For the original GAN loss, gradients vanish as the discriminator improves, creating a learning dilemma (Theorem 2.4).  
- For the popular -log(D) objective, the paper derives it optimizes KL(Pg||Pr) - 2 JSD(Pg||Pr), explaining sharp samples and mode dropping (Theorem 2.5).  
- Under a noisy but near-optimal discriminator, generator gradients follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance, explaining observed instability (Theorem 2.6).  
- Adding noise to discriminator inputs smooths distributions, prevents a perfect discriminator, and yields meaningful gradients (Corollary 3.2).  
- The paper links minimizing JSD on noisy distributions to bounding the Wasserstein distance between true distributions, giving theoretical justification for instance noise (Theorem 3.3).  
- Decision: Accept — paper provides a deep theoretical diagnosis and principled, novel solutions for GAN instability.  
- Key reason 1: Provides a clear mathematical explanation that low-dimensional supports cause training failure, reframing community understanding.  
- Key reason 2: Proposes a practical, theory-motivated fix (instance noise) and connects it to JSD and Wasserstein metrics, opening new research directions.  
- Supporting argument: Theorems 2.1–2.2 formally show disjoint or non-aligned manifold supports admit a perfect discriminator with zero gradients.  
- Supporting argument: Theorem 2.5 is claimed to be the first formal derivation of the -log(D) objective's implicit divergence, clarifying its empirical behaviors.  
- Supporting argument: Theorem 3.3 provides a bound linking noisy JSD minimization to reducing the noiseless Wasserstein distance, motivating the training procedure.  
- Question 1: Clarify whether the gradient calculation in Theorem 2.5 is a true global gradient of the derived objective or only a local/pseudo-gradient at θ0.  
- Question 2: Comment on robustness of Theorem 2.6 when discriminator error is structured/correlated rather than independent Gaussian (white noise).  
- Question 3: Recommend practical guidance for choosing noise variance V (σ^2), and whether an annealing schedule for noise follows from the theory.
