### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper provides a timely and rigorous theoretical analysis of the training difficulties commonly observed in Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors move beyond heuristic explanations to provide a formal account of why GAN training can be unstable. The core contribution is identifying that the generator distribution and the real data distribution often have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which are likely to be disjoint. The paper proves that this disjoint support structure allows for the existence of a "perfect" discriminator that can easily separate the two distributions.

The authors then demonstrate the critical consequences of this fact for two standard GAN cost functions. For the original minimax objective, they prove that as the discriminator approaches optimality, the generator's gradients vanish, halting the learning process. For the popular `-log D` alternative, they prove that the gradient updates can have infinite variance, leading to the massive instability widely reported in practice.

Finally, the paper proposes a principled solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This technique smoothens the distributions, ensuring their supports overlap and preventing the existence of a perfect discriminator. The authors show that the resulting gradients are smooth and have an intuitive interpretation, effectively pulling generated samples towards the data manifold. Crucially, they connect this approach to the Wasserstein distance, providing a theoretical justification that minimizing the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) between the noisy distributions serves as an upper bound for the Wasserstein distance between the original, noiseless distributions.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to our understanding of GANs. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Rigorous Diagnosis of a Critical Problem:** The paper provides the first formal and compelling explanation for the instability of GAN training. It replaces common intuitions with rigorous proofs, identifying the disjoint low-dimensional supports of the real and generated distributions as the root cause of both vanishing and exploding gradients.
2.  **Theoretically-Grounded Solution:** The proposed solution of adding noise to the discriminator inputs is not presented as another heuristic. It is directly motivated by the theoretical analysis and is shown to be a principled way to stabilize training. The connection to the Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is particularly insightful, as it provides a tangible metric that the training procedure aims to minimize.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its clear, logical progression from problem diagnosis to a principled solution, supported by strong theoretical arguments.

The analysis in Section 2 is the paper's core. Lemma 1 establishes that the generator's output lies on a low-dimensional manifold. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 then show that when the supports of the real and generated distributions are disjoint or lie on non-aligned manifolds, a perfect discriminator with zero gradients on these supports exists. This is a powerful insight. It elegantly explains why training the discriminator "too well" is problematic.

The consequences are then expertly laid out. Theorem 2.4 formally proves the vanishing gradient problem for the original cost function, explaining why learning stalls. This aligns perfectly with empirical observations. Theorem 2.6 provides a novel and compelling explanation for the instability of the `-log D` cost function, showing that noisy approximations to the optimal discriminator can lead to updates with infinite variance (modeled as a Cauchy distribution). Furthermore, Theorem 2.5 clarifies what this alternative objective optimizes, providing a theoretical basis for the common belief that it prioritizes sample quality over mode coverage.

The proposed solution in Section 3 is a direct and logical consequence of the preceding analysis. By adding noise, the supports of the distributions are guaranteed to overlap (Theorem 3.1), which prevents the existence of a perfect, zero-gradient discriminator. Corollary 3.2 provides an intuitive interpretation of the resulting gradients, which act to pull generated samples towards the data manifold while pushing them away from other generated samples, akin to contrastive divergence. The final result, Theorem 3.3, is a significant contribution. It establishes a clear link between the practical training objective (minimizing JSD on noisy distributions) and a more robust, meaningful metric for comparing the original distributions (the Wasserstein distance). This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method and opens up new avenues for evaluating generative models.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Readability and Structure:** The paper is quite dense and technical. To improve accessibility for a broader audience, I would suggest moving more of the detailed proofs to the appendix. For example, the proofs for Lemma 1, Lemma 2, and Theorem 2.1, while important, could be summarized in the main text with the full details deferred. This would allow the main narrative—from problem to solution—to flow more smoothly.
*   **Minor Typos and Numbering:** I found a few minor issues that could be corrected:
    *   Page 2, second bullet point under KL divergence: "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" should be "for generating implausible pictures" or "for generating pictures that do not look plausible."
    *   Page 7: "relly" should be "rely".
    *   Corollary 2.1 refers to "Theorem 1.3," which does not seem to exist. I believe this should be Theorem 2.4.
    *   The theorem numbering (e.g., Theorem 2.1, 2.2, 2.3, 2.4...) is a bit dense. Grouping some results as propositions or lemmas might improve clarity.
    *   Theorem 2.5: "Therefore" might be better phrased as "Then".
    *   Theorem 2.6: "is a centered Cauchy distribution" is more standard than "Is a...".

**Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **Manifold Boundaries:** In Theorem 2.2, you assume the manifolds M and P are closed. Does "closed" here imply they are compact and without boundary, or do your results also hold for manifolds with boundaries? The proof appears to handle both cases by considering interiors and boundaries separately, but clarifying this in the theorem statement would be helpful.
2.  **Generality of Lemma 1:** The proof of Lemma 1 focuses on rectifiers, leaky rectifiers, and smooth strictly increasing functions. Could you comment on the generality of this result? It seems plausible that it would hold for a wider class of piecewise-smooth or analytic activation functions common in neural networks.
3.  **Prior Distribution:** In the statement of Theorem 2.4, it would be helpful for the reader if you briefly state that `p(z)` is the prior distribution from which the generator's latent codes `z` are sampled.
4.  **Practical Implications:** The theoretical framework suggests annealing the noise variance over time to tighten the bound in Theorem 3.3. Have you experimented with such a schedule, and could you comment on its practical effects on stability and final sample quality?
