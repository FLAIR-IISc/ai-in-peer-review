### **Summary**

This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution towards understanding the difficulties encountered when training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors rigorously analyze the training dynamics, offering a compelling explanation for the prevalent issues of instability and vanishing gradients. The core of their argument is that the data distribution and the generator's distribution often have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which can be disjoint. The paper demonstrates how this condition leads to the existence of a perfect discriminator whose gradients are uninformative, thereby stalling the generator's learning process. Building on this diagnosis, the paper proposes a practical and theoretically-grounded solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This approach smooths the distributions, preventing the discriminator from becoming perfect and ensuring that the generator receives stable, meaningful gradients. Finally, the authors introduce a novel connection between this smoothed training objective and the Wasserstein distance, suggesting a new path for both training and evaluating generative models.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  The paper provides a much-needed, rigorous theoretical explanation for the widely-observed instability in GAN training. It moves the discussion from empirical heuristics to a principled analysis of the underlying geometry of the data and generator distributions.
2.  The proposed solution is elegant, simple to implement, and directly addresses the identified theoretical problems. The connection drawn to the Wasserstein distance is particularly insightful and opens up promising new research directions.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its thorough theoretical analysis. The authors begin by establishing that the generator distribution, defined by a neural network, is supported on a low-dimensional manifold (Lemma 1). This insight, combined with the common assumption that natural data also lies on a low-dimensional manifold, forms the foundation for their main argument. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 formalize the critical consequence: if the supports of the real and generated distributions are disjoint or lie on non-aligned low-dimensional manifolds, an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate them with gradients that are zero almost everywhere.

This result elegantly explains the practical difficulties of GAN training. For the original cost function proposed by Goodfellow et al., the authors prove that as the discriminator approaches optimality, the generator's gradients vanish (Theorem 2.4). For the popular `-log D` alternative, the paper shows that this objective corresponds to optimizing a combination of KL and JS divergences (Theorem 2.5), which explains the tendency for mode dropping. More critically, it proves that noisy approximations to a perfect discriminator lead to updates with infinite variance, modeled as a Cauchy distribution (Theorem 2.6), which directly explains the massive instability observed in practice.

The proposed solution in Section 3 is a direct and logical consequence of this analysis. By adding noise to the real and generated samples, the resulting distributions become absolutely continuous and their supports overlap. This prevents the existence of a perfect discriminator and, as shown in Corollary 3.2, yields smooth and interpretable gradients for the generator. This gradient drives generated samples towards the data manifold while pushing them away from regions where the generator's density is too high, creating a stable dynamic similar to contrastive divergence.

Finally, the connection to the Wasserstein distance is a novel and important contribution. Theorem 3.3 provides a bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original (noiseless) distributions as a function of the noise variance and the JS divergence between the smoothed distributions. This is significant because it provides a theoretical justification for the noise-based training procedure as a way to minimize a proper metric between the distributions of interest, and it suggests a potential path towards more principled evaluation of generative models.

### **Additional Feedback**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further improving the paper's clarity and impact. They are not critical for my decision.

*   **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is dense with technical results. To improve the flow of the main text, I suggest moving the proofs of more standard results, such as Urysohn's Lemma in Theorem 2.1 and the transversality argument in Lemma 2, to the appendix. Citing the relevant theorems from differential topology and analysis might be sufficient for the main body.
*   **Minor Typos and Wording:**
    *   On page 1, "rely on heuristics" instead of "relly on heuristics".
    *   On page 2, the phrase "pay a high cost for generating not plausibly looking pictures" is slightly awkward. It could be rephrased as "assign a high cost to generating implausible samples."
    *   In Corollary 2.1, there is a reference to a non-existent "Theorem 1.3". This should be corrected to Theorem 2.4.
    *   The numbering of theorems seems inconsistent in places. A careful check would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:**
    *   In Theorem 2.4, it would be helpful to briefly remind the reader what `p(z)` is (the prior distribution over the latent space).
    *   The generality of Lemma 1 could be emphasized, as it likely holds for a broader class of activation functions.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Your response to these questions would help clarify my understanding and strengthen my assessment of the paper.

1.  In Theorem 2.2, you assume the supports lie on closed manifolds. Could you clarify if these manifolds are assumed to be with or without a boundary? The proof in the appendix appears to handle both cases by analyzing the interior and boundary separately, but stating this assumption clearly in the theorem statement would be helpful.
2.  The proposed solution of adding noise is very compelling. From a practical standpoint, how should the noise variance be chosen? Does your theory suggest a strategy for setting this hyperparameter, or perhaps annealing it over the course of training?
3.  The connection to the Wasserstein distance in Theorem 3.3 is fascinating. Do you see a practical way to use this bound directly as a training objective or an evaluation metric, beyond the justification it provides for the noise injection method?
