This paper provides a much-needed theoretical analysis of the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors move beyond heuristic explanations and rigorously investigate the sources of instability and saturation that are commonly observed in practice. The core contribution is to demonstrate that when the data distribution and the generator distribution have supports on low-dimensional manifolds that are disjoint, the standard GAN objectives fail. Specifically, the paper proves that this scenario leads to a perfect discriminator whose gradients are either zero or highly unstable, depending on the cost function used. The work then proposes a practical and theoretically grounded solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This approach is shown to create smooth, well-behaved distributions, providing meaningful gradients and connecting the training objective to the Wasserstein distance, which is a more sensible metric for comparing distributions on manifolds.

I recommend that this paper be **Accepted**. My decision is based on two key strengths:
1.  The paper provides a clear and rigorous theoretical explanation for the widely-observed instability of GAN training, a significant open problem in the field.
2.  It introduces a well-motivated and practical solution that not only stabilizes training but also provides a principled connection to more suitable distance metrics between distributions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's primary strength lies in its theoretical contributions in Section 2. The authors formalize the common intuition that the data distribution ($P_r$) and the generator's distribution ($P_g$) lie on low-dimensional manifolds. Lemma 1 provides a formal proof for this for the generator. The key insight is that if these manifolds have disjoint supports, an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate them (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2). This has dire consequences for training. As shown in Theorem 2.4, if the generator is trained with the original `log(1-D)` cost function, the gradients vanish as the discriminator improves, halting learning. This creates a fundamental dilemma where the generator's cost function is only a good approximation of the JSD when the discriminator is optimal, but an optimal discriminator provides no gradient.

Furthermore, the paper provides the first formal analysis of the popular `-log(D)` alternative cost function. Theorem 2.5 reveals that this objective relates to a combination of the reverse KL divergence `KL(P_g || P_r)` and the JSD. This elegantly explains the observed behavior of GANs, such as their tendency to produce high-quality samples at the cost of mode dropping. Crucially, Theorem 2.6 shows that while this alternative avoids vanishing gradients, it suffers from updates with infinite variance when the discriminator is near-optimal and noisy, explaining the massive instability seen in practice.

The proposed solution in Section 3 is both simple and powerful. By adding continuous noise to the real and generated samples, the resulting distributions become absolutely continuous, breaking the assumptions that lead to the problems above (Theorem 3.1). Corollary 3.2 shows that this provides smooth, interpretable gradients that act to pull generated samples towards the data manifold. The connection established in Theorem 3.3 is particularly compelling: it bounds the Wasserstein distance between the true distributions ($P_r$ and $P_g$) by a term dependent on the noise variance and the JSD of the smoothed distributions. This provides a clear theoretical justification for the proposed training procedure: by training a GAN on noisy inputs and gradually annealing the noise, one can effectively minimize a more appropriate distance metric between the underlying distributions of interest.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

The following points are suggestions aimed at improving the clarity and impact of the paper. They are not critical for my decision but could strengthen the final version.

*   **Readability and Structure:** The paper is quite dense and technical. To improve readability for a broader audience, I suggest moving some of the more standard proofs from analysis and topology to the appendix. For instance, the proofs of Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 could be replaced with references in the main text, allowing the reader to focus on the core new results.
*   **Clarity on KL Divergence:** On page 2, in the second bullet point discussing `KL(P_r || P_g)`, the argument is that the cost is low when `P_r(x) -> 0` and `P_g(x) > 0`. It might be worth adding a sentence to clarify that for the KL divergence to be finite, `P_g` must integrate to 1, implying that if `P_g` is high in some low-data regions, it must be correspondingly low in some high-data regions, which also contributes to the divergence.
*   **Minor Corrections and Typos:** The paper would benefit from a final proofreading pass to catch minor errors. For example:
    *   In Corollary 2.1, the text refers to "Theorem 1.3," which does not exist. This should likely be Theorem 2.4. The theorem numbering scheme in general could be reviewed for consistency.
    *   On page 2: "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" should be "for generating not plausibly looking pictures".
    *   A few word choices could be improved (e.g., "Therefore" -> "Then" in Theorem 2.5).

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   In Theorem 2.2, you state that the manifolds M and P are "closed." Could you clarify if this implies they are without boundary, or if the theorem also holds for manifolds with boundaries? The surrounding discussion in Definition 2.2 and Lemma 3 suggests the theorem is intended to cover manifolds with boundaries, but an explicit statement in the theorem's assumptions would be helpful for clarity.
