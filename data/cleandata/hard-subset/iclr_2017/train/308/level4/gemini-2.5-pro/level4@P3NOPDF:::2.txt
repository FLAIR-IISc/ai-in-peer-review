This paper presents a valuable analysis of the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), providing a clearer understanding of the challenges inherent in their training. The authors offer a compelling theoretical perspective on why the original GAN formulation can suffer from vanishing gradients, while certain variants exhibit unstable gradients. More importantly, the paper proposes a solution to mitigate these difficulties by introducing a perturbation term during training. I believe this work offers important insights that could inspire more principled and stable approaches to GAN training in the future.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its clear theoretical dissection of the gradient issues that commonly plague GAN training. This provides a much-needed formal understanding of phenomena that, until now, have largely been observed and addressed empirically. The proposed solution—introducing a perturbation to stabilize the training process—is novel and well-motivated by the preceding analysis. It offers a concrete method to address the identified problems at their root.

While the theoretical contributions are significant, the paper could be substantially strengthened in a few areas:

1.  **Connection to Existing Regularization Techniques:** I am particularly interested in the proposed perturbation technique. This method is conceptually related to other regularization methods that involve injecting noise, such as dropout, where the noise can be interpreted as coming from a Bernoulli distribution. A discussion of the connections and differences between the proposed method and established techniques like dropout would enrich the paper and place the contribution in a broader context. It would be valuable for the authors to elaborate on whether their method can be seen as a generalization of or a distinct alternative to these approaches.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** A notable weakness of the current draft is the absence of empirical validation for the proposed perturbation method. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, its practical utility remains undemonstrated. To make a stronger case for the proposed solution, the paper requires experimental evidence showing its effectiveness in practice.

3.  **Direct Experimental Comparison:** To directly support the theoretical claims, it would be highly beneficial to include experiments analogous to those presented in Figures 2 and 3, but this time for a GAN trained with the proposed perturbation. Such a direct comparison would clearly illustrate whether the perturbation successfully resolves the gradient vanishing and instability issues highlighted in the analysis. This would provide compelling evidence that the proposed method not only makes sense in theory but also works as intended in practice, thereby solidifying the paper's main contribution.
