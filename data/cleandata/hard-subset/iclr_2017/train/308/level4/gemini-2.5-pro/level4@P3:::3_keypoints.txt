- Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis explaining GAN training instability.  
- Identifies that generator and real data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds that are likely disjoint.  
- Shows a “perfect” discriminator can exist that separates the two distributions.  
- Proves that for the original minimax GAN objective, generator gradients vanish as the discriminator approaches optimality.  
- Proves that for the common -log D objective, gradient updates can have infinite variance, causing instability.  
- Proposes adding noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and force support overlap.  
- Shows noise produces smooth, interpretable gradients that pull generated samples toward the data manifold (contrastive-like effect).  
- Proves that minimizing JSD on noisy distributions upper-bounds the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions (Theorem 3.3).  
- Decision: Accept — judged a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions.  
- Key reason 1: First formal, rigorous diagnosis identifying disjoint low-dimensional supports as the root cause of vanishing/exploding gradients.  
- Key reason 2: The proposed noise-based solution is theoretically grounded and linked to the Wasserstein metric.  
- Supporting theory: Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1/2.2 establish low-dimensional support and existence of perfect discriminators.  
- Supporting theory: Theorem 2.4 proves vanishing gradients; Theorem 2.6 shows infinite-variance updates for -log D; Theorem 2.5 clarifies -log D’s optimization focus.  
- Supporting theory: Theorem 3.1 ensures overlap with noise; Corollary 3.2 gives gradient interpretation; Theorem 3.3 links to Wasserstein.  
- Suggestion: Improve readability by moving lengthy proofs to the appendix and summarizing results in the main text.  
- Minor issues: several typos and theorem/numbering mistakes need correction (specific examples given).  
- Questions for authors: clarify whether “closed” manifolds allow boundaries, generality of Lemma 1 across activations, explicitly state prior p(z), and report effects of annealing noise variance in practice.
