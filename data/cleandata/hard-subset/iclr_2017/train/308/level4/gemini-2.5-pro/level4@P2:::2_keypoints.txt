- Rigorous theoretical analysis explaining GAN training instability.
- Main claim 1: when data and generator lie on low-dimensional, disjoint manifolds, the optimal discriminator's gradients vanish, blocking generator learning.
- Main claim 2: the popular -log D objective effectively optimizes a mix of KL(Pg||Pr) and JS, which penalizes unrealistic samples but weakly penalizes mode collapse and can produce unstable, high-variance gradients.
- Practical solution: add continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and produce informative gradients.
- Theoretical link: minimizing JS between noisy distributions provides an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions (Theorem 3.3).
- Decision: Accept — paper offers fundamental insight and a principled, actionable fix.
- Theorems 2.1/2.2 and Corollary 2.1 formalize existence of a perfect discriminator and the resulting vanishing-gradient problem for manifold-supported distributions.
- Theorem 2.5 characterizes the -log D objective as KL+JS; Theorem 2.6 shows realistic discriminator noise can cause infinite-variance gradients.
- Theorem 3.1 and Corollary 3.2 prove that convolving with continuous noise makes distributions absolutely continuous with overlapping support, yielding smooth gradients.
- Recommendation: add empirical experiments showing input noise stabilizes generator gradient norms and improves sample quality.
- Suggestion: discuss connections to other noise/regularization techniques (e.g., dropout, adversarial training).
- Request: provide practical guidance on choosing noise type and variance and on principled annealing strategies guided by the theoretical bounds.
