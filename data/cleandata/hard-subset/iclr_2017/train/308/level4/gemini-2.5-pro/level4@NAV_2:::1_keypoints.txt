- Paper shows that when real and generated data lie on low-dimensional manifolds, their supports are almost surely disjoint, enabling a perfect discriminator.  
- Proves a perfect discriminator causes vanishing gradients for the original GAN objective (training stalls).  
- Demonstrates the -log(D) objective equates to a combination of reverse KL and JSD and can yield unstable generator updates with infinite variance.  
- Models discriminator approximation error as noise and shows gradients can follow a Cauchy distribution (infinite variance), explaining chaotic training.  
- Proposes adding noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions, prevent perfect discrimination, and ensure informative gradients.  
- Connects noise injection to minimizing an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between true noiseless and generator distributions.  
- Decision: Accept — based on rigorous theoretical analysis and a practical, theory-backed solution tied to Wasserstein distance.  
- Section 2 formalizes the low-dimensional manifold assumption (Lemma 1) and proves disjoint supports (Theorems 2.1, 2.2).  
- Theorem 2.4 formalizes the vanishing-gradient problem for the generator under the original cost.  
- Theorems 2.5–2.6 derive the -log(D) objective’s form and prove its instability under approximation noise.  
- Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2 show noise makes distributions absolutely continuous and gradients well-behaved.  
- Theorem 3.3 bounds W(Pr,Pg) by JSD(Pr+,Pg+), linking the practical trick to a principled metric.  
- Suggestion: Provide guidance on choosing and annealing noise distribution and variance (e.g., Gaussian vs Cauchy).  
- Suggestion: Discuss the white-noise assumption in Theorem 2.6 and whether conclusions hold under correlated/noise.  
- Suggestion: Include a short discussion of how noise injection affects the discriminator’s optimization landscape.  
- Question: Provide a detailed derivation or citation for the KL(Pg‖Pr) identity using the optimal discriminator D* in Theorem 2.5.  
- Question: Clarify how Theorem 3.3’s dependence on a bounded diameter C should be interpreted for Gaussian (unbounded) noise and whether the bound holds for unbounded supports.
