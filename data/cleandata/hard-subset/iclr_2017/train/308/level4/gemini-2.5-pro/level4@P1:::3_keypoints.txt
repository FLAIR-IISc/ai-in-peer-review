- Paper analyzes GAN training instabilities, showing optimal discriminator gradients are uninformative when data/generator supports are disjoint or lie on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- Proposes adding noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions, yielding more stable, meaningful gradients.  
- Connects the noise-smoothing approach to the Wasserstein distance as a more suitable evaluation metric.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main justification: provides rigorous theoretical foundation explaining common GAN failures beyond heuristics.  
- Main justification: instance noise is simple, intuitive, and theoretically well-grounded as a stabilizing technique.  
- Strength: uses analysis and differential topology to prove optimal discriminators can be perfect yet provide no useful gradients, explaining vanishing gradients and high-variance with −log(D).  
- Theorems (e.g., 2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 2.6) formalize long-standing empirical observations about GAN instability.  
- Section 3’s theoretical analysis showing noise smoothing yields gradients related to Wasserstein is a significant contribution for future work.  
- Clarity suggestion: the paper is dense—move standard proofs (e.g., Theorem 2.1, Lemma 2) to the appendix and cite standard literature to streamline Section 2.1.  
- KL remark: explicitly state that if P_g(x) > P_r(x) in some regions then P_g(x) < P_r(x) elsewhere, and those regions contribute to non-zero KL divergence.  
- Suggestion: Lemma 1 could potentially be generalized further.  
- Reminder: briefly restate the prior p(z) in Theorem 2.4 for reader convenience.  
- Technical fix: specify the domain of random variables η and η' in Lemma 2.  
- Formatting: make theorem/lemma numbering consistent (e.g., Theorem 2.1, Lemma 2.2).  
- Typos/wording to correct: rephrase awkward phrase on page 2, fix Corollary 2.1 reference to non-existent Theorem 1.3, change “Therefore”→“Then” in Theorem 2.5, and fix capitalization in Theorem 2.6.  
- Question for authors: In Theorem 2.2, are the closed manifolds M and P assumed to have boundaries or to be boundary-less?
