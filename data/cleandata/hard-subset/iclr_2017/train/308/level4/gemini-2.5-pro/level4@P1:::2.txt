### Review

**Summary**

This paper provides a valuable theoretical analysis of the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors aim to move beyond heuristic-based approaches for stabilizing GANs by developing a more fundamental understanding of why training is often unstable. The core of their argument is that both the real data distribution and the generator's distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds. This property allows a sufficiently powerful discriminator to separate them perfectly, leading to gradients that are either zero or ill-defined.

The paper rigorously demonstrates how this situation causes two well-known problems:
1.  For the original GAN cost function, the generator's gradients vanish as the discriminator improves, halting the learning process.
2.  For the commonly used `-log(D)` alternative cost function, the gradients become unstable, with infinite variance, which also derails training.

Having diagnosed the source of these instabilities, the authors propose a practical and theoretically-grounded solution: adding noise to the inputs of both the generator and discriminator. This technique smooths the underlying distributions, preventing perfect discrimination and ensuring that the generator receives smooth, informative gradients. The authors show that this approach effectively optimizes the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the smoothed distributions, which in turn provides an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original, noiseless distributions. I believe this work makes a significant contribution towards building a more principled foundation for GAN research.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper presents the first rigorous theoretical explanation for the training instabilities widely observed in GANs. This provides crucial insight into failure modes that were previously addressed only by heuristics.
2.  It proposes a well-motivated solution to these problems and grounds it in solid theory, connecting the practical fix of adding noise to established concepts like the Wasserstein distance.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contributions. The analysis in Section 2, which proves that a perfect discriminator can exist when distributions are supported on low-dimensional manifolds (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2), is a foundational insight. The authors then build on this to show precisely why training fails. Theorem 2.4 provides a formal proof for the vanishing gradient problem in the original GAN formulation, while Theorem 2.6 offers a novel and compelling explanation for the unstable updates seen with the `-log(D)` objective, showing that the expected gradients follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance. These results elegantly connect theory to the practical difficulties that many researchers have experienced.

Furthermore, the proposed solution in Section 3 is not just an ad-hoc trick. By adding noise to the distributions, the authors guarantee that they become absolutely continuous, which circumvents the issues caused by low-dimensional supports. The analysis in Theorem 3.2 reveals the intuitive nature of the resulting gradients, which push generated samples towards the data manifold while also pushing them away from regions where the generator's density is too high. The connection drawn to the Wasserstein distance in Theorem 3.3 is particularly powerful, as it provides a clear objective: minimizing an upper bound on a proper distance metric between the true and generated distributions. This establishes a principled path toward stable GAN training.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept it.

*   **Connection to other noise-based methods:** The proposed technique of adding noise to the discriminator's inputs is reminiscent of other regularization methods, most notably Dropout, where the perturbation can be viewed as noise from a Bernoulli distribution. It would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss this potential connection, perhaps positioning their method within the broader context of noise-based regularization in neural networks.

*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper is primarily theoretical, which is a significant contribution in itself. However, the claims about the proposed solution would be substantially strengthened by empirical evidence. While the theory is compelling, demonstrating its practical effectiveness would make the paper more complete. Could the authors provide experiments showing that the proposed method indeed stabilizes training and avoids the issues diagnosed in Section 2? For example, it would be highly effective to include plots for the perturbed GAN that are analogous to Figures 2 and 3, directly comparing the gradient norms during training against the baseline methods. This would provide clear, empirical validation that the proposed solution works as predicted by the theory.
