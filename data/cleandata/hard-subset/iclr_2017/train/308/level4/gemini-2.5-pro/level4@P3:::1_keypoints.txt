1. Rigorously identifies disjoint low-dimensional supports of real and generated data as the root cause of GAN instability and vanishing gradients.  
2. Proves that an optimal discriminator can be perfect (accuracy 1) with zero gradients on data manifolds when supports are disjoint (Thms 2.1–2.2).  
3. Shows the original minimax cost log(1−D) leads to vanishing generator gradients as the discriminator improves (Thm 2.4).  
4. Demonstrates the −log(D) alternative optimizes a KL+JSD combination that encourages mode-seeking and can yield unstable, infinite-variance updates under noisy near-optimal discriminators (Thms 2.5–2.6).  
5. Proposes adding continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions, prevent perfect flat discriminators, and produce well-behaved gradients (Thm 3.2).  
6. Introduces the Wasserstein distance for manifold-supported distributions and bounds it via the JSD of noise-smoothed distributions, suggesting a principled optimization path (Thm 3.3).  
7. Decision: Accept — the paper is a strong, foundational theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics.  
8. Main merits: provides deep theoretical insight moving GAN discussion from heuristics to geometry/optimization.  
9. Main merits: novel, impactful theorems directly addressing GAN stability and explaining mode collapse and instability phenomena.  
10. Supporting evidence: Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1–2.2 convincingly link generator architectures to low-dimensional supports and perfect discriminators.  
11. Supporting evidence: Section 2.2 rigorously explains vanishing gradients and the pathological behavior of common generator objectives.  
12. Supporting evidence: Section 3’s analysis shows noise-driven smoothing guides generated samples toward data manifolds and links to Wasserstein evaluation.  
13. Suggestion: The white-noise assumption in Theorem 2.6 is strong; discuss effects under more realistic (structured/correlated) noise models.  
14. Suggestion: Include experiments demonstrating the stabilizing effect of input noise (e.g., gradient norms during training) to validate Theorem 3.2.  
15. Suggestion: Clarify regularity conditions used to interchange gradient and expectation in Theorem 2.5 (assumptions on gθ and p(z)).  
16. Question: Have you considered annealing the noise variance during training to progressively minimize Wasserstein via JSD of smoothed distributions?  
17. Question: How sensitive is the Cauchy/infinite-variance result in Theorem 2.6 to the Gaussian noise assumption—would other noise types still cause pathological instability?  
18. Question: Does the disjoint low-dimensional support issue universally complicate estimation/optimization for other divergences/metrics, or is it specific to the JS/KL family analyzed?
