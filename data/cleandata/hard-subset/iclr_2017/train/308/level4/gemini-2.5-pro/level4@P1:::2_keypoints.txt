- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of GAN training instabilities.  
- Core claim: real and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, enabling a perfect discriminator and causing bad gradients.  
- For the original GAN objective, gradients vanish as the discriminator improves (Theorem 2.4).  
- For the -log(D) objective, generator gradients have infinite variance and follow a Cauchy-like distribution, causing unstable updates (Theorem 2.6).  
- Proposed solution: add noise to inputs of both generator and discriminator to smooth distributions.  
- Noise makes distributions absolutely continuous, preventing perfect discrimination and yielding informative, smooth gradients.  
- Theorem 3.2 shows smoothed gradients push samples toward the data manifold and away from overly dense generator regions.  
- Theorem 3.3 connects optimizing Jensen-Shannon on smoothed distributions to an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between original distributions.  
- Reviewer decision: Accept — paper is strong and makes a significant contribution.  
- Main strengths: first rigorous explanation of known GAN failure modes and a theoretically grounded fix.  
- Suggested improvement: discuss connections to other noise-based regularization methods (e.g., Dropout).  
- Suggested improvement: add empirical experiments (e.g., gradient-norm plots) to validate that the noise-based fix stabilizes training.
