## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical explanation for the instability of GAN training, addressing a fundamental gap in the literature. The authors demonstrate that the data distribution Pr and generator distribution Pg typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, leading to a perfect discriminator that causes vanishing gradients for the generator. They prove that even with the alternative cost function used in modern GANs, gradient updates become unstable as the discriminator improves. The paper's key contribution is a principled solution: adding continuous noise to inputs to create "noisy" distributions Pr+ and Pg+ that are continuous and have meaningful divergences. The authors establish a theoretical connection between the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions, showing that training on noisy inputs provides smooth gradients while still minimizing the distance between the original distributions. This work provides a solid theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability and offers a promising direction for stable GAN training.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics, which has been a persistent challenge in the field. The theoretical framework is well-motivated, rigorously developed, and addresses a fundamental question that has been largely unaddressed in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in its theoretical contributions, which are the core strength of the work. The authors provide a clear and compelling explanation for GAN instability based on the geometric properties of the data and generator distributions. Their proof that the optimal discriminator is perfect and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the support manifolds (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) is particularly elegant and well-constructed. 

The key theoretical insight is the demonstration that the original cost function leads to vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) while the alternative cost function leads to unstable updates with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6). These results directly explain the practical difficulties of GAN training that have been observed in practice but not previously understood theoretically.

The proposed solution of adding noise to create continuous "noisy" distributions is theoretically sound and well-motivated. The connection between the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3) is particularly valuable, as it provides a principled way to train GANs without the instability issues. The authors effectively show that the noise can be annealed to recover the original distributions while still benefiting from the stability of training on continuous distributions.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building on the foundational work of Goodfellow et al. (2014) and the recent advances in understanding generative models (Theis et al., 2016; Huszar, 2015). The authors correctly identify the gap in theoretical understanding that their work addresses, and they provide a comprehensive theoretical framework that could guide future research in GANs.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would benefit from several improvements to enhance its impact:

1. **Empirical Validation**: The paper lacks substantial empirical results demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed noise-based approach. While Figures 2 and 3 support the theoretical claims about gradient behavior, the authors should include experiments comparing GANs trained with noise to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., WGAN-GP, BigGAN) in terms of FID scores, inception scores, and qualitative sample quality. This would significantly strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

2. **Clarity of Technical Proofs**: Some proofs in the appendix (particularly Lemma 1) are quite technical and would benefit from additional intuitive explanations or examples. For instance, the proof that neural networks map to low-dimensional manifolds could be made more accessible with a simple illustrative example.

3. **Computational Considerations**: The paper should discuss the computational cost of adding noise during training. While theoretically sound, the additional noise sampling and processing might impact training time, especially for large-scale applications. A brief discussion of this trade-off would be valuable for practitioners.

4. **Connection to Recent Work**: While the paper mentions the Wasserstein metric, it could more explicitly connect to recent advances like WGAN-GP and discuss how their approach might complement or extend these methods. This would strengthen the paper's placement within current GAN research.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that the noise can be annealed to recover the original distributions. What is the optimal annealing schedule for this process, and how sensitive is the performance to this schedule?

2. The authors claim that the noise-based approach can be used with any GAN architecture. Have you tested this with different architectures (e.g., StyleGAN, Progressive GAN) to verify its general applicability?

3. The paper discusses the connection to the Wasserstein metric, but doesn't explicitly compare the proposed method to WGAN or WGAN-GP. How does the noise-based approach differ from these methods in terms of the underlying theoretical guarantees and practical performance?

4. In the proof of Theorem 3.3, the authors assume that Pr+ and Pg+ have support contained on a ball of diameter C. Is this assumption reasonable for realistic data distributions, and how does the diameter C affect the bound?

## Conclusion

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, providing a rigorous explanation for the observed training difficulties and a principled solution. While the theoretical framework is strong and well-developed, additional empirical validation would strengthen the practical impact of the work. The paper addresses a fundamental question in GAN training that has long been neglected in the literature, and the proposed solution has the potential to guide future research in stable GAN development. With the suggested minor revisions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the ICLR community.
