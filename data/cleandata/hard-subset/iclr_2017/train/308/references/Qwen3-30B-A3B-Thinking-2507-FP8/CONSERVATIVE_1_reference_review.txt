

# Review of "Theoretical Foundations of GAN Instability and a Path to Stable Training"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper claims to provide a theoretical understanding of GAN instability by analyzing the support properties of data and generator distributions. The authors argue that because both distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds (due to the generator being a neural network with fewer parameters than output dimension), the optimal discriminator can perfectly distinguish between real and generated samples, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator. They propose adding noise to the discriminator inputs as a solution to create "softer metrics" that would avoid these instability issues, and they develop theoretical bounds relating the Wasserstein distance between original distributions to the Jensen-Shannon divergence of their noisy versions. The paper claims to provide a "principled research direction" for stable GAN training.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's theoretical claims are insufficiently supported by rigorous mathematical proof, and it lacks empirical validation of the proposed approach. The paper presents a novel theoretical framework but fails to demonstrate that this framework leads to a practical solution that outperforms established baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main theoretical contribution (Lemma 1) is problematic. The authors claim that neural networks with common activation functions map low-dimensional spaces to low-dimensional manifolds. However, this claim is not properly proven and relies on intuitive arguments rather than rigorous mathematical demonstration. The authors acknowledge counterexamples like Peano curves but fail to adequately address them. The proof in Appendix A is incomplete and makes questionable assumptions about the behavior of neural networks.

The paper's analysis of vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) and the instability of the alternative cost function (Theorem 2.6) relies on the assumption that the discriminator becomes "perfect" in the limit, which is not the case in practice. The paper does not provide empirical evidence that the vanishing gradient problem occurs as described, nor does it demonstrate that the proposed solution actually mitigates this issue.

Most critically, the paper makes a strong claim that adding noise to the discriminator inputs "solves" the instability problem, yet it provides no empirical evidence that this approach works better than existing methods. The paper references "Figures 2 and 3" but does not include them, so there is no way to verify the claims about gradient behavior and sample quality. Without empirical validation, the theoretical analysis remains speculative.

The paper's proposed solution (adding noise to the discriminator inputs) is not truly novel. Similar approaches have been explored in the literature, such as in the context of adversarial training (e.g., Goodfellow et al., 2014b) or in methods like "Jacobian regularization" (Arjovsky et al., 2017). The paper fails to properly contextualize its contribution relative to these existing works.

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper would benefit from:

1. A complete and rigorous proof of Lemma 1 that addresses the counterexamples mentioned (Peano curves, etc.). The current proof is insufficiently detailed.

2. Empirical validation of the proposed noise-based approach. The authors should compare their method against established GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization) on standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10, LSUN, etc.) and provide quantitative results on sample quality, training stability, and convergence speed.

3. A clearer explanation of how their "softer metric" differs from existing approaches that also use noise or regularization (e.g., the noise injection in the "Jacobian regularization" method).

4. A proper discussion of the computational cost of their approach compared to existing methods, as adding noise to the discriminator inputs would increase computational complexity.

5. Clarification on the use of "continuous" vs. "absolutely continuous" random variables, which is a critical distinction in the paper's theoretical claims.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide a rigorous proof of Lemma 1 that addresses the counterexamples to the claim that neural networks map low-dimensional spaces to low-dimensional manifolds?

2. Can you provide empirical evidence (including specific quantitative results) showing that your noise-based approach improves GAN training stability and sample quality compared to established baselines?

3. How does your proposed method differ from existing approaches that use noise injection in GAN training (e.g., Goodfellow et al., 2014b; Arjovsky et al., 2017), and what specific advantages does it offer?

4. Can you explain why the vanishing gradient problem described in Theorem 2.4 is actually observed in practice, given that discriminators never truly become "perfect" as assumed in the theorem?

The paper's theoretical analysis is interesting, but without empirical validation of the proposed solution, it remains a speculative framework rather than a practical contribution to the field. For an ICLR paper, the authors should provide both rigorous theoretical claims and thorough empirical validation to support their approach.
