- Paper provides a fundamental theoretical explanation for GAN instability tied to distributions on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- When Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional, disjoint manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere.  
- This explains vanishing gradients for the original GAN loss and infinite-variance unstable updates for the alternative (-log D) loss.  
- Authors prove these claims rigorously (Lemma 1, Theorems 2.1–2.6) with clear, well-motivated analysis.  
- Proposed solution: add continuous noise to inputs to smooth distributions and restore meaningful gradients.  
- Corollary 3.2 shows noise yields smooth gradient updates; Theorem 3.3 links noisy distributions to the Wasserstein distance.  
- Paper is well-written and situates its contributions properly within related work.  
- Decision: Accept — deep theoretical insight and rigorous proofs addressing a core GAN problem.  
- Recommendation: add extensive empirical validation on benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN) and comparisons to SOTA stabilization methods.  
- Recommendation: provide practical guidance on choosing noise parameter σ and annealing schedules.  
- Recommendation: include detailed comparisons with techniques like spectral normalization, gradient penalty, and label smoothing.  
- Question: How does σ affect sample quality and what is the practical optimal range across datasets/architectures?  
- Question: Have the authors tested the method on standard benchmarks and how does it compare to state-of-the-art?  
- Question: How does training the discriminator to optimality scale to large datasets where that is computationally costly?  
- Question: How does adding noise to the generator concretely mitigate susceptibility to adversarial examples in practice?
