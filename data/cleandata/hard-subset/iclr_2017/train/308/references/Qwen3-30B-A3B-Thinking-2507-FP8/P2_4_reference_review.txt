

# Review of "Theoretical Foundations of GAN Instability"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training instability. The authors identify a fundamental issue: when generator and data distributions have supports on low-dimensional manifolds (which they prove is inevitable for neural network generators with lower-dimensional latent spaces), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (with accuracy 1) and its gradient vanishes almost everywhere on the support of the generator distribution. This theoretical explanation accounts for why GAN training becomes unstable as the discriminator improves. The paper further shows that both the original and the commonly used alternative cost functions suffer from either vanishing gradients or unstable updates due to this phenomenon. The authors propose a solution by adding continuous noise to inputs, leading to a softened metric (Wasserstein distance) that enables stable training without requiring careful balancing of discriminator and generator updates.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to understanding a fundamental problem in GAN training, and the proposed solution is both theoretically grounded and practically promising.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a critical gap in GAN theory with rigorous mathematical analysis. The authors demonstrate through Lemma 1 that neural network generators inevitably produce distributions with supports on low-dimensional manifolds (when the latent space dimension is less than the data space dimension), which is the core reason for the instability they analyze. This is a significant contribution that clarifies why GANs behave as they do.

The theoretical framework is exceptionally well-developed. The Perfect Discrimination Theorems (2.1 and 2.2) establish that under natural conditions, the optimal discriminator will be perfect (with accuracy 1) and have zero gradient on the support of the generator distribution. The subsequent theorems (2.4-2.6) rigorously show why this leads to vanishing gradients for the original cost function and unstable updates for the alternative cost function.

The proposed solution (adding noise to inputs) is theoretically sound and well-motivated by their analysis. Theorem 3.3 provides a crucial theoretical guarantee that the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions can be controlled by the noise level and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions. This explains why the proposed approach works and provides a principled way to train GANs without the instability issues.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. It builds on and clarifies previous work (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Theis et al., 2016) while providing the first comprehensive theoretical explanation for GAN instability. The authors clearly situate their work in relation to related approaches like VAEs and maximum likelihood methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper would benefit from more concrete empirical validation of the proposed solution. The authors mention Figures 2 and 3 but don't provide the actual results in the text. A brief empirical demonstration of the noise-based approach (even on simple datasets) would strengthen the paper's practical impact.

The paper could also clarify the practical implementation of the noise addition. Specifically, how should the noise level be chosen in practice? The authors mention annealing the noise, but the details of this annealing schedule would be helpful for practitioners.

I would recommend the authors to explicitly state the computational cost of their approach. Adding noise to inputs requires additional computation during training, and it would be valuable to know whether this overhead is negligible compared to the benefits.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide a brief empirical comparison between your noise-based approach and standard GAN training on a simple dataset (e.g., MNIST or CIFAR-10), showing the stability improvements you claim?

2. What is your recommended schedule for annealing the noise level during training, and how does this compare to the typical training schedules for standard GANs?

3. The paper mentions that the generator should backpropagate through noisy samples. How does this affect the quality of the generated samples compared to standard GAN training?

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training, with clear implications for practical implementation. The rigorous mathematical analysis and principled solution address a fundamental problem in generative modeling, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR. The theoretical insights will guide future work on stable GAN training, and the proposed solution has the potential to significantly improve the usability of GANs across different domains.
