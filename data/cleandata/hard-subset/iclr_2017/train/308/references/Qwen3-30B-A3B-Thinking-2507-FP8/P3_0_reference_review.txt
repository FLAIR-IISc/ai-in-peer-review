

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental instability of GAN training. The authors provide a rigorous geometric explanation for why GAN training is so difficult, connecting the instability to the geometry of the data distribution and generator distribution. They argue that both the true data distribution and the generator distribution typically have supports lying on low-dimensional manifolds (not full-dimensional in the ambient space), which leads to a perfect discriminator with zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the distributions.

The key theoretical contributions include:
- Proving that when distributions have disjoint or low-dimensional supports, the optimal discriminator is perfect (accuracy 1) and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the distributions
- Showing that the original GAN cost function leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves
- Demonstrating that the alternative cost function (-log D) leads to unstable updates with infinite variance
- Proposing a solution of adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator), which makes the distributions continuous and allows for smooth gradients
- Providing theoretical bounds that connect the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions to the JSD of the noisy distributions

The paper is well-motivated, builds on relevant prior work (e.g., Narayanan & Mitter, 2010 on data manifolds, Theis et al., 2016 on divergences), and provides rigorous proofs for its theoretical claims. This is a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of GANs.

## 2. Decision

**Weak Accept**

**Key reasons for this decision:**
1. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a rigorous explanation for GAN training instability that has been a major practical challenge.
2. The theoretical results are well-motivated, well-structured, and rigorously proven.
3. The paper identifies a fundamental problem in GAN training that has been widely observed but not theoretically explained.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

### Strengths:

- **Theoretical Novelty:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GAN training dynamics. The connection between the geometry of the data manifold and GAN instability is compelling and well-supported by the proofs.
  
- **Rigor:** The paper provides detailed proofs for all theorems and lemmas, making it a strong theoretical contribution. The proofs in the appendix are thorough and address the mathematical details.

- **Clear Problem Statement:** The authors clearly identify the problem (instability in GAN training) and provide a well-motivated explanation for why it occurs (low-dimensional manifold structure of data and generator distributions).

- **Practical Implications:** The proposed solution (adding noise to inputs) has clear practical implications for stabilizing GAN training, and the paper provides theoretical guarantees for why it should work.

### Weaknesses:

- **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical and does not provide any empirical results to validate the theoretical claims. While this is sometimes acceptable for theory papers, the paper makes practical claims about GAN training that would benefit from empirical demonstration.

- **Strong Assumptions:** Theorem 2.6 assumes that the discriminator error is a centered Gaussian process, which is a very strong assumption that may not hold in practice. The paper doesn't provide evidence that this assumption is reasonable or explain how it can be relaxed.

- **Limited Practical Discussion:** The paper doesn't compare the proposed solution to existing stabilization techniques for GANs (e.g., spectral normalization, batch norm, etc.), which would help contextualize the contribution.

- **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't address the computational cost of adding noise to the discriminator inputs, which could be significant for large-scale applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

### Key Areas for Improvement:

1. **Empirical Validation:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by including empirical results that validate the theoretical claims. For example, showing that adding noise to the discriminator inputs leads to more stable training and better quality samples on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet).

2. **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The authors should compare their proposed solution to existing stabilization techniques for GANs (e.g., spectral normalization, batch norm, gradient penalty, etc.) in terms of training stability, sample quality, and computational efficiency.

3. **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper should provide more practical implementation details for the proposed solution, including how to choose the noise distribution and variance, and how to anneal the noise.

4. **Clarify Relationship to Wasserstein GANs:** The paper mentions the Wasserstein metric but doesn't clearly explain how it relates to the popular Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) approach. Clarifying this relationship would be helpful for readers.

5. **Address Computational Cost:** The paper should discuss the computational cost of adding noise to the discriminator inputs, especially for large-scale applications.

### Specific Suggestions:

- Add a section with empirical results showing that the proposed noise-based approach leads to more stable training and better quality samples compared to standard GAN training.

- Include a table comparing the proposed method to existing stabilization techniques in terms of key metrics (training stability, sample quality, computational cost).

- Provide a more detailed discussion of how the noise distribution affects the learning process, including examples of different noise distributions and their effects.

- Clarify the relationship between the proposed method and the WGAN approach, and explain how they complement each other.

- Address the issue of how the noise variance should be chosen in practice, and how it should be annealed during training.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that adding noise to the discriminator inputs solves the instability problem, but doesn't provide empirical evidence. Can you provide results showing that this approach leads to more stable training and better quality samples on standard benchmarks?

2. Theorem 2.6 assumes that the discriminator error is a centered Gaussian process. Can you provide evidence that this assumption holds in practice, or explain how it can be relaxed to better reflect real-world training dynamics?

3. How does your proposed solution compare to existing stabilization techniques like spectral normalization or gradient penalty in terms of training stability and sample quality?

4. The paper states that "the JSD between them is maxed out" when supports are disjoint. How does this theoretical claim relate to the practical observation of GAN training, where GANs sometimes train stably for some time before becoming unstable?

5. The paper mentions the Wasserstein metric but doesn't clearly explain how it differs from the Earth Mover's Distance (which is what the Wasserstein metric is). Can you clarify this distinction and its relevance to the paper's contribution?

---

**Final Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in GAN training. While the lack of empirical validation is a notable limitation, the paper's theoretical rigor and clear explanation of the instability problem make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend a Weak Accept, with the understanding that the authors should address the empirical validation and comparison to existing methods in a revised version.
