- Paper provides a rigorous geometric explanation for GAN instability, linking it to low-dimensional data/generator manifolds and perfect discriminators with zero gradient on supports.  
- Proves that disjoint or low-dimensional supports yield an optimal discriminator with accuracy 1 and zero gradient almost everywhere.  
- Shows the original GAN cost leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves.  
- Demonstrates the alternative (-log D) cost produces unstable updates with infinite variance.  
- Proposes adding continuous input noise to discriminator and generator to make distributions continuous and restore smooth gradients.  
- Derives theoretical bounds relating the Wasserstein distance between original distributions to the JSD of the noise-smoothed distributions.  
- Work is well-motivated, builds on relevant prior literature, and provides detailed, rigorous proofs.  
- Reviewer decision: Weak Accept.  
- Key reasons for decision: significant theoretical contribution, rigorous proofs, and identification of a fundamental, previously unexplained problem.  
- Strength: clear theoretical novelty connecting data manifold geometry to GAN instability.  
- Strength: high mathematical rigor with thorough proofs in the appendix.  
- Strength: clear problem statement and practical implications of the proposed noise-based remedy.  
- Weakness: purely theoretical paper with no empirical validation of practical claims.  
- Weakness: relies on strong assumptions (e.g., Theorem 2.6 assumes discriminator error is a centered Gaussian process) without justification or relaxation.  
- Weakness: limited discussion comparing the proposed solution to existing GAN stabilization techniques (spectral norm, batch norm, gradient penalty, etc.).  
- Weakness: does not address computational cost of adding noise at scale.  
- Suggested improvement: include empirical results showing noise-based stabilization improves training stability and sample quality on standard benchmarks.  
- Suggested improvement: compare proposed method to existing stabilization techniques in terms of stability, sample quality, and computational efficiency.  
- Suggested improvement: provide implementation details for noise choice, variance selection, and annealing strategies.  
- Suggested improvement: clarify relationship to Wasserstein GANs and the role of the Wasserstein metric in the analysis.  
- Suggested improvement: discuss computational cost implications for large-scale applications.  
- Specific suggestions: add experiments, comparison table to existing methods, examples of different noise distributions and effects, and practical guidance on noise scheduling.  
- Questions for authors: provide empirical evidence that noise stabilizes training and improves samples on benchmarks?  
- Question: can you justify or relax the centered Gaussian process assumption for discriminator error in Theorem 2.6?  
- Question: how does the proposed method compare empirically to spectral normalization, gradient penalty, etc.?  
- Question: how does the theoretical claim that JSD is maxed out for disjoint supports relate to empirical observations where GANs sometimes train stably before failing?  
- Question: clarify how the paper uses “Wasserstein metric” and its relation to Earth Mover’s Distance and WGAN.  
- Final assessment: strong theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental GAN problem; recommend Weak Accept but urge empirical validation and comparisons in revision.
