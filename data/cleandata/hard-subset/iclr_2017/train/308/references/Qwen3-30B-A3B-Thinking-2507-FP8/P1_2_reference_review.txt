

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental instability issues in GAN training. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical explanation for why GAN training is unstable, demonstrating that the instability stems from the fact that both the true data distribution and the generator's distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. 

The key contributions of the paper include:
- A proof that when distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect (accuracy 1) and has vanishing gradients almost everywhere on these manifolds.
- A theoretical analysis showing that the original GAN cost function leads to vanishing gradients for the generator as the discriminator approaches optimality.
- An analysis of the alternative cost function (-log D) showing it leads to unstable updates with infinite variance due to noise in the discriminator approximation.
- A proposed solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator) to create "noisy" distributions that are absolutely continuous, allowing for meaningful gradient updates.
- A theoretical connection between the proposed solution and the Wasserstein distance as a more suitable metric for comparing distributions on manifolds.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical formulations and rigorous proofs. The theoretical framework provides a solid foundation for understanding GAN instability, and the proposed solution offers a principled direction for future research.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of GAN instability, and the proposed solution is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The main reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper provides a fundamental theoretical explanation for a long-standing empirical problem in GAN training, filling a significant gap in the literature.
2. The theoretical framework is rigorous and well-motivated, connecting to existing work while providing new insights.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main strength is its theoretical rigor in explaining GAN instability. The authors systematically build their argument:

- They first establish that generator distributions (as defined by neural networks) typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1), which is a crucial insight that's not often explicitly stated in the literature.
- They then prove that when data and generator distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect with vanishing gradients (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2), explaining why the generator's gradients vanish as the discriminator improves.
- They analyze both the original GAN cost function (showing vanishing gradients) and the alternative cost function (-log D) (showing unstable updates with infinite variance), providing a complete theoretical explanation of both common approaches.

The proposed solution (adding noise to inputs) is theoretically well-justified. The authors show that this noise creates absolutely continuous distributions, allowing for meaningful gradient updates (Corollary 3.2), and they connect this to the Wasserstein distance as a more suitable metric (Theorem 3.3). This provides a principled direction for future research rather than just another heuristic fix.

The paper's theoretical analysis is well-placed in the literature, building on and extending previous work by Goodfellow et al. (2014), Theis et al. (2016), and Huszar (2015). The authors clearly situate their work within the existing theoretical framework of generative models.

While the paper is primarily theoretical, the empirical observations in Figures 1-3 align with their theoretical claims, providing some supporting evidence. The paper correctly acknowledges that the proposed solution requires empirical validation, which would be a natural next step.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong theoretically, I would recommend the following improvements to enhance its impact:

1. **Empirical Validation**: The paper makes a strong theoretical case for the proposed solution (adding noise), but lacks empirical validation. Including experiments showing the proposed method improves GAN training stability and sample quality would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors should consider adding a section with experiments comparing standard GAN training with the noise-adding approach.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper could benefit from more discussion about the practical implementation of the noise-adding approach. For example:
   - How much noise should be added? What's the optimal variance?
   - How does the noise annealing schedule affect training?
   - What are the computational costs of this approach?

3. **Comparison to Related Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it could more explicitly compare their theoretical framework to other recent theoretical analyses of GANs (e.g., Arora et al.'s work on GAN convergence, or the work on Wasserstein GANs).

4. **Limitations of the Approach**: The authors should discuss potential limitations of their approach. For example, does adding noise affect the quality of generated samples? How does it interact with other stabilization techniques (like spectral normalization, batch normalization, etc.)?

5. **Clarification on "Perfect Discrimination"**: The paper uses the term "perfect discrimination" to describe discriminators that achieve accuracy 1. While mathematically precise, this term might be confusing to some readers who associate "perfect discrimination" with discriminators that perfectly separate the classes (which is what the authors describe, but the term "perfect" might be misinterpreted as meaning the discriminator has perfect accuracy on all samples, not just those in the supports of Pr and Pg).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and assess the paper more fully, I'd like to ask the authors the following questions:

1. In Section 3, the authors propose adding noise to both the discriminator inputs and generator outputs. How does this differ from existing approaches like adding noise to the generator output (e.g., in the "Denoising GAN" approach by Zhang et al., 2018)? What are the specific advantages of their proposed noise addition scheme over these existing methods?

2. The authors state that "as the discriminator gets better, the updates to the generator get consistently worse" in practice. However, they only provide theoretical evidence for this phenomenon. Could the authors provide more empirical evidence (e.g., from the original GAN paper or other standard benchmarks) showing this effect, particularly for the two cost functions they analyze?

3. In Theorem 3.3, the authors derive a bound relating the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions to the JSD between the noisy distributions. How does this bound behave as the noise variance approaches zero? What practical implications does this have for the annealing schedule of the noise?

4. The paper discusses the importance of using the noisy distributions for training, but doesn't address how this might affect the final model when the noise is annealed out. How can we ensure that the generator doesn't "forget" the manifold structure when the noise is reduced to zero?

5. The paper states that "the only way this [JSD being maxed out] can happen is if the distributions are not continuous or they have disjoint supports." Could the authors elaborate on why the distributions being continuous (in the absolute sense) is important for the JSD to be finite? How does this relate to the concept of "manifold hypothesis" in machine learning?

---

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution to the GAN literature that provides fundamental insights into why GAN training is unstable. The paper is well-written, mathematically rigorous, and makes a significant contribution to our understanding of generative models. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors could strengthen the paper by adding some empirical validation of their proposed solution.
