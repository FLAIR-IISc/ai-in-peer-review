

# Review of "A Theoretical Analysis of GAN Training Instability"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the instability in GAN training, arguing that this instability arises because both the data distribution and the generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. The authors prove that when distributions lie on such manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect (with accuracy 1) and has zero gradient almost everywhere, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator. The paper then analyzes the two common cost functions used in GANs (the original and the alternative) and shows both suffer from fundamental issues. The authors propose a solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator), which smooths the distributions and yields stable gradients. They provide theoretical proofs for their claims and suggest this noise-based approach could lead to a principled way to train GANs.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The key reason for rejection is the lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous and interesting, the paper fails to provide sufficient experimental evidence to demonstrate that the proposed solution actually works in practice.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a theoretically compelling argument that the instability in GAN training stems from the fact that both distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. The proofs provided in the appendix appear mathematically sound, and the theoretical analysis builds logically on existing work. The paper is well-placed in the literature and cites relevant prior work on GANs and manifold learning.

However, the paper's theoretical contribution is insufficient without empirical validation. The authors claim to "provide a solid understanding of these issues, and create principled research directions," but they fail to demonstrate that their proposed solution actually addresses the problem in practice. The paper mentions Figures 2 and 3 showing experimental results, but these are not described in sufficient detail to assess their significance. Crucially, the paper lacks:

1. Comparisons to established GAN training approaches (DCGAN, WGAN, Spectral Normalization, etc.)
2. Quantitative metrics (FID score, Inception score) to demonstrate improvements
3. Ablation studies to determine the optimal amount of noise to add
4. Analysis of the computational overhead of the proposed method

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I cannot endorse a paper that presents a theoretically interesting but empirically unverified solution. The field has seen numerous theoretically motivated GAN improvements that ultimately failed to demonstrate practical benefits, and this paper falls into the same category without sufficient evidence.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

1. Conduct comprehensive experiments on standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10, ImageNet, etc.) comparing the proposed noise-based method against established baselines. Include quantitative metrics like FID, Inception Score, and visual comparisons.

2. Provide ablation studies showing how different noise parameters (variance, type of noise) affect performance.

3. Include analysis of the computational overhead of the proposed method compared to standard GAN training.

4. Discuss how the proposed approach relates to existing stabilized GAN methods (WGAN, Spectral Normalization, etc.) and whether it could be combined with them.

5. Clarify whether the noise needs to be added during both training and inference, and how this affects the quality of generated samples.

**Questions for Authors:**

1. How does the proposed noise-based approach compare to established stabilized GAN methods (like WGAN-GP or Spectral Normalization) on standard benchmarks in terms of sample quality and training stability?

2. What is the optimal amount of noise to add, and how does this vary across different architectures and datasets? Do you have evidence that this optimal amount is easily tunable in practice?

3. How does your approach handle the trade-off between noise addition (which may degrade sample quality) and gradient stability?

4. Can you provide evidence that the proposed method actually solves the problem of mode collapse, which is a major issue in GANs beyond just training instability?

Without addressing these questions and providing the requested empirical validation, it is difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed solution, which is essential for a paper in a conference like ICLR that values both theoretical contribution and empirical validation.
