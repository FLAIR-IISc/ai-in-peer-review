- Core claim: GAN instability arises because real and generated distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, violating standard GAN assumptions.  
- Theorem 2.4: If Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is constant almost everywhere, causing vanishing generator gradients.  
- Theorem 2.6: The alternative -logD cost leads to unstable updates with infinite variance.  
- Proposed fix: Adding continuous noise to discriminator inputs smooths distributions and yields well-behaved gradients (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2).  
- Theorem 3.3: The Wasserstein metric is useful for measuring distances between distributions supported on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- Overall contribution: Provides a rigorous, well-motivated theoretical framework explaining GAN training difficulties with formal proofs.  
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends acceptance due to significant theoretical impact and a novel, practical solution.  
- Significance: Addresses a fundamental unanswered question about why GANs are hard to train despite empirical success.  
- Technical rigor: Proofs are carefully developed using appropriate mathematical tools (Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, etc.).  
- Practicality: The noise-addition approach is simple to implement and theoretically justified.  
- Missing empirical validation: Paper should include experiments showing noise improves training stability and sample quality (e.g., FID, training curves).  
- Noise ablation: Need analysis of how noise magnitude and annealing affect sample quality and stability.  
- Baseline comparisons: Compare proposed method to other stabilization techniques (spectral normalization, gradient penalty).  
- Implementation details: Clarify where to add noise, how to choose noise distribution and variance.  
- Open questions for authors: Provide concrete stability comparisons, optimal noise trade-offs, relation to Earth Mover’s/Wasserstein distance approximations, effect on mode collapse/diversity, and evidence that careful training schedules are unnecessary across architectures.
