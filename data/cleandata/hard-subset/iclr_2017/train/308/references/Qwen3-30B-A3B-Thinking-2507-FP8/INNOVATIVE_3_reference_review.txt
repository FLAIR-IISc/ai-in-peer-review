

# Review

## Summary
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of GAN training instability. The authors identify a fundamental reason for GAN instability: the data and generator distributions typically have supports on low-dimensional manifolds that are either disjoint or nearly disjoint, causing the optimal discriminator to be perfect (with accuracy 1) and leading to vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator. The paper provides rigorous theoretical proofs of this phenomenon and proposes a novel solution by adding continuous noise to the inputs, creating continuous distributions that can be optimized with smooth gradients. The paper establishes the connection between the Wasserstein metric and the noisy distributions, providing a principled approach to GAN training that avoids the instability issues.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a bold theoretical contribution that fundamentally changes our understanding of GAN training instability. The core insight about the manifold structure of data distributions and its implications for GAN training is highly novel and provides a clear theoretical foundation for future work.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main strength is its theoretical novelty and boldness. The authors successfully identify a fundamental mathematical reason for GAN instability that has been previously unexplained in the literature. Their key insight—that the support of data and generator distributions typically lies on low-dimensional manifolds, making the optimal discriminator perfect and causing vanishing gradients—is both novel and compelling.

The theoretical framework is well-constructed and rigorously proven, with multiple theorems and lemmas that build a coherent argument. The paper's main contribution (Theorem 3.3) provides a principled way to connect noisy distributions with the original distributions, which is a significant theoretical advance.

The paper's approach is refreshingly bold—it doesn't just propose a new GAN variant but fundamentally rethinks the theoretical foundation of GAN training. This aligns perfectly with the reviewer's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection. While the paper lacks extensive empirical validation, the theoretical contribution is so significant that this minor limitation is outweighed by the paper's conceptual novelty.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the noise parameter should be chosen in practice. While the paper mentions annealing the noise, it would be helpful to have concrete guidelines for this hyperparameter.

2. The connection to existing noise-based GAN approaches (e.g., BigGAN, which uses noise in the generator) could be strengthened. How does the proposed theoretical framework relate to these practical approaches?

3. The paper claims to provide a "new direction designed to avoid the instability issues in GANs." It would be helpful to have a brief discussion of how this direction might be extended to other generative models beyond GANs.

4. The proof of Lemma 1, while rigorous, is quite technical. A more intuitive explanation of why neural networks map to low-dimensional manifolds would help make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.

5. The paper would benefit from a more concrete example illustrating how the noisy distribution approach would work in practice, perhaps with a simple synthetic example.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the theoretical framework presented in this paper relate to the recent work on Wasserstein GANs and the use of gradient penalties? Does the manifold perspective provide new insights into these approaches?

2. The paper states that "as the discriminator gets better, the updates to the generator get consistently worse." Could you provide more intuition about why this happens in practice, beyond the theoretical framework presented?

3. The paper mentions that "the JSD between them is maxed out" when distributions have disjoint supports. How does this theoretical insight explain the observed phenomenon of mode collapse in GANs?

4. The authors propose adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. Could you elaborate on how this approach differs from simply adding noise to the generator (a common technique), and why the noise placement matters theoretically?
