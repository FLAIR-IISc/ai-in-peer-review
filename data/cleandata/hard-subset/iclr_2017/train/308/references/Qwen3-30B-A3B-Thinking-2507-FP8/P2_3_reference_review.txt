

# Review of "Theoretical Analysis of GAN Training Instability"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a fundamental theoretical analysis of the instability issues in GAN training. The authors identify that GANs suffer from instability because both the real data distribution and the generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. This leads to optimal discriminators that are perfect (accuracy 1) and have zero gradients almost everywhere on these manifolds, causing vanishing gradients for the generator with the original cost function and unstable updates with the alternative cost function (−log D). The paper rigorously proves these theoretical claims and proposes a principled solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator and generator. This noise creates continuous distributions that allow for meaningful gradient updates, connecting the approach to the Wasserstein distance as a more appropriate metric for comparing distributions on low-dimensional manifolds. The authors also provide a theoretical framework for understanding the relationship between the noisy distributions and the original distributions.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The key reasons are:
1. The paper provides a fundamental theoretical understanding of a long-standing practical problem in GAN training, filling a significant gap in the literature.
2. The theoretical contributions are rigorous, well-motivated, and directly address the core instability issues in GANs.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes several important contributions that significantly advance our understanding of GANs. First, it correctly identifies the root cause of GAN instability as the low-dimensional manifold structure of data and generator distributions. The authors prove Lemma 1 showing that the generator's output distribution (when using neural networks) must lie on a low-dimensional manifold when the latent space dimension is less than the data space dimension. This is a crucial insight that explains why the distributions typically have disjoint supports.

The paper's most significant theoretical contribution is Theorems 2.1-2.3, which rigorously establish that when distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect (accuracy 1) with zero gradients almost everywhere on the manifolds. This explains why gradients vanish for the generator with the original cost function (Theorem 2.4 and Corollary 2.1) and why the alternative cost function (−log D) leads to unstable updates with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6). The authors provide empirical evidence (Figures 2-3) that supports these theoretical claims.

The proposed solution (adding noise) is both theoretically sound and practically motivated. The authors prove that with noise, the distributions become continuous, allowing for meaningful gradient updates (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2). The connection to the Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is particularly important, as it provides a principled way to measure distance between distributions that lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The paper's framework explains why the noise-based approach can stabilize GAN training without requiring careful training schedules.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building on prior work by Goodfellow et al. (2014), Theis et al. (2016), and others while providing a deeper theoretical foundation. The mathematical proofs are rigorous, and the writing is clear and accessible despite the complex mathematics.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

- Include more practical guidance on implementing the noise-based approach. How much noise is typically needed? How does noise magnitude affect sample quality and training stability? What are the computational costs compared to standard GAN training?
- Provide concrete empirical results comparing the noise-based approach with standard GAN training on benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) to demonstrate practical benefits.
- Discuss how the noise-based approach relates to other stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties) and whether they can be combined for better results.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the noise-based approach "makes the two noisy distributions match" through annealing. Could you provide more details on how the noise annealing schedule should be designed in practice? How does the noise magnitude affect the quality of generated samples?

2. The paper mentions that the proposed approach "will make Pr and Pg match as well" as noise is annealed. Could you provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of this approach? What conditions are required for the noisy distributions to converge to the original distributions?

3. The paper shows that the gradient updates with the alternative cost function have infinite variance (Theorem 2.6). Could you provide more detailed empirical evidence of this infinite variance property (e.g., by plotting the distribution of gradient updates) to strengthen the connection between theory and practice?

4. How does the noise-based approach compare to other recent GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization) in terms of sample quality, training stability, and computational efficiency? Could you include a brief comparison in the paper?

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of GAN training, with the potential to guide future research and development of more stable generative models. Its rigorous analysis of the root causes of instability and principled solution make it a valuable addition to the GAN literature.
