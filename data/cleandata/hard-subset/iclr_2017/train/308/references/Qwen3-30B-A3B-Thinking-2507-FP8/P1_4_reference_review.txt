## 1. Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the instability issues in GAN training, identifying the root cause as the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The authors prove that when distributions are supported on such manifolds (which is almost always the case for GANs), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (with accuracy 1) and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the generator distribution. This explains why generator gradients vanish as the discriminator improves - a key instability issue that has plagued GAN training for years.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) rigorous theoretical proofs explaining the vanishing gradients problem and the instability of the -logD cost function; (2) a principled solution involving adding continuous noise to inputs to smooth the distributions; and (3) a connection to the Wasserstein metric as a more meaningful measure of distance between distributions. The authors show that this noise-smoothed approach leads to well-behaved gradients for the generator, with theoretical guarantees that the gradient is proportional to the derivative of the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the smoothed distributions.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper provides a fundamental theoretical explanation for a long-standing problem in GAN training that has been widely acknowledged but not properly understood.

2. The proposed solution (adding noise to inputs) is theoretically grounded and supported by rigorous proofs, with potential for immediate practical application.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes several significant contributions that justify acceptance:

First, the theoretical analysis is exceptionally thorough and well-executed. The authors prove that when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is almost always the case for GANs), the optimal discriminator is perfect (accuracy 1) and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the generator distribution. This explains why the generator's gradients vanish as the discriminator improves - a key instability issue in GAN training that has been observed but not properly explained.

The paper also provides a clear explanation for why the original GAN cost function (using log(1-D)) leads to vanishing gradients, while the alternative cost function (using -logD) leads to unstable updates with infinite variance. The authors prove both of these phenomena theoretically (Theorems 2.4 and 2.6), which is a significant contribution to the understanding of GAN training dynamics.

The proposed solution of adding noise to inputs is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The authors show that this noise-smoothed approach leads to well-behaved gradients for the generator (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2) and that it connects to the Wasserstein metric, which is known to be a more meaningful measure of distance between distributions (Theorem 3.3). This connection is particularly valuable as it provides a theoretical justification for why methods like WGAN (which use the Wasserstein distance) have been successful.

The paper's contributions are significant because they move beyond the heuristic "fixes" that have been proposed for GAN training, providing a principled theoretical framework that explains why these heuristics work (or don't work) and how to design better training procedures. This is a major step toward making GAN training more reliable and easier to use in new domains.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to improve it:

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the noise parameter (σ in the Gaussian noise) should be chosen in practice. The paper mentions annealing the noise, but doesn't provide guidance on the initial value, annealing schedule, or how to tune it for different datasets or architectures.

2. While the theoretical analysis is excellent, it would be helpful to have a few more empirical results showing the practical benefits of the proposed method compared to existing approaches. The paper mentions Figure 3 showing the gradient norm growing as the discriminator improves, but it would be valuable to see a direct comparison of sample quality between standard GAN training and the noise-smoothed approach.

3. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is interesting, but the paper could more explicitly discuss how this approach relates to WGAN and WGAN-GP. It would be helpful to see whether the noise-smoothed approach could be seen as a way to approximate the Wasserstein distance in a more practical manner.

4. The paper could briefly discuss potential limitations of the proposed method. For example, does adding noise to the inputs affect the quality of the generated samples in any way? How does it compare to other approaches for handling mode collapse?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Figure 3, the paper shows that the gradient norm grows as the discriminator gets closer to optimality. Could you provide more details about the specific GAN architecture and dataset used for this experiment? How consistent is this behavior across different architectures and datasets?

2. The paper suggests annealing the noise parameter. What is your recommended annealing schedule, and how does it affect the final sample quality?

3. How does the noise-smoothed approach compare to other methods for stabilizing GAN training, such as Spectral Normalization, gradient penalty, or adaptive learning rates? Would the noise-smoothed approach be compatible with these methods, and if so, would it provide additional benefits?

4. The paper mentions that the generator's backprop term should be through noisy samples. Could you elaborate on how this would be implemented in practice, especially for architectures that don't naturally incorporate noise during generation?

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the understanding of GAN training dynamics and provides a principled solution to a fundamental problem in the field. The theoretical rigor is impressive, and the practical implications are significant. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
