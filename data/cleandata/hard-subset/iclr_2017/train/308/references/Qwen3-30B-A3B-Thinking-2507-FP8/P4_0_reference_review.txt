

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding the instability in GAN training. The authors demonstrate that the instability arises from the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. This leads to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients almost everywhere on the generator's support, explaining why generator updates deteriorate as the discriminator improves. The paper analyzes both the original GAN cost function (showing vanishing gradients) and the -logD alternative (showing unstable updates with infinite variance), and proposes a principled solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator) to create continuous distributions that can be meaningfully optimized. The authors prove that the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions can be bounded by the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions plus a term depending on noise variance, providing a theoretical justification for their approach.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training instability. The arguments are mathematically rigorous, the proofs are sound, and the results provide a principled explanation for long-observed phenomena in GAN training.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical contributions are novel, well-motivated, and rigorously proven. The key strength is the clear identification of the root cause of GAN instability (low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports) and the mathematical demonstration that this leads to the observed empirical behaviors (vanishing gradients, unstable updates). 

The authors' analysis of the two common cost functions (Theorems 2.4 and 2.6) provides a fundamental explanation for why GAN training is difficult, going beyond heuristic observations. Their proposed solution (adding noise) is both theoretically justified and directly follows from their theoretical insights. Theorem 3.3 provides a crucial bound relating the Wasserstein distance (a meaningful metric for generative models) to the JSD of the noisy distributions, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building on prior work while addressing a critical gap in theoretical understanding. The mathematical arguments are thorough, with complete proofs provided in the appendix. The empirical evidence in Figures 2 and 3 supports the theoretical claims about vanishing gradients and unstable updates.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the practical implementation of noise addition, including guidance on noise scale and annealing schedules. While the theoretical foundation is strong, the practical guidance is limited.
- Including empirical results demonstrating improved GAN training using the proposed noise addition approach would strengthen the paper by showing practical benefits alongside the theoretical insights.
- The relationship between the proposed noise addition and existing methods that use noise (e.g., adding noise to the generator in DCGAN) could be more explicitly connected to existing work.

## 5. Clarification Questions
1. In Appendix B, the paper distinguishes between "continuous" and "absolutely continuous" random variables. The paper states that "a random variable concentrated on a low dimensional manifold such as a plane can have this property [continuous], but will not be absolutely continuous." Could the authors clarify why they use "continuous" rather than "absolutely continuous" in the main text, given the distinction they make in Appendix B?

2. Theorem 3.3 states that "if Pr+ and Pg+ have support contained on a ball of diameter C." How large is C typically in practice for real-world datasets? This parameter appears critical for the bound, but the paper doesn't discuss its practical implications.

3. The paper states that "the generator's backprop term will be through samples on a set of positive measure that the discriminator will care about" when noise is added to both the discriminator and generator. Could the authors provide intuition for why this specific noise addition (to both) is better than other approaches (e.g., noise only to the generator)?
