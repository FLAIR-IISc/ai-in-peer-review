

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of what the paper claims to do/contribute

This paper provides a comprehensive theoretical explanation for the instability issues in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors identify that the core problem stems from the fact that both the data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg) typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds, rather than being continuous distributions over the entire space. This leads to a fundamental mismatch between the theoretical assumptions of the GAN framework and the actual behavior of practical implementations.

The paper makes several key theoretical contributions:
- Proves that when Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator becomes constant almost everywhere on the support of these distributions, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator (Theorem 2.4).
- Shows that the alternative cost function (using -logD) leads to unstable updates with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6).
- Proposes a novel solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator, which smooths the distributions and leads to well-behaved gradients (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2).
- Introduces the Wasserstein metric as a useful tool for measuring the distance between distributions even when their supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Theorem 3.3).

The authors provide a clear theoretical framework that explains why GAN training is so difficult and offers a principled approach to addressing the instability issues. The paper is well-motivated, builds on existing literature, and presents rigorous mathematical proofs for its claims.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, with well-founded proofs that explain the observed practical difficulties. The proposed solution is novel, theoretically grounded, and has the potential to impact both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of GANs.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's contribution is significant for three main reasons:

First, it addresses a fundamental question that has been largely unanswered in the GAN literature: why are GANs so difficult to train despite their empirical success? The authors provide a rigorous theoretical explanation for the instability issues, which has been a long-standing concern in the field.

Second, the paper's theoretical framework is well-constructed and carefully developed. The authors build on relevant mathematical concepts (Urysohn's lemma, Sard's lemma, etc.) and provide detailed proofs for their key claims. The theorems are clearly stated and the proofs are logically sound.

Third, the proposed solution is both novel and practical. The authors don't just identify the problem but also provide a theoretically grounded solution that can be implemented in existing GAN frameworks. The noise addition approach is simple to implement yet has profound theoretical justification.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, citing and building on key prior work in GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), theoretical analysis of generative models (Theis et al., 2016), and related mathematical concepts. The authors clearly position their work within the existing literature and identify the specific gap their work fills.

## 4. Additional feedback to improve the paper

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including some empirical validation of the proposed approach. The following suggestions would improve the paper:

- Add empirical results showing that adding noise to the discriminator inputs leads to more stable training and better sample quality compared to standard GAN training. This could include training curves showing reduced instability and quantitative metrics (e.g., FID scores) comparing the proposed approach with standard GAN training.
- Include a discussion of how the amount of noise affects the quality of generated samples. The paper mentions that the noise can be annealed, but it would be helpful to show how different noise levels impact the results.
- Provide a comparison with other techniques for stabilizing GANs (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) to demonstrate the relative advantages of the proposed approach.
- Clarify the implementation details of adding noise to the discriminator inputs. For example, should the noise be added to the input of the discriminator or to the generated samples before they reach the discriminator? How should the noise distribution be chosen (e.g., Gaussian with specific variance)?

## 5. Questions for the authors

1. The paper mentions that the proposed approach avoids the need for careful training schedules, but it would be helpful to see a concrete example where this is demonstrated. Could the authors provide a comparison of training stability (e.g., using standard deviation of losses over time) between their approach and standard GAN training on a specific dataset?

2. The paper claims that adding noise leads to gradients that are proportional to ∇θJSD(Pr+ || Pg+), which is a sensible cost function to minimize. However, the authors also note that this is a noisy version of the original distributions. How does the quality of generated samples relate to the amount of noise added? Is there an optimal noise level that balances stability and sample quality?

3. The paper states that "the Wasserstein metric incorporates the notion of distance between the elements in the support of P and the ones in the support of Q." How does this relate to the concept of the "Earth Mover's Distance" that is commonly used in GAN literature (e.g., in WGAN)? Does the proposed approach provide a more efficient way to approximate the Wasserstein distance?

4. The paper shows that the gradient updates become well-behaved with the proposed noise addition, but it doesn't discuss how this might affect the diversity of generated samples. Given the known issue of mode dropping in GANs, does the proposed approach exacerbate or alleviate this problem?

5. The authors claim that their approach "avoids the need for careful training schedules," but the paper doesn't provide any evidence of this. Could the authors provide a comparison of training stability across different architectures (e.g., DCGAN, ResNet-based GANs) using both standard training and their proposed approach?
