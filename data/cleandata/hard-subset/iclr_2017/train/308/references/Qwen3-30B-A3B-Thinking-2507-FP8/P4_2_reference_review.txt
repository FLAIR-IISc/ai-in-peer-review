

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training instability, addressing the fundamental question of why GANs are difficult to train despite their empirical success. The authors demonstrate that the instability arises because data distributions and generator distributions typically have disjoint supports on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients for the generator. They prove that both the original cost function (leading to vanishing gradients) and the alternative cost function (leading to unstable updates with infinite variance) suffer from this fundamental issue. The paper proposes a noise-regularized approach to stabilize training by adding continuous noise to inputs, transforming the distributions into continuous ones that can be compared using standard divergences. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for this approach, including a bound relating the Wasserstein distance between original distributions to the Jensen-Shannon divergence of noisy distributions.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GAN training dynamics. The problem is well-motivated, as instability remains a major obstacle to practical GAN application despite their success. The authors correctly identify the core issue: the supports of data and generator distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, which leads to the observed instability.

The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported. The key theorems (2.1-2.6) are carefully proven with detailed mathematical arguments in the appendix. The paper correctly identifies that when supports are disjoint or lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (accuracy 1) with zero gradient almost everywhere on the supports, leading to vanishing gradients for the original cost function and unstable updates for the alternative cost function. The noise-regularized approach is theoretically sound and provides a principled solution to the instability problem.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with proper contextualization of related work (Theis et al., Huszar, Goodfellow et al.). The authors correctly identify the theoretical gaps in previous work and provide a comprehensive analysis of the underlying mathematical mechanisms causing instability.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation of the noise-regularized approach. Specifically, how should the noise level be chosen in practice, and how does it affect the quality of generated samples?
- Consider adding a comparison with existing stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) to highlight the unique advantages of the noise-regularized approach.
- The paper could provide more intuition about why the noise-regularized approach leads to stable optimization behavior, beyond the theoretical guarantees.
- Clarify the relationship between this work and Wasserstein GANs, which also aim to address GAN instability through different theoretical mechanisms.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In the noise-regularized approach, how does the amount of noise affect the quality of generated samples? Is there an optimal noise level, or does it depend on the dataset and model architecture?
2. Theorem 3.3 includes a term involving the diameter C of the support of Pr+ and Pg+. How does C scale with the dimensionality of the data space, and how can it be bounded in practice?
3. The paper states that "the optimal discriminator will be perfect and its gradient will be zero almost everywhere." How does the noise-regularized approach specifically prevent this from happening, and what is the mechanism that leads to stable gradient updates?
4. In Figure 3, the paper shows that gradient norm grows drastically as the discriminator gets closer to optimality. How does the noise-regularized approach affect this behavior, and what empirical evidence supports its effectiveness in practice?
