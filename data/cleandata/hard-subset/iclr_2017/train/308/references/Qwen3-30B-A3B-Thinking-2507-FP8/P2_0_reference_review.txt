

# Review of "Understanding and Improving GAN Training Stability"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper provides a fundamental theoretical analysis of why GANs are difficult to train, identifying the core issue as the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. The authors prove that this leads to perfect discriminators that provide vanishing gradients for the original GAN objective and unstable gradients for the alternative objective (using -log D). 

The paper makes several significant contributions:
- Rigorous theoretical proof that the support of a GAN generator is contained in a countable union of low-dimensional manifolds
- Formal demonstration that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds that don't perfectly align (which is typical), the optimal discriminator is perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere
- Proof that the original GAN objective suffers from vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves, while the alternative objective (-log D) leads to unstable gradients with infinite variance
- Proposal of a practical solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator), which creates continuous distributions allowing for meaningful gradients
- Derivation of a theoretical connection between the Wasserstein distance and the Jensen-Shannon divergence between noisy distributions
- A principled training procedure that adds noise during training and anneals it out, leading to stable training without the need for careful hyperparameter tuning

This work provides a solid theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability, which has been a long-standing issue in the field, moving the understanding of GANs from heuristic to principled.

## 2. Decision: Accept

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, which has been a major challenge in the field for several years. The theoretical framework is rigorous, well-motivated, and addresses a fundamental gap in our understanding of GANs.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper excels in its theoretical contribution and scientific rigor. The key reasons for acceptance are:

**a) Rigorous theoretical foundation for a major practical problem:** The paper identifies a fundamental theoretical reason for GAN instability that has been widely observed but poorly understood. The authors prove that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical for GANs), the optimal discriminator is perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere on the manifolds. This explains why the original GAN objective suffers from vanishing gradients and why the alternative objective leads to unstable updates.

**b) Strong mathematical framework:** The paper uses standard tools from manifold theory, measure theory, and probability theory to build a solid theoretical framework. The proofs are detailed, rigorous, and well-structured, with key theorems logically following from the assumptions. The paper's mathematical development is exceptionally clear and well-executed.

**c) Practical implications of theoretical insights:** The paper doesn't just identify the problem but also provides a principled solution that is both theoretically grounded and practically implementable. The noise addition approach is simple yet powerful, and the theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric provides a principled way to evaluate generative models.

**d) Well-placed in the literature:** The paper properly situates itself within the existing literature on GANs, referencing key papers (Goodfellow et al., Radford et al., Salimans et al.) and related theoretical work (Theis et al., Huszar, Narayanan & Mitter). It clearly identifies the gap in theoretical understanding that it aims to fill.

The paper's main contribution is theoretical, and it successfully fills a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of GANs. While empirical validation is mentioned, the paper's primary contribution is the theoretical insight, which is thoroughly and rigorously developed.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

The paper is already strong, but these suggestions would further enhance it:

- **Include empirical validation:** The paper mentions Figures 2 and 3 showing empirical verification of vanishing gradients and instability, but these figures aren't included in the provided text. Including these would strengthen the paper by providing direct empirical evidence for the theoretical claims.

- **More detailed comparison with existing methods:** While the paper is primarily theoretical, a brief empirical comparison with existing methods that address GAN instability (Wasserstein GAN, LSGAN, spectral normalization) would help contextualize the proposed method. This could be a short section showing how the noise-based approach compares to these methods.

- **Discussion of computational overhead:** The paper mentions adding noise during training, but doesn't discuss the computational cost. A brief analysis of how much this affects training time compared to standard GAN training would be helpful for practitioners.

- **Clarification on noise schedule:** The paper mentions annealing the noise, but doesn't provide specific guidance on the noise schedule. A brief discussion of recommended noise schedules and sensitivity analysis would be valuable for practitioners.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To better understand and assess the paper, I would like to ask the authors:

1. Can you provide more details about the empirical validation mentioned in Figures 2 and 3? Specifically, what datasets and architectures were used, and how did the noise affect the training dynamics?

2. How does the proposed method compare to Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) in terms of sample quality and training stability? The paper mentions the connection to Wasserstein distance, but it would be helpful to have a direct comparison.

3. In Theorem 3.3, the authors bound the Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions using the JSD of noisy distributions. How does this bound behave as the noise variance decreases? Is there a theoretical guarantee that the bound becomes tight as the noise goes to zero?

4. The paper proposes annealing the noise during training. What is the recommended noise schedule, and how sensitive is the final sample quality to the choice of noise schedule?

5. The authors mention that the generator's backprop should be through noisy samples to avoid adversarial examples. Can they provide specific examples or intuition about how this prevents adversarial examples in practice?

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of GAN training dynamics. The paper is well-written, rigorously developed, and makes a substantial contribution to the field of generative modeling.
