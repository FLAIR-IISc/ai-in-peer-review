

# Peer Review: "Understanding and Mitigating Instability in GAN Training"

## Summary of Contributions
This paper provides a fundamental theoretical explanation for the instability issues in GAN training. The authors correctly identify that both the data distribution (Pr) and generator distribution (Pg) typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds due to the generator's parameterization, leading to disjoint supports. They rigorously prove that when this occurs, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (100% accurate) with zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the distributions. This explains why the original GAN cost function suffers from vanishing gradients and why the alternative cost function (-log D) leads to unstable updates with infinite variance. The authors propose a principled solution—adding continuous noise to inputs—which smooths the distributions and enables meaningful gradient updates. They establish a theoretical connection between the noisy distributions and the Wasserstein distance, providing a principled metric for evaluating generative models.

## Decision
**Accept** with the following key reasons:
1. The paper provides a deep theoretical understanding of a fundamental problem in GAN training that has persisted since the inception of GANs.
2. The theoretical results are rigorously proven and well-motivated, correctly identifying the low-dimensional manifold assumption as the core issue.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper makes several significant contributions. First, Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1-2.3 provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding why GANs are unstable. The authors correctly identify that when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical for GANs), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the distributions. This explains the vanishing gradients observed in the original GAN cost function (Theorem 2.4) and the unstable updates with infinite variance seen with the alternative cost function (Theorem 2.6).

The proposed solution of adding continuous noise to inputs is elegantly simple yet theoretically sound. Corollary 3.2 demonstrates that with noise, the gradient updates become smooth and meaningful, directly addressing the core instability issues. Theorem 3.3 provides a crucial theoretical link between the noisy distributions and the Wasserstein distance, establishing a principled metric for evaluating generative models that incorporates distance between points on the manifolds.

The paper is well-written, with clear proofs and proper contextualization within the literature. The authors have correctly identified the key issue (low-dimensional manifold assumption) and built a compelling theoretical framework around it, avoiding the common pitfalls of heuristic approaches.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Empirical validation**: While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper lacks extensive empirical results demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the noise-based approach on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN). Providing quantitative comparisons with state-of-the-art GAN stabilization methods would significantly strengthen the practical impact.

2. **Practical guidance**: The paper should provide more specific guidance on choosing the noise parameter σ and annealing schedule. While the theoretical framework supports annealing, practical implementation details would help readers apply the method.

3. **Comparison with existing techniques**: A more detailed comparison with existing approaches to GAN instability (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty, label smoothing) would help situate the contribution within the broader landscape of GAN research.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the noise parameter σ affect the quality of generated samples, and what is the optimal range for σ in practice across different datasets and architectures?

2. Have you tested the proposed method on standard GAN benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet)? If so, how do the results compare to state-of-the-art methods?

3. The paper claims that "we can train the discriminator till optimality without any problems" when using the noise-based approach. How does this scale to large datasets where training a perfect discriminator might be computationally expensive?

4. The paper mentions that "g will be optimizing its cost only on that space [g(Z)]" leading to susceptibility to adversarial examples. How does adding noise to the generator (Corollary 3.2) specifically address this issue in practice?

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to our understanding of GAN instability, providing a clear explanation for a long-standing issue and a principled solution that addresses the core mathematical problem. The theoretical results are rigorous, well-motivated, and directly relevant to the practical challenges in GAN training. The proposed noise-based approach has the potential to significantly impact future GAN research and development.
