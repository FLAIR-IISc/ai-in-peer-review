

# Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training instability, addressing fundamental questions about why GANs are difficult to train and why standard approaches fail. The authors establish that the instability arises because the data and generator distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, leading to perfect discriminators that produce vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator. They prove that under these conditions, standard divergences (JSD, KL) become maximized regardless of how close the manifolds are. The paper then proposes a principled solution: adding continuous noise to the discriminator inputs to create "softer" metrics that avoid the instability issues while still minimizing a Wasserstein-like distance between the original distributions. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for their approach, showing that with appropriate noise annealing, the method can stabilize GAN training without requiring complex scheduling.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, providing rigorous proofs for key claims about the geometry of GAN training. The theoretical framework is well-motivated and addresses a fundamental gap in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper successfully addresses the three key questions for assessment:

1. The specific question tackled is clearly defined: "Why do updates get worse as the discriminator gets better? Why is GAN training massively unstable? Is there a way to avoid these issues?"

2. The approach is exceptionally well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors properly situate their work within the existing GAN literature, citing key papers and clearly articulating the theoretical gaps their work addresses. The connection to the geometric properties of data distributions (low-dimensional manifolds) is well-justified by existing evidence in the field.

3. The paper thoroughly supports its claims with rigorous theoretical results. The main theorems (2.1, 2.2, 2.3, 2.4, 2.5, 2.6, 3.2, 3.3) are proven in the appendix with appropriate mathematical machinery. The proofs appear technically sound, and the theoretical framework provides a coherent explanation for the empirical observations in GAN training. The paper successfully demonstrates that the instability is a fundamental geometric property of the problem, not merely a practical implementation issue.

The paper's novelty lies in providing this geometric explanation for GAN instability, which has been a long-standing challenge in the field, and proposing a theoretically grounded solution. The theoretical results are comprehensive and address the core issues with GAN training.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper would benefit from:

1. More explicit connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation. A brief discussion of how the proposed noise-based solution would be implemented in practice (including the choice of noise distribution and annealing schedule) would strengthen the paper's applicability.

2. A short discussion of the computational cost implications of the proposed method compared to existing approaches. While the paper focuses on theoretical stability, it would be helpful to address whether the proposed solution incurs significant computational overhead.

3. A brief comparison to related work on GAN stability (e.g., WGAN, spectral normalization) to clarify how this theoretical framework complements or differs from existing practical approaches.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the proposed noise-based approach "will make the two noisy distributions match" as noise is annealed. How does this approach compare to the WGAN approach, which also aims to match distributions but uses a different distance metric? Are there theoretical guarantees that your method converges to the same solution as WGAN?

2. In Theorem 3.3, the bound involves the diameter of the support of Pr+ and Pg+. How would the bound change if the distributions are supported on non-compact manifolds (e.g., for natural images)? Would this affect the practical implementation of the proposed method?

3. The paper demonstrates that adding noise solves the vanishing gradient problem, but what about the mode collapse issue that GANs are known for? Does the proposed solution also address mode collapse, or is this a separate issue that requires additional mechanisms?
