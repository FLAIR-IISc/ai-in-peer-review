

# Review of "Understanding the Instability of GAN Training"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental instability issues in GAN training. The authors provide a rigorous geometric explanation for why GANs are difficult to train, identifying that the data distribution and generator distribution typically have supports on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint or nearly disjoint supports. They prove that this geometric property leads to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients for the generator as the discriminator improves.

The paper's key contributions are:
- A theoretical analysis showing that when distributions have disjoint supports on low-dimensional manifolds, the Jensen-Shannon divergence (which GANs approximate) is maximized, making gradient-based optimization impossible.
- A proof that the gradient vanishing issue occurs with the original cost function as the discriminator approaches optimality.
- An alternative analysis showing that the common modification to the generator cost function (using -log D(x) instead of log(1-D(x))) leads to unstable updates with infinite variance.
- A principled solution involving adding noise to the discriminator inputs to create "softer metrics" that allow meaningful gradient updates.
- A theoretical connection between the noisy discriminator approach and the Wasserstein distance, showing that the method optimizes a bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions.

This work provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability, moving beyond heuristic fixes to a principled approach.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical explanation for a fundamental problem in GAN training that has persisted for years, addressing a critical gap in the field's understanding.
2. The proposed solution (adding noise to the discriminator inputs) is theoretically grounded, principled, and backed by mathematical proofs rather than heuristic fixes.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical contributions are substantial and well-justified. The authors correctly identify that the core issue with GAN instability is the geometry of the data and generator distributions, specifically that both are typically supported on low-dimensional manifolds that are disjoint or nearly disjoint. This geometric perspective is crucial for understanding why GAN training is unstable.

The mathematical proofs are rigorous and well-constructed. Lemma 1 about the generator's support being a low-dimensional manifold is particularly important, as it explains why the generator distribution can't be continuous when the latent space dimension is less than the data space dimension. The authors' use of concepts from differential geometry (manifolds, transversality) to analyze the GAN problem is appropriate and adds depth to the analysis.

The connection between the noisy discriminator approach and the Wasserstein distance is strong. Theorem 3.3 shows that the noisy approach can be used to minimize a bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions, which is a meaningful metric for comparing distributions with disjoint supports. This provides a clear theoretical justification for why the noise addition helps.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, building on previous work by Theis et al. (2016) and Huszar (2015) while providing a more comprehensive theoretical foundation. The authors correctly situate their work within the broader context of generative modeling and GAN research.

The empirical evidence (Figures 2 and 3, as referenced) supports the theoretical claims about vanishing gradients and instability. The paper's approach is not just a theoretical curiosity but has practical implications for GAN training.

## 4. Additional Feedback

I believe this paper would be even stronger with the following improvements:

- **Noise parameter selection**: The paper mentions annealing the noise but doesn't provide concrete guidelines for how to choose the noise parameter σ in practice. A brief discussion of practical considerations for choosing σ and the annealing schedule would be helpful for practitioners.

- **Comparison with WGAN**: The paper mentions the Wasserstein metric but doesn't explicitly connect to the WGAN approach (Arjovsky et al., 2017). A brief comparison would clarify how this work relates to and potentially extends the WGAN framework.

- **Clarification of "matching"**: The paper states that the noisy discriminator "makes the two noisy distributions match." It would be helpful to clarify what "match" means in this context and how this relates to convergence to the data distribution.

- **Mode collapse connection**: The paper mentions that the approach addresses "mode dropping" but doesn't explicitly connect the noisy discriminator method to mode collapse. A brief discussion of how this method prevents mode collapse would strengthen the practical relevance.

- **Terminology clarification**: The paper uses "continuous" to mean "absolutely continuous" in some places, which is confusing. The paper does clarify this in Appendix B, but it would be better to use the precise term "absolutely continuous" throughout to avoid confusion.

## Questions for the Authors

1. How does the noisy discriminator approach compare to the WGAN (Wasserstein GAN) approach in terms of stability, sample quality, and theoretical guarantees? Is there a formal relationship between the two methods?

2. The paper mentions that the noise can be chosen in different ways (Corollary 3.1). What are the practical implications of choosing different noise distributions (e.g., Gaussian vs. Cauchy)? How does the choice of noise affect the quality of the generated samples?

3. In Theorem 3.3, the bound on the Wasserstein distance depends on the diameter C of the support of the noisy distributions. How can C be estimated in practice, and how does this affect the annealing schedule for the noise?

4. The paper claims that the proposed method "no longer has to worry about training schedules." How does this compare to the WGAN approach, which also claims to alleviate training schedule concerns?

5. The paper states that "the gradient updates with this noise addition correspond to optimizing the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions." Can the authors provide a more precise statement about the relationship between the optimization objective with noise and the Wasserstein distance?

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training instability, providing a principled solution that addresses a fundamental problem in the field. It is well-written, rigorously proven, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
