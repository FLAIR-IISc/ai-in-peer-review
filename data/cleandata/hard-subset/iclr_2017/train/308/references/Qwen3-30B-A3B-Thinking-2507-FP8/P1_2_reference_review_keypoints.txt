- Main claim: GAN instability arises because real and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional, disjoint manifolds.
- Contribution: proof that when supports are disjoint, the optimal discriminator is perfect (accuracy 1) and has vanishing gradients on these manifolds.
- Contribution: theoretical analysis showing the original GAN loss yields vanishing generator gradients as the discriminator improves.
- Contribution: analysis showing the alternative (-log D) loss produces unstable updates with infinite variance due to discriminator noise.
- Proposed solution: add continuous noise to discriminator and generator inputs to make distributions absolutely continuous and restore meaningful gradients.
- Theoretical link: connecting the noisy-distribution approach to Wasserstein distance as a more appropriate metric for manifold distributions.
- Writing and rigor: paper is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical formulations and rigorous proofs.
- Decision: Accept — strong fundamental theoretical contribution and well-motivated solution.
- Key technical insight (Lemma 1): generator distributions from neural nets typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds.
- Key results (Theorems 2.1/2.2): perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients when supports are disjoint.
- Comprehensive analysis: covers both original GAN cost and the common alternative, explaining both vanishing and unstable behaviors.
- Corollary 3.2: adding noise produces absolutely continuous distributions enabling usable gradients.
- Theorem 3.3: provides a bound relating Wasserstein distance of original distributions to JSD of noisy distributions.
- Empirical support: Figures 1–3 align with theory but are limited and not a full empirical validation.
- Recommendation: add empirical experiments comparing standard GAN training with the noise-adding approach.
- Recommendation: provide practical implementation details (noise magnitude, annealing schedule, computational cost).
- Recommendation: more explicit comparison to related theoretical work (e.g., Arora et al., WGAN literature).
- Recommendation: discuss limitations and interactions with other stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral/batch norm).
- Clarification request: better define "perfect discrimination" to avoid potential misinterpretation.
- Questions for authors: how this noise scheme differs from prior denoising/noise methods; empirical evidence for worsening generator updates; behavior of the Wasserstein–JSD bound as noise variance → 0 and annealing implications; how to prevent generator "forgetting" manifold as noise is removed; why continuity matters for finite JSD and relation to the manifold hypothesis.
