

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the fundamental instability issues in GAN training. The authors make several important contributions: (1) they prove that the instability arises because the data distribution and generator distribution typically have supports on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients; (2) they formally demonstrate why the original GAN cost function leads to vanishing gradients and why the modified cost function leads to unstable updates; and (3) they propose a principled solution by adding noise to inputs, which smooths the distributions and enables meaningful gradient updates. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN behavior, with well-motivated proofs that connect the geometry of the distributions to the optimization dynamics.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, with rigorous proofs that address fundamental questions about GAN training dynamics. The paper is well-placed in the literature and provides a solid foundation for future work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical framework is exceptionally strong. The key insight—showing that the generator's output and data distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds (due to the dimensionality reduction in the generator network)—is both novel and well-supported. The authors prove that this geometric property leads to perfect discriminators that have zero gradients on the manifolds (Theorems 2.1-2.3), explaining the vanishing gradients observed in practice.

The paper's analysis of the two cost functions (original and modified) is particularly compelling. Theorem 2.4 rigorously demonstrates why the original cost function suffers from vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves, while Theorem 2.6 explains why the modified cost function leads to unstable updates with infinite variance. These results provide a theoretical foundation for the empirical observations that have driven heuristic improvements in GAN training.

The proposed solution (adding noise to inputs) is theoretically sound and well-justified. Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2 show that the noisy version of the discriminator provides meaningful gradient updates that move samples toward the data manifold. The connection to the Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is particularly insightful, as it provides a principled way to measure the distance between distributions with low-dimensional supports.

The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and rigorous proofs. The theoretical results are well-motivated by the empirical observations in the literature, and the connections to related work (e.g., Theis et al., 2016) are appropriately made.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would benefit from more thorough empirical validation of the proposed solution. The authors mention that adding noise avoids the instability issues, but they don't provide extensive experiments showing how this improves GAN performance on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) compared to existing methods. The practical implications of the theoretical results need to be demonstrated more concretely.

The paper also doesn't address how the amount of noise should be chosen in practice. Theoretical results depend on the noise variance, but the paper doesn't discuss how to select this hyperparameter for different datasets and architectures. A simple empirical analysis of noise sensitivity would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

The connection to existing GAN architectures (like DCGAN) is somewhat tenuous. The paper references DCGAN in Figure 3 but doesn't show how their theoretical insights translate to practical implementation details. A brief discussion of how to incorporate the noise addition into standard GAN training procedures would improve the paper's impact.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you recommend selecting the noise variance in practice? Would the optimal value be dataset-dependent, and if so, how might one estimate it?

2. The paper states that "we can train the discriminator till optimality without any problems" with the noisy input approach. Have you tested this on a variety of architectures (e.g., StyleGAN, BigGAN) to confirm this claim?

3. The paper mentions that "the generator's backprop term will be through samples on a set of positive measure that the discriminator will care about." How does this compare to the "feature matching" technique (Denton et al., 2015), which also aims to make the discriminator more stable?

4. The paper claims that "the two terms in equation (6) can be controlled." Could you elaborate on how one would practically implement this control during training (e.g., annealing the noise)?

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that provides fundamental insights into GAN training dynamics. With some additional empirical validation and discussion of practical implementation details, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the GAN literature.
