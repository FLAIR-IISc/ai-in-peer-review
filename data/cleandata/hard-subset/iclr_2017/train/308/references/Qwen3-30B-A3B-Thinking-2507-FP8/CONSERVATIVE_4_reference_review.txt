

**Review for ICLR**

**Summary of Contributions:**
The paper claims to provide a theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability by analyzing the support of generator and data distributions as low-dimensional manifolds. It identifies that when these manifolds don't perfectly align, the optimal discriminator becomes constant almost everywhere on these manifolds, causing vanishing gradients for the generator. The authors propose a solution of adding noise to the discriminator inputs to create "softer" metrics that avoid these instability issues. They prove theoretically that this noise injection leads to smooth gradients and provide a connection to Wasserstein distance.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation of the proposed method against established baselines. The theoretical claims are interesting but not convincingly supported by thorough experimental evidence.
2. The proposed solution (adding noise to discriminator inputs) is not sufficiently novel, as similar approaches have been previously explored in the GAN literature.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The paper's theoretical analysis is technically sound and provides a valuable perspective on GAN instability. However, as a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the lack of empirical validation concerning. The authors claim that "the only way this can happen is if the distributions are not continuous" and then propose adding noise to make the distributions continuous, but they don't demonstrate that this leads to significant practical improvements over existing methods.

The paper states in Section 3 that "we no longer have to worry about training schedules" because the noise allows training the discriminator to optimality without issues. However, the paper provides no empirical evidence to support this claim. The authors reference Figure 3 to show that gradient norms grow as the discriminator gets closer to optimality, but they don't demonstrate that their proposed solution (adding noise) actually leads to better results in practice. Without comparative experiments against established GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN, Spectral Normalization, Label Smoothing), it's impossible to assess whether the theoretical insights translate to practical benefits.

Furthermore, the proposed solution is not novel. The idea of adding noise to the discriminator inputs is related to techniques like label smoothing (Müller et al., 2019) and noise injection (Zhang et al., 2019), which have been previously explored and shown to improve GAN stability. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon these existing methods.

The paper also makes strong claims about the "fundamental" causes of GAN instability but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that their analysis is more comprehensive than existing work. For example, the paper states "the problem is far from closed" but doesn't adequately address how their analysis improves upon prior work on GAN stability (e.g., the work on Wasserstein GANs, which also addresses the instability problem through a different theoretical lens).

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**
1. The paper should include comprehensive empirical results comparing the proposed noise-injection approach against established GAN training methods on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN, ImageNet).
2. The authors should clarify how their theoretical insights translate to practical improvements beyond what existing methods already achieve.
3. The paper should more thoroughly contextualize their work within the existing literature on GAN stability, clearly articulating the novelty and advantages over prior approaches.
4. The paper should provide more detailed ablation studies to demonstrate the specific contribution of each component of their proposed method.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1. How does your proposed noise-injection approach specifically improve upon existing methods like label smoothing or noise injection in GANs? What are the quantitative advantages in terms of FID scores, inception scores, or training stability?
2. Can you provide empirical evidence that the "softer metrics" proposed in your paper lead to better sample quality or more stable training compared to existing methods on standard benchmarks?
3. How does your theoretical analysis differ from or improve upon the work on Wasserstein GANs, which also addresses the instability of GAN training through a different theoretical lens?
4. The paper claims that "we no longer have to worry about training schedules" with your approach. Can you provide evidence that this is true, and if so, what specific training schedule improvements do you observe?
