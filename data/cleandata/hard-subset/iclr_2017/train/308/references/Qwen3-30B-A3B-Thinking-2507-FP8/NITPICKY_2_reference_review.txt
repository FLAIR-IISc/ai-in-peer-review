

# Review

## Summary of Contribution

This paper claims to provide a theoretical foundation for understanding GAN training instability, arguing that it arises from the fact that both the real data distribution and generated distribution support lie on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients. The authors propose a solution involving adding continuous noise to inputs to "smooth" the distributions and create a stable training process. The paper presents theoretical results showing that with noise, the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the noisy distributions becomes well-behaved, and the gradients to the generator become stable.

## Decision

Reject. While the paper addresses an important problem in GAN training, it contains numerous technical inaccuracies, formatting inconsistencies, and lacks empirical validation that undermine its claims.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper contains multiple critical mathematical errors that invalidate its core arguments. The most glaring issue is the incorrect formulation of the Jensen-Shannon divergence: "JSD(Pr‖Pg) = 1 2 KL(Pr‖PA) + 2 KL(Pg‖PA)" should be "JSD(Pr‖Pg) = 1/2 KL(Pr‖PA) + 1/2 KL(Pg‖PA)" (with the 1/2 coefficient for both terms). This error appears early in the paper and propagates through the theoretical arguments.

The paper contains numerous other mathematical inconsistencies:
- In Theorem 2.4, the inequality "‖∇θEz∼p(z)[log(1−D(gθ(z)))]‖2 < M −" is missing the ε in the denominator
- In Theorem 3.2, the proof incorrectly uses "∇θ‖gθ(z)− y‖2" without specifying it's the squared norm
- Theorem 3.3 contains a mathematical error: "W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2V^1/2 + 2C √JSD(Pr+ ‖Pg+ )" should have the square root only on the JSD term, not on the C

The paper also contains numerous typos and formatting issues throughout, including:
- "symetrical" instead of "symmetric" (appearing 4 times)
- "adressing" instead of "addressing"
- "arize" instead of "arise"
- Inconsistent use of "discriminator" vs "discriminator"
- Missing parentheses and improper spacing in mathematical expressions

The paper claims to have empirical validation ("Figure 2", "Figure 3") but provides no actual figures or data to support these claims. Without empirical results to validate the theoretical claims, the paper's contribution is incomplete.

The paper fails to properly define key concepts like "perfectly align" (Definition 2.2), which is central to the main theoretical arguments. The definition is overly complex and lacks intuitive explanation, making it difficult for readers to follow the main argument.

The paper does not properly situate itself in the literature. It references recent GAN works but fails to engage with the most relevant recent papers on GAN stability (e.g., WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization), which would strengthen its contribution.

## Additional Feedback

1. Correct all mathematical formulations, particularly the Jensen-Shannon divergence formula, which is fundamental to the paper's theoretical foundation.

2. Fix all typos and formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper. These minor errors undermine the paper's credibility as a rigorous theoretical contribution.

3. Provide proper empirical validation to support the theoretical claims. The paper mentions Figures 2 and 3 but doesn't include them, making it impossible to assess the empirical claims.

4. Simplify the definition of "perfectly align" and provide more intuitive explanation. The current definition is unnecessarily complex for the paper's purpose.

5. Properly define all mathematical notation (e.g., "δ" for total variation distance in Theorem 3.3) and terms used throughout the paper.

6. Engage more thoroughly with the recent literature on GAN stability, particularly works that address similar problems (WGAN, Spectral Normalization, etc.).

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide a corrected proof for Theorem 2.6 that explicitly shows why the gradient updates follow a Cauchy distribution, rather than merely stating it as fact?

2. Can you provide the empirical results (Figures 2 and 3) that were referenced in the paper but not included in the submission?

3. How does your noise-based approach specifically address the mode collapse problem, rather than just improving gradient stability? Current GAN stabilization techniques often fail to address mode collapse, so this is a critical distinction.

4. How does the proposed approach compare to recent GAN stabilization methods like WGAN-GP or Spectral Normalization in terms of both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance?

5. Can you clarify how the noise parameter σ affects the relationship between the noisy distributions and the original distributions, and how one should choose σ in practice?
