

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training instability. It identifies a fundamental issue: the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, causing the discriminator to become perfect (with zero gradient on the generator's support) and leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical framework to explain this phenomenon, proving that when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the Jensen-Shannon divergence is maximized regardless of how close the manifolds are, and both KL divergences become infinite. The paper then proposes a principled solution: adding continuous noise to inputs, which creates continuous distributions that can be properly compared using standard divergences. The authors prove that with this noise, the gradient updates become well-behaved and provide a theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric. This represents a bold theoretical advance that could reshape how GANs are understood and trained.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons: This paper makes a genuinely novel theoretical contribution to GANs that explains a fundamental problem with significant practical consequences. The theoretical framework is both rigorous and insightful, offering a new lens through which to understand GAN training dynamics. The paper doesn't just propose another technical tweak but provides a principled theoretical foundation that could guide future research in generative modeling.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper excels in novelty and theoretical insight, which align with ICLR's emphasis on bold new directions. The core insight—that GAN instability stems from the low-dimensional nature of the data and generator distributions, causing the discriminator to become perfectly discriminative with zero gradients—is both elegant and profound. The paper systematically builds this argument through a series of theorems and lemmas that are rigorously proven.

The authors' theoretical framework provides a complete explanation for multiple observed phenomena in GAN training:
- Why gradients vanish as the discriminator improves
- Why the original GAN cost function leads to mode collapse
- Why the alternative cost function (using -log D) causes instability
- Why standard divergences (KL, JSD) fail to capture meaningful similarity between GAN distributions

Most importantly, the paper doesn't just diagnose the problem but offers a principled solution with theoretical guarantees. The proposed noise addition approach is elegantly simple yet profound, and the paper provides a rigorous theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric that makes the solution both mathematically sound and practically promising.

This represents a significant theoretical advance that could fundamentally change how we think about GAN training and generative modeling more broadly. The paper's contribution is far more substantial than incremental improvements to existing GAN architectures or training procedures.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong theoretically, there are a few areas where it could be strengthened:

1. The empirical validation could be more comprehensive. While Figures 1-3 provide some evidence of the theoretical claims, additional experiments comparing the proposed noise-based approach with state-of-the-art GAN training methods would strengthen the practical impact.

2. The connection to practical implementation could be expanded. The paper presents a theoretical solution, but it would be helpful to discuss the practical considerations of choosing the noise variance, how it affects training speed, and how it compares to other stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties).

3. The paper could more explicitly discuss limitations of the noise approach. For example, does adding noise affect the quality of generated samples? How does the noise interact with other GAN training techniques?

4. The writing would benefit from a clearer roadmap that explicitly connects the theoretical results to the proposed solution, making it easier for readers to follow the logical progression.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How does the proposed noise-based approach compare to other stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and practical performance? Could your approach be combined with these techniques?

2. The paper shows that adding noise creates a continuous distribution that can be compared with standard divergences. However, in practice, how should the noise variance be chosen? Is it a hyperparameter that needs tuning, or can it be automatically adjusted during training?

3. The theoretical analysis assumes the discriminator is trained to optimality. In practice, discriminators are not fully trained. How does this affect the practical implementation of your approach?

4. The paper shows that the gradient updates become well-behaved with noise, but how does the noise affect the convergence rate of the generator compared to standard GAN training?
