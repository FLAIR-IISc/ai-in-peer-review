

# Review of "Theoretical Foundations of GAN Instability and a New Direction for Stable Training"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by fundamentally explaining why GAN training is unstable. The authors identify that both the real data distribution and the generated distribution lie on low-dimensional manifolds due to the generator's architecture, leading to disjoint supports and perfect discriminators. This theoretical insight explains the long-standing practical difficulties in GAN training, including vanishing gradients and unstable updates. The paper then proposes a novel solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator) to smooth the distributions, making them absolutely continuous. The authors derive theoretical bounds showing how the noisy distributions can be used to minimize the distance between the original distributions using Wasserstein distance. This provides a principled theoretical framework for stable GAN training that goes beyond heuristic approaches.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons for acceptance: The paper provides a fundamental theoretical explanation for GAN instability that is both novel and well-motivated. It offers a bold new direction for research rather than just incremental improvements to existing methods.

## 3. Supporting arguments

This paper's contribution is exceptional in its theoretical novelty and depth. The core insight about low-dimensional manifold structure leading to disjoint supports is a fresh perspective that elegantly explains the instability problem. The mathematical framework connecting differential geometry, probability theory, and GAN training is rigorous and comprehensive.

The theoretical results (Theorems 2.1-2.6, 3.1-3.3) are well-developed and provide a solid foundation for understanding GAN instability. The paper's key contribution is not just identifying the problem but providing a principled theoretical solution that has the potential to guide future research in GANs.

The paper's approach is bold in its theoretical scope—it doesn't just propose a new algorithm but provides a new theoretical lens through which to view the entire problem of GAN training. This aligns perfectly with ICLR's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection.

While the paper doesn't provide extensive empirical validation of the proposed noise-based approach, the theoretical framework is so strong that it would be a major contribution to the field even without these experiments. The theoretical explanation alone is significant enough to warrant acceptance.

## 4. Additional feedback

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation of the noise-based approach. How should the noise parameter σ be selected? Is there a principled way to anneal the noise, or does it require careful tuning for different datasets?

- A brief comparison with other recent theoretical approaches to GAN stability (e.g., Wasserstein GANs, gradient penalty methods) would help position this work within the broader literature.

- The paper could provide more intuition about why the noise-based approach avoids the adversarial vulnerability mentioned in the text. A simple example would help readers understand this important point.

- While the mathematical rigor is excellent, some readers may find the differential geometry concepts challenging. A slightly more intuitive explanation of key concepts like "transversality" and "manifolds" would broaden the paper's accessibility.

## Questions for authors

1. In the paper, you state that "the only way this can happen [JSD being maxed out] is if the distributions are not continuous or they have disjoint supports." Could you provide more intuition about why disjoint supports (which you argue are common in GANs) lead to this issue, beyond what's in the theorems?

2. The paper claims that the proposed noise-based approach "will make the two noisy distributions match" and that "as we anneal the noise, this will make Pr and Pg match as well." Could you elaborate on the annealing schedule and how it would be implemented in practice?

3. In Theorem 3.3, you show that W(Pr, Pg) ≤ 2V^(1/2) + 2C√JSD(Pr+, Pg+). How does this bound compare to the convergence rates provided by other approaches (e.g., the standard Wasserstein GAN), and what practical implications does it have for training?
