

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper provides a theoretically rigorous analysis of GAN training instability, identifying the root cause as the fact that generator and data distributions typically have disjoint supports on low-dimensional manifolds. The authors prove that this leads to vanishing gradients for the generator when using the standard GAN loss, and that the alternative loss (-log D) leads to unstable updates due to Cauchy-distributed gradients. They propose adding noise to inputs to create continuous distributions, enabling smooth gradient updates and providing a theoretical connection to the Wasserstein distance. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that clarifies fundamental issues in GAN training, with potential implications for developing more stable generative models.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The key reason for rejection is the lack of empirical validation to support the theoretical claims. While the theoretical arguments are strong and well-executed, the paper does not provide any experiments to demonstrate the practical implications of the theory or validate the proposed solution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's theoretical contributions are significant and well-executed. The proofs of the vanishing gradient phenomenon and the instability of the -log D loss are technically sound and address a fundamental gap in understanding GAN training. The connection to the Wasserstein distance is theoretically interesting and provides a fresh perspective on the problem.

However, the absence of empirical validation is a critical flaw for a paper making strong claims about a practical machine learning problem. The authors claim that adding noise to inputs would solve instability issues, but provide no experiments showing this actually works in practice. Without empirical evidence, it's impossible to assess whether the theoretical insights translate to practical improvements in GAN training. 

The paper would need to demonstrate:
1. That the vanishing gradient phenomenon occurs as predicted during standard GAN training
2. That the -log D loss leads to unstable updates as claimed
3. That the noise-based approach improves training stability and sample quality on standard benchmarks

Without these experiments, the paper fails to provide a complete picture of the problem and its solution. While theoretical work is valuable at ICLR, it must be supported by empirical evidence when making claims about practical machine learning problems. The lack of experiments is particularly problematic given the paper's strong claim to "change" how GANs are trained.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- **Empirical Validation is Critical**: The paper should include experiments demonstrating the theoretical claims. This would significantly strengthen the paper and make it more impactful for the machine learning community. The authors should test the vanishing gradient phenomenon on standard datasets and architectures, and validate their proposed noise-based solution.

- **Connection to Existing Work**: The paper makes a strong theoretical connection to the Wasserstein distance, but doesn't clearly position itself relative to existing work on Wasserstein GANs (WGAN, WGAN-GP). It would be valuable to explain how the noise-based approach differs from or complements these methods.

- **Practical Implementation Guidance**: The paper would benefit from providing more concrete guidance on how to implement the noise-based approach in practice, including recommended noise schedules and parameters.

- **Clarity of Key Insights**: While the theoretical arguments are strong, some key insights (e.g., the connection between the noise-based approach and the Wasserstein distance) could be made more accessible to readers by including more intuitive explanations.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- Why did you choose to focus on the noise-based approach rather than other existing techniques for improving GAN stability (e.g., WGAN, spectral normalization)? How does your theoretical framework help explain why those techniques work?

- Can you provide more intuition for why the gradient distribution is Cauchy (Theorem 2.6) rather than something with finite variance, given that the noise is Gaussian?

- How would you suggest implementing the noise-based approach in practice? What are the appropriate noise schedules, and how do they relate to the theoretical guarantees provided in the paper?

- Have you considered the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to existing methods? Would the noise-based approach require more training time or memory?

The theoretical insights in this paper are valuable and address an important gap in understanding GAN training. With empirical validation and clearer connections to practical implementation, this work could make a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, the lack of empirical evidence prevents me from recommending acceptance.
