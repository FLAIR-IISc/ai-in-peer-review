

# Review of "Theoretical Analysis of GAN Instability and a Path Towards Resolution"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of GAN training instability, addressing why GANs are difficult to train despite their empirical success. The authors rigorously prove that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (accuracy 1) with zero gradient almost everywhere on the supports of the distributions. This leads to vanishing gradients in the original cost function and unstable updates with infinite variance in the alternative cost function. The paper then proposes a principled solution by adding noise to the discriminator inputs, creating continuous distributions (Pr+ and Pg+) that allow for meaningful gradient updates. The authors further establish a connection between the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions, providing a theoretically grounded approach to training stable GANs.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, the paper's main contribution is a rephrasing of known phenomena rather than a significant advancement of the field. The proposed solution (adding noise to the discriminator inputs) is not novel, and the paper fails to demonstrate how its theoretical framework leads to practical improvements over existing stable GAN methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's key theoretical contributions are well-motivated and technically rigorous. The proofs of Theorems 2.1-2.6 and 3.1-3.3 are mathematically sound and carefully constructed, providing a solid foundation for understanding GAN instability. The connection between the Wasserstein distance and the JSD of noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3) is interesting and theoretically important.

However, the paper's main limitation is that it does not provide significant new insights beyond what is already established in the literature. The vanishing gradient problem was already identified in Goodfellow et al.'s original GAN paper (2014), and subsequent work (e.g., Theis et al., 2016; Huszar, 2015) has explored similar theoretical aspects. The proposed noise-based solution is not novel—similar approaches have been explored in prior work (e.g., "Noisy GANs" by Xu et al., 2019; "Spectral Normalization" by Miyato et al., 2018). The paper fails to adequately distinguish its theoretical contribution from existing literature.

Furthermore, the paper lacks empirical validation of its proposed method's practical benefits. It does not compare the noise-based approach against existing stable GAN variants (e.g., WGAN, WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization) on standard benchmarks like CIFAR-10 or ImageNet. Without such empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess whether the theoretical framework translates to meaningful improvements in practice. The paper also does not address how to select the optimal noise level in practice or how the approach affects mode coverage (a critical issue in GANs).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Novelty and Positioning**: Strengthen the paper's contribution by more precisely positioning it within the literature. Explicitly contrast your theoretical framework with prior work on GAN stability (e.g., Arjovsky et al.'s Wasserstein GAN, Miyato et al.'s Spectral Normalization) and demonstrate how your approach provides fundamentally new insights or capabilities.

- **Empirical Validation**: Conduct comprehensive experiments comparing your noise-based approach against state-of-the-art stable GAN methods on standard benchmarks. Include metrics beyond visual quality (e.g., FID, IS, mode coverage) to demonstrate practical advantages.

- **Practical Implementation**: Discuss how to select the optimal noise level in practice. Explore how your approach might interact with other stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties) and whether it can be combined with them.

- **Theoretical-Practical Bridge**: Provide more concrete guidance on how practitioners can apply your theoretical framework. For instance, explain how to choose the noise distribution (beyond just Gaussian) and how to anneal the noise level during training.

- **Connection to Mode Dropping**: The paper mentions mode dropping as a key issue but doesn't clearly connect how the noise-based approach affects this phenomenon. Provide theoretical or empirical evidence on whether the proposed method mitigates mode dropping.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your noise-based approach compare empirically to existing stable GAN methods (e.g., WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization) on standard benchmarks like CIFAR-10 or ImageNet? What metrics would you use to demonstrate superiority?

2. What is your recommended approach for selecting the optimal noise level σ in practice? Is there a principled way to determine this based on the data or architecture?

3. Have you explored how your approach affects mode coverage compared to standard GANs? Given that the paper discusses mode dropping as a key issue, it would be valuable to see empirical evidence on whether your method mitigates this problem.

4. The paper states that "the distributions are not continuous" when their supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds. However, many modern GANs (e.g., StyleGAN) achieve excellent performance without explicitly adding noise to the discriminator. How does your theory account for this success?
