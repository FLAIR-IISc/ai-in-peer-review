- Paper provides rigorous theory explaining GAN instability due to data and generator distributions lying on low-dimensional manifolds, causing perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients.  
- Shows original GAN loss leads to vanishing gradients as discriminator improves, while the common modified loss causes unstable updates with infinite variance.  
- Proposes adding input noise to smooth distributions, enabling meaningful discriminator gradients and generator updates.  
- Connects the noisy-discriminator framework to Wasserstein distance, offering a principled distance measure for low-dimensional supports.  
- Theoretical results are well-motivated, rigorously proven (Theorems 2.1–3.3, Corollary 3.2) and clearly written with solid notation.  
- Decision: Accept — significant theoretical contribution that advances understanding of GAN training dynamics.  
- Major strength: novel geometric insight that generator outputs and data lie on low-dimensional manifolds due to generator architecture.  
- Major strength: clear analysis distinguishing failure modes of original vs. modified GAN objectives.  
- Proposed solution is theoretically sound but lacks extensive empirical validation on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet).  
- Paper does not provide practical guidance on selecting noise variance or hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.  
- Connection to existing architectures (e.g., DCGAN) and concrete implementation details for incorporating noise are underdeveloped.  
- Question: How should noise variance be chosen in practice and is it dataset-dependent?  
- Question: Have noisy-input claims (e.g., training discriminator to optimality) been tested on diverse architectures like StyleGAN or BigGAN?  
- Question: How does the noisy-input approach compare to feature matching and other stability techniques?  
- Question: How can the two terms in equation (6) be practically controlled during training (e.g., annealing noise)?
