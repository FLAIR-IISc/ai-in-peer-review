

# Review of "A Theoretical Analysis of GAN Instability and a Path Towards Resolution"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training instability. The authors argue that the fundamental issue stems from the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, leading to perfect discriminators that provide vanishing gradients for the generator. The paper provides rigorous theorems establishing this phenomenon and proposes a solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator to smooth the distributions and avoid the problematic manifolds. The paper also introduces a novel connection between the Wasserstein distance and the noisy GAN training approach. Overall, the paper presents a theoretically rich perspective on GAN instability that could guide future research.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains multiple critical technical errors in its proofs and theorems that undermine its central claims. The mathematical inconsistencies are severe enough to prevent confident assessment of the paper's theoretical contributions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Rejection

The paper's main theoretical claims are undermined by several serious technical errors:

1. **Critical Error in Theorem 2.4 (Vanishing Gradients):** The paper claims:
   ```
   ‖∇θEz∼p(z)[log(1−D(gθ(z)))]‖2 < M/ε
   ```
   This inequality is mathematically meaningless because the denominator ε (a small positive number) would be subtracted from 1, resulting in a negative value when ε < 1. The inequality would then state that a positive quantity (left-hand side) is less than a negative quantity (right-hand side), which is impossible. This is a fundamental error that invalidates the entire theorem.

2. **Incomplete Proof in Theorem 3.2:** The paper claims "a(z) and b(z) are positive functions" but fails to provide a proper justification. While it's trivial that these quantities would be positive given their definitions, the omission of this basic verification is unprofessional for a theoretical paper.

3. **Misstatement in Theorem 2.3:** The paper claims "JSD(Pr‖Pg) = log 2" without clarifying that this only holds when Pr and Pg have completely disjoint supports, which is the very condition that the theorems are proving. The Jensen-Shannon divergence is always bounded between 0 and log 2, and the maximum value is achieved precisely when the distributions are completely disjoint. This misstatement creates confusion about the actual value of the divergence.

4. **Multiple Typos and Formatting Inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous minor errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading:
   - "symetrical" instead of "symmetrical"
   - "adressing" instead of "addressing"
   - Inconsistent use of "manifolds" vs. "manifolds"
   - Inconsistent reference formatting (some with "?", some without)
   - Inconsistent mathematical notation (e.g., "dimZ" vs. "dim Z")

5. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** While the paper makes strong theoretical claims, it provides no empirical evidence to support the proposed noise-based solution. Theoretical claims about how adding noise would resolve training instability should be validated with experiments, which are absent.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

The paper's theoretical perspective is promising, but the following improvements would be necessary before it could be considered for publication:

1. **Correct the mathematical errors in Theorems 2.4 and 3.2.** The inequality in Theorem 2.4 must be fixed to make mathematical sense. The proof of positivity for a(z) and b(z) should be explicitly provided.

2. **Clarify the conditions under which JSD(Pr||Pg) = log 2.** The paper should explicitly state that this maximum value occurs only when Pr and Pg have completely disjoint supports, which is the very condition being analyzed.

3. **Add empirical validation.** The paper should include experiments demonstrating that adding noise to the discriminator inputs resolves the instability issues it describes. This would strengthen the connection between the theory and practice.

4. **Improve proofreading.** The paper needs careful editing to fix typos, inconsistent notation, and formatting issues. These are not merely cosmetic but affect the paper's credibility.

5. **Clarify the relationship between the Wasserstein metric and the proposed approach.** The paper claims to relate Wasserstein distance to the noisy GAN approach, but the connection is not clearly articulated.

## Questions for Authors

1. How would you fix the inequality in Theorem 2.4 to make it mathematically meaningful? What was the intended inequality?

2. The paper claims that "KL(Pr‖Pg) = +∞" in Theorem 2.3. Can you clarify the exact conditions under which this holds, and how it relates to the practical behavior of GAN training?

3. Could you provide experimental evidence demonstrating that adding noise to the discriminator inputs resolves the instability issues described in the paper?

4. The paper states that "the support of Pg has to be contained in g(Z)" (line 161). However, for neural networks, the support of Pg is typically the image of g, not just the image of g(Z) (which would be the same as the support). Could you clarify this point?

These issues are substantial enough that I cannot recommend acceptance of the paper in its current form. The theoretical contributions are interesting, but the mathematical errors undermine the paper's credibility. I would recommend the authors address these technical issues and provide empirical validation before resubmitting.
