

# Review of "Theoretical Analysis of GAN Instability and a Path to Softer Metrics"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the instability issues in GAN training, identifying that the core problem stems from the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. The authors prove that under these conditions, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (accuracy 1) with zero gradients almost everywhere, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator. They then propose a solution involving adding noise to both generator and discriminator inputs to create "softer" metrics that avoid these instability issues. The paper makes several theoretical contributions, including proving the properties of the Jensen-Shannon divergence in this context, showing the vanishing gradient phenomenon, and establishing a relationship between the Wasserstein distance and the noisy distributions.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains numerous minor but pervasive technical errors and formatting inconsistencies that undermine the confidence in its theoretical claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical contributions are interesting and potentially valuable, but the numerous minor technical errors and inconsistencies throughout the paper prevent me from confidently endorsing it for publication. These issues include:

1. **Mathematical inconsistencies**: The paper incorrectly states "JSD(Pr‖Pg) = log 2" in multiple places when the standard Jensen-Shannon divergence is bounded by log 2 (with log 2 being the maximum value). The correct statement should be "JSD(Pr‖Pg) = log 2 - 1" or similar, but the paper fails to clarify this distinction. This is a significant error in the theoretical foundation of the paper.

2. **Theoretical errors**: In Theorem 2.6, the paper claims the gradient updates follow a "centered Cauchy distribution with infinite expectation and variance." This is technically incorrect—the Cauchy distribution has an undefined expectation (not infinite), and the paper should have been precise about this statistical detail.

3. **Formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains numerous spelling and grammatical errors throughout, including "adressing" (should be "addressing"), "symetrical" (should be "symmetric"), "imposible" (should be "impossible"), and "maxed out" (should be "maximized"). These are not merely cosmetic but indicate a lack of careful proofreading.

4. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the Jensen-Shannon divergence, sometimes writing "JSD(Pr‖Pg) = 1" when it should be "JSD(Pr‖Pg) = 1/2" (the standard definition). The paper also inconsistently capitalizes "Jensen-Shannon" and uses inconsistent spacing in references.

5. **Proof errors**: In Theorem 2.4, the bound should be "Mε/(1-ε)^2" but the paper writes "M/ε" in the statement. The proof correctly derives the bound, but the statement is incorrect. In Theorem 3.3, the square root is incorrectly placed inside the JSD term.

These errors are pervasive throughout the paper, appearing in both the main text and the proofs. While each individual error might seem minor, their cumulative effect significantly undermines the paper's credibility and makes it difficult to trust the theoretical claims. The paper's contribution is potentially valuable, but the numerous technical inconsistencies prevent me from recommending acceptance.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

1. Carefully proofread the paper to correct all spelling, grammatical, and typographical errors. This is especially important for a theoretical paper where precision matters.

2. Clarify all mathematical statements, particularly regarding the Jensen-Shannon divergence. Explicitly define the standard JSD and clarify when it equals log 2 versus when it's bounded by log 2.

3. Correct the statistical description of the Cauchy distribution in Theorem 2.6—specify that the expectation is undefined, not infinite.

4. Standardize all notation throughout the paper, including consistent capitalization of "Jensen-Shannon" and consistent spacing in references.

5. Verify all proofs for mathematical precision, particularly the bounds in Theorems 2.4 and 3.3.

6. Ensure consistent use of "Pr" and "Pg" throughout the paper, avoiding confusing notation where "P" is also used for probability.

I believe these corrections would significantly strengthen the paper and make it suitable for publication. However, given the current state of the paper, I must recommend rejection due to the numerous technical inconsistencies that undermine the theoretical claims.
