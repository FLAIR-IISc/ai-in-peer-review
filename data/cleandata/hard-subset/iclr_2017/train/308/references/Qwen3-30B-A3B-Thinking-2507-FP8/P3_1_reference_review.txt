## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of GAN training instability. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for why GANs are difficult to train, fundamentally explaining the instability through the lens of the geometric properties of data and generator distributions. The key contributions are:

1. A theoretical demonstration that both data (Pr) and generator (Pg) distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds (due to the generator's input dimensionality being lower than the output space), leading to disjoint supports.

2. The development of "perfect discrimination theorems" showing that when distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds, there exists a perfect discriminator that is constant almost everywhere on these manifolds, causing vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator.

3. A detailed analysis of both the original GAN cost function (leading to vanishing gradients) and the -logD alternative (leading to unstable gradients with infinite variance).

4. A proposed solution involving adding continuous noise to inputs, which smooths the distributions and creates continuous Pr+ and Pg+ with non-maximal divergences, enabling stable gradient updates.

5. A connection between the noisy GAN approach and the Wasserstein distance, showing that the distance between the original distributions can be bounded by the distance between the noisy distributions plus a term related to noise variance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical statements, rigorous proofs, and insightful connections to established concepts like the Wasserstein metric. The authors successfully bridge theoretical analysis with practical implications for GAN training.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, and the proposed theoretical framework is both novel and well-supported. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. It provides the first rigorous theoretical explanation for GAN training instability that addresses the core geometric properties of the distributions involved.
2. The analysis of gradient vanishing and instability is thorough, precise, and well-motivated by the geometric properties of the distributions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### Strengths of the Theoretical Contribution

The paper's greatest strength is its clear identification of the root cause of GAN instability: the low-dimensional manifold structure of data and generator distributions leading to disjoint supports. This is a fundamental insight that explains why GANs behave differently from maximum likelihood approaches.

The perfect discrimination theorems (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) are particularly compelling. They rigorously prove that when distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical for GANs), the optimal discriminator will be perfect and constant almost everywhere on the manifolds, leading to the gradient issues observed in practice.

The analysis of both cost functions (original and -logD) is comprehensive and addresses a key question that has been debated in the community. Theorem 2.6's demonstration that the -logD gradient updates follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance is particularly noteworthy and explains the instability observed in practice.

The noisy GAN approach (Section 3) is a natural and elegant solution that follows directly from the theoretical analysis. The connection to the Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is insightful and provides a meaningful theoretical justification for the approach.

### Minor Concerns Addressed

While the paper is strong theoretically, I have a few minor concerns that can be addressed with minor revisions:

1. The paper could more clearly connect the theoretical findings to existing empirical observations about GAN instability. While the paper references empirical evidence in Figures 2 and 3, a more explicit discussion of how these observations align with the theoretical framework would strengthen the paper.

2. The practical implementation details of the noisy GAN approach (e.g., how to choose the noise level) are not fully developed. While this is expected in a theoretical paper, a brief discussion of practical considerations would be helpful.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

### Minor Revisions for Clarity and Impact

1. **Section 2.2.2 Discussion**: The paper could more explicitly state how the theoretical analysis explains the common practice of training the discriminator more frequently than the generator (a common heuristic to avoid vanishing gradients). The paper mentions this in the discussion of Corollary 2.1, but a more direct connection would strengthen the practical relevance.

2. **Empirical Validation**: While the paper is primarily theoretical, adding a single empirical example demonstrating the noisy GAN approach on a small dataset (e.g., MNIST) would significantly strengthen the practical relevance. This could be a simple ablation study showing that adding noise leads to more stable training.

3. **Comparison to Related Work**: The paper should more explicitly compare its approach to other recent stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty, or the improved training techniques by Salimans et al.). This would better situate the paper within the current literature.

4. **Noise Level Discussion**: The paper should discuss how the noise level (σ in the Gaussian noise) should be chosen in practice. The theoretical analysis shows that annealing the noise (reducing it over time) leads to convergence to the original distributions, but the paper could discuss practical considerations for this annealing schedule.

5. **Clarify "Manifold" Terminology**: The paper uses "manifold" throughout, but it's worth clarifying that this refers to low-dimensional submanifolds of the ambient space (not necessarily smooth manifolds in the strict mathematical sense), as this is a common point of confusion.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me better understand and assess the paper, I'd appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. The paper states that "the support of Pg has to be contained in g(Z)" and argues that if dim(Z) < dim(X), then g(Z) has measure 0 in X. This is a critical assumption for the paper's argument. Could the authors provide a concrete example where a neural network with dim(Z) < dim(X) does not produce a distribution with support on a low-dimensional manifold? (This would help clarify the generality of the assumption.)

2. The paper proves that the gradient updates for the -logD cost function follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6). However, in practice, the noise is not exactly white Gaussian but is limited by finite precision. How would this finite precision affect the practical behavior of the gradient updates?

3. The paper shows that adding noise to both the discriminator inputs and generator outputs (Corollary 3.2) leads to smooth gradients. How does this differ from simply adding noise to the discriminator inputs (which is a common practice)? What is the specific benefit of also adding noise to the generator?

4. The paper establishes a bound between the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions and the JSD of the noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3). How tight is this bound in practice? For example, if we want to achieve a certain Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg, how much noise would need to be added to get a reasonable JSD between Pr+ and Pg+?

5. The paper mentions that "the generator's backprop term will be through samples on a set of positive measure that the discriminator will care about" when noise is added to the generator. How does this affect the final samples (i.e., when the noise is annealed out)? Will the final samples still lie on the data manifold?

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of generative modeling, and I believe it will be an important reference for future work on GANs. With the minor revisions suggested above, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
