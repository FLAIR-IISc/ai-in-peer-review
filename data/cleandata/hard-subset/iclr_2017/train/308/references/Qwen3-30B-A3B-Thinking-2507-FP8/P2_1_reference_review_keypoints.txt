- Paper provides a rigorous geometric explanation for GAN instability: data and generator live on low-dimensional, (nearly) disjoint manifolds causing training problems.
- Proves that disjoint low-dimensional supports make Jensen-Shannon divergence maximized and gradient-based optimization impossible as discriminator improves.
- Shows the original GAN cost leads to vanishing gradients for the generator when the discriminator is near-optimal.
- Shows the common generator objective modification (-log D) yields unstable updates with infinite variance.
- Proposes adding noise to discriminator inputs to create "softer metrics" that enable meaningful gradient updates.
- Proves a theoretical connection between the noisy-discriminator method and the Wasserstein distance, showing it minimizes a bound on Wasserstein distance.
- Paper moves beyond heuristics to provide a principled, mathematically backed solution to GAN instability.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reasons for acceptance: fills a critical theoretical gap and offers a principled, proof-backed solution (noise addition).
- Strong theoretical contributions: Lemma on generator support as low-dimensional manifold and use of differential geometry are well-motivated and rigorous.
- Empirical results (figures) support claims about vanishing gradients and instability.
- Good positioning in literature, building on and extending prior work (Theis et al., Huszar).
- Requests clarification/guidance on selecting and annealing the noise parameter σ in practice.
- Suggests an explicit comparison with WGAN to clarify relationships and differences.
- Asks for clarification of what "matching" noisy distributions means and how it implies convergence to data distribution.
- Suggests explicating how the noisy-discriminator approach addresses mode collapse/mode dropping.
- Recommends using the precise term "absolutely continuous" rather than "continuous" to avoid confusion.
- Questions for authors: comparison to WGAN on stability/quality/theory; practical effects of different noise distributions (Gaussian vs Cauchy); how to estimate support diameter C and choose annealing schedules; how claim about not worrying about training schedules compares to WGAN; and a more precise statement relating noisy optimization to the Wasserstein distance.
