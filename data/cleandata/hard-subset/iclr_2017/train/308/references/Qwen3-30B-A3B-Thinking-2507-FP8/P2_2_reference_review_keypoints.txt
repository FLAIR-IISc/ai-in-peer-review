- Main claim: GAN instability arises because data and generator distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds, causing pathological discriminator behavior.  
- Theoretical contribution: Proof that common neural nets map low-dimensional inputs to low-dimensional (measure-zero) manifolds in output space.  
- Result: When supports are disjoint, the optimal discriminator is perfect and has zero gradient on the generator's support.  
- Explanation: The original GAN cost leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves.  
- Explanation: The −log D alternative yields unstable updates due to gradients following a Cauchy distribution.  
- Proposed fix: Add noise to discriminator (and generator) inputs to produce “softer” metrics that avoid the pathological behavior.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary rejection reason: No empirical validation provided to support the theoretical claims.  
- Primary rejection reason: Limited practical contribution—no new, implemented algorithm or evaluated method.  
- Supporting praise: Proofs are technically sound and the theoretical framework is insightful.  
- Supporting criticism: Figures referenced (e.g., Fig. 2, Fig. 3) are missing, preventing assessment of any empirical claims.  
- Supporting criticism: No comparison to existing GAN stabilization methods (WGAN, WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization).  
- Supporting criticism: Lacks practical implementation details (e.g., noise variance, annealing schedule).  
- Concern: Hard to assess practical significance of theory without experiments demonstrating improvements.  
- Recommendation: Add empirical experiments showing the noise-based approach stabilizes training and improves sample quality (quantitative and qualitative).  
- Recommendation: Provide concrete implementation guidance (σ², annealing schedule, integration tips).  
- Recommendation: Thoroughly compare to related work and position the theoretical contributions relative to recent advances.  
- Recommendation: Clarify and expand analysis of how adding noise prevents discriminator failures on g(Z).  
- Questions to authors: Can you provide quantitative/qualitative empirical evidence (FID, IS, samples) for the noise approach?  
- Questions to authors: How does the noise-based method compare empirically to WGAN, WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization?  
- Questions to authors: How exactly does adding noise prevent the discriminator from disregarding errors on g(Z) and adversarial examples?  
- Questions to authors: How does training the discriminator to optimality with noise compare to practical WGAN training (multiple D steps per G step)?  
- Questions to authors: What is the recommended annealing schedule for noise variance and its effect on final sample quality?
