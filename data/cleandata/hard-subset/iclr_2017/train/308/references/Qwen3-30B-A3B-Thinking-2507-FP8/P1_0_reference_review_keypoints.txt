- Core claim: GAN instability arises because data and generator distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds, yielding perfect discrimination and problematic gradients.  
- Contribution: Proves optimal discriminator is perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- Contribution: Shows the original GAN loss leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates the alternative loss (-log D) yields unstable gradient updates due to discriminator approximation noise.  
- Contribution: Proposes adding input noise to the discriminator to create a smoothed distribution that permits meaningful gradients.  
- Contribution: Establishes a theoretical connection between the noise-adding approach and the Wasserstein distance.
