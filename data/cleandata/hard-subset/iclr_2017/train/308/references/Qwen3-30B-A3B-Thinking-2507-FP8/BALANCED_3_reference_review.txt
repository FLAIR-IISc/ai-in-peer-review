

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the instability of GAN training. The authors identify a fundamental issue: the generator and data distributions typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, leading to perfect discriminators that provide vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator. They provide rigorous theorems showing why the original GAN cost function leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves, and why the alternative cost function (-log D) leads to unstable updates with infinite variance. The paper proposes a principled solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator), which smooths the distributions and makes them absolutely continuous, allowing for meaningful gradient updates. The authors connect this to the Wasserstein metric, showing how the noise can be annealed to minimize the distance between the original distributions.

## 2. Decision

Reject.

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. The paper lacks substantial empirical validation of its theoretical claims, particularly regarding the practical benefits of the proposed noise addition approach.
2. The paper makes strong theoretical claims but doesn't adequately address how these findings translate to practical GAN training improvements.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify that generator distributions, when parameterized by neural networks with lower-dimensional latent spaces, necessarily lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1), which leads to the problematic behavior described in Theorems 2.1-2.3. The proofs for the vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) and instability of the -log D cost (Theorem 2.6) are rigorous and important contributions to the theoretical understanding of GANs.

However, the paper fails to bridge the gap between theory and practice. While the theoretical framework is sound, the authors provide no empirical evidence that their proposed noise addition actually improves GAN training in practice. The paper references Figures 2 and 3, but these appear to be minimal illustrations rather than comprehensive experimental results. Without demonstrating that their approach leads to better sample quality, faster convergence, or improved stability compared to existing methods (like spectral normalization, gradient penalty, or other stabilization techniques), the practical relevance of the work remains unproven.

Furthermore, the paper makes a strong claim about the nature of real-world GAN distributions (that they lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports), but provides no evidence for this beyond theoretical reasoning. While the theoretical argument is compelling, it would be valuable to see empirical evidence that this is indeed the case for typical GANs trained on standard datasets.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Add empirical validation**: Conduct comprehensive experiments comparing the proposed noise addition method with state-of-the-art GAN stabilization techniques on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet). Include metrics like FID, IS, and visual quality assessment.

2. **Address practical implementation**: The paper should discuss how the noise parameter (σ) should be chosen in practice, how it should be annealed, and how it affects training dynamics beyond theoretical guarantees.

3. **Compare with existing work**: Provide a thorough comparison with related work on GAN stability, particularly papers that address similar theoretical issues (e.g., Arjovsky et al.'s Wasserstein GAN, Gulrajani et al.'s improved training).

4. **Clarify the connection to practical GANs**: While the theoretical framework is elegant, the paper should better explain how the theory directly informs practical GAN implementation. For example, what specific changes would a practitioner make to their code based on these insights?

5. **Expand the discussion of noise**: The paper focuses on Gaussian noise, but it would be valuable to discuss how other noise distributions might affect the results and whether there are principled ways to choose the noise distribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide empirical evidence that the distributions of real data and GAN-generated samples indeed lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports in practice? How would you measure this for a given GAN training setup?

2. How does the proposed noise addition approach compare to existing methods like gradient penalty or spectral normalization in terms of sample quality (FID/IS) and training stability across multiple datasets?

3. The paper claims that "as we decrease the noise, PX and PX+ become more similar" (Lemma 4). How would you practically implement this annealing schedule in a training loop, and how would you determine the optimal annealing rate?

4. The authors state that "the discriminator will disregard errors that lie exactly in g(Z), since this is a set of measure 0." How would you address this issue in practice, given that real neural networks don't perfectly represent the theoretical manifold?

5. The paper shows that the gradient updates follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6). How would you practically handle this in training, given that optimization algorithms cannot directly work with infinite variance?
