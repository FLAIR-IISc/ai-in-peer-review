

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims and Contributions

This paper provides a theoretically rigorous analysis of the fundamental instability issues in GAN training. The authors make a compelling argument that the core problem stems from the fact that both the real data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg) typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds in the high-dimensional input space. This leads to the optimal discriminator being perfect (with accuracy 1) and having zero gradients almost everywhere on the manifolds, which in turn causes either vanishing gradients for the original GAN cost function or unstable gradient updates with infinite variance for the alternative cost function (-log D).

The paper's key theoretical contributions include:
- Formal proofs showing that neural network generators produce distributions with support on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1)
- Theorems establishing that perfect discriminators exist when distributions lie on non-transversally intersecting manifolds (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2)
- Analysis showing vanishing gradients for the original cost function (Theorem 2.4) and unstable updates for the alternative cost function (Theorem 2.6)
- A proposed solution involving adding noise to both generator and discriminator inputs, which creates "softer" metrics that avoid the instability issues
- Theoretical connection between the Wasserstein distance (a desirable metric for generative modeling) and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3)

The paper provides a well-structured theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability, which has been a persistent challenge in the field. The authors have clearly identified a fundamental issue that explains many of the empirical challenges observed in GAN training.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

The paper's theoretical contributions are interesting and well-justified, but the paper is rejected for two primary reasons:
1. The main theoretical contribution (the explanation of GAN instability via low-dimensional manifolds) is not sufficiently novel compared to existing work in the field.
2. The proposed solution lacks empirical validation and meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art techniques for stable GAN training.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

### Novelty Concerns
While the paper provides a rigorous formalization of the intuition that GANs struggle with low-dimensional manifolds, this concept has been discussed in the literature before. Theis et al. (2016) already noted that "GANs are able to produce visually convincing samples, while VAEs and other likelihood-based methods produce blurry images," which implicitly acknowledges the manifold issue. More directly, Arjovsky and Bottou (2017) and subsequent work on the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) literature has already provided a theoretical understanding of GAN instability through the lens of the Wasserstein distance and distributional geometry. The paper's core insight is not new, and the formalization doesn't provide significant additional theoretical insight beyond what's already known.

### Lack of Empirical Validation
The paper's proposed solution (adding noise to inputs) is purely theoretical. It lacks empirical validation showing that this approach actually improves GAN training in practice. The authors don't compare their method against existing state-of-the-art techniques like WGAN, WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, or gradient penalty methods, which have been widely adopted in the field. Without empirical evidence demonstrating that the noise-based approach improves sample quality or training stability compared to these methods, the proposal lacks practical relevance.

### Limited Practical Impact
The paper claims that "this will make the two noisy distributions match. As we anneal the noise, this will make Pr and Pg match as well." However, the paper doesn't address how to effectively anneal the noise, what the optimal annealing schedule would be, or how this compares to existing approaches for gradually improving GAN training. The connection to Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is interesting but doesn't provide a practical method for estimating or minimizing the Wasserstein distance, which was already the focus of the WGAN literature.

### Incomplete Literature Context
The paper fails to properly contextualize its contribution within the broader literature on GAN stability. It doesn't discuss how its theoretical framework relates to or improves upon the WGAN framework (Arjovsky et al., 2017), which has been the dominant approach to addressing GAN instability in the past few years. The paper also doesn't reference the extensive work on the geometric properties of data manifolds (e.g., by Narayanan and Mitter, 2010) that directly supports the paper's main premise.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

### Theoretical Contributions
- The paper would benefit from a more precise mathematical treatment of the "low-dimensional manifold" assumption. While the paper claims this is typical, it lacks empirical evidence showing that real data distributions (e.g., from CIFAR-10 or ImageNet) actually lie on low-dimensional manifolds. A simple experiment showing the intrinsic dimension of these datasets would strengthen the paper significantly.
- The paper's analysis of the alternative cost function (-log D) assumes the discriminator is "close to optimal." In practice, the discriminator is never perfectly optimal, so the paper should discuss how this affects the practical behavior of the gradient updates.

### Empirical Validation
- The paper needs to provide empirical validation of its theoretical claims. At minimum, it should show that the gradients of the generator indeed vanish as the discriminator improves (as claimed in Theorem 2.4) and that the alternative cost function leads to unstable updates (as claimed in Theorem 2.6).
- The authors should compare their proposed noise-based approach with existing state-of-the-art techniques (WGAN, WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, etc.) on standard benchmark datasets (CIFAR-10, LSUN, etc.) to demonstrate practical improvements.

### Connection to Existing Work
- The paper should more thoroughly contextualize its contribution within the WGAN literature. The connection to Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is interesting, but it doesn't explain how this differs from or improves upon the WGAN approach.
- The paper should discuss how its theoretical framework relates to the mode collapse problem, which is a well-known consequence of GAN instability. The paper mentions mode dropping but doesn't connect it to the manifold theory.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims "the only way this can happen is if the distributions are not continuous or they have disjoint supports." How does this account for cases where the real data distribution and the generator's distribution have overlapping supports but still lie on low-dimensional manifolds? For example, if both distributions are supported on the same low-dimensional manifold, how does this affect the discriminator and the training dynamics?

2. The paper suggests that adding noise to both generator and discriminator inputs creates "softer" metrics. What is the optimal amount of noise to add, and how does this depend on the specific dataset and network architecture? What is the theoretical basis for choosing the noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian)?

3. The paper claims that "the Wasserstein distance also goes by other names, most commonly the transportation metric and the earth mover's distance." Given that the WGAN paper (Arjovsky et al., 2017) directly used the Wasserstein distance to address GAN instability, how does your approach differ from or improve upon WGAN?

4. The paper states that "the gradient passed to the generator has the form" in Theorem 3.2. How does this gradient compare to the gradient used in WGAN-GP, which has been shown to provide stable training and high-quality samples in practice?

5. The paper claims that "as we anneal the noise, this will make Pr and Pg match as well." What is the theoretical basis for this annealing schedule, and how does it compare to the learning rate schedules commonly used in practice for GAN training?
