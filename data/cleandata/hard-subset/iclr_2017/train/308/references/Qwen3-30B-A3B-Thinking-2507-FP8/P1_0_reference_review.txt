

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental instability issues in GAN training. The authors provide a rigorous analysis showing that the core problem stems from the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. This leads to perfect discrimination, which in turn causes vanishing gradients for the original GAN cost function and unstable gradient updates for the alternative cost function (-log D).

The paper makes several important contributions:
- It proves that when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect (with accuracy 1) and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the distributions.
- It formally demonstrates why the original cost function leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves.
- It shows why the alternative cost function (-log D) leads to unstable gradient updates due to noise in the discriminator approximation.
- It proposes a principled solution by adding noise to the discriminator inputs, which creates a smoothed distribution that allows for meaningful gradient updates.
- It establishes a theoretical connection between this noise-adding approach and the Wasserstein distance as a more appropriate metric for comparing distributions on low-dimensional manifolds.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear theorems, rigorous proofs, and strong connections to related work. The theoretical framework is comprehensive and provides a solid foundation for understanding GAN instability.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

This paper makes a fundamental theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, which is a major challenge in the field. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, well-motivated, and addresses a critical gap in the literature. The proposed solution is principled, simple, and has clear implications for future research.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution**: The paper identifies and rigorously proves a fundamental issue in GAN training that has been observed empirically but lacked a solid theoretical explanation. The analysis of why gradients vanish and why the alternative cost function leads to unstable updates is particularly compelling and fills a critical theoretical gap.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper's theoretical framework is exceptionally well-structured, with clear theorems, rigorous proofs, and careful attention to mathematical details. The authors properly address the technical challenges (e.g., manifolds, transversality, measure theory) that are crucial for a proper analysis of this problem.

3. **Well-Motivated and Well-Placed in Literature**: The paper begins with a thorough review of related work, clearly positioning itself within the current literature. It acknowledges the limitations of previous approaches (like the original GAN paper's heuristic solution) and shows how the current work provides a more principled understanding.

4. **Practical Implications**: While the paper is primarily theoretical, it offers a clear, simple, and principled solution to the instability problem that could be immediately implemented by practitioners. The noise-adding approach provides a practical path forward for more stable GAN training.

5. **Connection to Wasserstein Distance**: The paper's connection to the Wasserstein distance as a more appropriate metric for comparing distributions on low-dimensional manifolds is particularly insightful and provides a bridge between theory and practice.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is exceptionally strong, these suggestions could further enhance its impact:

- **More Detailed Experimental Validation**: While the paper includes some empirical evidence (Figures 2 and 3), additional experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of the noise-adding approach in practice would strengthen the paper. Specifically, comparing the proposed method against state-of-the-art GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties, WGAN variants) would provide concrete evidence of its practical value.

- **Discussion of Computational Cost**: The paper should discuss the computational cost of adding noise to the discriminator inputs and how it affects training speed. While the noise-adding approach is theoretically elegant, practitioners need to understand the practical trade-offs.

- **Practical Guidance for Noise Parameters**: The paper mentions annealing the noise but doesn't provide guidance on how to choose the optimal noise distribution and variance. Adding a brief discussion of practical considerations for implementing this approach would make the paper more valuable to practitioners.

- **Clarification of Connection to Contrastive Divergence**: The paper mentions that the gradient update resembles contrastive divergence. A brief elaboration on how this connection could lead to new algorithmic insights would be valuable.

- **Discussion of Limitations**: While the paper is strong, it would be helpful to explicitly discuss any limitations of the noise-adding approach, such as potential trade-offs between stability and sample quality.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that the noise can be annealed, but doesn't provide guidance on how to choose the annealing schedule. Could you provide some empirical evidence or theoretical insights about how the annealing schedule affects the final quality of generated samples?

2. In the case of conditional GANs, where the generator takes additional conditioning variables, how would the proposed noise-adding approach need to be modified? Would the noise need to be added to the conditioning variables as well?

3. The paper shows that the noise-adding approach connects to the Wasserstein distance. Could you elaborate on how this connection might lead to more efficient approximations of the Wasserstein distance in practice?

4. The paper mentions that the approach could be applied to other generative models beyond GANs. Could you provide specific examples of how this theoretical framework might help improve other generative modeling approaches?

This paper represents a major advance in our theoretical understanding of GANs. The insights it provides will likely influence future research on GAN training and generative modeling more broadly. I strongly recommend acceptance.
