

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a rigorous explanation for the instability of GAN training. The authors demonstrate that the fundamental source of instability is the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, leading to perfect discriminators that cause either vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator. The paper develops a comprehensive theoretical framework showing that the Jensen-Shannon divergence (and related divergences) between distributions on disjoint manifolds are maximized, making gradient-based optimization impossible. The authors then propose a noise-adding solution to smooth the distributions, making them continuous and enabling stable gradient updates. The work provides a principled theoretical foundation for understanding GAN behavior that goes beyond the typical heuristic approaches in the field.

## 2. Decision
Reject

Key reasons: While the theoretical framework is elegant and well-motivated, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation of the core theoretical claims and does not adequately address how their proposed solution would perform in practical GAN training scenarios. The theoretical contribution is significant but incomplete without strong empirical support.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors successfully demonstrate that when distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds (as is typically the case in GANs), the optimal discriminator is perfect (accuracy 1) and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the support of the generator distribution. This explains the vanishing gradient problem in the original GAN cost function (Theorem 2.4) and the instability of the alternative cost function (Theorem 2.6). The noise-based solution (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2) is theoretically sound and provides a principled approach to stabilizing GAN training.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent acceptance. First, the empirical validation is extremely limited—only Figures 1-3 are provided, which show minimal evidence of the theoretical claims. These figures don't demonstrate the proposed solution's effectiveness in generating high-quality samples or compare it to state-of-the-art GAN training methods. Second, the paper fails to address how the noise parameter should be chosen in practice, how it would affect sample quality, or whether it would require additional hyperparameter tuning that might negate the theoretical advantage. Third, the paper doesn't compare their noise-based approach with existing stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties, or label smoothing), which are known to be effective in practice. Finally, the paper claims to provide a "solid understanding" but doesn't translate this understanding into concrete, implementable improvements for practitioners.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper for future submission, I recommend:

1. Conduct extensive empirical experiments comparing the noise-based approach with multiple state-of-the-art GAN training techniques on standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10, LSUN, etc.). Include quantitative metrics (FID, IS) and qualitative examples.

2. Provide a clear practical implementation guide for the noise-based method, including how to choose the noise variance, whether it should be annealed, and how it interacts with other stabilization techniques.

3. Include ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of the noise and the theoretical claims about gradient behavior.

4. Discuss the computational overhead of the proposed method and whether it would be practical for large-scale applications.

5. Address the connection to existing GAN stabilization methods more explicitly, showing how their theoretical framework relates to and potentially improves upon these approaches.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims that adding noise makes the distributions continuous and enables stable training, but Figure 3 only shows gradient magnitudes without demonstrating actual sample quality improvements. Could you provide quantitative results (FID, IS) comparing your method to standard GAN training on a standard dataset?

2. How would the noise parameter σ be chosen in practice? Would it need to be tuned per dataset or model architecture, and how would this affect the practicality of your approach?

3. The paper states that "we no longer have to worry about training schedules," but does your method require a different training schedule than standard GANs? For example, would you need to anneal the noise parameter over time?

4. How does your noise-based approach compare to existing techniques like gradient penalty (Gulrajani et al., 2017) or spectral normalization (Miyato et al., 2018) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance?

5. The paper mentions that "the discriminator will disregard errors that lie exactly in g(Z)" but then proposes adding noise to the generator as well. How does this noise addition affect the theoretical guarantees presented in Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2?
