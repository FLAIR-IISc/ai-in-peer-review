

# Review of "Understanding the Instability of GAN Training"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental instability issues in GAN training. The authors provide a rigorous analysis showing that the instability arises because the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. This causes the optimal discriminator to become perfect (with zero gradient on the generator's support), which leads to vanishing gradients for the generator when using the original GAN cost function, and unstable updates when using the alternative cost function (−log D).

The paper makes several key contributions:
- A proof that neural networks with common nonlinearities map low-dimensional input spaces to low-dimensional manifolds (measure zero in the output space)
- Theoretical analysis showing that when distributions lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect and has zero gradient on the generator's support
- Explanation of why the original GAN cost function leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves
- Explanation of why the −log D alternative leads to unstable updates due to the Cauchy distribution of gradients
- A proposed solution by adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator (and generator) to create "softer" metrics that avoid the pathological behavior of the original GAN formulation

The paper provides a deep theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability, which has been a long-standing issue in the field. The authors correctly identify the gap in the literature: "Despite their success, there is little to no theory explaining the unstable behaviour of GAN training."

## 2. Decision: Reject

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper is primarily theoretical with no empirical results to support the theoretical claims. ICLR values both theoretical and empirical contributions, and the absence of empirical evidence significantly weakens the paper's impact.
2. **Limited practical contribution:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper doesn't provide a new algorithm or method that can be practically applied to improve GAN training. The proposed noise-based solution is mentioned but not implemented or evaluated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's theoretical contribution is impressive and well-executed. The proofs in the appendix are technically sound, and the theoretical framework provides a deep understanding of why GAN training is unstable. The insight about low-dimensional manifolds and the resulting pathological behavior of the KL divergence is important and well-justified.

However, as an ICLR submission, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent it from being accepted:

- **No empirical results:** ICLR is a top machine learning conference that values empirical validation. The paper claims to provide a solution to GAN instability but provides no evidence that this solution actually works in practice. The paper mentions "Figure 2" and "Figure 3" but doesn't include them, so I cannot assess whether the empirical evidence supports the theoretical claims.

- **Comparison to existing methods:** The paper doesn't compare its theoretical framework to other recent approaches to GAN stability (e.g., WGAN, WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization). Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the relative significance of the authors' theoretical insights.

- **Lack of practical implementation details:** The paper mentions "annealing the noise" but doesn't provide details on how this should be implemented in practice. For a paper to be useful to the community, it should provide concrete guidance on how to apply the theoretical insights.

- **Theoretical contribution without practical impact:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, it's hard to assess its practical significance without empirical validation. Many theoretical papers on GANs have been published, but those that are accepted at ICLR typically include both theoretical and empirical contributions.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To make this paper suitable for ICLR, the authors should address the following points:

1. **Add empirical validation:** The most critical improvement would be to include empirical results demonstrating how the noise-based approach improves GAN training. The authors should provide experiments showing:
   - How the noise-based approach stabilizes training compared to standard GANs
   - How the proposed method compares to existing stabilization techniques (WGAN, WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization, etc.)
   - Visual examples of generated samples showing improved quality and diversity

2. **Provide implementation details:** The authors should include a clear description of how to implement the noise-based approach, including:
   - How to choose the noise variance (σ²)
   - How to anneal the noise during training
   - Practical tips for integrating this approach into existing GAN frameworks

3. **Compare to related work:** The paper should more thoroughly compare its theoretical framework to recent advances in GAN stability. This would help position the paper within the current literature and demonstrate its unique contributions.

4. **Clarify the practical significance:** The authors should better articulate how their theoretical insights translate into practical improvements for GAN training. What specific problems do their insights solve that weren't addressed by previous work?

5. **Expand on the noise-based solution:** The paper mentions that "the discriminator will disregard errors that lie exactly in g(Z)" but doesn't clearly explain how adding noise to the generator inputs prevents this issue. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of how the noise-based approach specifically addresses the theoretical problems they identified.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. Can you provide empirical evidence demonstrating that adding noise to the discriminator inputs improves GAN training stability and sample quality compared to standard GAN approaches? Specifically, can you show quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, Inception Score) and qualitative examples?

2. How does the noise-based approach proposed in the paper compare to other recent methods for improving GAN stability (e.g., WGAN, WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization)? Have you conducted experiments to compare these methods?

3. The paper states that "the discriminator will disregard errors that lie exactly in g(Z), since this is a set of measure 0." How does adding noise to the generator inputs (as proposed in Corollary 3.2) actually prevent the discriminator from being susceptible to adversarial examples?

4. The authors mention that "we can train the discriminator till optimality without any problems" using the noise-based approach. How does this compare to the practical implementation of WGAN, where the discriminator is typically trained for multiple steps before each generator update?

5. What is the recommended schedule for annealing the noise variance during training? How does this schedule affect the final quality of generated samples?

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, but the lack of empirical validation prevents it from being suitable for ICLR. With the addition of empirical results and implementation details, this work could make a significant contribution to the field.
