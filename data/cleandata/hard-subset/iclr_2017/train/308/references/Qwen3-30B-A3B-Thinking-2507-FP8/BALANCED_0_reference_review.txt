

# Review of "Theoretical Foundations of GAN Training Instability"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a rigorous explanation for the instability in GAN training. It demonstrates that the core issue arises because both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, leading to perfect discriminators that provide vanishing or unstable gradients. The authors prove that under these conditions, the Jensen-Shannon divergence is maximized, making traditional divergence-based optimization infeasible. They then propose a solution involving adding noise to inputs to create continuous distributions, which enables stable training by providing smooth gradients. The theoretical framework presented is comprehensive and addresses fundamental questions about GAN training dynamics.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper makes important theoretical contributions, the lack of empirical validation, unclear novelty compared to prior work, and presentation issues prevent it from meeting ICLR's standards for acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical analysis. The authors successfully prove that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical in practice), the optimal discriminator is perfect and has zero gradient almost everywhere, explaining the vanishing gradients problem. Their analysis of the alternative loss function (-log D) showing it leads to unstable updates with infinite variance is also compelling. These theoretical insights are valuable for understanding GAN training dynamics.

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses:

First, the proposed solution (adding noise to inputs) is not novel. Previous work has explored similar approaches, such as the Gaussian noise injection in the work by Metz et al. (2016) on improved GAN training, and the noise-based approaches in the context of Wasserstein GANs. The paper fails to adequately distinguish its contribution from these prior works, making it difficult to assess the novelty of the proposed solution.

Second, and most critically, the paper lacks empirical validation. The authors claim their theoretical framework leads to more stable GAN training, but provide no experiments demonstrating this. Without empirical results showing that their noise-based approach actually improves training stability or sample quality compared to existing methods, the theoretical contribution remains incomplete. ICLR requires both theoretical and empirical validation for methodological contributions.

Third, the paper's presentation is extremely dense and difficult to follow. The mathematical notation is inconsistent, key concepts are not clearly explained, and the connection between theory and practice is poorly articulated. The authors make strong claims about the practical implications of their theory without providing sufficient evidence or examples to support them.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Clarify the novelty of their approach compared to existing noise-based methods in GAN literature. A thorough comparison with prior work would help establish the unique contribution of this paper.

2. Include empirical experiments demonstrating that their theoretical approach leads to more stable training and better sample quality. This could involve comparing their noise-based method with standard GAN training and other noise-injection approaches on standard benchmarks.

3. Improve the presentation by providing more intuitive explanations of key theoretical results. The paper would benefit from more visualizations and concrete examples to illustrate the manifold structure of data and generator distributions.

4. Clarify the practical implementation details of the noise annealing process they propose. How should the noise be gradually reduced during training? What are the optimal noise parameters?

5. Address the potential downside of adding noise to inputs: while it may stabilize training, it could also degrade sample quality. The authors should discuss this trade-off and provide evidence that their method maintains or improves sample quality.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your noise-based approach differ from the Gaussian noise injection methods already used in GAN training (e.g., Metz et al., 2016), and why is your approach theoretically superior?

2. Can you provide empirical evidence that your proposed method leads to more stable training and better sample quality compared to standard GAN training and other noise-based approaches?

3. The paper claims that "as we anneal the noise, this will make Pr and Pg match as well," but this is not demonstrated. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of how the noise annealing process would work in practice and how it affects the generator's ability to match the data distribution?

4. What is the relationship between your theoretical framework and the recently proposed Wasserstein GAN with gradient penalty (WGAN-GP) approach, which also addresses GAN training instability? How does your theory complement or differ from WGAN-GP?

This paper has the potential to make an important theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics, but it requires significant improvements in novelty articulation, empirical validation, and presentation to meet ICLR's standards. With these improvements, it could become a valuable contribution to the field.
