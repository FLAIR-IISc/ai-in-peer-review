

# Review

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contributions

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding the instability issues in GAN training. The authors identify that the core problem stems from the fact that both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. They prove that under these conditions, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (with accuracy 1) and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the supports of the distributions. This leads to vanishing gradients for the generator in the original GAN cost function and unstable updates with infinite variance in the alternative cost function used to avoid vanishing gradients. The authors then propose a principled solution by adding noise to the discriminator inputs, which smooths the distributions and creates stable gradient updates. They theoretically justify this approach by showing that the noisy distributions have continuous densities, allowing for meaningful gradient-based optimization. The paper also introduces the Wasserstein metric as a more suitable measure for comparing distributions with low-dimensional supports, providing theoretical guarantees for the relationship between the noiseless distributions and the noisy variants.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability, which has been a fundamental barrier to practical GAN application. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, well-motivated, and directly addresses a core problem in the field. The proposed solution is principled and well-supported by mathematical arguments.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's key contributions are both theoretically sound and practically relevant. The authors provide rigorous proofs for their main theorems, including:

- Theorem 2.1 and 2.2 demonstrating that when distributions have disjoint or low-dimensional supports, the optimal discriminator has accuracy 1 and zero gradient almost everywhere.
- Theorem 2.4 proving vanishing gradients for the generator in the original cost function as the discriminator approaches optimality.
- Theorem 2.5 and 2.6 explaining why the alternative cost function leads to unstable updates with infinite variance.
- Theorem 3.3 establishing the relationship between the Wasserstein distance of noiseless distributions and the JSD of noisy variants, which provides a theoretical basis for the proposed solution.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building on Goodfellow et al. (2014) and Theis et al. (2016) while addressing the critical gap in theoretical understanding of GAN instability. The authors clearly articulate why traditional divergence measures (KL and JSD) are inadequate for the distributions typically encountered in GAN training, which is a significant insight for the field. The theoretical results are supported by experimental evidence in Figures 2 and 3, which visually demonstrate the vanishing gradients and unstable updates as the discriminator improves.

## 4. Additional feedback for improvement

- The paper could benefit from more detailed experimental validation of the proposed noise-based approach. While Figures 2 and 3 support the theoretical claims about vanishing gradients and unstable updates, additional experiments comparing the proposed method with existing GAN training techniques would strengthen the practical contribution.
- The authors might want to provide more intuition about why the noise-based solution works, particularly in relation to the specific noise distributions they consider (e.g., Gaussian noise).
- The paper mentions "annealing the noise" in the conclusion but doesn't provide details on how this would be implemented in practice. A brief discussion of the practical implementation of this annealing schedule would be helpful.
- The paper could clarify the relationship between their noise-based approach and existing techniques like label smoothing or gradient penalty, which also aim to stabilize GAN training.

## 5. Questions for the authors

1. Can you provide more details on the practical implementation of the noise-based approach, particularly regarding the annealing schedule for the noise variance?
2. How do the theoretical guarantees of your approach compare to those of other stabilization techniques, such as gradient penalty or label smoothing?
3. Are there any limitations to your approach, particularly for very high-dimensional data or complex data distributions where the low-dimensional manifold assumption might not hold as strongly?
4. Can you provide more intuition about why the noise-based approach leads to stable gradients, beyond the mathematical derivation provided in the paper?
