

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of GAN training instability, explaining why gradients vanish as the discriminator improves and why GAN training remains unstable. The authors demonstrate that the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds (due to the generator's parameterization), leading to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients. They prove that both the original GAN cost function and the alternative cost function (-log D) suffer from fundamental instability issues, with the former exhibiting vanishing gradients and the latter producing updates with infinite variance. The paper proposes a theoretically grounded solution by adding noise to the discriminator inputs (and generator outputs), which smooths the distributions and enables meaningful gradient updates. The authors provide rigorous mathematical proofs for all claims, including theorems on perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients, and the properties of the noisy distributions.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN instability with rigorous proofs, well-motivated analysis, and clear implications for practical GAN training.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's key contributions are well-supported by thorough mathematical analysis. The authors correctly identify the core problem (low-dimensional manifold support of distributions) and provide theorems demonstrating how this leads to the observed instability. Lemma 1 rigorously proves that generator outputs lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which is foundational to the paper's argument. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 establish the existence of perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients, while Theorems 2.4-2.6 demonstrate the specific instability issues with both cost functions. The proposed solution (adding noise) is theoretically justified by Theorems 3.1-3.3, which show how noise creates continuous distributions with well-behaved gradients.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building on foundational GAN work (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and related theoretical work on divergences (Theis et al., 2016). The authors correctly situate their contribution as filling a critical theoretical gap in understanding GAN instability. The mathematical proofs are sound and appropriately referenced to relevant mathematical literature (Sard's Lemma, Morse Lemma, etc.).

The novelty lies in providing a unified theoretical framework explaining GAN instability, rather than just proposing a new algorithm. The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial and addresses a fundamental open question in GAN research.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- The paper could include a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation of the noise-based approach, particularly regarding the choice of noise distribution and variance. While the theoretical analysis is strong, a brief discussion of how this would be implemented in practice would be valuable.

- A small empirical validation of the theoretical claims (e.g., showing how gradient norms behave with increasing discriminator quality) would strengthen the paper's practical relevance, though the theoretical contribution is strong enough on its own.

- The connection to other GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) could be more explicitly discussed to position this work within the broader landscape of GAN training improvements.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that adding noise solves the instability problem theoretically, but how does this approach compare to existing stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) in terms of empirical performance on standard benchmarks like CIFAR-10 or ImageNet?

2. The theoretical guarantees depend on the noise variance. What is the recommended strategy for annealing the noise parameter during training, and how sensitive is the approach to this choice?

3. The paper shows that the gradient updates become proportional to ∇θJSD(Pr+ || Pg+), but what is the relationship between JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) and the quality of generated samples? Is there a theoretical connection between minimizing this divergence and sample quality?
