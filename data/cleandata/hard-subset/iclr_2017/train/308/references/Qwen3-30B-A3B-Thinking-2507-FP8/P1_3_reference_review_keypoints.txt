- Provides a rigorous theoretical explanation for GAN instability due to Pr and Pg lying on low-dimensional, disjoint manifolds that yield a perfect discriminator and vanishing generator gradients.  
- Proves the alternative GAN cost leads to unstable gradient updates with infinite variance as the discriminator improves.  
- Proposes adding continuous noise to inputs to form Pr+ and Pg+, making distributions continuous and enabling meaningful divergences and smooth gradients.  
- Shows a theoretical link between the Wasserstein distance of original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3), and that noise can be annealed to recover original distributions.  
- Recommends acceptance with minor revisions, citing a significant and well-motivated theoretical contribution.  
- Core strength is rigorous, elegant proofs (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 2.6) explaining practical GAN training difficulties.  
- The noise-based solution is theoretically sound and provides a principled route to stable GAN training.  
- Lacks substantial empirical validation; needs experiments comparing noisy-GANs to SOTA (WGAN-GP, BigGAN) using FID, IS, and qualitative samples.  
- Some technical proofs (e.g., Lemma 1) are dense and would benefit from more intuitive explanations or simple illustrative examples.  
- Should discuss computational costs and training-time trade-offs of adding noise, especially for large-scale applications.  
- Needs a clearer connection to recent work like WGAN-GP and discussion of how the approach complements or extends those methods.  
- Asks for guidance on optimal annealing schedules and sensitivity of performance to scheduling.  
- Requests evidence that the noise approach generalizes across architectures (e.g., StyleGAN, Progressive GAN).  
- Seeks explicit comparison (theoretical and empirical) between the noise-based method and WGAN/WGAN-GP.  
- Questions reasonableness of the assumption that Pr+ and Pg+ support lie within a ball of diameter C and how C affects the bound.  
- Concludes the paper is a valuable theoretical contribution that, with added empirical validation and minor clarifications, would be a strong ICLR submission.
