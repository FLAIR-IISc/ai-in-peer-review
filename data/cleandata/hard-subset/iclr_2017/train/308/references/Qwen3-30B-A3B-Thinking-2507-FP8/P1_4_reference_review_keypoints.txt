- Paper explains GAN instability by showing data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, causing problems in training.  
- Authors prove optimal discriminator becomes perfect (accuracy 1) and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the generator support.  
- This zero-gradient result explains why generator gradients vanish as the discriminator improves.  
- The paper theoretically analyzes why the original GAN cost (log(1-D)) vanishes gradients while the -logD cost yields unstable updates with infinite variance (Theorems 2.4 and 2.6).  
- Proposed principled solution: add continuous (Gaussian) noise to inputs to smooth distributions and restore meaningful gradients.  
- The authors prove noise-smoothed training yields well-behaved generator gradients proportional to the derivative of the Jensen–Shannon divergence (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2).  
- The paper connects the noise-smoothed approach to the Wasserstein metric, providing theoretical justification for WGAN-style methods (Theorem 3.3).  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance, citing the paper’s fundamental theoretical contributions and practical potential.  
- Suggestion: provide practical guidance on choosing and annealing the noise parameter σ (initial value, schedule, tuning).  
- Suggestion: include more empirical comparisons showing sample quality improvement versus standard GANs.  
- Suggestion: more explicitly relate the noise-smoothed approach to WGAN and WGAN-GP and whether it approximates Wasserstein distance practically.  
- Suggestion: discuss potential limitations, e.g., effects of added noise on sample quality and implications for mode collapse.  
- Question: provide architecture/dataset details for Figure 3 and consistency of the observed gradient-norm behavior across settings.  
- Question: recommend an annealing schedule for σ and describe its effect on final sample quality.  
- Question: compare noise-smoothing to other stabilization methods (Spectral Norm, gradient penalty, adaptive LR) and discuss compatibility.  
- Question: clarify practical implementation of backpropagating generator gradients through noisy samples, especially for architectures not using noise in generation.
