This paper tackles the fundamental problem of instability in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). It argues that this instability stems from the theoretical properties of the cost functions used in GAN training, particularly when the generator and discriminator operate on distributions with low-dimensional supports – a common scenario in practice. The authors provide theoretical justification for why standard training methods often fail, demonstrating vanishing gradients or unstable updates. Importantly, they propose a novel approach based on adding noise and utilizing a Wasserstein distance-inspired metric to mitigate these issues, offering a principled direction for future research.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a significant and long-standing problem with GAN training and presents a detailed theoretical analysis, the proposed solution feels incomplete and lacks sufficient empirical validation. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the connection to a practical, demonstrably superior training methodology is not fully established. The level of mathematical detail is high, making accessibility challenging, and the jump from the theoretical analysis to the proposed noisy metric isn’t fully convincing without more concrete experimental results showing improvement over existing techniques.

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics. The authors successfully demonstrate why the original GAN cost function, and even its modifications, suffer from inherent instability when distributions have low-dimensional supports. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 clearly articulate the existence of perfect discriminators and the resulting gradient issues. The detailed breakdown of the problems with the original and -logD cost functions (Theorems 2.4 and 2.5) is insightful. The introduction of the softer metric and its theoretical properties (Theorems 3.2 and 3.3) are promising, building on concepts like Wasserstein distance to address the shortcomings of traditional approaches.

However, the paper’s key weakness is the lack of substantial empirical evaluation. While the authors mention expected improvements (e.g., more stable gradients), they don’t present convincing experimental evidence demonstrating that their proposed method outperforms established GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, spectral normalization). The figures (1, 2, 3) primarily serve to illustrate the theoretical points but don't showcase the practical benefits of the proposed noisy metric. The authors acknowledge the complexity of implementation but this doesn’t excuse the lack of comparative results. Furthermore, the explanation regarding how to choose and tune the noise distribution is somewhat vague. The discussion on the Wasserstein distance and its connection to the proposed method is strong theoretically, but it’s unclear how this translates into a practical training recipe.  The mathematical notation is dense, which may hinder broader accessibility and understanding. The paper also relies heavily on established results and references, which is positive, but could benefit from a more focused discussion on how this work extends or significantly deviates from existing literature beyond merely identifying the underlying problems.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Empirical Validation:** The most critical improvement would be to include comprehensive experimental results comparing the proposed method with state-of-the-art GAN training techniques on standard benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA). Include metrics like Inception Score and FID to quantify the quality of generated images.
*   **Noise Distribution Tuning:** Provide more guidance on how to choose and tune the parameters of the noise distribution (e.g., variance). A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of different noise levels on performance would be valuable.
*   **Implementation Details:** While a full implementation might be extensive, providing pseudocode or more detailed algorithmic descriptions would enhance reproducibility and understanding.
*   **Accessibility:** Consider simplifying some of the mathematical notation and providing more intuitive explanations of key concepts. Adding a visual summary of the theoretical findings could also improve comprehension.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** Strengthen the discussion of related work by explicitly comparing and contrasting the proposed approach with existing methods in more detail, highlighting the unique contributions of this paper.
*   **Clarify connection to Wasserstein GAN:** The paper references the Wasserstein distance, but doesn’t clearly articulate how this approach differs from, or improves upon, existing Wasserstein GAN implementations (e.g., WGAN-GP).

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  What specific challenges did you encounter when attempting to implement and evaluate the proposed method empirically?
2.  How does the choice of the noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian, uniform) impact the performance of the method, and what are the guidelines for selecting an appropriate distribution?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the proposed method relates to and differs from existing Wasserstein GAN techniques?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the level of noise added, and what strategies can be used to adaptively adjust the noise level during training?
5.  The paper mentions that the discriminator is trained until optimality. How is “optimality” defined and measured in practice, and how does this affect the training process?
