This paper attempts to provide a theoretical understanding of the instability issues plaguing Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and proposes a direction to address them. It argues that the core problem stems from the perfect discrimination achievable when the generator and real data distributions have low-dimensional supports, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates during training. The authors introduce a noise-based approach to soften the distributions and enable more stable learning via a modified cost function and analysis of the Wasserstein distance.

**Reject.** While the identified problem – perfect discrimination – is a valid concern, the paper’s analysis feels largely confirmatory of already known issues and the proposed solution, while theoretically grounded, lacks sufficient empirical validation to warrant acceptance. The extensive theoretical development isn’t balanced by compelling experimental results demonstrating a significant advantage over established GAN training techniques. 

The core argument, that GANs struggle when distributions have low-dimensional supports, is well-known and has motivated many prior works on manifold learning and regularization. The paper spends considerable effort rigorously demonstrating this, but doesn't offer substantially *new* insight beyond this established understanding. The proposed solution of adding noise is a reasonable heuristic, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate its practical superiority. The theorems presented, while mathematically sound, largely describe *why* standard GAN training fails rather than offering a transformative solution. The reliance on theoretical arguments without robust empirical backing is a significant weakness. The derivations, while meticulous, delve into complex mathematical territory without a clear demonstration of the practical benefit, and the connection between the theory and observed GAN behavior remains somewhat tenuous. 

Specifically, the claimed instability issues relating to the original and alternative cost functions, while analytically presented, are not shown to be significantly mitigated by the proposed noise injection in a comparative empirical setting. The presented figures (Figure 1 & 2) are not compelling – Figure 1 simply visualizes a known phenomenon, and Figure 2 lacks sufficient detail regarding the setup and baselines. The paper alludes to experimental verification of instability, but the results are not presented in a rigorous, quantifiable way. The leap from theoretical analysis to the claim of a "new direction" feels unsupported. The final sections concerning the Wasserstein metric and its connection to the noisy distributions show promise, but again, lack accompanying empirical results validating this connection.

Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Stronger Empirical Evaluation:** Include a comprehensive empirical evaluation comparing the proposed method to established GAN training techniques (e.g., DCGAN, WGAN-GP, spectral normalization) on several benchmark datasets. Quantify improvements in sample quality (using metrics like FID, IS) and training stability (using metrics like gradient norm, learning curves).
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to isolate the impact of the noise injection and assess the sensitivity to noise level.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Clearly establish the performance of existing GANs under similar conditions as the proposed method.
*   **Clarify Practical Implementation Details:** Provide more details on how the noise is added and the resulting computational cost.
*   **Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup:** including hyperparameter settings, network architectures and training details.
*   **Further justification for the choice of noise distribution:** Explain why a Gaussian distribution was chosen and how it impacts the theoretical results.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** More explicitly discuss how this work builds upon or differs from prior research on GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., gradient penalties, spectral normalization).

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  What specific hyperparameter tuning was performed to find the optimal noise level, and how sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the proposed noise injection avoids the instabilities observed in the original GAN cost functions, beyond the theoretical analysis?
3.  The paper mentions that the noise injection impacts computational cost. Can you quantify this overhead and discuss its scalability to larger datasets and models?
4.  Have you considered alternative noise distributions besides Gaussian noise, and if so, what were the results?
5.  Beyond the qualitative observations in Figure 3, can you provide quantitative data demonstrating the improved gradient behavior during training with the proposed method?
