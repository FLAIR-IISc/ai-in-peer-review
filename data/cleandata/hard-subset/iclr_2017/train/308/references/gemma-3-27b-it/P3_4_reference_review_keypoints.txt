- Paper analyzes GAN instability, arguing perfect discrimination occurs when generator and data lie on low-dimensional supports.  
- Proposes adding noise to smooth distributions, linking this smoothing to Wasserstein distance and more stable training.  
- Decision: Reject — strong theory but insufficient empirical validation and unclear practical benefit.  
- Strength: Rigorous, well-structured mathematical analysis explaining vanishing gradients and instability for common GAN losses.  
- Strength: Convincing proofs about perfect discrimination with low-dimensional supports.  
- Claim: Noise smoothing offers a principled pathway to stability, but this connection is underexploited.  
- Main weakness: Lack of compelling experiments on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) and missing FID/Inception comparisons.  
- Experiments provided are illustrative (gradient behavior) and do not show significant improvements over established methods.  
- Presentation is overly dense with mathematical notation, potentially obscuring core insights for non-expert readers.  
- Novelty concern: Noise addition is not clearly superior to existing stabilization techniques like spectral normalization or gradient penalties.  
- Suggestion: Run comprehensive benchmark comparisons against WGAN-GP, SN-GAN, etc., and report standard metrics (FID, IS).  
- Suggestion: Perform ablation studies on noise type and level to quantify contributions of each component.  
- Suggestion: Provide practical guidelines for choosing noise parameters and discuss computational costs at scale.  
- Suggestion: Simplify presentation and include intuitive explanations to make theoretical results more accessible.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly compare and contrast the proposed method with prior stabilization techniques and discuss limitations.  
- Questions: What is the computational cost of adding noise for high-resolution generation?  
- Questions: Have alternative noise distributions (e.g., Laplacian) been tried and how do they affect results?  
- Questions: Is there empirical evidence that the method reduces mode collapse and how does it compare to methods like Minibatch Discrimination or Unrolled GANs?  
- Question: Could the Wasserstein distance be used directly as a training objective to obviate the discriminator?
