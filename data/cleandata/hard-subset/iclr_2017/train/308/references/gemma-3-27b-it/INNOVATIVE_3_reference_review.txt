This paper tackles the notoriously unstable training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), aiming to provide a theoretical understanding of the issues and a principled direction for improvement. It argues that the instability stems from the interaction between the discriminator becoming *too* good (approaching perfect discrimination due to the low-dimensional nature of generated data manifolds) and the cost functions employed, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates. The authors propose a novel analysis based on softening the distributions with added noise and explore the use of the Wasserstein metric as a more stable alternative for evaluating and training GANs.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a crucial and long-standing problem in the GAN literature and presents a compelling theoretical analysis, it feels incomplete and relies heavily on intuition without sufficient empirical validation. The proposed solution, while promising, isn’t convincingly demonstrated to outperform existing methods. The theoretical contributions, while interesting, feel somewhat disconnected from practical implications without more extensive experimentation.

The core strength lies in the insightful analysis of the interplay between discriminator accuracy and generator updates. The theorems demonstrating perfect discrimination and the associated gradient issues are well-argued and provide a strong theoretical foundation for understanding the instability. The identification of the problematic behavior of the alternative cost function (the one replacing the original GAN cost) is a significant contribution.  The introduction of adding noise to soften the distributions and leveraging the Wasserstein metric is a logical and potentially impactful direction. However, the paper lacks a solid empirical evaluation of this approach.  The presented figures (1, 2, and 3) are insightful, but don’t provide a quantitative comparison against established GAN training techniques. The claims about instability are largely supported by theoretical argument, but a corresponding experimental demonstration showing *how* the proposed method mitigates this instability with improved sample quality and training stability is missing.  The jump to Wasserstein distance feels somewhat abrupt; while theoretically sound, a more gradual demonstration of its benefits through intermediate steps or comparisons to other regularisation techniques would strengthen the argument.

Additional Feedback:

*   **Empirical Validation is Crucial:** The most significant weakness is the lack of robust empirical results. The authors should include experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of their proposed method (adding noise, leveraging Wasserstein distance) on standard GAN benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA). Comparisons to state-of-the-art GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization) are essential.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper discusses theoretical properties, but doesn’t fully address the practical considerations of implementing the proposed approach.  How does the amount of noise added affect performance? What are sensible choices for the noise distribution? How does this impact computational cost?
*   **Clarify the Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper references relevant work, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the proposed approach relates to existing techniques for stabilizing GAN training.  For example, how does this relate to techniques like feature matching or minibatch discrimination?
*   **Formalize the "Softening" Process:** The noise addition is presented as a way to avoid perfect discrimination. However, the authors should more formally analyze the impact of different noise distributions and levels on the properties of the generated distribution and the discriminator's performance.
*  **Expand on the Wasserstein Distance:** While the theoretical justification for using the Wasserstein distance is solid, a more detailed explanation of how to efficiently compute or approximate it in practice would be valuable.



Questions for the authors:

1.  Can you provide quantitative results demonstrating the performance of your proposed method (noise addition and Wasserstein distance) on standard GAN benchmarks compared to existing state-of-the-art techniques?
2.  What are the practical guidelines for choosing the appropriate amount and distribution of noise to add to the discriminator's input?
3.  How does the computational cost of training a GAN with the proposed method compare to that of training a GAN with existing techniques like WGAN-GP or Spectral Normalization?
4.  The paper suggests that adding noise can prevent perfect discrimination. However, wouldn’t a sufficiently powerful discriminator still be able to learn to discriminate even with noise? If so, how do you prevent this in practice?
5.   What are the limitations of your approach? Are there specific types of datasets or GAN architectures where it might not be effective?
