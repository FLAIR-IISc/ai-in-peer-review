## Review of "Understanding and Improving GAN Training"

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper tackles the notoriously difficult problem of GAN training instability. It argues that the core issue stems from the inherent properties of the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) optimized by standard GANs, coupled with the tendency for generator and discriminator distributions to have low-dimensional supports (specifically, lying on manifolds).  The authors theoretically demonstrate that perfect discrimination is possible under these conditions, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates. They then propose a novel approach – introducing noise to the distributions – to soften the divergences and enable more stable training. They provide theoretical justification for why this approach works and suggest it opens new avenues for analyzing and improving GANs.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the paper raises important theoretical points about GAN training, its presentation is overly dense and the connection between the theoretical results and a practical, implementable solution feels tenuous.  The theoretical analysis, while interesting, feels disconnected from current empirical observations, and the proposed "solution" (adding noise) is a well-known technique, presented without substantial novelty in its application or demonstrable advantage over existing methods.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Overly Dense and Difficult to Follow:** The paper is extremely theoretical and reads like a highly technical manuscript.  The constant barrage of theorems, lemmas, and proofs makes it difficult to grasp the core ideas without significant effort.  The introduction, while aiming to set the stage, is itself quite dense. A more accessible presentation of the core insights would significantly improve its impact.
* **Theoretical Focus Without Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper spends the vast majority of its length on theoretical analysis. While this analysis is presented rigorously, there's a concerning lack of empirical validation beyond a single figure (Figure 1) demonstrating the discriminator error approaching zero.  The impact of the proposed noise injection on actual GAN performance (image quality, training stability, mode collapse) isn’t demonstrated. Saying that adding noise will "protect the discriminator against measure 0 adversarial examples" isn't enough; *showing* it is crucial.
* **Incremental Contribution of the Noise Solution:** The suggestion to add noise to the distributions to smooth the divergences isn’t fundamentally new. Techniques like adding label smoothing or using different loss functions (e.g., Wasserstein GAN) already implicitly address the issue of sharp divergences. The authors don't clearly articulate *how* their approach is distinct or superior to these existing methods.  The theoretical justification is valuable, but the practical benefit needs to be demonstrably shown.
* **Unclear Link Between Theory and Practice:** The paper establishes conditions under which the standard GAN objective function breaks down. However, the link between these theoretical conditions and *real-world* GAN training is not sufficiently explored. Do common architectures actually satisfy these conditions? Is the magnitude of instability directly correlated with the dimensionality of the manifolds formed by the data and generator distributions?

**4. Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

* **Improve Accessibility:**  Break down the theoretical analysis into smaller, more digestible chunks.  Use more intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs. Consider adding visual aids to illustrate key concepts.
* **Conduct Thorough Experiments:**  Implement the proposed noise injection technique and compare its performance against state-of-the-art GAN training methods (e.g., WGAN-GP, SN-GAN).  Report quantitative results (FID scores, IS scores) and qualitative results (sample images) to demonstrate its effectiveness.
* **Clarify Novelty:**  Clearly articulate how the proposed approach differs from and improves upon existing techniques for stabilizing GAN training.
* **Address Practical Considerations:**  Discuss the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameters (e.g., the variance of the noise).  Provide guidelines for choosing appropriate noise distributions.
* **Connect to Existing Literature:** A more thorough discussion of related work would strengthen the paper.  For instance, how does the analysis relate to the non-convex optimization landscape of GAN training?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  What specific architectures (e.g., DCGAN, StyleGAN) were considered when developing these theoretical results? Do these architectures inherently satisfy the conditions of low-dimensional supports as described in the paper?
*  Can you provide a more concrete explanation of *how* the proposed noise injection differs from existing techniques like label smoothing or the use of Wasserstein distance?
*  What is the computational cost of adding noise, and how does it impact training time?
*  How does the proposed approach perform on datasets with varying dimensionality and complexity?
*  Is the choice of noise distribution (Gaussian in the paper) critical?  Have you explored other distributions and their impact on performance?



In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical investigation into the challenges of GAN training, it falls short of providing a novel and demonstrably effective solution. The lack of empirical validation and the overly dense presentation hinder its impact, leading to a rejection. Substantial revisions addressing the points above would be necessary for reconsideration.
