This paper tackles the fundamental problem of instability in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). It argues that the instability stems from the properties of the cost functions used in GAN training, particularly the Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD), and the tendency for the generator and discriminator to operate in disjoint support spaces due to the dimensionality of the latent space. The authors aim to provide a theoretical understanding of these issues and propose a direction to address them, focusing on alternative metrics and a novel training approach based on noise injection.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the problem addressed is highly relevant and the initial observations regarding the potential for perfect discrimination and its impact on gradient flow are insightful, the paper suffers from a significant lack of empirical validation and the proposed solution, while theoretically motivated, lacks sufficient detail and justification. The numerous theorems and lemmas feel disconnected from a clear, practical advancement in GAN training.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical analysis. The authors correctly identify the issue of potential perfect discrimination when the generator and discriminator operate on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds. The derivation demonstrating how the original and alternative GAN cost functions lead to either vanishing gradients or unstable updates is a valuable contribution to understanding the underlying dynamics of GAN training. The discussion regarding the shortcomings of KL divergence and JSD, and the motivation for exploring alternative metrics, is well-articulated. The mathematical formalism presented is rigorous, with proofs provided for the key theorems.

However, the paper’s primary weakness is the complete absence of experimental results. The authors claim their theoretical analysis leads to a new direction, involving noise injection and alternative metrics (specifically the Wasserstein metric), but provide no evidence demonstrating its effectiveness. The discussion of the Wasserstein metric feels largely theoretical, lacking any implementation details or comparative results against existing GAN training techniques. The claims about instability observed in practice are not supported by any empirical data presented within the paper itself. While figures are referenced (Figures 1, 2, and 3), they are not included, rendering them inaccessible for review. Even if the figures were present, they wouldn't necessarily constitute a complete empirical validation of the proposed approach. The paper heavily relies on assertions without backing them up with tangible evidence. Furthermore, the sheer number of theorems and lemmas makes the paper dense and difficult to follow, obscuring the core arguments. The discussion on the theoretical properties of different divergences feels somewhat tangential to the primary goal of improving GAN training.

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1.  **Provide comprehensive experimental results:** This is crucial. The proposed noise injection and Wasserstein-based approach needs to be implemented and compared against standard GAN training techniques on benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, MNIST). Metrics like Inception Score and FID should be used to evaluate the quality of generated samples.
2.  **Include the referenced figures:** The figures (1, 2, and 3) are essential for understanding the presented analysis and should be included in the submission.
3.  **Streamline the theoretical arguments:** While rigor is important, the paper could benefit from a more concise presentation of the theoretical results. Focus on the most relevant theorems and lemmas and clearly articulate their connection to the proposed solution.
4.  **Provide more details on the implementation:** If the proposed approach is implemented, describe the specific details of the implementation, including the noise distribution, the choice of hyperparameters, and the optimization algorithm used.
5.  **Clarify the connection between theory and practice:** Explain more clearly how the theoretical analysis translates into practical guidelines for training GANs.

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  What specific noise distribution was used when exploring the Wasserstein metric approach, and why was that distribution chosen?
2.  What are the computational costs associated with the proposed noise injection technique compared to standard GAN training?
3.  How sensitive is the proposed approach to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the noise variance?
4.  The paper mentions a potential connection between the vanishing gradient problem and the saturation issue discussed in the original GAN paper. Can you elaborate on this connection?
5.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 3.3, focusing on how the Wasserstein distance relates to the quality of generated samples?
