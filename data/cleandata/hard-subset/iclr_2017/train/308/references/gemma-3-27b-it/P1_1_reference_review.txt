## ICLR 2024 Review: “Understanding and Stabilizing Generative Adversarial Networks”

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles the long-standing problem of instability in Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating that the inherent instability stems from the tendency of the discriminator to perfectly separate the real and generated distributions when they lie on low-dimensional manifolds – a common scenario in practice. They further demonstrate that standard GAN training objectives either lead to vanishing gradients or highly unstable updates under these conditions.  The core contribution is a novel approach using a softened metric via adding noise to the distributions, which allows for stable training and provides a theoretical framework for understanding the relationship between divergence measures, Wasserstein distance, and gradient behavior.  The paper proposes a new training paradigm that aims to bridge the gap between theoretical analysis and practical GAN training.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** This paper presents a compelling theoretical analysis of GAN instability and proposes a principled approach to address it. The arguments are well-constructed, the mathematical derivations appear sound, and the proposed solution – adding noise and using a softened metric – is intuitively appealing and theoretically justified. The paper tackles a fundamental problem in the field and provides a valuable contribution to our understanding of GANs.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds a rigorous theoretical framework demonstrating why standard GAN training fails when the generator and discriminator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The proofs of Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 2.5 and 2.6 are thorough and insightful, convincingly illustrating the issues of perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients, and unstable updates.
* **Novel Insights:** The analysis of the original and alternative GAN cost functions (specifically the -logD alternative) is particularly strong. Demonstrating that the latter optimizes a problematic divergence with inherent mode-dropping tendencies is a key insight.
* **Principled Solution:** Introducing noise and leveraging the Wasserstein distance as a proxy for divergence offers a more stable and well-behaved training objective. The connection between the Wasserstein distance and the divergence of the noisy distributions is clearly established.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:**  The paper addresses multiple facets of the problem, from the mathematical reasons behind instability to the practical implications for gradient updates.  The discussion of adversarial examples and the proposed mitigation strategy through noisy gradients is particularly relevant.
* **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally well-written, with clear explanations and a logical flow. The inclusion of proofs in the appendix is appreciated.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical arguments are strong, the paper lacks substantial empirical validation.  The figures included (Figure 1 & 2) are demonstrative, but more extensive experiments comparing the proposed method with state-of-the-art GAN training techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) on benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) are crucial. Providing quantitative results (FID, Inception Score) would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Noise Schedule:** The paper doesn’t detail how the noise variance should be scheduled during training. Investigating different noise schedules and their impact on performance would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the proposed approach (e.g., the cost of calculating the Wasserstein distance approximation).
* **Clarify Connection to Existing Work:**  While the paper cites relevant work, a more explicit discussion of how the proposed approach relates to other stabilization techniques (e.g., those based on regularization or architectural changes) would be beneficial.
* **Appendix Organization:** The appendix is quite dense. Breaking up the proofs into smaller, more digestible chunks with introductory remarks would improve readability.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding Empirical Validation:** Do you have preliminary empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of your proposed method compared to existing GAN training techniques? If so, could you share them?
* **Regarding Noise Schedule:**  Have you explored different noise schedules during training? If so, what did you find to be the most effective?  What is the sensitivity of the method to the choice of noise level?
* **Regarding Computational Cost:** What is the approximate computational overhead introduced by the proposed approach compared to standard GAN training?
* **Regarding Wasserstein Approximation:** You mention the Wasserstein distance is approximated in practice.  How sensitive is the performance to the accuracy of this approximation? Are there methods to efficiently and accurately approximate the Wasserstein distance in high-dimensional spaces?
* **Regarding Theoretical Guarantees:** Could you elaborate on the assumptions underlying Theorem 3.3 (e.g., the bound on the diameter of the support)? How robust are the results to violations of these assumptions?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GAN training. Addressing the suggested improvements, particularly the addition of more comprehensive empirical validation, would further enhance the impact and appeal of the work.
