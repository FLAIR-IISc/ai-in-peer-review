## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper Title:** Understanding and Stabilizing Generative Adversarial Networks

**1. Summary:**

This paper delves into the theoretical underpinnings of the training instability observed in Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors argue that the standard divergence functions used (KL, JSD) are ill-suited for GAN training due to the low-dimensional nature of the generator's output manifold and the potential for disjoint support between the real and generated distributions. They demonstrate that, under these conditions, the optimal discriminator becomes "perfect" (accuracy 1), leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates depending on the cost function used. The paper then proposes a novel approach based on smoothing the distributions via adding noise, and proposes utilizing the Wasserstein metric as a measure of distance between the real and generated data distributions, offering a path towards more stable and theoretically grounded GAN training.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper raises important theoretical points and provides insightful analysis, the presented theory feels incomplete and lacks sufficient empirical validation to justify the proposed solution convincingly. The sheer depth of mathematical detail coupled with a lack of compelling experimental results leaves the reader feeling that the theory is built in a vacuum.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Theoretical Motivation, But…:** The paper effectively articulates the theoretical issues surrounding GAN training and builds a compelling argument for why standard divergence measures might fail. The proofs regarding perfect discrimination and gradient behavior are mathematically sound and contribute to a better understanding of the problem. However, these results rely heavily on assumptions about the generator's output lying on low-dimensional manifolds and the distributions having disjoint support or nearly disjoint support. While plausible, these assumptions need stronger justification, especially given the diversity of GAN architectures and datasets.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness lies in the lack of substantial empirical evidence. The paper mentions Figure 1 and 2, and Figure 3 but they aren't thoroughly discussed. A crucial part of demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed smoothing technique and Wasserstein-based approach would be to show improved training stability and sample quality on standard GAN benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) compared to existing methods. Showing results using the theoretical framework presented would greatly strengthen the paper’s contribution. The authors need to demonstrate that their approach genuinely addresses the identified issues and leads to tangible improvements.
* **Complexity and Accessibility:** The paper is extremely dense with mathematical notation and proofs. While rigor is appreciated, the presentation could be made more accessible to a broader audience. Clearer explanations and intuition behind the theorems and lemmas would significantly enhance the paper's impact. More visual aids illustrating the concepts could also be helpful.
* **Incomplete Solution:** The proposed smoothing technique, while theoretically appealing, lacks detail regarding the implementation and tuning of the noise distribution. The connection between the noise level, the Wasserstein distance, and the resulting sample quality requires further investigation.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify Assumptions:** Explicitly discuss the limitations of the assumptions made regarding the low-dimensional manifold and disjoint support. Address how the proposed approach performs when these assumptions are not met.
* **Robustness of Noise:** A more thorough analysis of the effect of the added noise is needed. How does the choice of the noise distribution (Gaussian, uniform, etc.) affect performance? What are the optimal noise parameters?
* **Comparison to Existing Techniques:** Position the proposed approach relative to existing GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty). How does it compare in terms of performance and computational cost?
* **Detailed Experimental Setup:** Provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including the network architectures, hyperparameters, and training procedures used.
* **Visualization of Manifold:** Consider providing a visualization (even a simplified one) illustrating the low-dimensional manifold concept and how it relates to the generator’s output.
* **Explore Connection to Mode Collapse:** How does the theoretical framework and proposed solution address the issue of mode collapse?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more intuitive explanation for why adding noise helps alleviate the issues associated with "perfect" discrimination?
* What specific experiments have you conducted to validate the proposed smoothing technique, and what were the results?  Please provide detailed quantitative results.
* How sensitive is the performance of the proposed approach to the choice of the noise distribution and its parameters?
* How does the computational cost of using the Wasserstein metric compare to other GAN training methods?
* Considering the assumption of low-dimensional manifolds, how can this framework be extended to address scenarios where the generated distribution occupies a higher-dimensional space?
* Could you elaborate on the practical challenges of estimating the Wasserstein distance and how these were addressed in your implementation?
