This paper investigates the theoretical underpinnings of Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training instability. It identifies a fundamental issue arising from the discrepancy between the supports of the real and generated data distributions, leading to perfect discrimination and vanishing/unstable gradients. The authors propose a novel approach involving adding noise to both distributions to mitigate these issues, and provide a theoretical framework for analyzing this approach using Wasserstein distance and Jensen-Shannon divergence.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents a significant theoretical investigation into GAN training, but the presented results, while interesting, do not offer a practically implementable solution or a demonstrably superior method compared to existing techniques. The proposed noise addition, while theoretically sound, feels like a re-introduction of concepts already explored within variational approaches and doesn’t offer a clear advantage in a practical setting. Furthermore, the extensive theoretical derivations lack sufficient connection to empirical validation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** Despite the comprehensive theoretical analysis, the paper lacks compelling empirical evidence to support its claims. Figures 1, 2, and 3 are presented, but the experimental setup and the significance of the observations are not sufficiently detailed. Figure 1 simply shows discriminator error going to zero; this is known. Figure 2 demonstrates vanishing gradients, a known issue, without a comparison to the proposed solution. Figure 3 shows gradient norm increasing, and again lacks a comparison to the proposed approach. Without quantitative results demonstrating the benefits of the proposed method (noise addition and Wasserstein-based analysis) on standard GAN benchmarks, the theoretical contributions remain largely speculative.
2. **Re-introduction of Existing Concepts:** The core idea of adding noise to both distributions to achieve a smoother optimization landscape is reminiscent of techniques used in variational autoencoders (VAEs) and other probabilistic generative models. The paper doesn't adequately explain how its approach differs from or improves upon these existing methods. The connection to Wasserstein distance is noted, but its practical application beyond theoretical justification remains unclear. The proposed approach doesn't introduce substantial novelty in the core methodology; instead, it provides a theoretical lens through which to view existing techniques.
3. **Theoretical Complexity vs. Practical Impact:** The paper is heavily laden with theoretical derivations and proofs. While mathematically rigorous, the complexity of the analysis might hinder its accessibility and immediate applicability for practitioners. The leap from the theoretical framework to actionable insights for GAN training is not clearly established. A more focused presentation and clearer connections between theory and practice would have strengthened the paper.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the Noise Addition Procedure:** The paper introduces noise addition, but lacks details on how to choose the noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian, uniform) and its variance. Providing guidelines for selecting these parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Connect to Existing Literature:** Explicitly discuss how the proposed approach relates to existing techniques like WGAN, WGAN-GP, and other GAN stabilization methods. Highlight the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach compared to these alternatives.
*   **Provide a Concrete Algorithm:** Develop a concrete algorithm based on the proposed theory that can be easily implemented and tested.
*   **Detailed Experimental Setup:** If empirical results are included, provide a detailed description of the experimental setup, including the datasets used, network architectures, hyperparameter settings, and evaluation metrics. Include quantitative results (e.g., Inception Score, FID) to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Address the Computational Cost:** Adding noise and calculating Wasserstein distance can be computationally expensive. Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed approach and potential strategies for mitigating this cost.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How does the choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian, uniform) impact the performance of the proposed method?
*   What are the practical guidelines for selecting the variance of the noise?
*   How does the proposed approach compare to existing GAN stabilization techniques like WGAN-GP in terms of performance and computational cost?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the Wasserstein distance calculation is performed in practice, especially considering the infinite-dimensional nature of the distributions?
*   Could you elaborate on how the proposed method might be applicable to other generative models beyond GANs?
