This paper attempts to provide a theoretical understanding of the instability issues plaguing Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), arguing that these stem from the Jensen-Shannon divergence being maximized when the discriminator becomes too effective, and that the supports of the generator and data distributions often lie on low-dimensional manifolds. It proposes analyzing the problem through the lens of perfect discrimination and introduces a novel approach using noise to stabilize training and define a more meaningful metric for evaluating generative models – the Wasserstein distance.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies genuinely important issues in GAN training, its theoretical analysis feels overly focused on idealized scenarios and, crucially, lacks convincing empirical validation. The proposed solution, while theoretically sound, is not demonstrated to outperform established techniques, and the lack of comparison to baselines weakens the contribution significantly. The paper presents a lot of theory without sufficient evidence that it translates into practical improvements.

The core argument regarding perfect discrimination and the resulting vanishing/unstable gradients is well-articulated, and the mathematical derivations appear correct. The identification of low-dimensional manifolds as a contributing factor is also reasonable and connects to existing literature. However, the paper dwells heavily on scenarios where the discriminator *can* perfectly separate the distributions, which is a highly idealized setting. Real-world GANs operate with imperfect discriminators and complex data distributions, where perfect separation is unlikely. The analysis of the original and alternative cost functions is interesting but ultimately reinforces known problems without offering immediately actionable solutions beyond the proposed noise injection. 

The introduction of noise and the shift to the Wasserstein distance are promising directions. However, the paper does not demonstrate that this approach consistently improves sample quality or training stability compared to existing GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN, improved training procedures for standard GANs, spectral normalization).  The claim of stabilizing the gradient through noise injection needs to be backed up with experimental results showcasing improved convergence and reduced mode collapse. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein distance is interesting but ultimately relies on the assumption of smoothness provided by the noise, and the benefit isn’t clearly quantified or demonstrated. The paper establishes a theoretical link but does not prove that it's a *better* link than existing approaches.

Here are some points for improvement:

*   **Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the lack of extensive experimental results. The paper *must* include comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., WGAN-GP, SN-GAN) on standard datasets (CIFAR-10, CelebA). Quantitative metrics (FID, IS) and qualitative assessments are crucial.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Investigate the impact of different noise levels and noise distributions on training dynamics and sample quality. How sensitive is the approach to the noise parameters?
*   **Practical Considerations:** Discuss the computational cost of the proposed method. Adding noise and calculating Wasserstein distances may be more expensive than standard GAN training.
*   **Clarify the Connection to Existing Work:** More clearly position the proposed approach relative to existing techniques that also aim to stabilize GAN training, such as gradient penalty methods. Explain why this approach is superior or complementary.
*   **Address the Assumption of Smoothness:** While the introduction of noise is intended to create smoother gradients, it’s important to acknowledge the trade-off between smoothness and the fidelity of the generated samples.



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Beyond the theoretical arguments presented, what specific practical advantages does your approach offer over well-established GAN training techniques like WGAN-GP or SN-GAN? Can you provide empirical evidence to support this claim?
2.  How sensitive is your proposed method to the choice of noise distribution and its parameters (e.g., variance)? Do you observe a significant difference in performance with different noise types?
3.  The paper mentions the potential for increased computational cost due to the Wasserstein distance calculation. How does this impact training time, and are there any strategies to mitigate this cost?
4.  Can you provide more intuition on how the choice of noise affects the generated samples? Does a higher variance of noise lead to more diverse but potentially lower-quality samples, or vice versa?
5.  The proofs rely heavily on assumptions of smoothness and specific noise distributions. How robust are the theoretical results to deviations from these assumptions in a real-world setting?
