This paper tackles the fundamental problem of instability in Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training. It argues that current understanding is lacking, relying on heuristics, and proposes a theoretical framework to analyze and address these issues. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that perfect discrimination arises when the generator and real data distributions have disjoint supports or reside on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates depending on the cost function used. The paper then proposes a novel approach leveraging noise injection and a modified cost function related to the Wasserstein distance to mitigate these problems.

**Reject** with a recommendation for substantial revision. While the paper identifies a crucial problem and offers valuable theoretical insights, the presentation is dense and lacks sufficient empirical validation to convince the reader of the proposed solution’s effectiveness. The theoretical results, while interesting, feel somewhat disconnected from practical GAN training challenges, and the link between the theoretical analysis and the proposed mitigation strategy isn't fully convincing without stronger experimental support.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor, but Limited Connection to Practice:** The paper’s strength lies in its mathematical analysis of the problem. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 elegantly demonstrate the possibility of perfect discrimination, and the subsequent analysis of gradient behavior with different cost functions is insightful. However, the assumptions underlying these theorems (e.g., perfectly aligned manifolds) feel restrictive in the context of real-world GAN applications. It’s not clear how often these conditions strictly hold, and the paper doesn’t address the robustness of its analysis to deviations from these assumptions.
*   **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks substantial empirical validation. Figures 1 and 2 provide some qualitative illustration of the theoretical claims but are insufficient to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed noise injection and Wasserstein-based approach. There's no comparison to state-of-the-art GAN training techniques, and the results don't clearly show improved sample quality or training stability compared to existing methods. A critical missing piece is a thorough experimental section with quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) and ablation studies to assess the impact of different parameters (noise level, etc.).
*   **Density and Complexity:** The paper is very dense and uses a lot of mathematical notation without sufficient explanation. While targeting a theoretically inclined audience, it risks alienating readers who might be interested in the practical implications of the work. A more accessible presentation with more intuitive explanations of the key concepts and results would significantly improve the paper’s impact.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Clarify Assumptions:** Explicitly discuss the limitations of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis. How sensitive are the results to deviations from perfect disjointness of supports or perfect alignment of manifolds?
*   **Strengthen Empirical Validation:** Conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation comparing the proposed method to established GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, spectral normalization) on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA). Use quantitative metrics to demonstrate improvements in sample quality and training stability.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different components of the proposed approach (e.g., noise injection level, choice of noise distribution).
*   **Practical Considerations:** Discuss practical considerations for implementing the proposed method. How does the noise injection affect the computational cost of training? How should the noise level be tuned?
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, it would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of how the proposed approach relates to and builds upon existing methods for stabilizing GAN training. Specifically, how does this approach contrast with, or complement, techniques like spectral normalization or gradient penalty methods?
*   **Simplify Presentation:** Rewrite sections with dense mathematical notation in a more accessible manner. Consider adding more intuitive explanations and visual aids.
*   **Address Open Questions:** The paper mentions several open questions. Expanding on these questions and outlining potential avenues for future research would strengthen the contribution of the work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the main theoretical results (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) to deviations from the idealized conditions of disjoint supports or perfectly aligned manifolds? Can you provide any bounds on the error introduced by relaxing these assumptions?
2.  Can you elaborate on the choice of the noise distribution (Gaussian) and justify its suitability for the proposed approach? Have you explored other noise distributions and their impact on performance?
3.  What is the computational overhead associated with the noise injection step? How does this affect the overall training time compared to standard GAN training techniques?
4.  Could you provide more details on how the noise level should be tuned? Is there a principled way to determine the optimal noise level, or does it require extensive hyperparameter search?
5.  What are the key differences between the proposed approach and existing methods for stabilizing GAN training, such as WGAN-GP or spectral normalization? Where does your approach offer a clear advantage?
