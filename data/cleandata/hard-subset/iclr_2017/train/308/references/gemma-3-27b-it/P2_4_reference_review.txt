## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Understanding and Improving GAN Training Stability

**1. Summary:**

This paper dives into the theoretical underpinnings of GAN training instability, identifying key issues stemming from the mismatch between the ideal Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) optimization and the practical realities of discriminator training. The authors demonstrate that perfect discrimination is often achievable when generator and discriminator supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates depending on the cost function used. They then propose a novel approach involving adding noise to the input distributions to soften the divergence and enable more stable training, theoretically grounding this approach in Wasserstein distance and providing a framework for analysis and future research.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the paper presents a thoughtful theoretical analysis of GAN training instability and proposes a potentially valuable direction for improvement, the presentation feels incomplete and lacks sufficient empirical validation to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The theoretical insights are interesting, but their practical impact is not convincingly demonstrated.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Theoretical Focus, Limited Empirical Validation:** The core of the paper is a substantial theoretical argument. However, the empirical evidence is primarily limited to figures illustrating the theoretical points (vanishing gradients) rather than a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed noise-based approach against state-of-the-art GAN training techniques on standard benchmark datasets.  The claim of improved stability and potential for avoiding mode collapse requires substantial empirical support. Figures 2 and 3 are helpful for illustrating the theory but don't constitute a convincing performance comparison.
* **Complexity & Accessibility:** The paper is densely theoretical and relies heavily on mathematical notation. While a degree of mathematical rigor is expected at ICLR, the complexity makes it difficult to follow the arguments and assess their practical relevance. The writing could benefit from more intuitive explanations and connections to existing GAN training challenges.
* **Novelty Incremental:** While the insights into perfect discrimination and the behavior of different cost functions are valuable, many of the core ideas (e.g., the benefits of adding noise, using Wasserstein distances) are not entirely novel within the GAN literature. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a substantial leap forward compared to existing knowledge.
* **Unclear Implementation Details:** The suggested noise-based approach, while theoretically sound, lacks concrete implementation details. How is the noise scaled? What kind of noise is used (Gaussian, uniform, etc.)? How does this interact with other common GAN training techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty)?  Without these details, it's difficult to assess the practicality of the proposal.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Strengthen Empirical Evaluation:** The most crucial improvement would be to conduct thorough experiments on established GAN benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) comparing the performance of the proposed noise-based approach against baselines like standard GAN training, WGAN-GP, and other popular stabilization techniques.  Report metrics such as FID, Inception Score, and visual inspection of generated samples.
* **Clarify Implementation Details:** Provide specific guidelines for implementing the proposed noise injection. What are the best practices for choosing the noise distribution and scaling factor?
* **Improve Accessibility:** Consider adding more intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical derivations.  A visual summary of the key theoretical findings would also be helpful.
* **Address Related Work More Thoroughly:** While the paper cites relevant work, a more detailed discussion of how this work relates to and builds upon existing literature on GAN stability and divergence metrics would strengthen the contribution.
* **Address the Scaling of the Noise:** The authors mention the importance of annealing the noise. However, the suggested scaling and the theoretical justification for the proposed annealing schedule is missing.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Empirical Results:** Can you provide empirical results demonstrating the performance of the proposed noise injection approach on standard GAN benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA)?
2. **Noise Scaling:** What is the recommended strategy for scaling the noise during training, and what is the theoretical justification for this scaling?
3. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational overhead of adding noise to the discriminator input, and how does this affect training time?
4. **Connection to Wasserstein GANs:**  How does the proposed approach relate to existing techniques like Wasserstein GANs (WGANs) that already utilize distance-based metrics? What advantages does this approach offer over WGANs?
5. **Practical Considerations:** Have you experimented with different noise distributions (e.g., Gaussian, uniform) and scaling factors? What were the results, and what recommendations can you offer for practical implementation?
