- Paper provides a theoretical analysis of GAN instability, showing perfect discrimination on low-dimensional supports leads to vanishing gradients or unstable updates.  
- Authors propose injecting noise into input distributions to soften divergences and stabilize training, with theoretical connections to Wasserstein distance.  
- Decision: Reject due to incomplete presentation and insufficient empirical validation.  
- Main critique: heavy theoretical focus with limited empirical evidence for the proposed noise-based method.  
- Supporting critique: experiments are mostly illustrative figures and lack benchmark comparisons to SOTA GANs.  
- Presentation is dense and mathematically heavy, reducing accessibility and practical assessment.  
- Novelty seen as incremental because noise injection and Wasserstein ideas exist in prior GAN literature.  
- Implementation details for the noise-based approach (type, scale, annealing) are unclear or missing.  
- Recommendation: run thorough experiments on benchmarks (CIFAR-10, CelebA), report FID/IS, and compare to baselines like WGAN-GP.  
- Recommendation: provide concrete implementation guidelines (noise distribution, scaling/annealing, interaction with spectral norm/GP).  
- Recommendation: improve exposition with intuitive explanations, visuals, and clearer relation to prior work on GAN stability.  
- Reviewer questions: request empirical results on benchmarks and clarification of noise-scaling strategy and theoretical justification.  
- Reviewer questions: ask about computational overhead of noise injection and its effect on training time.  
- Reviewer questions: request clarification of advantages over WGANs and results from experiments with different noise distributions and scaling.
