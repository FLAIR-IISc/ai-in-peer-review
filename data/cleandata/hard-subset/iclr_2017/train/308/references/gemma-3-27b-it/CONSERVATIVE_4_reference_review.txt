This paper investigates the instability of Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training, attributing it to the interaction between the discriminator’s ability to perfectly separate the generated and real distributions when their supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds, and the choice of cost function used to train the generator. The authors analyze the original GAN cost function and its common alternative, demonstrating issues with vanishing gradients and unstable updates, and propose a theoretical framework for stabilizing training by incorporating noise and considering Wasserstein distance.

**Reject.** While the identification of potential theoretical issues in GAN training is a worthwhile pursuit, this paper presents a largely theoretical analysis lacking sufficient empirical validation. The claims of instability and the proposed solution, while intuitively plausible, are not convincingly demonstrated through rigorous experimentation. The reliance on theoretical proofs without comprehensive empirical support significantly weakens the paper’s contribution.

The core argument centers around the perfect discrimination theorem and its consequences for gradient flow. While the mathematical derivations appear sound, the connection to practical GAN training dynamics is not sufficiently established. The authors acknowledge that their theoretical analysis hinges on assumptions about the dimensionality of the supports and the behavior of the discriminator, yet they do not provide adequate evidence to show these assumptions hold in real-world GAN scenarios. Specifically, the analysis appears highly sensitive to the continuity and manifold structure of the data and generated distributions, which are rarely perfectly satisfied in practice.

The proposed solution of adding noise and focusing on the Wasserstein distance is a reasonable direction, but it’s presented as largely theoretical. The paper lacks a compelling demonstration that this approach overcomes the identified instability issues in practice, especially compared to existing GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty). The connection between the theoretical analysis and the practical benefits of the proposed approach remains tenuous. The lack of comparison to these established methods is a significant shortcoming. The theoretical link between the noise addition and a practical gradient improvement needs to be illustrated with experiments.

Furthermore, the paper’s extensive mathematical treatment, while demonstrating technical proficiency, makes it less accessible to a broader audience. The lack of clear, intuitive explanations for some of the more complex concepts hinders understanding.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Empirical Validation:** The most critical improvement would be to include thorough experimental results. This should involve training GANs on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) with and without the proposed noise addition, and comparing the training stability and sample quality to those of established GAN variants. Quantitative metrics (e.g., FID score, Inception score) are essential.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** A comprehensive comparison to existing GAN stabilization techniques is necessary to demonstrate the superiority or unique advantages of the proposed approach.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** Investigate the sensitivity of the proposed method to different noise levels and distributions.
*   **Simplification and Intuition:** The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical concepts. Visualizations and simplified examples could help make the ideas more accessible.
*   **Clarify Assumptions:** More clearly articulate and justify the key assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis (e.g., dimensionality of the supports, continuity of distributions).
*   **Connection to Practical GAN Architectures:** Discuss how the proposed approach can be applied to common GAN architectures (e.g., DCGAN, StyleGAN).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide empirical evidence demonstrating the vanishing gradients or unstable updates predicted by your theorems in a practical GAN setting?
2.  How does the performance of your proposed method compare to established GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) in terms of training stability and sample quality? Please present quantitative results.
3.  What is the sensitivity of your approach to the choice of noise distribution and level?
4.  How do the assumptions about the dimensionality of the supports and the continuity of distributions affect the validity of your theoretical analysis in real-world applications?
5.  Can you provide an intuitive explanation for why adding noise, as opposed to other regularization techniques, addresses the identified instability issues?
