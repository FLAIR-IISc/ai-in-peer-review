This paper tackles the problem of understanding the instability of Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training. It argues that the core issue stems from the properties of the divergences used in GAN training (JSD, KL) and the tendency for the generator and discriminator to operate on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients. The authors propose a novel theoretical analysis and suggest mitigating the instability through the introduction of noise and employing the Wasserstein metric.

I recommend **Reject** for now, primarily due to a lack of empirical validation for the proposed solutions and the complexity of the theoretical arguments making it difficult to assess their practical relevance. While the theoretical contributions are potentially significant, the paper feels incomplete without demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed noise injection and Wasserstein-based approach on benchmark datasets.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to provide a formal theoretical grounding for the observed difficulties in GAN training. The analysis of the KL divergence's asymmetry and the implications of low-dimensional manifolds for discriminator accuracy are insightful. The theorems presented – concerning perfect discrimination and gradient behavior – are logically developed, building upon established mathematical concepts. The exploration of softer metrics like the Wasserstein distance and its relation to JSD through noise injection is a valuable direction. The detailed proofs in the appendix add to the rigor of the analysis. However, the paper heavily relies on theoretical results without providing corresponding empirical evidence. The figures (1, 2, 3) are insightful but insufficient to demonstrate the practical impact of the proposed solutions. Figure 2, while supporting the claim of vanishing gradients, doesn’t explicitly connect it to the theoretical framework. Figure 3 shows gradient instability but lacks a comparison to the proposed noise injection strategy. The conclusions about avoiding instability through the proposed methods are, therefore, not convincingly supported. The level of mathematical detail, while admirable, may also hinder accessibility for some readers and might benefit from more intuitive explanations. The connections between the theoretical results and practical GAN training could be made clearer.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

1. **Empirical Validation:** Conduct experiments on standard GAN benchmark datasets (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10, CelebA) to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed noise injection technique and Wasserstein-based approach. Compare the performance (sample quality, training stability) with traditional GAN training methods.
2. **Clarify the Noise Distribution:** Provide more specific guidance on the choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian, uniform) and its parameters (e.g., variance). Justify the choice based on the theoretical analysis and experimental results.
3. **Wasserstein Distance Implementation:**  Detail the practical implementation of the Wasserstein distance within the GAN training loop. Address potential computational challenges and optimization strategies.
4. **Address the Adversarial Examples Discussion:** Expand the discussion on adversarial examples and their relation to the proposed approach. Clarify how the noise injection mitigates this issue.
5. **Simplify Mathematical Notation:**  Consider using more concise and intuitive notation where possible to improve readability. Add more intermediate steps in complex derivations.
6. **Provide Intuitive Explanations:** Offer more intuitive explanations of the theoretical results, focusing on their practical implications for GAN training.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  What specific choices of noise distributions and parameters were explored in preliminary experiments (even if not included in the final submission), and what were the observed effects on training stability and sample quality?
2.  What are the computational challenges associated with implementing the Wasserstein distance in a GAN training loop, and how can these be addressed?
3.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 3.3 and how it connects the Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg to the JSD between their noisy counterparts Pr+ and Pg+?
4.  How does the proposed method compare to other existing techniques for stabilizing GAN training, such as spectral normalization or gradient penalty?
5.  The paper mentions a connection to contrastive divergence. Could you elaborate on this connection and how the proposed approach differs from traditional contrastive divergence methods?
