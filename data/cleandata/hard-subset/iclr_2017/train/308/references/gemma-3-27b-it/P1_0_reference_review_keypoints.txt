- Paper identifies GAN instability caused by the discriminator becoming "too good" too quickly, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable generator updates.
- Theoretical analysis attributes this to mismatches between GAN objectives and data/generator geometry (disjoint supports, low-dimensional manifolds).
- Authors propose adding continuous noise to discriminator inputs and generator samples to smooth distributions and stabilize training.
- The noisy-training perspective is linked to optimal transport and suggested as a metric beyond standard divergences.
- Decision: Reject due to insufficient empirical validation despite strong theoretical contributions.
- Strength: Rigorous, insightful theoretical derivations elucidating why JSD and standard GAN training can fail.
- Weakness: Limited experimental results that do not convincingly show the noisy approach outperforming established stabilization methods.
- Missing baselines and quantitative comparisons (e.g., FID, Inception Score) on standard datasets like CIFAR-10 or CelebA.
- Presentation is dense and technically demanding; needs more intuitive explanations and clearer theory-to-practice connections.
- Recommended: Add thorough empirical evaluation comparing to WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, etc., with quantitative and qualitative results.
- Recommended: Conduct ablation studies over noise distributions and noise levels to show sensitivity and best practices.
- Recommended: Discuss practical considerations such as computational overhead and effect on training time.
- Recommended: Clarify positioning relative to existing GAN stabilization literature and how the method complements or differs from others.
- Questions for authors: provide code/hyperparameters for Figures 1–3, report experiments on different noise types/levels, quantify computational overhead, give an intuitive explanation of gradient mitigation, and compare performance and cost to WGAN-GP/spectral normalization.
