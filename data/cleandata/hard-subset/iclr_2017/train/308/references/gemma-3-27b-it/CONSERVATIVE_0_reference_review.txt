This paper attempts to provide a theoretical understanding of the instabilities inherent in Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training and proposes a new direction to address these issues. The authors identify that the supports of the generator and real data distributions often lie on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discrimination by the discriminator and consequently, vanishing or unstable gradients. They then propose adding noise to smooth the distributions and facilitate more stable training, relating the resulting performance to the Wasserstein distance.

**Reject.** While the identification of the low-dimensional manifold problem is a valid observation, the analysis feels overly focused on theoretical edge cases and ultimately lacks convincing empirical validation. The proposed solution, while intuitively reasonable, isn’t presented with sufficient experimental results to demonstrate its effectiveness over existing, well-established GAN training techniques. The extensive theoretical development, particularly concerning perfect discrimination, feels disconnected from practical GAN training realities.

The central argument rests on the idea of perfect discrimination arising from low-dimensional supports. While mathematically sound under specific conditions, the practical implication – that this *always* occurs and is the *primary* cause of instability – is not convincingly demonstrated. The authors acknowledge that this might not always be the case, yet build much of their analysis upon this assumption. Moreover, the suggested fix of adding noise is not novel; similar techniques (e.g., adding noise to discriminator inputs, spectral normalization) are already common practice in the GAN literature and are presented without a clear comparative advantage.

The extensive theoretical derivations, while rigorous, often feel unnecessarily complex and abstract. The connection between the theoretical results and practical GAN training is not always clear. For example, the discussion of divergences and gradients feels muddled, and the claims about instability are not substantiated with robust experimental evidence. The derivations rely heavily on specific assumptions (e.g., smoothness, Gaussian noise) that may not hold in real-world scenarios. The link to the Wasserstein metric is a positive step but isn’t fully explored in terms of practical computation or comparison to other metrics. The inclusion of several theorems and lemmas without thoroughly connecting them to the core arguments hinders clarity.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the lack of extensive experiments. The authors must demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed noise addition method by comparing it to established GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, different optimizers) on a variety of datasets. Include quantitative results (e.g., FID, Inception Score) and qualitative examples.
*   **Clarify Practical Implications:** Translate the theoretical findings into actionable insights for practitioners. How does one determine the appropriate level of noise to add? What are the trade-offs between noise level and sample quality?
*   **Connect to Existing Literature:** Position the work more clearly within the broader context of GAN training. Discuss how the proposed approach relates to and improves upon existing techniques. Why is this approach superior to, for example, simply using a gradient penalty?
*   **Simplify Theoretical Presentation:** While rigor is appreciated, the theoretical derivations can be streamlined to improve clarity. Focus on the core ideas and avoid unnecessary mathematical complexity.
*   **Address Assumptions:** Explicitly discuss the limitations of the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis. How robust are the results to violations of these assumptions?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper heavily relies on the assumption that both Pr and Pg have supports on low-dimensional manifolds. Can you provide empirical evidence to support this claim for various datasets and GAN architectures?
2.  How does the performance of your proposed noise addition method compare to existing techniques like WGAN-GP or spectral normalization in terms of sample quality (FID, Inception Score) and training stability? Please provide quantitative results.
3.  How does the optimal amount of noise vary with the dimensionality of the data and the complexity of the GAN architecture? Is there a principled way to determine this value, or is it simply a hyperparameter that needs to be tuned?
4.  The theoretical analysis focuses on continuous distributions. How does the approach perform when dealing with discrete data or distributions with complex support structures?
5.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of how the Wasserstein distance relates to the perceived quality of generated samples and how it can be used to guide GAN training?
