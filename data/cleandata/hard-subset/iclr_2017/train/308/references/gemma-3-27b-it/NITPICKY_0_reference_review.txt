This paper attempts to provide a theoretical understanding of the instability issues plaguing Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training, attributing them to the supports of the generator and real data distributions frequently lying on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients or unstable updates. The authors propose adding noise to mitigate these issues and introduce a new metric based on Wasserstein distance to evaluate the quality of generated samples.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a valid concern regarding the geometry of the distributions involved in GAN training, the presentation is overly dense, the technical rigor occasionally slips, and the proposed solution, while intuitively reasonable, lacks sufficient empirical validation and feels somewhat incremental given the extensive theoretical development. The sheer number of minor flaws and inconsistencies distract from the core argument.

The primary issues stem from the paper’s meticulous but ultimately distracting detail. For example, the extensive discussion of absolute continuity vs. continuity feels pedantic and adds little to the core argument. The proof of Lemma 1 is overly complex, and relies on a somewhat hand-wavy application of the inverse function theorem. The derivation of Theorem 2.5, while correct, feels unnecessarily convoluted. The leap from theoretical observations regarding vanishing gradients or unstable updates to the proposed noise injection solution feels abrupt and lacks compelling justification – why *this* particular noise distribution, and what’s the optimal annealing schedule? The proofs, while present, are often densely packed, making it challenging to follow the logic and verify the correctness of each step. 

The claim that the proposed method is “a new direction designed to avoid instability” feels overstated. Adding noise is a standard regularization technique, and while the theoretical justification provided is novel, the practical impact remains unclear. The paper also suffers from a lack of comparison to existing stabilization techniques (e.g., WGAN, spectral normalization) – how does this approach compare in terms of performance and computational cost?  Figure 1 and 2 are mentioned, but not included, making it impossible to evaluate their claims. The lack of experiments beyond the theoretical results is a significant weakness, and the discussion of Wasserstein distance feels tacked on rather than integrated into a comprehensive evaluation framework. The authors mention the gradient norm growing drastically in Figure 3, but do not properly discuss it, only mentioning it as confirmation.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity and Conciseness:** The paper would benefit from significant streamlining. Removing redundant explanations and simplifying complex proofs would greatly improve readability.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:**  A thorough experimental evaluation is crucial. This should include comparisons to state-of-the-art GAN training techniques on standard benchmark datasets.  The benefits of the proposed noise injection strategy need to be demonstrated empirically.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** Explicitly discuss how this work relates to existing GAN stabilization methods.  Highlight the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach compared to alternatives.
*   **Figures:** Include all referenced figures.
*   **Notation:** Be consistent with notation throughout the paper.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** Carefully review the proofs for potential errors or inconsistencies.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the proposed approach and potential areas for future research.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation for the choice of Gaussian noise, and explain the sensitivity of the results to the variance parameter?
2.  How does the performance of the proposed method compare to WGAN and spectral normalization in terms of sample quality and training stability?
3.  What is the computational overhead associated with adding noise during training, and how does it scale with the dimensionality of the input data?
4.  The claim about the vanishing gradients and infinite variances is very strong. Is it possible to get a sense of the severity of these issues empirically (e.g., plotting gradient norms during training)?
5.  Could you provide an illustrative example of how the Wasserstein distance metric helps to evaluate the quality of generated samples in a scenario where the JSD fails?
6.  Why did you choose to prove Lemma 1 when the result for affine transformations and pointwise non-linearities is a known result in manifold theory?
