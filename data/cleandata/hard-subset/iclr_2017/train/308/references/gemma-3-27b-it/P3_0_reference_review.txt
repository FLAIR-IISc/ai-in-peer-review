## ICLR 2024 Review: Understanding and Stabilizing GANs

**1. Summary:**

This paper delves into the theoretical underpinnings of the notoriously unstable training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). It argues that the instability stems from the inherent properties of the training objective – specifically, the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) – when the generator and data distributions have low-dimensional supports, a common scenario in practice. The authors rigorously demonstrate that perfect discriminators emerge under these conditions, leading to vanishing or unstable gradients during generator updates. Furthermore, the paper proposes a novel approach based on smoothing the distributions with added noise, leading to a more stable training process and a Wasserstein distance-based metric for evaluating progress. The work offers a solid theoretical framework for understanding GAN training, identifies key challenges, and proposes a promising direction for improvement.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to our understanding of GANs. The detailed analysis of the JSD, the demonstration of perfect discrimination, and the proposed smoothing technique are all compelling. While the paper is dense and requires careful reading, the insights gained are valuable and address a core issue in the field. The proposed method shows strong promise, and the mathematical rigor is commendable.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of GAN training dynamics. The proofs of Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 2.5, and 2.6 are well-constructed and convincingly demonstrate the core arguments regarding perfect discrimination and gradient instability. The connection to manifold learning and the implications of low-dimensional supports are particularly insightful.
* **Clear Identification of the Problem:** The authors clearly articulate the core problem: the JSD’s susceptibility to issues when distributions have non-overlapping supports or lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The discussion of vanishing gradients with the original cost function and unstable updates with the alternative cost function is well-explained and directly addresses commonly observed issues in GAN training.
* **Novel Solution & Theoretical Justification:** The introduction of noise to smooth the distributions is a clever idea, theoretically grounded in the analysis of Wasserstein distance and its relationship to the JSD of the smoothed distributions. This approach avoids the pitfalls of perfect discrimination and offers a pathway to more stable training.
* **Comprehensive Analysis:** The paper doesn’t stop at identifying the problem and proposing a solution; it also delves into the limitations of existing metrics and proposes an alternative (Wasserstein distance) for more reliable evaluation.
* **Well-Referenced:** The paper thoroughly cites relevant work, demonstrating a strong understanding of the existing literature.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical analysis is compelling, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including some empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed smoothing technique. Even a limited set of experiments on standard datasets (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10) would bolster the claims. Visual comparisons of generated samples with and without smoothing, as well as learning curves, would be valuable.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't discuss the practical challenges of implementing the smoothing technique, such as choosing an appropriate noise distribution and variance. A discussion of these considerations would be helpful for practitioners.
* **Clarify Notation:**  While generally well-written, some notation could be made more consistent and accessible.  For example, clarifying the distinction between 'Pr' and 'Pg' versus 'Pr+' and 'Pg+' earlier and more consistently would improve readability.
* **Simplify Proofs (Where Possible):** The proofs are rigorous but dense.  Exploring opportunities to simplify some of the steps or provide more intuitive explanations could broaden the paper’s accessibility.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding Empirical Validation:** Do you have any preliminary experimental results that support the effectiveness of the proposed smoothing technique? If so, could you briefly describe them? If not, what are the main obstacles to conducting such experiments?
* **Noise Selection:** How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian, uniform)?  Are there guidelines for selecting an appropriate noise variance?
* **Computational Cost:** Does adding the noise significantly increase the computational cost of training?
* **Connection to Existing Work:**  The paper briefly mentions contrastive divergence.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between your proposed approach and contrastive divergence, and how your method differs or improves upon it?
* **Wasserstein Distance Approximation:** While advocating for Wasserstein distance, the paper doesn't discuss practical challenges of directly computing it. How can the Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg be efficiently approximated in practice within the context of your proposed method?
* **Generalization to Other Architectures:** Do you anticipate the proposed smoothing technique being equally effective with different GAN architectures (e.g., StyleGAN, BigGAN)? Are there any architectures where it might be less applicable?



Overall, this is a well-written, insightful, and theoretically grounded paper that addresses a critical issue in the GAN literature. Addressing the suggested points for improvement would further enhance its impact and make it an even more valuable contribution to the field.
