This paper investigates the theoretical underpinnings of Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training instability, identifying a fundamental issue stemming from the low-dimensional nature of the generator’s output support and the resulting “perfect discrimination” problem. It provides a detailed analysis of the original and alternative GAN cost functions, demonstrating how they lead to vanishing gradients or unstable updates. Finally, it proposes a solution involving the addition of noise to the input of the discriminator to mitigate these issues and introduces a novel metric based on Wasserstein distance for evaluating generative models.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents a compelling theoretical analysis of GAN training, identifying key issues contributing to instability. However, the work lacks sufficient empirical validation to convincingly demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed solution. While the theoretical derivations are thorough, the connection to practical GAN training and improvements remains weak.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The core contribution revolves around a theoretical framework and a proposed solution (adding noise and using a Wasserstein-based metric). However, the paper primarily presents theoretical proofs and lacks extensive empirical results demonstrating the benefits of this approach. Figures 1, 2, and 3 provide some initial evidence, but are insufficient to validate the claims of stability and improved sample quality. A thorough comparison with established GAN training techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, spectral normalization) on standard benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) is crucial. The claim of noise mitigating instability needs to be quantitatively demonstrated.

2. **Lack of Novelty in Proposed Solution:** The concept of adding noise to stabilize GAN training isn’t entirely new. Techniques like adding Gaussian noise to the discriminator's input or using techniques like differential privacy have been explored previously. The paper does not sufficiently highlight how its approach differs from or improves upon these existing techniques, nor does it justify the specific choice of noise distribution and level. While the theoretical justification is provided, a clear advantage over existing methods isn’t apparent.

3. **Complexity and Accessibility:** The paper is dense with mathematical notation and theoretical derivations. While rigor is appreciated, it makes the core ideas less accessible to a broader audience. Some of the arguments, particularly in the later sections concerning Wasserstein distance and its relationship to the JSD of noisy distributions, are difficult to follow without a strong background in optimal transport theory. A more intuitive explanation of the key concepts would enhance the paper’s impact.



**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct comprehensive experiments on standard GAN benchmarks, comparing the proposed method with state-of-the-art GAN training techniques. Provide quantitative results (e.g., Inception Score, FID score) and qualitative comparisons (e.g., generated sample visualization).
* **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to analyze the impact of different noise levels and noise distributions on GAN training stability and sample quality.
* **Connection to Existing Work:**  Clearly articulate the relationship between the proposed approach and existing GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, spectral normalization). Highlight the advantages of the proposed method over these existing approaches.
* **Clarify Notation and Intuition:** Simplify the mathematical notation and provide more intuitive explanations of the core concepts, particularly those related to optimal transport and Wasserstein distance.
* **Address Limitations:** Discuss the limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, how does the noise injection affect the training speed or the resolution of generated images?
* **Provide Implementation Details:** Include details about the implementation of the proposed method, such as the choice of hyperparameters and the optimization algorithm used.
* **Expand discussion of Theorem 3.3:** The statement "The two terms in equation (6) can be controlled" is vague. Elaborate on how precisely these terms can be controlled and provide guidelines for selecting appropriate parameters.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. What specific strategies were considered (and discarded) for empirically validating the theoretical findings, and why were those strategies not pursued?
2. How does the proposed noise injection method compare in terms of computational cost to existing GAN stabilization techniques like gradient penalty or spectral normalization?
3. The paper mentions that the choice of noise distribution is important. Can you provide more specific guidance on how to select an appropriate noise distribution for a given dataset or GAN architecture?
4.  How does the level of noise affect the final sample quality? Is there a trade-off between stability and fidelity, and if so, how can it be optimized?
5.  Could you clarify the practical implications of Theorem 3.3 and provide a concrete example of how it can be used to evaluate and compare different GAN models?
