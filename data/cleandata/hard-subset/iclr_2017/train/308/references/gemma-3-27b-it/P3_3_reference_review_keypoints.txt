- Paper targets GAN instability, attributing it to non-overlapping supports/low-dimensional manifolds causing perfect discriminators and vanishing or unstable gradients.  
- Authors propose perturbing distributions with noise, analyze resulting Wasserstein distance, and suggest a stabilized gradient update to avoid mode dropping.  
- Theoretical contributions are rigorous, proving perfect discrimination results and analyzing gradient behavior under different cost functions.  
- Decision: Reject — strong theory but incomplete and insufficiently validated empirically.  
- Main weakness: limited empirical support; proposed noise-based solution is not shown to outperform existing techniques on standard benchmarks.  
- Figures illustrate vanishing/growing gradients but do not demonstrate that the proposed method alleviates these issues or compare to baselines.  
- Presentation is mathematically dense and hard to follow; needs clearer intuition, examples, and visual aids.  
- Paper does not adequately situate contributions relative to existing stabilization methods (e.g., spectral normalization, WGAN-GP, gradient penalties).  
- Unclear whether the noise-based approach is orthogonal to or better than established regularization techniques.  
- Practicality concerns: choice of noise distribution (covariance), impact on training speed and sample quality, and concrete training procedure are not explored.  
- The Wasserstein-distance-based metrics are introduced but not translated into a clear, implementable training algorithm.  
- Revision should include comprehensive experiments (CIFAR-10, CelebA), FID/IS metrics, and ablation studies over noise levels and distributions.  
- Authors should provide a concise algorithm, hyperparameter guidance, and discuss computational cost of Wasserstein approximation.  
- Limitations need explicit discussion, including computational cost and reliance on the "almost optimal" discriminator assumption.  
- Open questions for authors: combining with other stabilization methods, guidance on noise selection, impact of noise level, scalability to higher-dimensional architectures, and justification for the discriminator optimality assumption.
