- Summary: The paper analyzes GAN instability when real/generated distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds and proposes adding noise to soften the metric, linking divergences, Wasserstein distance, and gradient behavior to stabilize training.

- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — strong theoretical contribution and principled solution, but needs some additional empirical and clarifying work.

- Strong theoretical foundation: Rigorous proofs (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4–2.6) explain perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients, and unstable updates.

- Novel insights: Analysis shows the -logD alternative optimizes a problematic divergence that encourages mode dropping.

- Principled solution: Adding noise and using Wasserstein distance for noisy distributions provides a more stable, well-behaved training objective.

- Comprehensive analysis: Addresses mathematical causes of instability, practical gradient implications, and adversarial examples with mitigation via noisy gradients.

- Presentation quality: The paper is generally well-written with proofs included in the appendix.

- Empirical validation needed: Lacks extensive experiments comparing the method to state-of-the-art techniques and reporting metrics like FID/Inception Score on benchmarks.

- Noise schedule unclear: Paper does not specify how to set or schedule noise variance during training or sensitivity to noise level.

- Computational cost unclear: Needs discussion/estimates of overhead from the proposed approach and Wasserstein approximation.

- Relation to prior work: Should more explicitly compare and contrast with other stabilization techniques (regularization, architecture changes).

- Appendix organization: Appendix is dense; proofs should be broken into smaller sections with introductory remarks for readability.

- Q (empirical): Do you have preliminary empirical comparisons to existing GAN training techniques?

- Q (noise schedule): Have you explored different noise schedules and sensitivity to noise variance?

- Q (computational cost): What is the approximate computational overhead versus standard GAN training?

- Q (Wasserstein approximation): How sensitive is performance to the accuracy of the Wasserstein approximation and are there efficient high-dimensional approximations?

- Q (theoretical assumptions): Can you clarify assumptions in Theorem 3.3 (e.g., diameter bound) and robustness to assumption violations?
