This paper tackles the notoriously unstable training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), offering a theoretical analysis of the underlying causes – specifically, the tendency for the discriminator to become ‘too good’ and the resulting vanishing/unstable gradients. It convincingly argues that the core issue stems from the supports of the generated and real distributions often lying on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to a perfect discriminator and a breakdown of the intended adversarial learning process. Crucially, the authors propose a novel approach involving adding noise to the inputs, leading to smoother distributions and more stable training, alongside a new metric – the Wasserstein distance – to properly evaluate and guide the learning process.

**Reject.** While the theoretical insights are compelling and the proposed direction is genuinely novel, the paper feels incomplete. The connection between the theoretical analysis and the proposed solution (adding noise and using the Wasserstein metric) is not fully fleshed out. The practical demonstration feels limited, lacking a comprehensive empirical validation across diverse datasets and architectures. The current presentation relies heavily on theoretical arguments, leaving a gap in convincing evidence for the practical efficacy of their approach.

The paper’s strength lies in its bold attempt to provide a theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability. The analysis of the Jensen-Shannon divergence and the identification of the perfect discriminator scenario are insightful. The theorems presented (2.1, 2.2, 2.6) are well-articulated and contribute significantly to the understanding of GAN training dynamics. However, the jump from identifying these problems to proposing noise addition feels somewhat abrupt. The rationale for *why* this specific noise addition solves the identified issues isn’t fully explained, beyond the general idea of smoothing distributions. 

The introduction of the Wasserstein metric is a strong point, and the theoretical justification for its relevance in this context (Theorem 3.3) is excellent. However, the paper doesn’t demonstrate how this metric is actually used in practice – is it used as a loss function? How is it computed efficiently? These practical details are missing. The claim that using the noisy distributions allows for stable training and avoids vanishing gradients (Corollary 3.2) needs further empirical support. The figures included (1, 2, 3) are helpful in illustrating the theoretical points, but lack quantitative comparisons to established GAN training methods and baselines.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Elaborate on the noise addition:** Provide a more detailed explanation of how the noise is added, the choice of noise distribution, and the impact of different noise levels on performance.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Significantly expand the experimental section. Include results on diverse datasets (CIFAR-10, CelebA, ImageNet) and compare the proposed method against standard GAN training techniques (e.g., DCGAN with various learning rate schedules, WGAN). Report quantitative metrics (Inception Score, FID) to demonstrate the improvement in sample quality and training stability.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead of using the Wasserstein metric and the noise addition technique. Is it significantly more expensive than standard GAN training?
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** Explicitly discuss the relationship between this work and other approaches to stabilizing GAN training, such as spectral normalization or gradient penalties.
*   **Clarify Theorem 3.3:** The statement regarding the Wasserstein distance bound (Theorem 3.3) feels somewhat disconnected from the practical implementation. Explain how this theoretical bound translates into a tangible benefit during training.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* adding noise to the inputs specifically addresses the issue of the perfect discriminator and vanishing gradients?
*   What is the optimal noise level (variance) in practice? Is there a strategy for adaptively adjusting the noise level during training?
*   How is the Wasserstein distance computed in practice, and what techniques are used to make the computation efficient?
*   What is the impact of the noise addition on the diversity of the generated samples? Does it introduce any undesirable artifacts or biases?
*   Have you explored alternative noise distributions besides the Gaussian distribution, and if so, what were the results?
