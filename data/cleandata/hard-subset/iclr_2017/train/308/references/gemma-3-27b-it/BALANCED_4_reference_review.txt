This paper tackles the challenging problem of understanding and improving the training instability of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors aim to provide a theoretical grounding for these instabilities and propose a new research direction to address them. They meticulously analyze the cost functions used in GAN training, demonstrating that the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD), while theoretically appealing, often leads to vanishing gradients or unstable updates in practice when the generator and discriminator distributions have disjoint supports or lie on low-dimensional manifolds – a common scenario in reality. They then propose a novel approach involving adding continuous noise to smooth the distributions and leveraging the Wasserstein metric to address these issues.

**Reject** – While the paper presents a detailed theoretical analysis and proposes an intriguing approach, the lack of empirical validation significantly weakens its contribution. The theoretical arguments are compelling, but their practical relevance remains unproven. The paper largely consists of theoretical derivations and analyses without demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed noise-based approach through experiments.

The paper's strength lies in its thorough and rigorous mathematical analysis of GAN training dynamics. The authors correctly identify the core issue: the tendency of the discriminator to become “too good” too quickly, leading to either vanishing gradients or unstable updates. The explanations regarding the limitations of the JSD and the behavior of different cost functions are well-articulated and insightful. The introduction of the Wasserstein metric and its connection to smoothed distributions provides a promising alternative. The theoretical results regarding perfect discrimination theorems are particularly strong and provide valuable insights. The proofs are generally well-structured and the inclusion of an appendix dedicated to proofs is commendable.

However, the significant weakness is the complete absence of experimental results. The entire paper is built on theoretical arguments. Demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method—even on a simple dataset—is crucial. Without empirical evidence, the proposed solution remains speculative. The authors claim the proposed method avoids the problems of vanishing gradients and instability, but this assertion is not supported by any experimental data. The connection between the theoretical results and practical GAN training is not clearly established. Furthermore, while the detailed mathematical derivations are appreciated, they sometimes overshadow the core ideas, making the paper difficult to follow for a broader audience. The extensive discussion of related work within the introduction is helpful for context but could be slightly condensed. The discussion of different noise distributions and their impact on the metric could also be expanded.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Implement and evaluate the proposed approach:** This is the most crucial step. Conduct experiments on standard datasets (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10) to demonstrate the benefits of adding noise and using the Wasserstein metric. Compare the performance with existing GAN training techniques.
*   **Provide a clearer connection between theory and practice:** Explain how the theoretical results translate into practical guidelines for GAN training. For example, how should the noise level be chosen? How does the choice of noise distribution affect performance?
*   **Simplify the presentation:** While the mathematical rigor is appreciated, strive for a more accessible presentation. Focus on conveying the core ideas in a clear and concise manner. Consider using visual aids to illustrate the theoretical concepts.
*   **Address the computational cost:** Adding noise and computing the Wasserstein metric might be computationally expensive. Discuss the practical implications of these costs.
*   **Clarify the connection to existing work:** Explicitly discuss how the proposed approach relates to other methods for stabilizing GAN training, such as WGAN and improved training techniques.
*   **Expand on the choice of noise:** You mention several possible distributions, but don't provide guidance on which is most suitable. A deeper dive into the properties of these distributions and their impact on the Wasserstein distance would be helpful.
*   **Consider ablation studies:** If experiments are conducted, performing ablation studies to assess the individual contributions of the noise addition and the Wasserstein metric would be valuable.

Questions for the authors:

*   What is the expected computational overhead of implementing the proposed approach compared to standard GAN training?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the choice of noise distribution and variance?
*   Have you considered using alternative metrics besides the Wasserstein metric, and if so, what were the results?
*   Could you elaborate on the challenges of applying this approach to high-dimensional datasets?
*   Do you anticipate that the optimal noise level will change during training, and if so, how would you adaptively adjust it?
