This paper tackles the notoriously difficult problem of training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), aiming to provide a theoretical understanding of their instability and propose a principled approach to address it. It argues that the core issue stems from the misalignment of the supports of the generator and real data distributions, leading to perfect discrimination and vanishing/unstable gradients. The authors then introduce a smoothing technique using noise, framing it within a Wasserstein distance framework, to enable more stable training and a meaningful divergence metric.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a valid and long-standing problem in GAN training, and the attempt to provide a theoretical grounding is admirable, the core contribution feels more like a sophisticated re-framing of known issues rather than a truly novel breakthrough. The proposed solution, adding noise and shifting to a Wasserstein-like metric, is not entirely new and feels somewhat incremental, relying heavily on existing concepts. Though the theoretical analysis is extensive, it largely confirms intuitions already prevalent in the GAN research community.

The primary argument centers around perfect discrimination, a consequence of distributions residing on low-dimensional manifolds. This is a reasonable observation, but the conclusion that it *explains* all observed instabilities feels overstated. The authors acknowledge the issues with the original and alternative GAN loss functions, but the insights offered—vanishing gradients with the original, instability with the modified—are already well-understood. The introduction of noise as a smoothing technique, while potentially beneficial, lacks a compelling demonstration of substantial improvement over existing regularization or normalization techniques used in GANs (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty). The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein distance is interesting, but the leap from a theoretical bound to practical algorithmic improvement isn't fully substantiated. The proofs, while detailed, are quite dense and would benefit from more intuitive explanations. The discussion regarding adversarial examples feels somewhat tangential and weakens the central argument.

Despite these criticisms, the paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying theory. The detailed analysis of the cost functions and the attempts to rigorously connect them to observed behavior are commendable. The mathematical treatment is generally sound, although the extensive reliance on lemmas and theorems makes the paper somewhat difficult to follow.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper is *almost entirely* theoretical. A more compelling argument would be significantly strengthened by empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed noise-based approach on standard GAN benchmarks. Showing improvements in sample quality, training stability, or convergence speed would be crucial.
*   **Comparison to Existing Methods:** A more thorough comparison to existing techniques for stabilizing GAN training (e.g., WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, SN-GAN) is needed. How does the proposed approach compare in terms of performance and computational cost?
*   **Clarity and Intuition:** Simplify the explanations of the theoretical results. Provide more intuitive explanations alongside the rigorous mathematical derivations. Focus on the key takeaways rather than getting bogged down in technical details.
*   **Wasserstein Distance Connection:** Expand on the practical implications of the Wasserstein distance connection. How can this be used to guide the design of GAN architectures or training procedures?
*   **Noise Schedule:** The paper doesn't discuss how the amount of noise should be scheduled during training. This is a critical parameter that could significantly impact performance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Have you conducted any empirical evaluations of the proposed noise-based approach on standard GAN benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA)? If so, could you present those results?
2.  How does the performance of the proposed method compare to existing GAN stabilization techniques like WGAN-GP or spectral normalization in terms of sample quality, training stability, and computational cost?
3.  What is a good strategy for scheduling the amount of noise during training? Does the optimal noise schedule depend on the dataset or network architecture?
4.  The paper mentions the potential for adversarial examples. Could you elaborate on how the proposed approach mitigates this issue and how this was evaluated?
