- Paper attributes GAN instability to properties of the JSD when data and generator lie on low-dimensional supports, causing perfect discriminators and vanishing/unstable gradients.  
- Proposes smoothing distributions by adding noise to prevent perfect discrimination and suggests a Wasserstein-distance-based metric for evaluation.  
- Recommendation: Accept with Minor Revisions.  
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4–2.6) supporting core claims.  
- Clearly identifies the JSD’s failure modes for non-overlapping or low-dimensional supports and explains issues with both original and alternative cost functions.  
- Novel, theoretically justified solution: noise smoothing links smoothed JSD behavior to Wasserstein distance and stabilizes training.  
- Provides comprehensive analysis, including critique of existing metrics and proposing a more reliable evaluation approach.  
- Well-referenced and grounded in existing literature.  
- Major improvement needed: include empirical validation (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10), sample visuals, and learning curves to support theoretical claims.  
- Needs practical guidance on implementing smoothing (choice of noise distribution and variance).  
- Notation inconsistencies (e.g., Pr vs Pr+ and Pg vs Pg+) should be clarified earlier and more consistently.  
- Proofs are rigorous but dense; offer simpler or more intuitive explanations where possible to improve accessibility.  
- Questions for authors: Do you have preliminary experimental results or obstacles to running them?  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive is performance to noise distribution/variance, and are there selection guidelines?  
- Questions for authors: Does adding noise significantly increase computational cost?  
- Questions for authors: Clarify connection to contrastive divergence and how the approach differs or improves upon it.  
- Questions for authors: How can Wasserstein distance be efficiently approximated in practice within this framework?  
- Questions for authors: Do you expect the smoothing technique to generalize across GAN architectures (e.g., StyleGAN, BigGAN), or are there limitations?
