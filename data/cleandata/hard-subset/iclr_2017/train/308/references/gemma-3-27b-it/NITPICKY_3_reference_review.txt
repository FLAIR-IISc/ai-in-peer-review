This paper attempts to provide a theoretical understanding of the instabilities inherent in Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training, attributing them to the low-dimensional nature of the generator’s output manifold and the consequent possibility of perfect discrimination. The authors then propose a smoothing technique via adding noise to the discriminator’s inputs, aiming to mitigate these issues and provide a more stable training process.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a valid issue – the potential for perfect discrimination when generator and discriminator supports are non-overlapping or lie on low-dimensional manifolds – the analysis feels overly focused on idealized scenarios and mathematical derivations that don’t fully translate to practical GAN training. The proposed solution, while theoretically sound, is presented without sufficient empirical validation demonstrating its superiority over existing stabilization techniques. The sheer density of theorems and proofs, coupled with numerous unsubstantiated claims and minor inconsistencies, overshadows the core contribution.

The core argument, that perfect discrimination leads to vanishing gradients or instability, is well-established in the GAN literature, but the paper’s contribution to *understanding why* this happens beyond the manifold argument feels limited.  The derivation of the gradient update with the alternative cost function (Section 2.2.2) is mathematically dense and the conclusion – that it leads to mode dropping – feels like a restatement of known GAN weaknesses rather than a novel insight. The shift to analyzing the Wasserstein distance and its relationship to the noisy distributions (Section 3) is a positive step, but the presented theorem (Theorem 3.3) relies on strong assumptions (diameter bound) and feels disconnected from practical implementation details.

Numerous points require clarification and raise concerns:

* **The assertion that “the optimal discriminator has accuracy 1” (Theorems 2.1 & 2.2)** is a strong claim. While mathematically valid *under the stated conditions*, these conditions are rarely perfectly met in practice.  GAN training operates in a continuous spectrum of discriminator performance, not a binary optimal/non-optimal state.
* **The handling of continuity and absolute continuity.** The paper frequently switches between these concepts without clearly distinguishing them, which is crucial for the validity of certain arguments.
* **The leap from theoretical analysis to practical implications.** The paper often states that theoretical results *explain* practical GAN behavior without providing convincing empirical evidence.  For example, the claim that Theorem 2.6's Cauchy distribution of gradients explains observed instability needs to be demonstrated with experimental results. Figure 3 feels insufficient.
* **The noise addition proposal lacks detail.** How should the noise variance be chosen and scheduled?  Is it different from existing techniques like label smoothing or adding noise to the generator inputs?  Why is this particular noise distribution (Gaussian) chosen?
* **Minor technical inconsistencies:** There's a typo in equation (1) where the second log term should be log(1-D(x)). Theorem 2.5 states that the gradient is 2JSD, when it is really -2JSD.



Here are suggestions for improvement:

* **Focus on empirical validation:** Include a comprehensive experimental section comparing the proposed method to established GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, spectral normalization) on a variety of datasets.
* **Provide a more intuitive explanation of the core argument:**  Reduce the reliance on dense mathematical derivations and focus on conveying the key ideas in a more accessible manner.
* **Clarify the connection to existing literature:** Explicitly discuss how the proposed approach builds upon or differs from existing GAN stabilization methods.
* **Address the limitations of the theoretical analysis:** Acknowledge the strong assumptions made in the theoretical derivations and discuss their potential impact on the practical relevance of the results.
* **Refine the noise addition proposal:** Provide a detailed description of the noise addition strategy, including guidance on choosing the noise variance and scheduling it during training.
* **Address the typos and minor inconsistencies.**

Specifically, I would like the authors to:

1.  Provide empirical evidence demonstrating the Cauchy gradient behavior predicted by Theorem 2.6 in a real GAN training scenario.
2.  Show a comparison of the proposed noise addition method against WGAN-GP in terms of sample quality (e.g., FID score) and training stability.
3.  Explain why the theoretical guarantee of the Wasserstein distance bound in Theorem 3.3 holds in practice, given the typical non-compact support of real-world data distributions.
