This paper attempts to provide a theoretical understanding of the instabilities plaguing Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training and proposes a novel approach to address them. The authors argue that the core issue stems from the interplay between the discriminator becoming too powerful, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates, and the inherent properties of the divergences used in GAN training (KL, JSD). They demonstrate that under certain conditions, perfect discriminators emerge, rendering gradient-based training ineffective, and offer a framework for analyzing these issues through the lens of manifold geometry. They propose adding noise to the inputs to soften the divergences and stabilize training, linking it to the Wasserstein distance.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a valid and well-known problem – the instability of GAN training – and attempts a theoretical analysis, the presentation is overly dense and relies heavily on established mathematical concepts without providing sufficient novelty or convincing empirical validation. The proposed solution, adding noise, is not new (and is already a common practice, albeit without the same theoretical justification) and lacks a compelling demonstration of superiority over existing stabilization techniques. The extensive theoretical derivations, while potentially insightful, are not convincingly connected to practical improvements in GAN training performance.

The core argument regarding perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients is insightful, echoing concerns voiced in the GAN literature. However, the authors’ reliance on idealized conditions (disjoint supports, low-dimensional manifolds) and the subsequent theorems feel somewhat detached from the complexities of real-world GAN training. The leap from these theoretical results to a practical solution feels unmotivated, as the proposal to add noise is hardly groundbreaking. The theoretical justification offered is complex and hard to follow. While the authors attempt to link the approach to the Wasserstein distance, the connection isn’t clearly established in a way that offers a clear advantage for practical implementations. 

The authors thoroughly review existing GAN training issues and divergences. The core mathematical arguments, while extensive, lack a clear demonstration of their impact on practical GAN training scenarios. The paper demonstrates a strong grasp of relevant mathematical concepts (divergences, manifolds, optimal transport), but fails to translate this theoretical understanding into a compelling practical contribution. The theoretical novelty, while present, is overshadowed by a lack of rigorous empirical validation and a relatively incremental contribution.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper sorely lacks empirical results. Demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed noise addition scheme on standard GAN benchmarks (CIFAR-10, CelebA) and comparing its performance against established techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization) is crucial. The figures are primarily theoretical illustrations and don’t showcase practical improvements.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper should more thoroughly discuss how this work relates to existing GAN stabilization techniques. Specifically, it needs to articulate how the proposed approach offers advantages over or complements existing methods.
*   **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is excessively dense and mathematical. Simplifying the presentation and providing more intuitive explanations would significantly improve its accessibility.
*   **Practical Guidance:** If the noise addition is the key practical takeaway, the paper should provide clear guidelines on how to choose the noise distribution (e.g., variance, type) and how it interacts with other hyperparameters.
*   **Justification for Assumptions:** The assumptions about disjoint supports and low-dimensional manifolds should be explicitly justified in the context of practical GAN applications. If these assumptions are unrealistic, the relevance of the theoretical results is diminished.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Have you conducted any empirical evaluations of the proposed noise addition scheme on standard GAN benchmarks? If so, could you provide quantitative results comparing its performance to existing stabilization techniques?
2.  How does the choice of the noise distribution (e.g., variance, type) affect the performance of the proposed approach? Can you provide guidelines for selecting appropriate parameters?
3.  The paper extensively discusses the limitations of KL and JSD divergences. Have you explored alternative divergences (e.g., Maximum Mean Discrepancy) in the context of this theoretical framework?
4.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of the connection between the proposed noise addition scheme and the Wasserstein distance? How does minimizing the Wasserstein distance translate to improved GAN training stability?
5.  Given the theoretical focus on low-dimensional manifolds, how does the method perform on datasets with high-dimensional, complex data distributions where the manifold assumption is less valid?
