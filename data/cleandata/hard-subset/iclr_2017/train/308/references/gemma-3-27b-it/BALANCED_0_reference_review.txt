This paper tackles the fundamental problem of instability in Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training. The authors argue that this instability stems from theoretical properties of the commonly used cost functions (KL divergence, JSD) and the inherent low-dimensionality of the generated data’s support. They provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating perfect discrimination when distributions have disjoint supports or lie on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates. Furthermore, they propose a novel approach involving adding noise to smooth the distributions, offering a potential path towards more stable training and proposing a Wasserstein distance-based metric for evaluating progress.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper identifies a crucial problem and offers insightful theoretical analysis, its practical implications and proposed solution require significant further development and empirical validation. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the paper feels incomplete in bridging the gap between theory and practice.

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous theoretical treatment of GAN training dynamics. The analysis of perfect discrimination under specific conditions, and the consequences for gradient behavior, is a significant contribution to understanding why GANs are so difficult to train. The identification of the issues with both the original and alternative cost functions is also insightful.  The introduction of a noise-based approach and the justification for utilizing the Wasserstein distance are promising directions.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the connection between the theoretical findings and practical GAN training is not sufficiently elaborated. While the authors demonstrate theoretically why gradients vanish or become unstable, a more detailed discussion of how this manifests in practice with common architectures (e.g., DCGANs) is needed.  Secondly, the proposed noise-based approach, while theoretically motivated, lacks substantial empirical validation. The paper alludes to experimentation (e.g., Figure 3), but lacks concrete results demonstrating the effectiveness of adding noise in stabilizing training and improving sample quality across a range of datasets and architectures.  A comparative study against established GAN stabilization techniques is crucial. Thirdly, the mathematical formalism is dense and could be made more accessible. While rigor is appreciated, some explanations are terse and assume a high level of prior knowledge.  Finally, the manuscript contains some repetition, especially in sections 2.1 and 2.2, which could be streamlined.

Here are additional suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Empirical Validation:** Conduct comprehensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed noise-based approach. Compare its performance against existing GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) on standard datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet). Quantify improvements in training stability (e.g., reduced oscillations in loss curves) and sample quality (e.g., using Inception Score or FID).
*   **Architecture Specificity:** Analyze how the theoretical findings manifest in specific GAN architectures like DCGANs. Discuss how the dimensionality of the latent space and the network depth affect the observed instability.
*   **Noise Schedule:** Investigate different noise schedules (e.g., linearly decreasing noise variance) and their impact on training stability and sample quality.
*   **Wasserstein Distance Implementation:** Provide details on how the Wasserstein distance is calculated in practice and used to guide training.
*   **Accessibility:**  Reword some of the more technical passages to improve clarity and accessibility for a wider audience.  Consider adding intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical derivations.
*   **Streamline Repetition:**  Consolidate redundant sections (e.g., 2.1 and 2.2) to improve the flow and conciseness of the paper.
*   **Clarify Assumptions:** Explicitly state the assumptions made in the proofs and discuss their limitations.
*   **Appendix Material:** Expand the appendix to include more detailed proofs and additional experimental results.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of noise distribution (Gaussian in this case) and justify its properties for smoothing the distributions? Are there other noise distributions that might be more effective?
2.  The paper mentions that the optimal discriminator becomes ‘perfect’ under certain conditions. What happens if we intentionally regularize the discriminator to prevent it from becoming too perfect, and how does that impact the generator's training?
3.  How does the proposed noise-based approach interact with other common GAN stabilization techniques, such as gradient penalty or spectral normalization? Can they be combined, and if so, what is the optimal way to do so?
4.  Could you provide a more concrete explanation of how the Wasserstein distance is used to evaluate the progress of training? Is it computed directly, or is it used as a surrogate for another metric?
5.  The proofs are mathematically rigorous but dense. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of Lemma 1, particularly regarding the dimension reduction properties of neural networks?
