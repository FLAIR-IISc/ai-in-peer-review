This paper tackles the fundamental problem of instability in Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training, arguing that current approaches are largely heuristic and lack a solid theoretical grounding. The authors aim to provide a theoretical understanding of these issues and propose principled research directions to address them. They begin by analyzing the divergences used in GAN training – KL divergence and Jensen-Shannon Divergence – and highlight the asymmetrical weighting of errors, leading to mode dropping. They then delve into the mathematical conditions under which perfect discrimination is possible, demonstrating how this leads to vanishing gradients or unstable updates depending on the cost function used. Finally, they propose leveraging noise and the Wasserstein metric as a way to mitigate these problems.

**Reject**. While the paper raises important and fundamental questions about GAN training, and develops some interesting theoretical insights, the work feels incomplete and doesn’t deliver a concrete, readily applicable solution. The presented theoretical analysis, while mathematically sound, primarily *explains* the problems rather than providing a fully realized path towards solving them. The proposed approach using noise and the Wasserstein metric, while promising, is presented at a high level and lacks a comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating its superiority over existing stabilization techniques.

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous mathematical analysis. The authors correctly identify the issues with the KL divergence and the potential for perfect discrimination when distributions have low-dimensional supports. The theorems and lemmas are well-defined and the proofs seem logical (though a deeper verification would require expert knowledge in the field). The connection to existing issues, like mode collapse and vanishing gradients, is clearly articulated. The exploration of alternative metrics like the Wasserstein distance is also a valuable contribution.

However, the weaknesses are substantial. The numerous theorems and lemmas, while individually interesting, create a somewhat dense and overwhelming narrative. More importantly, the proposed solution feels underdeveloped. Simply stating the benefits of adding noise and utilizing the Wasserstein metric isn’t sufficient. A strong empirical evaluation comparing this approach to established GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) is missing. The practical implications of choosing the noise distribution and the computational cost of using the Wasserstein distance are not discussed in detail. The conclusion primarily restates open questions rather than presenting a clear path forward. Finally, while the discussion of adversarial examples is insightful, it feels somewhat tangential to the core argument. 

**Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper:**

*   **Empirical Validation:** A significant addition would be a thorough empirical evaluation. Implement the proposed noise injection and Wasserstein distance-based training on standard GAN benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) and compare performance (FID score, Inception Score, sample quality) with existing state-of-the-art GAN training methods.
*   **Practical Considerations:** Discuss the practical challenges associated with implementing the proposed approach. What is a suitable choice for the noise distribution and its parameters? How does the computational cost of evaluating the Wasserstein distance compare to other methods?
*   **Clarify the Connection to Existing Work:** Explicitly position the work within the broader landscape of GAN stabilization techniques. How does the proposed approach complement or differ from methods like spectral normalization, gradient penalty, and architectural modifications?
*   **Streamline the Presentation:** The paper is very dense with mathematical notation. Consider simplifying the presentation and focusing on the most crucial insights. Perhaps deferring some of the detailed proofs to the appendix would improve readability.
*   **Clarify the Role of Continuous vs. Absolute Continuity:** The distinction between continuous and absolutely continuous random variables is important, but it's discussed in an appendix. It needs to be more prominently featured in the main text, especially as it's central to the argument.
*   **Elaborate on the choice of noise:** The paper uses a Gaussian noise assumption. Is this optimal? Why? How does the variance of the noise affect performance?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific choices of noise distribution and parameters (e.g., variance) have you explored, and how do these choices affect the performance of the proposed approach?
*   How does the computational cost of evaluating the Wasserstein distance (or an approximation thereof) compare to existing GAN training methods, and how can this cost be mitigated?
*   Have you considered alternative divergence measures beyond the Wasserstein distance?
*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why adding noise leads to more stable training, beyond the mathematical arguments presented?
*   The paper mentions potential connections to contrastive divergence. Could you elaborate on this connection and how the proposed method relates to contrastive learning?
