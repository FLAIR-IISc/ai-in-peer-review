This paper tackles the notoriously unstable training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), aiming to provide a theoretical understanding of the issues and propose principled directions for improvement. The core argument revolves around the observation that the supports of the generator and real data distributions often lie on low-dimensional manifolds, leading to perfect discriminators and subsequently, vanishing or unstable gradients during training. The authors demonstrate this mathematically and propose a novel approach based on adding noise and considering Wasserstein distance to mitigate these problems.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a genuinely important problem and offers a compelling theoretical analysis, the proposed solution feels incomplete and lacks sufficient empirical validation to warrant acceptance at this stage. The mathematical arguments are strong, but their practical implications aren’t fully explored.

The paper's strength lies in its insightful theoretical analysis. The demonstration that perfect discrimination arises when distributions have low-dimensional supports is a significant contribution, offering a clear explanation for the instability observed in GAN training. The detailed examination of the cost functions and their associated gradient behavior is also well-executed and elucidates why standard GAN training can fail. The introduction of noise and the shift towards Wasserstein distance are reasonable approaches suggested by the theory. 

However, the paper feels somewhat disconnected between theory and practice. While the theoretical framework is developed extensively, the empirical evaluation is limited. Figure 1 and 2 are illustrative but not conclusive. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing the proposed method to state-of-the-art GAN training techniques on several datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) is crucial. The authors mention that the proposed method avoids adversarial examples but don’t provide evidence. The claim that noisy samples will stabilize gradients, requiring less hyperparameter tuning, also lacks supporting evidence. The discussion regarding the Wasserstein distance feels underdeveloped; the connection between the theoretical bounds and actual performance improvements isn't clearly established.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct thorough experiments on standard datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Compare performance to baseline GANs trained with established stabilization techniques (e.g., WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization).
*   **Clarify Practical Implementation:** Provide more details on how to effectively implement the proposed noise injection and Wasserstein distance-based training in practice. What is the optimal noise distribution and scale? How does this affect computational cost?
*   **Strengthen Connection to Existing Work:** More explicitly relate the findings to existing literature on GAN training stability and adversarial robustness. Acknowledge the limitations of the proposed method and discuss potential avenues for further research.
*   **Address the vanishing gradient issue**: Although you claim that the proposed approach avoids vanishing gradients, theorem 2.4 shows that it’s inevitable if the discriminator gets better. Provide a detailed explanation as to why the proposed method can avoid this.

Questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of noise distribution and its impact on the theoretical bounds and empirical performance?
*   How does the computational cost of the proposed method compare to standard GAN training techniques?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the proposed method to the level of noise injected?
*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the Wasserstein distance mitigates the adversarial example problem?
*   Theorem 2.4 states that vanishing gradients are inevitable. Can you provide a more nuanced explanation of how the proposed method avoids it?
