- Paper identifies GAN instability as arising from generator and real-data distributions lying on low-dimensional manifolds, causing perfect discrimination and vanishing/unstable gradients.  
- Theoretical analysis explains why original GAN and -logD objectives lead to these instability issues.  
- Proposes noise injection plus Wasserstein distance as a principled approach to avoid perfect discrimination and stabilize training.  
- Provides rigorous theorems linking perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients, and manifold structure.  
- Frames GAN instability within manifold learning, shifting focus from heuristics to theory.  
- Argues that Wasserstein distance between noisy distributions relates compellingly to KL divergence and yields more stable gradients.  
- Thorough literature review situates the work within generative modeling research.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written despite mathematical complexity.  
- Lacks substantial empirical validation on standard GAN benchmarks and comparisons to stabilization methods like WGAN-GP or spectral normalization.  
- Needs practical guidance and ablation studies on choice and variance of injected noise (e.g., Gaussian σ²) and whether annealing is required.  
- Should address potential increased computational cost from noise injection and Wasserstein computations and suggest optimization strategies.  
- Clarify the abstract notion of “perfect alignment” with simple intuitive examples.  
- Expand discussion on the choice of the Sobolev norm ‖D‖ and its relation to universal approximation.  
- Questions for authors: challenges for empirical evaluation and target datasets/architectures, how to select/anneal noise variance, exact computational overhead vs. original GAN, impact on mode collapse, and relation to recent divergence/metric-based GAN work.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on added empirical results and clarification of practical considerations.
