**Summary of Contributions**

The paper attempts to provide a rigorous theoretical explanation for the widely observed instabilities in Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training. The authors argue that these instabilities arise because the data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg) often have supports on low-dimensional manifolds, which are likely disjoint. They prove that under such conditions, a perfect discriminator exists whose gradients are zero almost everywhere, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator. The paper then analyzes the popular `-log(D)` alternative cost function, showing it optimizes a combination of KL and JSD divergences and can lead to unstable updates with infinite variance under certain noise assumptions. As a solution, the authors propose adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator, which smoothens the distributions. They connect this approach to minimizing the Wasserstein distance between the original (noiseless) distributions, providing a novel theoretical bound (Theorem 3.3) that links this distance to the JSD of the smoothed distributions.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper addresses a crucial problem and presents several potentially insightful ideas, I must recommend rejection. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  There are critical technical inaccuracies in the paper's main theoretical result (Theorem 3.3) concerning the Wasserstein distance, which undermines the core justification for the proposed method.
2.  The manuscript is marred by an extensive number of typographical errors, formatting inconsistencies, imprecise statements, and a corrupted bibliography. This pervasive lack of care and rigor falls significantly below the publication standards for ICLR and erodes confidence in the correctness of the work as a whole.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's ambition is commendable, but the execution is deeply flawed.

**1. Critical Technical Errors:**
The theoretical foundation of the proposed solution in Section 3 is unsound.
*   **Incorrect Definition of Wasserstein Metric:** Definition 3.1 defines the Wasserstein metric `W(P,Q)` using an integrand of `‖x− y‖^2`. This is the definition of the squared 2-Wasserstein distance, `W_2(P,Q)^2`, not `W_1` or `W_2`. This is a fundamental error.
*   **Flawed Proof of Theorem 3.3:** The proof of the paper's capstone result, which bounds `W(Pr, Pg)`, is incorrect as it improperly mixes properties of different Wasserstein metrics.
    *   The inequality `W(P,Q) ≤ Cδ(P,Q)` arises from the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality, which applies specifically to the 1-Wasserstein distance (`W_1`), not the metric defined in the paper.
    *   The triangle inequality is applied, but it is applied to the incorrectly defined metric.
    *   The application of Pinsker's inequality appears to have incorrect constants. The inequality is `δ(P,Q) <= sqrt(0.5 * KL(P||Q))`. The authors seem to have omitted the `0.5` factor.
    *   These combined errors render the proof invalid and call into question the central claim that their proposed objective minimizes a valid upper bound on the Wasserstein distance.

**2. Lack of Rigor and Professionalism:**
The manuscript is replete with issues that, in aggregate, make it impossible to trust the results.
*   **Dismissive Proofs:** The proof for Theorem 2.3 is stated to be "trivial and left as an exercise to the reader." This is unacceptable in a formal publication. Important results, even if standard, must be proven or cited appropriately for completeness and verifiability.
*   **Unjustified Assumptions:** The analysis in Theorem 2.6 relies on a highly specific and unrealistic assumption that the error between the learned and optimal discriminator is a "centered Gaussian process indexed by x and independent for every x (popularly known as white noise)." This assumption is not justified, and the significant technical details required to make this model coherent are relegated to a hand-wavy footnote (Footnote 4).
*   **Pervasive Typographical and Grammatical Errors:** The paper is littered with errors, such as "adressing," "symetrical," "arize," "imposible," "arbitrarilly," "reallistically," "inacurate," "prescence," "corolary," and "luckilly." While minor individually, their sheer volume suggests a hastily prepared and un-proofread manuscript.
*   **Corrupted Bibliography:** The reference section is unusable. Entries like `Bottou et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Bottou et al\.,` are nonsensical. Furthermore, a key citation, `Huszar (2016)`, used in the proof of Theorem 2.5, is missing from the list entirely. This prevents basic verification of the paper's claims and placement within the literature.
*   **Imprecise and Ambiguous Notation:** The notation is frequently confusing. For instance, in the proof of Theorem 2.2, a ball is denoted `B(x, x)`, where `x` is a point, not a scalar radius. In Theorem 2.4, the norm of a Jacobian is written as `‖...‖22`, which is non-standard.

**Additional Feedback**

The core idea—that GAN instability is rooted in the geometry of the support of the distributions and can be alleviated by noise injection—is valuable and intuitive. The attempt to connect this heuristic to a principled metric like the Wasserstein distance is the most promising aspect of this work. For the paper to be publishable, a complete and thorough revision is required.

*   First and foremost, the authors must correct the definitions and proofs in Section 3. They should decide which Wasserstein metric (`W_1` or `W_2`) they intend to use, define it correctly, and build a valid proof for Theorem 3.3 using the appropriate supporting lemmas (e.g., Kantorovich-Rubinstein for `W_1`, different bounds for `W_2`).
*   The authors must provide complete proofs for all theorems, including Theorem 2.3.
*   The entire manuscript needs to be meticulously proofread to correct all typographical, grammatical, and notational errors.
*   The bibliography must be completely reconstructed and properly formatted. All citations must be present and correct.
*   The authors should consider softening the language around the noise model in Theorem 2.6, acknowledging it as a simplifying assumption and discussing its limitations.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify whether you intended to work with the 1-Wasserstein or 2-Wasserstein distance? Could you provide a corrected proof of Theorem 3.3 using the appropriate definition and associated inequalities?
2.  The proof of Theorem 2.3 was omitted. Could you please provide it in the appendix?
3.  The construction in the proof of Theorem 2.2 defines `M̂ = ⋃ B(x, x/3)`. The radius `x/3` appears to be a typo where the radius depends on the point `x`. Could you clarify this notation and the subsequent construction of the discriminator `D*`, which is claimed to be smooth but defined piecewise?
