### Review

**Summary:**
This paper presents a theoretical investigation into the instability of Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training. The authors argue that the core problem lies in the nature of the data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg), which are hypothesized to have supports on low-dimensional manifolds. This property allows for the existence of a "perfect" discriminator that can separate the two distributions with high accuracy. The paper demonstrates that such a perfect discriminator leads to vanishing gradients for the generator under the original GAN objective and unstable, high-variance gradients under the commonly used `-log D` alternative. To address this, the authors propose adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator, which smooths the distributions and guarantees well-behaved gradients. The paper provides a theoretical justification for this approach by linking the minimization of the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) between the noisy distributions to an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original, noiseless distributions.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1.  The proposed solution—adding instance noise to discriminator inputs—is a known heuristic. The paper's primary contribution is a novel theoretical framework to justify this heuristic, connecting it to the Wasserstein distance. However, the proposed method is indirect, optimizing an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance rather than the metric itself. This makes the approach an unproven and indirect method framed as a principled solution.
2.  The paper is entirely theoretical and lacks any empirical validation. It makes strong claims about solving the practical instabilities of GAN training but provides no experiments to support these claims. Without a comparison to established GAN training methods and stabilization techniques on standard benchmarks, the practical significance and effectiveness of the proposed approach remain purely speculative.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The theoretical analysis in Section 2 is the most compelling part of the manuscript. The formalization of the data and generator distributions as lying on low-dimensional manifolds provides a clear and intuitive explanation for why the discriminator can become too powerful, leading to vanishing or exploding gradients. Theorem 2.4 (vanishing gradients) and Theorem 2.6 (instability from infinite variance gradients) offer sound theoretical underpinnings for widely observed empirical phenomena. This analysis is a valuable contribution to the community's understanding of GANs.

However, the paper falters in its transition from diagnosis to prescription in Section 3. The proposed solution is to add noise to the inputs, thereby making the distributions Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous and ensuring the JSD is well-behaved. While the derivation in Corollary 3.2 shows that this yields smooth gradients, the method itself is not novel and has been used heuristically.

The main theoretical novelty is Theorem 3.3, which connects this noise-based training to the Wasserstein distance. This is an interesting result, but it serves only as a justification for an existing technique. The theorem provides an upper bound, `W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2V^1/2 + 2C * sqrt(JSD(Pr+, Pg+))`, which is then minimized by training a standard GAN on noisy data. This is a very indirect way to optimize the Wasserstein distance. A more methodologically sound contribution would be to propose an algorithm that directly minimizes `W(Pr,Pg)`. As it stands, the paper provides a complex justification for a simple, pre-existing heuristic.

Crucially, the paper presents no empirical evidence. A theoretical paper that claims to solve a practical problem must include experiments to validate its theory. The authors should have demonstrated that their proposed method leads to more stable training and produces higher-quality samples compared to established baselines (e.g., a well-tuned DCGAN). Questions about the sensitivity to the noise schedule, the final sample quality (e.g., measured by FID or Inception Score), and the prevention of mode collapse are critical but are left completely unanswered. The absence of an experimental section makes it impossible to assess whether the theoretical insights translate into any practical benefit.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors:**
The following points are intended to help improve the manuscript.
*   The theoretical analysis of the problem is strong. This portion of the paper should be highlighted as its core contribution.
*   The connection to Wasserstein distance is the most innovative part of the proposal. I would encourage the authors to consider whether there is a way to optimize this metric more directly, perhaps by leveraging the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality, which might lead to a more principled and novel algorithm.
*   The assumption in Theorem 2.6 that the error terms for the discriminator's output and its gradient are independent white noise is a strong simplification. Real-world neural network approximators will have structured, correlated errors. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the limitations of this assumption and how it might affect the conclusion that the gradients follow a Cauchy distribution.
*   To be considered for publication, the paper requires an extensive experimental section. This should include, at a minimum:
    *   A comparison against a standard GAN baseline (e.g., DCGAN) on datasets like CIFAR-10 and CelebA. The comparison should focus on training stability (plots of generator and discriminator loss over time) and final sample quality.
    *   An ablation study on the effect of the noise variance `V` and the annealing schedule. How critical is the choice of schedule to achieving stable training?

**Clarifying Questions:**
1.  Theorem 3.3 provides an upper bound on `W(Pr,Pg)`. How tight is this bound expected to be in practice? Is there a risk that minimizing the bound does not correspond to a significant reduction in the actual Wasserstein distance?
2.  Your framework provides a new lens for analyzing GAN training. How does it explain the success of other widely-used stabilization techniques, such as feature matching, minibatch discrimination, or label smoothing? Does your theory suggest these are also implicitly optimizing a "softer" metric?
