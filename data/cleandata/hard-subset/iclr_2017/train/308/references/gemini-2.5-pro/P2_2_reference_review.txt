### Summary

This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training. It rigorously identifies a fundamental reason for the widely-observed instability in GANs: the fact that the real data distribution and the generator's distribution often have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which are disjoint or intersect on a set of measure zero. The authors prove that under these conditions, a perfect discriminator exists whose gradients are zero almost everywhere, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator. They analyze the two most common GAN cost functions, proving that the original `log(1-D)` objective suffers from vanishing gradients, while the alternative `-log(D)` objective leads to updates with infinite variance, explaining its unstable behavior.

Building on this analysis, the paper proposes a solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator and to the generated samples. This effectively smoothes the distributions, making them absolutely continuous and ensuring their supports overlap. The authors show that this leads to well-behaved, informative gradients. Most importantly, they connect this practical solution to a more principled objective by introducing the Wasserstein distance as a more suitable metric for comparing distributions on low-dimensional manifolds. The main theoretical result (Theorem 3.3) provides an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between the true distributions in terms of the noise variance and the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) of the noisy distributions, justifying the proposed training procedure as a principled way to minimize a more sensible distance measure.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Strong Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Fundamental Theoretical Insight:** The paper offers the first rigorous and compelling theoretical explanation for the instability of GAN training. By formalizing the intuition that data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1) and proving the consequences—the existence of a perfect, uninformative discriminator (Theorems 2.1, 2.2) and the resulting pathological gradients (Theorems 2.4, 2.6)—the work moves the field from a collection of heuristics to a principled understanding of the core problem.

2.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Solution:** The proposed method is not just another heuristic. The connection established between adding noise, stabilizing the JSD objective, and minimizing an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) is novel and powerful. It introduces a more appropriate geometric distance for evaluating generative models and provides a clear theoretical pathway to optimizing it.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's claims are exceptionally well-supported by rigorous mathematical arguments. The line of reasoning is clear and builds logically from problem identification to a justified solution.

The analysis in Section 2 is the paper's cornerstone. The proofs that a perfect discriminator exists and that its gradients vanish are critical. This cleanly explains why, in practice, one cannot simply train the discriminator to optimality before updating the generator. The subsequent analysis of the two standard cost functions is particularly insightful. Theorem 2.4 (vanishing gradients for `log(1-D)`) and Theorem 2.6 (infinite variance for `-log(D)`) are not just theoretical curiosities; they precisely describe the failure modes that practitioners have struggled with for years.

Section 3 effectively leverages this analysis to build a solution. The introduction of the Wasserstein distance is very well-motivated. The authors correctly point out that standard divergences like KL and JSD are ill-suited for distributions on non-overlapping manifolds (Theorem 2.3), whereas the Wasserstein distance provides a sensible measure of how "far apart" these manifolds are. Theorem 3.3 is the climax of the paper, elegantly tying the practical algorithm (training a GAN on noisy inputs) to the theoretical goal (minimizing the Wasserstein distance between the true distributions). This provides a solid foundation for future research and a clear recipe for achieving more stable training.

The clarity of the proofs and the depth of the mathematical machinery used (e.g., leveraging Sard's Lemma, Urysohn's Lemma, and properties of transversal intersections) demonstrate a high level of scientific rigor. The paper doesn't just identify a problem; it provides the tools to analyze it and a principled direction to solve it.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an outstanding theoretical paper, its presentation could be strengthened in a few areas to maximize its impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my strong recommendation for acceptance.

1.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper is almost entirely theoretical. While it alludes to figures showing empirical verification of vanishing and exploding gradients, the core claims would be immensely strengthened by a small set of experiments. For instance, demonstrating that a GAN trained with the proposed noise-injection method is more stable than a standard DCGAN on a simple dataset would provide powerful empirical support for the theory.

2.  **Practical Guidance:** Theorem 3.3 suggests a training strategy: minimize JSD on noisy distributions while annealing the noise variance `V`. However, the paper offers no practical guidance on this annealing. A discussion of potential schedules (e.g., fixed noise, exponential decay) or an ablation study showing the effect of the noise magnitude would make the method more accessible to practitioners.

3.  **Future Work Exposition:** The introduction of the Wasserstein distance is a major contribution. The paper could more explicitly frame the future research direction it opens up: instead of optimizing an *upper bound* on `W(Pr, Pg)`, could one optimize `W(Pr, Pg)` *directly*? This would be a natural and exciting follow-up, and briefly mentioning this possibility (perhaps by referencing the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality) could further highlight the significance of this new perspective.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide the empirical results referenced in the text (Figures 1, 2, 3)? Specifically, how were these experiments conducted, and how do they demonstrate the gradient pathologies predicted by Theorems 2.4 and 2.6?

2.  Have you performed any experiments training a full generative model using the proposed objective of minimizing `JSD(Pr+ || Pg+)`? If so, how does its stability and final sample quality compare to established baselines?

3.  The proof of Theorem 2.6 relies on a model of independent Gaussian noise (white noise) for the discriminator's output and gradients. How do you expect the results to change under more structured or correlated noise, which might be more representative of the errors from a finite-capacity network trained with SGD?
