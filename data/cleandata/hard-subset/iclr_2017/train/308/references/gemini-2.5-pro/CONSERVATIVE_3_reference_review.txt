### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper presents a theoretical investigation into the sources of instability commonly observed when training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors' primary contribution is a rigorous argument that the data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg) often have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which are disjoint with high probability. They prove that under these conditions, a perfect discriminator exists, leading to gradients that either vanish (for the original GAN objective) or become pathologically unstable (for the `-log(D)` alternative). This provides a formal explanation for well-known empirical difficulties.

Building on this diagnosis, the paper critiques the use of traditional divergences like Jensen-Shannon (JSD) and Kullback-Leibler (KL) in this setting, as they fail to provide a useful measure of "distance" when supports are disjoint. The authors then propose a new direction for stabilizing training by adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. They show that this approach effectively smooths the distributions, making the JSD meaningful again. Finally, they introduce the Wasserstein distance as a more suitable metric for measuring the distance between distributions on low-dimensional manifolds and provide a theorem that bounds this distance by the JSD of the noise-perturbed distributions, thus providing a theoretical justification for their proposed method.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a compelling theoretical diagnosis of a known problem but fails to substantiate its proposed solution with empirical evidence. The claims of improved stability and a principled research direction are not supported by experiments comparing the proposed method against established and well-tuned GAN baselines (e.g., DCGAN with heuristics from Salimans et al., 2016).
2.  **The Proposed Solution is Not Fully Developed:** The paper proposes moving "towards softer metrics" by adding noise, which is justified post-hoc by a bound involving the Wasserstein distance. However, the method actually optimized is still the JSD between two noise-perturbed distributions. This approach is indirect and introduces new, unanalyzed practical challenges, such as the selection and annealing of the noise variance, which feels like replacing one set of heuristics with another.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The theoretical analysis in Section 2 is the strongest part of the paper. The proofs showing that the supports of Pr and Pg likely lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1) and that this guarantees the existence of a perfect discriminator with vanishing gradients (Theorems 2.1, 2.2, 2.4) are insightful. This provides a valuable formalization of phenomena that practitioners have long observed.

However, a paper that proposes a new method or direction to solve a practical problem must demonstrate its efficacy. Section 3 introduces the idea of adding noise to the discriminator's inputs. While this is not an entirely new idea in machine learning, the theoretical motivation provided here is novel. Yet, the paper stops short of providing a concrete algorithm and, critically, any experimental results. Without experiments, it is impossible to assess whether this method actually solves the instability problem in practice. Does it produce better samples? Is it more stable across different datasets and architectures? Does it train faster or more reliably than existing methods that rely on carefully tuned heuristics? The paper claims to provide a "principled research direction," but without any evidence of its success, it remains a purely theoretical conjecture. Established methods, despite their heuristic nature, have been shown to work on complex datasets. A new proposal must be held to the same standard.

Furthermore, the connection to the Wasserstein distance, while elegant, feels somewhat tacked on. The core proposal is to add noise and optimize `JSD(Pr+ || Pg+)`. Theorem 3.3 shows that this objective serves as an upper bound on `W(Pr, Pg)`. This is an indirect optimization at best. The practical implications of this are unclear. The bound depends on the noise variance `V`, suggesting an annealing schedule is needed to tighten the bound. This introduces a new critical hyperparameter (`V` and its schedule) that may be just as difficult to tune as the generator-discriminator balance the paper seeks to remedy. The paper critiques existing methods as "heuristics that are extremely sensitive," but it is not clear that the proposed approach is any less so.

In its current form, the paper is a strong theoretical analysis of a problem followed by an unproven and underdeveloped proposal for a solution. While the analysis is valuable, the lack of empirical support for the proposed method makes the paper's central claims of providing a viable alternative unsubstantiated.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The theoretical analysis in Section 2 is of high quality and could potentially stand on its own as a contribution explaining GAN failure modes. The authors should consider highlighting this as their primary contribution if the experimental side is not ready.
*   To support the claims made in Section 3, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including experiments, even on toy datasets (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians), to clearly demonstrate the stabilized gradient behavior compared to standard GANs.
*   The ultimate test would be a comparison against a strong baseline like DCGAN on a standard image dataset (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA). The authors should demonstrate that their method provides tangible benefits in terms of training stability or final sample quality.
*   The discussion of the Wasserstein distance is intriguing. A more powerful approach might involve developing an algorithm that more directly optimizes or approximates the Wasserstein distance, rather than optimizing an upper bound. This could lead to a more principled and potentially more effective method.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide any empirical results, even preliminary ones, that demonstrate the stability benefits of adding noise to the discriminator inputs as proposed? Specifically, how does the norm of the generator gradient behave during training compared to the standard `-log(D)` objective shown in your Figure 3?
2.  The effectiveness of your method seems to rely on the choice of noise distribution and its variance, `V`. How is this hyperparameter selected? Is an annealing schedule for `V` required, and if so, how sensitive is the model's performance to the specifics of this schedule?
3.  You argue that your method avoids the difficult balancing act of discriminator and generator training. Does this mean the discriminator can be trained to optimality at every step without causing issues for the generator? Have you tested this empirically?
4.  The objective you minimize is `JSD(Pr+ || Pg+)`. Given that this is an upper bound on `W(Pr, Pg)`, what is the justification for believing this is a better proxy for minimizing `W(Pr, Pg)` than other potential objectives?
