### Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution towards understanding the difficulties encountered when training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors claim to identify the root causes of training instability and propose a principled direction for alleviating these issues.

The core of their argument is that both the real data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg) have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds within the high-dimensional sample space. The paper rigorously proves that under this condition, the supports are almost certainly disjoint. This leads to the existence of a "perfect" discriminator that can separate real from generated samples with 100% accuracy.

The authors then analyze the consequences of such a perfect discriminator on the generator's gradients for the two most common GAN cost functions:
1.  For the original `log(1-D)` cost, they prove that as the discriminator approaches optimality, the generator's gradients vanish, providing no useful signal for learning.
2.  For the popular `-log(D)` alternative, they show that this gradient step corresponds to minimizing a combination of KL(Pg||Pr) and JSD(Pg||Pr). More critically, they prove that with a near-optimal discriminator, the generator's gradients exhibit extremely high variance, modeling them as a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance and expectation. This provides a formal explanation for the widely observed unstable behavior of GAN training.

Finally, the paper proposes a solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This effectively "smears" the low-dimensional manifolds, creating two distributions with overlapping support. The authors show that training a GAN on these noisy distributions provides smooth, well-behaved gradients. They connect this approach to a more suitable metric for comparing distributions, the Wasserstein distance, by proving that minimizing the JSD between the noisy distributions minimizes an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original, non-noisy distributions.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Rigorous Theoretical Explanation:** The paper provides the first principled theoretical explanation for the widely observed instability in GAN training. It moves the field beyond heuristic-based fixes by identifying the underlying mathematical reasons (disjoint low-dimensional supports) for issues like vanishing or exploding gradients.
2.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Solution:** The proposed solution of adding instance noise is not an ad-hoc trick but a direct consequence of the theoretical analysis. The connection established between this practical solution and the minimization of the more appropriate Wasserstein distance provides a strong theoretical foundation for future research in GANs.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength is its rigorous diagnosis of the GAN training problem. The formalization of the data and generator distributions as lying on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1) is a critical and well-justified starting point. The subsequent theorems (2.1 and 2.2) demonstrating the existence of a perfect discriminator whose gradient is zero almost everywhere on these manifolds are compelling. This insight alone is a major contribution, as it reveals that standard divergences like JSD and KL are fundamentally ill-suited for measuring progress when the distribution supports do not substantially overlap, a condition the paper argues is the norm.

The analysis in Section 2.2 is the highlight of the paper. Theorem 2.4 provides a clear proof for the "vanishing gradients" problem with the original GAN formulation, explaining why training must be delicately balanced to prevent the discriminator from becoming too strong. Even more impressive is the analysis of the `-log(D)` objective. Theorem 2.6, which shows that the gradients can follow a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance, is a powerful result that provides a concrete mathematical explanation for the chaotic and unstable updates practitioners often observe. This shifts the understanding of GAN instability from a vague notion of "mode collapse" or "oscillations" to a well-defined problem of ill-behaved gradients.

The proposed solution in Section 3 is elegant and directly motivated by the diagnosis. By adding noise, the core assumption of disjoint supports is broken, allowing for a well-defined JSD and, consequently, meaningful gradients. The final piece of the argument, Theorem 3.3, is crucial. It connects the practical fix (training on noisy samples) to a more robust theoretical concept (the Wasserstein distance), which is known to be a more sensible metric for comparing distributions with disjoint supports. This provides a clear research direction: by minimizing the JSD of noisy distributions, we are making progress on minimizing the Wasserstein distance of the true distributions.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

1.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper is overwhelmingly theoretical. While the theory is strong and the arguments are persuasive, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including empirical results that validate the proposed method. For example, demonstrating that a GAN trained with the proposed instance noise is more stable (e.g., has lower gradient variance) and achieves better results (e.g., lower FID scores, though FID didn't exist at the time, perhaps better Inception Scores or qualitative results) than a baseline DCGAN would provide powerful evidence for the claims. The paper shows the *problem* in Figure 3 but doesn't show the *solution* in action.

2.  **Practical Guidance:** The paper introduces the concept of adding noise with variance `V` and annealing it. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion on the practical implications of this. How should one choose the initial `V`? What is a reasonable annealing schedule? Does the type of noise (e.g., Gaussian vs. uniform) matter significantly? While a full exploration is likely beyond the scope, a short discussion would improve the paper's utility for practitioners.

3.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is mathematically dense, leveraging concepts from measure theory and differential geometry. While this is necessary for the proofs, the main text could benefit from building more intuition. For instance, a more intuitive explanation of transversality and why it's a reasonable assumption could help readers less familiar with the topic. The appendix is helpful, but integrating some of these clarifications into the main body could broaden the paper's impact.

### Questions for Authors

1.  Your theoretical results are very compelling. Could you provide some empirical evidence showing that training with instance noise, as proposed in Section 3, leads to more stable training dynamics (e.g., gradient norms) and improved sample quality compared to a standard GAN baseline?

2.  Theorem 3.3 provides a bound on the Wasserstein distance that depends on the noise variance `V`. This suggests a trade-off: higher `V` might make the JSD easier to optimize but loosens the bound. How do you envision setting and annealing `V` in practice to effectively minimize `W(Pr, Pg)`?

3.  The proof of Theorem 2.6 models the error in the discriminator and its gradient as independent white noise processes. How critical is this assumption to the conclusion that the updates follow a Cauchy distribution? Would more realistic, correlated noise still lead to similarly unstable updates with very high variance?
