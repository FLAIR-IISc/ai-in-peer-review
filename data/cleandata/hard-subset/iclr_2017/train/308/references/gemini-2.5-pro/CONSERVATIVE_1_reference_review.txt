### Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a theoretical investigation into the sources of instability commonly observed when training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors argue that the standard divergences optimized by GANs, such as the Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD), are ill-suited when the data distribution (Pr) and the generator distribution (Pg) have supports that are disjoint or lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The paper proves that in such common scenarios, an optimal discriminator can exist that perfectly separates the two distributions, leading to vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator.

Specifically, the paper demonstrates that for the original GAN cost function, the generator's gradients vanish as the discriminator improves. For the alternative `-log D` cost function, the paper shows that the updates can be modeled as samples from a Cauchy distribution, which has infinite variance and expectation, explaining the observed instability.

As a remedy, the authors propose adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This effectively smooths the distributions Pr and Pg, ensuring their supports overlap and making the JSD a more sensible training objective. The paper formally proves that this procedure provides smooth, non-vanishing gradients that intuitively push the generator's samples towards the data manifold. Finally, the authors introduce the Wasserstein distance as a more suitable metric for measuring the distance between distributions on low-dimensional manifolds and provide a key theorem (Theorem 3.3) that bounds the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions (Pr, Pg) by a function of the JSD between the noise-perturbed distributions (Pr+, Pg+). This provides a principled motivation for the instance noise technique as a means of minimizing a more robust distance metric.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The core practical recommendation of the paper—adding noise to discriminator inputs—is a known heuristic. While the paper provides a novel and insightful theoretical justification for it, it does not present a new, functional algorithm.
2.  The paper lacks any empirical validation. The theoretical claims, while compelling, are not supported by experiments that demonstrate improved stability or sample quality over established GAN training methods. The introduction of the Wasserstein distance is theoretically interesting, but no algorithm to directly minimize it is proposed or tested.

### Supporting Arguments

The main strength of this paper is its theoretical diagnosis of GAN training pathologies. The analysis in Section 2, which proves the existence of a perfect discriminator when supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds and connects this to vanishing (Theorem 2.4) and unstable (Theorem 2.6) gradients, is a significant contribution to our understanding of GANs.

However, the paper falls short in translating these theoretical insights into a novel, validated method. The proposed solution is to add noise to the inputs of the discriminator. While the connection to smoothing the underlying distributions and bounding the Wasserstein distance is elegant, instance noise itself is not a new technique and has been used as a heuristic for stabilizing GAN training in prior work. A truly "principled" method should emerge from the theory and demonstrate clear advantages over existing ad-hoc solutions. Here, the theory serves more as a post-hoc explanation for an existing trick rather than a generator of a new, superior algorithm.

The most significant weakness is the complete absence of empirical results. The paper makes strong claims about avoiding instability, but provides no evidence. A convincing paper in this area must compare its proposed method against strong, established baselines (e.g., DCGAN with the stabilization techniques from Salimans et al., 2016) on standard image generation benchmarks. Without experiments, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed approach offers any practical benefit. Does training with instance noise, as motivated by this theory, actually lead to more stable convergence? Does it produce higher-quality samples as measured by metrics like Inception Score or FID? The paper leaves these critical questions unanswered.

Furthermore, there is a disconnect between the theoretical setup and the practical takeaway. The paper spends considerable effort motivating the Wasserstein distance as a superior metric, culminating in Theorem 3.3. Yet, the proposed algorithm does not minimize the Wasserstein distance, but rather the JSD of smoothed distributions, which only serves as part of an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance. This feels like an indirect and incomplete solution. A stronger paper would have used the theory to derive an algorithm that more directly targets the Wasserstein distance itself.

In summary, the paper presents a valuable theoretical analysis but fails to deliver a complete contribution. It identifies a problem, theorizes about a solution, but does not provide the crucial validation needed to demonstrate that the solution is effective in practice.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The theoretical work in Section 2 is excellent and could form the basis of a strong paper. I strongly encourage the authors to build upon this by including a thorough experimental section. Even a simple set of experiments on CIFAR-10 or CelebA demonstrating improved training stability compared to a standard DCGAN baseline would greatly strengthen the paper's claims.
*   The connection to the Wasserstein distance is the most novel part of the proposed solution. I would suggest exploring methods that optimize this distance more directly. The Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality offers a path to re-framing the Wasserstein distance in a way that might be more amenable to optimization within the GAN framework. This seems like a more promising research direction than simply adding noise.
*   The assumption in Theorem 2.6 that the error in the discriminator and its gradient can be modeled as independent white noise processes is very strong. While used for illustrative purposes, a discussion of the limitations of this model would be appropriate.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Theorem 3.3 presents a bound: `W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2V^(1/2) + 2C * sqrt(JSD(Pr+, Pg+))`. This suggests an inherent trade-off related to the noise variance `V`. How do you propose selecting `V` in practice? Have you considered an annealing schedule for the noise, and if so, how does your theory guide the design of such a schedule?
2.  Could you elaborate on why you stopped at using instance noise to optimize an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance, rather than proposing a method to optimize it more directly? Were there specific technical challenges that made a direct approach intractable?
3.  How does your proposed method compare empirically to other GAN stabilization techniques, such as feature matching, minibatch discrimination, or label smoothing, as described in Salimans et al. (2016)?
