### Review

**Summary of Contributions:**
This paper presents a theoretical investigation into the instability commonly observed during the training of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors' central thesis is that these instabilities arise when the data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg) have supports that are disjoint or lie on low-dimensional manifolds. They prove that under such conditions, a perfect discriminator exists, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator under the original cost function, or unstable, high-variance gradients under the commonly used `-log(D)` alternative. The paper further analyzes the divergences being optimized by these cost functions. As a solution, the authors propose adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator, which smoothens the distributions. They derive the resulting generator gradient, showing it corresponds to moving generated samples towards the data manifold. Finally, the paper introduces the Wasserstein distance as a more sensible metric for comparing distributions on low-dimensional manifolds and provides a theorem bounding this distance by terms that can be optimized in their proposed framework.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1.  The paper is marred by a pervasive lack of rigor in its mathematical presentation. Key proofs contain significant typographical errors, notational ambiguities, and confusing derivations that severely undermine the reader's confidence in the correctness of the theoretical claims.
2.  The manuscript exhibits a concerning number of formatting errors, inconsistencies, and general sloppiness, from mangled equations and a broken reference list to informal language. This lack of polish is not commensurate with the standards of a top-tier publication and makes a thorough evaluation of the work's merits exceptionally difficult.

**Supporting Arguments:**
My decision is based on a meticulous examination of the paper's technical details and presentation. While the high-level ideas are compelling, the execution is critically flawed.

*   **Mathematical Errors and Lack of Clarity:** The paper's theoretical core is compromised by numerous issues.
    *   In the proof of Theorem 2.5, the derivation for the gradient of `KL(Pgθ‖Pr)` relies on the expression `−Ex∼Pgθ [log D∗(x) / −D∗(x)]`. This appears to be a severe typo for `log(D∗(x) / (1−D∗(x)))`. This error makes the entire proof, a cornerstone of the paper's analysis of the alternative cost function, opaque and unverifiable without significant reconstruction by the reader.
    *   The assumption in Theorem 2.6 that the error in the discriminator and its gradient can be modeled as independent white noise processes is extremely strong and not well-justified. The authors themselves concede in footnote 4 that this model is technically problematic ("the remaining process is not measurable in x"), yet they proceed with the analysis. This hand-waving of a fundamental technical issue is not acceptable.
    *   The proof of Theorem 3.2 is presented with poorly formatted and confusing intermediate steps. For instance, the expression for the gradient inside the expectation is written as a long, un-bracketed line of terms that is difficult to parse and appears to contain typos. A clean, step-by-step derivation is required.
    *   Several proofs are dismissed with phrases like "left as an exercise to the reader" (Theorem 2.3) or "the proof is identical" (Corollary 3.2). This is inappropriate for a formal submission and shirks the authors' responsibility to provide complete and verifiable arguments.

*   **Notational and Formatting Inconsistencies:**
    *   The reference list is completely broken, with entries like `Bottou et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Bottou et al\.,`. This suggests a catastrophic failure in the bibliography generation process and a complete lack of proofreading.
    *   Basic mathematical typesetting is incorrect. The JSD definition in the introduction is missing the `1/2` coefficients, and the fraction for the optimal discriminator in equation (2) is malformed.
    *   Notation is used inconsistently. The paper defines a Wasserstein metric `W(P,Q)` but is ambiguous as to whether it is the 1-Wasserstein or 2-Wasserstein distance. The norm notation `‖Jθgθ(z)‖22` in Theorem 2.4 is also non-standard and unclear. In the proof of Theorem 2.2, `B(x, x)` is used to denote a ball of radius `x` around a point `x`, which is confusing.
    *   There are numerous typographical and grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., "arize," "symetrical," "reallistically," "transversallity," "aboslutely," "as it typically done").

While I recognize the potential importance of understanding GAN instability through the lens of manifold supports, the sheer volume of minor and major flaws prevents me from recommending acceptance. A paper's claims are only as strong as their supporting evidence, and when that evidence is presented in such a careless and error-prone manner, it cannot be trusted.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**
The conceptual direction of this paper is promising. The idea of using noise to smooth distributions on manifolds and relating the resulting objective to a proper metric like the Wasserstein distance is a valuable contribution. To improve the paper, I would strongly recommend the following:
1.  **Complete Overhaul of Proofs:** Every single mathematical derivation needs to be re-written with extreme care, ensuring each step is explicit, correct, and uses clear, consistent notation. Remove all "exercise for the reader" statements and provide full proofs.
2.  **Rigorous Proofreading:** The paper must be proofread from start to finish to eliminate all typographical, grammatical, and formatting errors. The reference list must be correctly generated.
3.  **Strengthen Assumptions:** The theoretical assumptions, particularly the noise model in Theorem 2.6, must be better justified or replaced with more realistic ones. If a model has known technical issues, they must be addressed directly rather than relegated to a dismissive footnote.
4.  **Improve Clarity:** Formalize the language, especially in the appendices (e.g., avoid phrases like "The annoying part is..."). Ensure all non-standard notation is explicitly defined.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  Could you please provide a corrected, step-by-step derivation for the proof of Theorem 2.5, clarifying the relationship between `KL(Pg‖Pr)` and the optimal discriminator `D*`?
2.  Can you provide a more rigorous justification for modeling the discriminator error as a white noise process in Theorem 2.6? Have you considered an alternative analysis under a weaker, more realistic noise model?
3.  Please specify which Wasserstein metric (`W_1` or `W_2`) is used in Section 3. The current notation is ambiguous, which impacts the interpretation of Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3.
