### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the instability commonly observed when training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors' central thesis is that these instabilities arise from the fact that both the real data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg) have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds within the high-dimensional data space.

The key contributions are:
1.  **Theoretical Explanation of Instability:** The paper formally proves that if Pr and Pg have supports on low-dimensional manifolds, an optimal discriminator exists that is perfect (achieves 100% accuracy) and has gradients that are zero almost everywhere on the supports of the two distributions.
2.  **Analysis of Standard Cost Functions:** Based on this result, the paper demonstrates that:
    *   The original GAN cost function (`log(1-D)`) suffers from vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves, because the generator's update depends on the discriminator's gradient, which goes to zero.
    *   The commonly used alternative (`-log(D)`) optimizes a combination of the reverse KL-divergence and the JSD, explaining its tendency to produce high-quality samples at the cost of mode-dropping. More importantly, the paper provides a novel argument that this objective leads to unstable training with high-variance gradients, modeling the updates as following a Cauchy distribution.
3.  **A Principled Stabilization Method:** The authors propose adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This effectively "smears out" the manifold-supported distributions, ensuring their supports overlap and making the optimization problem well-posed. The paper derives the resulting generator gradient, showing it has a stabilizing form that intuitively pulls generated samples towards the data manifold.
4.  **Connection to Wasserstein Distance:** The paper introduces the Wasserstein distance as a more sensible metric for comparing distributions on manifolds. It then proves a key theorem (Theorem 3.3) that upper-bounds the Wasserstein distance between the true distributions (Pr, Pg) by a sum of two terms: one related to the variance of the added noise, and the other to the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions (Pr+, Pg+). This provides a strong theoretical motivation for the proposed training method, as minimizing the JSD of the noisy distributions corresponds to minimizing an upper bound on a more suitable metric for the original distributions.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper provides a novel and technically sound theoretical framework that, for the first time, offers a rigorous explanation for the widely observed training instabilities in GANs. It moves the field beyond heuristic-based solutions by identifying the low-dimensional manifold hypothesis as the root cause of the problem.
2.  The proposed solution—adding noise to distributions to enable stable optimization—is directly motivated by the theoretical analysis. The connection established between this practical training procedure and the minimization of the more theoretically-sound Wasserstein distance provides a principled foundation for future work on GAN stabilization.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous and clear progression from identifying a fundamental problem to developing a theoretically-grounded solution.

First, the analysis in Section 2 is a significant contribution. The "Perfect Discrimination Theorems" (2.1 and 2.2) formalize the intuition that it is easy for a discriminator to separate two distributions whose supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The consequence—that the optimal discriminator's gradient is zero almost everywhere—is a crucial insight. This insight provides a direct and compelling explanation for the vanishing gradients observed when using the original GAN cost function (Theorem 2.4). Furthermore, the analysis of the `-log(D)` cost function is particularly novel. Theorem 2.5 identifies the unusual objective function being optimized, and Theorem 2.6 provides a powerful explanation for the unstable, high-variance gradients by showing they can be modeled by a Cauchy distribution, which has infinite variance. This theoretical finding aligns perfectly with the empirical behavior of GANs reported in the literature.

Second, the proposed solution in Section 3 is not just an ad-hoc fix but a direct consequence of the preceding analysis. By demonstrating that adding continuous noise makes the distributions absolutely continuous (Theorem 3.1), the authors show how to bypass the conditions that lead to a perfect, zero-gradient discriminator. The resulting gradient for the generator (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2) is shown to be smooth and interpretable, providing a clear path towards stable optimization.

Finally, the connection to the Wasserstein distance elevates the paper from a simple stability fix to a more profound contribution. Traditional divergences like KL and JSD are shown to be ill-suited for distributions on manifolds (Theorem 2.3). By contrast, the Wasserstein distance is well-behaved. Theorem 3.3 provides the paper's capstone result, creating a direct link between the practical algorithm (training a GAN on noisy data to minimize JSD) and the theoretical goal (minimizing the Wasserstein distance between the true data distributions). This result is highly significant as it provides a principled objective for GAN training that is robust to the manifold nature of the data.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Assumptions in Theorem 2.6:** The modeling of discriminator error as independent, centered Gaussian processes for both the function value and its gradient is a simplification that makes the proof elegant. Could you discuss whether the main conclusion of instability (e.g., extremely high-variance gradients) holds under weaker or more general assumptions about the nature of the approximation error?
*   **Alternative Optimization:** The paper proposes optimizing an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance by minimizing the JSD of noisy distributions. Did the authors consider methods to optimize the Wasserstein distance more directly, perhaps by leveraging its dual formulation (the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality)? This could potentially avoid the need for adding and annealing noise.
*   **Practical Guidance on Noise:** The proposed method relies on adding noise, which should likely be annealed over time. Could you provide some practical guidance or intuition on selecting the initial noise variance and a suitable annealing schedule? How sensitive is the stability of the training procedure to these choices?
*   **Interpretation of Diameter C:** The bound in Theorem 3.3 depends on `C`, the diameter of the support of the noisy distributions. When using unbounded noise like a Gaussian, the support is technically infinite. How should `C` be interpreted in this context? Does it refer to a high-probability region, and how does that affect the tightness of the bound?
*   **Clarification on Manifold Assumptions:** The proofs of the perfect discrimination theorems rely on properties of regular submanifolds. While this is a reasonable mathematical model, could you comment on whether the conclusions would hold for more complex, non-regular geometric structures that might better represent real-world data manifolds?
