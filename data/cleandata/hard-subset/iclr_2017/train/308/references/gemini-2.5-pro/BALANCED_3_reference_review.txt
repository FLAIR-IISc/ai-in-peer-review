### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a foundational theoretical analysis of the difficulties and instabilities inherent in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors make several key contributions. First, they argue convincingly that the distributions of real data (Pr) and generated data (Pg) have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which are almost certainly disjoint. They rigorously prove that under these conditions, an optimal discriminator exists that can perfectly separate the two distributions, leading to gradients that are zero almost everywhere. This provides a clear explanation for the vanishing gradient problem observed when training the generator with the original minimax objective.

Second, the paper analyzes the commonly used `-log D` alternative cost function for the generator. It provides the first formal derivation showing that this objective corresponds to minimizing a combination of KL(Pg‖Pr) and the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD), explaining the empirical tendency of GANs to produce high-quality samples at the cost of mode collapse. The authors further prove that this alternative objective, while avoiding vanishing gradients, suffers from pathologically unstable updates, showing that the gradients can have infinite expectation and variance.

Finally, based on this diagnosis, the paper proposes a principled solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This effectively smooths the underlying distributions, preventing the discriminator from becoming perfect and ensuring that the generator receives smooth, informative gradients. The paper culminates by linking this noise-injection strategy to the Wasserstein distance, proving that minimizing the JSD between the noisy distributions minimizes an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance between the true, noiseless distributions. This provides a more sensible metric for evaluating generative models whose supports do not overlap.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a significant and timely contribution to the understanding of generative adversarial networks. My decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Rigorous Theoretical Diagnosis:** The paper provides the first comprehensive theoretical framework that explains the widely observed empirical instabilities of GAN training. It moves the field beyond heuristics by identifying a fundamental cause of the problem—the nature of the support of the distributions—and rigorously proving its consequences for different GAN objective functions.
2.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Solution:** The proposed solution of using instance noise is a direct and elegant consequence of the theoretical analysis. The connection drawn to the Wasserstein distance is particularly insightful, as it introduces a more appropriate metric for comparing distributions on non-overlapping manifolds and provides a principled path toward more stable training regimes.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strengths are numerous and substantial. The core theoretical argument, developed through Section 2, is a major highlight. The insight that the supports of Pr and Pg likely lie on low-dimensional manifolds and do not "perfectly align" is crucial. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 formalize the intuition that a perfect separator must exist in this setting, and that its gradients will be zero where they are needed most. This directly leads to the vanishing gradient result for the original cost function, elegantly proven in Theorem 2.4. This analysis is clear, mathematically sound, and provides a powerful explanation for a well-known practical problem.

The analysis of the `-log D` cost function is equally impressive. Theorem 2.5, which identifies the true objective being optimized, is a critical piece of theory that explains the observed behavior of modern GANs (good sample quality, but mode dropping). Furthermore, Theorem 2.6 provides a compelling explanation for the massive instability of this objective. While the assumption of white noise is an idealization, it powerfully illustrates why gradients can become erratic and have infinite variance as the discriminator improves, a phenomenon familiar to anyone who has trained a GAN.

Finally, the proposed solution in Section 3 is not just another training trick. It is a direct remedy to the problems identified in Section 2. By adding noise, the supports of the distributions are thickened, ensuring they overlap and that the JSD becomes a well-behaved objective. Theorem 3.2 provides a beautiful interpretation of the resulting gradients as a force pulling generated samples towards the data manifold. The connection to the Wasserstein distance via Theorem 3.3 is the capstone of the paper, providing a principled objective for future research: minimizing a proper distance metric between the distributions, which this work shows is achievable by optimizing a JSD-based objective on smoothed distributions.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could help further strengthen it. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   **On the Noise Model in Theorem 2.6:** The assumption that the error in the discriminator and its gradient are independent, centered Gaussian processes (white noise) is a strong simplification. While it effectively demonstrates the potential for infinite variance, the error from a finite-capacity neural network is likely to be more structured. A brief discussion on whether the conclusions would hold under more realistic, correlated noise models would add nuance and strengthen the claim's generality.
*   **Intuition for Proofs:** The paper is dense with mathematical results. While the proofs are rightly deferred to the appendix, including a one- or two-sentence intuitive summary of the proof strategy immediately after each theorem statement in the main text could significantly improve readability and accessibility for a broader audience.
*   **Practical Implications:** The paper is primarily theoretical, which is its great strength. However, a small section discussing the practical implementation of the proposed noise-based training could be beneficial. For example, guidance on the choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian), the scheduling of noise variance (annealing), and how it interacts with other stabilization techniques would be valuable for practitioners looking to apply these ideas.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:

1.  Theorem 3.3 provides an upper bound on the Wasserstein distance, W(Pr, Pg), composed of a term related to the noise variance (V) and a term related to the JSD of the noisy distributions. How tight do you expect this bound to be in practice? Is there a tension where decreasing V to tighten the first part of the bound makes the JSD term harder to minimize, as the distributions become "peakier"?
2.  Your theory suggests that for a sufficiently good discriminator, the gradients for the `-log D` objective should become extremely large. Your Figure 3 shows this trend, but have you tried to empirically characterize the distribution of these gradients to see if they exhibit the heavy tails predicted by the Cauchy distribution derived in Theorem 2.6?
3.  The solution proposed involves adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. Have you performed any preliminary experiments to validate that this approach leads to more stable training in practice compared to a baseline DCGAN? Specifically, does it mitigate the gradient explosion shown in Figure 3?
