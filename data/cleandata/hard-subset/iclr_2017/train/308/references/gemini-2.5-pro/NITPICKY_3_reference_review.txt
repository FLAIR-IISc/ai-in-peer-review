### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a theoretical investigation into the well-known instabilities of Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training. The authors make several key claims. First, they argue that the core issue stems from the fact that the real data distribution (Pr) and the generator's distribution (Pg) have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which are likely disjoint. They provide theoretical results (Theorems 2.1, 2.2) to show that this disjointness allows for the existence of a "perfect" discriminator that separates the distributions with 100% accuracy, and whose gradient is zero almost everywhere on the supports of Pr and Pg.

Second, the paper analyzes the consequences of this for generator updates. For the original GAN cost function, this leads to vanishing gradients as the discriminator improves (Theorem 2.4). For the popular `-log D` alternative, the authors provide a novel derivation showing it optimizes a combination of KL and JSD divergences (Theorem 2.5) and argue that it leads to unstable, high-variance gradients under a noisy discriminator approximation (Theorem 2.6).

Finally, the authors propose a solution: adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This makes the distributions absolutely continuous, ensuring their supports overlap. They analyze the resulting gradients (Theorem 3.2) and provide a key theoretical result (Theorem 3.3) that bounds the Wasserstein distance between the original (noiseless) distributions by a function of the JSD of the noisy distributions, suggesting that minimizing the latter provides a principled way to minimize the former.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Critical Flaws in the Main Theoretical Justification:** The paper's primary proposal rests on bridging the JSD of noisy distributions to the Wasserstein distance of the original ones. However, the proof of the main result, Theorem 3.3, is mathematically unsound. It conflates properties of the 1-Wasserstein (W1) and 2-Wasserstein (W2) distances within a single inequality chain, rendering the entire argument invalid. This is compounded by a cascade of notational inconsistencies, starting with an incorrect definition for the Wasserstein metric itself (Definition 3.1 defines W2^2, Lemma 4 proves a result for W2, and Theorem 3.3 uses a property of W1).
2.  **Pervasive Lack of Rigor and Polish:** The manuscript is marred by an exceptionally high number of typographical errors, grammatical mistakes, formatting inconsistencies, and mathematical inaccuracies. These range from minor typos ("symetrical", "adressing") to significant errors in core equations (e.g., the definition of the JSD is missing coefficients) and proofs (a sign error in the derivation for Theorem 2.5). The bibliography is completely corrupted, suggesting a severe lack of care in the paper's preparation. This sheer volume of minor flaws undermines confidence in the more complex, unscrutinized technical details presented in the appendices.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The core of my recommendation stems from the issues in Section 3. Definition 3.1 defines `W(P,Q)` using `‖x− y‖2`, which corresponds to the squared 2-Wasserstein distance, `W_2^2(P,Q)`. However, the proof of Theorem 3.3 relies on the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality `W(P,Q) ≤ Cδ(P,Q)`, which holds for the 1-Wasserstein distance (`W_1`), not `W_2` or its square. The proof then combines this with Lemma 4, which is a result concerning `W_2`. It is not valid to mix different metrics (`W_1` and `W_2`) in this manner within a single application of the triangle inequality. This fundamental error invalidates the main theoretical claim that connects the proposed noisy training scheme to a meaningful metric between the original, noiseless distributions.

Beyond this fatal flaw, the paper demonstrates a pattern of imprecision. For instance:
*   The JSD definition is stated as `1/2 KL(Pr‖PA) + KL(Pg‖PA)`, where the `1/2` is missing from the second term.
*   The proof of Theorem 2.5 contains a sign error in an intermediate step, stating that `log(Pgθ0/Pr)` is related to `log(D*/(1-D*))` when it should be `log((1-D*)/D*)`. While the final conclusion of the theorem may be correct, the erroneous derivation is concerning.
*   Statements like "proof is trivial and left as an exercise to the reader" (Theorem 2.3) and "The proof is identical... so we leave it to the reader" (Corollary 3.2) are not appropriate for a peer-reviewed publication and suggest incomplete work.
*   The formatting is sloppy. Section numbering is incorrect (".2.2 THE − logD ALTERNATIVE"), spacing around mathematical operators is inconsistent (e.g., `that∇xD∗(x) ≡ 0`), and the entire reference list is unusable due to apparent BibTeX compilation errors (`Bottou et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Bottou et al\.,`).

While the conceptual ideas presented are promising, the execution is critically flawed. In its current state, the paper does not meet the standards of rigor required for publication.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The foundational ideas in this paper are compelling and deserve a more careful treatment. The analysis in Section 2, which links training instability to the manifold hypothesis, is particularly insightful. I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the manuscript with the following points in mind:

*   **Correct the Wasserstein metric theory:** The authors must decide on a single metric (either W1 or W2) and rewrite Section 3 accordingly. All definitions, lemmas, theorems, and proofs must be made consistent with this choice. This is the most critical change required.
*   **Thorough Proofreading:** The paper needs to be meticulously proofread to eliminate the numerous typographical, grammatical, and formatting errors. A clean, professional presentation is essential for the reader to trust the technical content.
*   **Complete All Proofs:** Claims should not be left as "exercises for the reader." At a minimum, provide proof sketches or full proofs in the appendix for all stated results.
*   **Improve Clarity and Formality:** Replace informal or overly aggressive language ("lousy meaningless samples," "answer to all these questions") with more standard academic phrasing. Technical points glossed over in footnotes (e.g., Footnote 4's mention of stochastic differential equations) should be properly elaborated upon in the appendix if they are crucial to the argument's validity.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a corrected version of the proof for Theorem 3.3, starting with a single, consistent definition for the Wasserstein distance and using only properties of that specific metric throughout the derivation?
2.  In the proof of Theorem 2.6, the assumption that the noise on the discriminator's value (`ε`) and its gradient (`r`) are independent seems quite strong. How would your analysis change if these noise terms were correlated, as one might expect from an imperfect neural network approximation?
3.  In the proof of Theorem 2.2, the definition `M̂ = ⋃x∈M\L B(x, x/3)` appears to use the point `x` itself as the radius of the ball. Is this a typo for some `ε_x`? Please clarify the construction of the sets `M̂` and `P̂`.
