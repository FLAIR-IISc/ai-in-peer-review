Summary and contributions
- The paper analyzes instability in GAN training. It argues that when the real (Pr) and generator (Pg) distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (or have disjoint supports), the optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant on the supports, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator under the original objective and instability under the non-saturating objective. It formalizes this with “perfect discrimination” theorems and a vanishing-gradient bound (Thm 2.4), and claims the non-saturating gradient corresponds to the gradient of KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr) (Thm 2.5).
- It proposes adding noise (“instance noise”) to both data and generator outputs to obtain absolutely continuous “noisy” distributions Pr+ and Pg+, providing smoother, meaningful gradients and allowing discriminator training to optimality. It derives the gradient structure under noise (Thm 3.2, Cor 3.2).
- It motivates Wasserstein distance as a softer metric, and proves a bound relating W(Pr,Pg) to noise variance and JSD(Pr+||Pg+) (Thm 3.3), plus a continuity bound W(PX,PX+ ) ≤ sqrt(V) (Lemma 4).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Numerous technical and mathematical inaccuracies and unproven/incorrect steps (including a key definition/inequality for Wasserstein), (2) missing empirical evidence and referenced figures, (3) substantial presentation/formatting issues (typos, inconsistent notation, incomplete/broken references) that collectively undermine confidence in the results.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty/positioning: The manifold-support/perfect discriminator intuition is known in the GAN literature, but the paper attempts more rigorous statements. The instance noise idea is known in practice and has been discussed historically; related prior work (e.g., “instance noise”/Gaussian noise to inputs) is not properly cited. The Wasserstein motivation is related to later WGAN developments, but here the main technical bound contains errors (see below).
- Mathematical issues:
  - Definition 3.1 defines W(P,Q) = inf ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ (squared cost), but Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 use properties of the 1-Wasserstein (L1) metric (e.g., W ≤ E||ε||, and W ≤ C·TV, KR dual). With squared cost, the inequalities as written do not hold; dimensions/constants are inconsistent. Either redefine W as W1 or fix all inequalities and constants accordingly (e.g., for bounded diameter C, ∫||x−y||^2 ≤ C ∫||x−y||, etc.). As written, Lemma 4’s bound W ≤ sqrt(V) contradicts the squared-cost definition (it would give W ≤ V if using squared cost).
  - Pinsker constants are mishandled (δ ≤ sqrt(KL/2)), but the paper uses δ ≤ sqrt(KL) without the 1/2 consistently.
  - Theorem 2.5: the identity Ez[−∇θ log D*(gθ(z))]|θ0 = ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD]|θ0 is delicate. The derivation cites Goodfellow (2014) and Huszár (2016), but the steps and regularity assumptions are not fully justified. Also, the conclusion that the JSD term “pushes distributions apart” from the sign alone is not convincing without careful local analysis at θ0 (since both terms are evaluated at θ0 and JSD is symmetric). This needs a thorough, clean derivation with explicit assumptions.
  - Theorem 2.6 assumes white-noise, pointwise-independent Gaussian processes ε(x), r(x) for the discriminator and its gradient; this is unrealistic for neural networks and makes measurability/integration problematic (acknowledged in a footnote). The claim that the z-averaged update is Cauchy relies on independence across z and stability of sums, which again is not justified under the stated setup. As a result, the “infinite variance” conclusion is not credible as stated.
  - Lemma 1 (image of neural network lies in a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z): the piecewise-linear case is plausible via finiteness of linear regions, but the proof glosses over the fact that the number of linear regions can be large but still finite for fixed architecture; this should be stated carefully (and depends on bounded input domains). For smooth monotonically increasing activations, the argument via coordinate projections and “critical points are isolated” invokes Morse-type arguments without genericity assumptions; critical sets need not be isolated in general. The claim in full generality is not established rigorously.
  - Norm ‖D‖ = supx |D(x)| + ||∇x D(x)||2 is used over X without specifying compactness; elsewhere, compact supports are assumed sometimes but not consistently. Approximation in this “Sobolev-like” norm is asserted without conditions.
- Missing/incorrect content:
  - Figures 1–3 are referenced but not provided. No empirical validation is shown despite claims about observing vanishing gradients and instability growth. For a paper making prescriptive training recommendations (instance noise, annealing), experiments are essential.
  - Several key references are missing or malformed (e.g., Huszár (2016) cited in text but not in references; Goodfellow et al. (2014b) cited but not listed; many entries have corrupted metadata). This makes it hard to verify related work and derivations.
  - JSD definition early on is misstated: JSD(P||Q) = 1/2 KL(P||M) + 1/2 KL(Q||M); the paper’s formula omits the 1/2 factors.
- Presentation/notation issues:
  - Many typos and grammatical errors (“adress”, “symetrical”, “transversallity”, “aboslutely”, “Luckilly”, “arize”, etc.).
  - Overloaded symbols (M for manifold and for a bound; P for distribution and density; D for discriminator and diameter C later).
  - Ambiguous assumptions: whether X is compact; whether supports are compact; whether densities exist in ambient space or only on manifolds (“continuous in their manifold”).
  - The proof sketches often swap expectations/integrals without dominated convergence or measurability conditions, especially when mixing random processes with z-expectations.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Decide clearly whether you use W1 or W2 (or W2^2), fix the definition, and then rigorously re-derive Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 with correct constants and assumptions (bounded support, Lipschitz conditions). Include a self-contained proof or precise citations.
- Provide complete, rigorous proofs or cleanly state the required genericity assumptions (e.g., for Lemma 1). Alternatively, constrain claims (e.g., piecewise-linear networks on bounded domains).
- Replace the white-noise model in Theorem 2.6 with a more realistic perturbation model (e.g., bounded approximation error in a Sobolev norm; stochastic gradient noise), and analyze stability under that model.
- Include actual experiments demonstrating: (a) vanishing gradients as D improves under original loss, (b) instability growth under −log D with stronger D (with proper variance metrics), (c) stabilization via instance noise and annealing, and (d) the relationship between JSD(Pr+,Pg+) and sample quality as σ→0. Provide the missing figures and experimental details.
- Clarify the training prescription: how to choose noise distribution and annealing schedule; how to prevent discriminator overfitting to noisy data; impact on sample quality and mode dropping.

Questions for the authors
1. Please correct and clarify the Wasserstein definition and all subsequent inequalities. Which Wasserstein metric is used, and under what assumptions do your bounds hold?
2. Can you provide a rigorous, assumption-complete proof of Lemma 1 (especially the smooth activation case), or restrict the claim appropriately?
3. Theorem 2.5: could you supply a detailed derivation and clarify the role/sign of the JSD term and the exact conditions under which the identity holds?
4. Theorem 2.6 relies on a white-noise discriminator error model. Can you justify this modeling choice or replace it with a realistic approximation model? How sensitive are your conclusions to this assumption?
5. Please provide the missing figures and an empirical section validating instance noise and the annealing schedule. How does your method compare to standard GAN heuristics on standard datasets?
