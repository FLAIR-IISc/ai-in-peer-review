Summary and contributions
The paper tackles a central question: why is GAN training unstable and why do generator gradients deteriorate as the discriminator improves? The authors take a principled approach grounded in measure theory and differential geometry to analyze GAN objectives when the real and model distributions are supported on low-dimensional manifolds. The main contributions are:
- “Perfect discrimination” results: under generic conditions (disjoint or non-transversally intersecting manifolds of lower-than-ambient dimension), the optimal discriminator attains accuracy 1 and is locally constant with zero gradient almost everywhere on the supports. This yields vanishing generator gradients for the original log(1−D) objective as D→D*.
- A characterization of the −log D generator update: locally (with D fixed at the optimal D* for θ0), the update equals the gradient of KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr), explaining empirically observed mode dropping (inverted KL) and the perverse sign on JSD.
- An instability result: under a noise model for discriminator approximation error (white noise and independence of value/gradient errors), the −log D update has Cauchy-distributed coordinates with infinite variance and zero mean, explaining high-variance, unstable updates.
- A proposed remedy: add continuous “instance noise” to both real and generated samples so Pr* and Pg* become absolutely continuous and the discriminator no longer saturates. The induced generator gradient decomposes into attraction to high-density regions of Pr and repulsion from high-density regions of Pg with a distance-weighted kernel, and equals 2∇θ JSD(Pr* || Pg*) if noise is also added to generator outputs during backprop.
- A link to Wasserstein distance: the authors argue that JSD(Pr* || Pg*) can serve as a proxy for W(Pr, Pg), deriving a bound of the form W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2 Var(ε)1/2 + 2 C √JSD(Pr* || Pg*), suggesting annealing noise while minimizing JSD(·) yields small W distance between the original noiseless distributions.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) there are significant mathematical inconsistencies in the Wasserstein section that undermine a central bound; (2) the proposed remedy (instance noise) lacks convincing empirical validation and is not positioned against closely related prior work; (3) several assumptions in the instability result are unrealistic and not stress-tested, and figures referenced in the text are not provided.

Supporting arguments
- Wasserstein metric inconsistency: The paper defines W(P,Q) = infγ ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ (a squared L2 cost). It then uses triangle inequality and bounds that hold for W1 (Earth Mover’s with cost ||x−y||) or for W2 (with a square root outside the integral), but not for the squared-cost functional. In Lemma 4, W ≤ E[||ε||^2] is correct under the squared-cost definition, but the step yielding ≤ V1/2 is dimensionally inconsistent and mathematically incorrect unless W is W1. The subsequent inequalities W ≤ C δ, Pinsker, and the triangle inequality in Theorem 3.3 also align with W1. This needs a consistent choice (preferably W1 via Kantorovich–Rubinstein), with all proofs and constants corrected accordingly.
- Scope and realism of assumptions: Theorems 2.1–2.3 rely on manifold support and “no perfect alignment” (transversality). While generic, these results apply to the idealized infinite-capacity/optimal discriminator regime. The instability result (Theorem 2.6) assumes white, independent noise for value and gradient of D over x—an unrealistic model for neural network approximation errors—so the Cauchy conclusion should be presented as a heuristic extreme-case rather than definitive. The paper partially acknowledges this but still overstates the conclusion.
- Empirical support is missing: The paper cites figures (gradient norms, vanishing gradients) but provides no experimental section or quantitative validation. The key practical claim—that adding instance noise and annealing it removes discriminator scheduling pathologies and improves stability/quality—needs empirical evidence on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, LSUN), with ablations (noise on D only vs D+G, noise schedules, σ selection), comparisons to existing stabilizations (feature matching, label smoothing, gradient penalty), and measures of mode coverage (FID, precision/recall).
- Relation to prior work: Adding noise to inputs of GAN discriminators (“instance noise”) has been known and used; the paper should cite and differentiate from prior empirical reports (e.g., Sønderby et al., 2016) and connect more carefully to the broader family of IPMs (MMD-GAN, WGAN), which directly address the JSD/Wasserstein gap without noise by changing the divergence and enforcing Lipschitz critics. The paper references Villani but does not discuss the KR dual or practical enforcement of Lipschitz constraints (gradient penalty).
- Clarity and rigor: There are several typos and notational inconsistencies; some proofs rely on compactness or smoothness without clearly specifying ambient assumptions. Lemma 1’s ReLU case treats the union over activation patterns as images of linear maps of Z; for Z=Rk, those images are affine subspaces only when the map is rank-deficient, but more generally they are k-dimensional manifolds with boundary (images of polyhedral regions). This does not affect the core message (image has measure zero if k<d) but should be stated precisely.

Constructive feedback for improvement
- Fix the Wasserstein section: choose W1 or W2 consistently. If W1, define W(P,Q)=inf ∫||x−y|| dγ, then Lemma 4 becomes W ≤ E||ε|| ≤ √E||ε||^2, triangle inequality holds, and the TV bound W ≤ C δ is standard. Re-derive Theorem 3.3 with correct constants.
- Provide an experimental section validating instance noise: demonstrate training stability, sensitivity to σ and annealing schedule, and improvements in sample quality and diversity. Compare to strong baselines and report standard metrics.
- Temper Theorem 2.6: relax the independence/whiteness assumptions and, if possible, provide a bound on the variance of updates under more realistic correlated noise models. Alternatively, present this as an illustrative limiting case, supported by empirical gradient statistics.
- Clarify the need to inject noise into generator outputs for backprop and discuss the variance implications of stochastic gradients; describe reparameterization and its computational cost.
- Tighten and polish proofs: clearly state assumptions (compact supports, smoothness), correct typos, and make explicit when manifolds with boundary are used. In Lemma 1, specify that the image is a countable union of ≤k-dimensional submanifolds-with-boundary.
- Position relative to IPM-based approaches (MMD-GAN, WGAN): discuss pros/cons of instance noise versus changing the divergence, and whether both can be combined.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify which Wasserstein distance you intend to use (W1 vs W2), and correct Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 accordingly. Can you provide a corrected bound with explicit constants?
- Do you have empirical results demonstrating that instance noise with annealing improves stability and mode coverage on standard datasets, and how does it compare to feature matching, label smoothing, spectral norm/gradient penalty, and WGAN-GP?
- How sensitive is training to the choice of noise distribution, covariance, and annealing schedule? Can you provide practical guidelines?
- In Theorem 2.6, how robust is the instability conclusion when the noise on D and ∇D is correlated and spatially structured, as is likely for neural networks?
- Can the attraction/repulsion gradient form in Theorem 3.2 be connected to a kernel density view, and does that suggest alternative, learnable kernels beyond Gaussian noise?
- Under finite-capacity discriminators and generators, do your perfect discrimination and vanishing gradient results still dominate, or do you observe different regimes?

Overall, the paper offers valuable insights into why GANs can suffer from vanishing gradients and unstable updates and presents a promising smoothing-based direction. However, the current version has a key theoretical inconsistency and lacks the empirical validation needed for an ICLR acceptance.
