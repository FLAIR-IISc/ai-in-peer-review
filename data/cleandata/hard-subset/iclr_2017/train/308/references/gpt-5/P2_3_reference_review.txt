Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper analyzes why GAN training is unstable and why generator gradients degrade as the discriminator improves. It formalizes the observation that Pr and Pg often live on low-dimensional manifolds with (nearly) disjoint supports, implying the existence of near-perfect discriminators that are locally flat on the supports.
- It proves: (i) “perfect discrimination” theorems when supports are disjoint or lie on non-aligning manifolds, (ii) vanishing generator gradients under the original GAN loss as the discriminator approaches optimality, and (iii) instability for the −log D update when the discriminator is noisy, modeling the resulting gradient as Cauchy-distributed.
- It proposes adding input noise (“instance noise”) to make the convolved distributions absolutely continuous and overlapping, derives the resulting generator gradient as a weighted attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from the model manifold, and recommends backpropagating through noise on the generator too. It also connects this to the Wasserstein distance via a bound: W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2 Var(ε)1/2 + 2C sqrt(JSD(Pr+‖Pg+)).
- Overall, the paper aims to provide a principled theoretical foundation for observed GAN pathologies and a theoretically motivated stabilization strategy with an annealed noise schedule.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Several core theoretical claims rely on strong or unrealistic assumptions (e.g., white-noise approximation for discriminator errors, independence of D and ∇D noise, and idealized manifold conditions), and some arguments are loose (e.g., measure-theoretic subtleties, constants and conditions in bounds). This limits confidence that the results explain practical training behavior broadly.
2) The proposed remedy (instance noise with annealing) is not sufficiently novel and is not supported by compelling, comprehensive empirical validation in the submission. The paper refers to figures, but there is no rigorous experimental section demonstrating clear, robust gains over strong baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The problem—GAN instability and mode dropping—is important and well motivated. The paper situates divergences (KL, reverse KL, JSD) and provides useful intuition. However, related work is incomplete. Notably absent are broader f-divergence perspectives, MMD-based approaches, and Wasserstein GAN approaches with Lipschitz constraints and gradient penalties, which directly target some of the same issues and would contextualize the contribution.
- Correctness and rigor:
  - The “perfect discriminator” results exploit manifold support and Urysohn’s lemma; the intuition is sound and well known: if supports are disjoint/low-dimensional, optimal discriminators can be locally flat on supports, leading to vanishing gradients. The mathematical statements hinge on regularity and transversality assumptions that are reasonable as idealizations.
  - The vanishing gradient theorem for the original loss follows from D∗ being constant on the model support; the result is consistent with prior intuition.
  - The analysis of the −log D update (Theorem 2.5) relating it to ∇θ[KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr)] at θ0 is interesting but requires careful conditions (differentiability under the integral, fixed D∗ at θ0, etc.). The negative sign on the JSD term warrants more discussion: it suggests a push to separate distributions, but the result holds only locally at θ0 and assumes optimal D∗ fixed. This should be clarified and checked more rigorously.
  - The instability result (Theorem 2.6) that per-coordinate updates become Cauchy under white-noise discriminator errors assumes independence of numerator and denominator Gaussian noises and independence over x and z. These assumptions are very strong and unrealistic for trained discriminators; the authors acknowledge measurability issues. As a qualitative explanation it’s suggestive, but as a “theorem explaining practical instability” it is too idealized.
  - The noise-convolution results (Theorem 3.1, Corollary 3.2) are correct and useful: they formalize that convolving with continuous noise yields overlap and smooth gradients that can be interpreted as distance-weighted attraction/repulsion along manifolds. The recommendation to also add noise to the generator side avoids measure-zero adversarial directions and is a good practical insight.
  - The Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) qualitatively aligns with the idea that decreasing noise variance and decreasing JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) can reduce W(Pr,Pg). The proof uses standard inequalities (triangle inequality in W, TV bounds via KR duality, Pinsker), but important constants/conditions (e.g., support diameter C, exact constants in Pinsker) are glossed over. The bound is useful conceptually but not yet tight/practical as a training objective guarantee.
- Empirical support: The submission references figures showing discriminator error and gradient behavior, but the provided text contains no systematic experiments, no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., gradient penalties, spectral normalization, WGAN variants), and no metrics across datasets. Without rigorous empirical evidence, it is hard to assess the practical significance of the proposed method.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not all gating the decision)
- Expand related work: discuss f-GAN, MMD-GAN, spectral normalization, gradient penalties, and Wasserstein approaches, clarifying similarities/differences and when instance noise is preferable.
- Strengthen rigor:
  - Tighten Theorem 2.5’s derivation and assumptions; carefully state conditions for exchanging gradients and expectations.
  - Revisit Theorem 2.6 with a more realistic error model (correlated noise, Lipschitz-constrained discriminators) or provide empirical evidence that the empirical distribution of update magnitudes exhibits heavy tails.
  - Make constants explicit in Theorem 3.3, specify the exact version of Pinsker used, and discuss how C and V scale in practice.
- Experiments:
  - Provide a thorough empirical section across datasets (CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN) comparing: baseline GAN, GAN with instance noise (input only vs both sides), annealing schedules, and strong contemporary stabilizers (SN-GAN, WGAN-GP).
  - Report FID/IS, precision/recall for generative models, and training stability metrics; include ablations on noise variance and annealing.
  - Validate predicted phenomena: vanishing gradients with the original loss as D improves; variance/heavy-tailed updates for −log D; stabilization with noise; correlation between JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) and sample quality.
- Clarify scope: discuss when manifold-assumption-based pathologies are mitigated in practice (data augmentation, encoder noise, continuous observation models) and how that affects the conclusions.
- Improve presentation: fix typos, unify notation, and move key proofs into the main text where space permits.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is the proposed noise-annealing scheme to the variance schedule and to the choice of noise distribution? Can you provide guidelines and ablations?
2) Can you empirically validate the heavy-tailed nature of generator gradient updates under −log D when D is near-optimal (e.g., QQ plots, tail index estimates)?
3) How do your findings interact with Lipschitz-constrained discriminators (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties), which explicitly prevent overly sharp D? Do these methods alleviate the same issues your theorems target?
4) In Theorem 2.5, can you provide a more detailed derivation and clarify the role of holding D∗ fixed at θ0? How predictive is the KL − 2JSD local objective for actual training dynamics?
5) For Theorem 3.3, can you tighten the bound and discuss practical constants? Does minimizing empirical JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) correlate with reduced W(Pr,Pg) in experiments?
6) Does adding noise to both real and generated samples degrade ultimate fidelity? How do you ensure that annealing to zero noise does not reintroduce instability late in training?
