1) Summary of contributions
This paper tackles the instability of GAN training by analyzing the geometry of the data and model distributions, and the consequences for the discriminator and generator updates. The authors’ main claims and contributions are:
- When the data distribution and generator distribution lie on low-dimensional manifolds (as is typical), the optimal discriminator is perfect with probability 1 and locally constant almost everywhere on the supports. This makes gradients through D to the generator either vanish (for the original generator loss) or become highly unstable (for the −log D alternative).
- The paper formalizes these effects: (i) vanishing gradients for the original generator objective as the discriminator approaches optimality; (ii) Cauchy-distributed (infinite variance) updates for −log D under a simple noisy approximation model of D, explaining observed instability.
- To address this, the authors propose adding continuous noise (“instance noise”) to both real and generated samples, making the distributions absolutely continuous and restoring meaningful gradients, with an annealing schedule that reduces the noise over time.
- They show the generator gradient under instance noise has an attractive-repulsive structure toward the data manifold, and that training under noise corresponds to minimizing JSD between the smoothed distributions Pr+ and Pg+. They further provide a bound relating Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg to the noise variance and JSD(Pr+ || Pg+), offering a principled metric-based rationale for annealed instance noise.
Overall, the paper presents an appealing theoretical narrative connecting manifold geometry, discriminator optimality, gradient pathologies, and a principled smoothing-based fix.

2) Decision: Reject (weak)
Key reasons:
- The approach is insightful but the central remedy (adding and annealing noise) is only weakly validated: the paper relies almost entirely on theory, with references to figures but no substantial empirical evidence or systematic evaluation. The proposed method is practical and widely relevant, so demonstrating its effectiveness across architectures/datasets is essential.
- Several core theorems rely on strong idealizations (e.g., independence and white-noise assumptions for D and ∇D; exact manifold assumptions; compact supports), and the practical implications under finite network capacity, finite data, and correlated estimator noise are not carefully addressed.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and literature placement: The paper is very well motivated and addresses timely questions. It cites relevant GAN work and divergence comparisons. However, comparison to closely related concurrent lines (e.g., f-GANs, MMD/Moment-matching GANs, and later Lipschitz-based reformulations like WGAN/gradient penalty) is limited. The positioning would be stronger if it clarified when smoothing-based JSD minimization is preferable compared to enforcing Lipschitz constraints and optimizing alternative distances.
- Correctness and rigor:
  - Theorems 2.1–2.4: The argument that low-dimensional supports induce perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients is compelling and technically plausible. The use of transversality and measure-zero intersections is sound in spirit.
  - Theorem 2.6: The Cauchy result stems from taking a ratio of independent Gaussians; however, the independence and white-noise assumptions for the discriminator and its gradient are unrealistic in practice. This makes the infinite variance conclusion illustrative rather than definitive. A treatment with correlated noise or finite-capacity D would strengthen the claim.
  - Section 3: The smoothing results and their link to JSD and Wasserstein are conceptually strong. The bound relating W(Pr, Pg) to noise variance and JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) is valuable, though constants and conditions (support boundedness, Pinsker constants) should be carefully audited. The gradient expressions (attraction to data manifold and repulsion from model density) are insightful and align with intuition about smoothing as a regularizer.
- Empirical support: The paper references figures evidencing discriminator saturation, vanishing gradients, and unstable gradient norms; however, no systematic experiments, benchmarks, or ablations are presented here. Given the practicality of the proposed remedy, strong empirical validation is expected for ICLR.
- Clarity and presentation: There are typos and formatting issues, and several proofs are deferred. Some notational choices are nonstandard and assumptions are scattered. Still, the main story is clear and the intuition is well conveyed.

4) Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Empirics:
  - Please include comprehensive experiments demonstrating that instance noise with annealing materially improves stability, sample quality, and mode coverage across datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA) and architectures. Compare against standard baselines (feature matching, label smoothing, gradient penalty, spectral normalization, etc.).
  - Provide ablations on noise type (Gaussian vs. other tails), variance schedule, whether to add noise to G only, D only, or both, and sensitivity to the schedule.
  - Report quantitative metrics (FID, precision/recall for generative models, density measures if applicable) and qualitative samples, plus training dynamics (gradients, discriminator accuracy) to validate the theory.
- Assumptions and theory:
  - The manifold assumptions: How sensitive are the main results to mild violations (e.g., supports that are mixtures of low-dimensional components plus small full-dimensional noise)? Can you provide approximate statements under finite sample support?
  - Theorem 2.6 relies on independent white noise for D and ∇D. Can you relax this to correlated noise or noise induced by finite data/SGD? Even a perturbation analysis or simulation would help.
  - Compactness assumptions and Urysohn’s lemma: Many datasets are effectively unbounded; can the results be restated with appropriate tail conditions?
  - Constants and inequalities in Theorem 3.3: Please verify constants in the TV-to-KL/JSD step and clarify the diameter assumption. Could a version with sub-Gaussian tails replace compact support?
- Connections to related work:
  - Clarify relations to f-GANs, MMD-based GANs, and Lipschitz-constrained objectives (Wasserstein/gradient penalty/spectral norm). In what regimes does smoothing-and-JSD outperform or complement enforcing Lipschitzness?
  - Prior art has informally used instance noise; position your theoretical results as formalizing and validating that heuristic.
- Practical guidance:
  - Provide concrete default settings for noise variance and annealing, and explain diagnostics for when to reduce noise.
  - Discuss how smoothing interacts with batch norm, spectral norm, and data augmentation.
  - Outline how to estimate or monitor JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) via the discriminator in practice.
- Clarification questions:
  - Do your results imply convergence guarantees for Pg → Pr under an annealing schedule and optimal D training, or only heuristic guidance?
  - In Corollary 3.2, can you formally justify that the generator update equals 2∇θJSD(Pr+ || Pg+), including conditions under which exchanging gradients and integrals is valid?
  - How robust is the attractive-repulsive gradient structure when using finite-capacity discriminators and minibatch estimates?
  - Can you comment on whether smoothing reduces adversarial vulnerability in D in practice, and how this impacts gradient quality?

I believe this paper offers valuable theoretical insights into GAN instability and points to a principled and practical remedy. With stronger empirical validation, tighter assumptions, and clearer positioning relative to alternative approaches, it could become an influential contribution.
