Summary and contributions
- The paper presents a theoretically grounded analysis of why GAN training is unstable. The central thesis is that, in realistic settings, both the data distribution and the generator distribution concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds with (almost surely) disjoint support, which implies: (i) an optimal discriminator exists that is essentially perfect and flat (zero gradient) on the supports; (ii) the Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD) saturates to log 2 and both directed KL divergences become infinite, even when the manifolds are very close; (iii) consequently, the generator receives either vanishing gradients under the original objective, or highly unstable gradients under the non-saturating -log D objective.
- The paper formalizes these claims via “perfect discrimination” theorems using transversality arguments, proves vanishing-gradient behavior for the original generator loss as the discriminator approaches optimality, and shows that, under a simple noise model for discriminator approximation error, the -log D gradients can be heavy-tailed (Cauchy), explaining training instabilities.
- As a remedy, it proposes adding continuous noise (instance noise) to both real and generated inputs to the discriminator, and, crucially, also to the generator outputs during backpropagation. This smooths the distributions, ensures overlapping support, and yields meaningful gradients interpretable as distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold and repulsion where the model is overconfident.
- Finally, it motivates this approach via an upper bound that relates the Wasserstein distance between the original (noiseless) distributions to the variance of the added noise and the JSD between the convolved (noisy) distributions, thereby justifying annealing the noise while minimizing JSD on smoothed distributions.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons
- The paper addresses a core, persistent problem in GANs with a principled, largely rigorous analysis, offering clear explanations for widely observed pathologies and a remedy (instance noise with annealing) that is conceptually simple and practically actionable.
- The connection to optimal transport as a proxy objective through a bound provides useful intuition and a path toward more stable training, bridging a gap between theory and practice.

Supporting arguments
- The manifold-support arguments and transversality-based “perfect discrimination” results are compelling and align with empirical observations. The observation that JSD saturates and KLs diverge under support mismatch is correct and important.
- The vanishing-gradient result for the original generator objective as D→D* is well motivated; using a Sobolev-type norm to quantify D’s approximation quality is reasonable.
- The characterization of the -log D update at θ0 as the gradient of KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr) clarifies why this heuristic encourages realism (via inverted KL) while also potentially destabilizing (due to the negative JSD term).
- The instance noise analysis is insightful: the derived gradient form (a distance-weighted integral over data and model supports) matches the intuition of attracting samples to high-density regions of the data while repelling from overly likely regions of the model. Adding noise to the generator during backprop addresses the adversarial “measure-zero” loophole.
- The high-level idea of bounding W(Pr,Pg) by terms depending on the noise variance and JSD between the smoothed distributions gives a principled reason to anneal instance noise and to train the discriminator to near-optimality on the smoothed problem.

However:
- Some assumptions are strong. The “white-noise” independence of D*−D and its gradient in Theorem 2.6 is unrealistic; while the heavy-tailed conclusion is suggestive, it may overstate the typical variance blow-up. A weaker, more realistic stochastic model would strengthen the result.
- There is a likely inconsistency in the definition and use of the Wasserstein metric and subsequent inequalities. Definition 3.1 uses a squared Euclidean cost in the integral, while Lemma 4 and the bound W≤Cδ appear to rely on the 1-Wasserstein metric with linear cost. This should be disambiguated (W1 vs W2) and constants/inequalities adjusted accordingly.
- The paper is light on empirical validation. Claims about gradient norms and stability reference figures, but no quantitative evaluation is shown here. Given the practical import of the remedy (instance noise + annealing), empirical evidence on standard benchmarks and comparisons to existing heuristics would materially strengthen the paper.
- Related work coverage is incomplete. Prior work on stabilizing GANs with input noise/instance noise, f-divergence GANs (f-GAN), energy-based and feature matching methods, and the contemporary line of work on Wasserstein GANs should be discussed to place the contributions precisely.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Clarify the Wasserstein metric used (W1 vs W2) and fix the inequalities in Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 accordingly. If using W1, state the bound W1(PX,PX+ ) ≤ E||ε|| and W1 ≤ C δ; if using squared cost, adjust the statements and proof steps.
- In Lemma 1, specify domain assumptions on Z (e.g., compactness) and on the network (finite width/depth) to justify “finite union” of linear manifolds for ReLU networks; otherwise note it as a countable union.
- Temper Theorem 2.6: either justify a more realistic noise model (e.g., correlated, bounded variance, Lipschitz discriminator class) and derive high-variance or ill-conditioning results, or clearly label the current result as heuristic and provide empirical corroboration beyond anecdotal plots.
- Expand related work: connect to f-GAN (Nowozin et al.), GAN stabilization via noise/label smoothing/regularization, and the optimal transport perspective (and its practical instantiations such as WGAN).
- Provide empirical validation of the instance-noise proposal: ablations on noise level and annealing schedule; metrics (e.g., FID, precision/recall for generative models); comparison to baseline tricks (feature matching, gradient penalties). Demonstrate that training D to near-optimality is indeed stable under the proposed smoothing.
- Improve exposition and polish: fix typos, standardize notation (e.g., JSD definition), and make explicit when D is treated as fixed vs. re-optimized.

Questions for the authors
1. Wasserstein discrepancy: Are you using W1 or W2? Please reconcile the definition in 3.1 with Lemma 4 and the TV inequality, and provide the correct constants.
2. Instance noise schedule: How sensitive is performance to the choice of initial noise variance and annealing rate? Can you provide guidance or theory (e.g., rates ensuring convergence as variance→0)?
3. Robustness of Theorem 2.6: Can you derive instability (e.g., large variance or ill-conditioned updates) under a more realistic model where D’s approximation error is bounded, correlated with inputs, and Lipschitz?
4. Empirics: Can you show quantitative improvements (e.g., FID, precision/recall) and training stability curves on CIFAR-10/LSUN/ImageNet for the proposed method vs. common baselines?
5. Relation to WGAN: How does optimizing JSD on smoothed distributions compare empirically and theoretically with directly optimizing a Wasserstein surrogate under a Lipschitz constraint?
6. Practicalities: Do you add noise at inference? If not, how do you ensure the generator trained with noisy backprop produces high-quality samples at test time without noise?
