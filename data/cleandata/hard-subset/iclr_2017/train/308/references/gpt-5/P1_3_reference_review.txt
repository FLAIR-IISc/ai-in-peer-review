Summary and contributions
The paper tackles a central question in GAN training: why optimization becomes unstable or ineffective as the discriminator improves, and how to obtain principled, stable training signals for the generator. The authors:

- Argue that typical GAN setups induce data and model distributions supported on low-dimensional manifolds in high-dimensional ambient spaces, leading to disjoint or nearly disjoint supports.
- Prove “perfect discrimination” results: under mild conditions, there exist smooth discriminators that are constant (with zero gradient) on the supports almost everywhere, explaining vanishing gradients for the generator as the discriminator approaches optimality.
- Quantify the two widely used generator objectives: show vanishing gradients for the original log(1−D) objective as D→D*, and show that the “non-saturating” −log D update corresponds locally to optimizing KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr), thereby explaining mode dropping and instability. They further show that with noisy discriminator approximations, the −log D gradient can become heavy-tailed (Cauchy) and thus unstable.
- Propose a principled remedy: add continuous noise to both real and generated samples (instance noise), making Pr and Pg absolutely continuous, ensuring well-behaved, informative gradients. They derive the generator gradient under Gaussian noise as a distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from the model manifold, and show that backpropagating through noise makes the generator follow ∇ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+).
- Connect this scheme to the Wasserstein metric by proving an inequality bounding W(Pr,Pg) by a term depending on the noise variance and the JSD between the smoothed distributions, thus justifying annealed noise schedules and training the discriminator to optimality without destabilizing the generator.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated theoretical analysis that explains core GAN pathologies (vanishing gradients, instability, mode dropping) under realistic assumptions; (2) Clear, principled training remedy (instance noise with annealing) supported by theory and connected to a meaningful metric (Wasserstein), creating actionable guidance for practice.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and placement: The instability of GAN training and sensitivity to heuristics are major obstacles. The paper situates its work among likelihood-based divergences, JSD in GANs, and prior empirical heuristics, aiming to replace ad hoc fixes with principled analysis. The use of manifold support arguments is both plausible and now standard in deep generative modeling.
- Theoretical correctness and rigor: The perfect discrimination theorems rely on standard tools (Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, transversality) and well-stated assumptions (non-full-dimensional closed manifolds, non-alignment under perturbations). The proofs for vanishing gradients under the original objective are straightforward given ∇x D* = 0 on supports. The “non-saturating” gradient characterization at θ0 is insightful and aligns with intuition about KL(Pg||Pr) encouraging sample plausibility and mode dropping. The heavy-tail (Cauchy) result under white-noise discriminator error is idealized but captures the practical issue that ratio-of-noise leads to unstable updates; the paper explicitly acknowledges technical measurability caveats. The smoothing results are clean (convolution with noise) and yield interpretable generator gradients. The Wasserstein upper bound via TV, Pinsker, and triangle inequality is standard; the bounded-support assumption is clear.
- Motivation and impact: The instance-noise prescription is concrete, easy to implement, and theoretically justified. The connection to Wasserstein anticipates subsequent developments and strengthens the conceptual foundation for using metrics that reflect support geometry. The analysis provides a compelling explanation of empirical phenomena widely observed by practitioners.
- Empirical support: The paper refers to figures demonstrating discriminator error collapse, vanishing gradients, and increasing gradient variance, though details are sparse in the text. While the contribution is mainly theoretical, the claims about training behavior are consistent with practice, and the proposed remedy is testable.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not required for acceptance)
- Provide a more systematic empirical evaluation of instance noise: datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA), noise schedules, comparison to baselines (original GAN, non-saturating loss, DCGAN tricks), and metrics for both fidelity and coverage (e.g., FID, precision/recall or coverage metrics). Show sensitivity to the noise schedule and demonstrate annealing effectiveness.
- Clarify assumptions and scope:
  - Precisely state the class of generator architectures covered by Lemma 1 (e.g., convolutional layers with padding/strides, batch norm), and whether the manifold-union result extends to common practices.
  - Discuss the case where Pr has full-dimensional support but is highly concentrated near a manifold—do the main conclusions persist?
  - Comment on discrete data (e.g., text): what smoothing kernels or relaxations are appropriate, and does the analysis extend?
- Theorem 2.6: The white-noise and independence assumptions across x and between D and ∇D are strong; consider presenting a weaker, still realistic set of conditions (e.g., local Gaussian approximation with nonzero correlation) and a numerical simulation showing heavy-tailed update distributions.
- Theorem 2.5: Provide a brief derivation in the main text (or a clearer pointer) and further intuition for the counteracting JSD term. A small synthetic example illustrating how the gradient decomposes would help.
- Theorem 3.3: Comment on the tightness of constants and the bounded-support assumption; discuss extensions to sub-Gaussian tails or moments-based bounds. Note that W ≤ C·TV requires bounded domain; clarify the exact inequality used.
- Practical guidance: Include pseudocode for training with instance noise (where to add noise, schedule, handling batch norm), and discuss how to avoid the adversarial-example pitfall when only noisy D is used (you propose also adding noise in G; make the default recommendation explicit).

Questions for the authors
1. How should practitioners choose and anneal the noise variance in practice? Do you recommend a schedule tied to discriminator accuracy, JSD estimates, or a fixed decay?
2. Can you provide empirical evidence that adding noise to both D and G inputs (as per Corollary 3.2) avoids adversarial-example exploitation and improves diversity versus adding noise only to D?
3. How robust are the vanishing/instability phenomena when Pr has small but nonzero density off the manifold (e.g., due to observation noise)? Do your theorems admit approximate versions under such conditions?
4. The Cauchy-gradient result assumes independent white noise in D and ∇D. What qualitative conclusions survive if the errors are correlated or colored (e.g., due to finite data and smooth function approximators)?
5. Does your analysis suggest a principled stopping rule or training-to-optimality strategy for D once noise is introduced? Do you observe empirical benefits from fully optimizing D each iteration under instance noise?
6. Could you quantify mode coverage improvements under instance noise, beyond visual fidelity, to validate the KL(Pg||Pr)−2JSD analysis?

Overall, the paper offers a compelling, principled view of GAN training pathologies and a theoretically justified remedy with clear practical implications.
