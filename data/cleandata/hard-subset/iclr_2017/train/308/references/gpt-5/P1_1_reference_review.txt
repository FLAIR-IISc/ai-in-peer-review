Summary and contributions
The paper seeks principled explanations for well-known instabilities in GAN training and proposes a theoretically grounded remedy. The main claims are:
- Manifold support and perfect discrimination: For typical neural generators gθ mapping a low-dimensional latent space to a higher-dimensional data space, the support of Pg lies in a union of low-dimensional manifolds. Combined with the manifold structure of Pr, this leads to disjoint or almost-disjoint supports, implying that an optimal discriminator can achieve accuracy 1 and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the supports. Consequences include JSD = log 2 and KL divergences being infinite, regardless of manifold proximity.
- Vanishing and unstable gradients: With the original generator objective, as D approaches optimality, generator gradients vanish (Theorem 2.4). With the −log D trick, the paper identifies the implicit objective gradient at θ0 as ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)], providing a rationale for mode dropping and showing that noisy discriminator approximations can yield heavy-tailed, high-variance generator updates (Theorem 2.6).
- Remedy via smoothing: Adding continuous noise to inputs of both D and G makes Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous and restores meaningful gradients. The paper derives gradient expressions (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2) showing an attractive “move samples toward data and away from overrepresented model regions” behavior, and argues that training with noise targets JSD(Pr+||Pg+), which is well-behaved, while annealing noise shrinks a bound on W(Pr,Pg) (Theorem 3.3). This provides a principled training protocol where D can be trained to near-optimality while gradients remain stable.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Theoretical gaps and inconsistencies undermine central claims (notably in metric definitions/bounds and noise assumptions).
- The proposed remedy (noise-based smoothing and annealing) lacks rigorous empirical validation and clear practical guidance in this submission.

Supporting arguments
- Assumptions and proof rigor:
  - Lemma 1: The argument for g(Z) being a countable union of ≤ dim Z-dimensional manifolds is appealing but mixes “affine” and “linear” transformations and simplifies bias handling. For piecewise-linear networks, the image structure is the union of affine images of regions; calling these “linear manifolds” and omitting biases makes the statement imprecise. The rectifier case also implicitly assumes a finite number of activation patterns for the given network, which should be stated and tied to boundedness of Z or architectural finiteness.
  - Theorem 2.6: The “white noise” assumption—independent Gaussian perturbations of D(x) and ∇xD(x) at each x and mutual independence—is highly unrealistic for neural discriminators trained with SGD. The conclusion (Cauchy-distributed coordinates of the gradient) relies crucially on independence of numerator and denominator. As the authors note, measurability issues are also brushed aside. While the result is pedagogically useful, its strength as an explanation for practice is limited.
  - Theorem 2.5: The identity linking −∇θ log D* to ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD] at θ0 is interesting but requires careful conditions (interchange of differentiation and integration, support conditions) that are not fully spelled out, especially given earlier emphasis that Pg may be singular.
- Metrics and bounds:
  - Definition 3.1 defines the “Wasserstein metric” with an integrand ‖x−y‖^2, while subsequent arguments (e.g., W ≤ C·TV, Theorem 3.3) are standard for W1 (cost ‖x−y‖) or for W2 with a square root. Lemma 4 bounds W(PX,PX+ ) by V^1/2 with V = E‖ε‖^2, which matches a W2-type quantity but not W defined as ∫‖x−y‖^2. The proof of Theorem 3.3 mixes properties of W1 (Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality and bounded cost) and Pinsker’s inequality, leading to a mismatch with the provided definition. This needs careful correction: choose W1 or W2 consistently and adjust constants and inequalities accordingly.
- Empirical support and practicality:
  - The paper references figures showing discriminator behavior and gradient norms but does not provide experimental details here. More importantly, the proposed training scheme (add noise to both D inputs and G outputs and anneal) is not validated empirically in this submission: no quantitative results, ablations, or comparisons (e.g., stability, sample quality, mode coverage) are presented to support claims that it “solves” instability in practice.
  - Practical guidance is limited: how to choose noise distribution/covariance, annealing schedules, interaction with architecture/optimization, and sensitivity to hyperparameters are all open.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Fix the Wasserstein section: clearly define whether you use W1 or W2, and ensure all lemmas and inequalities apply to that choice. Provide rigorous proofs with correct constants and assumptions.
- Clarify and tighten assumptions in Theorems 2.5 and 2.6. For 2.6, consider weaker and more realistic noise models (e.g., small correlated perturbations, finite-precision quantization) and derive tail/variance bounds that still show problematic behavior without requiring independence. For 2.5, state conditions for differentiating under the integral and how singular Pg is handled (e.g., via smoothing).
- Improve Lemma 1’s proof to account for biases and the affine nature of regionwise mappings. If “union of affine submanifolds” is intended, state it precisely and discuss whether the union is finite for a given architecture and latent support.
- Provide empirical validation of the proposed smoothing-annealing training protocol:
  - Datasets and architectures (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA; DCGAN).
  - Compare to baseline GAN training with and without the −log D trick.
  - Show training dynamics (gradient norms, discriminator optimality, stability), mode coverage metrics, FID/IS or suitable alternatives, and qualitative samples.
  - Ablations on noise type, covariance, and annealing schedule; demonstrate robustness.
- Discuss connections to and differences from alternative regularizations that also aim to stabilize D (e.g., Lipschitz constraints, gradient penalties), even briefly, to position your approach in the broader landscape.
- Clean up typos and references (several entries are malformed), and ensure figures referenced are included with captions.

Questions for the authors
- Which Wasserstein metric do you intend to use in Theorem 3.3? Can you provide a corrected, self-consistent statement and proof with explicit constants and assumptions?
- How sensitive is the proposed noise-annealing method to the choice of noise variance schedule and covariance? Do different geometries (anisotropic noise) materially change outcomes?
- Can you provide empirical results demonstrating that training D close to optimality is safe under your smoothing scheme across multiple datasets and architectures?
- In Theorem 2.6, can you relax the white-noise independence assumptions and still obtain meaningful high-variance or heavy-tailed update conclusions?
- For Lemma 1, can you formalize the role of biases and the finiteness of activation patterns to justify the “countable union of manifolds” claim?

Overall, the paper raises important issues and provides thought-provoking analyses and intuitions. With corrected theory and solid empirical validation of the smoothing approach, it could make a strong contribution. In its current form, the theoretical inconsistencies and lack of empirical evidence prevent acceptance.
