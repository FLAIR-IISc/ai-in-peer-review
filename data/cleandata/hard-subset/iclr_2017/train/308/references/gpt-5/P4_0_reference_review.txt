1. Summary of the paper’s contributions
The paper analyzes instability in GAN training from a principled perspective. It argues that, in common settings, both the real data distribution and the generator distribution lie on low-dimensional manifolds; when these manifolds are disjoint or intersect only on measure-zero sets, an optimal discriminator exists that is essentially perfect and locally constant on each support. This yields two key consequences: (i) with the original GAN generator loss, gradients vanish as the discriminator approaches optimality; (ii) with the “−log D” alternative, updates become unstable and high-variance, potentially explaining empirical difficulties and mode dropping.

The paper provides:
- Theorems establishing perfect discrimination and vanishing generator gradients under manifold-support assumptions.
- An analysis relating the “−log D” generator update to the optimization of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr), which emphasizes penalizing unrealistic samples but tolerates mode dropping, matching empirical observations.
- A stylized instability result showing Cauchy-distributed gradient components under a simple white-noise discriminator error model.
- A proposal to add noise to inputs (and to generator outputs during backprop), making distributions absolutely continuous, restoring meaningful gradients and enabling training the discriminator to optimality without destabilizing the generator. The paper connects this to minimizing JSD between the noisy distributions and bounds the Wasserstein distance between the original noiseless distributions in terms of the noise variance and JSD of the noisy distributions.

Overall, the paper aims to provide a theoretical framing of GAN instability and a principled direction (noise injection/annealing and softer metrics) to mitigate it.

2. Decision
Reject, primarily due to:
- Technical soundness concerns in the metric/bounds section (inconsistencies in the Wasserstein distance definition and inequalities used, which affect key results).
- Insufficient empirical support for the proposed remedies and assumptions (results rely on strong modeling assumptions and a stylized noise model, with limited experimental validation provided).

3. Supporting arguments
- Metric definition inconsistency: The paper defines W(P,Q) = infγ ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ(x,y) but then uses properties (triangle inequality, W ≤ C·TV, and scalings with √·) that hold for W1 (or W2 with the appropriate 1/2 power). Lemma 4 concludes W ≤ √V, consistent with W1, but inconsistent with the stated squared-distance definition. The subsequent Theorem 3.3 also appears to use W1-type bounds (via total variation and Pinsker). This mismatch undermines the rigor of the central bound relating W(Pr,Pg) to JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) and noise variance.
- Assumptions for instability result (Theorem 2.6): The analysis assumes white noise, independence across x, and independence between value and gradient errors. This is not a realistic error model for neural discriminators (where errors are structured, correlated, and tied to shared weights). The conclusion (Cauchy-distributed updates with infinite variance) is very sensitive to this assumption. While the result is suggestive, it is not strong evidence for practical instability mechanisms without further justification or ablation.
- Manifold-support assumptions: The arguments rely on the generator support being contained in a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z and the data also concentrated on low-dimensional manifolds. While plausible, some steps are nontrivial (e.g., Lemma 1 for smooth monotone activations and projection arguments) and depend on smoothness/regularity conditions that are only sketched. The results’ practical applicability may hinge on how closely these assumptions hold in typical high-dimensional datasets and modern architectures (which often include noise, data augmentation, and stochastic components).
- Limited empirical evidence: The text references figures indicating vanishing gradients and increasing gradient norms variance as the discriminator improves, but the paper does not provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating that the proposed noise-injection strategy reliably stabilizes GAN training and improves sample quality or diversity over strong baselines. No ablation studies, quantitative metrics, or comparisons are supplied here.
- Clarity and presentation: Despite many clear statements and proofs, the paper contains several typographical errors and notational inconsistencies (e.g., “aboslutely,” “arize,” reference formatting), and crucial technical definitions (Wasserstein) are inconsistent with subsequent use. These issues reduce confidence in the formal rigor.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Fix the Wasserstein section: Clearly specify whether you use W1 or W2. If W2, define W2(P,Q) = (infγ ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ)^(1/2) and adjust all bounds accordingly; if W1, use ∫ ||x−y|| and keep the inequalities consistent (W1 ≤ C·TV and Pinsker to connect TV and KL). Re-derive Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 with the correct metric and constants.
- Strengthen the discriminator-noise error model: Provide a more realistic approximation error model (e.g., correlated Gaussian processes or bounds based on function-class capacity/estimation error) and show instability phenomena persist under weaker assumptions.
- Empirical validation: Implement the proposed noise injection on both discriminator inputs and generator outputs with annealing and compare to strong baselines on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA). Report sample quality and diversity metrics, training stability measures, and ablations on noise level and annealing schedules. Demonstrating that the method allows training the discriminator closer to optimality without destabilizing the generator would substantiate the claims.
- Clarify assumptions and scope: State precisely when the manifold-support assumptions hold (e.g., with/without injected noise in the generator, with stochastic layers, or with specific priors), and discuss limitations (e.g., Peano curve-like counterexamples). Clarify smoothness requirements in Lemmas 2–3 (transversality, Sard’s lemma) and whether they hold for piecewise-linear activations everywhere.
- Editing and references: Fix typos, unify notation, and clean up references and figure captions.

Questions for the authors
- Wasserstein definition: Which Wasserstein metric do you intend to use (W1 or W2)? Please correct the definition and re-derive Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 with consistent assumptions and constants.
- Empirical evidence: Can you provide experiments demonstrating that adding noise to both discriminator inputs and generator outputs with annealing stabilizes training and improves mode coverage compared to standard GAN training, across multiple datasets and architectures?
- Instability theorem robustness: How sensitive is Theorem 2.6 to relaxing the white-noise and independence assumptions? Can you provide bounds or simulations with correlated, structured discriminator errors that more closely resemble learned discriminators?
- Practical algorithm: What is the recommended noise distribution, initial variance, and annealing schedule? How does the method interact with common stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalties, batch norm)?
- Manifold assumptions: In modern settings with data augmentation and injected noise in generators, do your perfect discrimination theorems still apply, or do they already break the measure-zero support issue? Can you quantify when the vanishing-gradient phenomenon disappears as a function of noise level?
