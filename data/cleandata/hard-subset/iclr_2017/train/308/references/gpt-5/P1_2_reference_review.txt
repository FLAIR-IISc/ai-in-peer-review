Summary and contributions
- The paper analyzes why GAN training is unstable and why generator gradients degrade as the discriminator improves. The core argument is geometric: both the data distribution Pr and the generator distribution Pg often concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds in high-dimensional ambient space. Under mild conditions (disjoint or transversally intersecting manifolds of lower dimension), there exists a smooth perfect discriminator that is constant almost everywhere on the supports, leading to zero gradients for the generator with the original log(1−D) objective as D→D* (Theorem 2.4).
- For the alternative generator update −log D, the paper shows the update equals the gradient of KL(Pg||Pr)−2 JSD(Pg||Pr) at θ0 (Theorem 2.5), arguing this encourages sharp, plausible samples but exacerbates mode dropping. With discriminator noise, the generator update becomes Cauchy-distributed with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6), explaining practical instability.
- To address this, the paper proposes smoothing both data and generated distributions by adding noise to the discriminator inputs (and to generator outputs during backprop). This renders Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous, enabling meaningful divergences and stable gradients. The gradient through log(1−D*) becomes a kernel-weighted attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from the model manifold (Theorem 3.2), and with generator noise is proportional to ∇θ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) (Corollary 3.2).
- Finally, the paper links the JSD between smoothed distributions to the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions: W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2 V^1/2 + 2 C sqrt(JSD(Pr+ || Pg+)) (Theorem 3.3), suggesting one can anneal noise while minimizing JSD(Pr+ , Pg+) to reduce W(Pr,Pg). The work offers a principled direction to stabilize GAN training via noise and connects to optimal transport.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Lack of empirical validation or concrete algorithmic instantiation. The paper refers to figures and claims about observed behavior, but no experiments, ablations, or comparisons are presented to substantiate the proposed noise-annealed training procedure or the theoretical predictions.
2) Several theoretical assumptions are strong or under-specified, and some arguments are hand-wavy or depend on idealized conditions (e.g., white noise discriminator errors, optimal discriminator, compactness). This undermines the practical relevance without experiments.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The problem is important and timely. The manifold-support perspective and its consequences for discriminator optimality, JSD saturation, and KL divergence blow-up are well motivated and align with community intuitions. The paper is broadly placed in the GAN literature and classical math (transversality, Sard’s lemma, optimal transport).
- Correctness and rigor: Many results are plausible and insightful, but some are not fully rigorous as presented:
  - The perfect discrimination results rely on compactness and ideal separations; practical neural discriminators are finite and trained with SGD. The transition from function-existence to the behavior of finite networks is not addressed.
  - The vanishing gradient bound (Theorem 2.4) uses a norm closeness assumption ‖D−D*‖<ε including gradient terms, which is a strong requirement for neural networks trained from finite data. The dependence on denominators (1−D) near 1 is fine, but constants and conditions are not fully spelled out.
  - The instability result (Theorem 2.6) assumes independent white noise in D and ∇D across x and independence between numerator and denominator, which is unrealistic; the conclusion is qualitative rather than robust. A more general treatment (e.g., correlated Gaussian processes with Lipschitz assumptions) would strengthen the claim.
  - The claim that with generator noise the gradient is “proportional to ∇θ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+)” is asserted without a full derivation; proportionality constants and conditions matter for optimization.
  - The Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) is conceptually nice, but the constants (e.g., Pinsker factor) and support conditions (bounded diameter C) are restrictive and may not hold in common image domains.
- Contribution strength: The paper’s main value is conceptual clarification and a proposed direction (noise smoothing + annealing). However, without an actual training algorithm and empirical evidence that this stabilizes GANs and reduces mode dropping relative to standard baselines, the contribution is incomplete for ICLR. Additionally, the connection to Wasserstein suggests an alternative metric to directly optimize, yet the paper stops short of proposing or testing such an approach.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a concrete algorithmic instantiation:
  - Specify the noise distributions for both discriminator inputs and generator outputs, the annealing schedule (e.g., σ(t)), how to estimate D and update G, and how to prevent adversarial pathologies during annealing.
  - Include extensive experiments on standard datasets (CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN) with metrics (e.g., FID/IS, precision/recall) and qualitative samples. Compare to strong baselines and stabilization techniques (batch norm, spectral norm, gradient penalties, label smoothing).
  - Ablate: no noise vs D-only noise vs D+G noise; different σ schedules; kernel shapes (Gaussian vs heavy-tailed) as suggested by Corollary 3.1; sensitivity analyses.
- Tighten theory:
  - Relax the independence/white-noise assumption in Theorem 2.6; give conditions (e.g., bounded correlation, sub-Gaussian) under which heavy-tailed behavior or exploding variance persists.
  - Formalize Corollary 3.2’s proportionality to ∇θ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+), including constants and assumptions ensuring unbiasedness and stability.
  - Clarify Lemma 1’s scope for ReLU networks at non-differentiable boundaries; argue measure-zero boundary sets don’t affect training conclusions.
  - Make constants explicit in Theorem 3.3 (Pinsker factor, dependence on σ via V), and discuss how the bound behaves as σ→0 and its practical interpretability.
- Improve clarity and presentation:
  - Include the referenced figures. Clean up references formatting. Define all norms and expectations precisely. Move key proofs into the main text or provide more intuition where heavy tools are used (Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, transversality).
  - Discuss how finite sample estimation and limited discriminator capacity affect the “perfect discriminator” conclusions and the vanishing gradient phenomena.

Questions for the authors
1) Algorithmic details: What exact training procedure do you propose? What noise distributions and annealing schedule do you recommend, and how do you choose them in practice?
2) Practical discriminator: How sensitive are your conclusions to discriminator capacity and imperfect optimization? Do you observe vanishing gradients and instability trends as D improves in your own experiments?
3) Generator noise: Can you rigorously derive that adding noise to generator outputs makes the gradient equal to ∇θ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+), and quantify the constants? Does this hold with finite-capacity D?
4) Robustness of Theorem 2.6: How do the results change if D and ∇D noise are correlated or not white? Can you provide weaker assumptions yielding high variance or bias in −log D updates?
5) Wasserstein connection: Can you empirically validate inequality (6) by tracking JSD(Pr+ , Pg+) during training and correlating it with an estimate of W(Pr,Pg) or sample quality? How does the bound guide choosing σ(t)?
6) Mode dropping: Can you quantify how the proposed method alleviates mode dropping (e.g., via precision/recall or coverage metrics) compared to standard GAN training?

Overall, the paper offers valuable theoretical insights and a promising direction, but it needs a concrete method and thorough empirical evidence to meet ICLR standards.
