Summary of contributions
- Problem tackled: Why GAN training becomes unstable as the discriminator improves, and how to design objectives/metrics that yield informative, stable gradients when data and model lie on low-dimensional manifolds.
- Main ideas and claims:
  1) If Pr and Pg have support on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds that do not perfectly align, an optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant on the supports; JSD saturates at log 2 and both KLs diverge. This explains vanishing gradients for the generator with the original GAN loss and the empirical brittleness of “train D to optimality.”
  2) For the non-saturating -log D generator update, the paper shows it corresponds (locally) to optimizing KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr), biasing toward sharp but mode-dropping solutions; with noisy D estimates, the update can have Cauchy-distributed heavy tails (instability).
  3) Proposed direction: inject continuous noise into discriminator (and generator) inputs so that Pr+ and Pg+ become absolutely continuous; derive gradients that act like distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from over-dense model regions. Show that with noise in both paths, the generator effectively optimizes JSD(Pr+‖Pg+), yielding stable gradients, and with annealed noise one can still minimize a bound on the Wasserstein distance W(Pr,Pg).
  4) Provide a bound tying W(Pr,Pg) to the noise variance and JSD(Pr+‖Pg+), motivating noise annealing and suggesting principled evaluation via Wasserstein.
Overall, the paper offers a clear theoretical diagnosis of GAN failure modes and a principled, testable remedy (noise smoothing + annealing), plus a bridge to OT metrics.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, novel theoretical perspective that explains widely observed GAN pathologies and unifies them under a manifold-support view.
- Bold, principled training direction (noise smoothing with annealing) and a metric connection (to Wasserstein) that can guide future algorithmic designs and evaluations.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well grounded in the known empirical issues of GANs and situates the divergences (KL/JSD) appropriately. The manifold-support perspective and transversality arguments are insightful and timely.
- Correctness and rigor: Most claims align with established measure-theoretic and differential-topology facts (e.g., JSD saturation/log 2, KL infinities with disjoint supports; transversality/perturbation arguments; convolution with noise yields absolute continuity). The vanishing-gradient theorem is consistent with practice. The heavy-tailed update result under a white-noise discriminator is a useful stylized model, though the independence assumption is strong.
- Proposed fix: Adding noise to both D and G inputs to smooth distributions is principled. The derived gradients (distance-weighted attraction/repulsion) are intuitive and address the zero-measure adversarial issue. The annealing strategy is compelling and compatible with training D to near-optimality.
- Novelty: The connection to Wasserstein and the explicit bound linking W(Pr,Pg) to JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) under noise is conceptually impactful and suggests a new research direction and evaluation lens.

Caveats and limitations (not decision-critical)
- Theorem 2.6 assumes independent white noise for D and ∇D; this idealization likely overstates heavy tails. Empirical evidence is referenced but not shown here; more systematic experiments would strengthen the claim.
- In Theorem 3.3 there appears to be a potential mismatch of constants/units: since W is defined as an infimum over squared distances, the bound via total variation on a set with diameter C should likely involve C^2 rather than C. Please check the exact Wasserstein order/definition (W2 vs W1) and constants.
- Empirical validation is light in the text (figures referenced but not included). A stronger empirical section would help (e.g., robustness to D optimality, annealing schedules, mode coverage).

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the Wasserstein definition you use (W1 vs W2 vs squared W2) and ensure constants are dimensionally consistent throughout Theorem 3.3. If using W2, consider presenting the result in terms of W2 or W1 explicitly.
- For Theorem 2.5, add a self-contained derivation or an appendix proof, and make the locality assumptions around θ0 and regularity conditions explicit.
- Discuss the realism of the noise assumptions in Theorem 2.6. A weaker assumption (e.g., small, correlated perturbations with bounded covariance and some mixing) would make the instability claim more compelling.
- Provide practical guidance: how to inject noise (distribution, covariance shaping), how to choose and anneal σ, and how sensitive performance is to these choices. A brief algorithmic schematic would be welcome.
- Empirics: show that noisy training (noise in both D and G paths) stabilizes updates, improves mode coverage, and reduces sensitivity to D/G update ratios across datasets and architectures. Include comparisons to strong baselines.
- Notation and minor issues: fix typos, ensure all references are properly formatted, and clarify norms/spaces for the function approximations (Sobolev norms vs sup norms).

Questions for the authors
1) Theorem 3.3: Which Wasserstein variant is intended? If it is the squared W2, shouldn’t the TV-based bound scale with C^2? Please clarify the constants and the bounded-diameter assumption.
2) How critical is adding noise to the generator path (beyond the discriminator)? Can you quantify the adversarial-example failure mode if noise is used only on D?
3) What annealing schedules (σ(t)) worked best empirically? How sensitive are results to σ0 and the decay rate?
4) Can you provide experiments that directly measure gradient norms/variances as D approaches optimality, comparing standard GAN vs your noisy training?
5) Do the manifold and transversality assumptions reasonably approximate modern image datasets and generators (e.g., with convolutions and skip connections)? Any counterexamples where supports are not low dimensional?
6) How would your analysis extend to Lipschitz-constrained discriminators, and does that alter the vanishing-gradient conclusions?

Overall, this paper advances a fresh, theoretically grounded view of GAN training and proposes a principled fix. Despite some technical assumptions and limited empirical validation, the conceptual novelty and clarity justify acceptance.
