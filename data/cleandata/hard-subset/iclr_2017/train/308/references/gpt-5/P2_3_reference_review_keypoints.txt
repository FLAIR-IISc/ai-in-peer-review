- Paper analyzes GAN instability, formalizing that Pr and Pg often lie on low-dimensional, nearly disjoint manifolds leading to near-perfect, locally flat discriminators and vanishing generator gradients.
- Proven claims: (i) “perfect discrimination” theorems for disjoint/non-aligning manifolds, (ii) vanishing generator gradients under the original GAN loss as D→optimal, (iii) instability for the −log D update under noisy D modeled as Cauchy-distributed gradients.
- Proposed remedy: add input “instance noise” (to both real and generated samples), derive smoothed generator gradients as distance-weighted attraction/repulsion, recommend backpropagating through generator noise, and present a Wasserstein bound linking noise variance and JSD to W(Pr,Pg).
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary reason 1: Core theoretical claims rely on strong/unrealistic assumptions (white-noise discriminator errors, independence of D and ∇D noise, idealized manifold/transversality and measurability conditions) that limit practical applicability.
- Primary reason 2: The instance-noise remedy is not novel enough and lacks comprehensive, rigorous empirical validation against strong baselines.
- Related-work gap: Missing discussion of broader f-divergence literature, MMD-GAN, and Wasserstein-GAN approaches with Lipschitz constraints and gradient penalties.
- Correctness note: Perfect discriminator and vanishing-gradient intuitions are sound as idealizations but depend on strong regularity assumptions.
- Theorem 2.5 (−log D): Interesting link to ∇θ[KL − 2 JSD] but requires stricter conditions (exchange of gradient and integral, fixed D∗ at θ0) and clearer interpretation of the negative JSD term.
- Theorem 2.6 (instability/Cauchy): Uses unrealistic independence/Gaussian noise assumptions and is suggestive qualitatively but too idealized to explain practical instability rigorously.
- Noise-convolution results (Theorem 3.1, Corollary 3.2): Correct and useful—show smoothing yields overlap and interpretable gradients; recommending noise on generator is practical insight.
- Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3): Conceptually aligns noise/JSD reduction with reduced W(Pr,Pg) but glosses constants/conditions (support diameter C, exact Pinsker usage) making it non-tight/unpractical as a training guarantee.
- Empirical shortcomings: No systematic experiments, missing comparisons to SN-GAN/WGAN-GP and other stabilizers, and no standard metrics (FID/IS/precision-recall) or dataset-scale evaluations.
- Suggested improvements: expand related work, tighten theoretical assumptions/derivations (esp. Theorems 2.5/2.6), make constants explicit in bounds, and add comprehensive experiments and ablations across datasets and baselines.
- Suggested specifics for experiments: compare baseline GAN vs instance-noise variants and modern stabilizers on CIFAR-10/CelebA/LSUN, report FID/IS and stability metrics, and ablate noise variance and annealing schedules.
- Clarify scope: discuss practical situations that already mitigate manifold-pathologies (augmentation, encoders, continuous observation models) and how conclusions change.
- Presentation fixes: unify notation, fix typos, and move key proofs into main text where possible.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity and ablation of noise-annealing schedule and noise distribution; empirical evidence of heavy-tailed generator updates under −log D; interaction with Lipschitz-constrained discriminators (SN, GP); detailed derivation and predictive value of Theorem 2.5; tightening/better constants for Theorem 3.3 and empirical correlation between JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) and W; does noise on both sides hurt final fidelity and how to avoid late-stage instability when annealing.
