Summary and contributions
- The paper analyzes instability in GAN training via a geometric/measure-theoretic lens. It argues that when Pr and Pg live on low-dimensional manifolds with (almost surely) disjoint support, an optimal discriminator exists that is essentially perfect and flat almost everywhere on the supports, implying vanishing gradients for the generator under the original log(1−D) objective. It then shows that the commonly used −log D update corresponds (at θ0) to the gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr), explaining empirically observed mode dropping and instability.
- The paper further shows that noisy approximations to the optimal discriminator yield highly unstable generator updates (Cauchy-distributed), formalizing the high variance practitioners observe. As a remedy, it proposes adding noise to both real and generated samples (instance noise), derives that this leads to optimizing JSD between smoothed distributions Pr+ and Pg+, and proves a bound relating the Wasserstein distance W(Pr,Pg) to the noise variance and JSD(Pr+‖Pg+), suggesting that annealing the noise can yield a principled and stable training signal.
- The manuscript provides several theorems (with proofs or references) supporting this narrative: image of latent under common NN generators is a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z; perfect discrimination theorems on manifolds; vanishing gradients; instability under noisy −log D updates; smoothing makes divergences well-behaved; and a bound linking W(Pr,Pg) to JSD on smoothed distributions.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Insufficient empirical validation and comparison: The central practical claim—that adding instance noise and annealing it stabilizes GAN training and improves outcomes—lacks rigorous experiments against established baselines. References to “Figure 1/2/3” are not accompanied by a thorough, quantitative evaluation, and there is no concrete training algorithm or empirical study demonstrating superior performance.
2) Technical assumptions and rigor: Several core results rely on restrictive or unrealistic assumptions (e.g., discriminator approximation errors modeled as spatially white independent Gaussian processes leading to Cauchy updates), and some important statements are left as exercises or are hand-wavy about measurability/regularity. The theoretical development does not convincingly transfer to the finite-capacity, finite-data setting used in practice.

Supporting arguments
- Practical impact not demonstrated: The proposal reduces to instance noise with annealing. While the theory is suggestive, there is no systematic empirical study on standard datasets and metrics (e.g., FID, precision/recall for generative models) nor comparisons to established stabilization methods (e.g., gradient penalties, spectral normalization, two-time-scale updates). Claims such as “we can train the discriminator till optimality” under noise are not supported experimentally.
- Unrealistic noise model in Theorem 2.6: Modeling D∗−D and its gradients as independent white Gaussian processes in x is a very strong and arguably unrealistic assumption for trained neural discriminators. The authors themselves note measurability issues. The resulting Cauchy-distribution conclusion thus has tenuous practical relevance.
- Gaps in rigor and completeness: Some results (e.g., Theorem 2.3) are “left as an exercise,” and several proofs depend on strong regularity or compactness assumptions (e.g., compact supports, smooth Urysohn functions) that are not reconciled with practical image domains or network parameterizations. The bounded-support assumption in Theorem 3.3 (smoothed distributions lie in a ball of diameter C) conflicts with Gaussian instance noise unless one clips or otherwise modifies the setting; this needs careful treatment.
- Limited novelty relative to known ideas: The manifold-support explanation for perfect discrimination/vanishing gradients and the use of instance noise to induce overlap are known in the community, and the link to Wasserstein metrics is suggestive but stops short of delivering a practically implementable, principled alternative (e.g., no Lipschitz-constrained critic). The gradient identity for −log D leans on known observations (and is closely related to prior analyses).
- Scope of lemmas: Lemma 1’s structural statement about g(Z) being a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z under typical activations is interesting, but the implications for realistic convolutional architectures with various layers, noise injections, and normalization deserve more precise scope.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a clear algorithmic instantiation: specify the exact training procedure with instance noise, schedules for noise annealing, and whether noise is added to G’s inputs/outputs and to real data. Clarify how discriminator and generator updates are interleaved.
- Add strong empirical validation: benchmark on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN), report FID and precision/recall curves versus baselines (vanilla GAN, spectral norm, gradient penalty, WGAN-GP), and conduct ablations on noise level/schedule. Measure gradient norms/variance and stability metrics to substantiate the theory.
- Relax and justify assumptions: replace the white-noise discriminator error model with a more realistic approximation error model (e.g., correlated, bounded, Lipschitz) and re-derive stability conclusions. Address measurability and capacity explicitly.
- Tighten the theory around Theorem 3.3: handle unbounded supports induced by Gaussian noise (e.g., via sub-Gaussian tail bounds or truncation) and quantify constants. Discuss whether the bound can guide noise schedules in practice.
- Clarify novelty and relation to prior work: situate the contribution relative to earlier analyses of GAN divergences, instance noise practices, and Wasserstein-based approaches. Discuss advantages/limitations versus Lipschitz-constrained critics.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the noise distribution and annealing schedule? Can you provide empirical guidance or a theoretically grounded schedule that works reliably across datasets?
2) Does adding noise to both real and generated samples degrade sample quality or slow convergence in practice? Please quantify.
3) Can you reproduce the claimed stability when the discriminator is trained “to optimality” under noise, compared to standard heuristics (e.g., limited D steps)?
4) How robust are your conclusions under more realistic discriminator approximation errors (correlated, bounded, Lipschitz)? Can you avoid the white-noise assumption and still show heavy-tailed updates or instability?
5) Can you compare directly against WGAN(-GP) and spectral normalization, both theoretically (assumption sets) and empirically (metrics, stability), to support the claim that your approach is principled and competitive?
6) The bound in Theorem 3.3 requires smoothed distributions to lie in a bounded ball. How do you satisfy this with Gaussian noise? Do you clip or use compactly supported noise, and how does that affect practice?

Overall, the paper offers interesting theoretical insights, but the lack of rigorous, convincing empirical evidence and reliance on strong assumptions make it premature for acceptance.
