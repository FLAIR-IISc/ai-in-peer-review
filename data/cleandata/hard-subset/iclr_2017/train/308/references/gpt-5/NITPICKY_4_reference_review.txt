Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper analyzes instability in GAN training when the data distribution Pr and the generator distribution Pg lie on (typically different) low-dimensional manifolds. It argues that in such settings an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate Pr and Pg and be (almost) constant on their supports, leading to vanishing generator gradients for the original GAN objective and unstable gradients for the “-log D” alternative.
- It formalizes “perfect discrimination” on disjoint or non-transversally intersecting manifolds, provides lemmas about the image of latent spaces under neural networks (support lies in a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z), and proves vanishing gradients with near-optimal discriminators for the original generator objective.
- It analyzes the “-log D” generator update, deriving a link to ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)] at θ0 and shows, under a particular noise model on the discriminator, the generator updates have Cauchy-like heavy tails (instability).
- It proposes adding noise (instance noise) to both discriminator and generator inputs to mollify/smooth the distributions (Pr+ and Pg+), obtaining meaningful JSD and non-degenerate gradients, and derives gradient expressions as weighted integrals that push samples toward the data manifold and away from regions dominated by Pg.
- It introduces a bound relating the Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg to the variance of the injected noise and JSD(Pr+||Pg+), suggesting a principled annealing schedule.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons
1) Foundational inconsistencies and mathematical inaccuracies in the Wasserstein section (definitions, constants, and inequalities appear incorrect), undermining a central contribution.
2) Heavy reliance on highly unrealistic assumptions (e.g., white-noise discriminator errors independent at each x; measurability hand-waving), several hand-wavy or incomplete proofs, and numerous notation/formatting errors. The presentation quality significantly impairs clarity and confidence.
3) Lack of empirical evidence. Figures referenced in the text are not present; there is no quantitative evaluation of the proposed instance-noise method, annealing schedule, or claimed improvements in stability/mode dropping.

Supporting arguments
- Wasserstein definition/problem: The paper defines W(P,Q) = infγ ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ. This is the squared 2-Wasserstein cost (not a metric), whereas standard W2 is the square root of that infimum and W1 uses ||x−y||. Subsequent inequalities (e.g., W ≤ C δ, KR dual, Pinsker) are typically stated for W1 (or with adjusted constants), and do not apply as written to the squared-cost formulation. The bound in Lemma 4 (W(PX,PX+ ) ≤ V1/2 with V = E||ε||^2) mixes norms and moments inconsistently with the earlier definition; the derivation conflates E||ε|| with E||ε||^2 and applies Jensen incorrectly. Theorem 3.3 builds on these and thus inherits the definitional errors and incorrect constants. This is a major flaw because these bounds are central to the proposed rationale for noise-annealed training.
- Unrealistic/noisy discriminator model: Theorem 2.6 assumes D*−D and ∇xD*−∇xD are independent white-noise Gaussian processes, independent at every x and mutually independent. This assumption is not realistic for neural network discriminators. The authors acknowledge non-measurability and “bypass” it. The conclusion (Cauchy-like updates with infinite variance) critically depends on these assumptions; as such it does not strongly support claims about practical instability.
- “Perfect discriminator” existence vs optimization: Theorems 2.1/2.2 show existence of smooth D* constant on neighborhoods of supports under manifold assumptions. However, this says little about whether realistic discriminators trained with finite data/capacity approximate such D* in the required Sobolev norm for the generator-gradient claims to apply. The paper’s claims about “training D to optimality” and JSD “maxing out” are also asserted without adequate empirical support.
- Lemma 1 (image of Z under a neural network): The ReLU case claims g(Z) ⊆ finite union of linear manifolds via a finite set of diagonal activation masks. This ignores that the set of possible activation patterns can be extremely large and, over unbounded Z, may be infinite; more importantly, the proof mixes global and local arguments and relies on a nontrivial Morse-theory claim (“critical points of πi are isolated”) without conditions. The smooth-monotone activation case also relies on diffeomorphism arguments that could be made precise but are not here; overall the lemma is plausible but under-justified.
- Several derivations lack rigor or have sign/constant ambiguities (e.g., Theorem 2.4’s bound uses undefined M and ε with mis-typed inequalities; Theorem 2.5’s decomposition relies on a blog-note identity and deserves a careful, self-contained proof).
- Presentation quality issues: Many typos and grammatical errors (“symetrical,” “adresssing,” “transversallity,” “aboslutely,” etc.), broken section numbering (.2.2), missing figures, and broken/bare references with placeholders (e.g., “Goodfellow et al.,? \Q2014\E”). These issues complicate verification and detract from clarity.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Fix the Wasserstein section thoroughly. Choose W1 or W2, define it correctly, and re-derive all bounds with correct constants and assumptions. If using W1, state KR dual and ensure inequalities (W1 ≤ C δ, Pinsker) are valid as used. If using W2, adjust constants and steps accordingly.
- Replace the white-noise discriminator error model with a more realistic assumption set (e.g., bounded approximation error in L∞/Sobolev norms; correlated noise), and ensure measurability. Revisit the heavy-tail conclusion under realistic conditions or present simulations that support the qualitative claim.
- Provide complete, precise proofs or move technical claims to an appendix with all necessary conditions (e.g., Lemma 1, use of Morse theory, “smooth Urysohn lemma” conditions, measure-zero arguments).
- Include empirical evidence: experiments demonstrating instance noise + annealing improves stability, reduces mode dropping, and matches the theoretical intuitions; provide metrics (e.g., FID/KID), ablations, and sensitivity to noise schedule.
- Clean up notation, fix all typos, correct broken references, and include the referenced figures. Clarify assumptions on Z, compactness, supports, and densities.
- Position relative to prior work on f-divergences, instance noise, gradient penalties, and Wasserstein-based training; discuss pros/cons and when your approach is preferable.

Questions for the authors
1) Which Wasserstein distance do you intend (W1 or W2)? Please provide corrected definitions and all subsequent bounds with proper constants and assumptions.
2) Can you provide rigorous, self-contained proofs for Theorem 2.5 and Lemma 1 (including conditions under which the projection critical points are isolated)?
3) How sensitive are your conclusions to the discriminator error model? Can you relax the independence/white-noise assumptions and still obtain instability (or at least high-variance) results?
4) What empirical protocol and noise-annealing schedule do you recommend? Please provide experiments validating your theory (stability curves, FID/KID, mode coverage) and compare to strong baselines.
5) In Theorem 2.4, please clarify the norms and constants (M, ε). The inequality as written is hard to parse and appears to contain typos.
