1) Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the instability of GAN training from a principled perspective. It makes the case that, in typical settings, both the data distribution and the generator distribution are supported on low-dimensional manifolds in high-dimensional ambient space. The authors show that under such support mismatch, the optimal discriminator attains perfect classification and is locally constant on the supports, which (i) saturates divergences such as KL and JS, (ii) leads to vanishing generator gradients under the original GAN loss, and (iii) causes highly unstable updates under the −log D surrogate when the discriminator is noisy. To mitigate these issues, the paper proposes injecting continuous noise into the inputs of the discriminator (and to the generator for backprop), which smooths the distributions so the discriminator meaningfully overlaps the two supports. It shows that the generator gradients then correspond to moving samples toward the data manifold while repelling overrepresented model regions. Finally, it connects this “instance noise” approach to the Wasserstein metric by bounding W(Pr,Pg) via a term involving the JSD of the smoothed distributions plus a term depending on the noise variance, suggesting an annealing strategy: minimize JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) while decaying the noise.

2) Decision
Reject. Key reasons:
- The paper’s central claims, while insightful, rely on strong and sometimes unrealistic assumptions, and the empirical support is very limited compared to the breadth of the theoretical claims.
- The proposed practical remedy (noise injection/annealing) is under-specified and not evaluated rigorously against existing stabilization methods.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem formulation and motivation: The paper is well motivated and addresses a highly relevant question. The intuition that Pr and Pg often live on low-dimensional manifolds is well argued, and the lemma that neural-network generators map to countable unions of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z is useful.
- Placement in literature: The theoretical framing around JS saturation and mode dropping aligns with prior observations in GANs, and the connection to Wasserstein distance is timely. However, related lines such as instance/noise regularization and other stabilization strategies (e.g., alternative f-divergences, Lipschitz-enforcing critics) are not sufficiently covered or compared.
- Theoretical results: 
  - The “perfect discriminator” theorems under disjoint/low-dimensional supports are plausible and technically interesting; they formalize a long-held intuition about gradient starvation.
  - The vanishing gradient result for the original generator objective under a near-optimal discriminator is compelling and consistent with practice.
  - The instability result for −log D hinges on an overly strong and nonstandard noise model (white, independent Gaussian processes for D and ∇D at each x, with acknowledged measurability issues). While the conclusion (heavy-tailed, high-variance updates) fits empirical reports, the assumptions limit how much weight one can place on the theorem.
  - The smoothed-distribution gradient expressions and their interpretation (attraction to high-density data regions, repulsion from overrepresented model regions) are illuminating. The equivalence to minimizing JSD between convolved distributions when noise is added to generator inputs is a nice clarifying result.
  - The bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) is interesting as a conceptual bridge, but constants are loose, and the result is not turned into a concrete, validated algorithmic guarantee.
- Empirical evidence: The paper references figures demonstrating discriminator saturation, gradient vanishing, and gradient explosion under −log D, but the experimental section is sparse and lacks systematic quantitative evaluation. There is no rigorous comparison of the proposed noise-annealed training against standard baselines or recent stabilization techniques. This gap is significant given the paper’s practical claims.
- Clarity and presentation: The writing is generally clear on the high-level story, but there are many typos/formatting issues and several heavy mathematical arguments that depend on nontrivial geometric/topological assumptions (compactness, transversality, Sard’s lemma). Some proofs are deferred; some assumptions (e.g., white-noise discriminator approximation) are not well justified for realistic training.

4) Suggestions to improve (constructive, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Provide a concrete algorithmic specification for the proposed method: where precisely to inject noise (real, fake, both), what noise families and variances to use, and an annealing schedule. Include pseudo-code and practical guidance (e.g., relation to batch norm, data pre-processing).
- Substantially expand the empirical section:
  - Compare the noise-annealed training against strong baselines on standard datasets, with metrics for both fidelity and mode coverage (e.g., FID, precision/recall for generative models).
  - Ablate the noise variance schedule, noise type (Gaussian vs others), and whether noise is added to generator inputs during backprop.
  - Visualize the training dynamics (discriminator accuracy, gradient norms, stability) under different settings.
- Relax and/or justify theoretical assumptions:
  - Replace the white-noise discriminator model with more realistic approximations (e.g., bounded approximation error in Sobolev norms), and derive bounds on gradient variance under those assumptions.
  - Discuss the genericity of the manifold/transversality assumptions, and clarify exactly which activations and architectures satisfy Lemma 1.
- Connect to and compare with related work:
  - Prior uses of input/instance noise or data augmentation for GAN stabilization.
  - Alternative divergences and Wasserstein-based approaches; clarify similarities/differences and when the proposed strategy is preferable.
- Strengthen the Wasserstein connection:
  - Tighten constants if possible, or provide empirical evidence that minimizing JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) while annealing indeed correlates with decreasing W(Pr,Pg).
  - Consider whether the bound can guide an adaptive annealing schedule.
- Improve presentation quality: fix references, typos, figure availability, and clarify technical footnotes (e.g., measurability issues in Theorem 2.6).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is the proposed method to the choice of noise variance and its schedule? Can you provide guidance or an adaptive rule supported by experiments?
- Do you add noise to both real and generated samples during discriminator training, and to generator outputs during backprop? Does adding noise only to one side break the theoretical guarantees?
- How does the approach compare empirically to other stabilization methods (e.g., alternative losses, Lipschitz constraints, gradient penalties) on standard benchmarks in terms of stability, sample quality, and mode coverage?
- Can the instability analysis of −log D be reproduced under weaker and more realistic assumptions on the discriminator approximation error?
- In practice, modern generators often use skip connections, attention, and non-pointwise components. Do the manifold-support results (Lemma 1) extend straightforwardly to those architectures?
- Can you provide empirical evidence that the JSD between smoothed distributions decreases as training proceeds and that this correlates with reductions in an estimate of W(Pr,Pg)?

Overall, the paper offers valuable intuition and several interesting theoretical results, but it needs stronger empirical validation, clearer algorithmic specification, and more realistic assumptions to meet the bar for ICLR.
