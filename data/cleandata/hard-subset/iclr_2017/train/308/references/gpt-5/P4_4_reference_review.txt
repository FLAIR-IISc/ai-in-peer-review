1. Summary of contributions
The paper addresses instability in GAN training by analyzing why, as the discriminator approaches optimality, generator updates either vanish (with the original GAN objective) or become highly unstable (with the “−log D” alternative). The main technical contributions are:
- Manifold-support analysis: It formalizes that, under common architectures, the generator distribution lies on a low-dimensional manifold; combined with the widely hypothesized low-dimensional structure of real data, this implies disjoint or low-overlap supports. The paper proves the existence of smooth discriminators that perfectly separate such supports and are (almost everywhere) constant on them, explaining vanishing/discontinuous gradients.
- Gradient pathologies: It shows vanishing generator gradients for the original objective as the discriminator improves (Theorem 2.4), and argues that with a noisy approximation of the optimal discriminator the “−log D” updates have Cauchy-like heavy tails (Theorem 2.6), leading to instability.
- Smoothing via instance noise: It proposes adding continuous noise to both discriminator inputs and generator outputs, making the smoothed distributions absolutely continuous. It derives the resulting generator gradient as a weighted attraction/repulsion to data/model manifolds (Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2), relating it to optimizing JSD between smoothed distributions.
- Bridge to Wasserstein: It motivates the Wasserstein distance as an intrinsic measure between the original non-overlapping distributions and provides a bound relating W(Pr,Pg) to the variance of the added noise and JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) (Theorem 3.3). This provides a principled rationale for annealing noise while minimizing a divergence between smoothed distributions.

Overall, the paper offers a coherent theoretical narrative for known GAN training pathologies and a principled route (instance noise + annealing) for stabilizing training, with connections to integral probability metrics.

2. Decision
Reject. Key reasons:
- Insufficient empirical evidence: The paper makes strong claims about training dynamics and stabilization, but provides no quantitative experiments and references figures that are not included.
- Technical issues and assumptions: Some derivations rely on restrictive or questionable assumptions (e.g., white-noise, independent perturbations of D and ∇D; measurability caveats), and there appear to be inconsistencies in the Wasserstein bound relative to the defined cost.

3. Supporting arguments
- On empirical support: The paper repeatedly references Figure 1–3 as empirical validation of discriminator behavior and gradient pathologies, but these are not provided. No experiments demonstrate that the proposed noise scheme improves GAN training across datasets, architectures, or compare against standard heuristics. For ICLR, where empirical validation is typically required even for theory-driven contributions that target practice, this is a significant gap.
- On technical soundness:
  - Theorem 2.6 assumes D*−D and ∇D*−∇D are independent white Gaussian processes indexed by x, and acknowledges non-measurability issues “commonly bypassed.” This is a strong and arguably unrealistic assumption in the neural network setting; discriminator approximation errors are structured and correlated over x. The resulting Cauchy conclusion is thus not robust.
  - The definition of the Wasserstein metric uses squared Euclidean cost ∫||x−y||^2 dγ. Later steps invoke W ≤ C δ(P,Q) via Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality and Pinsker-type bounds that typically apply to 1-Wasserstein (linear cost). With squared cost, the bound should scale with C^2 and constants differ; the argument as written appears to mix W1/W2 settings and omits constants. This weakens Theorem 3.3 as stated.
  - Several results depend on idealized manifold assumptions (exact low-dimensional supports, transversality, compactness) and perfect smooth discriminators, which may not hold in practical high-dimensional, finite-data regimes or under common regularization.
- On clarity and placement:
  - While the paper is generally well written and mathematically motivated, there are numerous typos and missing references to related work that already suggested instance noise for GAN stabilization. The paper would benefit from a clearer algorithmic prescription (how to add noise, annealing schedules, implementation details) and a discussion contrasting with other stabilization techniques.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarify the Wasserstein definition and bounds:
  - Specify whether you use W1 or W2 (squared cost). If using squared cost, adjust the inequality chain (W ≤ C δ, Pinsker constants) with correct constants and assumptions (bounded support vs. bounded diameter). Provide a self-contained lemma for that step or cite a precise source.
- Strengthen Theorem 2.6 or re-scope its claim:
  - Replace white-noise independence with a more realistic approximation model (e.g., Lipschitz-bounded approximation error with spatial correlation), or present a robustness argument showing heavy-tailed behavior persists under weaker assumptions. Alternatively, move it to a heuristic section and de-emphasize formal claims.
- Provide empirical validation:
  - Implement the proposed instance noise in standard GANs, compare against baselines on MNIST/CIFAR/ImageNet subsets, and show its effect on training stability, gradient norms, mode coverage (e.g., Inception Score, FID, precision/recall), and sensitivity to annealing schedules.
  - Ablate noise magnitudes, covariance structures, and whether noise is added to G, D, or both.
- Algorithmic clarity:
  - Present a concise algorithm box describing training with noise, including annealing schedule and how it interfaces with discriminator updates. Discuss computational overhead and any interaction with batch normalization and regularization.
- Related work:
  - Discuss prior uses of input noise/instance noise in GANs and connections to other smoothing/regularization techniques (e.g., label smoothing, gradient penalties, spectral normalization). Situate your theoretical justification versus earlier empirical observations.
- Notational and presentation polish:
  - Fix typos, ensure all figures and appendices are included, and standardize notation (Pr, Pg, densities). Clearly separate assumptions from conclusions in theorem statements.

Questions for the authors
- Can you reconcile the definition of the Wasserstein metric used (squared cost) with the later use of KR duality and total variation bounds typically tied to W1? Please provide a corrected, constant-tight version of Theorem 3.3.
- How sensitive is the proposed method to the noise distribution and annealing schedule? Can you provide empirical plots showing JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) decreasing and the corresponding behavior of W estimates or surrogate metrics as noise is annealed?
- For Theorem 2.6, can you offer a version under weaker, structured approximation errors (e.g., bounded, correlated noise) and still conclude heavy-tailed or high-variance updates? If not, how should practitioners interpret this result?
- Do you have experiments demonstrating improved mode coverage and stability from adding noise to both G and D, compared with adding noise only to D?
- How does your approach compare empirically with alternative stabilizations (feature matching, gradient penalties, spectral normalization) and with training objectives targeting Wasserstein/IPM divergences?

Overall, the paper offers a valuable theoretical perspective and a promising direction (instance noise + annealing, with links to Wasserstein), but it currently lacks rigorous empirical validation and contains technical issues that should be resolved before acceptance.
