Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles a central question in GANs: why training is unstable and why generator gradients often vanish or explode as the discriminator improves. The authors argue that a key culprit is the mismatch of supports: both the data distribution Pr and the generator distribution Pg typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds in input space, yielding disjoint or near-disjoint supports. They formalize this and prove that, under mild conditions on the generator network, Pg is supported on a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z, hence measure zero in X when dim Z < dim X.
- The “perfect discrimination” theorems (for disjoint supports and for non-perfectly aligned manifolds) show that an optimal discriminator can achieve accuracy 1 and be locally constant (zero gradient) almost everywhere on the supports. This yields a clean explanation for vanishing generator gradients under the original minimax objective; the authors quantify this via Theorem 2.4, showing generator gradient norms go to zero as D→D*.
- For the non-saturating loss (−log D), they derive that, at a fixed θ0 with optimal D*, the generator gradient equals ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)] evaluated at θ0. This helps explain realistic samples with mode dropping (emphasis on KL(Pg||Pr)) and the counter-productive JSD term sign. They also analyze noisy discriminator approximations (Theorem 2.6), arguing that under white-noise approximation errors, per-coordinate updates are Cauchy distributed (infinite variance), rationalizing empirically observed instability.
- To address these issues, they propose adding continuous noise (instance noise) to both discriminator inputs and the generator outputs used for backprop, making Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous and enabling stable, informative gradients. They derive the smoothed gradient form, showing it pulls samples towards the data manifold while repelling from overrepresented model regions, and that with noise on both sides it is proportional to ∇θ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+), enabling training D to (near-)optimality without saturation or instability.
- Finally, they motivate Wasserstein distance as a more intrinsic measure of distribution mismatch when supports are disjoint, proving a bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) (V is noise variance, C is support diameter), justifying annealing the noise while minimizing the JSD of smoothed distributions.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) Theoretical clarity: the paper offers a compelling, principled analysis of GAN pathologies tied to support geometry, with proofs that explain both vanishing and unstable gradients. (2) Actionable guidance: instance noise on both D and generator, plus annealing, is well-motivated and accompanied by derivations and a Wasserstein-based bound that connects the proposed training objective to the true goal.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper addresses a widely recognized instability of GANs and situates its approach with respect to KL/JSD behavior (Theis et al.; Huszár) and known heuristics for stabilizing training. The manifold-support view is well aligned with the manifold hypothesis and the generator architecture.
- Rigor: The manifold arguments (Lemma 1; Theorems 2.1/2.2) are careful and use standard tools (Urysohn’s lemma, transversality, Sard’s lemma). The vanishing gradient bound (Theorem 2.4) is clear and relies on a Sobolev-type approximation norm and bounded generator Jacobian. The gradient identity for the non-saturating loss (Theorem 2.5) clarifies what is being optimized at θ0. The instance-noise results (Theorem 3.1 and Corollary 3.2) correctly yield absolutely continuous smoothed distributions and a gradient proportional to JSD(Pr+ || Pg+). The Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) connects annealed instance-noise training to a metric meaningful even under disjoint supports.
- Caveats: The instability result (Theorem 2.6) assumes independent white-noise errors in D and its gradient across x; this is a strong and unrealistic assumption, and the authors note measurability issues. Thus, it is more illustrative than conclusive. The manifold-alignment assumptions and compactness may not hold exactly in finite-resolution image spaces or with sensor noise; still, the qualitative conclusions likely persist. The gradient identity in Theorem 2.5 is local (at θ0) and should be presented with that nuance. The reliance on integrability and interchange of limits in Section 3 could use additional conditions stated explicitly.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Empirical validation: The theory strongly motivates “instance noise + annealing + training D to optimality.” Please include systematic experiments across datasets and architectures, with:
  - Baselines: vanilla minimax and non-saturating GAN without noise; GAN with noise only in D; noise in both D and generator as proposed.
  - Metrics over training: gradient norms/variance, discriminator loss, sample quality (e.g., FID/IS or nearest-neighbor analyses), mode coverage metrics.
  - Ablations: noise distribution (Gaussian vs heavy-tailed), covariance structure, and annealing schedules; sensitivity analyses.
  - Evidence that JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) decreases and correlates with sample quality; monitor the Wasserstein bound terms if possible.
- Clarify the Wasserstein definition and constants. The notation ‖·‖2 appears to denote the L2 norm; ensure consistency with duality-based bounds to total variation, which are standard for the 1-Wasserstein metric. If you intend the L2 norm (not squared), the inequalities in Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 are more consistent; otherwise adjust constants and cite the precise inequality used.
- Theorem 2.6: Consider relaxing the independence assumptions. Can you prove heavy-tailed or high-variance updates under broader, more realistic error models (e.g., correlated Gaussian processes, small but biased errors)?
- Broaden applicability: Discuss conditional GANs and discrete data, and whether the same smoothing arguments apply. Comment on interaction with other stabilization methods (gradient penalties, Lipschitz constraints).
- Notation and presentation: Some symbols collide (‖·‖2 as norm vs squared norm); clarify. Add missing figures referenced in text and clean up references.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the choice of noise distribution and the annealing schedule? Do you observe a trade-off between bias (from smoothing) and variance (from instability) empirically?
2) In practice, do you backprop through generator noise as in Corollary 3.2? How does it compare to adding noise only at D’s input?
3) Can you provide quantitative evidence that reducing JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) during training correlates with improved sample quality and mode coverage?
4) Can Theorem 2.6 be extended to more realistic discriminator error models (e.g., small correlated Gaussian perturbations, bounded adversarial errors)?
5) In Theorem 3.2/Corollary 3.2, what regularity assumptions ensure interchanging gradient and integrals is valid? Please state them explicitly.
6) For Theorem 3.3, which Wasserstein variant is used (W1 vs W2), and which duality/inequality justifies the step W ≤ C·TV? Please align notation and constants.
7) Have you compared the proposed instance-noise approach to other Lipschitz-enforcing or smoothing techniques? How do the gradients and stability differ in practice?

Overall, this paper offers a clear theoretical lens on GAN pathologies and a principled, practical remedy via smoothing and annealing, with a useful connection to Wasserstein distance. Strengthening empirical validation and tightening a few assumptions would make it a strong contribution.
