Summary and contributions
The paper analyzes why GAN training is unstable and how discriminator optimality often harms generator learning. The authors formalize the “manifold support” view: if real data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional, (almost surely) non-aligned manifolds in high-dimensional space, then an optimal discriminator achieves perfect separation with zero gradient almost everywhere on the supports. They prove:
- Perfect discrimination theorems under disjoint or non-transversally intersecting supports, implying JSD=log 2 and KL divergences are infinite even when manifolds are close.
- Generator gradient vanishing for the original log(1−D) objective as D→D*, with a quantitative bound (Theorem 2.4).
- The “non-saturating” −log D update, when evaluated with the optimal D*, corresponds to the gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr) at θ0 (Theorem 2.5), rationalizing mode dropping and realism but not coverage.
- Under a noisy approximation of the optimal discriminator with independent white noise in D and its gradient, the generator update distribution becomes Cauchy with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6), explaining instability.
To address these issues, the paper proposes adding input noise (“instance noise”) to both real and generated samples and, crucially, to the generator outputs during backprop. This makes Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous, yielding smooth non-vanishing gradients and allowing training D to (near) optimality. They derive the gradient form under Gaussian noise (Theorem 3.2), showing attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from model mass. Finally, they connect annealed instance noise to the Wasserstein distance: W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C sqrt(JSD(Pr+‖Pg+)), where V is noise variance and C a diameter bound (Theorem 3.3), suggesting a principled training scheme: minimize JSD between smoothed distributions while annealing noise.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Strong and insightful theory, but several core assumptions are unrealistic or unsupported empirically, and the proposed remedy (instance noise/annealing) is not adequately validated or positioned relative to existing literature.
- Some theoretical claims rely on idealized or brittle conditions (e.g., white-noise, independence, optimal D, compact supports) and the paper lacks robustness analyses or experiments demonstrating practical impact.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper tackles a central and relevant question: why GANs are unstable and how the discriminator’s optimality can harm training. The manifold-support argument and saturation of f-divergences when supports are disjoint are known intuitions that the paper formalizes rigorously using transversality and Sard’s lemma; this is a strength. However, the related work is incomplete: missing are f-GAN (Nowozin et al.), MMD/IPM-based GANs, LSGAN, EBGAN, and prior discussions of instance noise/regularization of D. This omission weakens the placement and novelty of the proposed remedy.
- Correctness and rigor: Many proofs are careful and technically sound (e.g., Lemma 1 under common nonlinearities, transversality arguments, absolute continuity under noise). However:
  - Theorem 2.4 assumes approximation in a Sobolev-like norm and uniform control of the generator Jacobian. While reasonable, the practical link between finite-capacity discriminators and this norm control is not demonstrated empirically.
  - Theorem 2.5 gives an identity at θ0 using D*(θ0). This clarifies what the non-saturating update targets locally, but because D is always changing with θ, the practical relevance is limited; this should be discussed more explicitly.
  - Theorem 2.6 assumes D*−D and ∇xD*−∇xD are independent white Gaussian processes, independent across x and across numerator/denominator. This is a very strong and likely false assumption for neural networks trained on finite data; the qualitative conclusion (heavy-tailed, high-variance updates) is plausible, but the result as stated is fragile. A weaker assumption (e.g., correlated noise or finite bandwidth) would be more convincing.
  - Theorem 3.3 uses a chain of inequalities (W ≤ C·TV, Pinsker to relate TV to KL, then JSD) that lead to crude constants and diameter dependence C. Although qualitatively reasonable, the bound is loose; more discussion of tightness and regimes where it is informative is needed.
- Empirical support: The paper references figures indicating vanishing gradients and increasing variance, but provides no concrete experimental section, datasets, or quantitative metrics. There is no systematic evaluation of the instance-noise proposal (choice of σ, annealing schedule, robustness across architectures/datasets, comparison to other stabilization methods). Claims like “we can train the discriminator till optimality” are not backed by experiments, and the risk of biasing the learned distribution with noise is only qualitatively discussed.
- Significance: The conceptual message—that f-divergences can saturate with non-overlapping supports and that smoothing resolves this while connecting to Wasserstein distance—is important. However, in the absence of robust empirical evidence and with incomplete positioning relative to contemporary alternatives (IPMs, gradient penalties, spectral normalization), it is hard to assess practical impact.

Feedback to improve the paper (not all affecting the decision)
- Strengthen related work: discuss f-GAN (f-divergence family and support mismatch), MMD/Kernel GANs, IPM-based GANs (including Wasserstein/Kantorovich–Rubinstein dual), LSGAN, EBGAN/BEGAN, unrolled GANs, and discriminator regularization techniques (gradient penalty, spectral normalization). Clarify how instance noise compares theoretically and empirically.
- Empirics: provide a rigorous experimental section:
  - Datasets: CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN, and a synthetic multimodal distribution for mode coverage analysis.
  - Baselines: DCGAN, feature matching, gradient penalty (if allowed), spectral norm (if allowed), and −log D with/without instance noise.
  - Metrics: FID, Inception score, Precision/Recall for generative models or improved coverage measures; ablations for σ and annealing schedules; show discriminator training to near-optimality does not harm generator progress under noise.
  - Verify Theorem 2.4 (vanishing gradients as D improves) and the stabilization from noise with quantitative plots and controlled studies.
- Weaken assumptions in Theorem 2.6: replace white noise and independence with more realistic models (e.g., mean-square bounded, correlated errors) and derive heavy-tail or variance inflation conclusions under these assumptions.
- Clarify the role of adding noise to generator outputs during backprop (Section 3): formalize the statement that this makes the generator optimize JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) and avoids measure-zero adversarial directions; provide empirical evidence that it prevents adversarial exploitation of D.
- Discuss the regime where the manifold assumption may fail (e.g., dequantized continuous data, models with explicit observation noise) and how the results adapt.
- Tighten presentation: many typos and formatting issues; some claims labeled “trivial” should be fully justified; ensure all figures and appendices are included and legible.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of noise distribution and the annealing schedule? Can you provide guidelines or an adaptive procedure?
- Do you observe that training D “to optimality” under instance noise genuinely improves generator convergence in practice? Please quantify versus early-stopped D.
- How does the proposed method compare to IPM/Wasserstein-based objectives and to discriminator regularization (e.g., gradient penalty) in terms of stability, sample quality, and mode coverage?
- Can you relax the independence/white-noise assumptions in Theorem 2.6 and still obtain instability or heavy-tailed updates?
- Does adding noise to the generator path during backprop degrade final sample sharpness? How do you ensure that annealing recovers clean samples without residual blurring?
- Can you empirically validate Theorem 3.3 by tracking JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) and estimating the induced Wasserstein distance during training?

Overall, the paper offers valuable theoretical insights, but needs stronger empirical validation, broader positioning, and more robust assumptions to meet the bar for acceptance.
