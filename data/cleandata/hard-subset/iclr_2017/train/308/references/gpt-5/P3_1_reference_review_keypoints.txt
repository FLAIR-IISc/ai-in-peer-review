- Paper formalizes the “manifold support” view: real and generated data lie on low-dimensional, non-aligned manifolds, causing optimal discriminators to perfectly separate with zero gradients on supports.
- Proves perfect-discrimination theorems for disjoint or non-transversal supports, implying JSD=log 2 and infinite KL divergences even when manifolds are close.
- Shows generator gradient vanishing for the original log(1−D) objective as D→D*, with a quantitative bound (Theorem 2.4).
- Interprets the non-saturating −log D update at D*(θ0) as gradient of KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2·JSD(Pg‖Pr), explaining mode dropping and realism but not coverage (Theorem 2.5).
- Demonstrates that under a white-noise approximation of D and ∇D errors, generator updates become Cauchy with infinite variance, offering an instability explanation (Theorem 2.6).
- Proposes instance noise (adding input noise to real and generated samples and during generator backprop) to make distributions absolutely continuous and restore smooth, non-vanishing gradients.
- Derives gradient form under Gaussian noise showing attraction to data manifold and repulsion from model mass (Theorem 3.2).
- Connects annealed instance noise to Wasserstein distance via W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+‖Pg+), motivating minimizing JSD between smoothed distributions (Theorem 3.3).
- Decision: Reject.
- Main reason for rejection: strong theory but unrealistic/core brittle assumptions and inadequate empirical validation of instance-noise remedy.
- Related work is incomplete—missing f-GAN, MMD/IPM GANs, LSGAN, EBGAN, and prior instance-noise/regularization literature—weakening novelty claims.
- Some theoretical assumptions are unrealistic or unsupported: Sobolev-like norm control and uniform generator Jacobian (Theorem 2.4) lack empirical linkage.
- Theorem 2.5’s local identity at θ0 is limited in practice because D changes with θ; practical relevance is not fully discussed.
- Theorem 2.6’s white Gaussian, independent-noise assumptions across x and between numerator/denominator are very strong and likely false for neural nets; result is fragile.
- Theorem 3.3 relies on loose inequalities (W≤C·TV, Pinsker, JSD) producing crude constants and diameter dependence C; tightness and informative regimes are unclear.
- Empirical support is lacking: no experiments, datasets, quantitative metrics, or systematic evaluation of instance noise (σ selection, annealing schedules, robustness, baselines).
- Claims like “train D to optimality” under noise are unsubstantiated experimentally, and risk of biasing learned distribution with noise is not quantified.
- Suggested improvements: strengthen related work coverage; add rigorous empirical section with CIFAR-10/CelebA/LSUN and synthetic multimodal tests; compare to DCGAN, feature matching, gradient penalty, spectral norm; report FID/Inception/Precision-Recall and ablations on σ and schedules.
- Suggested theoretical fixes: relax white-noise independence in Theorem 2.6 to more realistic correlated/finite-bandwidth noise and derive heavy-tail/variance inflation under weaker assumptions.
- Clarify and formalize the role of adding noise during generator backprop and provide empirical evidence it prevents adversarial exploitation and optimizes JSD(Pr+‖Pg+).
- Discuss regimes where manifold assumptions fail (e.g., dequantized data, explicit observation noise) and how results adapt.
- Presentation fixes: fix typos, include all figures/appendices, avoid labeling nontrivial claims as “trivial.”
- Questions for authors to address: sensitivity to noise distribution and annealing schedule; empirical benefit of training D to near-optimality under instance noise; comparisons to IPM/Wasserstein and discriminator regularization; relaxing independence in Theorem 2.6; impact of noise on final sample sharpness and recovery via annealing; empirical validation of Theorem 3.3.
