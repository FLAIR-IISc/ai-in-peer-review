- Paper addresses GAN instability and vanishing/exploding generator gradients, attributing them to mismatched, low-dimensional supports of Pr and Pg.
- Formal result: under mild generator assumptions Pg lies on a countable union of manifolds of dim ≤ dim Z, hence measure zero in X when dim Z < dim X.
- “Perfect discrimination” theorems show optimal discriminator can be locally constant on supports, explaining vanishing generator gradients.
- Theorem 2.4 quantifies vanishing generator gradient norms as D → D* under the original minimax objective.
- For the non-saturating loss, Theorem 2.5 gives generator gradient = ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)] at θ0, explaining mode dropping and a counterproductive JSD term.
- Theorem 2.6 argues that white-noise discriminator approximation errors yield Cauchy (infinite-variance) per-coordinate updates, rationalizing instability.
- Proposal: add continuous instance noise to both discriminator inputs and generator outputs used for backprop to make Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous.
- Smoothed-gradient analysis shows noise pulls samples toward the data manifold and repels overrepresented model regions; with noise on both sides it is proportional to ∇θ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+).
- They motivate annealing the noise and prove a Wasserstein bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+ || Pg+), connecting smoothed JSD minimization to true distributional distance.
- Decision: Weak accept.
- Key strengths: theoretical clarity linking support geometry to GAN pathologies and actionable guidance (instance noise + annealing + training D).
- Supporting rigor: manifold arguments use standard topology tools; vanishing-gradient and smoothing theorems are carefully derived.
- Caveat: Theorem 2.6’s independent white-noise error assumption is strong and unrealistic; result is illustrative rather than conclusive.
- Caveat: manifold-alignment, compactness, and some integrability/interchange assumptions may not hold exactly in practical image spaces.
- Caveat: Theorem 2.5 is a local identity at θ0 and should be presented with that nuance.
- Suggestion: add systematic empirical validation comparing baselines, variants (noise only in D vs both), metrics (gradient norms, FID/IS, mode coverage), and ablations (noise type, covariance, annealing).
- Suggestion: clarify Wasserstein definition, norms, constants, and the inequality W ≤ C·TV or the exact inequality used.
- Suggestion: try to relax independence assumptions in Theorem 2.6 to more realistic error models (correlated Gaussian, bounded errors).
- Suggestion: discuss applicability to conditional GANs, discrete data, and interaction with other stabilization methods (gradient penalties, Lipschitz constraints).
- Presentation fixes: resolve notation collisions (‖·‖2), state regularity conditions for interchange of gradient/integral, and add missing figures/references.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to noise distribution and annealing schedule and bias–variance trade-offs?
- Question: do you backprop through generator noise in practice, and how does that compare to adding noise only to D’s input?
- Question: provide quantitative evidence that decreasing JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) correlates with improved sample quality and mode coverage?
- Question: can Theorem 2.6 be extended to more realistic discriminator error models (correlated Gaussian, bounded adversarial errors)?
- Question: what regularity assumptions justify interchanging gradient and integrals in Theorem/Corollary 3.2?
- Question: which Wasserstein variant (W1 vs W2) is used in Theorem 3.3, and which duality/inequality justifies the W ≤ C·TV step?
- Question: have you empirically compared instance-noise to other Lipschitz-enforcing or smoothing techniques, and how do gradients/stability differ?
