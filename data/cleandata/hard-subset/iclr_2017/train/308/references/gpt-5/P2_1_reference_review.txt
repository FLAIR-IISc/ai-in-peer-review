Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles a central open question in GANs: why training is unstable and why generator gradients deteriorate as the discriminator improves. It provides a principled theoretical account rooted in the manifold structure of data and generator distributions.
- Main theoretical results:
  - If Pr and Pg lie on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds that do not “perfectly align,” there exist smooth, perfect discriminators that are locally constant on the supports (Theorems 2.1–2.2). Consequently, JSD is saturated and KL divergences are infinite (Theorem 2.3).
  - For the original generator objective, as the discriminator approaches optimality, generator gradients vanish (Theorem 2.4).
  - For the commonly used −log D update, the paper links the update at θ0 to ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD], explaining the tendency for mode dropping and pushing away from data (Theorem 2.5).
  - With a noisy approximation to the optimal discriminator, the −log D update becomes a heavy-tailed (Cauchy) random variable under strong independence assumptions, yielding extreme instability (Theorem 2.6).
  - Adding input noise (“instance noise”) makes the distributions absolutely continuous; the paper derives the resulting generator gradient, which moves samples toward the data manifold while repelling overly likely model regions (Theorem 3.2; Corollary 3.2).
  - It relates the Wasserstein distance between noiseless Pr and Pg to JSD between their noisy versions plus a term depending on the noise variance (Theorem 3.3), motivating training with noise and annealing to zero.
- The work thus offers a cohesive theoretical explanation of practical GAN pathologies and proposes a principled direction: train on noisy inputs (possibly both G and D), anneal the noise, and interpret progress through distances that behave well under manifold mismatch (e.g., Wasserstein).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Insufficient empirical validation for the proposed remedies and for the strength of the claimed phenomena; several figures are referenced but not provided, and no quantitative comparisons are shown.
2) Some central results rely on strong, arguably unrealistic assumptions (e.g., white, independent noise for D and ∇D; idealized manifold settings; measurability caveats acknowledged by the authors), and the paper does not sufficiently stress their practical implications or limitations.

Supporting arguments
- Significance and clarity: The theoretical narrative is compelling and addresses a highly relevant problem. The use of geometric/topological tools to formalize “manifolds with disjoint supports” and the consequences for JSD and KL is insightful. The link between −log D training and KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD helps explain observed mode dropping behavior.
- Novelty and relation to literature: The manifold/JSD critique is closely aligned with arguments that led to Wasserstein GANs; the instance noise idea has also appeared contemporaneously (or earlier) in practice. The paper cites Huszár and Theis appropriately, but does not sufficiently position itself relative to prior/parallel work on instance noise and Wasserstein-based training. The JSD-noisy-to-Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) is a useful bridge but seems loose and its practical import is not empirically substantiated.
- Rigor vs. realism: Several proofs invoke strong hypotheses (compact disjoint supports; transversality; white, independent Gaussian noise for the discriminator and its gradient; a “non-measurability” caveat in Theorem 2.6). While acceptable in a theory paper, the link to practice is weakened without experiments that test sensitivity to these assumptions.
- Empirical support: The text references Figures 1–3 and claims qualitatively consistent behavior (vanishing gradients, gradient explosion/variance with −log D), but these are not included, and there are no controlled experiments comparing: vanilla GAN, −log D, instance noise with annealing, and alternative objectives (e.g., gradient penalties or Wasserstein). No quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, IS, precision/recall, coverage) are reported.

Suggestions to improve (constructive, not all decision-critical)
- Provide thorough empirical validation:
  - Demonstrate that adding noise to both G and D with an annealing schedule yields more stable training and better coverage (e.g., improved recall with controlled mode-dropping benchmarks like Stacked MNIST, mixtures of Gaussians) and better image quality (FID/precision).
  - Compare against state-of-the-art stabilization techniques (WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, gradient penalties, DRAGAN, DiffAugment) to position the method.
  - Show ablations on noise distribution, variance, and annealing schedules; analyze sensitivity and failure modes.
- Clarify and relax assumptions where possible:
  - Replace the white/independent noise assumption in Theorem 2.6 with a more realistic model (e.g., bounded approximation error with correlation), or provide empirical evidence that gradient noise is heavy-tailed in practice.
  - Improve the discussion of the “smooth Urysohn lemma” and transversality assumptions, perhaps relegating topology-heavy parts to an appendix with more intuition in the main text.
- Strengthen positioning:
  - Discuss relations and differences to WGAN and f-GANs. Explain when optimizing JSD on noisy distributions is preferable to directly optimizing Wasserstein with Lipschitz constraints.
  - Cite and compare to prior “instance noise” work if applicable, clarifying your theoretical contribution beyond existing heuristics.
- Presentation and correctness:
  - Fix typos, notation inconsistencies, and incomplete references. Include the missing figures and ensure they support the claims.
  - In Theorem 2.5, discuss practical implications more carefully: the sign and relative weight of the KL and JSD terms, and conditions under which the mode-dropping bias manifests.
  - Comment on computational aspects of the gradient formulas in Theorem 3.2/Corollary 3.2 (approximating integrals, sample complexity).

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide the missing figures and a quantitative experimental section demonstrating that annealed instance noise improves stability and coverage across diverse datasets relative to standard baselines (including WGAN-GP)?
- How sensitive are the results to the noise distribution and the annealing schedule? Is there a principled way to choose σ(t)?
- Can Theorem 2.6 be extended to weaker, more realistic approximation error models for D (correlated noise, bounded bias), or can you empirically demonstrate heavy-tailed gradient statistics during −log D training?
- How does your proposal relate to and perform against methods that enforce Lipschitz constraints (e.g., spectral norm, gradient penalty), which also aim to avoid vanishing/exploding gradients?
- In Theorem 3.3, how tight is the bound in practice? Can you empirically validate that minimizing JSD(Pr+||Pg+) while annealing noise reduces W(Pr,Pg)?
- Do you foresee failure cases where adding noise harms learning (e.g., limited model capacity, high-dimensional settings), and how would you detect/adapt?

Overall, the paper provides valuable theoretical insights but needs solid empirical substantiation and clearer positioning relative to concurrent advances to merit acceptance.
