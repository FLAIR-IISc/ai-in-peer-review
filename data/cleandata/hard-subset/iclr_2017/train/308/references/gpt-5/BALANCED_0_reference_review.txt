Summary and contributions
The paper tackles a central question in GANs: why training is unstable and why generator gradients vanish or explode as the discriminator improves. The authors provide a clean theoretical account grounded in the geometry of probability measures on low-dimensional manifolds. The main contributions are:
- Formal “perfect discrimination” theorems showing that when Pr and Pg live on disjoint or non-transversally intersecting low-dimensional manifolds (the typical case in GANs with dim(Z) < dim(X)), the optimal discriminator is smooth, achieves accuracy 1, and has zero gradient almost everywhere on the supports. This directly explains vanishing generator gradients under the original GAN objective.
- A proof that the generator’s “non-saturating” gradient corresponds (locally) to the gradient of KL(Pg||Pr) − 2·JSD(Pg||Pr), clarifying its bias toward plausible-looking samples and mode-dropping tendencies, and a theorem suggesting its instability under noisy discriminator approximations (gradient coordinates becoming Cauchy distributed).
- A constructive remedy: inject continuous noise to both data and generated samples so the discriminator trains on smoothed distributions Pr+ and Pg+. The authors derive the resulting generator gradients, show they act like distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold counterbalanced by repulsion from model modes, and argue that this stabilizes training.
- A link to Wasserstein distance: they bound W(Pr,Pg) by the variance of the injected noise and JSD(Pr+||Pg+), suggesting that minimizing JSD between smoothed distributions while annealing noise decreases W(Pr,Pg) of the original distributions, with practical implications for stable training.

Decision: Reject (promising theory, insufficient empirical validation and some strong assumptions)
Key reasons:
1) The theoretical analysis is insightful, but several results rely on strong or unrealistic assumptions (e.g., white-noise discriminator errors independent across x) and omit practical constraints (parametric discriminators not at optimum, optimization dynamics), and
2) There is no substantial empirical evaluation of the proposed noise-annealed training to demonstrate robustness, sample quality, mode coverage, or practical guidance on noise scheduling.

Supporting arguments
- Problem relevance and motivation: The paper addresses widely observed GAN pathologies (vanishing gradients, instability, mode collapse) and anchors them in the manifold hypothesis and divergence geometry. This is well motivated and fits the literature.
- Correctness and rigor: The “perfect discrimination” results and the vanishing gradient theorem are plausible and well argued, with suitable measure-theoretic care (manifolds, transversality). The identification of the non-saturating gradient with a KL–JSD combination clarifies a long-standing heuristic. However:
  - The instability result (Cauchy-distributed updates) assumes independent white noise errors for D and ∇D across x, which is not realistic for neural discriminators trained on finite data. As the authors note, measurability and independence issues complicate the argument. This limits the external validity of the instability conclusion.
  - The Wasserstein bound (W ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+||Pg+)) is insightful but loose (uses total variation and Pinsker) and depends on bounding the diameter C of supports; it does not yield algorithmic guidance beyond “reduce JSD for smoothed distributions and anneal noise.”
  - The lemma on generator images being a countable union of ≤dim(Z) manifolds under ReLU/monotone nonlinearities is reasonable, but details for manifolds-with-boundary and global behavior in deep compositions deserve more careful treatment (some deferred to the appendix).
- Placement in literature: The paper mentions JSD/KL differences and Wasserstein distance but does not situate its approach relative to contemporaneous alternatives that also seek stability (e.g., Wasserstein GAN with Lipschitz constraints/gradient penalty, f-GANs, regularization and spectral normalization). Explicit comparisons would strengthen significance.
- Empirical support: The text references figures demonstrating vanishing gradients and gradient variance growth, but no experimental section is provided here. Crucially, the proposed remedy—training on noisy inputs with annealing—needs empirical validation across datasets/architectures, with ablations on noise distributions and schedules, and comparisons to baseline stabilizers.

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide a concrete training algorithm for the proposed approach:
  - Where to inject noise (input space vs. feature space layers), noise distribution choices (Gaussian vs. heavy-tailed), and an annealing schedule with principled criteria.
  - A practical estimator of JSD(Pr+||Pg+) and how you optimize it within the GAN game.
- Empirical evaluation:
  - Demonstrate stability (gradient norms, loss signals), sample quality (FID/KID), and mode coverage (e.g., precision/recall for generative models, CIFAR-10 modes) under different noise schedules and distributions.
  - Compare against (i) standard GAN with tricks, (ii) WGAN/GP, (iii) gradient penalties or spectral norm constraints, (iv) feature-space noise injection.
  - Ablate the effect of adding noise to only the discriminator, only the generator, and both (as your theory suggests both are needed).
- Clarify scope and assumptions:
  - Discuss how far from optimal the discriminator can be while your vanishing gradient and instability arguments still hold approximately.
  - Replace the white-noise assumption with a more realistic error model (e.g., correlated Gaussian process with smooth kernel, or parametric approximation bounds), or at least provide empirical evidence that gradient updates display heavy-tailed behavior as D improves.
- Tighten the Wasserstein connection:
  - Consider using W1 with its KR dual and Lipschitz discriminators to avoid loose TV/Pinsker steps. Discuss how your smoothing approach relates to enforcing Lipschitz continuity (e.g., gradient penalties).
  - Explore whether your method approximates a kernel-smoothed optimal transport (sliced-Wasserstein, entropic OT) and whether that suggests better objectives or regularizers.
- Clarity and writing:
  - Fix typos, notation, and references; define all symbols on first use; move key assumptions upfront (e.g., absolute continuity on manifolds).
  - Strengthen the discussion on “manifold with boundary” cases and how it affects the discriminator’s gradient behavior.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is the proposed method to the choice of noise distribution and variance schedule? Can you provide empirical guidance or adaptive rules for annealing?
2) Have you compared your noise-annealed GAN against WGAN-GP or spectral normalization in terms of stability, mode coverage, and sample quality?
3) Where do you inject noise in practice? Pixel space noise can degrade perceptual quality; would feature-space noise preserve structure while satisfying your theoretical assumptions?
4) Can you relax the white-noise assumption in Theorem 2.6 to a more realistic correlated error model and still obtain heavy-tailed or high-variance updates?
5) Beyond the loose bound in Theorem 3.3, can you show empirically that reducing JSD(Pr+||Pg+) while annealing noise correlates with decreases in W(Pr,Pg) (or improved sample metrics)?
6) How do your results change if dim(Z) is close to dim(X) or if the generator injects noise internally (thus making Pg absolutely continuous)? Does vanishing gradient still dominate?

Overall, the paper offers valuable theoretical insights and a promising direction via smoothing and Wasserstein connections, but it needs stronger empirical validation and clarification of assumptions to meet the bar for ICLR.
