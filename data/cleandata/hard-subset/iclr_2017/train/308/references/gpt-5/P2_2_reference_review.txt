Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles a central question: why GAN training is unstable and why generator gradients degrade as the discriminator improves. It offers a theoretical treatment grounded in manifold geometry and divergence properties.
- Main claims:
  1) If Pr and Pg have disjoint supports or lie on low-dimensional manifolds that do not “perfectly align,” there exist smooth, perfect discriminators whose gradients are zero almost everywhere on the supports. Consequently, standard GAN training faces either vanishing or uninformative gradients as D approaches optimality.
  2) For the original (saturating) generator loss, gradients vanish as D→D*. For the non-saturating loss (−log D), the paper links the generator update at θ0 to the gradient of KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr), arguing this promotes sharp samples and mode dropping; and under a strong “white-noise” approximation error model for D, the updates become Cauchy-distributed with infinite variance (instability).
  3) Adding noise to inputs (and also to generator outputs during backprop) yields smooth densities Pr+ and Pg+, restores meaningful gradients (attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from model mass), and makes the generator gradient proportional to ∇θ JSD(Pr+||Pg+). An annealing scheme is suggested.
  4) The paper motivates Wasserstein distance as a softer metric and proves a bound: W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+||Pg+), relating the true distributional distance to noise variance and the JSD between smoothed distributions.
- This is a thoughtful and ambitious attempt to give principled explanations for GAN instability and to point toward better-conditioned objectives.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient novelty relative to existing/near-concurrent literature and folklore; several core insights and prescriptions are already known.
- Claims about practical stabilization via instance noise/annealing and the usefulness of the proposed bounds are not empirically supported; figures are referenced but absent.
- Some theoretical assumptions are unrealistic and parts of the analysis are hand-wavy or incomplete, weakening the scientific rigor.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and placement:
  - The manifold-support explanation for JSD saturation, perfect discrimination, and vanishing gradients has been articulated in prior/parallel work (e.g., discussions by Huszár, Theis et al., and notably the WGAN line of work), including the recommendation to use Wasserstein distance or add “instance noise” to smooth distributions. The paper cites some of these, but the positioning undersells the overlap and does not clearly delineate what is genuinely new beyond known arguments.
  - The link between the non-saturating loss and a KL-like objective around a fixed D* has been discussed before (Huszár). The paper’s Theorem 2.5 formalizes this, but the contribution seems incremental.
- Correctness and rigor:
  - The perfect-discriminator theorems rely on standard topological/measure arguments; they are plausible, but the practical relevance hinges on how closely finite-capacity discriminators approximate D* and how training dynamics interact, aspects not fully resolved here.
  - The “instability” theorem (Cauchy gradients) assumes D*−D and ∇D*−∇D are independent, spatially white Gaussian processes and even acknowledges non-measurability issues. This model is unrealistic for neural discriminators, making the conclusion more illustrative than rigorous for practice.
  - Several proofs defer key steps or rely on strong assumptions (compact supports, smoothness, boundary conditions); some derivations (e.g., the precise gradient identity for −log D) would benefit from tighter conditions and clearer statements. Norms and constants (e.g., a(z), b(z)) are defined but their magnitudes/conditioning are not quantified.
  - Referenced figures are missing, and some claims are asserted as “left to the reader.” This undermines the evaluation of correctness and scientific rigor.
- Empirical support:
  - The paper hinges on explaining and fixing instability, yet provides no quantitative experiments or ablations demonstrating that training with noise (and annealing) yields improved stability, mode coverage, or final performance across datasets/architectures. Without this, the practical relevance remains speculative.
  - No comparisons to established stabilizers (WGAN/gradient penalty, spectral normalization, regularization, architectural tricks) are presented.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clearly delineate novelty relative to: instance noise, WGAN/Wasserstein bounds, f-GANs and divergences on smoothed distributions, and Huszár’s analyses. If your gradient identities or manifold theorems provide new technical strength, highlight the exact advances and tighten proofs.
- Replace the white-noise discriminator error model with a more realistic approximation error model (e.g., Lipschitz-bounded errors, function class generalization bounds), and derive variance/conditioning results under those assumptions.
- Provide a concrete algorithmic recipe: how to add noise (type, covariance), where to add it (data, generator outputs), and how to anneal it. Validate with experiments demonstrating improved stability and sample quality, as well as reduced mode dropping, against strong baselines.
- Strengthen the Wasserstein–JSD-with-noise connection: can the bound be tightened or made scale-aware? Discuss sensitivity to C (support diameter) and provide empirical diagnostics relating JSD(Pr+,Pg+) to W(Pr,Pg).
- Improve clarity and rigor: include all referenced figures, fix typos/notation, move essential proofs into the main text or provide more detailed sketches, and define all assumptions (e.g., compactness, smoothness, support conditions) upfront.

Questions for the authors
1) What are the precise novelty claims vs. prior work on instance noise and WGAN? Which theorems or derivations here were not previously known?
2) Can you provide empirical evidence that training with input and generator noise, with annealing, improves stability and mode coverage across standard benchmarks, compared to WGAN-GP or spectral normalization?
3) How sensitive are your conclusions to the discriminator approximation error model? Can you derive instability or large-variance updates under milder, realistic assumptions?
4) How should practitioners choose the noise distribution and annealing schedule? Do you have diagnostics to adaptively control noise based on JSD(Pr+,Pg+) or other signals?
5) Your results assume supports on low-dimensional manifolds and various regularity conditions. How do these assumptions interact with typical convolutional nets and data augmentations in practice?
6) Is there a principled way to estimate or bound the constants (a(z), b(z), C) during training to guide hyperparameters or provide convergence guarantees?
