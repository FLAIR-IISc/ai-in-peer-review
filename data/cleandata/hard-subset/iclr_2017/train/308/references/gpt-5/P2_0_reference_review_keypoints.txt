- Main claim: GAN instability arises because Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional, non-overlapping manifolds, so optimal discriminators are nearly constant on supports, causing vanishing or erratic generator gradients.
- Lemma 1: Common neural-network generators map latent Z to a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z, hence measure-zero in X when dim Z < dim X.
- Theorems 2.1–2.3: Under mild non-alignment, near-perfect discriminators exist with zero gradients almost everywhere on supports; JSD saturates and KLs diverge.
- Theorem 2.4: With a near-optimal discriminator, the original GAN generator gradients vanish.
- Theorem 2.5: The −log D generator update locally corresponds to ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD], explaining mode dropping and a repulsive JSD term.
- Theorem 2.6: If discriminator approximation errors are idealized white noise, the −log D update has Cauchy-distributed coordinates (infinite variance), explaining training instability.
- Proposed remedy: Add continuous noise to discriminator inputs (and to generator outputs during backprop) to make Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous and yield smooth, informative gradients.
- Theorems 3.1–3.2: Noised gradients act as distance-weighted attractions to the data manifold with local repulsion where Pg+ dominates.
- Corollary 3.2: Links the noised update to the gradient of JSD(Pr+||Pg+).
- Theorem 3.3: Bounds Wasserstein distance W(Pr,Pg) by noise variance and JSD between noised distributions, motivating annealing noise while minimizing JSD(Pr+||Pg+).
- Decision: Reject (borderline) due to insufficient empirical validation and reliance on strong assumptions.
- Reason 1 for rejection: Central practical claim (stabilization via instance noise and annealing) lacks rigorous empirical validation (missing detailed experiments, metrics, baselines).
- Reason 2 for rejection: Several instability results depend on strong, arguably unrealistic assumptions (e.g., independent white-noise discriminator errors) and omit important edge cases.
- Significance/novelty: Provides a compelling geometric synthesis linking manifold support, discriminator optimality, gradient pathologies, and smoothing via noise; insightful connection to Wasserstein.
- Soundness (positive): Manifold-support arguments and the vanishing-gradient result are plausible; ReLU/leaky-ReLU case well motivated.
- Soundness (caveats): Smooth-activation claims need tighter diffeomorphism assumptions; Theorem 2.6’s whiteness/independence assumptions are strong and limit realism.
- Soundness (smoothing & Wasserstein): Smoothing results are clear and useful; Wasserstein bound is conceptually appealing but relies on loose inequalities and bounded-support assumptions.
- Clarity: Paper is ambitious and mathematically dense, with typos, overloaded terminology (e.g., “continuous” vs “absolutely continuous”), and proofs mainly in the appendix.
- Reproducibility/practical impact: The proposed remedy is easy to implement, but the paper lacks systematic experiments (datasets, architectures, schedules, FID/IS/mode metrics, ablations, baselines).
- Suggested experiments: Compare standard GAN vs discriminator-only noise vs noise on both D and generator backprop across datasets (CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN), report FID/precision/recall/mode coverage, analyze gradient norms and stability.
- Suggested theory improvements: Replace white-noise assumptions with more realistic correlated/bounded noise models, tighten Lemma 1 for smooth activations, and analyze overlapping-support (noisy observation) cases.
- Suggested metric discussion: Compare to other IPMs (Wasserstein, MMD), discuss looseness of constants in Theorem 3.3, and give practical guidance for σ-annealing.
- Presentation fixes: Correct typos, streamline notation, define “accuracy 1” and support assumptions early, avoid overloading “continuous,” and include referenced figures with clear captions.
- Questions for authors: Provide full experimental details and sensitivity to annealing; effects when Pr has observation noise; can Theorem 2.6 be relaxed to realistic discriminator errors; convergence guarantees for annealed JSD minimization; empirical comparisons to other IPM approaches.
