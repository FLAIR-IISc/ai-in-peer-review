Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper tackles why GAN training becomes unstable as the discriminator improves, and why different generator objectives (-log D vs log(1-D)) behave so differently. It develops a geometric view: Pr and Pg typically concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds with (almost surely) measure-zero intersections, which implies the existence of smooth perfect discriminators that are locally constant on each support. From this, it derives:
  - Vanishing gradients for the original generator loss as D→D*.
  - Instability (heavy-tailed, infinite-variance updates) for the -log D generator loss under noisy discriminator approximations.
- It proposes adding continuous noise (“instance noise”) to both real and generated samples, making Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous so that JSD is informative and discriminator gradients are well-behaved; and shows the generator gradient becomes a kernel-weighted attraction to the data manifold with a repulsion from the model manifold.
- It connects the (noisy) JSD to the (noiseless) Wasserstein distance via an upper bound depending on noise variance and JSD(Pr+||Pg+), suggesting an anneal-the-noise training strategy that indirectly minimizes W(Pr,Pg).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) There are serious technical issues and inconsistencies in the Wasserstein section (definitions and inequalities do not line up), and several proofs rely on overly strong or unrealistic assumptions. (2) The empirical support is insufficiently described to substantiate the strong claims about instability and the effectiveness of the proposed remedy.

Supporting arguments
- Correctness and rigor
  - The Wasserstein metric is defined in the paper as W(P,Q) = infγ ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ. Subsequent lemmas and inequalities (e.g., W ≤ V^1/2 and W ≤ C·TV) are standard for the 1-Wasserstein (cost ||x−y||), not for the squared-cost definition. The proof of Lemma 4, as written, uses Jensen in a way that only works if the cost is ||·||, not ||·||^2. This mismatch breaks the chain of inequalities feeding into Theorem 3.3.
  - The bound in Theorem 3.3 also omits constants (e.g., Pinsker’s). The step turning a sum of square roots of KL terms into 2·sqrt(JSD) needs a careful inequality; the constants are off as written.
  - The “white-noise” assumption in Theorem 2.6 (independence across x and between D and ∇D errors) is not only unrealistic for neural discriminators but explicitly acknowledged as non-measurable; the result (Cauchy updates) is then not compelling as a statement about practical training. The conclusion (infinite-variance gradients) critically hinges on those independence assumptions.
  - Several results assume compact supports, boundary-free regular manifolds, and smoothness (e.g., Urysohn’s smooth lemma application). For image domains, compactness is plausible, but the manifold regularity and boundary-free assumptions should be qualified, especially when ReLU networks induce piecewise-linear images with boundaries. Some proofs depend on these assumptions in essential ways.
- Novelty and positioning
  - The geometric argument that empirical data and generator supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds and are mutually singular is insightful and aligns with community intuitions. The vanishing-gradient result for the original generator loss and the -log D bias toward KL(Pg||Pr) are consistent with prior analyses (e.g., Huszár’s note). The paper integrates these ideas cohesively.
  - The proposal to add input noise (instance noise) to ensure overlap and stabilize training is a good, pragmatic idea with prior art in the community. However, the paper does not position itself relative to prior and concurrent work clearly (e.g., instance noise, label smoothing; and later Wasserstein GAN). The Wasserstein discussion is promising but underdeveloped and partially incorrect as noted.
- Empirical support
  - The text refers to figures showing discriminator error going to zero, vanishing gradients, and exploding gradient norms for -log D as D improves. These are important to substantiate the theory, but the manuscript lacks experimental details, baselines, and quantitative evaluations. There is no clear demonstration that the proposed noise-annealing recipe improves sample quality and stability across datasets/architectures, nor guidance for choosing noise schedules.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Fix the optimal transport section rigorously:
  - Decide whether you use W1 or W2 (or W2^2). If using W1 with cost ||·||, the bounds W1(PX,PX+ε) ≤ E||ε|| ≤ sqrt(E||ε||^2) are valid, and W1 ≤ C·TV and the JSD connection can be made precise with correct constants. If using W2, adjust bounds accordingly (they will involve sqrt(V) or V depending on the exact definition).
  - State constants (Pinsker’s inequality) precisely and carry them through Theorem 3.3.
- Clarify assumptions for Theorems 2.1–2.4:
  - Specify domain compactness (e.g., [0,1]^d), manifold regularity, and boundary conditions. For ReLU networks (Lemma 1), provide a precise argument that g(Z) is a finite/countable union of manifolds and how boundaries are handled.
- Reconsider Theorem 2.6 or weaken it:
  - Replace the non-measurable “white-noise over x” assumption with a more realistic local approximation (e.g., smooth errors with bounded variance and correlation length). Show a concentration or variance bound leading to instability without invoking Cauchy tails. Alternatively, move it to an illustrative toy model and emphasize it as intuition.
- Empirics:
  - Provide systematic experiments demonstrating: (a) vanishing vs unstable gradients as discriminator quality improves; (b) instance-noise training with an annealing schedule, compared against standard GAN training, across multiple datasets and architectures; (c) sensitivity to noise scale and schedule; and (d) whether the JSD(Pr+||Pg+) estimated by the discriminator correlates with sample quality and (if feasible) an OT-based metric estimate.
- Notation and exposition:
  - Define the discriminator approximation norm more clearly (Sobolev norm, precise domain). Clean up minor typos and missing constants. Ensure all lemmas cited (e.g., “Urysohn’s smooth lemma”) are standard and correctly named/referenced.

Questions for the authors
- Wasserstein section: Which Wasserstein metric do you intend to use (W1 vs W2)? Can you provide corrected, constant-tight bounds and a consistent definition that makes Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 valid?
- How sensitive are your conclusions to the “perfect alignment” and “manifold” assumptions? Do your theorems extend to piecewise-linear manifolds with boundaries induced by ReLU networks?
- For Theorem 2.6, can you provide a weaker, realistic noise model for D and ∇D and a finite-variance instability result (e.g., variance growing with discriminator sharpness)?
- Instance noise: What concrete annealing schedule do you recommend? How does performance vary with initial variance and decay rate? Do you add noise to both real and fake consistently at all times?
- Empirical validation: Can you include quantitative results demonstrating improved stability and sample quality with instance noise across multiple datasets and compare to other stabilizing techniques (e.g., feature matching, gradient penalties, WGAN variants)?
