1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper seeks to provide a principled theoretical account of why GAN training is unstable and why generator gradients degrade as the discriminator improves. The main contributions are:
- A manifold-support perspective: under standard GAN architectures, both data and generator distributions concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds in high-dimensional ambient spaces, often with disjoint or near-disjoint supports. The paper formalizes this for neural generators (Lemma 1) and shows “perfect” discriminators exist with gradients that vanish almost everywhere on the supports (Theorems 2.1–2.3), implying saturated divergences (JSD at log 2, KLs infinite) despite visually similar samples.
- Analysis of generator gradients: with the original generator objective, gradients vanish as D approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4). For the non-saturating −log D objective, the paper identifies the objective being optimized at a fixed θ and optimal D (Theorem 2.5), arguing it emphasizes KL(Pg‖Pr) and thus encourages mode dropping and realism at the expense of coverage. The paper also provides an instability result for −log D under a noisy discriminator model (Theorem 2.6), claiming heavy-tailed, infinite-variance updates.
- A “smoothing” remedy: adding input noise (instance noise) to both real and generated samples makes the convolved distributions absolutely continuous; gradients through the optimal discriminator then become smooth, distance-weighted averages that point samples toward the data manifold while pushing away over-represented generator modes (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2). 
- Links to Wasserstein distance: the paper proposes estimating and minimizing JSD between the noisy distributions while annealing noise, with an upper bound connecting W(Pr, Pg) to the JSD of the smoothed distributions plus a term controlled by the noise variance (Theorem 3.3).

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (i) Claims are primarily theoretical and hinge on strong, often unrealistic assumptions; the empirical validation is insufficient/nonexistent in the submission (figures are referenced but not presented, no quantitative comparisons to established baselines). (ii) The proposed remedy (instance noise + annealing) overlaps with known ideas and is not positioned or validated convincingly against established methods. The work’s insights are interesting but not, in their current form, sufficiently substantiated for acceptance.

3) Supporting arguments
- Empirical support: While the paper references figures purportedly showing discriminator behavior and gradient norms, the submission contains no actual experimental results, no datasets, no metrics (e.g., FID/Inception score), no ablations, and no comparisons to standard baselines (DCGAN, improved techniques, f-GAN, LSGAN, WGAN/WGAN-GP). Given the practical focus of GAN stability, rigorous empirical validation is essential.
- Strength of assumptions:
  - Perfect or near-perfect discriminators and disjoint/near-disjoint supports on manifolds are used repeatedly. Realistic settings involve finite data, limited model capacity, regularization, and stochastic optimization; the behavior of the alternating updates in those regimes is not analyzed.
  - Theorem 2.6 assumes discriminator error is independent white noise in both function value and gradient across x, and independent from each other—highly unrealistic. The heavy-tail conclusion appears driven by division by near-zero D(x) more than by a faithful model of discriminator approximation noise.
  - The “train D to optimality when noise is added” claim is asserted but not demonstrated; in practice, capacity/regularization matter and annealing schedules are critical.
- Novelty/positioning: Adding instance noise to stabilize GANs has been suggested before, and the general benefits of smoothing or blurring distributions are known. The Wasserstein distance discussion is relevant but the paper does not engage with the extensive literature on Wasserstein GANs and Lipschitz-constrained discriminators. The lack of systematic positioning versus prior art (e.g., f-divergence GANs, least-squares GANs, gradient penalty methods, spectral normalization) weakens the motivation and originality claims.
- Theoretical rigor and practical implications:
  - Several results are stated under idealized conditions (optimal D, equal supports perturbed, smooth manifolds) and then extrapolated to practice. The leap from these settings to operational training guidance is not convincingly bridged.
  - Theorem 3.3’s bound relies on a bounded support assumption (Pr+ and Pg+ in a ball of diameter C) and uses loose inequalities (Pinsker, total variation bound for W). As a result, the constants may be too pessimistic to be informative.
  - Some proofs/claims are left “as an exercise” or rely on advanced topology with minimal intuition for ML readers. There are also many typos and reference formatting issues that impede clarity.

4) Suggestions to improve the paper (constructive and not necessarily part of the decision)
- Empirics:
  - Provide comprehensive experiments on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN) comparing: baseline DCGAN, DCGAN + instance noise (with several schedules), and state-of-the-art stabilizations (feature matching, gradient penalty, spectral normalization, WGAN/WGAN-GP). Report FID, Inception Score, precision/recall for generative models, and mode coverage metrics.
  - Ablation: impact of noise variance, annealing rate, adding noise to data only vs. generator only vs. both, training D to near-optimality vs. standard alternating updates.
  - Empirically test the gradient behavior claims: track gradient norms and distributions as D improves; show whether heavy tails and variance growth occur in practice.
- Theory and positioning:
  - Clarify the relationship to existing “instance noise” and to Wasserstein GANs. What is strictly new here? How do your results complement or improve on prior theory?
  - Relax unrealistic assumptions in Theorem 2.6 or provide a more faithful model of discriminator approximation error. At minimum, bound the gradient in terms of D’s Lipschitz constant and a lower bound on D to avoid division by vanishing probabilities.
  - Provide convergence statements for the annealing scheme: under what conditions does minimizing JSD of noisy distributions while reducing noise recover Pr = Pg?
  - Discuss capacity-limited discriminators and the effect of regularization (e.g., gradient penalties) on your analysis.
- Exposition:
  - Fix typos and reference formatting; clean up notation; avoid leaving key claims as “exercise to the reader.”
  - Ensure the construction of smooth discriminators (Urysohn-type arguments) and their implications for “gradient zero almost everywhere” are stated rigorously and consistently.
  - Offer algorithmic pseudocode for the proposed training procedure with noise and annealing.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide quantitative experiments demonstrating that your noise-annealed JSD training stabilizes GANs and improves mode coverage relative to strong baselines (e.g., WGAN-GP, spectral normalization), across multiple datasets?
- How sensitive is performance to the noise schedule? Do you have principled guidance for setting/annealing variance?
- Can you relax the white-noise and independence assumptions in Theorem 2.6 and still obtain meaningful instability results?
- Does your analysis extend to Lipschitz-constrained discriminators (as in WGAN) or to f-divergence GANs with alternative generator objectives?
- Can you formalize convergence guarantees when annealing noise, perhaps in the sense of Γ-convergence or via bounds that tighten with variance → 0?
