Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the well-known instability of GAN training by providing a theoretical explanation for why the discriminator tends to become perfect (and thus uninformative for the generator) when the data and model distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint or nearly disjoint support. The main claims are:
- A formalization of “perfect discriminator” results when supports are disjoint or lie on low-dimensional manifolds that do not perfectly align; this yields zero gradients almost everywhere for the generator (vanishing gradients).
- An analysis of the generator’s alternative loss −log D, showing at the point where D is optimal that its gradient corresponds to ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)], helping to explain mode dropping tendencies.
- A result that with noisy approximations of D*, the −log D update can be heavy-tailed (Cauchy), suggesting instability.
- A proposal to add continuous noise to inputs of both discriminator and generator (“instance noise”), making the smoothed distributions absolutely continuous, restoring informative gradients; derivation of the resulting gradient as a distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from the model manifold.
- A bound relating the Wasserstein distance between the original (unsmoothed) distributions to the variance of the injected noise and the JSD between the smoothed distributions, suggesting a principled annealing scheme.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to substantiate its core practical claims and proposed remedy. (2) Several theoretical results rely on strong or unrealistic assumptions (e.g., white-noise discriminator errors, independence, compact supports) and some proofs/claims are incomplete or left as exercises, which undermines confidence in the rigor and practical relevance.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The instability of GANs is an important problem. The central observation that generator and data distributions often concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds is reasonable and consistent with prior discussions. Placing the discussion in terms of divergences that saturate (JSD, KL) is well motivated and relates to known issues.
- Placement in literature: The manuscript cites foundational GAN work and Theis et al. (2016), Huszár’s analysis, and OT references. However, it omits or underplays relevant stabilizing techniques and analyses (e.g., feature matching, gradient penalties, spectral normalization, Lipschitz constraints) and prior uses of instance noise. The proposal to inject noise and anneal is known in practice; the paper’s novelty would need to be established empirically and comparatively.
- Theoretical correctness and rigor:
  - The “perfect discrimination” theorems assume disjoint/low-dimensional manifolds, compactness, and smoothness; the use of Urysohn’s smooth lemma constructs a function with zero gradients in neighborhoods. While mathematically plausible, the link to realizable discriminators within standard neural network classes and finite-sample training is not addressed.
  - The vanishing gradient bound (Theorem 2.4) assumes closeness to D* in a Sobolev-like norm. The bound as written has typographical issues and is hard to parse. More importantly, it is unclear how this assumption can be verified or enforced during training.
  - The characterization of the −log D update (Theorem 2.5) at a fixed θ0 with optimal D* is in the spirit of prior analyses, but its practical relevance (D rarely optimal; coupling between D and G) is limited. The implication about mode dropping is plausible but not new.
  - The Cauchy instability result (Theorem 2.6) relies on modeling D*−D and ∇D*−∇D as independent white Gaussian processes and effectively treating D ≈ ε near 0, making the update proportional to r/ε. This is a very strong and arguably unrealistic assumption: D is bounded in [0,1], error fields are not white nor independent across x, and finite-precision and parameter sharing violate independence. While the qualitative point (heavy-tailed/unstable updates due to 1/D factors) is reasonable, the theorem as stated overreaches.
  - The smoothing/instance noise analysis (Theorem 3.1, 3.2; Cor. 3.2) is a useful formalization, showing that with Gaussian noise one obtains a distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold and a repulsion from the model manifold. This gives intuition for why instance noise can stabilize training.
  - The Wasserstein connection (Lemma 4, Theorem 3.3) is interesting, but the bound is quite loose and depends on a bounded support radius C and noise variance V. Without empirical calibration, it is unclear if the bound is non-vacuous in realistic settings. Also, the paper does not propose a concrete algorithm to minimize W, nor enforce the constraints that make Wasserstein estimation meaningful (e.g., Lipschitz critics).
- Empirical support: The manuscript references figures showing discriminator behavior and gradient norms, but no experiments are included in the submission. There is no quantitative evaluation of the proposed noise-annealing scheme, no comparisons to strong baselines, and no ablations to demonstrate the practical value or to test the Wasserstein–JSD bound. For a venue like ICLR, this significantly weakens the case.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide rigorous, reproducible experiments demonstrating that training with the proposed instance noise and annealing schedule leads to:
  - Improved stability and convergence vs. standard GAN, feature matching, minibatch discrimination, gradient penalty, spectral normalization.
  - Reduced mode dropping (e.g., precision/recall for generative models, coverage metrics, class-conditional recall).
  - Robustness to discriminator overfitting and discriminator strength.
- Precisely specify the algorithm: noise distributions, magnitudes, annealing schedule, whether noise is injected symmetrically into G and D at all times, and how this interacts with batch normalization.
- Tighten the theory: either relax or justify the strong assumptions (e.g., independence and whiteness of errors, compactness), or clearly mark those results as heuristic. Avoid leaving core claims “as an exercise.” Fix typos/notation (norm definitions, bounds in Theorem 2.4).
- Clarify the role of Wasserstein: either connect to a concrete method (e.g., Lipschitz-constrained critics) or present the bound strictly as a conceptual tool; otherwise the inclusion feels detached from the proposed training approach.
- Position relative to prior related ideas (instance noise, denoising objectives, score matching, f-GANs, WGAN/gradient penalty), and explain what is new beyond formalizing intuition.

Questions for the authors
1) Please include the missing figures and a full empirical section. Do the proposed noise injections and annealing measurably improve training stability and sample quality across datasets? How sensitive are results to the noise schedule?
2) How realistic is Theorem 2.6’s noise model for D and ∇D? Can you provide empirical evidence (e.g., gradient distribution histograms) that supports heavy-tailed behavior in practice?
3) Theorem 2.5 is evaluated at θ0 with optimal D*. How do your conclusions change when D is not optimal, as in actual training?
4) In Theorem 2.1/2.2, do your constructions yield discriminators realizable by standard neural networks under finite data/optimization? Can you quantify approximation error and its effect on generator gradients?
5) In Theorem 3.3, are the constants C and V small enough in practical regimes for the bound to be informative? Can you empirically validate the relationship between JSD(Pr+||Pg+) and an estimate of W(Pr,Pg) as noise is annealed?
6) How does your proposal compare empirically to modern stabilizers (e.g., gradient penalty, spectral norm)? Can they be combined?
