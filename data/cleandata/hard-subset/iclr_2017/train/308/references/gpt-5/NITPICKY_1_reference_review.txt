Summary and contributions
- The paper analyzes instability in GAN training. It argues that when Pr and Pg concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds (a typical setting for neural generators), the optimal discriminator is almost everywhere constant on each support, causing vanishing gradients for the minimax generator loss and heavy-tailed, unstable updates for the -log D variant. It formalizes “perfect discrimination” via transversality arguments, shows JSD is maximized and KL divergences are infinite under manifold support mismatch, and proves vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) and heavy-tailed generator updates under noisy discriminators (Theorem 2.6). 
- To mitigate, it proposes adding continuous noise to discriminator inputs (and optionally to generator outputs as well), making the convolved distributions absolutely continuous with overlapping support. It derives gradients that resemble attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from the model manifold (Theorem 3.2), and asserts that with noise added to both sides the generator minimizes JSD(Pr+ || Pg+). 
- Finally, it connects this noisy-JSD to a bound on the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions (Theorem 3.3), suggesting annealing the noise while training the discriminator to optimality on noisy inputs as a principled recipe.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Substantial mathematical and definitional inconsistencies (especially in the Wasserstein section) and unrigorous assumptions in key theorems; (2) Missing empirical evidence (figures referenced but not provided; no experiments demonstrating the proposed method); (3) Numerous formatting, notation, and citation issues that impede confidence and clarity.

Supporting arguments
- Correctness and rigor
  - JSD definition is misstated: the paper writes JSD(Pr||Pg)= 1 KL(Pr||PA)+ 2 KL(Pg||PA) while the standard definition is 1/2 for both terms. This is not a harmless typo—coefficients matter downstream.
  - Wasserstein metric is defined as W(P,Q)= inf ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ without a square root, but subsequent bounds behave as if using W2 (or W1). Lemma 4 bounds W by √V, and Theorem 3.3 uses W ≤ C·TV, both compatible with W1 or W2 with square root, but not with the squared-cost definition given. This inconsistency invalidates the stated inequalities as written.
  - Theorem 3.3 uses Pinsker’s inequality but the constants are garbled (appears as √(1 KL) and missing the 1/2), and the transition from W to TV also mismatches the chosen Wasserstein definition. As a result, the bound lacks a coherent, correct metric framework.
  - Theorem 2.6 assumes D−D* and ∇D−∇D* are independent white Gaussian processes, independent across x, and mutually independent, and then divides a Gaussian vector by an independent scalar Gaussian to claim a Cauchy distribution for each coordinate, then claims the average over z remains Cauchy. This relies on very strong and unrealistic independence/measurability assumptions (acknowledged in a footnote), ignores the boundedness of D in [0,1], and skips regularity conditions needed to justify exchanging gradients and expectations. Even if the stable-law result is qualitatively evocative, it is not a sound model of practical discriminators.
  - Several statements are left “as an exercise to the reader” (e.g., Theorem 2.3) or “proof identical to Theorem 3.2, omitted.” For a theory paper, this is not acceptable when those results are central.
  - The claim that adding noise and then backpropagating through noisy generator outputs yields exactly 2∇θ JSD(Pr+||Pg+) requires regularity and interchange of limits; it is stated without conditions or proof details.
  - Lemma 1’s “finite union of linear manifolds” argument for piecewise-linear activations is informal. The number of regions is finite per fixed architecture, but the “union” statement as written should specify dependence on layer-wise activation patterns; it would benefit from a precise statement (e.g., piecewise affine map with finitely many regions implies image is a finite union of images of affine maps applied to Z, hence a finite union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z).
- Empirical support
  - The text refers to Figures 1–3 showing behaviors (JSD “maxed out,” gradient norms growing, etc.) but no figures are included. No experiments demonstrate that training on noisy inputs with annealing yields improved stability or performance. No comparison to standard regularizations (e.g., gradient penalty, spectral normalization) or to Wasserstein GANs is provided. Claims about practical benefits are thus unsupported.
- Placement and motivation
  - The paper is well-motivated in analyzing instability and manifold mismatch, and the idea of injecting noise (instance noise) is known to mitigate discriminator overfitting. However, the discussion of related work is incomplete: there is no engagement with contemporaneous advances that formalize Wasserstein objectives for GANs (and regularization schemes), despite introducing Wasserstein in Section 3. This weakens the positioning.
- Clarity and presentation quality
  - Numerous typos and misspellings (e.g., symetrical, discrimator, transversallity, aboslutely, easilly, wih, arizing), inconsistent notation (Pr vs PX), stray footnote markers (1–6) appearing mid-text without clear placement, and broken equations (e.g., missing 1/2 factors, denominators). These are pervasive and materially affect understanding.
  - References are inconsistently formatted (venue fields, years, dangling commas; “CoRR,, Bottou et al.,? Q2016 E”), making it difficult to verify citations. 
  - Some arguments rely on advanced tools (Urysohn’s smooth lemma, Sard’s Lemma, general position) without precise conditions or citations to specific versions applicable in Rd.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Resolve the Wasserstein metric inconsistency. Decide whether you use W1 or W2; define it correctly; ensure all constants and inequalities (triangle inequality, W ≤ C·TV, Pinsker) line up. If you aim for a bound in W1, set cost=||x−y|| and adjust Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 accordingly.
- Fix the JSD definition and re-derive any results where coefficients matter. 
- Provide complete proofs for Theorem 2.3, Corollary 3.2, and any steps currently hand-waved (interchanging expectations and derivatives, measurability).
- Revisit Theorem 2.6. Replace the “white noise” assumption with a more realistic model (e.g., sub-Gaussian errors with Lipschitzness), and bound the variance or tail behavior of the gradient updates instead of claiming exact Cauchy laws. Alternatively, clearly label this as a stylized model and limit conclusions accordingly.
- Include the referenced figures and add empirical validation of the proposed noise-annealing procedure: ablation on σ schedules, comparisons to GAN baselines and regularizations, metrics (FID, IS) and training stability analyses. 
- Discuss connections to and differences from Wasserstein GANs and other divergence/metric choices (f-GANs, MMD GANs), and clarify whether your bound suggests a concrete algorithm distinct from existing methods.
- Substantially edit for clarity, fix typos, unify notation, and correct all references.

Questions for the authors
1. Which Wasserstein metric are you actually using in Section 3 (W1 or W2)? Can you provide a corrected definition and consistent constants in Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3?
2. Can you provide the missing figures and quantitative experiments demonstrating that adding noise to both real and generated samples, with annealing, improves stability and sample quality over strong baselines?
3. In Theorem 2.6, can you replace the white-noise model with assumptions compatible with bounded discriminators and provide high-probability bounds on the gradient magnitude/variance?
4. What conditions guarantee that ∇θ JSD(Pr+||Pg+) is exactly the generator gradient under your proposed procedure? Please state the regularity and approximation assumptions precisely.
5. How does your approach compare empirically to WGAN-GP or spectral normalization? Does instance noise alone suffice, or is it complementary?
6. Can you clarify the domain X and ensure sup norms in Theorem 2.4 are finite (e.g., compact X or Lipschitz constraints)?
