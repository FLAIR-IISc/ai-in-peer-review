Summary of contributions
- The paper analyzes why GAN training is unstable and why generator gradients degrade as the discriminator improves. It formalizes the “manifold mismatch” perspective: with typical generators g(z) mapping from low-dimensional Z to high-dimensional X, Pg lies on a low-dimensional set; if Pr also lies near a low-dimensional manifold, supports are typically disjoint or intersect on sets of measure zero. The paper proves that in such cases there exist smooth, perfect discriminators that are constant almost everywhere on the supports, making generator gradients either vanish (original “saturating” loss) or become highly unstable (non-saturating loss).
- It provides theorems showing: (i) D* achieves accuracy 1 with ∇xD* = 0 almost everywhere on the supports (Theorems 2.1–2.2); (ii) generator gradients vanish as D→D* under the saturating loss (Theorem 2.4); (iii) the non-saturating loss corresponds locally to optimizing KL(Pg||Pr) minus 2 JSD (Theorem 2.5), offering a mechanism for mode dropping; and (iv) noisy discriminator approximations induce heavy-tailed (Cauchy) gradient noise under strong independence assumptions (Theorem 2.6).
- To mitigate these issues, the paper proposes adding continuous noise (“instance noise”) to both real and generated samples, making Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous. It derives the optimal gradient structure under Gaussian noise (Theorem 3.2) and shows that backpropagating through noise in the generator leads to gradients proportional to ∇θ JSD(Pr+||Pg+) (Corollary 3.2), with an intuitive attraction to the data manifold and repulsion from overrepresented model regions.
- It connects this smoothing approach to the Wasserstein distance via an upper bound on W(Pr,Pg) in terms of the noise variance and the JSD between smoothed distributions (Theorem 3.3), suggesting one can anneal noise and reliably train D to optimality while still minimizing a meaningful distance between the original distributions.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient empirical evidence to substantiate the proposed remedy (noise injection and annealing) and its claimed stability/quality improvements; (2) Several technical and presentation issues, including inconsistent definitions (notably for the Wasserstein distance), strong or unrealistic assumptions (white-noise D errors), and some proofs/claims that need tightening.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The problem tackled (GAN instability/vanishing gradients) is important and well-motivated. The manifold-support viewpoint and its implications for divergences (JSD at log 2; KL divergences infinite) are clearly articulated and relevant to the literature. The idea of smoothing with noise is plausible and aligns with known heuristics, and the connection to transport distances is appealing.
- Technical soundness: Many arguments are sound in spirit and offer useful intuition; however:
  - Lemma 1’s claim that g(Z) is a countable union of ≤dim Z manifolds for common activations is plausible, but the proof relies on nontrivial geometric arguments (Sard/Morse) and piecewise-linear structure; it would benefit from explicit regularity assumptions and clearer handling of non-generic cases.
  - Theorems 2.1–2.2 rely on constructing perfect discriminators that are constant on open neighborhoods of each support. While valuable for intuition, this construction does not establish that such discriminators are realizable or approximable by the neural architectures used in practice under standard regularization; a capacity/approximation argument (with rates) would strengthen the claim that training pushes D toward such regimes.
  - Theorem 2.4’s vanishing-gradient result depends on a strong norm bound ‖D−D*‖ and a uniform bound on the generator Jacobian. This is fine as a local result but omits how close practical D get to D* and at what sample complexity.
  - Theorem 2.5’s identification of the non-saturating update with ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD] at θ0 is interesting; however, it is a local identity at a fixed D* and assumes differentiability of θ→Pg. The negative JSD term (pushing apart) is notable but would benefit from further analysis on its quantitative effect during learning with non-optimal D.
  - Theorem 2.6 uses very strong and unrealistic assumptions: D*−D and ∇xD*−∇xD are independent white Gaussian processes, independent across x and from each other. This yields a neat Cauchy statement but is a poor model of discriminator approximation errors; a weaker result under correlated noise or finite capacity would be more convincing.
  - Section 3: The convolution results (Theorem 3.1/Corollary) are standard. The gradient structure with Gaussian kernels (Theorem 3.2) is insightful. Corollary 3.2’s equality to 2∇θ JSD(Pr+||Pg+) deserves a more careful derivation given the earlier observation that L(D*,g) relates to JSD up to constants and the use of the saturating loss. The main concern is Theorem 3.3: the paper defines the Wasserstein metric as ∫||x−y||^2 dγ but then uses bounds consistent with the 1-Wasserstein distance (cost ||x−y||), leading to an inconsistency. This must be fixed (define W1 or W2 consistently and adjust constants/square roots). The reliance on total variation and Pinsker is standard but gives loose bounds; still acceptable if clearly stated.
- Empirical support: The paper references figures (vanishing gradients, variance growth) but does not present systematic experiments demonstrating that the proposed instance-noise training with annealing measurably improves stability, reduces mode collapse, or achieves competitive sample quality on standard benchmarks. Given the practical claims (e.g., “we can train D to optimality without problems”), quantitative evidence and ablations (noise levels, annealing schedules, kernel choices) are needed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all part of the decision)
- Clarify and fix the definition of the Wasserstein distance. If using W1, define with cost ||x−y|| and make Lemma 4/T3.3 consistent. If aiming for W2, include square roots appropriately.
- Provide empirical validation:
  - Implement the proposed training with instance noise on standard image datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA), include annealing strategies, and compare to baseline GAN training with/without tricks.
  - Measure training stability (gradient norms, D optimality proxy), mode coverage (e.g., precision/recall for generative models), and sample quality (FID/IS).
  - Ablate noise distribution (Gaussian vs. other tails), magnitude, annealing schedule, and whether noise is applied to generator inputs only, discriminator inputs only, or both.
- Relax Theorem 2.6’s assumptions. Analyze gradient noise under more realistic correlated errors (e.g., D errors arising from finite data/capacity), or provide concentration bounds rather than Cauchy exactness.
- Strengthen realizability arguments: discuss approximation of the “perfect discriminator” by common architectures and the role of Lipschitz/regularization. Explicitly connect Theorems 2.1–2.2 to function classes used in practice.
- Expand the discussion on the choice of noise: covariance shaping induces a metric; relate this to kernel density estimation and MMD, and discuss trade-offs in bias vs. variance and sample complexity.
- Improve clarity and correctness throughout: fix typos, ensure all claims are proven (avoid “left as an exercise” for key statements), and standardize notation (densities vs. measures, manifold assumptions, boundaries).

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide empirical results showing that adding/annealing noise and backpropagating through noise in both generator and discriminator stabilize training and improve mode coverage vs. strong baselines? What noise schedules and amplitudes work best?
- Which Wasserstein distance do you intend to use in Theorem 3.3 (W1 or W2)? Please correct the definition and bounds accordingly, and provide the precise constants.
- How sensitive are the theoretical conclusions (e.g., vanishing gradients) to the capacity of D and regularization (e.g., spectral norm, gradient penalties)? Do your results still indicate vanishing gradients when D is constrained to be Lipschitz?
- For Theorem 2.6, can you analyze a more realistic noise model where D−D* and ∇xD−∇xD* are correlated and spatially smooth? Do heavy tails persist, or can you at least establish high variance?
- Lemma 1: what are the minimal regularity assumptions on g and activations that ensure g(Z) is a union of ≤dim Z manifolds? How do you handle nondifferentiability at ReLU kink sets rigorously?
- Corollary 3.2: can you derive the exact proportionality constant relating the gradient of the saturating loss under noise to ∇θ JSD(Pr+||Pg+), and clarify any dependence on the optimal D* value offset?
