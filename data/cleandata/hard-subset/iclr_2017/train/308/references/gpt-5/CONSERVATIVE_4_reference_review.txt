Summary
The paper analyzes why GAN training is often unstable. It formalizes that when real and model distributions concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds (with non-overlapping support almost surely), an optimal discriminator exists that is constant almost everywhere on those supports, leading to vanishing gradients for the original (saturating) generator loss. For the non-saturating loss, the paper models discriminator approximation error as independent white noise and argues the generator update becomes Cauchy-distributed with infinite variance, explaining instability. As a remedy, it proposes adding noise to both data and generated samples (instance noise), which makes the distributions absolutely continuous and yields smooth, non-degenerate gradients; it derives a gradient expression that moves generated samples toward the data manifold and away from high-probability generator regions. Finally, it connects this “noisy JSD” training to the Wasserstein distance via a bound W(Pr, Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+ || Pg+), suggesting that minimizing JSD between the noise-smoothed distributions and annealing the noise reduces the true Wasserstein distance.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The work lacks empirical validation; no quantitative experiments compare the proposed approach to established baselines. (2) Several theoretical claims rely on strong and unrealistic assumptions (e.g., white-noise discriminator error independent across x), and important technical gaps are acknowledged by the authors themselves. (3) The proposed fix (instance noise with annealing) is not novel operationally and is insufficiently positioned relative to established alternatives such as WGAN/WGAN-GP and spectral normalization.

Supporting arguments
- Assumptions and rigor: The “perfect discriminator on low-dimensional manifolds” result and the vanishing gradients for the saturating loss are consistent with known observations, but much of the analysis formalizes folklore rather than breaking new theoretical ground. The instability result for the non-saturating loss (Cauchy updates) critically assumes discriminator value and gradient errors are independent white noise across x; this is a very strong and unrealistic modeling choice in modern neural networks, and the paper explicitly notes measurability issues. As presented, it does not constitute a convincing general explanation of instability.

- Proposed remedy and novelty: Adding Gaussian noise to inputs (and annealing) to smooth distributions (“instance noise”) is a known heuristic. The paper contributes intuition and some derivations (e.g., gradients proportional to noisy-JSD), but the step from these expressions to a robust, practical algorithm is missing. The corollary equating the gradient to 2∇θ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) is asserted “with identical proof” rather than established carefully, and hinges on an optimal discriminator and infinite capacity.

- Relation to established literature: The paper does not adequately position the proposal relative to WGAN (Earth Mover distance) and its stabilized variants (gradient penalty), spectral normalization, and f-GANs. The Wasserstein/JSD bound is loose (depends on compact support/diameter C, noise variance V) and does not lead to an operational training objective superior to WGAN-GP. Without empirical comparisons, it’s unclear why practitioners should prefer noisy-JSD over these well-established alternatives.

- Empirical evidence: The manuscript refers to figures but provides no results. There are no quantitative measures (FID/IS, precision-recall for generative models, mode coverage), no ablations on noise level/annealing schedule, and no comparisons to DCGAN, Improved GAN, WGAN(-GP), or spectral normalization. Given the field’s sensitivity to heuristics, strong empirical support is essential.

- Clarity and presentation: Some proofs are deferred and several claims are left “as an exercise.” The references are inconsistent; several citations are incomplete, and some relevant work appears to be missing. Notational choices and footnotes indicate unresolved technicalities that weaken the theoretical claims.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Provide thorough experiments on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN), with multiple random seeds and metrics (FID, precision-recall, coverage). Compare against DCGAN, Improved GAN, WGAN and WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, and existing instance-noise baselines. Ablate noise type, covariance, and annealing schedules.

- Relax the white-noise assumption. Derive instability conclusions under weaker, more realistic approximation errors (e.g., bounded additive errors, correlated processes, Lipschitz-bounded discriminators). Address the measurability issues rigorously or avoid them with cleaner assumptions.

- Strengthen the theoretical link to practice: formalize the optimization problem you actually propose to solve (noisy GAN with annealed noise), provide convergence analysis or at least stability guarantees under realistic capacity/optimization assumptions, and make clear how to choose the annealing schedule.

- Position the contribution relative to WGAN/WGAN-GP and spectral normalization. Explain theoretical and practical advantages (if any) of noisy-JSD over these methods, and demonstrate them empirically.

- Improve clarity, fix citation issues, and supply the missing figures. Provide complete, self-contained proofs for central claims (e.g., Corollary 3.2).

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the noise variance and annealing schedule? Is there a principled method to set σ(t), and what are the failure modes if σ is too small/large?

2) Can you obtain an instability result for the non-saturating loss under weaker, correlated, or bounded discriminator approximation errors (rather than independent white noise)? How would the conclusion change?

3) Do you have empirical evidence that minimizing JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) with annealing actually reduces W(Pr, Pg) in practice, as suggested by Theorem 3.3? Please provide quantitative trends of the bound’s terms during training.

4) How does the proposed method compare empirically to WGAN-GP and spectral normalization in terms of stability, sample quality, and mode coverage?

5) The compact-support/diameter C assumption in Theorem 3.3 is unrealistic in high-dimensional image spaces. Can you provide a version of the bound without that assumption or justify it in practice?

6) To what extent do your manifold and transversality arguments hold for modern convolutional generators and discriminators (e.g., residual connections, batch/instance normalization)? Can you provide a precise statement tailored to these architectures?
