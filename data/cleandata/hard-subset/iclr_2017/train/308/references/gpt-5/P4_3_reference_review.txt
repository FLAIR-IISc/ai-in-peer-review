Summary of contributions
- The paper analyzes why GAN training degrades as the discriminator improves. Under the manifold hypothesis (data Pr and generator Pg supported on low-dimensional sets that do not perfectly align), it proves that an optimal discriminator can be perfect while having zero gradient almost everywhere on both supports. From this, it derives: (i) the original generator loss suffers vanishing gradients as D→D*, and (ii) the commonly used −log D generator loss can yield highly unstable updates when D is approximated with noise.
- It formalizes these effects with theorems: vanishing generator gradients for the original loss as ‖D−D*‖→0, and heavy-tailed (Cauchy) update distributions for −log D under a white-noise approximation to D and ∇D. This provides a principled explanation for observed instability/mode dropping.
- The paper proposes smoothing both data and model distributions by injecting continuous noise into inputs of the discriminator (and symmetrically through the generator samples), making Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous and allowing a well-behaved JSD-based training signal. It shows the induced generator gradient resembles a kernel-weighted attraction towards the data manifold and repulsion from overrepresented model samples, and equals 2∇θ JSD(Pr+||Pg+) when backpropagating through noisy generator outputs as well.
- It advocates softer metrics that incorporate distances between supports, and derives a bound relating the Wasserstein distance W(Pr,Pg) to the variance of the injected noise and the JSD between the smoothed distributions: W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+||Pg+). This motivates annealing the noise while minimizing JSD(Pr+||Pg+), as a proxy objective that remains informative even when Pr and Pg lie on disjoint manifolds.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The central instability result relies on very strong, unrealistic noise assumptions for the discriminator and its gradient (independent white noise across x), without adequate empirical validation; (2) The proposed remedy (noise injection and annealing) is not substantiated by systematic experiments demonstrating stability improvements and competitive sample quality across benchmarks.

Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled and motivation: The paper addresses a timely and important question—why GAN training is unstable and what objective is effectively optimized. It is well motivated and grounded in known observations about JSD saturation and mode dropping. The placement in the literature is appropriate, contrasting likelihood-based KLs with JSD and motivating distance-aware alternatives (e.g., Wasserstein).
- Technical soundness:
  - The “perfect discrimination” results under manifold support and transversality assumptions are standard in spirit and plausibly correct given the provided lemmas (use of Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s and transversality arguments). They articulate clearly how D can be perfect yet locally flat on the supports.
  - The vanishing gradient result for the original generator loss under small ‖D−D*‖ and bounded Jacobian energy is sound and matches practice.
  - The derivation for the −log D gradient at a fixed θ0 equals ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr)−2 JSD] (evaluated at θ0 with D*=D*(θ0)) is interesting, but is local and hinges on the discriminator being exactly optimal for θ0 and fixed during the generator step. This limits its scope as a characterization of the actual training dynamics.
  - The instability theorem (Cauchy-tailed updates) assumes D(gθ(z))≈0 locally and that errors in D and ∇D are independent, spatially white Gaussian processes and mutually independent. These assumptions are not realistic for neural discriminators trained on finite data. The authors acknowledge the strength of these assumptions, but the result’s relevance to practical training remains unclear without empirical corroboration.
  - The smoothing results (making Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous; form of the induced gradient; equality to 2∇θJSD(Pr+||Pg+) when injecting noise on both sides) are well motivated and technically consistent. The Wasserstein upper bound derived via triangle inequality, total variation, and Pinsker is mathematically valid but very loose (depends on diameter C and does not exploit structure), so its quantitative guidance for practice is limited.
- Empirical support: The paper references figures indicating discriminator error behavior, gradient norms, and variance trends, but does not provide systematic experiments demonstrating that the proposed noisy training with annealing leads to stable training and strong sample quality compared to baselines. Without such evidence, the practical impact is uncertain.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not strictly part of the decision)
- Strengthen empirical validation:
  - Implement the proposed training scheme: discriminator and generator both trained on noisy samples, with an annealing schedule for noise variance. Evaluate stability (convergence of losses/gradients), mode coverage (e.g., metrics on synthetic multimodal data), and image quality/diversity (FID, precision/recall) on standard datasets. Compare to common heuristics (label smoothing, instance noise) and strong GAN baselines.
  - Provide ablation on noise variance schedules and covariance choices to confirm the predicted gradient structure and stability.
- Clarify assumptions and scope:
  - For Theorem 2.6, relax the white-noise/independence assumptions or empirically measure the joint distribution of D and ∇D errors in trained discriminators to justify the model. Alternatively, provide a more general result (e.g., heavy-tailed behavior under correlated noise).
  - Discuss settings where supports may overlap substantially (e.g., with data augmentation or stochastic generators) and how conclusions change.
- Expand algorithmic guidance:
  - Translate the insights into a concrete algorithm, including the exact loss, noise injection points, and annealing rule. Provide convergence heuristics and computational overhead analysis.
  - Discuss connections and differences with other smoothing approaches (instance noise, label noise) and with Wasserstein-GAN style objectives; clarify when your method is preferable.
- Improve clarity and presentation:
  - Fix minor typos and notational inconsistencies, and include all referenced figures. Clearly distinguish absolute continuity vs. continuity and consistently state the measure spaces and norms used. Ensure measurability issues mentioned around stochastic processes are addressed rigorously or avoided with different approximations.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is the proposed “noisy GAN” to the choice of noise distribution and annealing schedule? Can you provide experiments showing stability gains across datasets and architectures relative to standard GAN training and instance-noise baselines?
2) Theorem 2.6 assumes independent, spatially white Gaussian errors in D and ∇D. Can you either justify this model empirically (e.g., by measuring error correlations across x) or provide a version of the result under correlated, structured noise?
3) The Wasserstein bound is very loose and depends on the diameter C. Can you either tighten the bound (e.g., via Lipschitz assumptions on the smoothed densities) or empirically demonstrate that minimizing JSD(Pr+||Pg+) with annealed noise indeed tracks reductions in W(Pr,Pg) in practice?

Overall, the paper contains valuable theoretical insights into GAN pathologies and motivates smoothing and distance-aware objectives. However, key claims rely on strong assumptions, and the lack of systematic empirical validation of the proposed remedy limits confidence in practical impact.
