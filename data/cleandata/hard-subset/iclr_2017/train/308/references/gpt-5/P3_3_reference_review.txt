Summary and contributions
The paper tackles instability in GAN training by analyzing why optimal or near-optimal discriminators induce either vanishing or erratic gradients for the generator. The core claims are:
- For typical generator architectures, Pg is supported on a set of measure zero (a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim(Z)), and likely disjoint from Pr’s support. Under these conditions there exist smooth discriminators with accuracy 1 whose gradient is zero almost everywhere on both supports (Theorems 2.1–2.3).
- With the original generator loss log(1−D), gradients vanish as D approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4), formalizing the widely observed “better D → worse G updates” phenomenon.
- With the alternative −log D generator loss, the update at θ0 equals ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)], hence emphasizing the reverse KL and encouraging mode dropping while also including a term that locally repels distributions (Theorem 2.5). Moreover, with a noisy approximation to D*, the generator gradient can have a heavy-tailed distribution (Cauchy) under strong assumptions (Theorem 2.6), which qualitatively explains unstable updates.
- To mitigate these pathologies, the paper proposes adding continuous noise (“instance noise”) to D’s inputs (and to G’s outputs when backpropagating), smoothing the distributions so their supports overlap. It derives explicit forms for generator gradients under Gaussian noise, which effectively move samples toward high-density regions of Pr while repelling samples from high-density regions of Pg (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2).
- It argues for softer metrics by bounding the Wasserstein distance W(Pr,Pg) in terms of the noise variance and JSD between the smoothed distributions (Theorem 3.3), thereby justifying training with noisy inputs and annealing the noise.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The central empirical claim—instance noise stabilizes training and yields better gradients—is not supported by rigorous experiments in the submission. Figures are referenced but not provided; no quantitative evaluations or comparisons are shown.
- Some theoretical results rely on unrealistic or under-justified assumptions (e.g., white-noise discriminator error independent pointwise in x), and several derivations lack full rigor or clarity.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper addresses a central open problem and the overall motivation is compelling. The manifold-support argument explaining perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients is valuable and aligns with community intuition. The proposed remedy (instance noise with annealing) is sensible and has been echoed subsequently in the literature.
- Relation to prior work: The paper situates its discussion relative to maximum likelihood (KL), JS divergence in original GANs, and the role of reverse KL in −log D. However, it underplays connections to contemporaneous work on stabilizing GANs via alternative divergences/metrics (e.g., Wasserstein GAN) and f-GANs, or to earlier notions of input noise as regularization. A deeper comparison to these lines, conceptually and empirically, is needed to delineate novelty and practical impact.
- Theoretical correctness:
  - Lemma 1 (image of Z under typical NN architectures lies in a countable union of manifolds with dimension ≤ dim Z) is plausible; the proof outline is reasonable given piecewise-linear or diffeomorphic components, though care about finiteness vs countability of linear regions for unbounded inputs should be addressed.
  - Theorems 2.1–2.3 (perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients with original loss) are convincing and consistent with measure-theoretic facts; the transversality discussion is apt, though the manifold assumptions on data should be discussed critically (e.g., measurement noise).
  - Theorem 2.5 (−log D update equals gradient of reverse-KL minus 2 JSD at θ0 with D* fixed) is an interesting local identity with nontrivial implications for mode dropping. A fully explicit and careful derivation (signs, conditions for differentiation under the integral, dependence on θ0) would strengthen confidence.
  - Theorem 2.6 (Cauchy-distributed updates) depends on an assumption that the approximation errors in D and ∇D are independent white Gaussian processes across x and independent of z. This is not realistic for neural networks, raises measurability issues acknowledged by the authors, and limits the value of the conclusion beyond intuition-building.
  - The smoothing results (Theorem 3.1, 3.2, Corollary 3.2) are the most constructive part: they formalize how Gaussian smoothing induces overlapping densities and interpretable gradients. However, the key practical question—how to choose and anneal noise—remains empirical.
  - The Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) uses standard inequalities (triangle inequality for W, W ≤ C·TV, Pinsker). It communicates that small JSD between smoothed distributions plus small noise variance implies small W(Pr,Pg). The dependence on a diameter constant C and JSD’s sensitivity to smoothing should be discussed (tightness and practical interpretability).

- Rigor and presentation: The draft contains numerous typos, incomplete citations, and imprecise symbols (e.g., norms and constants in Theorem 2.4), and references to missing figures. Some proofs defer essential technicalities to appendices that are not fully rigorous. For a theory-heavy submission, higher precision is needed.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide a thorough empirical section:
  - Compare instance noise (with annealing) against strong GAN baselines on standard datasets, measuring stability, mode coverage (e.g., precision/recall for generative models), FID/IS, and gradient statistics. Include ablations on noise variance schedules and adding noise to G vs D only.
  - Show concrete improvements and failure modes relative to the original and −log D objectives.
- Clarify and tighten Theorem 2.4 (vanishing gradient bound): clearly state constants and norms, required smoothness/Lipschitz assumptions on D and g, and conditions under which the bound is nontrivial.
- Revisit Theorem 2.6: relax independence/white-noise assumptions. For example, assume bounded approximation error with Lipschitz D and derive high-variance or bias bounds instead of Cauchy tails.
- Discuss practical guidance for noise schedules. Connect to annealed denoising, score matching, and diffusion-style smoothing for better intuition and possible extensions.
- Position more explicitly relative to Wasserstein-based training and f-divergence GANs: when is instance noise preferable, complementary, or inferior?
- Improve exposition: fix typos and references; define all measures and norms; ensure all lemmas specify conditions (compactness, measurability, boundedness).

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are the results to the manifold-support assumption? If Pr has small measurement noise (hence absolute continuity), do your vanishing/instability results still hold approximately, and do the smoothing prescriptions still help?
2) Can you provide rigorous bounds on generator gradient variance when D is near-optimal but not modeled as white noise? For example, under bounded approximation error in a Sobolev norm.
3) What annealing schedule for the noise do you recommend in practice? How do you detect when to reduce σ? Please provide experimental evidence.
4) How does adding noise to D only compare to adding noise to both D and G (as you advocate) in terms of stability and sample quality?
5) Can you empirically validate the push-pull interpretation from Theorem 3.2 (attraction to Pr, repulsion from Pg) using diagnostics on learned samples?
6) How does the proposed approach compare empirically to enforcing Lipschitz constraints (e.g., gradient penalties) or directly optimizing Wasserstein distances?
7) Theorem 2.5 is a local statement at θ0 with D* fixed. How predictive is it of actual gradient directions during training where D is not optimal? Any empirical correlation?
