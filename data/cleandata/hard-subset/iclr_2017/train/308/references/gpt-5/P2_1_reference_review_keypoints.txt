- Tackles why GAN training is unstable and generator gradients deteriorate as discriminator improves, using manifold structure of data and generator.
- Shows that when Pr and Pg lie on disjoint or misaligned low-dimensional manifolds, smooth perfect discriminators can be locally constant on supports (Thms. 2.1–2.2).
- Proves JSD saturation and infinite KL divergences under manifold mismatch (Thm. 2.3).
- Proves that for the original GAN generator objective, generator gradients vanish as the discriminator approaches optimality (Thm. 2.4).
- Links the common −log D update to ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD], explaining mode dropping and pushing away from data (Thm. 2.5).
- Shows that noisy approximations to the optimal discriminator can make the −log D update heavy-tailed (Cauchy) under strong independence assumptions, causing extreme instability (Thm. 2.6).
- Demonstrates that adding input noise (instance noise) makes distributions absolutely continuous and yields generator gradients that move samples toward the data manifold while repelling overly likely model regions (Thm. 3.2; Cor. 3.2).
- Relates Wasserstein distance between noiseless Pr and Pg to JSD between their noisy versions plus a noise-variance-dependent term, motivating noise training and annealing (Thm. 3.3).
- Overall argument: theoretical explanation for GAN pathologies and a principled remedy—train on noisy inputs and anneal noise to zero.
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary reason for rejection: insufficient empirical validation for proposed remedies and strength of claimed phenomena; referenced figures missing and no quantitative comparisons.
- Secondary reason: central results rely on strong, arguably unrealistic assumptions (white independent noise, idealized manifold settings, measurability caveats) not adequately discussed for practice.
- Strengths: compelling theoretical narrative, insightful geometric/topological formalization, and a useful link between −log D behavior and KL/JSD.
- Novelty caveat: manifold/JSD critique echoes motivations behind WGANs and instance noise has prior practical history; paper does not sufficiently position itself vs prior work.
- Practical import concern: JSD-noisy-to-Wasserstein bound appears loose and lacks empirical substantiation.
- Rigor vs realism concern: proofs use strong hypotheses (compact disjoint supports, transversality, white Gaussian noise, non-measurability caveats) that weaken practical linkage.
- Empirical support lacking: missing Figures 1–3, no controlled experiments comparing vanilla GAN, −log D, annealed instance noise, or metrics like FID/IS/precision-recall.
- Suggested experiments: demonstrate annealed noise improves stability and coverage on benchmarks (Stacked MNIST, Gaussian mixtures) and report quantitative metrics.
- Suggested comparisons: evaluate against stabilization techniques (WGAN-GP, spectral norm, gradient penalties, DRAGAN, DiffAugment).
- Suggested ablations: study noise distribution, variance, annealing schedules, sensitivity, and failure modes.
- Theoretical suggestions: relax white/independent noise assumption in Thm. 2.6 or provide empirical evidence of heavy-tailed gradients; clarify topology-heavy proofs and move technicalities to appendices with intuition.
- Positioning suggestions: explicitly compare and contrast with WGAN/f-GANs and prior instance-noise work; clarify when noisy-JSD optimization is preferable to Wasserstein optimization.
- Presentation fixes: include missing figures, correct typos and notation, and clarify Theorem 2.5’s practical implications (signs/weights of KL vs JSD) and computational aspects of Thm. 3.2/Cor. 3.2.
- Questions for authors: provide missing figures and quantitative experiments showing annealed instance noise helps vs baselines (including WGAN-GP).
- Questions for authors: how sensitive are results to noise distribution and annealing schedule, and is there a principled way to choose σ(t)?
- Questions for authors: can Thm. 2.6 be extended to correlated or bounded approximation errors, or can heavy-tailed gradient statistics be demonstrated empirically?
- Questions for authors: how does the proposal compare to Lipschitz-enforcing methods (spectral norm, gradient penalties) in avoiding vanishing/exploding gradients?
- Questions for authors: how tight is Thm. 3.3 in practice, and does minimizing noisy JSD while annealing actually reduce Wasserstein distance empirically?
- Questions for authors: are there failure cases where adding noise harms learning (limited capacity, high-dimensional settings), and how would one detect or adapt?
