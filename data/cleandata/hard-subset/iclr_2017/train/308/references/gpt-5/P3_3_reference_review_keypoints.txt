- Paper studies GAN instability, showing optimal/near-optimal discriminators can produce vanishing or erratic generator gradients.
- Claim: For typical generator architectures, Pg lies on a measure-zero union of manifolds (≤ dim Z), often disjoint from Pr, enabling smooth discriminators with perfect accuracy and zero gradients on supports (Thms 2.1–2.3).
- Claim: With the original generator loss log(1−D), gradients vanish as D→optimal, formalizing “better D → worse G” (Thm 2.4).
- Claim: With −log D, the generator update equals ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2·JSD(Pg||Pr)] at θ0, promoting reverse-KL behavior and mode dropping (Thm 2.5).
- Claim: Noisy approximations to D* can induce heavy-tailed (Cauchy) generator gradient distributions under strong independence assumptions (Thm 2.6).
- Proposal: Add continuous “instance noise” (to D inputs and G outputs during backprop) to smooth supports, derive Gaussian-noise gradients that attract samples to Pr and repel from Pg (Thm 3.2, Cor. 3.2).
- Theoretical justification: Bound Wasserstein W(Pr,Pg) by noise variance and JSD between smoothed distributions, motivating noisy training and annealing (Thm 3.3).
- Decision: Reject.
- Key reason for rejection: Central empirical claim that instance noise stabilizes training lacks rigorous experiments, quantitative evaluations, and referenced figures are missing.
- Key reason for rejection: Several theoretical results rely on unrealistic or under-justified assumptions (e.g., white-noise, pointwise independence), and some derivations lack full rigor or clarity.
- Positive: Motivation and manifold-support argument are compelling and align with community intuition about perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients.
- Negative: Paper underplays connections to contemporaneous stabilizing work (Wasserstein GANs, f-GANs) and earlier uses of input noise as regularization.
- Lemma 1 is plausible but needs care on finiteness vs countability of linear regions for unbounded inputs.
- Thms 2.1–2.3 convincing but manifold/data-noise assumptions should be critically discussed.
- Thm 2.5 is interesting but needs a more explicit, careful derivation (signs, differentiation under the integral, dependence on θ0).
- Thm 2.6 depends on unrealistic independence/white-noise assumptions and has measurability issues, limiting practical value.
- Smoothing results (Thms 3.1–3.2, Cor.3.2) are the most constructive part, but choosing and annealing noise remains an empirical question.
- Thm 3.3’s Wasserstein bound uses standard inequalities; dependence on diameter constant and JSD sensitivity to smoothing should be discussed.
- Presentation issues: numerous typos, incomplete citations, missing figures, imprecise symbols, and appendices that defer essential technicalities.
- Major revision suggestions: add thorough empirical comparisons (baselines, metrics, ablations), clarify constants/assumptions in Thm 2.4, relax unrealistic assumptions in Thm 2.6, and provide practical noise schedules and connections to denoising/score-matching.
- Improve positioning: explicitly compare to Wasserstein/f-divergence methods and state when instance noise is complementary or inferior.
- Exposition fixes: correct typos, define measures/norms, specify technical conditions (compactness, measurability, boundedness).
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to manifold-support assumption and measurement noise; rigorous gradient-variance bounds without white-noise; recommended annealing schedules and empirical evidence; impact of adding noise to D only vs both D and G; empirical validation of push–pull dynamics; comparison to Lipschitz constraints/Wasserstein methods; empirical relevance of Thm 2.5 when D is not optimal.
