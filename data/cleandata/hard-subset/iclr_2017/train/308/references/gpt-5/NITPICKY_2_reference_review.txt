Summary and contributions
The paper analyzes sources of instability in GAN training and argues that common divergences (KL, reverse-KL, JSD) behave poorly when the real and model distributions concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds with little or no overlap. The authors prove that in such settings (i) there exist perfect discriminators that are locally constant on the supports, causing vanishing gradients for the generator under the original GAN loss; (ii) the alternative generator update using −log D is linked to optimizing KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD, which emphasizes sample realism over mode coverage, and can lead to highly unstable updates under discriminator noise; and (iii) adding input noise (instance noise) makes both distributions absolutely continuous, yields non-degenerate gradients, and allows bounding Wasserstein distance between the original (noiseless) distributions by a function of the noise variance and JSD between the noised distributions. The paper provides several theorems to formalize these claims, and it advocates training with annealed input noise as a principled stabilization strategy.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Technical inconsistencies and errors (notational conflicts, incorrect/ambiguous definitions, and questionable steps in several key theorems/inequalities, notably the Wasserstein definition/bounds and the “Cauchy updates” result).
2) Presentation quality is significantly below venue standards (numerous typos, broken references/figures, overloaded notation, missing constants/factors in divergence formulas). The accumulation of minor issues undermines confidence in the correctness and reproducibility of the claims, even if the high-level ideas are interesting.

Supporting arguments
- Divergence definitions: The JSD formula (page 1) is incorrect/incomplete (missing 1/2 factors and mixing distribution names); KL/JSD formulae should be stated precisely. Several signs/factors reappear inconsistently later (e.g., L(D*,g) constants).
- Wasserstein distance: Definition 3.1 uses cost ||x−y||^2 (2-Wasserstein squared) but subsequent inequalities and bounds (Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3) behave like 1-Wasserstein (W1), e.g., W ≤ C·TV and Pinsker to get C·sqrt(JSD). With the squared cost, these steps are not valid as written. The chain combining V1/2 and C sqrt(JSD) appears dimensionally/definitionally inconsistent with the earlier definition. This is a serious correctness issue; the paper must clearly define which Wasserstein metric is used (W1 vs W2) and ensure all inequalities match that choice with correct constants.
- Theorem 2.6 (Cauchy update): The model assumes D* − D and ∇xD* − ∇xD are independent white-noise Gaussian processes, independent across x, and independent between function and gradient. This is unrealistic for neural network approximations to discriminators. The authors acknowledge non-measurability and independence as “albeit too strong,” which undermines the conclusion. Moreover, the object analyzed is Ez[−∇θ log D(gθ(z))], but the argument treats each coordinate as a ratio of independent Gaussians and then claims the average is Cauchy. Dependence across z and through gθ invalidates this derivation without further justification. As a result, the infinite-variance conclusion is not supported rigorously under plausible assumptions.
- Theorem 2.4: The bound’s final form is hard to parse (notation “< M − ” suggests typesetting error). The proof intermixes M as a manifold and as a constant bound on the generator Jacobian, creating confusion. The inequality chain relies on |1 − D(gθ(z))| in denominators; since D* = 0 on the support, this is fine, but the transition to (|1 − D*| − ε) requires careful justification of uniform approximation and staying away from zero, which is not fully fleshed out.
- Manifold arguments: Lemma 1 mixes rectifier/leaky-rectifier cases (piecewise linear) with “smooth strictly increasing” nonlinearities and claims images are countable unions of manifolds with dimension ≤ dim Z. While plausible in the piecewise-linear case, the smooth case relies on a sequence of diffeomorphism-projection arguments that are condensed and would benefit from precise statements and references (e.g., transversality/Morse theory claims). Some claims (e.g., “critical points of πi are isolated”) need hypotheses (properness/compactness) that are not stated. Theorems 2.1/2.2 invoke Urysohn’s smooth lemma (name and conditions unclear; the standard result is for smooth partitions of unity on manifolds; please cite precisely).
- Notation collisions: M and P denote both manifolds and probability distributions; P also denotes a density/kernel function; M also denotes a constant bound. This makes several derivations hard to follow and error-prone. Norms: the “Sobolev norm” is written as sup |D| + ||∇D||2 (not a standard Sobolev norm), but later referred to as such; please fix.
- Claims relying on “Figures 1–3”: The text cites empirical observations but the figures are absent. No experimental section demonstrates the practical impact of instance noise or the predicted behaviors (vanishing gradients, gradient variance blow-up, stabilization via noise). Given the practical thrust, this is a major omission.
- References: Many entries are incomplete (question marks, missing venues/years, malformed BibTex-like artifacts). This does not meet formatting standards.
- Typos and language: Frequent typographical errors and grammatical issues throughout (“adress,” “symetrical,” “transversallity,” “arize,” “easilly,” “Luckilly,” etc.).

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Choose one Wasserstein metric (W1 or W2), define it precisely, and ensure all subsequent inequalities and constants are correct. If you intend W1, change the definition to cost ||x−y|| and adjust Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 accordingly with clear constants and assumptions.
- Clean up notation to avoid overloading P/M; use distinct symbols for manifolds, measures, densities, and kernels. Define all constants (e.g., Z normalizers).
- Fix the JSD and KL formulas, including 1/2 factors and mixture definitions. Re-derive Theorem 2.5 with precise assumptions (regularity of densities, fixed D*, interchange of ∇ and expectation) and clarify the role of each term in the gradient decomposition.
- Replace Theorem 2.6’s white-noise assumption with a realistic discriminator approximation model (e.g., bounded error in appropriate norms, correlated errors) and provide concentration/variance bounds rather than a Cauchy-distribution claim. Ensure measurability and justify exchanging expectations/limits.
- Provide an experimental section: demonstrate that instance noise stabilizes GAN training, detail the annealing schedule, ablations on noise level, and comparisons to baseline GAN training. Include the referenced figures.
- Proof polishing: give precise statements and references for the manifold/topology lemmas used (transversality, Sard’s lemma, general position). Clarify conditions like compactness and boundary handling. Correct the “Urysohn’s smooth lemma” citation and hypotheses.
- Presentation: correct typos, fix references, and use consistent equation numbering.

Questions for the authors
1) Which Wasserstein distance are you using in Theorem 3.3 (W1 or W2)? Please provide a rigorous derivation with correct constants and assumptions, or adjust the definition to match the bounds you use.
2) Can you justify Theorem 2.6 under more realistic discriminator approximation errors (without independence/white-noise assumptions)? If not, can you provide weaker but rigorous variance bounds that still support instability?
3) In Theorem 2.5, under what regularity conditions can you exchange ∇θ and expectations and evaluate at θ0? Please provide a detailed derivation or appendix proof.
4) For Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1/2.2, please state the exact smoothness/compactness assumptions and provide precise references for the topological results invoked.
5) What concrete training procedure do you recommend for instance noise (noise type, covariance, annealing schedule)? Can you provide empirical evidence that your bound in Theorem 3.3 correlates with improved sample quality and stability in practice?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and contains insightful high-level ideas (support mismatch, instance noise, metric choice), but the current technical and presentation issues are substantial.
