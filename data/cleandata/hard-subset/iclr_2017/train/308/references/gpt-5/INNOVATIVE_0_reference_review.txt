Summary of contributions
The paper tackles a core open question in GANs: why training becomes unstable or uninformative as the discriminator improves, and how to obtain principled, stable gradients to the generator. The authors give a clear geometric/measures-theoretic account of the issue: under reasonable assumptions (manifold hypothesis and typical GAN architectures), Pr and Pg concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint or almost disjoint support; the optimal discriminator is perfect and locally constant on these supports; JSD is maxed out and both KLs are infinite, even when the manifolds are arbitrarily close. This yields (i) vanishing gradients for the saturating loss as D→D*, and (ii) heavy-tailed, unstable generator updates for the non-saturating loss in the presence of discriminator approximation noise. As a remedy, they propose adding noise to both real and generated samples (“instance noise”), which makes the convolved distributions absolutely continuous, yields meaningful and stabilizing gradients that move samples toward the data manifold while repelling from overrepresented generated regions, and allows training D near optimality without collapse. They also connect this to a more appropriate similarity measure, the Wasserstein distance, and prove an inequality bounding W(Pr,Pg) by a term depending on noise variance plus the JSD between smoothed distributions, motivating annealing of the noise while minimizing JSD(Pr+||Pg+). Overall this is a principled and ambitious reframing that provides new tools, intuition, and a concrete training direction.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, novel conceptual contribution: a convincing geometric analysis of why standard GAN divergences and gradients fail on manifold-supported distributions, and a principled remedy via noise and connections to Wasserstein distance. This anticipates and informs subsequent lines of work and opens a clear new direction.
- Theoretical results are substantial and generally sound, with clear implications for practice (train D to optimality with noise; anneal noise; interpret gradients; prefer metrics capturing support distance).

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: The paper precisely targets three questions: why gradients worsen as D improves; why training is unstable; what divergence the non-saturating loss follows. It answers them with theorems: perfect discrimination on disjoint/low-dim supports; vanishing gradients for the saturating loss as D→D*; and instability of the non-saturating loss under discriminator noise (Cauchy updates), plus the identification of the cost optimized by the non-saturating step (involving KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD), explaining mode dropping and visual fidelity bias.
- Motivation and placement: The analysis is well grounded in the manifold hypothesis and properties of neural networks (Lemma 1 on images of typical network layers), connects to prior observations about JSD/KL in generative modeling, and situates the proposed remedy within a broader metric perspective (Wasserstein). The choice to smooth distributions to re-enter the “good” regime for divergences is elegant and principled.
- Claims support: Theorems 2.1–2.4 and 2.6 are technically plausible; the assumptions are clearly stated (e.g., transversality, compactness, smooth discriminator existence via Urysohn-type lemma). The smoothing results (Theorems 3.1–3.3) are well motivated; the gradient formulas with Gaussian smoothing are insightful and directly link to a stabilizing training signal. The Wasserstein bound is especially compelling as it ties the proposed practice (noise + JSD minimization) to progress on the true, noiseless distance of interest. While empirical validation is light in the text, the narrative matches widely reported GAN phenomena.

That said, some assumptions are stronger than needed (white, independent discriminator noise across x in Theorem 2.6) and the empirical section is thin; however, given the conceptual novelty and clear theoretical pathway to algorithms (instance noise + annealing), I am willing to overlook these for ICLR.

Questions for the authors
- Instance noise schedule: How should σ be chosen and annealed in practice? Are there heuristics or theoretical criteria (e.g., monitor JSD(Pr+||Pg+), gradient norms) to adapt σ?
- Discriminator architecture: Does smoothing allow training D to near-optimality without overfitting to g(Z)? Any evidence on robustness to adversarial examples as claimed when adding noise to generator inputs too?
- Practical gains: Can you provide quantitative experiments showing improved stability and diversity (e.g., reduced mode dropping) versus standard GANs on CIFAR-10/LSUN with and without instance noise, and ablations on adding noise to D only vs both D and G?
- Beyond Gaussian noise: Your corollaries suggest different P(·) encode different notions of distance. Do heavy-tailed noises (e.g., Cauchy) or anisotropic covariances yield better trade-offs in high dimensions?
- Theorem 2.6 robustness: How sensitive is the “Cauchy updates” phenomenon to relaxing independence between D and ∇D noise, or to spatial correlations? Can you provide a weaker but still informative instability statement?

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to my decision)
- Clarify “smooth Urysohn lemma” citation and conditions (manifold structure, partitions of unity) to avoid confusion with the topological version.
- Discuss the case where data do not lie on a clean manifold (e.g., support with thickness). Which parts of the analysis still go through?
- Explicitly relate your proposal to later-used “instance noise” practices and to Wasserstein GAN; a brief comparison would help readers position the contribution.
- Provide algorithm pseudocode for training with noise and annealing, including practical tips (batchnorm, learning rates, penalty terms).
- Tighten presentation: fix minor typos and reference formatting; add empirical plots referenced in the text (Figures 1–3) with training curves and sample grids; report diversity metrics if possible.

Overall, this paper offers a bold, principled perspective on GAN failures and a clear path forward; despite some idealized assumptions and limited empirical depth, its conceptual impact is high. I recommend acceptance.
