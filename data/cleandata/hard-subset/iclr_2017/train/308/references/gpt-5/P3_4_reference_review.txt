Summary and contributions
The paper tackles the fundamental question of why GAN training is unstable and how to design principled remedies. The authors argue that, under the manifold hypothesis, both the data distribution and the generator’s distribution are supported on low-dimensional manifolds with (almost surely) disjoint supports. They prove that, in this regime, optimal discriminators are perfect and locally flat (zero gradient) almost everywhere on both supports, which leads to vanishing generator gradients under the minimax (log(1−D)) loss and to instability (heavy-tailed updates) under the non-saturating (−log D) loss when D is approximated with noise. They then propose to add instance noise to make both distributions absolutely continuous, derive the form of gradients under the noisy setting (interpretable as weighted attraction/repulsion to/from data/generated manifolds), and connect the JSD between the convolved (noisy) distributions to the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions, suggesting an annealing strategy to minimize a meaningful distance between Pr and Pg. Overall, the paper aims to provide a unified theoretical picture of GAN pathologies and a principled stabilization direction.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) There are critical theoretical inconsistencies/gaps (notably in the Wasserstein-based bounds and some manifold/image-of-network claims). (2) Empirical support is insufficient to validate the practical recommendations (instance noise, annealing, training to discriminator optimality) and to demonstrate that the proposed theory predicts/addresses observed failure modes beyond anecdotal figures. The conceptual insights are valuable, but the current form lacks rigor and validation commensurate with the strong claims.

Supporting arguments
- Perfect discrimination and vanishing gradients: Theorems 2.1/2.2/2.3 articulate that, with disjoint or low-dimensional supports that do not “perfectly align,” optimal discriminators can have accuracy 1 and are locally constant on supports, implying vanishing generator gradients for the minimax loss (Theorem 2.4). This cleanly formalizes a widely observed pathology. However, the assumptions (compact supports; existence of smooth separating D via Urysohn’s smooth lemma) are quite strong for typical image domains, and the “no perfect alignment” construction via random perturbations is plausible but glosses over measure-theoretic subtleties; nonetheless, the conclusions are qualitatively correct and insightful.

- On the generator image lying on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1): The piecewise-linear case is plausible (finite union of affine images of Z). The smooth monotone activation case is less convincing. The proof sketches rely on claims about projections having only isolated critical points (via Morse lemma) and mapping images being countable unions of manifolds. In general, images of smooth maps need not be manifolds, and coordinate projections restricted to arbitrary manifolds need not have isolated critical points without genericity assumptions. This part requires a more careful, rigorous treatment or a restriction to piecewise-linear networks to avoid overstating the result.

- Non-saturating loss and instability (Theorems 2.5/2.6): The derivation tying the −log D gradient (at D* fixed at θ0) to ∇θ(KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD) echoes prior blog analyses but needs careful checking; the sign and interpretation (that the JSD term pushes distributions apart) are surprising, and it is unclear they generalize beyond infinitesimal steps at θ0. The Cauchy-gradient result assumes D and ∇D errors are independent white noise at each x and independent across z, which is highly unrealistic for neural discriminators; the authors acknowledge the strength of the assumption. A weaker but realistic statement (growth of variance as D→D*) with empirical confirmation would be preferable.

- Noisy GAN and gradient form (Theorem 3.1, 3.2, Corollary 3.2): The convolution/absolute continuity result is standard, and the derived gradient form under Gaussian noise is intuitive and useful: it shows a kernel-weighted pull towards the data manifold and a repulsion from the generator manifold, with balanced forces near overlap. The need to inject noise into the generator outputs for backprop (to avoid measure-zero adversarial directions) is a good practical insight aligning with “instance noise” folklore.

- Wasserstein connection (Definition 3.1, Lemma 4, Theorem 3.3): Here, there are serious issues.
  • The paper defines W(P,Q) using squared Euclidean cost, i.e., an unrooted W2^2-type objective, yet later invokes Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality and a bound W ≤ C δ(P,Q), which hold for W1 (cost = ||x−y||), not for W2^2. This inconsistency affects Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3.
  • In Lemma 4, with the squared-cost definition, W(PX,PX+)=E||ε||^2 exactly for the natural coupling x→x+ε. The manuscript claims ≤ V^{1/2} for V=E||ε||^2, which is dimensionally and mathematically incorrect. If W1 were used, a √(E||ε||^2) bound would be plausible. This propagates to Theorem 3.3, likely invalidating constants and possibly the inequality as stated.
  • The claimed bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2 V^{1/2} + 2 C sqrt(JSD(Pr+||Pg+)) appears to mix W1-like bounds with a W2^2 definition. This needs a coherent choice (W1 or W2) and correct inequalities. As written, this is a critical flaw.

- Related work positioning: The paper cites Theis et al. and Huszár but omits key concurrent/related works: f-GAN (Nowozin et al.) on f-divergences; kernel/MMD GANs; prior stabilization via instance noise (e.g., Sønderby et al.); and Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al.), which more directly develops the Wasserstein-1 objective and its training benefits. The omission makes it harder to situate the contribution and assess novelty.

- Empirical support: The manuscript repeatedly refers to figures showing discriminator error, gradient norms, and sample quality under the proposed methods, but the provided text lacks sufficient experimental detail, datasets, architectures, ablations, or comparisons. Given the prominence of the practical prescription (add noise to D and G; anneal; train D to optimality), convincing empirical validation is essential.

Suggestions for improvement (constructive)
- Fix the Wasserstein section:
  • Choose W1 (cost ||x−y||) or W2 (with proper square root) consistently.
  • Re-derive Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 with correct definitions and constants, and cite appropriate inequalities (KR duality holds for W1; Pinsker bounds relate TV and KL; relate W1 to TV on bounded domains correctly).
- Tighten Lemma 1: Either (a) restrict claims to piecewise-linear networks where gating-region arguments are rigorous; or (b) provide a careful proof with proper genericity assumptions (Sard’s theorem, Whitney embedding/transversality) and clarify limits.
- Revisit Theorem 2.5: Double-check signs and provide intuition clarifying when and how the JSD term “pushes apart.” If the result is local at θ0, say so explicitly.
- Replace Theorem 2.6 with a more realistic variance analysis (e.g., show gradient variance grows as D approaches D*, under mild, empirically plausible noise models) and support with controlled experiments.
- Expand related work and clarify novelty relative to f-GAN, MMD-GANs, instance noise, and WGAN. If concurrent, state timelines.
- Provide solid experiments:
  • Quantify stability and sample quality improvements with instance noise + annealing across datasets (CIFAR-10, CelebA) and architectures.
  • Compare to standard GAN tricks and to WGAN.
  • Ablate noise magnitude, annealing schedule, and training-to-optimality of D.
  • Report metrics that align with the proposed theory (e.g., JSD between noisy distributions via held-out D; FID; precision/recall).

Questions for the authors
1) Please clarify the definition of the Wasserstein metric used and fix Lemma 4/Theorem 3.3 accordingly. Do your main conclusions still hold with W1?
2) In Lemma 1, what assumptions ensure that coordinate projections have only isolated critical points? Can you either restrict to ReLU networks or provide a rigorous, referenced proof?
3) For Theorem 2.5, can you verify the derivation and interpret the −2 JSD term? Under what conditions does the non-saturating gradient actually increase JSD locally?
4) How sensitive is the proposed instance-noise method to the noise distribution and annealing schedule? Can you provide empirical guidance?
5) Can you provide experiments demonstrating that training D closer to optimality under noise yields stable generator updates and improved sample quality, and compare against baselines (with and without noise, and against WGAN)?

The paper contains valuable insights and several compelling intuitions, but to be suitable for ICLR, the theoretical inconsistencies need to be corrected and the empirical evidence substantially strengthened.
