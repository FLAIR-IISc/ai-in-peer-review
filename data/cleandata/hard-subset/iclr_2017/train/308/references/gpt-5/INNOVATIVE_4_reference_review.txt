1. Summary and contributions
This paper tackles a central, longstanding problem: why GAN training is unstable and how to make it principled. The authors’ key claims and contributions are:
- A clean theoretical account of GAN instability when real and model distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds or have disjoint supports. They prove that in such cases the optimal discriminator is perfect, smooth, and locally constant on the supports, implying vanishing gradients for the generator under the original objective.
- For the non-saturating generator loss (−log D), they show the exact objective being optimized at the discriminator optimum (gradient equals ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)] at θ0), explaining mode dropping and “good-looking but incomplete” samples.
- They analyze instability of generator updates when using a noisy discriminator approximation, showing heavy-tailed (Cauchy) update distributions under a white-noise model of discriminator error.
- They propose a principled remedy: add input noise (instance noise) to both data and generator outputs, making the convolved distributions absolutely continuous, yielding stable, non-vanishing gradients that move samples toward the data manifold and away from the model manifold where appropriate. They prove the generator gradient equals 2 ∇θ JSD(Pr+ || Pg+) in this setting.
- They relate this “noise-annealed JSD” to the Wasserstein distance between the original, noiseless distributions, proving an upper bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2 Var(ε)1/2 + 2C sqrt(JSD(Pr+||Pg+)), which justifies annealing the noise while minimizing the JSD between smoothed distributions.

Overall, the paper offers a compelling theoretical narrative that unifies many empirical observations, introduces a clear conceptual fix (smoothing via instance noise with annealing), and connects to a softer metric (Wasserstein) to reason about convergence as noise decreases.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: The paper crystallizes why GANs fail in the manifold setting, provides nontrivial theorems (perfect discrimination with zero gradients; heavy-tailed instability of −log D updates under noisy critics), and proposes a principled, simple remedy with theoretical guarantees and a link to Wasserstein distance.
- Impact: The results offer a foundation for future stable GAN training methods and a rationale for instance noise/annealing, which can influence both theory and practice.

3. Supporting arguments
- The manifold-support results (Lemmas/Theorems 2.1–2.3) are insightful and align with the “data on low-dimensional manifolds” hypothesis. The construction of smooth perfect discriminators with zero gradients on supports is elegant and directly explains vanishing gradients and JSD saturation.
- The exact characterization of the non-saturating gradient (Theorem 2.5), tying it to inverted KL minus 2×JSD, gives a satisfying theoretical explanation for mode dropping tendencies and the empirical observation that GANs often prioritize realism over coverage.
- The heavy-tailed update result (Theorem 2.6), while relying on strong independence/white-noise assumptions, captures an essential instability mechanism: ratios of noisy quantities leading to unbounded variance updates, consistent with exploding gradients observed in practice.
- The instance-noise analysis (Theorem 3.1, Cor. 3.1, Theorem 3.2/Cor. 3.2) provides actionable, interpretable gradients that push samples toward the data manifold and away from overrepresented model regions; and the annealing connection to W(Pr,Pg) (Lemma 4, Theorem 3.3) is a compelling bridge between a tractable training signal (JSD of smoothed distributions) and the true target (distance between original distributions).

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not necessarily affecting the accept decision)
- Empirical validation: The paper references figures showing discriminator error, vanishing gradients, and gradient variance, but the submission does not include them here. A more thorough empirical study would strengthen the claims: ablations on instance noise magnitude/schedule; datasets beyond toy domains; quantitative coverage metrics; comparison to other stabilization techniques (e.g., label smoothing, gradient penalties).
- Assumptions in Theorem 2.6: The white-noise and independence assumptions for D*−D and ∇D*−∇D are strong. It would help to (a) relax them (e.g., correlated Gaussian processes, finite bandwidth noise), and/or (b) provide empirical evidence of heavy-tailed update distributions during training with near-optimal discriminators.
- Practical recipe: Provide a concrete algorithmic template for training with instance noise: how to set initial variance; annealing schedule; whether to use isotropic Gaussian vs. anisotropic noise; how to adapt to data dynamic range; interactions with batch normalization and architectural choices.
- Connections to broader divergences/metrics: The paper compellingly motivates Wasserstein as a soft metric and provides an upper bound via JSD of smoothed distributions. It would be valuable to discuss the relationship to integral probability metrics and Lipschitz critics, and whether the proposed instance-noise approach can be interpreted as a kernel smoothing that implicitly changes the function class used by the discriminator.
- Clarity and scope: The measure-theoretic sections are rigorous but dense. A more accessible exposition (proof sketches, figures) would broaden reach. Also, explicitly discussing when the compactness/manifold assumptions might fail in high-dimensional pixel spaces would be helpful.

5. Questions for the authors
- Can you provide the missing empirical figures and extend them to show: (i) effect of instance-noise annealing on FID/coverage metrics; (ii) histograms of generator gradient norms showing heavy tails near discriminator optimality; (iii) sensitivity to noise type and covariance?
- How robust is the proposed method to the choice of noise schedule? Is there a heuristic or adaptive rule (e.g., based on discriminator accuracy or gradient norms) that works reliably across datasets?
- Theorem 2.6 assumes independent white noise for value and gradient errors. Can you generalize the result to correlated or colored noise, or provide an empirical justification that the ratio-like pathology persists under more realistic critic approximation errors?
- In Theorem 3.3, can you tighten constants or avoid the diameter C dependence, perhaps via localized couplings or transportation inequalities? Any empirical correlation between sqrt(JSD of smoothed distributions) and perceptual sample quality as noise is annealed?
- Have you explored adding noise not only at the input/output but also in feature space of D (akin to data augmentation in representation spaces)? Would your analysis extend?

Overall, this paper advances a significant new conceptual framework for understanding and stabilizing GANs. Despite some assumptions and limited empirical validation, the theoretical insights are deep, coherent, and likely to catalyze further progress. I recommend acceptance.
