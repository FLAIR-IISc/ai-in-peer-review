Summary and contributions
The paper studies why GAN training is unstable and proposes a principled fix. The central thesis is that, in common settings, both the data and the generator distributions concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint (or almost disjoint) support. The authors prove “perfect discrimination” theorems showing that an optimal discriminator is constant almost everywhere on each manifold and thus conveys vanishing gradients to the generator for the saturating objective. For the non-saturating objective (−log D), they show that small discriminator estimation errors can lead to heavy-tailed, high-variance generator updates. To address this, they propose smoothing both real and generated samples with additive noise (“instance noise”), which makes the distributions absolutely continuous and yields stable, informative gradients. They further derive that the generator update becomes a contrastive term resembling the gradient of JSD between the smoothed distributions and provide a bound relating the Wasserstein distance between the original (unsmoothed) distributions and the JSD between their smoothed counterparts plus a term that depends on the noise variance. The work aims to offer a theoretical basis for common empirical heuristics and a pathway for stable training via annealed noise.

Decision: Reject (primary reasons: limited novelty relative to established literature and technical inconsistencies)
- Much of the theoretical diagnosis (support-mismatch, JSD saturation, vanishing/non-saturating gradient behavior) and the remedy via instance noise and Wasserstein-based reasoning are now well-known in the GAN literature. The paper does not sufficiently position or differentiate itself from prior work such as f-GANs, IPM-based GANs, WGAN/WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, Sobolev/MMD/Stein GANs, or prior analyses of instance noise and annealing.
- There are technical inconsistencies and correctness issues (notably around the Wasserstein distance definition and subsequent inequalities), strong assumptions (e.g., white-noise errors, perfect transversality), and a lack of empirical validation to support the claims and proposed recipe.

Supporting arguments
- Placement in literature: The paper cites early GAN work and Theis/Huszár on divergences, but omits key developments: (i) integral probability metrics and their estimators; (ii) WGAN/WGAN-GP and the 1-Lipschitz critic as a direct remedy for vanishing gradients; (iii) instance noise and noise-annealing as a practical stabilization trick; (iv) spectral normalization; (v) precision/recall-based evaluation. Without a clear contrast to these, the novelty is unclear.
- Technical correctness/clarity:
  - Wasserstein distance is defined as inf ∫ ||x−y||^2 dγ, but later lemmas and bounds treat it as the 1-Wasserstein (with ||x−y||), using inequalities that rely on E||ε|| ≤ (E||ε||^2)^{1/2}. This inconsistency propagates to Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3. The definition and all constants need to be unified (choose W1 or W2, not a squared-cost surrogate).
  - The “Urysohn smooth lemma” claim needs precise conditions (Urysohn’s lemma yields continuous, not necessarily smooth functions; smooth partitions of unity are needed on Rd). The mapping from existence to realizability by neural networks is not discussed.
  - The Cauchy-instability result (ratio of independent Gaussian errors pointwise in x) relies on (i) a white-noise model independent across x and independent from gradient noise, and (ii) measurability issues are relegated to a footnote. While illustrative, this stylized assumption weakens the practical relevance of the conclusion.
  - Several results depend on idealized manifold and transversality assumptions; while they convey intuition, the implications under finite data, approximate critics, and finite-capacity networks are not addressed.
- Empirical support: The text refers to figures (e.g., vanishing gradients; exploding gradient norms) but provides no experiments in the submission. Given the practical claims (stability with noise, annealing schedules), empirical verification against strong baselines is essential.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all part of the decision)
- Literature integration and positioning:
  - Clearly relate your smoothing approach to instance noise and to WGAN/WGAN-GP. Explain when optimizing JSD between smoothed distributions is preferable to optimizing an IPM directly (e.g., W1) and whether the two can be combined.
  - Discuss links to MMD-, Sobolev-, and Stein-based critics that also address support mismatch and gradient informativeness.
- Fix technical issues:
  - Decide on W1 or W2 and use a consistent definition and constants throughout Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3. If you use W1, the bound W(PX,PX+ ) ≤ (E||ε||^2)^{1/2} is fine; if W2, adjust statements accordingly.
  - Clarify the smooth discriminator construction: specify the regularity assumptions and provide a network approximation argument (e.g., universal approximation in Sobolev norms) to argue realizability and the effect of finite capacity.
  - Replace the white-noise assumption with a more realistic error model (e.g., bounded/Lipschitz perturbations or correlated Gaussian processes) and quantify how variance of updates scales with critic error.
  - Tighten the derivation that the non-saturating gradient equals ∇ KL(Pg||Pr) − 2∇ JSD at θ0; specify regularity conditions and clarify signs and constants.
- Experiments:
  - Evaluate the proposed instance-noise-plus-annealing scheme versus DCGAN, WGAN-GP, spectral normalization, and noise baselines. Report FID, precision/recall for generative models, gradient norms, and training stability across datasets.
  - Ablate noise scale, covariance structure (isotropic vs anisotropic), and annealing schedule; test sensitivity and provide guidance.
- Broaden theory:
  - Provide convergence guarantees as noise is annealed: under what conditions does minimizing JSD(Pr+||Pg+) imply convergence in W1 (or weak convergence) of Pg to Pr?
  - Discuss the interaction between smoothing and adversarial examples more formally; your Corollary 3.2 is promising—state precise assumptions under which ∇θ JSD(Pr+||Pg+) is recovered.

Questions for the authors
1. Which Wasserstein variant do you intend to use (W1 with Euclidean cost or W2)? Please revise Lemma 4 and Theorem 3.3 accordingly and provide the exact constants.
2. Can you provide empirical results demonstrating that your noise-annealed training improves stability and sample quality compared to WGAN-GP and spectral normalization, across multiple datasets?
3. How sensitive is performance to the choice of noise distribution and covariance? Does anisotropic noise aligned with data manifolds help?
4. Can Theorem 2.6 be extended to a more realistic critic error model (e.g., Lipschitz-bounded estimation error or correlated noise), and do heavy tails persist?
5. Are your “perfect discriminator” constructions approximable by standard neural architectures under practical parameter counts? Any approximation bounds?
6. Does minimizing JSD between smoothed distributions as σ→0 guarantee convergence of Pg to Pr in a standard metric? Under what assumptions?

Overall, the paper addresses a real and important problem with several insightful arguments, but it requires substantial revision for correctness, positioning, and empirical validation to meet ICLR standards.
