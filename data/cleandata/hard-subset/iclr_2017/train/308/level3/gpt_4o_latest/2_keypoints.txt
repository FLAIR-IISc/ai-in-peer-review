- Rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics identifying gradient vanishing and instability causes (low-dimensional manifolds, disjoint supports).  
- Proposes a perturbation method (adding noise to discriminator and generator inputs) that is theoretically shown to smooth gradients, stabilize training, and better align distributions.  
- Establishes connections between the perturbation method and Jensen-Shannon divergence, Wasserstein distance, and contrastive divergence, offering new analytical tools.  
- Empirical validation is limited and insufficient to support practical claims about the method’s effectiveness.  
- Decision: Reject—valuable theoretical contributions but lacking experimental evidence to justify acceptance.  
- Strength: Addresses a critical GAN problem with a novel, well-motivated method and rigorous proofs; insightful discussion of alternative metrics.  
- Weakness: Sparse experiments, underexplored dropout analogy, and dense technical presentation limiting accessibility.  
- Suggestion: Add comprehensive experiments across GAN architectures and datasets, report metrics like FID/IS, and compare against stabilization baselines (spectral norm, gradient penalty, TTUR).  
- Suggestion: Empirically investigate the dropout analogy and provide guidance/ablation on noise distribution and variance.  
- Suggestion: Improve clarity with intuitive explanations and visualizations illustrating manifolds and gradient flow.  
- Questions for authors: How does the method compare in computational overhead and performance to other techniques; tested on large-scale/complex GANs; empirical evidence for preventing generator adversarial examples; how to choose noise distribution/variance and sensitivity to it.
