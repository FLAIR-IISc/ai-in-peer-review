This paper tackles the challenging problem of understanding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) from a theoretical perspective. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the sources of instability in GAN training and propose a novel solution to mitigate these issues. The paper is well-motivated, and the approach is well-placed in the literature.

The authors start by highlighting the difficulties in training GANs, which are widely used for generating realistic images. They note that despite their success, there is a lack of theory explaining the unstable behavior of GAN training. The paper aims to fill this gap by providing a solid understanding of these issues and creating principled research directions towards addressing them.

The authors identify two main sources of instability in GAN training: (1) the use of asymmetric divergences, which can lead to vanishing gradients, and (2) the fact that the generator's distribution is not continuous, which can cause the discriminator to become perfect and lead to unstable updates.

To address these issues, the authors propose a smoothing-based alternative, which involves adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This approach is shown to lead to smoother gradients and more stable updates. The authors also provide a theoretical analysis of the proposed approach, including bounds on the Wasserstein distance between the generator's distribution and the data distribution.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The use of analysis and differential topology concepts is interesting and provides a new perspective on the problem of GAN training.

However, the paper is somewhat long and technical, and some parts and consequences need further development. The authors could consider shortening Section 2.1 and moving proofs to the appendix. Additionally, some of the notation and terminology could be clarified, and the numbering of the theorems could be improved.

Overall, this paper provides a significant contribution to the understanding of GAN training and proposes a practical solution to mitigate the instability issues. The authors' approach is well-motivated, and the paper is well-placed in the literature.

* The authors propose a smoothing-based alternative, which involves adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This approach is shown to lead to smoother gradients and more stable updates.
* The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the proposed approach, including bounds on the Wasserstein distance between the generator's distribution and the data distribution.

Additional feedback:

* Consider shortening Section 2.1 and moving proofs to the appendix.
* Clarify some of the notation and terminology, such as the definition of "closed manifolds" in Theorem 2.2.
* Improve the numbering of the theorems to avoid confusion.
* Consider noting that Lemma 1 holds in greater generality.
* Specify the domains of the random variables used in the paper.
* Fix typos, such as "relly" → "rely".

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more intuition on why the smoothing-based approach leads to smoother gradients and more stable updates?
* How does the proposed approach compare to other methods for stabilizing GAN training, such as batch normalization or spectral normalization?
* Can you provide more details on the experimental setup and the results of the experiments?
* How does the choice of noise distribution affect the performance of the proposed approach?
