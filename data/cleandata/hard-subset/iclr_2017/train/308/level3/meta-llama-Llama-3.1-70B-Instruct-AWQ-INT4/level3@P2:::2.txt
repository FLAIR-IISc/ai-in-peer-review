**Summary of the Paper**

This paper makes significant contributions to the understanding of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) by providing a theoretical analysis of their training dynamics. The authors identify the sources of instability in GAN training, including the vanishing gradients in the original GAN formulation and the unstable updates in variants. They introduce a perturbation trick to mitigate these issues, which involves adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis to support their proposed solution and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the identification of the sources of instability in GAN training. The authors show that the optimal discriminator in GANs is perfect and has a zero gradient almost everywhere, leading to vanishing gradients in the original GAN formulation. They also demonstrate that variants of GANs, which use a different gradient step for the generator, suffer from unstable updates.

The authors' proposed solution, the perturbation trick, involves adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This trick helps to avoid gradient vanishing and instability by introducing a notion of distance between the points in the manifolds. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis to support their proposed solution, including a proof that the perturbation trick leads to smooth gradients.

The paper also includes experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the perturbation trick. The authors show that the perturbation trick leads to more stable training and better sample quality compared to the original GAN formulation and its variants.
