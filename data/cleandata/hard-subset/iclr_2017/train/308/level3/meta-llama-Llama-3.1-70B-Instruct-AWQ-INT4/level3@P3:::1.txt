This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors tackle the problem of analyzing the theoretical convergence of GANs and discuss the stability issues that arise during training. The paper is well-motivated, and the approach is well-placed in the literature, as it addresses a crucial gap in the theoretical understanding of GANs.

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this decision are the paper's strong theoretical contribution, its focus on an important and timely problem, and the novelty of the results. The paper provides deep theoretical insights into the behavior of GANs, which is a significant departure from the mostly empirical work in the field.

The paper's main contributions are the analysis of the sources of instability in GANs, the proof of the perfect discrimination theorems, and the introduction of a new direction for solving these problems. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical framework for understanding the behavior of GANs, which is a significant step forward in the field.

The paper supports its claims through a combination of theoretical proofs and empirical experiments. The results are correct and scientifically rigorous, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their methods and findings.

One minor technical question I have is regarding the proof of Theorem 2.5. The authors assume that the optimal discriminator is fixed for a value θ0, but it is not clear how this assumption affects the generality of the result.

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors provide more intuition and explanation for the mathematical proofs, particularly for readers who may not be familiar with the technical details. Additionally, the authors could provide more discussion on the implications of their results for the practice of training GANs.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer to clarify my understanding of the paper are:

* Can the authors provide more details on how the perfect discrimination theorems relate to the practice of training GANs? How do these theorems affect the choice of architecture and hyperparameters?
* How do the authors' results relate to other work on GANs, such as the use of different loss functions or regularization techniques?
* Can the authors provide more discussion on the implications of their results for the evaluation of generative models? How can their results be used to improve the evaluation of GANs and other generative models?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GANs. With some minor revisions to address the technical question and provide more intuition and explanation, this paper has the potential to be a seminal work in the field.
