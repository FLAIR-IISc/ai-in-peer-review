This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the issues that arise during GAN training, including vanishing gradients and instability. They also propose a novel solution, a perturbation trick, to mitigate these problems.

The paper's main contribution is in providing a clear explanation of why gradients vanish in the original GAN formulation and become unstable in variants. The authors show that the optimal discriminator is a perfect discriminator, which leads to vanishing gradients when the discriminator is very confident. They also demonstrate that the alternative gradient step used in variants of GANs leads to unstable updates.

The perturbation trick proposed by the authors is an interesting solution to these problems. By adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator, they are able to break the assumptions that lead to vanishing gradients and instability. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the perturbation trick, showing that it leads to smooth gradients and avoids the problems of vanishing gradients and instability.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a clear and concise explanation of the theoretical concepts. The authors do an excellent job of breaking down complex ideas into easily understandable components, making the paper accessible to a wide range of readers.

The reviewer is particularly interested in the perturbation trick and its similarity to dropout/Bernoulli perturbations. The authors should discuss the connection between their perturbation trick and these other techniques, highlighting the similarities and differences.

To further strengthen the paper, the authors should provide empirical evaluations of the perturbation trick. Adding experiments that compare the performance of perturbed GANs to standard GANs would provide valuable insight into the effectiveness of the proposed solution. Figures similar to Fig. 2 and 3, which demonstrate the instability of GANs, would be particularly useful in illustrating the benefits of the perturbation trick.

Overall, this paper provides a valuable contribution to the understanding of GAN training dynamics. The authors' theoretical analysis and proposed solution have the potential to significantly impact the field of deep learning.
