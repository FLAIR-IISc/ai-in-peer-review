This paper tackles the fundamental problem of understanding the notoriously unstable training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors move beyond heuristic approaches and provide a theoretical analysis identifying the root causes of instability and saturation during GAN training. Crucially, they don’t just diagnose the problems; they propose a principled direction for solving them by introducing a novel technique involving adding noise to the inputs, which they theoretically ground and demonstrate empirically. The paper is well-structured, progressing logically from problem definition to theoretical analysis and finally to a proposed solution and future directions.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training, the empirical validation feels insufficient to confidently support the claims and justify acceptance at a competitive conference like ICLR. The proposed solution, while theoretically promising, lacks robust empirical demonstration demonstrating its superiority over existing techniques in a comprehensive manner. 

The core contribution lies in the insightful analysis of why gradients vanish or become unstable during GAN training, particularly highlighting the issues arising from perfect discrimination when the generator and discriminator distributions have low-dimensional supports. The rigorous proofs of Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 are particularly impressive, formally demonstrating the limitations of standard GAN training. The subsequent development of a noise-based perturbation technique to mitigate these issues is a logical and promising extension. The theoretical justification for this approach, linking it to Wasserstein distances and divergences, is well-articulated. 

However, the empirical evaluation primarily consists of figures illustrating the theoretical findings (Fig. 1, 2, and 3). While these figures are informative, they don’t fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed noise injection. Fig. 2 shows vanishing gradients, but lacks comparison to implementations *with* the proposed solution. Fig. 3 illustrates unstable gradients but doesn't benchmark against stabilized GAN architectures (e.g., WGAN, improved DCGANs) using the proposed perturbation. The paper mentions the perturbation should help address instability, yet lacks a side-by-side comparison to prove this point. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Connection to Dropout/Bernoulli Perturbations:** The proposed noise injection technique bears a strong resemblance to dropout or more generally, adding Bernoulli perturbations to the inputs. It would be valuable to discuss this connection explicitly. Is the theoretical justification unique to the continuous noise used here, or does it extend to discrete perturbations? How does the choice of noise distribution (Gaussian, uniform, etc.) impact performance?
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors should include more comprehensive experiments comparing GANs trained with and without the proposed noise injection. Specifically, it would be helpful to evaluate the impact of the perturbation on:
    *   Sample quality (e.g., using Inception Score, FID)
    *   Training stability (e.g., tracking generator and discriminator loss curves)
    *   Convergence speed
    *   Performance on established benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA)
    *   Compare performance against well-known GAN stabilization techniques (e.g., WGAN, spectral normalization).
*   **Noise Schedule:** The paper does not discuss how the variance of the noise should be scheduled during training. Is it fixed, annealed, or dynamically adjusted? Providing guidance on choosing an appropriate noise schedule would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** The addition of noise may slightly increase the computational cost of training. A brief discussion of the computational overhead would be helpful.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the connection between the proposed noise injection technique and existing regularization methods like dropout or Bernoulli perturbations? Are there theoretical differences in their impact on GAN training?
2.  Could you provide empirical results (e.g., similar to Fig. 2 and 3) comparing GANs trained with and without the proposed noise injection technique, specifically demonstrating its impact on training stability and sample quality on benchmark datasets?
3.  What guidance can you offer on selecting an appropriate noise schedule (i.e., how should the variance of the noise be adjusted during training)?
4.   Do you expect the proposed approach to be as effective when using discrete perturbations instead of Gaussian noise? If not, can you give an intuition of why?
