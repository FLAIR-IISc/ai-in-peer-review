## Review of "Towards Theoretical Understanding of Generative Adversarial Networks"

This paper presents a strong theoretical analysis of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), tackling the crucial problem of their training instability. The authors don't introduce a new algorithm but rather offer deep theoretical insight into the dynamics of GAN training, a welcome contribution given the largely heuristic nature of progress in this field. This work is among the first to rigorously analyze GAN convergence and stability, and provides a valuable foundation for future research.

**Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the analysis appears sound. The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in the field of GANs – understanding *why* they are so difficult to train – and provides a compelling argument for the underlying mathematical reasons.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper convincingly argues that instability in GAN training stems from the properties of the underlying distributions and the resulting perfect discriminators. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 elegantly demonstrate that when the generator and real data distributions have disjoint supports or lie on low-dimensional manifolds, a perfect discriminator can be constructed, leading to vanishing gradients or unstable updates. The subsequent theorems (2.4-2.6) build upon this foundation, detailing the consequences for different cost functions (original GAN loss vs. the -logD alternative). The analysis is mathematically rigorous and well-presented. 

The connection made between the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD), the original and alternative GAN cost functions, and the resulting mode dropping behavior (Theorem 2.5) is particularly insightful. Demonstrating that the generator updates are effectively minimizing a problematic divergence (KL(Pg||Pr)) explains the observed tendency of GANs to prioritize generating realistic *looking* samples at the expense of covering the full data distribution. 

The latter portion of the paper, proposing a softened metric based on Wasserstein distance and noise injection, offers a concrete direction for addressing the identified issues. The theoretical justification for this approach, along with Theorems 3.2 and 3.3, demonstrates a thoughtful attempt to move beyond the limitations of traditional divergences. The introduction of noise to smooth the distributions and the analysis of the resulting gradient behavior are particularly commendable.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could further enhance its clarity and impact.

* **Technical Details in Proofs:** While the proofs are generally sound, some steps (particularly in Theorem 2.6 regarding the Cauchy distribution of gradients) could benefit from more detailed explanation. A more explicit derivation or a reference to a standard result would increase confidence in the claims.
* **Experimental Validation:** The paper mentions targeted experiments to verify assumptions, but the results are only referenced briefly. Including more detailed experimental results, even as supplementary material, would strengthen the empirical grounding of the theoretical findings. Demonstrating the improved stability with the proposed noise injection strategy would be particularly compelling.
* **Relationship to Recent Work:** The field of GAN theory is rapidly evolving. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss how this work relates to more recent theoretical analyses of GANs, such as those focusing on spectral normalization or gradient penalties.
* **Clarify the Role of Assumptions:** The paper relies on assumptions about the smoothness and dimensionality of the distributions. Discussing the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the proof of Theorem 2.5: Could you elaborate on the justification for claiming that the gradient components become Cauchy distributed? Is there a specific theorem or result on Gaussian processes that supports this claim?
2.  In Theorem 3.3, the bound on the Wasserstein distance relies on the assumption that Pr+ and Pg+ have support contained in a ball of diameter C. How sensitive is this result to the choice of C, and how does it relate to the dimensionality of the original data space?
3.  The paper suggests using noise injection to address stability issues. What type of noise distribution (Gaussian, uniform, etc.) do you recommend, and how does the choice of noise variance affect the performance of the GAN?
4.  Could you provide a more concrete example illustrating how the proposed metric (based on Wasserstein distance) leads to improved sample quality compared to directly minimizing the JSD, even with the limitations of the perfect discriminator problem?
