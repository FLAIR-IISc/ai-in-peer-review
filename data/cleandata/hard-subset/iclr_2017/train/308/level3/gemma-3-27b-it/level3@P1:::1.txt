## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper tackles a crucial and timely problem: understanding the theoretical underpinnings of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). Despite the empirical successes of GANs across diverse applications, their training remains notoriously unstable and lacks a solid theoretical foundation. This work represents a significant step towards addressing this gap, providing rigorous analysis of the issues of instability and saturation that plague GAN training, and proposing a theoretically grounded direction for improvement.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a valuable and novel theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics, addressing a critical open problem in the field. The insights provided, and the proposed direction for future research are substantial and justify acceptance despite minor areas for clarification. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The strength of this submission lies in its commitment to a rigorous, theoretical approach to understanding GANs. Unlike the majority of work in this area, which focuses on heuristic improvements to architectures and training procedures, this paper delves into *why* GAN training is so challenging. The authors identify a fundamental issue: when the discriminator becomes too good, updates to the generator diminish or become unstable.  They demonstrate, through compelling theoretical arguments (Theorems 2.1 & 2.2), that perfect discrimination is often achievable due to the low-dimensional nature of the generator’s output manifold and disjoint supports of the real and generated distributions. 

The subsequent analysis (Theorems 2.4-2.6) rigorously connects these theoretical observations to practical problems like vanishing gradients and unstable updates. Specifically, the demonstration that the original GAN cost function leads to vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) and that the alternative cost function suffers from instability (Theorem 2.6) provides a clear explanation for the challenges faced by practitioners.  The mathematical tools employed – leveraging concepts from measure theory and differential geometry – are impressive and contribute to the novelty of the work. 

The final section offers a promising approach for mitigating these issues by introducing a novel metric and proposing the use of noisy samples. This provides a clear direction for future research and illustrates the potential of the theoretical insights developed in the earlier parts of the paper.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few points could be addressed to further enhance its clarity and impact.

*   **Theorem 2.5 Clarification:** The proof of Theorem 2.5 is somewhat condensed. Providing a slightly more detailed expansion of the steps involved, particularly the connection between the gradient and the KL and JSD divergences, would be beneficial.
*   **Intuition for Theorem 3.3:** While the theorem is mathematically sound, the practical implications of the relationship between the Wasserstein metric and the JSD of the noisy distributions could be further elucidated. A more intuitive explanation of how this connection can be leveraged in practice would be valuable.
*   **Experimental Validation:** While the authors acknowledge the focus on theoretical contributions, including some minimal experimental validation of the proposed noise-based approach (even on a small scale) would strengthen the paper and demonstrate the practical relevance of the findings.
* **Notational Consistency:** There is some inconsistent use of notation, particularly regarding the definition of absolute continuity. Consistently defining terms early on would improve readability. 

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding Theorem 2.5, could you elaborate on the assumption that the noise in the discriminator is a centered Gaussian process? How sensitive are the results to deviations from this assumption?
2.  In Section 3, you propose using noise to regularize the training process. How do you suggest selecting the appropriate level of noise (i.e., the variance of the noise distribution)?  Is there a principled way to tune this parameter?
3.  The paper focuses on the theoretical aspects of GAN training. Do you foresee any challenges in applying these theoretical insights to more complex GAN architectures, such as those used for image generation with high resolutions?
4. Could you provide more details on the experimental results shown in Figure 2? What specific settings and architectures were used to generate this figure?
