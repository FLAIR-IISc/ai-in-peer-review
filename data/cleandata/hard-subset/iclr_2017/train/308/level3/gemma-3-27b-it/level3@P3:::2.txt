## Review of “Understanding the Training Dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks”

**1. Summary:**

This paper tackles a fundamental problem in the field of GANs: their notorious training instability. The authors move beyond heuristic stabilization techniques and provide a theoretical analysis of the underlying causes of these issues, specifically gradient vanishing and instability. They demonstrate, through rigorous proofs, that these problems arise when the discriminator becomes “too good” – achieving perfect discrimination due to the generator’s distribution lying on a lower-dimensional manifold. Crucially, the paper doesn’t just diagnose the problem; it proposes a perturbation trick (adding noise to the inputs) to mitigate these issues, supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental evidence. The work represents a significant step towards a more principled understanding of GAN training, offering a foundation for future research and development.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics and offers a promising solution to address the prevalent instability issues. The clarity of the analysis and the theoretical grounding of the proposed perturbation trick are particularly strong.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully addresses a core challenge in GAN research. The theoretical explanation of why gradients vanish or become unstable as the discriminator improves is insightful and well-supported by the mathematical derivations. The link to the dimensionality of the generator’s output space is a key contribution. The introduction of the perturbation trick, and the demonstration of its ability to circumvent these issues, is a significant practical advancement. 

The experimental results, while not extensive, provide initial validation of the theoretical claims. Figures 2 and 3 effectively illustrate the gradient behaviour with and without the proposed method. The authors convincingly demonstrate the adverse effects of near-perfect discrimination in standard GAN training. 

The authors clearly articulate the problem and its importance, positioning their work well within the broader literature on GANs and generative modeling. The connection to divergences like KL and JSD is well-explained, clarifying why simply minimizing these metrics doesn't solve the training instability problem.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, some areas could be enhanced. 

* **Connection to Dropout/Bernoulli Perturbation:** The reviewer specifically asked about the relationship between the proposed perturbation trick and dropout or Bernoulli perturbations. This connection is not explicitly discussed, and should be addressed. The perturbation trick shares similarities with dropout in that it introduces noise during training, preventing the discriminator from becoming overly confident. Discussing the differences (e.g., applying noise to the discriminator *input* vs. internal activations) and potential benefits of the proposed method compared to dropout would strengthen the paper.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** Expanding the empirical evaluation would be highly beneficial. Specifically, experiments comparing GANs trained *with* and *without* the proposed perturbation, similar to Figs. 2 and 3 but using common GAN architectures (e.g., DCGAN) on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA), are needed. Quantifying the improvements in sample quality (e.g., using Inception Score or FID) and training stability (e.g., monitoring generator loss) would provide more compelling evidence. Investigating the effect of varying the noise level (sigma in the presented formalism) would also be valuable.
* **Wasserstein Distance Discussion:** The theoretical analysis culminating in Theorem 3.3 is intriguing. However, it's unclear how the proposed perturbation trick directly impacts the Wasserstein distance in practice. A more explicit discussion of this connection would be helpful.
* **Noise Type Justification:** The choice of Gaussian noise is justified, but exploring other noise distributions (e.g., uniform, Laplacian) and their potential effects could be considered.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the connection between your proposed perturbation trick and existing regularization techniques like dropout, specifically highlighting the key differences and advantages of your approach?
2.  What is the computational overhead associated with introducing the perturbation? Does it significantly impact training time, and if so, are there strategies to mitigate this?
3.  Regarding Theorem 3.3, how does the choice of the noise variance (σ²) impact the empirical performance of the GAN, and what guidelines can you provide for selecting an appropriate value?
4.  Have you explored the use of different noise distributions beyond Gaussian noise, and if so, what were the observed effects on training stability and sample quality?
