This paper tackles a fundamental and important problem: understanding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). Despite their empirical success, GANs are notoriously difficult to train, and a solid theoretical foundation for their behavior has been largely absent. This paper makes a significant step towards filling that gap by rigorously analyzing the sources of instability and saturation that plague GAN training, and proposing a theoretically grounded direction for improvement.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. Its strength lies in providing deep theoretical insight into a highly practical and rapidly evolving area of machine learning. Unlike many submissions focusing on incremental empirical improvements, this work offers a much-needed analytical lens for understanding *why* GANs behave as they do.

The paper is well-motivated, positioned within the current literature that largely consists of heuristics for GAN stabilization. The authors correctly identify the lack of theoretical understanding as a major impediment to progress and aim to address this directly. The connection to traditional generative modeling approaches (maximum likelihood, KL divergence) and the discussion of the Jensen-Shannon divergence provide useful context. The introduction clearly articulates the research questions the paper seeks to answer: why do updates worsen as the discriminator improves, why is GAN training unstable, and how can these issues be mitigated?

The paper’s claims are strongly supported. The theorems presented – particularly 2.1, 2.2, 2.4, 2.5, and 2.6 – provide a rigorous theoretical basis for understanding the observed instability. The proofs appear sound (though, as noted below, I have a question about the details of Theorem 2.5). The connection between the perfect discriminator scenarios, vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4), and unstable updates (Theorem 2.6) is convincingly demonstrated. The introduction of a noise-based approach (Section 3) and the associated theorems (3.1, 3.2, 3.3) offer a promising avenue for addressing these problems, supported by theoretical analysis linking the Wasserstein distance and JSD of noisy distributions. The careful consideration of the impact of the discriminator’s approximation quality and the discussion of adversarial examples demonstrate a thorough understanding of the nuances involved.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is generally well-written, though the density of theoretical content makes it challenging at times. Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the formal derivations would benefit a broader audience.

*   **Theorem 2.5:** The proof appears to rely on the assumption that the noise processes *r* and *η* are independent. While this is stated, I would appreciate a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the result to deviations from this assumption. In practice, the discriminator and generator are trained iteratively, potentially introducing correlations between the noise terms. Is the instability result robust to small correlations?
*   **Computational Cost:** While the theoretical framework is compelling, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the computational implications of the proposed noise-based approach. Does introducing noise significantly increase the computational burden of training?
*   **Experimental Validation:** The paper emphasizes theoretical analysis, which is a strength. However, some limited empirical validation of the proposed approach (e.g., comparing training curves or sample quality with and without noise) would further strengthen the claims. Even a small-scale experiment could be informative.
*   **Wasserstein Distance and Implementation:** Theorem 3.3 is excellent. A practical consideration, however, is the computational cost of calculating the Wasserstein distance directly. Could the authors briefly discuss potential approximations or efficient methods for estimating this distance in the context of GAN training?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the proof of Theorem 2.5, how sensitive is the result to correlations between the noise processes *r* and *η* that might arise during iterative training?
2.  What is the expected computational overhead of implementing the proposed noise-based approach in practice?
3.  Are there any plans for empirical validation of the theoretical results, even with a small-scale experiment?
4.  In the context of Theorem 3.3, are there efficient or approximate methods for calculating or estimating the Wasserstein distance that could be used in practice?
