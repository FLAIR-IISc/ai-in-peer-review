- Paper provides a theoretical analysis of GAN training instability, attributing gradient vanishing/instability to a too-powerful discriminator when the generator lies on a lower-dimensional manifold.  
- Authors propose a perturbation trick (adding input noise) with theoretical support and initial experiments to mitigate these issues.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions due to strong theory and promising practical solution.  
- Theoretical derivations convincingly explain why gradients vanish/unstable as discriminator improves, linking problems to output-space dimensionality.  
- Perturbation trick is a key practical contribution, shown theoretically and empirically to alleviate discriminator overconfidence.  
- Experimental results are limited but Figures 2–3 effectively illustrate gradient behavior with and without perturbation.  
- Paper situates well within GAN literature and clarifies why minimizing divergences (KL, JSD) alone doesn't resolve instability.  
- Reviewer requests explicit discussion of relation to dropout/Bernoulli perturbations and differences (input noise vs. internal activation noise).  
- Empirical evaluation should be expanded: comparisons on standard architectures/datasets (e.g., DCGAN on CIFAR-10, CelebA), metrics (Inception Score, FID), and stability measures.  
- Recommend experiments varying noise level (σ) to quantify its effect on training and sample quality.  
- Clarify how the perturbation trick affects Wasserstein distance in practice, especially relative to Theorem 3.3.  
- Justify choice of Gaussian noise and consider testing other noise distributions (uniform, Laplacian).  
- Questions for authors: clarify connection to dropout, report computational overhead of perturbation, give guidance on selecting σ², and share results for non-Gaussian noise.
