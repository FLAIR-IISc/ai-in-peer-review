This paper tackles a fundamental problem in the field of generative modeling: the instability of training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors don’t propose a novel GAN architecture, but instead provide a crucial theoretical understanding of *why* GAN training is so difficult, identifying the issues of vanishing gradients and instability stemming from the interplay between the discriminator’s accuracy and the generator’s update rule. They then propose a theoretically grounded solution – adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator – to mitigate these problems and pave the way for more stable training.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling theoretical analysis and offers a promising direction for improving GAN training. The identified issues – vanishing gradients and unstable updates – are well-known in practice, and the theoretical explanations provided here are valuable. The proposed solution is conceptually sound and appears to have a strong theoretical basis.

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous theoretical treatment of GAN training dynamics. The authors clearly articulate the problems with both the original GAN cost function and the subsequent modifications, demonstrating why updates become problematic as the discriminator improves. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2, establishing the existence of “perfect discriminators” under certain conditions, are particularly insightful. The analysis convincingly explains why traditional approaches often fail. The introduction of a “softer” metric based on adding noise is a compelling solution, supported by the theorems demonstrating improved gradient behavior. The connections drawn to related concepts like contrastive divergence and Wasserstein distance provide further context and strengthen the argument.

However, while the theoretical analysis is thorough, the empirical validation is somewhat limited. Figures 1, 2, and 3 provide some illustrative evidence, but more comprehensive experiments would significantly bolster the claims. Specifically, the paper would benefit from a more direct comparison of GANs trained *with* and *without* the proposed noise perturbation, demonstrating the resulting improvements in sample quality and training stability (e.g., measured by Inception Score or FID). A direct comparison to other stabilization techniques (e.g., Wasserstein GAN, Spectral Normalization) would also be valuable. 

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Connection to Dropout/Bernoulli Perturbation:** The proposed noise addition is reminiscent of dropout or more generally, Bernoulli perturbation, which is a common regularization technique in deep learning. It would be beneficial to discuss the connection to these existing methods. Are there differences in the theoretical underpinnings or practical implications? Is the noise used here qualitatively different, and if so, why?
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The addition of more extensive experimental results is crucial. Replicating Figures 2 and 3 with the proposed noise addition, and comparing them to standard GAN training, would provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach. Exploring different noise distributions (Gaussian with varying variances, for example) and their impact on performance would also be insightful.
*   **Clarify the Practical Implementation:** The paper focuses on the theoretical aspects. Providing more details on the practical considerations of implementing the proposed method – for example, how to choose the variance of the noise, or how to dynamically adjust it during training – would make the work more immediately applicable to practitioners.
*  **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Does adding the noise step introduce a significant computational overhead? Addressing this concern would strengthen the practicality of the proposed approach.



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  How does the proposed noise perturbation relate to other regularization techniques like dropout, and what are the key distinctions in the theoretical justification for each?
2.  Could you provide more details on the experimental setup used to generate Figures 2 and 3? Specifically, what GAN architecture was used, and what hyperparameters were employed?
3.  What guidance can you provide regarding the selection of the noise variance (σ2) in practice? Does the optimal value depend on the dataset or network architecture?
4.  Is there a specific choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian, uniform) that you recommend, and what are the tradeoffs associated with different distributions?
