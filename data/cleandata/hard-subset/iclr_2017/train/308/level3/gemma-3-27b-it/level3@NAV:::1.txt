## Review of “Theoretical Analysis of Generative Adversarial Networks”

This paper presents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), a field largely dominated by empirical progress. The authors tackle the fundamental question of why GAN training is so unstable, providing rigorous analysis of the issues of instability and saturation. They go beyond simply identifying these problems, offering new theoretical tools and insights into potential solutions. This work is particularly valuable as one of the first to approach GANs with a strong theoretical framework, filling a crucial gap in the literature.

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The theoretical depth and novelty of the analysis are exceptionally strong, and the insights provided are likely to have a lasting impact on the field. Specifically, Theorems 2.4–2.6 are innovative, and the approach of analyzing the gradient behavior through the lens of perfect discrimination is a compelling one.

The paper successfully establishes a theoretical foundation for understanding GAN instability. The authors convincingly demonstrate, through rigorous proofs, that the vanishing gradient and unstable updates observed in practice stem from the properties of the cost functions used in GAN training, particularly when the generator and discriminator distributions have low-dimensional supports. The introduction of novel theoretical tools for analyzing these issues, and the exploration of softer metrics like the Wasserstein distance, are excellent contributions. The connection drawn between the noise added to the discriminator and the stabilization of training is particularly insightful and suggests a promising avenue for future research. The progression of thought from identifying the problem (instability), to analyzing its causes (perfect discrimination, cost function properties), to proposing a potential remedy (noise injection) is well-structured and compelling. 

While the paper is strong, a few points could be further clarified. In Theorem 2.5, the claim of a Cauchy distribution for the generator gradient seems strong. Could the authors elaborate on the conditions under which this holds and potentially provide an intuition as to why a Cauchy distribution arises specifically? Additionally, the connection between the theoretical findings and practical implementation details (e.g., optimal noise levels) could be further fleshed out. Finally, while the authors mention the potential for adversarial examples, a deeper discussion of how their approach mitigates these vulnerabilities would be beneficial.
