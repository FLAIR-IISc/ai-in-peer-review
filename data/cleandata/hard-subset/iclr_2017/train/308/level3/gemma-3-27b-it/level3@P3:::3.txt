## Review of “Towards Understanding and Improving Generative Adversarial Networks”

This paper tackles a crucial and notoriously difficult problem: understanding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). It moves beyond heuristic approaches, offering a strong theoretical analysis of instability sources and proposing a smoothing-based solution. The work is ambitious in scope, leveraging concepts from differential topology to provide insightful answers to key questions regarding GAN training. Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution with the potential to guide future research in this area.

**Decision: Accept with Minor Revisions**

The paper’s theoretical depth and clear articulation of the root causes of GAN training instability are compelling. The proposed smoothing technique appears promising and grounded in the theoretical analysis. While the paper is dense and requires careful reading, the potential impact outweighs the challenges in accessibility.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully identifies asymmetric divergences as a core issue in GAN training. The detailed explanation of how these divergences lead to mode dropping and the generation of implausible samples is particularly insightful. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 rigorously demonstrate the existence of “perfect discriminators” under common scenarios, and the subsequent analysis of how these impact generator updates is both novel and convincing. 

The proposed smoothing approach (Section 3) offers a practical direction for addressing the identified instabilities. Framing the problem in terms of Wasserstein distance and leveraging noisy distributions to avoid vanishing gradients or unstable updates is a clever and theoretically motivated solution. The connections drawn to contrastive divergence further strengthen the argument. The derivation of the gradient update in Theorem 3.2 and its relation to minimizing JSD between smoothed distributions is a significant achievement. 

The authors' commitment to rigorous analysis is commendable. The detailed proofs, while lengthy, demonstrate a strong grasp of the underlying mathematical principles. The inclusion of experimental verification to substantiate the theoretical claims adds to the paper's credibility.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Section 2.1:** This section could be streamlined. The detailed exposition of the perfect discrimination theorems, while mathematically sound, feels somewhat lengthy for the overall flow of the paper. Consider focusing on the key implications rather than the complete proofs, moving the latter to the appendix.
* **KL Divergence Clarification:** Clarify that if *P<sub>g</sub>* is smaller than the data distribution *P<sub>r</sub>* at some *x*, the KL divergence remains non-zero. This point could be highlighted more explicitly in the introductory discussion of KL divergence.
* **Wording Fix:** Correct “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” to “for generating not plausibly looking pictures.”
* **Lemma 1 Generality:** Note that Lemma 1 holds in greater generality than stated.
* **Theorem 2.1 & 2.2:** Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 rely on basic analysis results. Cite relevant references instead of including full proofs.
* **Theorem 2.4 Domain:** In Theorem 2.4, explicitly remind the reader of the definition/domain of *p(z)*.
* **Domain Specification:** Specify the domains of the random variables used throughout the paper to improve clarity.
* **Typo:** Correct “relly” to “rely.”
* **Theorem 2.2 Manifolds:** Clarify whether closed manifolds in Theorem 2.2 are assumed to have a boundary.
* **Corollary 2.1 Reference:** Corollary 2.1 references “assumptions of Theorem 1.3,” but Theorem 1.3 is absent. Correct this reference.
* **Minor Wording:** In Theorem 2.5, change "Therefore" to "Then." In Theorem 2.6, change "Is a..." to "is a."
* **Theorem Numbering:** The numbering of the theorems is confusing. Consider a consistent and clear numbering scheme (e.g., numbering theorems within each section).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The smoothing approach introduces a hyperparameter (noise variance). How sensitive are the results to the choice of this parameter, and are there guidelines for selecting an appropriate value?
2.  In Theorem 3.3, the assumption that Pr+ and Pg+ have support contained within a ball of diameter C seems restrictive. How does the bound change when this assumption is relaxed, and what are the practical implications?
3.  The paper focuses primarily on theoretical analysis. Could the authors elaborate on the computational cost associated with the proposed smoothing technique compared to standard GAN training, and present any initial empirical results demonstrating its effectiveness on more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet)?
