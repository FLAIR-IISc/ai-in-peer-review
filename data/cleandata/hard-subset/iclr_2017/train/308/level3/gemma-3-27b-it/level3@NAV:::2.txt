## Review of "Towards Understanding GAN Training Dynamics"

This paper tackles a fundamental problem in the field of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs): the lack of theoretical understanding behind their notoriously unstable training process. The authors aim to clarify why gradients vanish in original GANs and become unstable in their variants, proposing a perturbation trick to mitigate these issues, supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. This is a valuable contribution, as a solid theoretical foundation is crucial for advancing and reliably applying GANs.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The core insight – that the interplay between discriminator accuracy and generator gradients leads to these instability issues – is compelling and well-articulated. The introduction of the perturbation trick appears promising, offering a principled approach to address the identified problems. The theoretical analysis provides strong support for their claims and sets this work apart from purely empirical investigations.

The strength of this paper lies in its attempt to move beyond heuristics and provide a foundational understanding of GAN training. The identification of perfect discrimination as a root cause of gradient issues, and the subsequent analysis of different cost functions, are particularly insightful. The theoretical framework, building on concepts like manifold dimensionality and optimal transport, is robust and convincingly presented. The connection between the proposed method and existing techniques, such as contrastive divergence, is also well-established.

However, the paper would benefit from further empirical validation. While the authors mention targeted experiments, the review would be strengthened by including more direct comparisons. Specifically, I encourage the authors to add experiments (similar to Figures 2 and 3) illustrating the impact of the proposed perturbation trick on training stability and sample quality. Comparing the performance of GANs *with* and *without* the perturbation, under various settings and architectures, would provide concrete evidence of its effectiveness.  

Additionally, the paper highlights a potential connection to dropout/Bernoulli perturbation. This connection, while intuitively plausible, is not fully explored. Elaborating on the similarities and differences between the proposed perturbation and standard dropout techniques would be highly valuable. Is the proposed approach equivalent to applying dropout to the discriminator’s input, or does it represent a fundamentally different perturbation strategy? 

Finally, the choice of the perturbation distribution (Gaussian noise) seems somewhat arbitrary. A discussion on the influence of different noise distributions on the training dynamics, and a justification for the chosen distribution, would further strengthen the paper.
