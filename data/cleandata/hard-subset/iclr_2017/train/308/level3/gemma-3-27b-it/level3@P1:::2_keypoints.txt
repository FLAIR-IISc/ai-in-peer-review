- Addresses GAN training instability with a theoretical analysis moving beyond heuristics.  
- Proposes input noise injection as a principled solution, theoretically linked to Wasserstein distances/divergences.  
- Paper is well-structured, progressing logically from problem to theory to solution.  
- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient empirical validation for a top-tier conference.  
- Strength: Rigorous proofs (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) characterizing gradient vanishing/instability when generator and discriminator supports are low-dimensional.  
- Strength: Logical proposal of noise-based perturbation to mitigate perfect-discriminator-induced failures.  
- Major weakness: Empirical evaluation is mostly illustrative (figures) and does not convincingly demonstrate practical benefits.  
- Fig.2 shows vanishing gradients but lacks comparison to models using the proposed noise injection.  
- Fig.3 shows unstable gradients but does not benchmark against stabilized GAN variants (e.g., WGAN, improved DCGANs).  
- Missing side-by-side comparisons and ablations to prove the perturbation’s superiority over existing stabilization techniques.  
- Reviewer asks for evaluations on sample quality (Inception Score, FID), training stability (loss curves), convergence speed, and benchmarks (CIFAR-10, CelebA).  
- Suggests direct comparisons to established stabilization methods (WGAN, spectral normalization) and inclusion of quantitative metrics.  
- Requests discussion of connection to dropout/Bernoulli perturbations and whether theoretical results extend to discrete noise or depend on noise distribution.  
- Asks for guidance on noise scheduling (fixed vs. annealed vs. dynamic) during training.  
- Notes lack of discussion on computational overhead introduced by noise injection.  
- Questions for authors: clarify relation to dropout/Bernoulli, provide empirical comparisons with/without noise on benchmarks, recommend a noise schedule, and state whether discrete perturbations are expected to be effective.
