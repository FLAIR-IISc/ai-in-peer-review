## Review of "Towards Understanding and Stabilizing Generative Adversarial Networks"

This paper tackles a crucial problem in the field of generative modeling: the instability of GAN training. It provides a strong theoretical analysis of the issues stemming from asymmetric divergences inherent in the GAN framework, identifies key sources of instability related to the dimensionality of the generator’s output space, and proposes a smoothing-based approach to mitigate these problems. The work is ambitious and represents a significant step towards a more principled understanding of GAN dynamics.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The theoretical contributions are substantial, offering insightful explanations for observed GAN behavior and proposing a practically motivated solution. The use of tools from differential topology is particularly impressive and sets this work apart.

The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous analysis. The identification of the issue regarding generator and discriminator support and dimensionality is well articulated, and the subsequent theorems clearly demonstrate how these factors lead to vanishing gradients or unstable updates. The proposed smoothing technique provides a concrete avenue to address these issues, grounded in a solid theoretical foundation. The inclusion of preliminary experiments further strengthens the claims.

However, the paper’s length and technical depth make it somewhat challenging to follow. Section 2.1, while thorough, could be streamlined for better readability. Moving the detailed proofs to the appendix would significantly improve the flow of the main text. A minor clarification is needed regarding the KL divergence – explicitly stating that if *P<sub>g</sub>* is smaller than the data distribution *P<sub>r</sub>* at some *x*, the KL divergence remains nonzero. A simple phrasing correction is needed: change “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” to “for generating not plausibly looking pictures.”

Several points could further enhance the paper. Lemma 1 appears to hold in greater generality and acknowledging this would broaden its impact. Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 rely on basic analysis results and citing existing literature instead of full proofs would be appropriate. In Theorem 2.4, briefly reminding the reader of the domain of *p(z)* would improve clarity. Specifying the domains of the random variables used throughout the paper would be beneficial. Finally, addressing the noted typos and inconsistencies (e.g., Theorem numbering, wording in 2.5 & 2.6, the erroneous reference to Theorem 1.3 in Corollary 2.1) is crucial.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost of implementing the proposed smoothing technique and how it scales with the dimensionality of the data?
*   The paper focuses on theoretical analyses. Have you explored the impact of different noise distributions (as suggested in Section 3) empirically, and if so, what insights did you gain?
*   How does the proposed smoothing technique interact with other common GAN stabilization techniques like spectral normalization or gradient penalty? Would combining these approaches yield further improvements?
