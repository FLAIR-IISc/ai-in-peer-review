- Paper explains GAN instability and why generator updates degrade as the discriminator improves, proposing smoothing and transportation-metric remedies.
- Shows optimal discriminators are nearly constant on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds, causing vanishing gradients; JSD saturates and KLs diverge (Thm 2.3).
- Theorem 2.4 formalizes vanishing gradients for the original GAN generator objective as discriminator approximation improves.
- Theorem 2.5 shows the −log D update corresponds (locally) to ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)], explaining good visual samples and mode dropping.
- Theorem 2.6 demonstrates noisy discriminator approximations can yield generator updates with Cauchy-distributed coordinates (infinite variance), explaining practical instability.
- Proposes adding noise to inputs and generator outputs to make distributions absolutely continuous, yielding well-behaved gradients (Thms 3.1–3.2, Cor. 3.2).
- Connects smoothing to Wasserstein distance: smoothing error decays with noise variance and W(Pr,Pg) is bounded by a noise term plus the JSD of smoothed distributions (Lemma 4, Thm 3.3).
- Introduces useful mathematical tools (transversality, Sard’s lemma, Urysohn’s smooth lemma, Gaussian perturbations) and supports claims with targeted experiments.
- Decision: Accept — strong, novel theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics and stability.
- Acceptance reason: insightful and rigorous theorems (esp. 2.4–2.6) plus a principled stabilization direction (noise smoothing + Wasserstein bounds).
- Paper specifically analyzes why GANs destabilize, what the −log D update truly optimizes, and how to recover stable, informative gradients.
- Motivation and placement are excellent; the paper sits well within prior GAN divergence and instability literature and appears to be among the first comprehensive theoretical treatments.
- Correctness and rigor: proofs are careful and mostly self-contained; theoretical claims and experiments align with predictions about vanishing gradients and instability.
- Impact: codifies the manifold-mismatch explanation for instability, clarifies the −log D trick’s effect, and provides a foundation for stable objectives and IPMs.
- Additional feedback: reconcile the regime where Pg is singular (low-dim manifold) with Theorem 2.5’s continuous-density assumption, e.g., treat it as local/idealized or justify via smoothing.
- Ask for regularity clarification in Theorem 2.5: specify conditions to interchange ∇θ with expectations and ensure differentiability/integrability of gθ and p(z).
- Theorem 2.6 feedback: independence/white-noise assumptions are idealized; discuss correlated/colored noise and whether heavy-tailed behavior persists, plus provide empirical variance diagnostics.
- Theorem 3.3 feedback: clarify bounded-support assumption under Gaussian smoothing, how diameter C is chosen (e.g., truncation/high-probability), and provide a sub-Gaussian-tail version.
- Practical guidance request: include empirical studies on noise schedules, covariance choices, annealing strategies, and their effects on mode coverage and FID-like metrics.
- Suggest relating the analysis to Wasserstein GAN and other IPM-based methods to highlight similarities and differences in stability mechanisms.
- Question: state minimal conditions for Theorem 2.5 to justify exchanging ∇θ and expectation and applicability when Pg is singular without smoothing.
- Question: robustness of Theorem 2.6 to discriminator errors with nonzero mean or correlated noise — do heavy-tailed updates persist, and what diagnostics detect this regime?
- Question: recommended smoothing/annealing schedules to reduce the first term in Thm 3.3 without reintroducing vanishing gradients, and evidence about annealing speed effects.
- Question: how anisotropic or heavy-tailed noise choices affect gradients in Thm 3.2 and the practical stability/coverage trade-off.
- Question: whether computing JSD between smoothed distributions (via a trained discriminator) during training correlates with sample quality and mode coverage.
- Question: for modern generators with skip connections or noise injection, does Pg remain concentrated on low-dimensional manifolds and how does Lemma 1 extend to common architectures?
