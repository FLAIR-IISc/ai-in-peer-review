1. Summary of contributions
The paper offers a principled theoretical account of why GAN training is difficult and how to mitigate it. The authors formalize the “manifold mismatch” between Pr and Pg and prove that, under mild assumptions, optimal discriminators are perfect and locally flat almost everywhere on the supports, which explains vanishing generator gradients for the original cost and instability for the −log D update. They show that when supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds that do not perfectly align, JSD is maximized and both KL divergences are infinite, even if manifolds are arbitrarily close—undermining the use of these divergences in typical GAN settings. They derive that the −log D update steps correspond to minimizing KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD, clarifying mode dropping and instability. A key positive contribution is a simple, theoretically grounded fix: inject continuous noise to smooth both Pr and Pg, making them absolutely continuous. This yields stable, meaningful gradients (proportional to JSD between the smoothed distributions) that attract generated samples toward the data manifold and repel overrepresented model regions. They also prove an upper bound connecting the Wasserstein distance between the original (unsmoothed) distributions to the JSD of the smoothed ones plus a term controlled by the noise variance, thereby justifying a noise-annealing schedule as training progresses. Targeted experiments validate the theory (vanishing gradients, exploding/unstable updates with −log D, and stabilization with noise).

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, original, and rigorous theoretical analysis that explains widely observed GAN pathologies and unifies several empirical heuristics.
- A simple, practical remedy (noise injection with annealing), anchored in clean proofs and with demonstrable stabilizing effect.

3. Supporting arguments
- Theoretical clarity: The “perfect discrimination” results (Theorems 2.1/2.2) and the manifold-based perspective explain why training deteriorates as D improves. The derivation linking −log D to KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD gives a satisfying explanation for plausible-looking but mode-collapsed samples.
- Stability analysis: The Cauchy-gradient result under discriminator noise (Theorem 2.6) precisely formalizes the empirically known instability of −log D and highlights sensitivity to estimation noise.
- Constructive path forward: Smoothing both sides and training D to optimality becomes safe and beneficial; gradients become attractive to the data manifold while balancing repulsion from overrepresented model regions. The Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) provides a meaningful connection to intrinsic distances between the unsmoothed distributions and motivates annealing.
- Placement in literature: The work situates GAN training relative to f-divergences, JSD, and Wasserstein metrics and leverages standard tools from analysis and differential topology to give precise statements. Section 3 is intuitive and actionable.

Caveats (not decision-critical): Empirical validation is targeted rather than extensive, and some theoretical sections are long and could be streamlined. A more thorough empirical exploration of the proposed noise strategy (schedules, datasets, comparisons) would enhance the paper’s practical impact.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Structure and presentation
  - Consider shortening Section 2.1 and moving proof details to the appendix; cite standard results (e.g., versions of Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, transversality/genericity) where appropriate. Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 are standard and can be referenced concisely.
  - Unify theorem/lemma numbering; fix the cross-reference error in Corollary 2.1 (“assumptions of Theorem 1.3”).
  - Clarify whether “closed manifolds” in Theorem 2.2 may have boundary; state boundary assumptions explicitly where used.
  - Remind the reader of the definition and domain of p(z) in Theorem 2.4; specify the spaces for X, Z, and the distributions, and the compactness assumptions when invoked.
- Wording and typos
  - Fix: “relly” → “rely.”
  - Theorem 2.5: “Therefore” → “Then.” Theorem 2.6: “Is a” → “is a.”
  - Clarify phrasing: change “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” to “for generating not plausibly looking pictures.”
  - Clarify that when Pg(x) < Pr(x), the relevant KL term remains nonzero; the discussion should carefully distinguish the direction of KL and its asymmetry.
- Mathematical scope
  - Lemma 1 likely holds in greater generality (e.g., for piecewise-linear networks and broader families of smooth, strictly monotone activations); consider stating the most general conditions you can support, and cite related structural results.
  - State the domains of all random variables (e.g., the noise , ′, their distributions, independence) when introduced; in Theorem 2.6, discuss the realism of the independence/white-noise assumptions and what weaker correlations would imply.
- Empirical section
  - Provide more details on noise schedules (initial σ, annealing rate), sensitivity analyses, and comparisons to common stabilization heuristics (feature matching, label smoothing, instance noise).
  - Quantify diversity/mode coverage (e.g., simple mode counting setups) in addition to visual quality to support claims about reduced mode dropping.
  - Discuss relation to Wasserstein-based training: how your smoothing-plus-JSD approach compares theoretically and empirically to directly optimizing Wasserstein (e.g., WGAN-style constraints).

Questions for the authors
- Assumptions and generality:
  - Could you formalize the minimal regularity/architecture conditions under which Lemma 1 holds? Does it extend to residual networks and common modern activations beyond those listed?
  - In Theorem 2.2, do “closed manifolds” allow boundaries? If so, please restate the boundary assumptions explicitly and clarify where boundary terms matter in the proofs.
  - How sensitive are Theorem 2.6’s conclusions to correlated noise between D and ∇D (more realistic for trained discriminators)? Can you provide a version with weak dependence assumptions?
- Practical guidance:
  - How should practitioners choose the noise distribution and annealing schedule? Any adaptive criterion tied to Pr+ vs Pg+ overlap or discriminator calibration?
  - Do you recommend adding noise to both discriminator inputs and generator outputs during backprop at all stages, or only during early training?
  - What is the empirical trade-off between sharpness and stability as σ varies, and how do you avoid over-smoothing?
- Evaluation and comparisons:
  - Can you provide quantitative evidence of improved mode coverage under your method (e.g., synthetic mixtures, CIFAR-10 class-conditional diversity)?
  - How does the proposed approach compare to Wasserstein-based training in practice (sample quality, stability, sensitivity to hyperparameters)?
- Clarifications:
  - Please restate the precise spaces for Z and X, and the distribution p(z), when first introduced in Section 2.4.
  - In Section 3, could you explicitly show the step establishing that the gradient equals 2∇θ JSD(Pr+‖Pg+) under noise to both generator and discriminator inputs, including regularity/exchange-of-derivative conditions?
