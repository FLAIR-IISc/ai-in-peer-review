1) Summary and contributions: This paper offers a clear and rigorous account of why GAN training is hard. It proves that when the data and model supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds that do not perfectly align, the optimal discriminator is almost-everywhere flat on each support, which explains vanishing gradients under the original minimax objective (Theorem 2.4). It further shows that the non-saturating update can become massively unstable due to heavy-tailed gradient noise (Cauchy, Theorem 2.6). To mitigate both issues, the paper proposes perturbing inputs (instance noise) so that the convolved distributions become absolutely continuous, yielding smooth, distance-weighted gradients (Theorem 3.2/Cor. 3.2) and a training objective whose minimization controls the true Wasserstein distance via an explicit bound (Theorem 3.3). The theoretical analysis is careful, insightful, and actionable.

2) Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (i) Strong, well-placed theory that unifies explanations of gradient vanishing and instability; (ii) A principled, simple perturbation trick with clear guarantees and a Wasserstein connection.

3) Support for decision: The problem is important and timely: instability has limited GAN applicability and design. The approach is well motivated by manifold support arguments and related divergences. The results are mathematically sound, with precise assumptions and useful corollaries (e.g., annealing noise). Targeted experiments qualitatively corroborate the theorems (gradients vanish or explode as D improves). While the empirical section is intentionally compact, the theory stands on its own and the mitigation is straightforward to implement.

4) Feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not required for my decision):
- Please expand on the relation between your perturbation trick and dropout/Bernoulli perturbations. Your guarantees rely on absolutely continuous noise to ensure Pr+ and Pg+ have densities. Does multiplicative Bernoulli/dropout preserve or break these guarantees? Could a small Gaussian added alongside dropout recover them?
- Provide ablations: perturb D only vs G only vs both; Gaussian vs Bernoulli vs Laplace; isotropic vs anisotropic covariances; and annealing schedules. Report gradient norm/variance curves (as in Figs. 2–3) and sample quality (e.g., IS/FID) across settings.
- Clarify where to inject noise (raw inputs, intermediate features, or both) and practical ranges for σ and annealing.
- Discuss robustness to finite-precision/approximation errors when the discriminator is near-optimal and noise processes are correlated rather than white.

Questions for authors:
- Can you formalize when Bernoulli/dropout-like perturbations suffice? Is absolute continuity strictly necessary, or can mixtures with continuous components deliver similar bounds?
- How sensitive are results to the latent dimension relative to data dimension?
- Do the JSD-of-noisy distributions and the Wasserstein bound track each other empirically during training as noise is annealed?
