Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the core question of why GAN training is unstable and why gradients either vanish or explode as the discriminator improves. The authors:
- Prove that, under realistic assumptions, GAN data and model distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds with (almost surely) disjoint or non-transversally intersecting supports; this implies the existence of smooth, perfect discriminators whose gradients are zero almost everywhere on the supports and that KL and JSD are maxed out despite arbitrarily close manifolds.
- Establish vanishing gradients for the original generator update as D→D*, and heavy-tailed, high-variance (Cauchy) updates for the -log D variant under a noisy discriminator approximation, explaining empirically observed instability.
- Propose a principled fix: add continuous noise (instance noise) to both real and generated samples (and to generator outputs during backprop) to make the distributions absolutely continuous, yielding smooth, informative gradients and avoiding measure-zero adversarial directions. They show that the resulting generator gradient corresponds to minimizing JSD between the noisy distributions.
- Connect this to a softer metric: they bound the true Wasserstein distance between noiseless Pr and Pg by a term that decays with the noise variance plus the JSD between the noisy distributions. This motivates annealing the noise while minimizing the noisy JSD via a GAN discriminator.
The work is careful, mathematically grounded, and offers actionable guidance for stabilizing GAN training.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
- Key reasons: (1) Strong, clear theoretical analysis that explains central GAN pathologies and unifies them; (2) A simple, principled mitigation (instance noise with annealing) backed by theory linking noisy JSD to the true Wasserstein distance. I would like to see stronger empirical validation of the proposed fix.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: The instability of GANs and sensitivity to objective/discriminator strength has been a major bottleneck. The paper addresses this head-on with precise statements and proofs.
- Placement/motivation: The paper builds on classic GAN theory (Goodfellow et al.), divergences, manifold assumptions, and connects to transport (Villani). The analysis of -log D clarifies widespread practice and links mode dropping to optimizing KL(Pg||Pr) locally minus 2 JSD.
- Rigor/correctness: The manifold lemmas and perfect discrimination theorems are plausible and technically sound given the stated assumptions. The vanishing gradient result for the original loss and the Cauchy-instability result for -log D (under stylized white-noise discriminator errors) convincingly capture observed phenomena (supported by reported plots). The instance-noise program is well motivated: noise renders distributions absolutely continuous; the gradient expressions show attractive behavior (attract to data manifold, repel overconfident model regions); adding noise to generator outputs during backprop addresses measure-zero adversarial directions. The Wasserstein–noisy-JSD bound provides a clean optimization target with an annealing schedule.
- Limitations: The instability theorem assumes independent white noise in D and ∇D; real discriminators have correlated, structure-dependent errors. Experiments verifying the efficacy of the proposed noise strategy are limited; broader benchmarks and ablations would strengthen the case.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Connection to dropout/Bernoulli perturbations:
  - Please discuss whether multiplicative Bernoulli dropout or Bernoulli input corruptions confer the same absolute continuity property as additive continuous noise. My understanding is that your proofs require an absolutely continuous density; Bernoulli/dropout noise is discrete and may leave distributions singular on unions of manifolds, so the theoretical guarantees likely do not carry over. Clarify whether (a) multiplicative Gaussian noise or (b) additive Laplace/Cauchy noise satisfy your assumptions, and whether discrete perturbations empirically help similarly.
  - If Bernoulli perturbations are applied early (as in standard dropout), does the final distribution at the discriminator input become absolutely continuous under typical activations? A brief analysis would be helpful.
- Empirical evaluation requests:
  - Provide experiments comparing baseline GAN vs instance-noise GAN on standard image benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA, LSUN). Include training stability metrics (gradient norms like Figs. 2–3, discriminator accuracy, loss curves), sample quality (Inception Score, FID), and mode coverage metrics where applicable.
  - Ablations: type of noise (Gaussian vs Laplace vs Bernoulli), magnitude and annealing schedule, adding noise to D only vs to both D and generator outputs during backprop, and sensitivity across architectures (DCGAN, ResNet).
  - Show that with noise you can train the discriminator closer to optimality without harming generator updates (contrast with noiseless).
- Relation to concurrent/related work:
  - Situate your approach alongside WGAN (Earth Mover’s distance), gradient penalty variants, f-GAN, MMD-GAN, and “feature matching” stabilization. Your bound ties noisy JSD minimization to Wasserstein distance; please clarify similarities/differences to optimizing EMD directly and whether instance noise synergizes with Lipschitz constraints/gradient penalties.
- Assumptions and generality:
  - Discuss robustness of your vanishing/instability results when D is not optimal and when discriminator error is structured/correlated. Can you relax the white-noise assumption and still obtain heavy tails or high variance?
  - Clarify the “perfect alignment” probability-one statements and how they interact with common normalization layers and skip connections.
- Practical guidance:
  - Provide a concrete recipe (pseudocode) for instance noise training with annealing: initial σ, decay schedule, when to stop annealing, and any dataset-dependent heuristics.
  - Interpret constants in the Wasserstein–noisy-JSD bound (e.g., role of C, how large it is in practice, can it be replaced by a Lipschitz-based constant?).

Questions for the authors
1. Dropout/Bernoulli perturbation: Does multiplicative Bernoulli noise at the discriminator input (or intermediate layers) make the input distribution absolutely continuous in your sense? If not, can you formalize why additive continuous noise is necessary? Would multiplicative Gaussian noise suffice?
2. Discriminator noise model: How do your instability conclusions change if D−D* and ∇D−∇D* are correlated Gaussian processes or bounded but biased? Can you provide a milder condition that still yields high-variance updates for -log D?
3. Annealing schedule: What schedules for σ(t) work best across datasets? How sensitive are results to initial σ0 and decay rate? Can σ be adapted online from gradient norms or JSD estimates?
4. Empirics: Can you add experiments directly comparing GAN with and without instance noise across multiple architectures, along with quantitative metrics and gradient norm plots similar to Figs. 2–3?
5. Metric choice: You note different noises induce different “distance” weightings. Have you tried anisotropic Gaussians or learned covariance to reflect perceptual metrics? Any pitfalls?
6. Bound tightness: Is the bound W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD tight in any regimes? Can C be replaced by a data-dependent Lipschitz diameter to reduce looseness?
7. Interaction with regularizers: How does instance noise interact with label smoothing, batch norm, spectral normalization, or gradient penalties? Any additive or redundant effects?

Overall, this paper offers a compelling, theory-driven account of GAN pathologies and a principled stabilization mechanism. With stronger empirical validation of the proposed perturbation strategy, it would make a solid contribution.
