- Paper addresses why GAN training is difficult by analyzing asymmetric divergences (KL, reverse KL, JSD) when model and data lie on low-dimensional manifolds and proposes smoothing by adding noise to both data and generator.  
- Identifies precise instability sources: perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients for the original generator loss, and heavy-tailed/unstable updates for the −log D loss.  
- Uses analysis and differential topology (transversality) to show supports are measure-zero and rarely align, explaining discriminator-driven instabilities.  
- Derives a bound relating JSD between smoothed distributions and the Wasserstein distance between original distributions and provides targeted experiments.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: strong, insightful theory framing long-standing empirical issues; simple, principled smoothing strategy that explains and mitigates instability with a clear optimization target.  
- Paper well-motivated and well-placed in literature (Goodfellow et al., Theis, Huszár, Villani).  
- Manifold/support and transversality arguments convincingly explain perfect discrimination and why gradients vanish or explode as D improves.  
- Derivation that −log D corresponds to KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD at the operating point explains mode dropping and instability.  
- Smoothing approach is elegant and practical: yields continuous densities, stable gradients, and a provable JSD–noise–Wasserstein trade-off.  
- Theoretical assumptions (e.g., white-noise errors in Theorem 2.6) are idealized but empirical evidence aligns with theory.  
- Suggestion: streamline Section 2.1 and move many proofs to the appendix.  
- Suggestion: cite standard results (Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, transversality) rather than full proofs for Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2.  
- Clarify KL behavior when Pg(x) < Pr(x) on a set of positive Pr-measure (KL(Pr||Pg) stays nonzero or can diverge).  
- Fix wording/typos (examples: “for generating not plausibly looking pictures”; “relly”→“rely”; Theorem 2.5 “Then”; Theorem 2.6 “is a”).  
- Note Lemma 1 likely holds in greater generality (e.g., convolutional/residual/skip architectures); mention this.  
- Theorem 2.1/2.2: specify whether “closed manifolds” means compact without boundary and clarify boundary assumptions.  
- Theorem 2.4: remind definition and domain of p(z) and specify domains for all random variables.  
- Fix cross-references and theorem numbering (e.g., Corollary 2.1 cites missing Theorem 1.3).  
- Theorem 3.3: discuss practicality of estimating constant C and whether alternatives (e.g., second moments) could be used.  
- Questions for authors: what annealing schedules and noise magnitudes work best and how sensitive are results?  
- Questions: how robust are Theorem 2.6 conclusions when discriminator errors are correlated and have finite variance?  
- Questions: can Lemma 1 be explicitly broadened to cover common architectures (conv, residual, skip connections)?  
- Questions: in Theorem 2.2, do “closed manifolds” have boundary — if not, state “compact without boundary”?  
- Questions: can you empirically validate the JSD–Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) by tracking JSD of smoothed models versus an OT proxy?
