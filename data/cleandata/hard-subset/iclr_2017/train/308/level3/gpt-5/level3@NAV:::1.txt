Summary and contributions
This paper offers a rare, deep theoretical treatment of GAN training dynamics, focusing on convergence and stability. It establishes when and why optimal discriminators become perfect and locally flat on (typically low-dimensional) supports, rigorously explaining vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) for the original GAN loss and instability for the non-saturating loss via heavy-tailed updates (Theorem 2.6). It further characterizes the generator update under the −log D heuristic (Theorem 2.5), revealing it tracks ∇θ[KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr)] at θ0, thereby illuminating mode-dropping tendencies. The paper proposes instance noise for both D and G, proves that this yields smooth, meaningful gradients (Corollary 3.2), and connects noisy JSD to the true Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions via an explicit bound (Theorem 3.3). Targeted experiments corroborate the theory. To my knowledge, this is among the first rigorous theoretical analyses of GAN stability.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) Novel, rigorous theorems (2.4–2.6) offering genuine insight into GAN instability; (2) Clear, principled stabilization via instance noise with supporting theory and experiments; (3) Important problem with broad impact.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem: The paper addresses why GAN updates deteriorate as D improves, the roots of instability/vanishing gradients, and how to obtain stable, meaningful training signals.
2) Motivation/literature: The work is well situated (Goodfellow et al., Theis et al.), contrasts KL/JSD behavior on low-dimensional supports, and uses classical tools (transversality, Sard’s lemma, optimal transport) thoughtfully. The stability issues are central given early GAN fragility.
3) Claims/support: Theorems 2.4–2.6 are technically novel and compelling; the manifold/disjoint-support analysis is careful; the noise-based remedy is well justified and linked to a Wasserstein control (Theorem 3.3). Targeted experiments (gradient norms, variance growth) align with predictions.

Feedback and questions (to help improve the paper)
- Theorem 2.5: Please clarify regularity needed to interchange differentiation and integration; the use of D*(x; θ0) while differentiating w.r.t. θ at θ0; and conditions ensuring KL(Pgθ‖Pgθ0) is differentiable with gradient zero at θ0.
- Theorem 2.6: The white-noise and independence assumptions for D and ∇D seem strong. Can you relax them (e.g., correlated Gaussian, bounded-variance noise) and still obtain heavy tails or instability? Any empirical evidence measuring the noise statistics of trained discriminators?
- Instance noise: Provide guidance on choosing σ and annealing schedules; sensitivity analyses across datasets/architectures; connections to adversarial robustness and gradient penalties.
- Norm on D: The Sobolev-type norm used in Theorem 2.4—please discuss approximation rates needed for practical network sizes.
- Empirics: Additional quantitative metrics (e.g., FID-like) under noise annealing would strengthen the case. Further discuss how your bound (Theorem 3.3) could inform evaluation protocols.
