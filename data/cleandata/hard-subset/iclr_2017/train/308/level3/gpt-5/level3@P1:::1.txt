Summary of contributions
- The paper offers a principled, theory-driven account of why GAN training is often unstable. It shows that when real and model distributions live on (near-)disjoint low-dimensional manifolds—a realistic scenario for deep nets—the optimal discriminator is essentially perfect and locally flat, which undermines generator learning.
- It formalizes this through clean manifold arguments: (i) typical generators confine probability mass to a countable union of manifolds of dimension at most dim Z (Lemma 1); (ii) under mild “no perfect alignment” conditions, Pr and Pg intersect in measure-zero sets (Lemmas 2–3), enabling perfect discrimination with zero gradient on supports (Theorems 2.1–2.2); (iii) classical divergences saturate in this regime (Theorem 2.3).
- It analyzes generator gradients for the two canonical GAN losses: the original saturating loss provably yields vanishing gradients as D approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4), while the non-saturating loss (-log D) is shown to optimize a surprising objective (KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD) at θ0 (Theorem 2.5), explaining mode dropping and divergence incentives; moreover, noisy discriminator approximation induces heavy-tailed, unstable generator updates (Cauchy) for -log D (Theorem 2.6).
- It proposes a theoretically grounded remedy: add continuous noise (instance noise) to smooth both data and model distributions so that the discriminator operates on absolutely continuous densities. This yields well-behaved generator gradients that move samples toward the data manifold while repelling overly likely model regions (Theorem 3.2; Corollary 3.2). Finally, it links this practice to a meaningful metric: the Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg is bounded by a term depending on noise variance and the JSD between smoothed distributions (Theorem 3.3), justifying annealing of noise during training.
- Targeted experiments corroborate the theoretical predictions (e.g., vanishing gradients with saturating loss; exploding variance under -log D with a better D).

Decision: Accept
- Primary reasons: (1) Strong, novel theoretical insights into GAN instability with clear, actionable implications (instance noise + annealing). (2) Rigor and originality of results (notably Theorems 2.4–2.6) addressing questions central to the field at this time.

Supporting arguments
- Importance and placement: Stability is the central bottleneck for early GANs. The paper is well-situated relative to likelihood-based divergences, JSD, and empirical observations (Theis et al., Huszár), and it explains, with proofs, phenomena widely observed but poorly understood.
- Novelty and depth: The manifold-support perspective, perfect discrimination with local flatness, and the derivation of the generator update for -log D as KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD at θ0 provide new understanding of mode dropping and instability. The Cauchy-gradient result under noisy D sharply characterizes heavy-tailed updates and aligns with empirical behavior.
- Constructive remedy: Smoothing via instance noise is motivated, analyzed, and connected to an optimization target (JSD between smoothed distributions) and to an intrinsic metric (Wasserstein), with a clear annealing strategy. The analysis of gradient structure under noise is insightful and practically relevant.
- Soundness: The theorems are carefully stated with appropriate measure-theoretic and manifold assumptions. While some assumptions are idealized (e.g., white-noise errors), the conclusions are plausible and supported by experiments designed to test the specific claims.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not decisive)
- Clarify assumptions and edge cases:
  - Theorem 2.5: spell out conditions ensuring interchange of derivative and expectation and handling of log D* near 0/1 (support mismatch). Are smoothing assumptions required for this derivation to be valid?
  - Theorem 2.4: give explicit conditions (and examples) under which M = (E||Jθ gθ(z)||^2)1/2 is finite for typical Gaussian priors and DCGAN-like architectures.
  - Theorems 2.1–2.2: discuss approximability of the smooth optimal discriminator by practical neural nets and implications for finite-capacity/finite-data settings.
- Assumptions in Theorem 2.6: independence and white-noise modeling of errors are strong. It would help to state weaker assumptions (e.g., sub-Gaussian numerator/denominator with limited dependence) that still yield heavy-tailed behavior, or provide empirical evidence probing correlation between D and ∇D errors.
- Practical guidance: provide a concise recipe for instance noise (initial σ, annealing schedule, whether to add noise to both real and generated inputs and to generator outputs, covariance choices), with ablations demonstrating sensitivity.
- Empirics: while the paper is theory-focused, a brief, controlled comparison showing consistent stabilization and mode coverage gains with instance noise across 2–3 datasets/architectures (with fixed capacity and tuning) would further bolster the practical takeaway. Consider reporting quantitative proxies (e.g., inception score diversity or coverage metrics) alongside qualitative samples.
- Bound tightness and constants: in Theorem 3.3, discuss the role of diameter C (unbounded images vs. practical clipping/normalization) and provide intuition or examples of tightness.

Questions for the authors
- Theorem 2.5: Could you formalize the regularity conditions under which Eq. (3) holds? In particular, how do you avoid undefined terms when D*(x) approaches 0 or 1 due to support mismatch, and what precisely justifies differentiating under the integral sign at θ0?
- Theorem 2.6: How sensitive is the Cauchy conclusion to the independence/white-noise assumption? Can you state a more general result (e.g., ratio of correlated Gaussians with bounded correlation) or provide empirical evidence that the gradient coordinate distributions exhibit heavy tails across training?
- Instance noise implementation: Do you recommend adding noise to inputs of D only, or also to generator outputs during backprop (as per Corollary 3.2)? What annealing schedules performed robustly in your experiments, and how do you choose σ relative to data scaling?
- Wasserstein–JSD bound: In typical image settings where supports are effectively bounded after normalization, what ranges of σ and C give informative bounds in practice? Did you attempt to monitor JSD(Pr+||Pg+) during training as a proxy for progress?
- Manifold assumptions: Lemma 1 hinges on common activations. How does the argument adapt with residual/skip connections and non-pointwise operations (e.g., pooling, upsampling)? Are there architectures where the “countable union of manifolds” conclusion could fail?
- Approximability of the smooth optimal D: Given Urysohn’s lemma ensures existence, do you have results or observations on how network depth/width and Lipschitz control affect the ability to approximate such D without inducing adversarial brittleness?

Overall, this is a timely, rigorous, and impactful paper that advances our theoretical understanding of GANs and offers a clear, principled stabilization strategy. I recommend acceptance.
