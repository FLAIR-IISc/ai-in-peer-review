Summary and contributions: This paper tackles a core open problem: why GAN training is difficult and unstable. It analyzes the effect of asymmetric divergences (KL, reverse KL, and JSD) when model and data live on low-dimensional manifolds, identifies precise instability sources (perfect discrimination, vanishing gradients for the original generator loss, and heavy-tailed/unstable updates for the −log D loss), and proposes a smoothing-based alternative by adding noise to both data and generator paths. The work combines tools from analysis and differential topology to show why supports are measure-zero and seldom align, and derives a practical, intuitive fix in Section 3 with a bound relating JSD between smoothed distributions and the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions. Theoretical claims are accompanied by targeted experiments.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (i) strong, insightful theory that frames long-standing empirical issues; (ii) a simple, principled smoothing strategy that both explains and mitigates instability with a clear optimization target.

Supporting arguments: The paper is well motivated and well placed in the literature (Goodfellow et al., Theis et al., Huszár, Villani). The manifold/support arguments and transversality perspective convincingly explain perfect discrimination and why gradients either vanish or explode as D improves. The derivation that −log D corresponds to KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD at the operating point explains mode dropping and instability. The smoothing approach in Section 3 is elegant and practical: it yields continuous densities, stable gradients, and a provable connection to the true distance between Pr and Pg via a noise-variance and JSD trade-off. While some assumptions (e.g., white-noise errors in Theorem 2.6) are idealized, the empirical evidence is targeted and consistent with the theory.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not decisive):
- Streamline and move many proofs to the appendix (shorten Section 2.1). Cite standard results (Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, transversality/general position) for Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 instead of full proofs.
- Clarify that if Pg(x) < Pr(x) on a set of positive Pr-measure, KL(Pr‖Pg) remains nonzero (and can diverge if Pg→0).
- Wording/typos: “for generating not plausibly looking pictures”; “rely” (not “relly”); Theorem 2.5 “Then”; Theorem 2.6 “is a”.
- Lemma 1 likely holds in greater generality (e.g., standard NN layers including conv/residual); note this.
- Theorem 2.1/2.2: specify whether “closed manifolds” means compact without boundary; clarify boundary assumptions.
- Theorem 2.4: remind the definition and domain of p(z); specify domains for all random variables.
- Fix cross-references (Corollary 2.1 cites missing Theorem 1.3) and clean up theorem numbering.
- Theorem 3.3: discuss practicality of estimating C; could alternatives use second moments?

Questions for the authors:
- What annealing schedule and noise magnitudes work best in practice, and how sensitive are results?
- How robust are Theorem 2.6 conclusions when discriminator errors are correlated and finite-variance?
- Can you broaden Lemma 1 to cover common architectures explicitly (conv, residual, skip connections)?
- In Theorem 2.2, do your “closed manifolds” have boundary? If not, please state “compact without boundary.”
- Can you empirically validate the JSD–Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) by tracking JSD of smoothed models versus an OT proxy?
