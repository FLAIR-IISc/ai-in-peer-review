Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles the core open problem of understanding and stabilizing GAN training. It provides a careful theoretical analysis that explains saturation, vanishing gradients, and instability as the discriminator improves, particularly when real and generated data live on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint or near-disjoint supports.
- Key contributions:
  1) Precise conditions under which the optimal discriminator is perfect and flat almost everywhere on the data and generator supports (Theorems 2.1–2.3), implying vanishing gradients for the generator with the original GAN objective (Theorem 2.4).
  2) A novel characterization of the −log D generator update (Theorem 2.5), showing it corresponds to ∇θ[KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg,Pr)] at the current θ, which elucidates both mode dropping and training instability (Theorem 2.6).
  3) A principled remedy via noise-regularization: add noise to both inputs and generator outputs to obtain continuous “smoothed” distributions Pr+ and Pg+, yielding stable, meaningful gradients (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2).
  4) A connection to the Wasserstein distance: a bound that relates W(Pr,Pg) to JSD(Pr+,Pg+) and the noise variance (Theorem 3.3), motivating training against smoothed distributions with noise annealing as a practical and theoretically grounded path to stable training.
- The work is among the first to provide nontrivial mathematical tools for GAN stability, and it includes targeted experiments that empirically corroborate the analysis (e.g., vanishing gradients as D improves, rising variance with −log D).

Decision
Accept. Reasons:
- Strong, novel theoretical insight into GAN instability that is both rigorous and practically consequential.
- A clear, principled direction (noise smoothing and annealing) supported by theory and targeted experiments.

Supporting arguments
- Importance: Understanding why GANs are unstable was a pressing question and a barrier to adoption. The paper addresses this head-on and explains widely observed phenomena (saturation, mode dropping, instability with strong discriminators).
- Placement in literature: The work is well situated with respect to GANs, divergences (KL/JSD), and prior empirical observations (Theis et al., Huszár); it also clearly contrasts ML-style KL with GAN’s JSD and articulates their practical implications.
- Novelty and rigor:
  - The manifold-support perspective and transversality-based arguments justify perfect discrimination and flat gradients almost everywhere, a key missing “why” behind saturating behavior.
  - The gradient identity for the −log D update (Theorem 2.5) is new and illuminating; it precisely links a commonly used training heuristic to an objective that explains mode dropping and instability.
  - The instability result (Theorem 2.6) formalizes heavy-tailed noisy updates from near-optimal discriminators under reasonable noise models, matching practice.
  - The smoothing analysis (Theorem 3.2/Corollary 3.2) and the bridge to Wasserstein (Theorem 3.3) are valuable tools suggesting robust procedures and metrics.
- Empirical support: While not exhaustive, the illustrative experiments are well chosen to test the paper’s claims (vanishing gradients with the original objective, rising gradient variance with −log D as D improves). This is appropriate for a theory-first contribution.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not essential to my accept decision)
- Assumptions and scope:
  - The white-noise and independence assumptions in Theorem 2.6 are idealized. It would help to discuss robustness to correlated noise or bounded, Lipschitz discriminator approximation errors, and whether heavy-tailed updates persist more generally.
  - Clarify the practical implications of Lemma 1 for networks with batch normalization, residual connections, pooling, and data-dependent operations; are these covered as compositions of affine maps and monotone nonlinearities?
  - The measure-theoretic caveats (measurability of the noise process, swapping gradients and expectations) are acknowledged in footnotes; a brief formal statement of sufficient conditions would increase rigor.
- Theorem 2.5:
  - Provide more detail on regularity conditions for exchanging gradients and expectations, and on boundary cases where densities vanish or supports overlap on sets of non-negligible measure.
  - A short derivation appendix would aid readers not familiar with the identity used to connect KL(Pg‖Pr) to D*.
- Bounds and constants:
  - The bound in Theorem 3.3 depends on the diameter C; for images, C can be large, making the bound loose. Discuss whether using Wasserstein-1 with Lipschitz constraints, or data-adaptive norms, can tighten constants.
  - Comment on dimensionality effects and whether alternative norms or structured noise (e.g., colored covariance) produce more informative bounds.
- Practical guidance:
  - Provide an explicit algorithmic recipe: where to inject noise (input to D, output of G, both), the annealing schedule, and default noise scales.
  - Expand the empirical section with ablations on the noise schedule and different noise distributions/covariances, and report quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, or nearest-neighbor distances) alongside qualitative samples.
- Related work:
  - Since you motivate Wasserstein distance, a brief comparison with training directly with a Wasserstein objective (and the implications for the gradient properties you analyze) would be valuable context for readers.

Questions for the authors
1) Theorem 2.5: What are the precise conditions under which the gradient identity holds (dominated convergence/interchange of ∇ and E, positivity of densities, support overlap)? Can you include a precise statement and proof sketch in the appendix?
2) Theorem 2.6: Does the centered Cauchy conclusion persist if the discriminator error is correlated across x (e.g., a Gaussian process with a smooth kernel) or bounded in Sobolev/Lipschitz norms? Can you show a weaker but still heavy-tailed result under more realistic error models?
3) Lemma 1: How do batch normalization, max pooling, and residual connections fit your assumptions? Are there settings where g(Z) might fail to be a countable union of manifolds of dimension ≤ dim Z?
4) Noise-regularized training: Please detail the exact noise injection strategy and annealing schedule used in your experiments. How sensitive are results to the schedule and to the choice of covariance? Did you try adding noise at intermediate layers?
5) Theorem 3.3: Can the bound be strengthened with Wasserstein-1 and a Lipschitz constant on the critic to avoid the potentially large C? Any empirical evidence that minimizing JSD(Pr+,Pg+) with noise annealing correlates with decreasing W(Pr,Pg) in practice?
6) Empirics: Beyond the targeted experiments, can you provide quantitative evidence that the proposed noise-based procedure improves stability and diversity (reduces mode dropping) relative to common baselines on standard datasets?

Overall, this is a strong, timely, and insightful paper that advances our theoretical understanding of GAN training and suggests principled stabilization techniques. I recommend acceptance.
