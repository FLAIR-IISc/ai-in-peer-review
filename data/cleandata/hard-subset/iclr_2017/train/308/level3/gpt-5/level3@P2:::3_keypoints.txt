- Paper analyzes why GAN training is unstable and prone to saturation/mode collapse, showing gradients vanish or explode as the discriminator improves, and proposes smoothing distributions as a fix with a connection to Wasserstein distance.

- Theorem: If Pr and Pg lie on low-dimensional or disjoint manifolds, smooth discriminators can be constant on each manifold, yielding zero generator gradients near its support.

- For the original minimax GAN objective, generator gradients vanish as the discriminator approaches optimality.

- For the non-saturating objective (−log D), small discriminator noise yields heavy-tailed, unstable gradient updates and biases the objective toward visually plausible samples with mode dropping.

- Smoothing remedy: adding continuous noise to inputs makes Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous, restoring stable, informative gradients and allowing optimal discriminator training without destabilizing the generator.

- Key bound (Theorem 3.3): relates true Wasserstein distance W(Pr,Pg) to noise variance and JSD(Pr+,Pg+), motivating minimizing JSD on noisy distributions while annealing noise.

- Paper combines analysis from analysis/differential topology with targeted experiments that qualitatively support the theory; Section 3 gives a simple, implementable strategy aligning with practice.

- Decision: Accept — strong, well-motivated theoretical analysis that explains GAN pathologies and offers a principled, practical training direction with theoretical guarantees.

- Problem significance and clarity: addresses a central GAN instability problem with clear questions and direct analysis.

- Context and motivation: situates work against ML divergence choices (KL asymmetry, JSD) and explains connection to mode dropping via KL(Pg‖Pr).

- Correctness and rigor: theorems are carefully stated using classical results (Urysohn, Sard, transversality); smoothing and Wasserstein bounds are technically sound.

- Empirical support: targeted experiments align with theoretical claims (e.g., discriminator error → 0, gradient norms grow under −log D).

- Exposition feedback: shorten/relocate parts of Section 2.1 and manifold results to appendix; cite standard results where possible and fix theorem numbering.

- Clarifications requested: specify whether “closed manifolds” include boundaries, state domains for p(z)/Z and other variables, and clarify independence/white-noise assumptions in Theorem 2.6.

- Generality suggestion: broaden Lemma 1 to piecewise C1/Lipschitz networks and cite function-approximation results or state precise sufficient conditions.

- Writing/typos: fix multiple phrasing and spelling errors and add standard citations for used lemmas and Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality.

- Questions for authors — theory: formalize the most general conditions for Lemma 1 and provide counterexamples from practical architectures; test robustness of Theorem 2.6 to correlated/noisy discriminator approximations.

- Questions for authors — smoothing practice: provide annealing schedules, sensitivity to noise covariance and final noise level, whether noise is added to real/fake and generator backprop, and ablation studies comparing smoothing to alternatives like gradient penalties.

- Questions for authors — Wasserstein bound and evaluation: report empirical correlations between the bound (2C√JSD+2√V) and sample quality/mode coverage as noise is annealed, assess bound tightness, and propose a protocol using smoothing to compare generative models (GANs vs VAEs).
