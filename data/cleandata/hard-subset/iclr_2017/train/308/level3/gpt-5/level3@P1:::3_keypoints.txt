- Paper gives a principled theory for GAN difficulty via “manifold mismatch”: optimal discriminators are perfect and locally flat, causing vanishing generator gradients and instability for −log D updates.  
- Shows JSD is maximized and KLs are infinite when Pr and Pg lie on nonaligned low-dimensional manifolds, even if arbitrarily close, undermining typical divergence-based GAN training.  
- Derives that the −log D update minimizes KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2·JSD, explaining mode dropping and instability.  
- Proposes a simple fix: inject continuous noise to smooth Pr and Pg, making them absolutely continuous and restoring meaningful, stable gradients.  
- Proves an upper bound linking Wasserstein distance between original distributions to JSD of smoothed ones plus a noise-variance term, motivating noise annealing.  
- Targeted experiments validate theoretical claims: vanishing gradients, −log D instability, and stabilization with noise.  
- Decision: Accept — due to strong, original, and rigorous theoretical analysis that unifies empirical heuristics.  
- Key reason for acceptance: practical remedy (noise injection + annealing) is simple, theoretically grounded, and empirically stabilizing.  
- Supporting argument: Theorems 2.1/2.2 and manifold perspective explain training deterioration as D improves.  
- Supporting argument: The Cauchy-gradient result (Theorem 2.6) formalizes instability of −log D and sensitivity to estimation noise.  
- Supporting argument: Smoothing both distributions makes discriminator training safe; gradients attract samples to data manifold and repel overrepresented regions.  
- Supporting argument: The paper situates GANs relative to f-divergences, JSD, and Wasserstein and uses analysis/differential topology for precise results.  
- Caveat: Empirical validation is targeted rather than extensive; more experiments would strengthen practical impact.  
- Caveat: Some theoretical sections are long and could be streamlined for readability.  
- Presentation: Shorten Section 2.1, move proof details to appendix, and cite standard results (Urysohn, Sard, transversality) where appropriate.  
- Presentation: Unify theorem/lemma numbering and fix cross-reference error in Corollary 2.1.  
- Presentation: Clarify whether “closed manifolds” allow boundaries and state boundary assumptions explicitly.  
- Presentation: Remind readers of p(z) definition in Theorem 2.4 and specify spaces and compactness assumptions for X, Z, and distributions.  
- Wording/typos: Fix small typos (“relly”→“rely”); adjust phrasing and minor wording in Theorems 2.5/2.6 and descriptive sentences.  
- Math scope: State broader conditions for Lemma 1 (e.g., piecewise-linear networks, common activations) and cite related structural results.  
- Math scope: Explicitly state domains and independence of all random variables when introduced; discuss realism of independence/white-noise in Theorem 2.6.  
- Empirical suggestions: Give noise schedule details (initial σ, annealing), sensitivity analyses, and comparisons to heuristics (feature matching, label smoothing, instance noise).  
- Empirical suggestions: Quantify mode coverage (mode counting setups) in addition to visual quality.  
- Empirical suggestions: Discuss and compare smoothing-plus-JSD versus Wasserstein-based training (theory and practice).

- Questions for authors: How sensitive are Theorem 2.6 conclusions to correlated noise between D and ∇D; can weaker dependence be handled?  
- Questions for authors: Practical guidance on choosing noise distribution and annealing schedule, and whether to add noise at all stages or only early training?  
- Questions for authors: What is the empirical sharpness-vs-stability trade-off as σ varies and how to avoid over-smoothing?  
- Questions for authors: Can you provide quantitative evidence of improved mode coverage (synthetic mixtures, CIFAR-10)?  
- Questions for authors: How does your approach compare empirically to Wasserstein training (sample quality, stability, hyperparameter sensitivity)?
