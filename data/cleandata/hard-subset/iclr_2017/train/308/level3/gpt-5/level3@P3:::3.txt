Summary and contributions
- The paper asks why GAN training is hard and provides a principled answer: when Pr and Pg live on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds that do not perfectly align, the optimal discriminator is perfect and flat almost everywhere on the supports, making generator gradients either vanish (original generator loss) or become highly unstable (-log D loss). This is tied to the behavior of asymmetric divergences.
- Using tools from analysis and differential topology (e.g., transversality, Sard’s lemma, Urysohn’s lemma), the paper formalizes “perfect discrimination theorems,” explains the blow-up/maxing-out of KLs and JSD in the manifold setting, and characterizes how discriminator approximation quality affects generator gradients.
- It gives an intuitive and simple practical direction: smooth both distributions by adding noise (instance noise), train against the smoothed distributions (so gradients are informative), and anneal the noise. It shows that the generator gradient becomes a weighted average of attractive/repulsive forces to data/model manifolds, and proves an upper bound on W(Pr,Pg) in terms of the noise variance and JSD(Pr+ || Pg+), connecting a tractable training signal to a meaningful distance.
- Strong theoretical contribution: the questions are well posed; the answers are insightful, technically sound, and practically actionable.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, rigorous diagnosis of instability sources grounded in analysis/topology; (2) A simple, theoretically justified smoothing-based fix with practical implications and a link to Wasserstein distance.

Supporting arguments
- Soundness: Theorems about perfect discrimination when supports are low-dimensional and non-aligned formalize a widely observed empirical reality. The vanishing-gradient result for the original generator loss and the heavy-tailed gradient argument for -log D explain common training pathologies.
- Insight: The identification of KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD in the -log D update (at fixed D*) provides a principled reason for mode dropping and instability. The smoothed-JSD/annealing approach yields gradients that reflect distances along/near the data manifold rather than measure-zero artifacts, aligning with intuition and practice.
- Practicality: The proposed remedy—additive noise to inputs (and generator) with annealing—is easy to implement and matches stabilization heuristics used by practitioners, now with theoretical backing. The bound relating W(Pr,Pg) to JSD on smoothed distributions is particularly valuable for both training and evaluation.
- Positioning: The work is well motivated in the GAN literature, complements analyses of f-divergences, and opens avenues toward metrics like Wasserstein.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all affect acceptance)
- Scope/length/structure
  - The paper is long and quite technical in parts; some consequences could be developed more, while some proofs can be shortened.
  - Suggest shortening Section 2.1 and moving its proofs to the appendix.
  - Theorem numbering is confusing; ensure consistent hierarchical numbering, and fix forward/back references.
- Clarity and definitions
  - Clarify early that if Pg(x) < Pr(x) on a set of positive Pr-measure, KL(Pr || Pg) remains strictly positive (nonzero), not only when Pg(x) → 0.
  - In Theorem 2.4, remind the reader of the definition and domain of p(z) and Z, and state the domains X, Z for all random variables and densities appearing in expectations and integrals.
  - In Theorem 2.2, be explicit about whether “closed manifolds” are meant as closed subsets of Rd or compact manifolds, and whether they may have boundary; spell out boundary assumptions precisely.
  - For Theorem 2.3, explicitly state that both KLs are +∞ whenever supports are separated by measure-zero intersection under your continuity-on-manifold assumption, even if manifolds are arbitrarily close.
  - In Theorem 2.5, emphasize that the derivative is evaluated at θ0 with D* fixed at θ0 (this helps avoid confusion).
  - In Theorem 2.6, discuss the realism of the white-noise assumption; if possible, relax to correlated noise and describe how the main phenomenon persists.
- Theorems/lemmas and citations
  - Lemma 1 holds in greater generality (e.g., with common layers like convolutions, skip connections, batch-norm under mild conditions). Consider stating a more general version or adding a remark about extensions.
  - Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 rely on standard analysis/topology results (e.g., Urysohn’s lemma, transversality/general position, Sard’s lemma). These could be cited with brief sketches rather than full proofs in the main text.
  - In Theorem 2.4, remind readers that Jθgθ is the Jacobian w.r.t. θ and specify norms; mention the integrability assumptions on z under p(z).
- Wording and typos
  - Change “for not generating plausibly looking pictures” to “for generating not plausibly looking pictures.”
  - Typo: “relly” → “rely.”
  - Theorem 2.5: “Therefore” → “Then.”
  - Theorem 2.6: “Is a...” → “is a.”
  - Corollary 2.1 references “assumptions of Theorem 1.3,” but Theorem 1.3 is not found; update the reference.
- Exposition/related work
  - Draw a clearer connection to prior “instance noise” work and to WGAN; discuss similarities/differences and when smoothing vs. Wasserstein objectives are preferable in practice.
  - Provide guidance for annealing schedules and report sensitivity.

Questions for the authors
1. Noise model and practice: How sensitive is stabilization to the choice of noise distribution/covariance? Can you provide empirical guidance on annealing schedules and final noise levels across datasets/models?
2. Discriminator noise model: Theorem 2.6 assumes independent white noise for D and ∇D. Do your conclusions hold (empirically/theoretically) when the approximation errors are correlated in space and across x?
3. Lemma 1 generality: Does the result extend to architectures with convolutions, residual connections, batch/layer normalization, and common non-linearities beyond those listed? Any conditions needed?
4. Bound tightness: In Theorem 3.3, how tight is W(Pr,Pg) ≤ 2√V + 2C√JSD(Pr+ || Pg+ ) in practice? Can you show empirical curves of JSD(Pr+ || Pg+ ) vs. sample quality and Wasserstein estimates as σ → 0?
5. Mode dropping: Can you quantify how smoothing reduces mode collapse (e.g., coverage metrics) beyond qualitative samples?
6. Evaluation: Since JSD(Pr || Pg) maxes out in the manifold setting, do you recommend training/evaluating with the smoothed JSD as a proxy? How does this compare to Wasserstein distance or precision/recall for generative models?
7. Clarification: Please confirm explicitly that KL(Pr || Pg) remains strictly positive whenever Pg underestimates Pr on a set of positive Pr-measure, not only in the limit Pg → 0.

Overall, this is a strong, thought-provoking paper with rigorous analysis and a practical solution. With the suggested edits for clarity, references, and presentation, it will be an impactful contribution.
