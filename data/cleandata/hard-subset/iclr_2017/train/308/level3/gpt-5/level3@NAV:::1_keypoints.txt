- Paper offers a rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics, explaining when discriminators become perfect and locally flat on low-dimensional supports.  
- Theorem 2.4 rigorously explains vanishing gradients for the original GAN loss via flat optimal discriminators.  
- Theorem 2.6 shows non-saturating GAN updates can be unstable due to heavy-tailed update statistics.  
- Theorem 2.5 characterizes the −log D generator update as tracking ∇θ[KL(Pg‖Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg‖Pr)] at θ0, shedding light on mode-dropping.  
- The paper proposes instance noise for both discriminator and generator and proves it yields smooth, meaningful gradients (Corollary 3.2).  
- Theorem 3.3 connects noisy JSD to the true Wasserstein distance between noiseless distributions via an explicit bound.  
- Targeted experiments (gradient norms, variance growth) corroborate the theoretical predictions.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reasons for acceptance: (1) Novel, rigorous theorems (2.4–2.6) providing genuine insight into GAN instability.  
- Reasons for acceptance: (2) Clear, principled stabilization via instance noise with supporting theory and experiments.  
- Reasons for acceptance: (3) Addresses an important problem with broad impact given early GAN fragility.  
- Supporting argument: Paper directly tackles why GAN updates degrade as discriminator improves and how to obtain stable training signals.  
- Supporting argument: Work is well situated in prior literature and uses classical mathematical tools (transversality, Sard’s lemma, optimal transport) thoughtfully.  
- Supporting argument: Theorems are technically novel, manifold/disjoint-support analysis is careful, and the noise-based remedy is justified and linked to Wasserstein control.  
- Request: For Theorem 2.5, clarify regularity needed to interchange differentiation and integration, use of D*(x; θ0) when differentiating at θ0, and conditions ensuring KL(Pgθ‖Pgθ0) is differentiable with zero gradient at θ0.  
- Request: For Theorem 2.6, justify or relax the white-noise and independence assumptions for D and ∇D (e.g., correlated Gaussian or bounded-variance noise) and provide empirical evidence of discriminator noise statistics.  
- Request: For instance noise, provide practical guidance on choosing σ and annealing schedules, sensitivity analyses across datasets/architectures, and connections to adversarial robustness and gradient penalties.  
- Request: For the Sobolev-type norm on D used in Theorem 2.4, discuss approximation rates required for practical network sizes.  
- Request: Empirics should include additional quantitative metrics (e.g., FID-like) under noise annealing and discuss how Theorem 3.3 could inform evaluation protocols.
