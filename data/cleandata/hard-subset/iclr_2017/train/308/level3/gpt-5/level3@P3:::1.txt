1. Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles a central question: why GAN training is often unstable and why generator updates degrade as the discriminator improves. It provides a principled theoretical account of these phenomena and proposes a remedy grounded in smoothing and transportation metrics.
- Section 2 develops “perfect discrimination” results when Pr and Pg live on disjoint or low-dimensional manifolds, showing that optimal discriminators are almost everywhere constant on the supports, which implies vanishing gradients. Theorem 2.3 clarifies that JSD saturates and both KLs diverge in such cases.
- Theorem 2.4 formalizes vanishing gradients for the original GAN generator objective as the discriminator approximation improves. Theorem 2.5 characterizes the generator update under the −log D trick, showing it corresponds (at the operating point) to ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)], which explains both good visuals and mode dropping. Theorem 2.6 shows that noisy discriminator approximations can yield generator updates with Cauchy-distributed coordinates (infinite variance), explaining practical instability.
- Section 3 proposes adding noise to inputs (and to generator outputs during backprop) to make both distributions absolutely continuous, yielding well-behaved gradients (Theorem 3.1, Theorem 3.2, Corollary 3.2). The paper further connects this to Wasserstein distance: Lemma 4 shows smoothing error decays with noise variance, and Theorem 3.3 upper-bounds W(Pr,Pg) by a sum of a noise term and the JSD of smoothed distributions. This provides a theoretically grounded stabilization strategy and a distance that reflects support geometry.
- The work offers deep theoretical insight, introduces useful mathematical tools (transversality, Sard’s lemma, Urysohn’s smooth lemma, Gaussian process perturbations), and validates key assumptions and claims with targeted experiments.

2. Decision: Accept
Reason 1: Strong and novel theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics and stability, addressing a pressing practical issue with clear, rigorous results (Theorems 2.4–2.6 are particularly insightful).
Reason 2: Presents a principled stabilization direction (noise smoothing and Wasserstein-based bounds) that informs both practice and future research.

3. Supporting arguments
- Specific problem: The paper analyzes why GANs destabilize—especially when the discriminator improves—and what objective the common −log D update really optimizes. It also asks how to recover stable, informative gradients.
- Motivation and placement: Excellent. It situates the analysis in the context of early GAN instability and the divergences used in likelihood vs. adversarial training, referencing Goodfellow et al., Theis et al., and Huszár. It appears to be among the first comprehensive theoretical treatments of GAN stability.
- Correctness and rigor: The proofs are careful and mostly self-contained, with appropriate use of measure-theoretic and differential-topological tools. Theorems 2.4–2.6 are novel and compelling; Theorem 2.5’s decomposition provides a clean explanation for mode dropping and apparent sample realism. The smoothing results and Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3) are well-motivated and technically sound under the stated assumptions. Experiments, while targeted rather than exhaustive, align with the theoretical predictions (vanishing gradients with the original objective; exploding variance/instability under −log D as D improves).
- Impact: The paper codifies the “manifold mismatch” explanation for instability, clarifies the true effect of the −log D trick, and offers a viable, theoretically grounded stabilization strategy. It sets a foundation for subsequent work on stable objectives and IPMs.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Assumption coherence: Early sections show that Pg typically lies on a low-dimensional manifold (no density), whereas Theorem 2.5 assumes Pr and Pgθ are continuous with densities. Please add an explicit discussion reconciling these regimes (e.g., view Theorem 2.5 as a local, idealized analysis; explain when densities exist or how smoothing justifies the step).
- Theorem 2.5 technicalities: Clarify the regularity needed to interchange ∇θ with expectations and logs, and specify conditions on gθ and p(z) to ensure differentiability and integrability at θ0. A short appendix line-by-line derivation (with dominated convergence assumptions) would resolve minor concerns.
- Theorem 2.6 noise model: The independence and white-noise assumptions for D and ∇D are idealized. Please discuss how the conclusions relax when noise is correlated or colored, and whether heavy-tailed behaviour persists. An empirical variance/degrees-of-freedom estimate for gradient components as D improves would strengthen the claim.
- Theorem 3.3 bounded support: Under Gaussian smoothing, Pr+ and Pg+ have full support. The bound assumes support in a ball of diameter C. Please clarify how C is chosen (e.g., high-probability truncation) and how the bound degrades with tail mass; a version with sub-Gaussian tails would be useful.
- Practical guidance: The smoothing proposal is promising; adding a short empirical study exploring noise schedules, covariance choices, and annealing strategies (and their effect on mode coverage and FID-like proxies) would increase practical impact.
- Connections: Briefly relate your analysis to Wasserstein GAN and other IPM-based methods, highlighting similarities/differences in the stability mechanisms.

Questions for the authors
- Assumptions in Theorem 2.5: Can you precisely state the minimal conditions to justify exchanging ∇θ and expectation, and the existence of densities for Pgθ at θ0? How do you envision using this result when Pg is singular (typical GAN) without smoothing?
- Robustness of Theorem 2.6: If the discriminator approximation error has nonzero mean or correlated noise with its gradient, what changes? Do you still expect heavy-tailed update distributions? Any empirical diagnostics to detect this regime during training?
- Smoothing schedule: What guidelines do you recommend for the noise variance annealing to ensure that the first term in Theorem 3.3 decays without reintroducing vanishing gradients? Any evidence that too-rapid annealing harms stability?
- Choice of noise distribution: Beyond isotropic Gaussian, how do anisotropic or heavy-tailed noises impact the gradients in Theorem 3.2 and the practical stability/coverage trade-off?
- Measuring progress: Have you tried computing the JSD between smoothed distributions (via a trained discriminator) during training to track the second term in Theorem 3.3? Does it correlate with sample quality and mode coverage?
- Manifold assumptions: For deep generators with skip connections or noise injection, do you expect Pg to remain concentrated on low-dimensional manifolds? Can you comment on how Lemma 1 extends to common modern architectures?

Overall, this is a strong, timely, and insightful submission. I recommend acceptance and retain my positive evaluation.
