Summary of contributions
The paper tackles a central open question in GANs: why discriminator improvements often hurt the generator by causing vanishing gradients or unstable updates, and how to obtain a principled, stable training signal. The authors provide a clear, rigorous analysis showing:
- Under realistic assumptions (data and generator distributions supported on low-dimensional manifolds), the optimal discriminator is perfect almost everywhere with zero gradient on both supports (Theorems 2.1–2.3), explaining both saturation of divergences (JSD = log 2, KLs infinite) and lack of informative gradients.
- For the original GAN objective, gradients to the generator vanish as the discriminator approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4 and Corollary 2.1), matching empirical observations.
- For the commonly used non-saturating alternative (−log D), the update equals ∇θ[KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr)] at θ0 (Theorem 2.5), explaining the bias toward sharp but mode-dropping samples. Moreover, noisy approximation to D leads to heavy-tailed, unstable generator updates (Theorem 2.6), again aligning with practice.
- A principled mitigation: add continuous noise (“instance noise”) to discriminator inputs (and crucially, to generator outputs when backpropagating) to make the convolved distributions absolutely continuous. The authors derive the resulting gradients (Theorem 3.2 and Corollary 3.2), showing they move samples toward the data manifold while repelling overrepresented model regions, and are proportional to ∇θ JSD of the smoothed distributions.
- They further relate this to a bound connecting the Wasserstein distance between true (noiseless) distributions and the JSD between their smoothed variants (Theorem 3.3), with a clean dependence on the noise variance, yielding a compelling training/evaluation perspective with annealed noise.
The analysis is well placed in the literature (Goodfellow et al., Theis et al., Huszár, Narayanan & Mitter, Hornik, Villani) and is accompanied by targeted experiments confirming vanishing gradients and increasing gradient variance as D improves.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, coherent theoretical account of GAN training pathologies grounded in manifold support and discriminator optimality, supported by theorems and sanity-check experiments. (2) A practical, theoretically justified mitigation (instance noise with annealing) that leads to smooth, stable gradients and a Wasserstein/JSD connection, along with tools to analyze training.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Understanding and stabilizing GAN training is central to broadening applicability beyond hand-tuned heuristics. The paper addresses exactly the missing theory behind common failure modes.
- Correctness and rigor: The proofs for perfect discrimination on disjoint/low-dimensional supports (Theorems 2.1–2.3) are mathematically careful, using Urysohn’s lemma, transversality, Sard’s lemma, and manifold arguments. Lemma 1’s characterization of g(Z) for neural network generators is well justified by composition of affine maps and monotone nonlinearities. The gradient results (Theorems 2.4, 2.5) are clear and illuminating, especially the re-interpretation of −log D updates. While Theorem 2.6 assumes decorrelated white noise for D and ∇D, the authors are transparent; the result still explains observed heavy-tailed, high-variance updates as D nears optimality. The smoothing results (Theorem 3.1, Theorem 3.2/Corollary 3.2) are technically sound and yield interpretable forces. The Wasserstein/JSD bound (Theorem 3.3), together with Lemma 4, provides a useful way to reason about annealing schedules and distances between noiseless distributions via discriminators trained on smoothed data.
- Motivation and placement: The work bridges empirical practice (GAN instability; heuristic −log D objective; instance noise-like ideas) and theory, connecting to divergence behavior (Theis et al.) and manifold hypotheses (Narayanan & Mitter). It also positions Wasserstein distance as a meaningful metric while retaining the practical benefits of discriminators via smoothed JSD.
- Empirical support: The figures demonstrating gradient vanishing (Fig. 2) and exploding variance (Fig. 3) are targeted and convincing for the claims they aim to test. I would like to see empirical validation of the proposed perturbation trick, but this is not a blocker given the theoretical depth and the paper’s stated focus.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the relation to dropout/Bernoulli perturbations: The proposed instance noise is additive at the input/output level to induce absolute continuity. Please discuss to what extent multiplicative Bernoulli noise (dropout) on inputs or features achieves similar smoothing of the induced distributions. Does dropout on x or gθ(z) approximate a smoothed density in any sense, or is additivity crucial for the convolution/density results (Theorem 3.1)? A short comparison (additive Gaussian vs Bernoulli-mask perturbations) would help practitioners choose.
- Empirical evaluation of the perturbation trick: Include experiments mirroring Figs. 2–3 that compare baseline GAN vs perturbed GAN with annealed noise. Suggested metrics: gradient norm statistics over training, discriminator accuracy/JSD estimates on smoothed distributions, sample quality and mode coverage on synthetic mixtures and standard image datasets, and sensitivity to noise variance and annealing schedules. An ablation contrasting noise on D-only vs on both D and G (as recommended) would be particularly valuable.
- Assumptions and scope: Discuss realism of the independent white-noise assumption in Theorem 2.6 and whether milder assumptions (e.g., bounded, correlated noise or Lipschitz-constrained discriminators) preserve heavy-tailed updates. Also comment on the compactness/smoothness assumptions in Theorem 2.1 and their implications for practical networks with ReLUs and batchnorm.
- Practical guidance: Provide heuristics for choosing initial noise scale, annealing schedule, and noise family (isotropic Gaussian vs anisotropic; Laplace; heavy-tailed kernels), and how to monitor JSD(Pr+||Pg+) in practice to adapt the schedule.
- Adversarial examples: The paper insightfully notes that backpropagating through noiseless gθ(z) can lead to measure-zero adversarial directions. A small empirical illustration comparing with/without generator-side noise would reinforce the point.
- Notation and exposition: Consider consolidating assumptions near each theorem; minor editorial issues in references can be cleaned up.

Questions for the authors
- How should one choose the initial noise variance and annealing schedule in practice? Can you propose a criterion based on the estimated JSD between smoothed distributions or gradient statistics to adaptively tune σ?
- Can you empirically compare additive Gaussian instance noise with Bernoulli/dropout-style perturbations (on inputs or features) for stability and sample quality? Does multiplicative noise yield similar smoothing benefits?
- In Theorem 2.6, how sensitive are the heavy-tailed updates to the independence/white-noise assumption? Do you observe Cauchy-like tails empirically with realistic discriminators?
- Can you provide experiments demonstrating improved mode coverage and stability on synthetic mixtures and standard image datasets when training with instance noise and annealing, including ablations (noise on D only vs D+G; different noise families)?
- How robust are the conclusions if Z has equal or higher dimension than X, or when gθ includes stochastic layers? Do your manifold arguments and perfect discriminator results still practically apply?
- At test time, do you recommend removing noise entirely? If so, can you quantify any sample quality/coverage trade-offs as σ→0 during training?

Overall, this paper offers a coherent theoretical framework and a principled stabilization technique for GANs, with clear implications and testable predictions. Adding the suggested empirical comparisons and clarifying the connection to dropout-style perturbations would further strengthen an already strong submission.
