1. Summary of contributions
The paper offers a clear, principled account of why GAN training often fails. The core insight is geometric: when Pr and Pg live on low-dimensional, nearly disjoint manifolds, there exists a smooth discriminator that is constant almost everywhere on their supports, which implies that (i) the original generator objective suffers vanishing gradients as D approaches optimality, and (ii) the popular −log D alternative induces highly unstable updates when D is noisy. The paper proves both effects: a vanishing-gradient bound for the original objective and a Cauchy-distributed update under noisy D for −log D. As a remedy, it proposes a simple perturbation trick: convolve both data and model with continuous noise (instance noise) and backpropagate through the perturbed generator outputs. This makes the smoothed distributions absolutely continuous, restores informative gradients, and lets one train D close to optimality. The paper further shows the generator gradient becomes a distance-weighted attraction to the data manifold minus repulsion from model samples, and proves a bound connecting the Wasserstein distance between Pr and Pg to the JSD between their smoothed versions plus a term controlled by the noise variance. Targeted experiments illustrate the vanishing/instability phenomena and motivate the mitigation.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons:
- Strong theoretical clarity on GAN pathologies (vanishing and instability) with precise assumptions and proofs.
- Simple, well-motivated perturbation mechanism with theory connecting it to stable, meaningful gradients and to a Wasserstein bound.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Explains why gradients vanish in the original GAN objective and why the −log D trick yields unstable and uninformative updates as D improves, especially when supports lie on low-dimensional manifolds.
- Motivation and placement: The work situates convincingly within the literature on GAN instability, moving beyond heuristics to proofs. It clarifies the role of manifold supports and the limitations of JSD/KL when supports are disjoint—an important, under-explained issue in prior work.
- Correctness and rigor: Theorems on perfect discrimination, vanishing gradient bounds, and instability under noisy discriminators are stated carefully. The smoothing analysis is compelling: adding continuous noise yields absolute continuity, makes JSD meaningful and differentiable, and the Wasserstein bound justifies annealing noise while minimizing a proxy objective that correlates with the true distance of interest.
- Contribution of the perturbation trick: The “instance noise” idea is both theoretically grounded and practical. The gradient expression shows a principled interpolation between attraction to data and repulsion from overly likely model regions, and annealing provides a clean training protocol.
- Empirical support: Current figures substantiate the two claimed pathologies (vanishing and explosion). Additional experiments (see below) would strengthen the empirical case for the perturbation mitigation.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper
These points aim to help strengthen the work; they are not prerequisites for acceptance.
- Clarify connection to dropout/Bernoulli perturbations:
  - The key property enabling your theory is absolute continuity of the smoothed distributions. Gaussian (or any continuous) instance noise achieves this via convolution in data space.
  - Standard dropout uses Bernoulli masks applied to internal activations; Bernoulli noise is discrete and, applied in feature space, does not induce absolute continuity of the distribution in input space. Thus, it does not guarantee the same smoothing of Pr and Pg seen by D, nor the same JSD/Wasserstein guarantees.
  - Input dropout (random pixel masking) produces a finite mixture over masked manifolds and still leaves a singular distribution; hence it’s unlikely to prevent perfect discrimination in your manifold setting.
  - Gaussian dropout or multiplicative log-normal noise within the discriminator can introduce continuous randomness in feature space, but unless noise is applied to the input that D sees (or to the generator outputs before D), the convolutional smoothing argument does not hold. Please discuss these distinctions explicitly and relate to denoising-based training (e.g., denoising autoencoders) and noise-contrastive intuitions.
- Empirical evaluation of the perturbation trick:
  - Repeat your Fig. 2 (vanishing gradient) and Fig. 3 (exploding/unstable gradient statistics) with instance noise enabled and annealed. Show that (i) gradient norms remain bounded and non-zero; (ii) gradient variance stabilizes; and (iii) updates correlate with improved sample quality.
  - Mode coverage benchmarks: mixtures of Gaussians on rings/grids to quantify mode dropping. Compare baseline GAN, −log D, instance noise, and annealed instance noise. Report recovered modes and KL to target mixture.
  - Image datasets: MNIST, CIFAR-10, CelebA. Track training stability (fraction of runs converging across seeds), gradient statistics, and standard metrics (at the time: Inception Score; optionally FID).
  - Ablations: type of noise (Gaussian vs Laplace), noise variance and annealing schedules, injecting noise only into D inputs vs also into G outputs (your analysis suggests both are needed), and sensitivity to architecture/optimizer.
  - Comparisons to contemporaneous stabilization methods (e.g., feature matching, label smoothing, WGAN if available) to position the practical benefits of instance noise.
- Assumptions in instability theorem: The “white noise” independence across x is idealized; consider analyzing correlated noise or providing empirical evidence that conclusions persist under realistic approximation errors in D and ∇D.
- Practical guidance: Provide a recommended noise schedule (initial σ, decay rule tied to D accuracy or epoch), and diagnostics for annealing (e.g., target JSD between smoothed distributions or gradient SNR).
- Relation to Wasserstein GANs: Since you motivate Wasserstein distance, briefly clarify how your bound and instance noise compare to directly optimizing a Wasserstein objective (with Lipschitz-constrained D). Position the approaches as complementary.

Questions for the authors
- Dropout connection: Can Bernoulli masking at the input or feature levels ever guarantee absolute continuity of the discriminator’s effective input distribution? If not, can you formalize why discrete perturbations fail to prevent perfect discrimination in your manifold setting?
- Noise design: Which noise families beyond Gaussian satisfy your theory and work well in practice? Any benefits to anisotropic covariance aligned with data manifolds?
- Where to inject noise: Your corollary suggests backpropagating through noisy generator outputs is crucial. How harmful is it to perturb only D’s inputs? Please quantify empirically.
- Annealing schedule: What schedule (initial σ, decay rate, stopping criterion) best balances early smoothing with late fidelity? How sensitive are results?
- Metrics: Beyond Inception Score, can you track gradient SNR and the JSD between smoothed distributions during training, to empirically validate your theoretical claims?
- Robustness: How do results vary across architectures and optimizers? Any adverse effects on sample sharpness if noise is not sufficiently annealed?
