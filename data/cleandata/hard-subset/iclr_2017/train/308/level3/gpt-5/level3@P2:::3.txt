Summary and contributions
- This paper tackles a fundamental question: why is GAN training unstable and prone to saturation and mode dropping? It offers a rigorous analysis of the training dynamics under realistic assumptions, explains why gradients vanish or explode as the discriminator improves, and proposes a principled fix via smoothing the distributions seen by the discriminator (and generator), together with a connection to Wasserstein distance.
- The core theoretical contributions are:
  - Perfect discrimination on low-dimensional supports: If Pr and Pg lie on (typically different) low-dimensional manifolds or disjoint supports, there exist smooth discriminators that are constant almost everywhere on each manifold, yielding zero gradients for the generator near its support (Theorems 2.1, 2.2).
  - Consequences for standard GAN objectives: For the original minimax objective, generator gradients vanish as D approaches optimality (Theorem 2.4). For the non-saturating objective (−log D), gradient updates become heavy-tailed and unstable under small discriminator noise (Theorem 2.6), and the implied objective biases toward good-looking samples with mode dropping (Theorem 2.5).
  - Smoothing-based remedy: Adding continuous noise to inputs makes Pr+ and Pg+ absolutely continuous, enabling stable and informative gradients (Theorem 3.1, Corollary 3.2) and allowing optimal D training without destabilizing the generator. A key bound relates the true Wasserstein distance W(Pr, Pg) to the noise variance and JSD(Pr+, Pg+) (Theorem 3.3), suggesting a practical training strategy (minimize JSD on noisy distributions while annealing noise).
- The paper combines tools from analysis and differential topology with targeted experiments that qualitatively support the theory. Section 3 provides a simple, intuitive, and implementable direction that aligns well with observed practice.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated theoretical analysis that explains widely observed GAN pathologies and reconciles them with the choice of divergences and model supports.
- A principled, practical training direction (smoothing) with theoretical guarantees and clear intuitions, plus a useful connection to Wasserstein distance.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and clarity: The instability of GAN training is a central, timely problem. The paper articulates clear questions (saturation, instability, divergence choice, how to avoid issues) and addresses them head-on.
- Context and motivation: The analysis is placed against maximum likelihood (KL asymmetry), Jensen–Shannon divergence in the original GAN, and common heuristics used to stabilize GANs. The connection to mode dropping via KL(Pg‖Pr) is well explained.
- Correctness and rigor: Theorems about supports on manifolds, perfect discrimination, and the resulting vanishing gradients are carefully stated, with appropriate use of classical results (Urysohn’s lemma, Sard’s lemma, transversality). The instability of −log D with noisy discriminators (Cauchy updates) is illuminating and matches practice. The smoothing results and the Wasserstein bound are technically sound and provide a clean path from theory to method.
- Empirical support: While not exhaustive, the targeted experiments align with the theoretical claims (e.g., discriminator error going to zero, gradient norms growing under −log D as D improves). Given the paper’s theoretical focus, this level of empirical validation is appropriate.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not decisive for my recommendation)
- Exposition and length:
  - Section 2.1 and parts of the manifold/discrimination results could be shortened in the main text, with proofs moved to the appendix. Some results (e.g., Theorem 2.1, Lemma 2) are standard and could be cited to classical references.
  - Clarify theorem numbering; currently Corollary 2.1 refers to “assumptions of Theorem 1.3,” which does not exist.
- Clarifications and precision:
  - In Theorem 2.2 and surrounding text, be explicit about whether “closed manifolds” have boundary. The paper later handles with/without boundary cases; please harmonize definitions and statements.
  - In Theorem 2.4, remind readers of the definition and domain of p(z) and Z; similarly specify domains for all random variables when first introduced.
  - Clarify assumptions in Theorem 2.6: independence and white-noise modeling of D and ∇D errors are strong; discuss to what extent correlated/noisy approximations maintain heavy-tailed updates, and relate to finite precision.
  - Lemma 1 likely holds under greater generality (e.g., piecewise smooth networks, broader classes of activations); consider stating the general conditions (piecewise C1, Lipschitz) and citing relevant function-approximation results.
- Writing and minor edits:
  - Fix phrasing: “for generating not plausibly looking pictures” (instead of “for not generating plausibly looking pictures”).
  - Clarify that when Pg underestimates Pr at x (Pg(x) < Pr(x)), the contribution to KL(Pr‖Pg) is nonzero (and can be large as Pg→0).
  - Replace “Therefore” with “Then” in Theorem 2.5 statement; in Theorem 2.6 use “is a” (lowercase).
  - Various typos: “arize” → “arise”, “symetrical” → “symmetrical”, “easilly” → “easily”, “relly” → “rely”, etc.
  - Where possible, cite standard references for Urysohn’s lemma, General Position Lemma/transversality, Sard’s lemma, and Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality.

Questions for the authors
- Scope of Lemma 1: Can you formalize the most general sufficient conditions on g (e.g., piecewise C1 networks with common activations) under which the image is a countable union of manifolds with dim ≤ dim Z? Any counterexamples within practical architectures?
- Theorem 2.6 robustness: How sensitive is the Cauchy-behavior result to relaxing the white-noise/independence assumptions? Can you provide empirical evidence of heavy-tailed coordinate updates under realistic discriminator approximations?
- Smoothing in practice:
  - What annealing schedules for the input noise work best in practice? How sensitive are results to the choice of covariance Σ and the final noise level?
  - Do you add noise to both real and generated samples during D training and also to generator outputs during backprop (as suggested by Corollary 3.2)? Please provide an ablation to quantify the benefit of adding noise to the generator path.
  - How does smoothing compare empirically with alternative regularizations (e.g., gradient penalties) in terms of stability, sample quality, and mode coverage?
- Wasserstein bound (Theorem 3.3): Can you report empirical correlations between 2C√JSD(Pr+, Pg+) + 2√V and sample quality or mode coverage as noise is annealed? How tight is the bound in practice?
- Evaluation: Since a goal is to enable principled evaluation beyond likelihood, can you propose a protocol using your smoothing approach to compare different generative models (GANs vs VAEs) on the same dataset?

Overall, this is a timely, insightful, and rigorous paper that advances our understanding of GAN training and proposes a simple, theoretically grounded path to more stable training. I recommend acceptance.
