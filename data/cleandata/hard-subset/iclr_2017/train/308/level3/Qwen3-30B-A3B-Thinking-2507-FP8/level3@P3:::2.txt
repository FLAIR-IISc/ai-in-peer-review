

# Review of "Understanding the Difficulty of Training GANs"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of why GAN training is unstable, explaining that when the data distribution and generator distribution have disjoint supports (or lie on low-dimensional manifolds), the optimal discriminator becomes constant almost everywhere on these supports. This leads to vanishing gradients in the original GAN formulation and unstable updates with infinite variance in the alternative formulation. The paper introduces a perturbation trick (adding noise to inputs) that creates "softer" metrics by making the distributions continuous, which avoids the theoretical problems identified in the paper. The theoretical analysis is supported by carefully designed experiments that illustrate the phenomena and validate the claims. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides both theoretical insights and practical guidance for GAN training.

## 2. Decision

Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a fundamental theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics, and the perturbation trick is both theoretically grounded and potentially impactful for practical GAN training.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a critical gap in the literature: the lack of theoretical understanding of GAN instability. The three key questions are answered thoroughly:

1. **Specific problem tackled**: The paper identifies and rigorously explains the fundamental causes of instability and vanishing gradients in GAN training, which has been a persistent challenge in the field.

2. **Well-motivated approach**: The paper is well-placed in the literature, building on foundational GAN work (Goodfellow et al., 2014) while connecting to broader concepts in generative modeling, optimal transport, and manifold learning. The theoretical analysis is both comprehensive and accessible.

3. **Support for claims**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical proofs supported by targeted experiments. The analysis of vanishing gradients (Theorem 2.4) and unstable updates (Theorem 2.6) is mathematically sound, and the experiments in Figures 2 and 3 effectively illustrate the theoretical claims. The connection to Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) provides a practical metric for evaluating GAN performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

- **Expand the discussion of the perturbation trick's relationship to dropout/Bernoulli perturbation**: The reviewer is particularly interested in understanding how the proposed perturbation relates to existing techniques like dropout. A more detailed comparison would clarify the theoretical novelty and practical implications.

- **Add empirical evaluations**: The reviewer specifically requests additional experiments comparing perturbed GANs to standard GANs (similar to Figures 2 and 3). These would provide stronger empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the perturbation trick.

- **Clarify the practical implementation**: The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how to implement the perturbation trick in practice, including recommendations for noise variance and how it might interact with other GAN stabilization techniques.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The perturbation trick resembles dropout in that both involve adding noise to inputs. Can you provide a more detailed theoretical comparison between the proposed perturbation and Bernoulli dropout, explaining the key differences in their mathematical properties and implications for GAN training?

2. Could you provide additional empirical results (similar to Figures 2 and 3) comparing the performance of perturbed GANs versus standard GANs across multiple datasets and architectures? This would help demonstrate the practical impact of your theoretical insights.

3. How does the choice of noise distribution (Gaussian vs. Bernoulli vs. other distributions) affect the performance of the perturbed GANs, and what are the theoretical implications of these choices?
