

This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training instability by rigorously analyzing the geometric properties of data and generator distributions. The authors demonstrate that GAN training difficulties arise because both the data distribution and generator distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, leading to perfect discriminators with vanishing gradients. They introduce novel tools from differential topology to analyze these phenomena and propose a smoothing-based alternative that adds noise to discriminator inputs, creating a continuous, well-behaved optimization landscape.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that successfully answers fundamental questions about GAN training instability. The authors pose insightful questions about why GANs are difficult to train and provide rigorous answers grounded in differential topology, which is a fresh perspective in this field. The proposed solution in Section 3 is elegant, intuitive, and theoretically grounded, offering a practical path to stable GAN training.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's theoretical framework fundamentally advances our understanding of GAN training dynamics, and the proposed smoothing technique provides both theoretical guarantees and practical implementation guidance. The paper's contribution is substantial and addresses a long-standing challenge in generative modeling.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing several minor issues: (1) Clarify that if P_g(x) < P_r(x), KL(P_r||P_g) remains nonzero; (2) Fix phrasing from "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" to "for generating not plausibly looking pictures"; (3) Address the typo "relly" → "rely"; (4) Clarify whether closed manifolds have boundaries in Theorem 2.2; (5) Correct the reference to "Theorem 1.3" in Corollary 2.1; (6) Fix wording in Theorem 2.5 ("Therefore" → "Then") and Theorem 2.6 ("Is a..." → "is a"); (7) Shorten Section 2.1 and move proofs to the appendix; (8) Specify domains of random variables; (9) Note that Lemma 1 holds in greater generality. Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 could cite references instead of providing full proofs. The paper's technical depth is commendable but would benefit from more concise presentation.
