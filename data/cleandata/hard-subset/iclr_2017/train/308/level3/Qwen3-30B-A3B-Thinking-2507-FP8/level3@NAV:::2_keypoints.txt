- Significant theoretical contribution: provides rigorous explanation for GAN instability and gradient vanishing.  
- Key result: when data and generator lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator is perfect (accuracy 1) and has zero gradients almost everywhere on the generator's support.  
- Explains failure modes: zero gradients cause vanishing with original cost; -log D produces unstable updates.  
- Core method: a perturbation trick (adding noise to generator and discriminator inputs) smooths distributions and yields theoretically sound, stable gradients.  
- Theoretical analysis is strong, with multiple theorems/proofs and good motivation and literature placement.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Primary concern: insufficient empirical validation of the perturbation trick; targeted experiments are mentioned but comparative results are lacking.  
- Requested experiments: add empirical comparisons between standard and perturbed GANs across multiple architectures/datasets and figures analogous to Figures 2 and 3.  
- Clarification needed on relation to existing techniques (e.g., dropout/Bernoulli perturbations): explain differences, theoretical connections, and when each is preferable.  
- Suggested additions to strengthen the paper: comprehensive empirical comparisons and a detailed discussion of perturbation vs. dropout/Bernoulli methods.
