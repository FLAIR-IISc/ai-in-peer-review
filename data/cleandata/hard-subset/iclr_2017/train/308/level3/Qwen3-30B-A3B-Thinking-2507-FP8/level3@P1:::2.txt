

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding GAN training dynamics, addressing fundamental questions about instability and saturation that have plagued practical implementations. The authors rigorously prove that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (as they inevitably do in GANs with limited latent dimensionality), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and gradient information vanishes for the generator, explaining the core challenges in GAN training. The paper further demonstrates that the commonly used alternative gradient update for the generator leads to unstable updates with infinite variance, providing the first theoretical explanation for this empirically observed phenomenon. Most importantly, the authors propose a theoretically grounded solution: adding noise to discriminator inputs to create continuous distributions that allow for smooth gradient updates, with the key insight that this approach minimizes the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that resolves fundamental questions about GAN behavior, and the proposed perturbation technique offers a principled approach to addressing instability issues. The mathematical analysis is rigorous, well-motivated, and properly positioned within the literature, with careful attention to the nuances of manifold geometry and distributional properties.

The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, with Lemma 1 establishing the critical fact that neural network-generated distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, and Theorems 2.4-2.6 providing the first rigorous explanation for vanishing gradients and instability in GAN training. The authors successfully bridge theoretical insights with practical implications through Theorem 3.3, which demonstrates that the noise-regularized approach effectively minimizes the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions. The connection to the Wasserstein metric provides a theoretically sound alternative to the problematic Jensen-Shannon divergence that has traditionally guided GAN development.

For improvement, I request the authors explicitly discuss the relationship between their perturbation technique and existing regularization methods like dropout or Bernoulli perturbations. While the paper mentions that their approach "adds continuous noise," it would be valuable to clarify how this differs from or relates to these established techniques, as this could help contextualize the contribution within the broader machine learning literature.

Additionally, I request empirical validation of the key theoretical claims. The paper presents theoretical arguments but lacks concrete experiments demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed noise-based regularization compared to standard GAN training. I suggest adding experiments similar to Figures 2 and 3 in the paper, but showing the effect of the proposed perturbation technique on standard GAN architectures (e.g., DCGAN) across multiple datasets. Specifically, it would be valuable to see: 1) a comparison of gradient magnitudes between standard GAN training and the perturbed approach, 2) visual comparisons of generated samples at different stages of training, and 3) quantitative metrics (like FID scores) showing the impact of the perturbation on sample quality and training stability.

I would also appreciate clarification on the practical implementation details of the noise perturbation. How does the choice of noise distribution (e.g., Gaussian vs. other) affect the results? What is the optimal noise variance for different architectures and datasets? The paper suggests annealing the noise, but the authors should provide guidance on how this could be practically implemented in standard GAN training pipelines.

Finally, while the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, how does the noise perturbation affect the generator's ability to capture fine-grained details in the data distribution, and what is the computational cost of implementing this technique? A brief discussion of these aspects would strengthen the paper's contribution to the practical GAN community.
