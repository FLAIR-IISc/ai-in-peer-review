

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors identify the core problem: GAN training instability stems from the fact that both the real data distribution and the generator's distribution typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports. This leads to perfect discrimination (the discriminator becomes optimal with zero gradient in most regions), causing vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator. The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis using differential topology concepts to explain these phenomena and proposes a practical solution through adding noise to smooth the distributions. The authors not only answer fundamental questions about GAN training dynamics but also provide new theoretical tools for analyzing these systems. The work represents a substantial advance in our theoretical understanding of GANs, moving beyond heuristic approaches to a principled framework.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental gap in the literature. The authors ask and answer the right questions with rigorous mathematical analysis, and their proposed solution is both theoretically sound and practically relevant.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the key problem: when the supports of the data and generator distributions are disjoint or lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical for GANs), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect with zero gradients almost everywhere on the supports, leading to vanishing or unstable generator updates. This explains why standard GAN training is difficult and why heuristic approaches are needed.

The paper's theoretical framework is rigorous, with careful proofs of key theorems. The analysis of both the original GAN cost function (which leads to vanishing gradients) and the alternative cost function (which leads to unstable updates) is particularly insightful. The authors' solution of adding noise to smooth the distributions is elegant and directly addresses the theoretical issues identified.

The paper is well-structured, with a logical progression from problem identification to theoretical analysis to practical solution. The theoretical tools developed (such as the use of manifolds and noise smoothing) are valuable for future work in generative modeling.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper is technically strong but could be improved in several ways:

- Shorten Section 2.1 and move some proofs to the appendix to improve readability.
- Clarify the relationship between P_g and the data distribution when discussing KL divergence (e.g., "if P_g is smaller than the data distribution at some x, the KL divergence remains nonzero").
- Fix the phrasing "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" to "for generating not plausibly looking pictures."
- Note that Lemma 1 holds in greater generality than stated.
- Reference standard results instead of proving Theorems 2.1 and Lemma 2 in full (these are basic analysis results).
- Clarify the domain of p(z) in Theorem 2.4.
- Specify domains for all random variables used in the paper.
- Correct the typo "relly" → "rely."
- Clarify whether closed manifolds in Theorem 2.2 have boundary.
- Correct the reference to "Theorem 1.3" in Corollary 2.1 (this theorem cannot be found).
- Fix wording in Theorem 2.5 ("Therefore" → "Then") and Theorem 2.6 ("Is a..." → "is a").
- Clarify the theorem numbering to avoid confusion.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your noise-based approach compare theoretically to other stabilization techniques like spectral normalization or gradient penalty?

2. Could you provide more intuition about why the gradient updates follow a Cauchy distribution in Theorem 2.6, particularly regarding the relationship between noise variance and gradient stability?

3. How sensitive is the proposed method to the choice of noise distribution (beyond Gaussian)? Are there theoretical guarantees about the optimal noise distribution for different data manifolds?

4. The paper mentions "annealing the noise" - what are the theoretical or empirical considerations for the annealing schedule?
