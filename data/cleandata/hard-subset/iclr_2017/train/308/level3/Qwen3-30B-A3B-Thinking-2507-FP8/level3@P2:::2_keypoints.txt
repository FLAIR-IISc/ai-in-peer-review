- The paper provides a significant theoretical explanation for GAN instability, attributing it to low-dimensional data manifolds and the resulting perfect discriminator problem.  
- It proves gradients vanish in the original GAN formulation when distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Theorem 2.4).  
- It shows the alternative cost induces unstable updates due to Cauchy-distributed gradients (Theorem 2.6).  
- Introduces a perturbation technique (adding noise to inputs) that makes distributions continuous and avoids perfect discrimination.  
- Theorem 3.3 demonstrates noise-perturbed training yields stable gradients while still minimizing the Wasserstein distance.  
- Connects GAN performance evaluation to the Wasserstein metric, providing theoretical grounding for assessment.  
- The proposed perturbation is a practical, easy-to-implement solution requiring no major architectural changes.  
- Decision: accept, based on strong theoretical contribution with practical implications.  
- Recommend a deeper comparison between the perturbation technique and existing methods like dropout or Bernoulli perturbations.  
- Recommend additional empirical evaluations comparing perturbed GANs to standard GANs on benchmark datasets.  
- Recommend more implementation guidance, including choosing noise variance and combining perturbation with techniques like spectral normalization or gradient penalty.  
- Request clarifications: how perturbation compares (theoretically/empirically) to other stabilization methods, how to choose/anneal noise variance, and how annealing should be scheduled to ensure convergence to the true data distribution.
