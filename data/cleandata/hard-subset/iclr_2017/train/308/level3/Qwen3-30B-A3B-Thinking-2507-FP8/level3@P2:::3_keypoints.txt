- Main contribution: The paper gives a rigorous theoretical explanation for GAN training instability, attributing it to real and generator distributions lying on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds.  
- Core insight: Disjoint supports make the optimal discriminator perfect with zero gradients almost everywhere, causing vanishing or unstable generator updates.  
- Theoretical tools: Uses differential topology and rigorous proofs to analyze GAN cost functions and training dynamics.  
- Practical solution: Proposes adding noise to smooth distributions, addressing the theoretical causes of instability.  
- Impact: Advances principled understanding of GANs beyond heuristics and provides tools useful for future generative-model analysis.  
- Decision: Recommend accept — strong theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental gap.  
- Supporting argument: Analysis covers both original GAN cost (vanishing gradients) and alternative cost (unstable updates), offering insightful contrasts.  
- Supporting argument: Theoretical framework is well-structured and proofs are careful and substantial.  
- Supporting argument: Noise-smoothing solution is elegant and practically relevant.  
- Readability suggestion: Shorten Section 2.1 and move some proofs to the appendix for clarity.  
- Clarification needed: Better explain relationship between P_g and data distribution in KL divergence discussion.  
- Minor edits: Fix phrasing (“for not generating plausibly looking pictures”), typos (“relly”→“rely”), and wording in several theorems.  
- Formality fixes: Note Lemma 1 holds more generally; reference standard results instead of reproving basic theorems.  
- Notation/domain issues: Clarify domain of p(z), specify domains for all random variables, and clarify whether closed manifolds have boundary in Theorem 2.2.  
- Reference/error corrections: Correct the missing/incorrect reference to “Theorem 1.3” in Corollary 2.1 and fix theorem numbering to avoid confusion.  
- Theorem wording fixes: Change “Therefore”→“Then” in Theorem 2.5 and correct capitalization in Theorem 2.6.  
- Question for authors: How does the noise-based approach compare theoretically to spectral normalization or gradient penalty?  
- Question for authors: Provide intuition for why gradient updates follow a Cauchy distribution in Theorem 2.6 and the role of noise variance.  
- Question for authors: How sensitive is the method to the choice of noise distribution and are there theoretical guarantees for optimal noise?  
- Question for authors: What are the theoretical or empirical guidelines for annealing the noise schedule?
