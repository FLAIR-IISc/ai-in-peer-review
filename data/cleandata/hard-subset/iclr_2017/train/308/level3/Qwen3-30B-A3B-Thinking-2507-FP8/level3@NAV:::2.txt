

# Review

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing a rigorous explanation for the instability and gradient vanishing issues in GAN training. The authors demonstrate that when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (a common scenario in practice), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect (accuracy 1) and has zero gradients almost everywhere on the generator's support. This explains why gradients vanish with the original cost function and why updates become unstable with the alternative cost function (-log D). The paper's core contribution is a perturbation trick—adding noise to both generator and discriminator inputs—that smooths the distributions and enables stable training with theoretically sound gradients.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The theoretical analysis is exceptionally strong, with multiple well-constructed theorems and proofs that clarify fundamental aspects of GAN training dynamics that have previously lacked theoretical grounding. The paper is well-motivated by the widespread instability issues in GAN training and properly situates itself within the relevant literature.

The primary concern is the limited empirical validation of the proposed perturbation trick. While the paper mentions "targeted experiments" in the abstract, the provided text lacks sufficient comparative results showing how the perturbation improves stability and sample quality. The reviewer specifically requests additional empirical evaluations, including comparisons similar to Figures 2 and 3, to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Additionally, the connection between the proposed perturbation trick and existing techniques like dropout or Bernoulli perturbations requires explicit discussion. The authors should clarify whether their approach is fundamentally different from or builds upon these techniques, and under what conditions one might be preferable to the other.

To strengthen the paper, please add:
1. Empirical comparisons between standard GANs and perturbed GANs across multiple architectures and datasets.
2. A detailed discussion of how the perturbation trick relates to dropout/Bernoulli perturbations, including potential theoretical connections or practical differences.

These additions would provide the necessary empirical evidence to fully support the paper's theoretical claims and strengthen its contribution to the field.
