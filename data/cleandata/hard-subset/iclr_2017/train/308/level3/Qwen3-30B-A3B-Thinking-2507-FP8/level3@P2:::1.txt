

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of generative adversarial networks (GANs), addressing a fundamental problem that has plagued practical applications: the instability of GAN training. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of why GAN training is prone to instability and saturation, showing that the problem arises from the nature of the supports of the data and generator distributions, which typically lie on low-dimensional manifolds. The paper demonstrates that under these conditions, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere, leading to vanishing or unstable gradients for the generator. The authors then propose a theoretically grounded solution involving adding noise to inputs to create "softer" metrics that can be optimized, with a thorough mathematical analysis of this approach. This work provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for understanding GAN training dynamics, moving beyond heuristic approaches to a principled analysis.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of deep generative models, addressing a critical problem with significant practical implications. The theoretical framework developed is novel, rigorous, and provides deep insight into why GAN training is unstable.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper tackles a fundamental problem that has been widely recognized but poorly understood: the instability of GAN training. The authors successfully demonstrate that the core issue arises from the fact that both the data and generator distributions typically have supports on low-dimensional manifolds (a consequence of the generator being a neural network mapping from a lower-dimensional space), which leads to the optimal discriminator being perfect with zero gradient almost everywhere. This explains the empirical observations of vanishing gradients and instability.

The theoretical results (Theorems 2.4-2.6) are particularly strong, providing mathematical proof for the observed phenomena. The paper's analysis of the original GAN cost function and the alternative -logD approach is especially insightful, showing why the latter suffers from instability despite avoiding vanishing gradients. The authors' introduction of a "softer" metric through noise addition is theoretically sound and provides a principled approach to addressing the instability problem.

This is one of the first theoretical papers on GANs to the reviewer's knowledge, and it makes a major contribution to the field. The paper successfully bridges the gap between empirical observations and theoretical understanding, providing the community with a new framework for analyzing GAN training dynamics.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation of the noise addition approach. The authors could provide more specific guidance on how to choose the noise parameters (variance, distribution) in different applications, and how this affects the trade-off between stability and sample quality.

The connection to existing practical approaches (like spectral normalization or gradient penalty) could also be strengthened. While the authors mention these in the introduction, a more explicit discussion of how their theoretical framework relates to and informs these practical techniques would help situate the work within the broader context of GAN research.

## 5. Questions for Authors

I have a minor question about the proof of Theorem 2.5. Specifically, in the step where you derive equation (3), could you provide more detail on how the relationship between the gradient and the KL divergence is established? The transition from the KL divergence to the JSD appears to be an important theoretical contribution, but the connection could be clarified with a few more steps in the proof.

Additionally, while the paper shows that adding noise creates "softer" metrics, it would be helpful to see more concrete examples of how this approach would be implemented in practice, including how the noise parameters might be tuned for specific datasets or architectures.
