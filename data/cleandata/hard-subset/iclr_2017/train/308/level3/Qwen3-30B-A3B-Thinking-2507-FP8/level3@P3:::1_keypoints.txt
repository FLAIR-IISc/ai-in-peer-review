- Provides a rigorous theoretical framework explaining GAN instability, vanishing gradients, and how to address them (via added noise).
- Introduces novel mathematical tools and key theorems (Theorems 2.4–2.6) analyzing generator/discriminator dynamics.
- Shows that when generator and data lie on low-dimensional manifolds the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and its gradients vanish.
- Theorem 2.5 identifies that the commonly used "−logD" gradient optimizes an inverted KL divergence, explaining mode dropping.
- Proposes adding noise to inputs to create “softer” metrics that avoid GAN training pathologies.
- Theorem 3.3 links noisy distributions to the Wasserstein distance, providing theoretical support for the noise-based approach.
- Recommendation: accept — substantial theoretical contribution that will serve as a foundation for future research.
- Strength: deep theoretical depth and novel insights compared to typical empirical/heuristic GAN work.
- Suggestion: add discussion connecting theoretical findings to practical GAN architectures.
- Suggestion: provide empirical validation of the proposed noise-based stabilization to strengthen practical relevance.
- Suggestion: make some proofs more intuitive (especially for readers less familiar with differential geometry).
- Clarifying Q1: effect of assuming a fixed discriminator D* (θ0) versus concurrent discriminator updates?
- Clarifying Q2: practical guidance for annealing the added noise in real applications?
