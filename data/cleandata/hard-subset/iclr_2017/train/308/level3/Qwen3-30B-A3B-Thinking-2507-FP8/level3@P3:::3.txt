

# Review of "Why Does GAN Training Fail?"

## 1. Summary

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental challenges in GAN training. Rather than proposing a specific algorithm, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for why GAN training is difficult, identifying the core instability mechanisms related to the geometric properties of data and generator distributions. The paper analyzes how asymmetric divergences (like KL and JSD) behave when distributions have disjoint supports on low-dimensional manifolds, which is typical in GANs. The authors then propose a principled smoothing-based solution that addresses the identified instability sources. The theoretical analysis is innovative, employing differential topology concepts to provide deep insights into GAN dynamics. The paper is well-motivated in the literature context, clearly explaining how current GAN approaches rely on heuristics without theoretical grounding. The authors successfully connect their theoretical analysis with practical implications, offering a promising direction for stable GAN training through the introduction of noise to the inputs.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in GAN training, with clear practical implications. The theoretical analysis is sound, the insights are valuable, and the proposed solution is well-motivated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully answers the key questions about GAN instability with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors correctly identify that the disjoint support of data and generator distributions (on low-dimensional manifolds) leads to perfect discrimination, causing vanishing gradients in the original GAN formulation and unstable gradients in the alternative formulation. This explanation resolves longstanding empirical observations about GAN training behavior.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building upon existing work on divergences in generative modeling while providing the first comprehensive theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of current approaches and position their work as a necessary foundation for future GAN research.

The paper supports its claims with both theoretical analysis and targeted experiments. The theoretical results are well-justified, with the differential topology approach providing novel insights that explain why GAN training is difficult. The connection between theory and practice is clear, with the proposed smoothing solution offering a principled way to address the identified instability sources.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, please address the following points:

- Shorten Section 2.1 and move detailed proofs to the appendix to improve readability.
- Clarify the statement about KL divergence: "if P_g is smaller than the data distribution at some x, the KL divergence remains nonzero" (currently phrased as "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" which should be "for generating not plausibly looking pictures").
- Note that Lemma 1 holds in greater generality (for any neural network architecture with standard nonlinearities).
- Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 are standard analysis results; cite appropriate references instead of providing full proofs.
- In Theorem 2.4, explicitly remind readers of the definition/domain of p(z).
- Specify the domains of all random variables used throughout the paper.
- Clarify whether closed manifolds in Theorem 2.2 have boundary.
- Fix the reference to "Theorem 1.3" in Corollary 2.1 (which appears to be a typo for a different theorem).
- Correct phrasing in Theorem 2.5 ("Therefore" → "Then") and Theorem 2.6 ("Is a..." → "is a").
- Clarify the theorem numbering system to avoid confusion.

The paper's technical depth is appropriate for ICLR, though the current length could be streamlined. The theoretical contributions are substantial and will be valuable to the GAN research community, providing a foundation for future work on stable GAN training. The proposed smoothing solution in Section 3 is particularly elegant and well-motivated, offering a promising direction for practical implementation.
