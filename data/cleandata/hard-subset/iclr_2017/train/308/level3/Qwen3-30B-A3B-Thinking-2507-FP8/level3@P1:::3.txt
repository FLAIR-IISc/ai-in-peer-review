

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the instability of GAN training dynamics. The authors identify the core problem as the low-dimensional nature of data distributions and the resulting perfect discrimination between real and generated samples, which causes vanishing gradients and instability. The paper provides rigorous mathematical analysis using differential topology to explain why GAN training is difficult, showing that the optimal discriminator becomes constant almost everywhere on the data and generator manifolds, leading to vanishing gradients. The authors then propose a practical solution involving adding noise to the discriminator inputs, which smooths the distributions and yields stable gradients. This work addresses fundamental questions about GAN training that have been largely ignored in the literature, providing a theoretically grounded foundation for future research.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical insights are novel and valuable, and the proposed solution is both elegant and practical. The main reasons for acceptance are the paper's strong theoretical contribution that answers fundamental questions about GAN training dynamics and its practical implications for improving GAN stability.

The paper's theoretical framework is well-constructed and rigorously proven. The authors correctly identify that the optimal discriminator becomes perfect when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which explains why gradients vanish as the discriminator improves. The analysis of both the original GAN cost function and the alternative -logD cost function is particularly insightful, showing how the latter leads to unstable updates due to the Cauchy distribution of gradients. The proposed solution of adding noise to the discriminator inputs is simple but powerful, and the paper provides rigorous justification for why this approach works, including bounds on the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is particularly valuable, as it provides a theoretically sound way to measure distance between distributions that is not affected by low-dimensional support issues.

The paper would benefit from several minor revisions to improve clarity and presentation. First, the paper could be shortened by moving some of the proofs to the appendix, particularly the proofs of Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2, which are standard results in analysis that could be cited rather than fully developed. Second, the paper should clarify the relationship between the generator's distribution and the data distribution regarding KL divergence, particularly the point that if P_g is smaller than the data distribution at some x, the KL divergence remains nonzero. Third, the phrasing "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" should be corrected to "for generating not plausibly looking pictures" to be grammatically correct.

The paper also contains several minor issues with theorem numbering and references that need to be fixed. Corollary 2.1 references "assumptions of Theorem 1.3," but Theorem 1.3 cannot be found in the paper. The numbering of the theorems is confusing and should be clarified throughout. Additionally, the paper should specify the domains of random variables used in the theoretical analysis, particularly in Theorem 2.4 where the definition/domain of p(z) should be reminded to the reader. The paper contains a typo ("relly" instead of "rely") that should be corrected.

The paper's use of differential topology concepts is impressive, but the authors could note that Lemma 1 holds in greater generality than stated, which would strengthen their theoretical foundation. The discussion of closed manifolds in Theorem 2.2 should clarify whether they have boundary, as this affects the interpretation of the results.

The authors should also consider adding a brief discussion of how their proposed noise injection method compares to existing approaches for stabilizing GAN training, such as spectral normalization or gradient penalties, to better situate their contribution within the broader literature.

The paper's main strength is its ability to provide a clear theoretical explanation for phenomena observed in practice, which has been lacking in the GAN literature. The proposed solution is simple yet powerful, and the paper provides the theoretical justification needed to understand why it works. The authors have successfully answered the key questions about GAN training dynamics that they posed in the introduction, and their work opens up new research directions for future work in this area.

The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial, and the proposed solution has immediate practical implications. With the minor revisions noted above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the ICLR 2017 proceedings. The theoretical insights provided here are valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of GANs, making this a strong candidate for acceptance.
