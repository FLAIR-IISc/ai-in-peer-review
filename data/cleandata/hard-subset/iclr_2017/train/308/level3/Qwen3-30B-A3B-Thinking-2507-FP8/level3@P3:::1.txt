

# Review of "Theoretical Analysis of Generative Adversarial Networks"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of generative adversarial networks (GANs) by addressing a critical gap in the field: the lack of theoretical understanding of GAN training dynamics. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical framework to analyze why GAN training is unstable, why gradients vanish, and how these issues can be theoretically addressed. The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, clearly articulating the importance of theoretical understanding for GANs' practical application. The authors introduce novel mathematical tools and prove key theorems that explain the fundamental challenges in GAN training, including Theorems 2.4-2.6 which analyze vanishing gradients and instability of generator updates. Crucially, they propose a theoretically grounded approach to stabilize GAN training by adding noise to inputs, creating "softer" metrics that avoid the theoretical pitfalls of standard GAN training. This work represents one of the first serious theoretical treatments of GANs, providing deep insights that go far beyond the typical empirical or heuristic approaches prevalent in the field.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the GAN literature, providing the first rigorous analysis of the fundamental stability issues that have plagued GAN training since their introduction. The theoretical framework established in this paper will serve as a foundation for future research in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper stands out for its theoretical depth and significance. Unlike most GAN papers, which focus on empirical improvements through architectural modifications, this work provides mathematical proofs that explain why GAN training is unstable. The authors identify a fundamental theoretical problem: when generator and data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (as is typical in GANs), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and its gradients vanish, causing the generator to receive no useful training signal. This insight explains why GANs are so difficult to train in practice.

The paper's most compelling contributions are Theorems 2.4-2.6, which rigorously analyze the gradient behavior of GANs under different cost functions. Theorem 2.5 is particularly important as it identifies the actual cost function being optimized by the commonly used "−logD" gradient, revealing that it involves an inverted KL divergence that explains GANs' notorious mode dropping behavior. The proof techniques employed are sophisticated yet accessible to the machine learning community, and the mathematical insights are genuinely novel.

The paper's proposal to add noise to inputs to create "softer" metrics is theoretically sound and provides a principled path toward stable GAN training. Theorem 3.3 establishes a crucial connection between the noisy distributions and the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions, providing a theoretical foundation for the noise-based stabilization approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few minor improvements would enhance its impact. The authors might consider adding a brief discussion of how their theoretical findings relate to practical GAN architectures currently in use. Some empirical validation of the proposed noise-based approach (beyond the theoretical analysis) would strengthen the connection between theory and practice. The presentation of some proofs could be slightly more intuitive, particularly for readers less familiar with differential geometry.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

To further solidify my confidence in the paper's theoretical claims, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. The proof of Theorem 2.5 assumes D* is fixed for a value θ0. How does this assumption affect the practical implementation of the gradient updates, and how would the analysis change if the discriminator is updated concurrently with the generator?

2. The authors note that the noise-based approach requires annealing the noise. What practical considerations should guide the annealing schedule in real-world applications?

3. The paper focuses on the case where generator and data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds. How do the authors expect their theoretical framework to extend to cases where the data manifold is not low-dimensional (e.g., when using high-dimensional priors)?

This paper represents a major theoretical contribution to the GAN literature and will serve as an important foundation for future work. Its rigorous analysis of GAN training dynamics addresses a critical gap in the field and provides valuable theoretical insights that will guide both research and practical applications.
