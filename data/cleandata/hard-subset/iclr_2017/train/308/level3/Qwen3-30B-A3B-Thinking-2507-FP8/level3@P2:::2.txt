

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the fundamental challenges in training generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors provide a rigorous analysis of why GAN training exhibits instability and saturation, revealing that these issues arise from the low-dimensional manifold structure of data distributions and the resulting perfect discrimination problem. The paper successfully answers key questions about GAN dynamics, including why gradients vanish in the original formulation and why updates become unstable in the modified cost function. Most importantly, the authors introduce a theoretically grounded perturbation technique (adding noise to inputs) that avoids these issues, providing a practical solution that can be implemented without significant architectural changes. The theoretical framework developed here represents a major step toward principled GAN training, moving beyond heuristic approaches that have dominated the field.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in GAN research with significant practical implications. The core insight about the manifold structure of data distributions and the resulting perfect discrimination problem provides a clear explanation for long-observed GAN behaviors, while the perturbation technique offers a theoretically sound and practically implementable solution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is its rigorous theoretical framework that explains GAN instability. The authors prove that when data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (which is typical in practice), the optimal discriminator becomes perfect and its gradient vanishes almost everywhere, causing the generator's gradients to vanish as well (Theorem 2.4). For the alternative cost function, they show that gradient updates become unstable due to the Cauchy distribution of gradients (Theorem 2.6), which explains the well-documented instability in practice. These theoretical results provide a solid foundation for understanding GAN training dynamics.

The perturbation technique introduced in Section 3 is particularly compelling. By adding noise to inputs, the authors transform the distributions into continuous ones, avoiding the perfect discrimination problem and enabling smooth gradient updates. The key result (Theorem 3.3) demonstrates that this approach allows for stable training while still minimizing the Wasserstein distance between the original distributions. The connection to the Wasserstein metric provides a theoretically sound way to evaluate GAN performance, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend:

1. A more detailed discussion of the connection between the proposed perturbation technique and existing methods like dropout or Bernoulli perturbations. While the paper mentions this similarity, a more thorough comparison would help contextualize the contribution.

2. Additional empirical evaluations comparing the perturbed GAN approach with standard GANs. The paper includes some theoretical analysis but would benefit from concrete experiments (similar to Figure 2 and 3) demonstrating the effectiveness of the perturbation technique on standard benchmark datasets.

3. More discussion of the practical implementation details, including how to choose the noise variance and whether the perturbation technique can be combined with other stabilization techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty).

## 5. Clarifying Questions

To better understand and evaluate the paper's contribution, I would appreciate clarification on:

1. How does the proposed perturbation technique compare to other stabilization methods (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance?

2. What is the optimal noise variance for different architectures and datasets? The paper mentions "annealing" the noise, but doesn't specify how this should be implemented in practice.

3. The paper states that the perturbation technique "makes the two noisy distributions match" (Corollary 3.2), but doesn't explain how the annealing schedule should be designed to ensure convergence to the true data distribution. A brief discussion of this would be helpful.
