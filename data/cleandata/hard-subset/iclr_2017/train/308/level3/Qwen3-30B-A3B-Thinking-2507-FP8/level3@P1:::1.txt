

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the training dynamics of generative adversarial networks (GANs). The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the instability and saturation problems that have plagued GAN training, introducing novel mathematical tools to characterize these phenomena. The paper's main contributions are threefold: first, it proves that GANs suffer from perfect discrimination when data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds; second, it demonstrates how this leads to vanishing gradients in the original GAN formulation and unstable updates in the alternative cost function; and third, it proposes a theoretically grounded solution using noisy distributions to create a softer metric for comparing distributions. This work is one of the first theoretical papers on GANs to the reviewer's knowledge, addressing a critical problem in the field with deep mathematical insights.

I recommend acceptance with strong enthusiasm. The paper's theoretical analysis is groundbreaking and provides the field with essential tools for understanding GAN training dynamics. The key reason for acceptance is that it fundamentally advances our theoretical understanding of GANs, moving beyond heuristic fixes to provide principled insights into why GANs behave as they do. A second key reason is that the paper's theoretical framework directly enables practical improvements, as evidenced by the proposed noisy distribution approach that addresses the instability issues.

The paper's theoretical framework is exceptionally strong. Theorem 2.3 is particularly insightful, showing that common divergences (JSD, KL) are maximized even when data and generator distributions are close, explaining why gradient-based optimization fails. Theorem 2.4 provides a rigorous proof of vanishing gradients as the discriminator approaches optimality, while Theorem 2.6 demonstrates the instability of the alternative cost function through a novel connection to Cauchy distributions. These theorems are not only mathematically elegant but also directly explain empirical observations in GAN training. The authors' approach of analyzing approximations to the optimal discriminator rather than the ideal case is particularly clever and well-motivated.

The proposed solution (Section 3) is equally compelling. Theorem 3.3 establishes a crucial relationship between the Wasserstein distance of the original distributions and the Jensen-Shannon divergence of the noisy distributions, showing how to control both terms to achieve stable training. The authors' insight that adding noise to the discriminator inputs creates a continuous distribution that avoids the perfect discrimination problem is both simple and profound, with clear practical implications.

Minor points for improvement: The derivation in Theorem 2.5 (Equation 3) could benefit from more detailed explanation of how the KL(Pg‖Pr) term emerges in the gradient calculation. While the authors reference Huszar (2016), the connection to the inverted KL minus two JSD could be made more explicit. Additionally, the assumption of independent Gaussian noise for D and ∇D in Theorem 2.6 might be overly restrictive; the authors could explore how this assumption affects the practical applicability of their instability result.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify two points: First, in Theorem 2.5, they state that "Ez∼p(z)[−∇θ logD∗(gθ(z))|θ=θ0] = ∇θ[KL(Pgθ‖Pr)− 2JSD(Pgθ‖Pr)]|θ=θ0." Could they provide more intuition about why this specific combination of divergences emerges in the gradient? Second, in the proof of Theorem 3.2, the authors state that "the only thing that depends on θ is gθ(z) for every z" when taking the gradient. Given that the discriminator D* is assumed fixed during backpropagation, this seems reasonable, but a brief clarification would strengthen the argument.

The paper's contribution is substantial and well-motivated, addressing a critical problem in deep generative modeling with rigorous mathematical analysis. The theoretical insights have immediate practical implications for improving GAN training, and the paper's framework provides a foundation for future research in the field. The authors have made a clear and important contribution to understanding why GANs behave as they do, which is essential for developing more stable and effective generative models. This work represents a significant step forward in the theoretical understanding of GANs and deserves acceptance.
