- Significant theoretical contribution explaining GAN training instability.
- Proof that real and generated distributions on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds yield a perfect discriminator and vanishing generator gradients (Theorems 2.1–2.2).
- Analysis showing the alternative "-log D" cost avoids saturation but produces extremely unstable (potentially infinite-variance) gradients.
- Proposed remedy: add continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and produce stable, informative gradients.
- Theoretical link showing the noise-regularized gradients relate to minimizing a meaningful distance between distributions bounded by the Wasserstein metric.
- Recommendation to accept based on clarifying long-standing problems and offering an elegant, theoretically justified solution.
- Suggest discussing connections between Gaussian input noise and other noise-based regularizers (e.g., Bernoulli perturbations like dropout).
- Recommend stronger empirical validation: directly compare "perturbed GAN" versus unperturbed GANs under both original and "-log D" losses.
- Advise adding visualizations (e.g., gradient norms and sample quality over training) for all compared methods to demonstrate stabilization effects.
- Request clarification on practical necessity of annealing noise variance versus using a fixed noise level, per Theorem 3.3.
