This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution towards understanding the difficulties of training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors move beyond heuristic solutions and seek to build a foundational understanding of the instability and saturation issues that plague GAN training. The paper makes several key contributions: it identifies that the distributions learned by generators and the real data distribution often have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds; it rigorously proves that this property leads to a perfect discriminator whose gradients are zero almost everywhere, causing training issues like vanishing gradients with the original cost function or unstable, high-variance gradients with the popular `-log D` alternative; and finally, it proposes a simple and theoretically grounded solution of adding noise to the inputs of the discriminator to create smoother distributions that overlap, thus enabling stable training. The use of concepts from analysis and differential topology to analyze these problems is particularly insightful.

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  It provides the first rigorous theoretical explanation for the widely observed instability in GAN training, connecting it to the geometry of the data and generator distributions.
2.  The proposed solution is not just another heuristic but is directly motivated by the theoretical analysis and offers a practical path towards more stable GAN training.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's core strength lies in its meticulous diagnosis of GAN training problems. The authors correctly identify that both the true data distribution and the generator's distribution are likely concentrated on low-dimensional manifolds within the high-dimensional sample space. Lemma 1 provides a formal argument for why this is true for generators that are neural networks.

Building on this insight, Theorems 2.1 and 2.2 establish a crucial result: if these manifolds do not perfectly align, an optimal discriminator can separate them perfectly. The critical consequence, which the authors highlight, is that such a perfect discriminator will have zero gradients almost everywhere on the supports of the two distributions. This single observation elegantly explains the primary difficulties in GAN training.

The paper then clearly analyzes the consequences for the two most common GAN cost functions. For the original minimax objective, Theorem 2.4 shows that as the discriminator approaches optimality, the generator's gradients vanish. This creates an impossible trade-off: a poor discriminator provides a weak learning signal, while a good discriminator provides no signal at all. For the alternative `-log D` objective, the analysis is equally compelling. Theorem 2.5 reveals that this objective function corresponds to minimizing a combination of KL and JSD terms that explains GANs' tendency to produce sharp samples at the cost of mode collapse. More critically, Theorem 2.6 demonstrates that under a near-optimal discriminator, the gradients for this objective have infinite variance (following a Cauchy distribution), explaining the massive instability observed in practice.

Finally, the proposed solution in Section 3 is a natural and well-motivated consequence of this analysis. By adding continuous noise to the inputs, the distributions are smoothed and their supports overlap, invalidating the conditions that lead to a perfect, zero-gradient discriminator. Theorem 3.2 provides an intuitive interpretation of the resulting gradients, showing how they pull generated samples towards the data manifold in a stable manner. The connection made to the Wasserstein distance provides a valuable perspective on measuring similarity between distributions on manifolds and motivates the proposed approach as a way to minimize a more sensible distance metric.

**Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper's presentation and clarity. They are not critical to the decision but could strengthen the final version.

*   **Presentation and Brevity:** The paper is quite long and technical. Section 2.1, in particular, could be condensed. Results like Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2 are relatively standard results in analysis and topology; it might be more effective to cite appropriate references (e.g., for Urysohn's lemma and the Transversality Theorem) and provide brief intuitions rather than full proofs in the main text. Moving more of the detailed proofs to the appendix would improve the flow.
*   **Technical Details and Typos:**
    *   In the discussion of KL divergence in the introduction, the phrasing "this cost function would pay a high cost for generating not plausibly looking pictures" is slightly awkward. It could be rephrased as "for generating implausible samples."
    *   In the same section, it is stated that if `Pr(x) > Pg(x)`, the KL term grows to infinity when `Pg(x) -> 0`. It should be clarified that if `Pg(x) < Pr(x)` but is non-zero, the integrand is still positive, contributing to the cost of mode-dropping.
    *   Lemma 1, regarding the manifold structure of a generator's output, may hold in greater generality than stated. A brief note on this could broaden the perceived applicability.
    *   Corollary 2.1 refers to "the same assumptions of Theorem 1.3," but there is no Theorem 1.3. This appears to be a typo and should be corrected (presumably to Theorem 2.4).
    *   Theorem numbering is confusing. There are multiple theorems numbered 2.1, 2.2, etc. This should be clarified to a single sequence (Theorem 1, 2, 3, etc.).
    *   In Theorem 2.2, it should be explicitly stated if "closed manifolds" are assumed to be with or without boundary.
    *   In Theorem 2.4, it would be helpful to remind the reader of the domain of `z ~ p(z)`.
    *   Minor wording: In Theorem 2.5, "Therefore," could be changed to "Then,". In Theorem 2.6, "is a centered Cauchy distribution" reads better than "Is a centered Cauchy distribution". There is a typo "relly" in the introduction that should be "rely".

**Questions for Authors**

1.  The analysis in Theorem 2.6 assumes the noise in the discriminator and its gradient is independent white noise. How crucial is this assumption? Would the conclusion about high-variance updates still hold under more structured noise models that might better reflect errors from a finite-capacity neural network approximator?
2.  Theorem 3.3 provides a compelling theoretical bound connecting the Wasserstein distance to the JSD of the noise-perturbed distributions. This suggests a training procedure where the noise variance `V` is annealed. Could you elaborate on the practical implications of this? Have you experimented with an annealing schedule, and how does it affect training stability and final sample quality?
3.  The introduction of the Wasserstein metric is very insightful. However, the practical algorithm proposed still minimizes the JSD between noisy distributions. Is the main contribution here to use the Wasserstein distance as a motivation and an evaluation tool, or do you see a direct path to an algorithm that explicitly optimizes an approximation of the Wasserstein distance itself?
