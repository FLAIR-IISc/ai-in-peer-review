This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution toward understanding the training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors rigorously analyze why GAN training is notoriously unstable. They prove that when the real and generated data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds with disjoint supports, the optimal discriminator becomes perfect, leading to vanishing gradients for the generator in the original formulation. The paper further shows that common alternative cost functions, while avoiding saturation, suffer from extremely unstable gradients. As a solution, the authors propose a theoretically-grounded method of adding noise to the discriminator's inputs, which smooths the distributions and provides stable, informative gradients.

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are its valuable clarification of long-standing, poorly understood problems in GAN training and the proposal of an elegant, theoretically-justified solution.

The paper’s main strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The theorems demonstrating the existence of a perfect discriminator with zero gradients on the data manifolds (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2) provide a clear and fundamental reason for the vanishing gradient problem. This insight alone is a substantial contribution. Furthermore, the analysis of the alternative "-log D" cost function, which demonstrates that its gradients can have infinite variance, provides a compelling explanation for the unstable updates observed in practice. The proposed mitigation strategy of adding continuous noise is not just a heuristic; the authors show that it leads to smooth, well-behaved gradients that relate to minimizing a meaningful distance between the underlying distributions, as bounded by the Wasserstein metric.

For improvement, I would suggest the following:
The proposed technique of adding noise to the discriminator inputs is highly effective. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the connection between this approach and other noise-based regularizers. Specifically, how does this Gaussian perturbation compare to Bernoulli perturbations like dropout? A brief discussion contextualizing your method within this broader family of techniques would be beneficial.

While the theoretical analysis is the core of this work, the empirical support could be strengthened. To better demonstrate the practical advantages of your method, I suggest adding experiments that directly compare a "perturbed GAN" against standard unperturbed GANs using both the original and the alternative "-log D" cost functions. Visualizations similar to Figures 2 and 3, but tracking gradient norms and sample quality over the course of training for all compared methods, would provide compelling evidence for the stabilization effect you describe.

Finally, could the authors elaborate on the practical necessity of annealing the noise variance? Theorem 3.3 implies a schedule where the noise is gradually reduced. Is this annealing process critical for achieving good final sample quality, or is a fixed noise level sufficient in practice?
