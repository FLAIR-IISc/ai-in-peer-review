### Summary

This paper provides a significant theoretical contribution towards understanding the difficulties encountered when training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors rigorously analyze the training dynamics and offer clear explanations for two primary failure modes: the vanishing gradients that can stall training with the original minimax objective, and the instability and mode-dropping associated with the alternative `-log D` objective. The core of the analysis rests on the insight that the distributions of real and generated data often have disjoint supports on low-dimensional manifolds, which allows the discriminator to become perfect and provide useless gradients. Building on this analysis, the paper proposes a simple yet principled solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This technique is shown to smooth the distributions, prevent disjoint supports, and result in smooth, stable gradients that meaningfully relate to the distance between the data manifolds.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Fundamental Theoretical Insight:** The paper provides the first rigorous theoretical explanation for the widely observed instability in GAN training. By formalizing the problem in the context of distributions on low-dimensional manifolds, it pinpoints the exact cause of vanishing and unstable gradients, moving the field from heuristic-based fixes to a more principled understanding.
2.  **Practical and Well-Grounded Solution:** The proposed solution of adding noise to the discriminator inputs is not just a heuristic but is directly motivated by the theoretical analysis. The theorems presented in Section 3 clearly show how this approach leads to well-behaved, informative gradients, effectively bridging the gap between theory and practice.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's argument is built methodically and convincingly. The authors first establish that for a typical GAN setup, where the generator maps from a low-dimensional latent space, the support of the generator's distribution (`Pg`) lies on a low-dimensional manifold (Lemma 1). Given that the real data distribution (`Pr`) is also believed to lie on a similar manifold, the paper proves that these two distributions will almost surely have disjoint supports or intersect on a set of measure zero (Theorems 2.1 and 2.2).

This key result has profound consequences. It implies that a nearly perfect discriminator can always exist, one that is constant (and thus has zero gradient) on the supports of `Pr` and `Pg`. This directly explains the vanishing gradient problem with the original GAN cost function, as formalized in Theorem 2.4. When the discriminator gets better, the generator's gradients disappear, making training impossible.

The paper then analyzes the commonly used `-log D` alternative cost function. Theorem 2.5 is a valuable contribution, showing that this objective corresponds to minimizing a combination of KL(Pg‖Pr) and JSD, which helps explain the tendency of GANs to produce high-quality samples at the cost of mode coverage. More critically, Theorem 2.6 demonstrates that using this objective with a near-optimal (but noisy) discriminator leads to gradients with infinite variance (a Cauchy distribution). This provides a compelling theoretical explanation for the massive instability observed in practice.

Finally, the proposed solution in Section 3 is elegant. By adding continuous noise to the inputs of both `Pr` and `Pg`, the resulting distributions become absolutely continuous and their supports overlap (Theorem 3.1). This simple trick breaks the core assumption of the earlier theorems and invalidates their negative conclusions. Corollary 3.2 shows that the resulting gradients for the generator are smooth and interpretable, encouraging the generator to move its probability mass toward the real data manifold. The connection made to the Wasserstein distance provides a powerful new tool for evaluating and optimizing generative models whose distributions may not overlap.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

1.  **Connection to Other Regularization Techniques:** The proposed method of adding continuous noise to the discriminator's inputs is a form of regularization. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on the relationship between this approach and other noise-based regularizers, such as dropout (which can be interpreted as multiplicative Bernoulli noise). Does continuous Gaussian noise offer specific theoretical advantages over other types of noise in this context?

2.  **Empirical Validation of the Solution:** The paper does an excellent job of empirically illustrating the *problems* with existing GAN objectives in Figures 2 and 3 (showing vanishing and exploding gradients, respectively). To make the paper's claims about the solution more compelling, it would be highly beneficial to include complementary experiments that validate the proposed method. For example, recreating plots similar to Figures 2 and 3 for a GAN trained with input noise would provide strong empirical evidence that the gradients are indeed stable and non-vanishing, as the theory predicts.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify the following points:

*   Could you elaborate on the conceptual and theoretical connections between your proposed noise-injection method and other regularization techniques like dropout?
*   To empirically substantiate the claims of improved stability, would it be possible to provide experimental results (perhaps analogous to Figures 2 and 3) that compare the gradient norms and their variance for a GAN trained with your proposed noise injection versus the standard formulations? This would powerfully demonstrate the practical benefit of your theoretical insights.
