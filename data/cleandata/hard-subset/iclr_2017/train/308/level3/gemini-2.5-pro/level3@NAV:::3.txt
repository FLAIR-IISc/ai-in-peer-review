This paper provides a much-needed theoretical investigation into the training instability of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors make several significant contributions: they rigorously analyze how common divergences behave when the data and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, identify this as a key source of vanishing or unstable gradients, and propose a simple yet powerful smoothing-based alternative to alleviate these problems. The work is theoretically deep, leveraging concepts from analysis and differential topology to provide insightful answers to fundamental questions about why GANs are so difficult to train.

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The primary reasons are its strong theoretical contribution to a widely recognized and important problem, and the elegant, practical solution it proposes that is directly motivated by the theoretical findings. The paper moves the field beyond heuristics and toward a principled understanding of GAN dynamics.

The core strength of this work lies in its rigorous analysis. The theorems in Section 2 cogently explain why a discriminator can become perfect when distributions have disjoint or low-dimensional supports, leading to useless gradients for the generator. This provides a clear theoretical foundation for phenomena that were previously only understood intuitively. Furthermore, the solution proposed in Section 3—adding noise to the inputs to smooth the distributions—is both simple and well-justified. The analysis showing how this method provides smooth, meaningful gradients that relate to the Wasserstein distance is particularly compelling.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the core ideas are excellent, the paper's presentation could be improved to increase its impact. These suggestions are intended to help strengthen the final version:
*   **Clarity and Conciseness:** The paper is quite dense and technical. Consider shortening Section 2.1 and moving detailed proofs, especially for standard results like Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2, to an appendix, citing appropriate references instead. This would significantly improve readability.
*   **Corrections:** The manuscript requires a thorough proofreading pass. Please address the following:
    *   Corollary 2.1 references a non-existent "Theorem 1.3".
    *   The theorem numbering is confusing and should be revised for clarity.
    *   There are several minor typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "relly" -> "rely"; "for not generating plausibly looking pictures" -> "for generating not plausibly looking pictures").
    *   Wording in theorems could be tightened (e.g., Theorem 2.5: "Therefore" -> "Then").
*   **Technical Clarifications:** To improve rigor, please clarify a few points:
    *   In Theorem 2.2, does "closed manifolds" imply manifolds without a boundary?
    *   Could you specify the domains for the random variables used throughout the analysis?
    *   It would be helpful to remind the reader of the definition of p(z) in Theorem 2.4.
