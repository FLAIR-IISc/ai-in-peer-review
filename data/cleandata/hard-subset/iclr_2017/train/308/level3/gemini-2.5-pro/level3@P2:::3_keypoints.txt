- Rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics and instability.  
- Shows a perfect discriminator exists when real and generated distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- Proves a near-optimal discriminator yields vanishing gradients for the original GAN cost.  
- Proves the −log D generator update produces pathologically unstable gradients with infinite variance.  
- Provides formal proof that the −log D update minimizes KL(Pg||Pr) − 2 JSD(Pg||Pr), explaining mode dropping.  
- Proposes adding instance noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and prevent perfect discrimination.  
- Proves noise yields well-behaved, meaningful gradients that pull Pg toward Pr (Theorem 3.2).  
- Connects noise-perturbed JSD to the Wasserstein distance and bounds Wasserstein by the JSD of noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3).  
- Decision: Accept, based on significant theoretical insight and a well-motivated practical solution.  
- Theorems in Section 2 show disjoint/non-aligned manifold supports cause perfect discrimination and zero gradients almost everywhere.  
- Theorem 2.4 and 2.6 explain the training trade-off: undertrained discriminator → inaccurate gradients; overtrained → useless or unstable gradients.  
- Theorem 3.2 interprets generator gradients as pulling samples toward the data manifold while repelling other generated samples (contrastive effect).  
- Training on noisy data is presented as an actionable strategy to minimize a meaningful metric on true data.
