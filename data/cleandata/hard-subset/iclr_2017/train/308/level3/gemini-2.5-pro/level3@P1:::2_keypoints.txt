- Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of GAN training dynamics and instabilities.  
- Shows that when real and generated data lie on low-dimensional manifolds, their supports are likely disjoint, allowing a perfect discriminator and producing zero gradients (vanishing gradient problem).  
- Analyzes the -log(D) objective and shows it avoids saturation but induces updates with infinite variance, explaining instability and mode-covering behavior (relates to minimizing KL(Pg||Pr)).  
- Proposes adding continuous noise to discriminator inputs to force overlapping supports, yielding smooth, meaningful gradients and stabilizing training.  
- Connects minimizing the JSD between noisy distributions to the Wasserstein distance, giving a theoretical metric for progress even when original supports do not overlap.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: First rigorous theoretical explanation for widely observed GAN instabilities (vanishing gradients and unstable updates).  
- Key reason: The noise-based solution is simple, practical, and directly motivated by the theoretical insights.  
- Supporting argument: Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1/2.2 formalize that perfect discriminators exist with zero gradients on manifold supports.  
- Supporting argument: Theorem 2.4 formalizes why original GAN training suffers from vanishing gradients.  
- Supporting argument: Theorems 2.5/2.6 show the -log(D) heuristic corresponds to KL minimization and yields Cauchy-distributed (infinite-variance) generator updates under a near-optimal noisy discriminator.  
- Supporting argument: Theorem 3.2 demonstrates noise produces smooth gradients that pull the generator manifold toward the data manifold.  
- Supporting argument: Theorem 3.3 provides a Wasserstein bound that depends on noise variance, linking noisy JSD minimization to distance between original distributions.  
- Feedback 1: Discuss connections to other noise/regularization methods (e.g., dropout, Bernoulli noise) and whether the theory offers a unified explanation.  
- Feedback 2: Add empirical validation of the proposed noisy-discriminator method (e.g., plots showing stable, non-zero generator gradient norms).  
- Feedback 3: Clarify the trade-off in Theorem 3.3 between noise variance and the Wasserstein bound, and whether this implies a practical noise-annealing schedule.
