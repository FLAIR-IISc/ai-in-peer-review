### Summary of the paper's claims and contributions

This paper provides a significant and much-needed theoretical contribution towards understanding the training dynamics and instabilities of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors move beyond the common practice of heuristic-driven architectural search and instead offer a rigorous analysis of why GAN training is notoriously difficult. The core contributions are threefold. First, the paper demonstrates that when the real and generated data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds (a common scenario), their supports are likely disjoint. This allows for a perfect discriminator to exist, which in turn leads to gradients that are zero almost everywhere, thus providing a clear reason for the vanishing gradient problem in the original GAN formulation. Second, the paper analyzes the popular `-log(D)` alternative cost function, showing that while it may not suffer from vanishing gradients, it introduces massive instability by producing updates with infinite variance. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the paper proposes a simple, elegant, and theoretically-grounded solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This technique ensures that the supports of the two distributions overlap, leading to a well-behaved discriminator and smooth, meaningful gradients for the generator. The introduction of the Wasserstein distance as a sensible metric for evaluating GANs, even in the non-overlapping support case, is another valuable contribution that connects the proposed solution to a robust measure of progress.

### Decision and key reasons

Decision: Accept

Key Reasons:
1.  The paper provides the first rigorous theoretical explanation for the widely observed training instabilities in GANs, clarifying both the vanishing gradient problem in the original formulation and the unstable updates in common variants. This foundational analysis is a major step forward for the field.
2.  The proposed solution of adding noise to discriminator inputs is simple, practical, and directly motivated by the theoretical insights. It offers a principled approach to stabilizing GAN training, moving beyond the heuristics that have dominated the area.

### Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's strength lies in its meticulous theoretical development. The argument begins by formalizing the intuition that the generator's distribution `Pg` and the data distribution `Pr` lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1). The subsequent theorems (2.1 and 2.2) convincingly show that under this condition, a perfect discriminator exists whose gradients are zero on the supports of `Pr` and `Pg`. This provides a clear and compelling explanation for the vanishing gradients observed in practice when using the original GAN cost function, as formalized in Theorem 2.4. This is a critical insight that explains why simply training the discriminator for more steps is often counterproductive.

Furthermore, the paper provides an excellent analysis of the `-log(D)` cost function, which is a common heuristic to avoid the saturation issue. Theorem 2.5 reveals that this alternative objective is related to minimizing `KL(Pg||Pr)`, which helps explain GANs' tendency to produce high-quality samples at the cost of mode coverage. More critically, Theorem 2.6 provides a novel explanation for the instability of this objective, showing that under a noisy but near-optimal discriminator, the generator's gradient updates follow a Cauchy distribution, which has infinite variance. This is a powerful result that formally explains the erratic training behavior many practitioners have experienced.

Finally, the proposed solution in Section 3 is a direct and elegant consequence of the preceding analysis. By adding noise, the supports of the distributions are guaranteed to overlap, making the distributions absolutely continuous and thus preventing the existence of a perfect discriminator with zero gradients. Theorem 3.2 shows that this leads to smooth and interpretable gradients that act to pull the generator's manifold towards the data's manifold. The connection made to the Wasserstein distance in Theorem 3.3 is particularly insightful, as it provides a theoretical framework for understanding how minimizing the JSD between the noisy distributions can serve as a proxy for minimizing a more sensible distance between the original, noiseless distributions.

### Additional feedback and questions for the authors

The paper is very well-written and the theoretical contributions are excellent. The following points are intended to help further strengthen the work and are not critical for the decision.

1.  **Connection to other noise injection methods:** The proposed technique of adding continuous noise to the discriminator inputs is highly effective. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion comparing this approach to other noise-based regularization methods, such as dropout or adding Bernoulli noise to the inputs. While your method is motivated by ensuring overlapping supports, these other methods are often motivated as ways to prevent discriminator overfitting. Are there deeper connections? Does your theoretical framework provide a new lens through which to understand the success of noise injection in adversarial training more generally?

2.  **Empirical validation of the solution:** The experiments in Figures 2 and 3 do an excellent job of empirically verifying the problems (vanishing and exploding gradients) with existing GAN formulations. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a parallel set of experiments for your proposed method. A plot analogous to Figure 3, showing that the norm of the generator's gradient remains stable and non-zero as the discriminator is trained closer to optimality, would provide powerful empirical support for your theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of your solution.

3.  **Clarification on the Wasserstein bound:** In Theorem 3.3, the bound on the Wasserstein distance `W(Pr, Pg)` depends on `V`, the variance of the noise. This suggests a trade-off: a larger `V` makes the JSD term easier to optimize (as the distributions overlap more) but adds a larger slack term `2V^(1/2)` to the bound. Could you comment on this trade-off and whether it suggests a practical annealing schedule for the noise variance during training?
