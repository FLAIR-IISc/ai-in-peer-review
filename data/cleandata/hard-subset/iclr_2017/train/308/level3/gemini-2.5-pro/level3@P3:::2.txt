### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper provides a significant and much-needed theoretical contribution to the understanding of training dynamics in Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors rigorously analyze why GAN training is often unstable and why gradients can either vanish or become unmanageably large. The core insight is that the distributions of real and generated data often have supports that lie on low-dimensional manifolds, which are likely disjoint. The paper proves that in this scenario, a perfect discriminator exists that can separate the two distributions with 100% accuracy. Crucially, the gradients from such a perfect discriminator are zero almost everywhere, providing no useful information for training the generator.

The authors formally show that this insight explains two key failure modes. For the original GAN cost function (`log(1-D)`), the generator's gradients vanish as the discriminator improves (Theorem 2.4). For the commonly used alternative cost (`-log(D)`), the paper demonstrates that while gradients do not vanish, they become massively unstable, with updates following a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance under a noisy discriminator approximation (Theorem 2.6).

To address these fundamental issues, the paper proposes a simple yet theoretically grounded solution: adding continuous noise to the inputs of the discriminator. This technique smooths the data distributions, ensuring their supports overlap and preventing the existence of a perfect discriminator. The authors prove that this leads to well-behaved, informative gradients that attract generated samples towards the data manifold while repelling them from other generated samples (Theorem 3.2). Finally, the paper connects this approach to the Wasserstein distance, providing a bound that justifies minimizing the Jensen-Shannon Divergence of the noise-perturbed distributions as a proxy for minimizing the distance between the true, noiseless distributions.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept due to its foundational contributions to GAN theory. The key reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Novel and Rigorous Diagnosis:** The paper is one of the first to provide a formal, mathematical explanation for the widely observed instabilities in GAN training. By identifying the problem of disjoint low-dimensional supports, it moves the field beyond heuristic-based fixes towards a principled understanding of the underlying issues.
2.  **Principled and Practical Solution:** The proposed mitigation strategy of adding instance noise is simple to implement but is directly derived from the theoretical analysis. It provides a clear, actionable method for stabilizing GAN training that is supported by strong theoretical arguments.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors build their argument step-by-step, starting with the very reasonable assumption that real-world data and generator outputs lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1). The subsequent proofs that a perfect discriminator with zero gradients must exist under these conditions (Theorems 2.1 & 2.2) are compelling and form the bedrock of the paper's contribution.

This analysis provides clear and satisfying explanations for long-standing empirical observations. The dilemma presented by the original cost function—where the generator's cost function is only a good approximation of the JSD when the discriminator is optimal, yet gradients vanish precisely in that regime—is elegantly explained by Theorem 2.4. Similarly, the characterization of the `-log(D)` cost's gradients as having infinite variance (Theorem 2.6) provides a powerful theoretical account for the extreme instability many practitioners have experienced.

The proposed solution in Section 3 is a direct and logical consequence of this analysis. By adding noise, the core assumption of disjoint supports is broken, and the pathological behavior is resolved. The derivation of the resulting generator gradient (Theorem 3.2) is particularly insightful, as it shows the gradient performs an intuitive push-pull dynamic, attracting generated samples towards real data points and separating them from other generated points. The final connection to the Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) elegantly ties the proposed training objective (minimizing JSD between noisy distributions) to a meaningful metric between the original, clean distributions, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the entire approach.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Connection to Other Noise-Based Regularizers:** The proposed method of adding continuous instance noise is an effective regularizer. It would be valuable to include a discussion comparing this technique to other common noise-based regularizers, particularly dropout or multiplicative Bernoulli noise. While the mechanisms differ (e.g., input vs. activation perturbation, continuous vs. discrete noise), the high-level goal is similar. A discussion on whether the theoretical guarantees provided in Section 3 are specific to continuous noise would further clarify the contribution and place it in a broader context.

*   **Empirical Validation of the Proposed Solution:** The paper provides excellent empirical illustrations of the *problems* with GAN training (e.g., Figures 2 and 3 showing gradient norms). However, it would be significantly strengthened by adding experiments that validate the *solution*. A direct comparison between a standard GAN (e.g., DCGAN) and the proposed version with instance noise would be highly impactful. Showing that the generator gradient norms for the perturbed GAN remain stable as the discriminator is trained to optimality would provide powerful empirical support for the theory. A qualitative comparison of the final generated samples would also demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed technique.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  The proposed method of adding continuous noise to the discriminator inputs is reminiscent of other noise-based regularizers like dropout. Could you elaborate on the theoretical and practical differences? Would the theoretical guarantees in Section 3 hold for discrete noise distributions (e.g., Bernoulli), and if not, why is continuous, absolutely continuous noise essential to the analysis?

2.  To further validate the proposed solution, could you provide empirical results comparing a baseline GAN against your proposed version with instance noise? Specifically, plots similar to Figures 2 and 3 showing the generator's gradient norm for the perturbed GAN would be very insightful, alongside a qualitative comparison of generated samples.

3.  Theorem 3.3 and the surrounding discussion suggest a training procedure where the noise variance is annealed over time. Did you experiment with such an annealing schedule? How sensitive is the training stability and final sample quality to the choice of noise variance and its schedule?
