- Rigorous theoretical explanation for GAN failures: vanishing gradients with the minimax objective and instability/mode-dropping with the -log D objective.  
- Core insight: real and generated data lie on low-dimensional manifolds whose supports are almost surely disjoint, letting the discriminator become perfect and give useless gradients.  
- Lemma 1 and Theorems 2.1/2.2: Pg and Pr typically have disjoint supports or intersect only on a measure-zero set.  
- Theorem 2.4: a near-optimal discriminator yields zero gradients for the generator under the original GAN objective (vanishing gradients).  
- Theorem 2.5: the -log D objective corresponds to minimizing a mix of KL(Pg||Pr) and JSD, explaining mode-seeking behavior and poor mode coverage.  
- Theorem 2.6: with a near-optimal (noisy) discriminator, gradients under -log D have infinite variance (Cauchy-like), explaining severe training instability.  
- Proposed fix: add continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and force overlapping supports.  
- Theorem 3.1 and Corollary 3.2: noise injection makes distributions absolutely continuous, produces smooth, informative generator gradients, and encourages moving mass toward the data manifold.  
- Link to Wasserstein distance: the noise-smoothed view enables a meaningful connection to Wasserstein metrics for evaluation/optimization.  
- Decision: Accept — due to the fundamental theoretical insight and a principled, practically motivated solution.  
- Requested clarification: discuss conceptual/theoretical relationships between continuous input noise and other regularizers (e.g., dropout) and whether Gaussian noise has specific advantages.
