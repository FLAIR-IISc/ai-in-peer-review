- Paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis explaining why GAN training is unstable, focusing on training dynamics rather than new architectures.
- Main insight: if real and generator distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate them.
- Perfect discrimination causes vanishing gradients for the original GAN loss and unstable/high-variance gradients for the −log D variant.
- Authors introduce new mathematical tools and theorems (notably Theorems 2.4–2.6) formalizing these instability and saturation phenomena.
- Proposed solution: add noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions, prevent perfect discrimination, and yield informative generator gradients.
- The paper connects noise-regularized training to the Wasserstein distance, providing bounds that relate true distribution distance to the JSD of noisy versions.
- Decision: Accept — work is judged strong, important, and timely for deepening theoretical understanding of GANs.
- Key reasons for acceptance: fundamental theoretical contribution explaining GAN failure modes and novel, impactful analysis linking manifolds to gradient behavior.
- Paper clearly motivates the problem and situates itself well within prior work on generative models and divergence measures.
- Claims are well-supported with rigorous proofs: Lemma 1 on manifolds, Theorems 2.1/2.2 on perfect discriminators, and Theorems 2.4/2.6 on gradient behavior follow logically.
- Suggested improvements: add higher-level intuition alongside dense formal exposition to improve accessibility, especially around Theorem 2.6 (infinite variance).
- Practical guidance requested: discuss implementation details for noise injection (choice of noise distribution and variance scheduling).
- Suggest elaborating why the Wasserstein metric is preferable for manifold-supported distributions, potentially with a simple illustrative example.
- Minor technical question for authors: request a step-by-step derivation of the gradient equality in Theorem 2.5 showing how the θ-derivative is moved inside the expectation and applied to log terms.
