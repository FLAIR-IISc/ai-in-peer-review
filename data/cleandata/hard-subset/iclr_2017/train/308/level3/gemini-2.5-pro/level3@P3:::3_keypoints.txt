- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of GAN training difficulties by modeling real and generated distributions as supported on low-dimensional manifolds.  
- It proves an optimal discriminator can perfectly separate these distributions, causing vanishing or pathological gradients.  
- For the original GAN loss, gradients passed to the generator vanish (Theorem 2.4).  
- For the -log(D) heuristic, gradients become unstable with infinite variance (Theorem 2.6).  
- The authors propose adding noise to discriminator inputs to thicken supports and ensure meaningful gradients.  
- Theorem 3.2 shows noise-induced gradients pull generated samples toward the data manifold.  
- Theorem 3.3 links the JSD of smoothed distributions (what the GAN minimizes) to the Wasserstein distance between original distributions.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: Strong theoretical foundation addressing fundamental questions about GAN instability.  
- Key reason: Actionable, principled solution (noise injection) with theoretical connection to Wasserstein metric.  
- Presentation suggestion: Move full proofs (e.g., Theorem 2.1, Lemma 2) to the appendix to improve readability.  
- Structure suggestion: Shorten Section 2.1 and reconsider theorem/proposition hierarchy to improve flow.

- Clarify Theorem 2.2: specify whether "closed manifolds" are assumed to be without boundary.  
- Clarify notation: remind readers of the domain of the prior p(z) (e.g., z ~ p(z) in Z) and other random-variable domains.
