- Paper gives a rigorous theoretical explanation for GAN instability: real and generated data lie on disjoint low-dimensional manifolds, enabling a perfect discriminator with zero gradients almost everywhere.  
- Shows for the original GAN cost log(1−D) the generator's gradients vanish as the discriminator improves (Theorem 2.4).  
- Shows for the alternative cost −log(D) gradients do not vanish but become highly unstable, following a Cauchy distribution with infinite variance under noisy discriminator approximation (Theorem 2.6).  
- Proposes adding continuous instance noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions so supports overlap and prevent a perfect discriminator.  
- Proves noise yields well-behaved, informative generator gradients that attract samples to the data manifold and repel other generated samples (Theorem 3.2).  
- Connects minimizing JSD of noise-perturbed distributions to bounding the Wasserstein distance between the true distributions (Theorem 3.3).  
- Decision: Accept — contribution is foundational, moving GAN theory beyond heuristics.  
- Key reasons for accept: novel, rigorous diagnosis of GAN failure modes and a simple, theoretically grounded, practical mitigation (instance noise).  
- Supporting analysis rests on the reasonable assumption that data and generator outputs lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1) and the subsequent proofs of perfect discriminator existence (Theorems 2.1 & 2.2).  
- Empirical illustrations convincingly show the problem (gradient norms), but the paper lacks experiments validating the proposed noisy-discriminator solution.  
- Suggest adding discussion comparing continuous instance noise to other noise-based regularizers (e.g., dropout, Bernoulli) and whether theoretical guarantees require continuous noise.  
- Recommend empirical validation: compare baseline GANs (e.g., DCGAN) to the instance-noise variant, show generator gradient norms and qualitative sample improvements.  
- Questions for authors: clarify differences between continuous instance noise and discrete/noise-based regularizers, provide empirical comparisons of perturbed vs. baseline GANs, and report on noise-variance annealing schedules and sensitivity.
