- Analyzes GAN training dynamics under the realistic assumption that real and generated data lie on low-dimensional manifolds, causing likely disjoint supports.  
- Proves the existence of a "perfect" discriminator that perfectly separates the distributions and has zero gradients almost everywhere on their supports.  
- Shows the original GAN cost suffers vanishing gradients as the discriminator approaches optimality, impairing learning.  
- Demonstrates the -log(D) alternative optimizes a KL + JSD combination but can produce unstable, infinite-variance (Cauchy) gradient updates when the discriminator is a noisy near-optimal approximation.  
- Proposes adding noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions, provide stable/meaningful gradients, and connect the objective to minimizing Wasserstein distance.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: Exceptional theoretical depth offering rigorous mathematical explanations rather than heuristics.  
- Key reason: High impact and relevance—addresses a central bottleneck in GAN training.  
- Problem addressed: Why GAN updates vanish or become erratic as the discriminator improves.  
- Motivation/positioning: Well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, building on Goodfellow et al. and empirical observations.  
- Evidence: Claims supported by novel theorems (Lemma 1; Theorems 2.1–2.2, 2.4–2.6; Theorems 3.2–3.3) with a clear logical progression.  
- Clarification requested: Theorem 2.5 assumes continuous densities—clarify compatibility with the paper’s manifold/disjoint-support premise.  
- Practical guidance requested: How to choose and possibly anneal the discriminator input noise variance σ² in practice.  
- Intuition requested: A more accessible explanation of why the gradient ratio leads to Cauchy (infinite-variance) behavior and its implications for optimizers.  
- Overall assessment: Excellent, foundational paper that brings rigorous theory to GAN training pathologies.
