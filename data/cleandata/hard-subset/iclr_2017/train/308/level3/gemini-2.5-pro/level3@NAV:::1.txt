This paper presents a rigorous and much-needed theoretical analysis of the training dynamics and instability of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors move beyond heuristic approaches to mathematically characterize why GAN training is notoriously difficult, particularly focusing on issues of saturation and instability. The paper's main contribution is to demonstrate that when data distributions lie on low-dimensional manifolds, the optimal discriminator can yield vanishing or unreliable gradients for the generator. It formally analyzes this phenomenon for different GAN cost functions and proposes a theoretically-grounded direction for achieving stability.

I strongly recommend acceptance and retain my original positive evaluation of this work. This is an outstanding submission that provides foundational theoretical insights into a highly important class of models. The primary reasons for this decision are the paper's deep theoretical contribution to a field largely driven by empirical heuristics and its direct relevance to solving the critical practical problem of GAN training instability.

To my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to offer a comprehensive mathematical explanation for the unstable behavior of GANs. The core argument—that distributions supported on low-dimensional manifolds can be perfectly discriminated, leading to useless gradients—is both powerful and elegantly proven. Theorems 2.4–2.6 are novel, introducing interesting techniques to formalize the vanishing gradient problem in the original cost function and the high-variance, unstable updates in the alternative "-log D" formulation. This analysis provides a clear theoretical justification for phenomena that practitioners have observed for years. Such foundational work is rare and exceptionally valuable to the community, providing a solid base for future research.

The following points are offered to help improve the paper further and are not conditions for acceptance.
The mathematical arguments are impressive, however, I have a minor question about the proof of Theorem 2.5. The derivation combines a result for the JSD gradient with another for the KL gradient. Could the authors briefly clarify the step where these two results are subtracted? Ensuring the assumptions for both derivations are perfectly aligned in the final step would make this important proof even more watertight. Answering this would solidify my confidence in this key part of the paper's contribution.
