1. Core contribution: proves GAN training issues stem from real and generated distributions lying on low-dimensional manifolds, enabling a perfect discriminator and pathological generator gradients.  
2. Theorems 2.1–2.2: show a perfect discriminator can exist and its gradient is zero almost everywhere on the supports.  
3. Theorem 2.4: original generator cost log(1−D) leads to vanishing gradients when the discriminator is optimal.  
4. Theorem 2.6: alternative cost −log(D) yields updates with infinite variance and expectation (Cauchy-like behavior), explaining instability.  
5. Proposed solution: add continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and ensure overlapping supports.  
6. Corollary 3.2: noise produces smooth, informative gradients that move generated samples toward the data manifold and away from other generative samples.  
7. Theorem 3.3: minimizing JSD on noisy distributions upper-bounds the Wasserstein distance between original noiseless distributions, connecting the fix to a principled metric.  
8. Decision: Accept.  
9. Key reason 1: provides one of the first deep, rigorous theoretical analyses of GAN dynamics, moving beyond heuristics.  
10. Key reason 2: theoretical insights directly motivate a practical, effective noise-injection method that stabilizes training and relates to Wasserstein minimization.  
11. Supporting argument: manifold assumption is well-motivated by prior work and underpins the novel, compelling proofs in Section 2.  
12. Supporting argument: Theorem 2.5 analysis highlights a KL(Pg||Pr) term and a −2JSD term; authors argue the latter can push distributions apart.  
13. Additional feedback: add a high-level roadmap in Section 2 to improve accessibility of the dense mathematical argument.  
14. Additional feedback: include a small-scale experiment (e.g., MNIST/CIFAR-10) showing practical benefits of noise injection on stability or sample quality.  
15. Additional feedback: expand discussion on interplay between the KL and −2JSD terms in Theorem 2.5 and when one term dominates.  
16. Question: how does assuming a fixed optimal discriminator D* (in Theorem 2.5) affect interpretation when, in practice, the discriminator is continuously updated?  
17. Question: any practical or theoretical guidance for scheduling/annealing the noise variance V during training to best minimize the Wasserstein bound?
