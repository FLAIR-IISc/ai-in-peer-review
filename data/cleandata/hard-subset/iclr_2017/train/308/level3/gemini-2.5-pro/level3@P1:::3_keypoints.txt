- Significant theoretical contribution explaining GAN training difficulties and moving beyond heuristics.  
- Identifies that generator and real data distributions often lie on low-dimensional manifolds (Lemma 1).  
- Proves misaligned manifolds admit a perfect discriminator that separates supports (Theorems 2.1/2.2).  
- Shows a perfect discriminator has zero gradients almost everywhere, causing vanishing gradients for the minimax objective (Theorem 2.4).  
- Analyzes the `-log D` objective as minimizing KL + JSD, explaining mode collapse and showing near-optimal discriminators yield infinite-variance (Cauchy) gradients and instability (Theorems 2.5/2.6).  
- Proposes adding continuous noise to discriminator inputs to smooth distributions and induce overlap, enabling stable training (Section 3; Theorem 3.2).  
- Connects the noise-based approach to the Wasserstein distance and provides a theoretical bound relating Wasserstein to JSD of noisy distributions (Theorem 3.3).  
- Recommends acceptance based on novel rigorous explanation and a theory-motivated practical solution.  
- Suggests improving presentation and brevity by moving standard/topological proofs to the appendix and condensing Section 2.1.  
- Lists technical/typo fixes and clarifications needed (KL wording, Lemma 1 generality, theorem numbering and references, manifold boundary assumptions, domain reminders, minor typos).  
- Asks how crucial the white-noise independence assumption in Theorem 2.6 is and whether results hold for structured/noisy discriminator approximators.  
- Requests practical details and experiments on annealing noise variance V as suggested by Theorem 3.3 and its effect on stability and sample quality.  
- Asks whether the Wasserstein discussion is primarily motivational/evaluative or if there is a clear path to algorithms that explicitly optimize an approximation of Wasserstein.
