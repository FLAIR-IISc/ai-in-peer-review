## Review of "Theoretical Analysis of Generative Adversarial Networks"

This paper presents a valuable theoretical investigation into the notoriously unstable training dynamics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors tackle the fundamental question of *why* GAN training is so difficult, moving beyond the purely heuristic approaches that dominate the field. They rigorously analyze the conditions leading to instability and saturation, offering novel insights into the behavior of the discriminator and generator during training. The work provides a strong theoretical foundation for understanding GANs, a topic largely unexplored until recently. 

I **recommend acceptance** of this paper. It represents a significant contribution to the field, providing much-needed theoretical grounding for a method that has largely been driven by empirical success. The analysis is deep and insightful, and the theorems presented (particularly 2.4-2.6) introduce interesting mathematical techniques to the problem. 

My recommendation is based on the following arguments: The paper clearly identifies a critical problem in the GAN literature – the lack of theoretical understanding of training instability. The approach is well-motivated, building upon the established framework of GANs and identifying a key issue: the potential for perfect discrimination when the generator and discriminator distributions have low-dimensional supports. The paper convincingly supports its claims through rigorous mathematical proofs and connects these theoretical findings to observed practical issues like vanishing gradients and unstable updates. The theorems regarding vanishing gradients (2.4) and instability (2.6) are particularly compelling and align with common experiences in GAN training. The introduction of softer metrics and the analysis of the Wasserstein distance (Theorem 3.3) offer a promising direction for improving GAN training stability.

To further strengthen the paper, I have a few suggestions. The proof of Theorem 2.5 is dense, and I would appreciate a more intuitive explanation of why the resulting gradient update leads to mode dropping. Could the authors elaborate on the specific properties of the KL divergence that contribute to this behavior? Additionally, while the discussion of noise addition is promising, a more detailed exploration of the practical implications of choosing different noise distributions (as hinted at in Section 3) would be beneficial. Finally, it would be helpful to briefly discuss the limitations of the theoretical assumptions (e.g., the smoothness of the discriminator) and how these might affect the applicability of the results in real-world scenarios.

Overall, this is a strong and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GANs. Addressing the minor suggestions above would further enhance its clarity and impact.
