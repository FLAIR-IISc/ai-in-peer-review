## Review of "Towards Understanding GAN Training Dynamics"

This paper tackles a fundamental problem in the field of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs): the lack of theoretical understanding behind their notoriously unstable training process. The authors make a valuable contribution by identifying the root cause of gradient vanishing and instability – the tendency of the discriminator to become “too good” when the generator’s support lies on a low-dimensional manifold. They then propose a perturbation trick, effectively smoothing the discriminator’s input, to mitigate these issues, supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper provides a compelling explanation for observed GAN training difficulties and a promising direction for improvement. The theoretical grounding is strong, and the insights gained are significant for the community. 

The strength of this work lies in its analytical approach. The authors convincingly demonstrate how perfect discrimination arises due to the low-dimensional nature of the generator’s output and how this leads to vanishing gradients or instability depending on the cost function used. The proposed perturbation trick is a clever solution, and the theoretical justification for its effectiveness is well-presented. 

However, the paper would benefit from empirical validation of the perturbation trick. While the authors provide some initial experiments (Figures 2 & 3), a more comprehensive comparison of GANs trained *with* and *without* the proposed perturbation, perhaps using a standard benchmark dataset like CIFAR-10 or ImageNet, would significantly strengthen the claims. Specifically, I would like to see experiments similar to Figures 2 and 3, but directly comparing the performance (e.g., FID score, Inception Score) of perturbed and non-perturbed GANs across different architectures and hyperparameters.

Furthermore, the connection between the proposed perturbation and existing regularization techniques like dropout or Bernoulli perturbation is not fully explored. The authors acknowledge this, and I agree it's a crucial point. A discussion of how the proposed perturbation relates to these techniques – are they fundamentally the same, or does the perturbation offer a unique advantage in the context of GAN training? – would be highly valuable. Is the proposed perturbation a specific instance of a more general principle?

Finally, could the authors elaborate on the choice of noise distribution for the perturbation? While Gaussian noise is a reasonable starting point, how sensitive are the results to the choice of distribution (e.g., uniform, Laplacian)? Does the optimal distribution depend on the specific dataset or GAN architecture?
