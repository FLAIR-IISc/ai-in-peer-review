## Review of "Towards Understanding GAN Training Dynamics"

This paper tackles a fundamental problem in the field of generative modeling: the notorious difficulty in training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). It moves beyond heuristic approaches by providing a rigorous theoretical analysis of the instability and saturation issues that plague GAN training. The authors identify the role of asymmetric divergences and low-dimensional manifolds in these problems, and propose a smoothing-based approach as a potential solution. This is a strong theoretical contribution, asking important questions and providing insightful answers, particularly through the use of analysis and differential topology concepts. The proposed solution offers a practical avenue for reducing instability, and the paper’s exploration of divergences and their properties is valuable.

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant step forward in understanding GAN training dynamics, and the theoretical insights are compelling. However, the paper’s length and technical depth currently hinder its accessibility. 

The core strength of the paper lies in its theoretical rigor. The analysis of divergences, the identification of perfect discriminators, and the subsequent exploration of gradient behavior are all well-motivated and contribute significantly to the field. The proposed smoothing technique, while needing further exploration, is grounded in the theoretical analysis and offers a promising direction. The inclusion of targeted experiments to validate the theoretical claims is also commendable.

However, the paper suffers from excessive length and technical detail. Section 2.1, in particular, feels overly verbose and could be significantly condensed. Moving some of the more detailed proofs to the appendix would improve readability. Several minor issues need addressing: the clarification regarding `P_g` being smaller than the data distribution is crucial, as is correcting the phrasing “for not generating plausibly looking pictures.” The authors should also note the greater generality of Lemma 1, cite references for Theorem 2.1 and Lemma 2, remind the reader of the domain of `p(z)` in Theorem 2.4, and specify the domains of random variables. Finally, the numbering of theorems is confusing and needs clarification, and typos (e.g., “relly”) should be corrected. The clarification regarding closed manifolds having boundaries in Theorem 2.2 is also needed. The reference to Theorem 1.3 in Corollary 2.1 needs to be corrected.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost of implementing the proposed smoothing technique? How does it scale with the dimensionality of the data and the size of the training set?
*   The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis. Do you have any preliminary empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the smoothing technique on standard GAN benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA)?
*   Regarding the choice of noise distribution in Section 3, how sensitive are the results to the variance of the noise? Is there an optimal range for the variance that balances smoothing and preservation of the original data distribution?
