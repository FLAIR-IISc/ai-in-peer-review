

This paper introduces a valuable testbed for systematically evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically addressing the critical gap between chit-chat success and goal-directed performance. The authors propose five well-designed tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that break down complex dialog capabilities into manageable subtasks, enabling reproducible comparisons and granular error analysis. They demonstrate the utility of their framework by evaluating multiple models (rule-based systems, classical IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks) across synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset), providing both per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The testbed fills a significant gap in the literature by offering a controlled, reproducible evaluation framework for goal-oriented dialog systems, which has been lacking despite the growing interest in end-to-end approaches. The paper provides scientifically rigorous evidence that while end-to-end models can learn non-trivial operations (like API calls and entity manipulation), they still fall short of achieving complete dialog success, particularly in interpreting knowledge base results to present options to users.

The paper's strengths include clear task definitions, thoughtful dataset construction with OOV challenges, and thorough evaluation across multiple model architectures. The authors effectively demonstrate that their synthetic testbed serves as a valid proxy for real-world performance, with consistent relative performance patterns across datasets. Their analysis of where end-to-end models succeed and fail provides crucial insights for the field.

For improvement, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the match type features specifically enable OOV handling beyond just mentioning their importance. The connection between the synthetic tasks and the specific challenges of real-world dialog systems could also be strengthened.

I have three questions to clarify the work: First, how would you quantify the difference in difficulty between the synthetic tasks and the real-world concierge data, particularly regarding the variability in user requests? Second, why do Tasks 3 and 5 remain challenging even with match type features, and what specific aspects of these tasks seem most difficult for current models? Third, given the authors' focus on evaluation rather than improvement, how do you envision the community building upon this testbed to drive future progress in end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems?
