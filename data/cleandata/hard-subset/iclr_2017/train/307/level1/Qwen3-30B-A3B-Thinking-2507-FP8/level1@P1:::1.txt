

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by proposing a well-designed testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. The authors correctly identify a critical gap in the literature: while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their performance in goal-oriented domains (such as restaurant reservations) remains unclear. The authors address this by creating a set of five tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that systematically break down the goal-oriented conversation into specific subtasks requiring manipulation of both natural language and symbols from a knowledge base. These tasks test crucial capabilities like issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing extra information, and conducting full dialogs. The paper demonstrates that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations in these tasks, though they still fall short in certain aspects. The authors validate their findings on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service) to confirm that the synthetic testbed provides a good proxy for real-world performance. The dataset, code, and detailed task definitions are made publicly available, promoting reproducibility and further research in this important area.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a significant contribution by creating a well-motivated, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings. The problem is well-defined and addresses a genuine gap in the literature—there is currently no standardized way to systematically evaluate these systems in goal-oriented contexts, making it difficult to compare approaches or understand where they truly excel or fail.

The experimental design is scientifically rigorous. The authors carefully create synthetic data with controlled variables, including an OOV test set to evaluate generalization to unseen entities. They compare multiple baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) across different tasks and datasets, and provide detailed error analysis. The key finding—that Memory Networks can learn to track implicit dialog states and manipulate symbols but struggle with interpreting API results to display options to users—is both novel and valuable for the field.

The paper also makes a strong case for the practical utility of their testbed by demonstrating that patterns observed on synthetic data correlate with performance on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and concierge data), suggesting their testbed is a valid evaluation proxy. This is particularly important as it addresses a key concern about the relevance of synthetic benchmarks for real-world applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

- **Generalizability Discussion**: While the paper focuses on restaurant reservation, the authors could provide more concrete suggestions for how their testbed could be adapted to other domains (e.g., travel booking, customer service). This would better demonstrate the broader applicability of their approach.

- **Limitations of Synthetic Data**: The authors briefly discuss the synthetic nature of their data but could elaborate on specific ways in which their synthetic data might differ from real human conversations (e.g., in terms of language diversity, error patterns, or conversational flow) and how these differences might affect model performance.

- **Comparative Analysis**: While the paper compares multiple models, it would be beneficial to include a more detailed comparison to recent end-to-end dialog models beyond Memory Networks (e.g., those using transformers or more advanced attention mechanisms) to better position their findings within the current state of the art.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that OOV performance improvements with match type features are due to better handling of known words, not OOV words themselves. Could the authors elaborate on why OOV words don't benefit from these features (beyond just not having embeddings), and whether this limitation could be addressed in future work?

2. In the Concierge dataset, API calls are not always used (e.g., operators might not use Yelp or OpenTable). How did the authors handle these cases in their evaluation, and how might this variability affect the model's ability to learn from this dataset compared to the more structured synthetic tasks?

3. The paper notes that Memory Networks with match type features perform worse on Task 2 (updating API calls) compared to without these features. Could the authors provide more insight into why this might be the case, and whether this is a general pattern or specific to Task 2?
