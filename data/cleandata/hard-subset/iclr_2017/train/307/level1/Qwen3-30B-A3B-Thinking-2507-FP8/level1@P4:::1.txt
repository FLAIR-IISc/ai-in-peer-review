

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contribution
This paper proposes a systematic testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications. The authors design five specific tasks within a restaurant reservation domain that require manipulating natural language and symbols (API calls), tracking dialog state, and interpreting knowledge base results. They create a simulation to generate synthetic data for these tasks, validate their findings on real-world datasets from the Dialog State Tracking Challenge (DSTC2) and an online concierge service, and compare multiple models (rule-based systems, classical IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks). The contribution is a well-designed testbed that enables reproducible evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings, addressing a clear gap in the literature where current evaluation methods for end-to-end models do not adequately assess their capabilities in goal-directed conversations.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a well-designed, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, with scientifically rigorous empirical validation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper is well-motivated and addresses a clear gap in the literature: while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat dialog, their performance in goal-oriented settings is unclear due to inadequate evaluation methods. The authors position their work as a systematic testbed, similar to bAbI for question answering, which enables reproducible comparison of models. The testbed design is thoughtful, with tasks that isolate specific capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog (e.g., issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information).

The methodology is scientifically rigorous. The authors create a simulation with a defined knowledge base, generate synthetic data with controlled properties (including out-of-vocabulary entities), and validate results on real-world datasets. They evaluate multiple models with appropriate baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) using both per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics. The ablation study (with and without match type features) provides valuable insights into model limitations. The results are clearly presented with appropriate statistical measures, and the findings are consistently replicated across synthetic and real-world datasets, confirming that the testbed serves as a valid proxy for real-world performance.

The paper demonstrates that Memory Networks can learn to perform non-trivial operations (issuing API calls, handling OOV entities) but still fall short in interpreting KB results to present options to users, which is a critical capability for goal-oriented dialog. This finding is significant as it provides a clear roadmap for future research.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could provide more detail on the limitations of the simulation (e.g., how the synthetic data might differ from real user behavior in terms of language patterns or conversational flow).
- The description of the Memory Networks implementation in Appendix A is quite detailed; the main text could briefly summarize key architectural choices to improve readability.
- The discussion of how the testbed could be extended to other domains beyond restaurant reservation would strengthen the paper's broader impact statement.
- The paper could elaborate on potential directions for improving current end-to-end models based on the error analysis provided.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How do you think the testbed could be extended to handle more complex user requests or multi-domain interactions beyond the restaurant reservation scenario?
- The paper identifies Task 3 (displaying options) as particularly challenging. Could you elaborate on the specific failure modes of the Memory Networks in this task, and whether these failures are related to the model architecture or data limitations?
- Given that the per-dialog accuracy remains low despite promising per-response accuracy, what specific model modifications or training strategies would you recommend to address this gap?
