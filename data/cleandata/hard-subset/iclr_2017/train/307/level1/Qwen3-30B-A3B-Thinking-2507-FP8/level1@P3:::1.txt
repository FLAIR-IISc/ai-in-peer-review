

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of conversational AI by proposing a systematic testbed to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors identify a critical gap in the literature: while end-to-end neural dialog models have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their performance in goal-oriented applications (requiring complex operations like API calls, KB querying, and result interpretation) remains unclear. To address this, they design a set of five structured tasks within a restaurant reservation domain, grounded in a knowledge base, that test specific capabilities of dialog systems. The testbed is designed to be reproducible, lightweight, and to enable error analysis by breaking down the goal-oriented conversation into component subtasks. The authors validate their testbed by showing that results on their synthetic tasks correlate with performance on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), demonstrating that the synthetic testbed is a useful proxy for real-world evaluation. Their key contribution is not to improve state-of-the-art for restaurant booking, but to provide a standardized benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

The paper makes a compelling case for the need for systematic evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, and provides a well-designed testbed that addresses this need. The most compelling reason for acceptance is that the authors successfully demonstrate that their testbed can systematically evaluate end-to-end models while providing meaningful error analysis, and that results on the synthetic tasks correlate with performance on real-world data. This represents a significant step forward in the evaluation of conversational agents.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The authors correctly identify that existing evaluation methods for end-to-end dialog systems (primarily chit-chat focused) do not adequately test the capabilities required for goal-oriented applications. Their justification for the testbed's design is strong, as they explicitly address the need for reproducibility, the importance of breaking down complex dialog capabilities into testable subtasks, and the necessity of validating the testbed against real-world data.

The methodology is scientifically rigorous. The authors carefully construct five distinct tasks that test specific dialog capabilities (issuing API calls, updating API calls, displaying options, providing information, and full dialogs), with a clear rationale for each. They design the testbed to include Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) scenarios to test generalization capabilities, which is particularly important for evaluating real-world applicability. The evaluation metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy) are well-chosen and directly address the goal-oriented nature of the tasks.

The empirical results are compelling and well-analyzed. The authors compare multiple model architectures (rule-based, IR methods, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) across all tasks, with ablation studies for match type features. The key finding that Memory Networks can learn to track implicit dialog state and manipulate symbols (e.g., issuing API calls) but struggle with interpreting KB results (e.g., displaying options) is well-supported by the data. Crucially, they demonstrate that the synthetic testbed results correlate with performance on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge), validating the testbed's usefulness as a proxy for real-world evaluation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper makes a strong contribution, a few improvements would enhance its impact. First, the authors could provide more detailed analysis of *why* Memory Networks fail on tasks like displaying options (Task 3) - is it the model architecture, training data limitations, or task complexity? A deeper error analysis would help guide future research. Second, the paper could more explicitly discuss the limitations of their testbed: for example, the restaurant reservation domain is quite constrained, and it's unclear how well the testbed would generalize to other domains (e.g., travel booking, customer service). Third, the authors might consider discussing how their testbed could be integrated with reinforcement learning frameworks, which are increasingly popular in dialogue systems.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The authors demonstrate that their synthetic testbed correlates with real-world performance, but they do not provide a detailed comparison of the specific error patterns between synthetic and real data. Could you elaborate on which types of errors are most common in both settings, and whether the synthetic testbed helps identify the same failure modes as real data?

2. The paper mentions that Memory Networks with match type features improve OOV performance, but the improvement is limited (e.g., on Task 4, performance increases from 58.2% to 67.3% per-response accuracy with match type features). What are the fundamental limitations of the current model architecture that prevent it from fully solving these tasks, and what architectural changes would you hypothesize could overcome these limitations?

3. The authors note that the rule-based system performs poorly on the real DSTC2 data (40.7% per-response accuracy), but the Memory Networks outperform it. However, they don't provide a detailed comparison of the *types* of errors made by the rule-based system versus the neural model. Could you share insights about how the error profiles differ between these approaches?
