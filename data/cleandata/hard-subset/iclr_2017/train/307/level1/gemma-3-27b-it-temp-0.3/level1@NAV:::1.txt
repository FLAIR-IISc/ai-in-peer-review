## Review of "Breaking Down the Challenge of End-to-End Dialog Systems"

This paper presents a valuable contribution by proposing a testbed for systematically evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors rightly point out the limitations of directly applying successes from chit-chat domains to more complex, task-oriented conversations, and aim to provide a controlled environment for analysis. The creation of five synthetic tasks, along with evaluation on existing datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset), is a strong point, offering both reproducibility and a bridge to real-world performance. The paper clearly articulates the motivation, highlighting the need to understand where end-to-end systems fall short compared to traditional methods.

**Reject.** While the work is promising and well-motivated, the results presented do not demonstrate a significant advancement in the field, and the performance of the Memory Network model, even with the proposed enhancements, remains limited. The core issue is that while the testbed is well-designed, the performance gap between the proposed model and a perfect rule-based system (on the synthetic tasks) is substantial, suggesting fundamental limitations in the current approach. The paper primarily *demonstrates* the testbed, rather than *leveraging* it to achieve a breakthrough in end-to-end dialog.

The authors convincingly show that the proposed tasks are challenging, and the Memory Network can learn some non-trivial operations. However, the low per-dialog accuracy across most tasks indicates a significant struggle with maintaining context and completing the overall goal. The addition of match-type features improves performance, particularly on out-of-vocabulary entities, but doesn’t fully address the core issue of dialog completion. The consistency of results across the synthetic tasks, DSTC2, and the concierge dataset is encouraging, but doesn't change the fundamental conclusion about the current limitations of the approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** A more detailed ablation study of the match-type features would be beneficial. How much does each type of feature contribute?
*   **Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis of the model's failures would be valuable. What specific types of errors are most common? Are they related to KB interaction, dialog state tracking, or natural language understanding?
*   **Model Capacity:** Was the capacity of the Memory Network (embedding dimension, number of hops, hidden layer sizes) adequately explored? Could a larger model achieve better performance?
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the rule-based system and IR methods are reasonable baselines, comparing against more recent and sophisticated end-to-end models (e.g., Transformers) would provide a more comprehensive assessment.
*   **Clarification on KB Access:** How is the KB accessed during inference? Is it a single API call, or can the model make multiple queries?
