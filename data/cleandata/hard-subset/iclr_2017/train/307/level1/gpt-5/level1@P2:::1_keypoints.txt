- Proposes a lightweight, reproducible restaurant-booking testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog, decomposed into five synthetic tasks plus two real datasets (DSTC2 conversion, Concierge).  
- Evaluates IR, supervised embeddings, and MemN2N with new “match-type” features; reports per-response and per-dialog metrics focused on goal completion.  
- Main finding: MemN2N outperforms baselines and learns API issuance/updates and entity handling with match types, but fails at KB reasoning/sorting and achieves low per-dialog success.  
- Synthetic task difficulty patterns transfer to DSTC2 and Concierge, supporting the suite as a useful proxy; data and generator will be released (Concierge excluded).  
- Decision: Accept — fills an important gap by providing a clear, reproducible benchmark and standardized evaluation for goal-oriented dialog.  
- Empirical analysis is thorough and insightful, identifying strengths/weaknesses of end-to-end methods and showing match-type features materially improve OOV performance.  
- Paper tackles whether end-to-end neural dialog systems can handle state tracking and KB manipulation — a timely, significant question.  
- Well positioned relative to prior work (chit-chat datasets, bAbI QA, POMDP, DSTC); extends bAbI-style tasks with dialog management, API calls, and KB use.  
- Results support claims: tasks isolate competencies, metrics reveal high per-response accuracy does not guarantee dialog success, and comparisons are consistent across datasets.  
- Match-type features demonstrably improve T4 and OOV performance; ablations show multiple hops aid iterative reasoning.  
- Caveats: templated language, deterministic bot question order, and ranking-based evaluation simplify realism; authors acknowledge these trade-offs and triangulate with real data.  
- Suggest increasing linguistic variability, non-deterministic slot request policies, and injecting noise (typos/disfluencies).  
- Recommend adding sequence-generation/copy/pointer baselines to compare with match-type features and test generalization to unseen entities.  
- Ask for deeper failure analysis: fine-grained error taxonomy, attention visualizations tied to errors, and per-turn confusion statistics (esp. for T3/T5).  
- Request more statistical rigor: confidence intervals, significance tests, and variance across random seeds given small training sets.  
- Clarify OOV protocol: specify exactly what KB/entity info models see at training and consider truly unseen surface-form OOV settings.  
- Evaluate candidate set construction impact: pooling candidates from all tasks/splits may bias ranking—test task-specific or varied candidate pools and sensitivity to pool size.  
- Release expectations: provide code, data-generation scripts, and DSTC2 conversion; if Concierge cannot be released, offer an anonymized synthetic subset to emulate it.  
- Questions for authors: details on OOV training access; sensitivity to deterministic dialog policies; diagnostics for T3 sorting failures; effects of restricting candidate sets; DSTC2 conversion noise quantification; match-type vs copy architectures; and domain transfer experiments (e.g., hotels).
