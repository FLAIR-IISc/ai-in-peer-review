Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces a lightweight, reproducible testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog in the restaurant reservation domain. It decomposes the overall task into five controllable subtasks (API call issuing, updating, option display with sorting, providing KB-derived info, and full dialogs), plus a converted DSTC2 task (Task 6) and an additional evaluation on a real, proprietary concierge dataset.
- The testbed is explicitly designed to probe capabilities that go beyond chit-chat: implicit dialog state tracking, KB grounding, symbolic manipulation (including sorting), and handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) entities. An OOV split tests generalization to unseen entities via access to a KB.
- Extensive baselines are provided: rule-based, TF-IDF, nearest-neighbor retrieval, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (MemN2N) with and without “match type” features that incorporate KB-aware exact-match signals. Evaluation is conducted in a ranking setting with per-response and per-dialog accuracy, the latter reflecting true goal completion.
- Results show MemN2N outperform classical IR and supervised embeddings, learning non-trivial behaviors (e.g., issuing/updating API calls), and match types substantially improve OOV handling and Task 4 (KB lookup answers). However, per-dialog accuracy remains low on harder tasks (e.g., displaying sorted options), highlighting current limits. Similar relative performance patterns hold on DSTC2 and the concierge dataset, supporting the testbed’s relevance.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A much-needed, carefully designed and reproducible benchmark that isolates key competencies for goal-oriented dialog, with meaningful OOV and KB grounding challenges.
- Solid and transparent empirical study across strong baselines, with clear, interpretable metrics and validation on real data, likely to catalyze progress and enable fair comparisons.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper clearly targets the gap between chit-chat end-to-end systems and goal-oriented dialog where state tracking, KB queries, and symbolic manipulation are essential. Prior resources either focus on state tracking (not end-to-end), are noisy/proprietary, or lack reproducibility. The modular tasks, OOV split, and KB grounding are well conceived and address a real evaluation need.
- Placement in literature: The work connects to bAbI tasks (QA), DSTC, and data-driven dialog, and fairly positions MemN2N against IR and supervised embedding baselines. The discussion of limitations of slot-filling/POMDP approaches and challenges in evaluating chit-chat is apt.
- Empirical support: The results substantiate claims that end-to-end models can handle some goal-oriented behaviors (Tasks 1–2), struggle with others (Tasks 3,5), and benefit from KB-aware type features, especially for OOV. Validation on DSTC2 and a real concierge dataset strengthens external validity; the similar relative ordering across methods is convincingly shown. Hyperparameters and ablations (number of hops; effect of match types) add credibility.
- Rigor and transparency: Clear metrics (per-response and per-dialog), explicit candidate ranking setup, OOV protocol, and availability of data for Tasks 1–6 make the study reproducible. The rule-based upper bound for synthetic tasks clarifies ceiling performance.

Caveats:
- The simulator uses deterministic bot behavior and template-based language, which can induce overfitting and may not reflect real linguistic variability.
- Evaluation is ranking-based rather than generation; this sidesteps response realization and may inflate perceived capability.
- The match-type mechanism presupposes access to a typed KB and hand-enumerated types; generality to new slots/types is unclear.
- Limited analysis of statistical significance, detailed error breakdown, and sensitivity to training data size/paraphrase diversity.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Expand error analysis: quantify which dialog steps dominate failures in Tasks 3 and 5 (e.g., sorting errors vs. grounding vs. state tracking). Provide breakdowns of per-turn accuracy within full dialogs.
- Robustness studies: vary simulator paraphrase diversity, introduce stochastic bot policies and noise (ASR-like errors), and report performance vs. training size to assess sample efficiency.
- Baselines: include competitive generative models with copy/pointer mechanisms (pointer-softmax, copy-attention) and entity-aware seq2seq; these are likely strong on OOV and KB grounding.
- Beyond ranking: complement with a generative evaluation and report success rate under constrained decoding; consider human evaluation on a subset to check response appropriateness.
- Generality: discuss extending match types to unseen slot types and multi-domain settings; consider a multi-domain variant (e.g., hotels + restaurants) to test scalability.
- DSTC2 conversion: add details on conversion choices (e.g., ratings injection) and potential noise they introduce; consider releasing conversion scripts.
- Candidate set sensitivity: report how accuracy scales with candidate set size and composition.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set: What is the average and maximum size of the candidate set used for ranking? How sensitive are the reported accuracies to candidate set expansion or pruning?
- Sorting in Task 3: Do models actually learn sorting or exploit artifacts (e.g., positional priors)? Could you report performance on a variant without sorting or with randomized rating tie-breaks?
- OOV assumptions: Do match type features require all entities to be present and typed in the KB at test time? How would the model behave with truly unseen strings not in the KB (e.g., novel restaurant names provided by the user)?
- Generalization to new slots/types: Can the match-type mechanism scale without manual addition of type tokens? Any evidence from experiments with additional slot types?
- Learning curves: How do per-dialog and per-response accuracies evolve with more training data and more paraphrase templates?
- DSTC2 conversion: How often do the injected ratings alter the original dialog flow, and could that bias evaluation of option presentation?
- Failure modes: In Tasks 5 and 6, what proportion of failures come from incorrect API arguments, misinterpretation of KB outputs, or wrong dialog management actions?
- Generative models: Did you try (or can you report plans to try) copy-augmented seq2seq baselines? If not, what are the anticipated challenges within this testbed?

Overall, this is a timely, impactful benchmark contribution with a carefully executed empirical study. Despite limited modeling novelty, the resource and analysis provide clear value to the community.
