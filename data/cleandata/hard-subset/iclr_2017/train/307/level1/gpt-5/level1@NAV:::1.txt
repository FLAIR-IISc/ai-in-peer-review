1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles the challenge of evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, where traditional slot-filling approaches do not scale. It proposes a reproducible testbed in the restaurant reservation domain that decomposes the problem into five tasks (issuing and updating API calls, sorting/displaying options, providing KB-derived facts, and full dialogs), plus two realistic datasets (converted DSTC2 and a human-human concierge set). The authors evaluate strong baselines (IR, supervised embeddings) and an end-to-end Memory Network (with type-matching features for entities), reporting per-response and per-dialog accuracies, including OOV splits to test generalization to unseen entities. The work claims: (i) an open, lightweight benchmark that isolates core capabilities (dialog management, KB use, handling OOV entities), (ii) MemNNs can learn non-trivial operations (API calls, entity manipulation) yet still fall short on full goal completion, and (iii) synthetic tasks reflect performance patterns observed on more realistic data.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) A well-motivated, reusable benchmark that addresses a pressing gap for goal-directed dialog evaluation with strong attention to OOV and KB reasoning; (2) Scientifically careful analysis with multiple baselines, ablations (match-type features, hops), and corroboration on real datasets.

3. Supporting arguments
- The problem is clearly defined and well-placed within the literature, bridging chit-chat success and the unmet needs of transactional tasks. The related work is comprehensive and honest about limitations of existing corpora and evaluations.
- Methodology is transparent: controlled synthetic generation, explicit task design for capability probing, OOV split via disjoint KB halves, and ranking-based evaluation to avoid generation confounds.
- Results support claims: MemNNs outperform IR and supervised embeddings, match-type features markedly improve OOV and KB fact retrieval (Task 4), but per-dialog success remains low for complex tasks (T3/T5), aligning with the stated limitations. The similar relative performance across synthetic tasks, DSTC2, and Concierge strengthens external validity.
- The release of tasks/data promotes reproducibility and community impact.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve; not core to decision)
- Clarify the candidate-response pool: including all utterances from test sets across tasks may inflate realism or introduce leakage; quantify its effect and consider a per-task candidate pool.
- Consider adding copy/pointer mechanisms or program induction baselines that may better handle sorting/displaying options (T3) and OOV entities.
- Provide a deeper error analysis for T3/T5 (e.g., sorting vs. state-tracking vs. stopping criteria).
- Discuss generation-based evaluation and human judgments as complementary to ranking.
- Report statistical significance and confidence intervals.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the determinism of bot prompts in T1/T2? Have you tried non-deterministic prompting orders?
- In DSTC2 conversion, how do ASR noise and inconsistent bot policies impact training targets, and could noisy labels cap performance?
- Does the model truly learn sorting in T3, or exploit artifacts (e.g., rating distributions)? Can you test with adversarially shuffled KB outputs?
- What is the performance impact when restricting the candidate set to only in-domain responses per task at test time?
- Can match-type features operate without full KB access at test time (e.g., partial KB or schema-only), and how does that affect OOV handling?
