### Review of "Towards End-to-End Learning for Dialog Systems"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses a critical gap in the development of goal-oriented dialog systems: the lack of a standardized, reproducible, and analyzable benchmark for training and evaluating end-to-end models. The authors argue that while end-to-end systems have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their ability to handle goal-oriented tasks requiring interaction with knowledge bases (KBs) and implicit state tracking is not well understood.

To this end, the paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It introduces a new public dataset and a suite of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, designed in the spirit of the bAbI tasks. These tasks systematically break down the dialog process to test specific capabilities like issuing API calls, handling user updates, interpreting KB results, and conducting a full conversation.
2.  It proposes a challenging Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) setting to test model generalization to new entities (e.g., restaurant names, cuisine types) not seen during training, a crucial requirement for real-world scalability.
3.  It provides a thorough empirical evaluation of several models on this new testbed, including classical IR methods, supervised embedding models, and an end-to-end Memory Network (MemNN).
4.  It validates the relevance of the synthetic tasks by showing that the relative performance of the tested models is consistent on two real-world datasets: a converted version of the Dialog State Tracking Challenge 2 (DSTC2) dataset and a proprietary dataset from an online concierge service.

The findings indicate that MemNNs, especially when augmented with "match type features" to handle entities, are a promising approach. However, even the best models struggle to achieve perfect dialog completion, highlighting key areas where future research is needed.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant Community Contribution:** The paper provides a much-needed, well-designed, and publicly available benchmark that will enable reproducible research and systematic analysis of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. This is a foundational contribution that can accelerate progress in the field.
2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing appropriate baselines on both synthetic and real-world data. The analysis successfully diagnoses the strengths and weaknesses of current models, substantiating the paper's claims and clearly motivating avenues for future work.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments

The decision to accept is based on the paper's high quality and impact potential.

First, the proposed benchmark is a major strength. The motivation is clear and compelling: existing datasets are often unsuitable for end-to-end learning due to noise, small scale, or lack of public access. The bAbI-style task design is an excellent methodological choice. By deconstructing a complex dialog into sub-tasks (T1-T5), the benchmark allows researchers to move beyond a single, opaque performance metric and instead pinpoint specific model failures. For instance, the results clearly show that models are relatively good at issuing API calls (T1) but fail at interpreting and presenting sorted results from a KB (T3), a crucial insight. The inclusion of an OOV test set is also a critical and well-executed feature, directly addressing the practical challenge of scaling dialog systems to new domains and entities.

Second, the experimental evaluation is thorough and scientifically sound. The authors compare a sensible hierarchy of models, from simple IR baselines to a strong end-to-end architecture (MemNNs). The analysis is nuanced, particularly in its use of both per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The stark difference between these two metrics effectively illustrates the brittleness of current systems, where a single error can cause the entire dialog to fail. This is a vital point for the community to address. Furthermore, the validation on DSTC2 and the Concierge dataset is a crucial component that grounds the synthetic benchmark in reality. The fact that the relative model rankings are preserved on this noisy, human-generated data provides strong evidence that the proposed tasks are a valid and useful proxy for real-world challenges. The introduction of "match type features" is a simple but clever engineering choice that directly addresses a known weakness of embedding models and demonstrably improves performance, adding another layer of value to the empirical study.

In summary, the paper does not just present a new model; it provides the community with a tool to build better models. Its careful design, thorough experiments, and clear analysis make it a standout contribution.

#### 4. Additional Feedback and Suggestions

These points are intended as constructive feedback for further improving the work and are not critical to the acceptance decision.

*   **Generative vs. Ranking Models:** The paper frames the problem as a ranking task over a candidate set. This is a practical and common evaluation setup. However, it would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on how this benchmark could be adapted for generative models. What challenges would arise, and how might evaluation (e.g., using BLEU, or semantic correctness checks) be handled?
*   **Comparison to Other End-to-End Architectures:** Memory Networks are a strong choice for this problem. However, sequence-to-sequence models with attention have also become a dominant paradigm in dialog research. A comparison against a well-tuned Seq2Seq baseline could further strengthen the paper's conclusions about the state of end-to-end models and provide more context for the MemNN results.
*   **Complexity of Rule-Based System:** The rule-based baseline for DSTC2 is an important point of comparison. To better contextualize the trade-off between hand-engineering and end-to-end learning, it would be insightful if the authors could provide an estimate of the human effort required to create this system (e.g., person-hours, number of rules refined).
*   **Task Determinism:** The paper notes that in Task 1, the bot asks for missing information in a deterministic order. This may simplify the task for the model. Have the authors considered a variant with a randomized questioning order to test model robustness to different dialog paths?

#### 5. Questions for Authors

*   The "match type features" are very effective. The paper states that during training, "systems have access to the training examples and both KBs." Does this mean the model has access to the entity list from the OOV KB during training, even though it never sees those entities in training *dialogs*? If so, could you elaborate on how this design choice impacts the interpretation of the OOV results?
*   The results for Task 3 (Displaying options) show that all learning-based models struggle, likely due to the implicit requirement to sort restaurants by rating. This is a classic example of a symbolic operation that is difficult for neural networks to learn. Do you have thoughts on what architectural changes (e.g., an external module, a different attention mechanism) might be necessary for end-to-end models to master such structured operations?
*   In Table 2, the MemNN with match type features shows a slight performance degradation on Task 2 (in-vocabulary) compared to the version without these features (98.6% vs. 99.4%). This is counter-intuitive. Do you have a hypothesis for this small drop in performance?
