This paper introduces a valuable and much-needed testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings. The authors correctly identify that the success of such systems in chit-chat may not translate to transactional dialogs, which require specific skills like querying knowledge bases (KBs) and managing dialog state. To this end, they propose a set of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, designed to systematically diagnose model capabilities such as issuing API calls, handling OOV entities, and using KB query results. The work provides a thorough empirical analysis, benchmarking a Memory Network model against several baselines, and validates its findings on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service). The results compellingly show that while modern end-to-end models are promising, they still fall short of reliable task completion, highlighting key areas for future research.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are: 1) It provides the research community with a well-designed, open-source diagnostic tool to systematically measure progress in goal-oriented dialog, addressing a significant gap in current evaluation resources. 2) The experimental analysis is rigorous and insightful, clearly demonstrating the specific strengths and, more importantly, the critical weaknesses of current end-to-end approaches.

The paper’s main strength lies in its structured approach to evaluation. By breaking down a complex goal-oriented dialog into subtasks (T1-T5), the authors enable a fine-grained error analysis that is often missing in end-to-end evaluations. The inclusion of both per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics is crucial, as it effectively illustrates the core challenge: models can achieve reasonable single-turn accuracy but fail to maintain consistency to complete a full dialog. The experiments on OOV entities and the introduction of "match type features" further strengthen the analysis by tackling a practical and challenging aspect of real-world deployment. The validation on DSTC2 and the Concierge dataset provides strong evidence that the patterns observed in the synthetic tasks are a good proxy for real-world performance.

For improving the paper, I suggest the following:
*   The paper states that candidates are ranked from a set of all bot utterances and API calls across all tasks. A clarification on the size of this candidate set would be helpful to understand the difficulty of the ranking task.
*   The "match type features" are an interesting addition. A brief discussion on their potential generalizability to other domains beyond restaurant reservation would strengthen the paper's broader impact.

I would like the authors to answer the following questions to clarify my understanding:
1.  How sensitive are the models, particularly the Memory Network, to the complexity of the user simulator? Since the simulator uses a fixed set of patterns, do you have a sense of how performance would degrade with increased linguistic variation from the user?
2.  Your results show MemNNs outperform the rule-based system on the noisy DSTC2 data. Could you elaborate on the types of errors the rule-based system makes that the learned model is able to overcome?
