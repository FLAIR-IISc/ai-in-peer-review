This paper introduces a testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors argue that while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat, their performance in goal-oriented tasks remains unclear. They propose a set of five tasks, along with two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), to systematically assess the capabilities of such systems. The authors demonstrate the testbed’s utility by evaluating a Memory Network model, comparing it to a rule-based baseline, and analyzing its strengths and weaknesses.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a valuable contribution in creating a testbed and providing initial results, the findings are not groundbreaking enough to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core argument – that end-to-end models still struggle with goal-oriented dialog – is already well-known in the field, and the paper doesn’t offer a significant advancement in model performance or a novel theoretical insight that justifies publication at this venue. The experiments, while thorough, largely confirm existing knowledge.

The paper’s strength lies in its systematic approach to evaluating end-to-end dialog systems. The breakdown of the goal-oriented task into smaller, more manageable subtasks is a clever way to identify specific areas of weakness. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets strengthens the validity of the findings. The comparison with a rule-based baseline provides a useful benchmark, and the detailed analysis of the Memory Network’s performance (including ablation studies with match-type features) is commendable. The fact that the authors make their data and tasks publicly available is a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.

However, several factors contribute to the rejection. Firstly, the performance of the Memory Network, while better than the baselines, is not particularly impressive. The per-dialog accuracy remains low, indicating significant limitations in handling complex conversational flows. Secondly, the paper doesn’t adequately address the limitations of the Memory Network architecture itself. It's unclear why this specific architecture was chosen, and the authors don't explore alternative end-to-end models that might yield better results. Third, the paper's focus is primarily empirical. While the authors identify areas where end-to-end models struggle, they don’t provide a theoretical explanation for *why* these difficulties arise. The discussion of match-type features is helpful, but it feels somewhat ad hoc, lacking a deeper analysis of the underlying issues.

**Additional Feedback & Questions:**

*   **Model Choice Justification:** Why was the Memory Network specifically chosen as the end-to-end model? Were other architectures considered (e.g., Transformers, LSTMs with attention)? A brief discussion of the rationale for this choice would be helpful.
*   **Error Analysis:** The paper mentions that the Memory Network struggles with interpreting API call results. Could the authors provide more specific examples of these failures? What types of errors are most common (e.g., incorrect restaurant selection, inability to handle ambiguous results)?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the hyperparameters are reported, a sensitivity analysis would be valuable. How do the results change with different learning rates, embedding dimensions, or number of hops in the Memory Network?
*   **KB Representation:** The paper briefly mentions the KB structure. Could the authors elaborate on how the KB is represented to the model? Is it simply a list of facts, or is there a more structured representation (e.g., a graph)? How does the KB representation affect the model's performance?
*   **Generalizability to Other Domains:** The paper focuses on restaurant reservation. How easily could this testbed be adapted to other goal-oriented domains (e.g., booking flights, finding hotels)? What modifications would be necessary?
*   **Per-Dialog Accuracy Interpretation:** The low per-dialog accuracy is a key finding. Is it possible to break down the causes of these failures? Are they concentrated at specific steps in the conversation (e.g., API call formulation, result interpretation)?
*  **Concierge Dataset Details:** The Concierge dataset is mentioned but less detailed. What is the size of the training set? What are the characteristics of the conversations in this dataset (e.g., average length, complexity)? How was the data anonymized, and are there any potential biases introduced by the anonymization process?
