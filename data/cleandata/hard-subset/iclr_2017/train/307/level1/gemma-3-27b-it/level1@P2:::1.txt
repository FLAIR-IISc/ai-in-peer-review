This paper presents a valuable testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors identify a crucial gap in existing research – the lack of standardized benchmarks for assessing these systems beyond chit-chat scenarios – and address it with a well-defined set of tasks and datasets. The focus on manipulating both natural language and symbolic knowledge (KB queries) is particularly insightful, as this highlights the challenges faced by end-to-end models in real-world applications. The comparison to a hand-crafted slot-filling baseline and evaluation on real-world data further strengthens the contribution.

**Reject.** While the paper is well-motivated and presents a solid contribution to the community, the empirical results do not demonstrate a significant breakthrough. The performance of the Memory Networks, while promising, remains substantially below that of traditional slot-filling methods on the DSTC2 dataset, and the per-dialog accuracy across all tasks is consistently low. This suggests that the current end-to-end approach is not yet competitive in this domain, and the paper, while thoroughly analyzing *where* it falls short, doesn’t offer compelling evidence of *how* to overcome those shortcomings. The core claim of the paper – that this testbed is useful for understanding the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end systems – is supported, but the *results* within that testbed don’t offer particularly optimistic conclusions.

Here’s a breakdown of the reasoning:

* **Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem: end-to-end dialog systems haven't translated their success from chit-chat to goal-oriented tasks. The motivation is strong, addressing the limitations of hand-crafted systems and the need for better evaluation benchmarks.
* **Approach & Literature:** The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging the strengths of both traditional POMDP-based systems and recent advancements in neural dialog models. The choice of Memory Networks as a baseline is reasonable, and the introduction of match-type features is a clever attempt to address the challenges of OOV entities.
* **Results & Scientific Rigor:** This is where the paper falls short. While the authors present extensive experimental results, the overall performance of the Memory Networks is disappointing.  The per-dialog accuracy is consistently low, indicating that the system struggles to maintain coherent conversations and achieve the desired goal. The improvements gained from match-type features are modest. The use of multiple datasets (simulated, DSTC2, concierge) strengthens the robustness of the findings, but the consistently low performance raises concerns about the fundamental limitations of the current approach. The detailed analysis in Appendix C regarding hyperparameter tuning adds to the rigor, but doesn’t change the overall conclusion.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Deeper Error Analysis:** While the paper identifies areas where the Memory Networks struggle, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Specifically, what types of errors are most common? Are there specific entities or dialog states that consistently cause problems? Providing concrete examples of failed dialogs and analyzing the underlying reasons for the failures could help guide future research.
* **Comparison to More Advanced Models:**  The Memory Network serves as a good starting point, but it would be valuable to compare the performance of this testbed to more recent and advanced end-to-end dialog models (e.g., Transformers, BART, T5). This would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the current state-of-the-art.
* **Ablation Study:** A more thorough ablation study of the match-type features could provide further insights into their effectiveness. For example, how does performance vary with different types of match-type features or different weighting schemes?
* **Data Analysis:** The paper states the training sets are small (1000 examples). Analyzing the impact of dataset size on performance, and the potential for data augmentation, would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper mentions the per-dialog accuracy is low. Is this a consistent problem across *all* types of goal-oriented tasks, or are some tasks inherently more difficult for end-to-end models?
2.  You introduced match-type features to address OOV issues.  Did you explore other techniques for handling OOV entities, such as subword tokenization or entity embeddings?  If so, what were the results?
3.  What are the limitations of the simulation environment?  How might the results differ if the system were evaluated in a more realistic, human-in-the-loop setting?
4. Given the limitations of the current Memory Network performance, what is your vision for how these types of end-to-end models can be improved to achieve competitive performance in goal-oriented dialog? What specific architectural or training modifications do you believe are most promising?
