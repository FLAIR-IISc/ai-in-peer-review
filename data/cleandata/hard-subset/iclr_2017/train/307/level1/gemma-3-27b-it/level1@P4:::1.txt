This paper introduces a benchmark for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors argue that existing benchmarks are either not reproducible or do not correlate well with human judgment. They propose a set of five tasks, simulated using a knowledge base, designed to test specific capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog, such as API calling, information retrieval, and dialog management. They also evaluate their approach on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service). The authors compare a Memory Network-based system to a hand-crafted slot-filling baseline and several other methods, finding that while end-to-end systems show promise, they still lag behind traditional methods in achieving full dialog success.

**Reject** – The paper presents a valuable contribution in terms of creating a controlled environment for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in the goal-oriented domain. However, the results, while insightful, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at a highly competitive conference like ICLR. The performance of the Memory Network, while outperforming simpler baselines, remains significantly below achieving robust goal completion, and the improvements gained through match type features are incremental rather than transformative. The paper primarily *demonstrates* a testbed rather than *advancing* the state-of-the-art in end-to-end dialog.

The primary supporting argument for rejection is the limited novelty and impact of the results. While the testbed itself is a useful contribution, the experiments conducted on it do not reveal significant breakthroughs. The Memory Network, despite improvements with match type features, still struggles with achieving high per-dialog accuracy, and the insights gained are largely confirmatory of prior knowledge: end-to-end models struggle with complex reasoning and knowledge integration. The incremental improvements observed are not sufficient to justify acceptance in a conference focused on cutting-edge research. The paper convincingly shows the *difficulty* of the task, but doesn’t sufficiently demonstrate progress towards solving it.

Another reason is the relative simplicity of the approach. While the testbed is well-designed, the selected model (Memory Network) is a relatively established architecture. The introduction of match type features is a sensible extension, but lacks significant innovation. The paper does not explore more recent or advanced neural architectures that might be better suited to tackle the challenges identified.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation studies:** A more detailed ablation study of the match type features would be beneficial. It is not entirely clear which aspect of the features (entity type matching vs. OOV handling) contributes most to the observed performance gains.
*   **Error Analysis:** While the paper mentions error analysis by design, a more systematic and detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the Memory Network would be valuable. This could provide insights into areas where the model struggles and guide future research. Specifically, examples illustrating where the model fails in interpreting KB results would be helpful.
*   **Comparison to stronger baselines:** Considering more recent and powerful baselines, such as transformer-based models, would provide a more comprehensive comparison and highlight the limitations of the Memory Network architecture.
*   **Scalability:** The paper focuses on a narrow domain. Discussing the potential challenges and strategies for scaling this approach to more complex domains would strengthen the paper.
*   **Code Release:** Releasing the code and datasets would significantly increase the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights the low per-dialog accuracy. What specific limitations of the Memory Network architecture do you believe are responsible for this low accuracy, and what alternative architectures might address these limitations?
2.  The match type features improve performance, especially on OOV entities. Do you have any insights into *why* these features are effective? Is it simply enabling the model to handle new entities, or is there a more nuanced effect related to the type information?
3.  The concierge dataset is described as noisy. How did you address this noise when converting it into the task format? What impact does the noise in this dataset have on the evaluation results?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., number of hops in the Memory Network)? Did you observe significant variations in performance with different hyperparameter settings?
5.  The simulated environment simplifies the real world. Have you considered introducing more realistic complexities (e.g., ambiguous user requests, incorrect KB entries) into the simulation? How might such complexities affect the performance of the models?
