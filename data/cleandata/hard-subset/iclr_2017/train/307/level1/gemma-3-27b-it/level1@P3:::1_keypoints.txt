- Introduces a useful testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog, focused on restaurant reservation.
- Motivation is clear: end-to-end models work for chit-chat but their KB interaction and structured reasoning remain underexplored.
- Authors design a staged set of tasks of increasing complexity to decompose goal-oriented dialog.
- Uses both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2, concierge) to assess generalizability.
- Comparison to a handcrafted baseline provides a practical benchmark.
- Recommendation: Reject — contribution is valuable but not a substantial research breakthrough.
- Core finding: Memory Networks struggle with goal-oriented dialog beyond basic API calls, which is unsurprising.
- Improvements from “match type features” are incremental and don’t fundamentally solve the shortcomings.
- Memory Networks outperform IR methods but per-dialog accuracy remains consistently low, failing to reliably complete tasks.
- Paper confirms known issues: end-to-end models have trouble with long-term dependencies and coherent dialog state tracking.
- Correlation between synthetic and real data validates the testbed but doesn’t yield novel insights.
- Comparison to rule-based systems is expected and lacks nuanced analysis of differences.
- Strength: systematic evaluation and decomposition of goal-oriented tasks into measurable components.
- Weakness: lacks deeper error analysis to identify specific failure modes and why models fail.
- Suggest exploring alternative architectures (e.g., transformers or dialog-specific trackers).
- Recommend more thorough ablation studies on Memory Network components (hops, embedding sizes).
- Advise better integration of KB methods (knowledge graph embeddings, reasoning mechanisms) rather than superficial use.
- Clarify and better justify the role and design of “match type features” with relation to entity resolution.
- Improve the connection to related work and clearly differentiate this approach from existing goal-oriented dialog methods.
- Questions for authors: provide granular error analysis (where failures occur), compare OOV handling methods, discuss generalization to other domains, and detail which metrics correlate between synthetic and real data.
