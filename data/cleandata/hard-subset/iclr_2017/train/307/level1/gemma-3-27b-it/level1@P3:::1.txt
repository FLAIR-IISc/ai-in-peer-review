This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of dialog systems by introducing a testbed for evaluating end-to-end models in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The motivation is clear: while end-to-end systems have shown promise in chit-chat, their performance in tasks requiring structured reasoning and KB interaction remains largely unexplored. The authors skillfully design a series of tasks, ranging in complexity, that break down the goal-oriented dialog process into manageable components. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2, concierge data) strengthens the work, offering a pathway to assess generalizability. The comparison to a handcrafted baseline provides a useful benchmark.

**Reject.** While the work is well-motivated and the testbed is a significant contribution, the core findings – that end-to-end models (specifically Memory Networks) struggle with goal-oriented dialog beyond basic API calls – are not particularly surprising and don't represent a substantial advancement in the field. The paper feels more like a careful engineering effort demonstrating known limitations rather than a novel research breakthrough. The improvements with "match type features" are incremental and don’t fundamentally address the shortcomings.

The primary support for this decision lies in the limited performance gains achieved by the Memory Networks. While they outperform classical IR methods, their per-dialog accuracy remains consistently low, indicating a failure to reliably complete the overall task. This confirms existing understanding that end-to-end models struggle with long-term dependencies and maintaining a coherent dialog state, especially when requiring multiple steps. The paper doesn’t offer a compelling reason why these limitations *couldn’t* be anticipated. The fact that performance on synthetic data correlates with real data further reinforces this point; it demonstrates the testbed’s validity, but doesn't highlight a novel insight. The comparison with rule-based systems, while expected, doesn't offer a particularly nuanced view of the differences between the two approaches. 

The paper's strength is its systematic approach to evaluation. The decomposition of the goal-oriented task into smaller, measurable components is valuable for future research. However, the absence of a more substantial improvement through the proposed Memory Network architecture or a more in-depth analysis of the failure modes limits the paper’s impact. The paper focuses heavily on *demonstrating* the difficulty, but less on *understanding* why the difficulties arise.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Deeper Error Analysis:** The paper mentions low per-dialog accuracy, but lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the models are failing. Identifying specific error patterns (e.g., incorrectly interpreting API results, failing to update the dialog state, making incorrect assumptions about user preferences) would be invaluable. Visualizing attention weights (as briefly presented in the appendix) for more examples could provide insights.
*   **Exploration of Alternative Architectures:** While Memory Networks are a reasonable starting point, the paper doesn't explore other potentially more suitable architectures, such as transformers or models specifically designed for dialog state tracking (e.g., TRADE).
*   **Ablation Studies:** A more thorough ablation study examining the impact of different components of the Memory Network (e.g., number of hops, embedding dimension) would be beneficial.
*   **Integration of External Knowledge:** The paper mentions the KB, but the integration feels superficial. Exploring methods for more effectively leveraging the KB during the dialog process (e.g., knowledge graph embeddings, reasoning mechanisms) could lead to significant improvements.
*   **Clarify the Role of “Match Type Features”:** While helpful, the explanation of these features feels somewhat ad-hoc. A clearer justification for their effectiveness, potentially linking them to known challenges in entity resolution, would be appreciated.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** While related work is mentioned, a more detailed discussion of existing approaches to goal-oriented dialog, and how this work builds upon or differs from those approaches, would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Beyond the observation that per-dialog accuracy is low, can you provide a more granular analysis of the common errors made by the Memory Networks? Specifically, where in the dialog process are they most likely to fail (e.g., API call generation, interpreting results, updating the dialog state)?
2.  The “match type features” seem to improve performance on OOV entities. Have you considered other methods for handling OOV entities, such as using character-level embeddings or subword tokenization? If so, how did those methods compare to the “match type features”?
3.  The paper focuses on a restaurant reservation domain. Do you anticipate that the findings would generalize to other goal-oriented tasks? If not, what specific challenges would need to be addressed to adapt the testbed and the models to different domains?
4.   The paper mentions that the results observed in the synthetic data correlate to the results in the real data. Can you expand on the specific metrics where correlation is strong/weak? Is there a specific type of error that appears in the synthetic data but is not observed in the real data?
