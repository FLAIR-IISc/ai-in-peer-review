## Review of "Breaking Down the Challenge of End-to-End Dialog Systems"

This paper presents a valuable contribution by introducing a testbed for systematically evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors identify the gap between promising results in chit-chat and the challenges of applying end-to-end models to tasks requiring manipulation of both language and structured knowledge. The proposed task suite, grounded in a KB and incorporating API calls, is a well-motivated approach to dissect the capabilities (and shortcomings) of these systems. The comparison to a hand-crafted baseline and evaluation on real-world data further strengthens the work.

**Reject** – While the paper presents a solid methodology and valuable insights, the results, while informative, don’t demonstrate a breakthrough in performance. The core finding – that end-to-end systems still struggle with goal-oriented dialog despite showing promise on sub-tasks – is known and the paper primarily *confirms* this rather than offering a substantial advance in overcoming it.

The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the motivation and experimental setup. The creation of a controlled environment with well-defined tasks is a significant strength, allowing for a focused analysis of system performance. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets adds credibility to the findings. The ablation study with match type features is also well-executed, highlighting the importance of handling out-of-vocabulary entities. However, the improvement offered by Memory Networks, even with the added features, is incremental and doesn't position them as a viable replacement for traditional slot-filling systems in this domain. The per-dialog accuracy remains low, indicating a fundamental limitation in achieving complete task success.

To improve the paper, the authors could: 1) Investigate more sophisticated model architectures beyond Memory Networks. 2) Explore methods for improving long-term dependency modeling to address the low per-dialog accuracy. 3) Provide a more in-depth error analysis of the tasks where the models fail, pinpointing the specific linguistic or reasoning challenges.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Given the consistently low per-dialog accuracy, what specific types of errors are most prevalent? Are these errors related to KB interaction, dialog state tracking, or natural language understanding?
*   The paper mentions the possibility of using the testbed to train end-to-end systems. Have you experimented with using this framework for reinforcement learning or other training paradigms to further improve performance?
*   What are the limitations of the synthetic data generation process, and how might these limitations impact the generalizability of the results to other goal-oriented domains?
*   How sensitive are the results to the size of the training data? Would scaling up the datasets significantly improve the performance of the Memory Networks?
