The paper proposes a testbed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, specifically in the context of restaurant reservation. The authors design a set of five tasks that require manipulating sentences and symbols to conduct conversations, issue API calls, and use the outputs of such calls. They also evaluate their system on data from the second Dialog State Tracking Challenge and an online concierge service. The paper aims to break down the strengths and shortcomings of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications.

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons for this choice: (1) the paper proposes a well-motivated and well-designed testbed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, and (2) the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their system using various models and datasets, including a comparison with a hand-crafted slot-filling baseline.

The paper is well-motivated because it addresses a significant limitation of traditional dialog systems, which require a lot of domain-specific handcrafting. The authors argue that end-to-end dialog systems can escape this limitation, but their performance in goal-oriented settings is unclear. The proposed testbed is designed to break down the strengths and shortcomings of end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented applications, making it easier to address these questions.

The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their system using various models, including Memory Networks, classical information retrieval models, and supervised embedding models. They also evaluate their system on multiple datasets, including the Dialog State Tracking Challenge and an online concierge service. The results show that Memory Networks outperform other models in most tasks, but still fall short in some key areas, such as interpreting knowledge about entities from returned API calls.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the errors made by their system. While they mention that the system fails to interpret knowledge about entities from returned API calls, they do not provide a detailed breakdown of the types of errors made. This would be useful in understanding the limitations of the system and identifying areas for future improvement.
