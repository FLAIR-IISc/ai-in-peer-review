### Review

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a new suite of tasks and datasets designed to benchmark end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue, correctly, that a major impediment to progress in this area is the lack of standardized, reproducible, and diagnostic evaluation frameworks. To address this, they propose a set of five synthetically generated tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, grounded in a knowledge base (KB). Inspired by the bAbI tasks, this suite is designed to test distinct capabilities required for goal-oriented conversation, such as managing dialog state, issuing API calls, and reasoning over KB results. The paper also introduces an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) setting to test generalization to unseen entities. To validate the relevance of their synthetic benchmark, the authors supplement it with two datasets derived from real-world dialogs (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), showing that performance patterns on the synthetic tasks are a good proxy for performance on real data. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of several baselines, including IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, demonstrating the utility of their testbed and analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of current end-to-end approaches. The core finding is that while models like Memory Networks are promising, they still fall short on tasks requiring reasoning over retrieved knowledge, highlighting clear directions for future work.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper represents a significant and bold contribution to the field of conversational AI. My primary reasons for acceptance are:

1.  **A Novel Conceptual Framework for Evaluation:** The paper's most important contribution is not merely the dataset itself, but the bAbI-style conceptualization of breaking down a complex, goal-oriented dialog problem into a series of prerequisite sub-tasks. This is a bold and highly valuable approach that shifts the focus from chasing a single metric on a monolithic, noisy dataset to enabling a fine-grained, diagnostic analysis of model capabilities. This framework allows researchers to pinpoint exactly where their models are failing, which is essential for making targeted progress.
2.  **Addressing a Critical Need with a High-Quality Resource:** The community has been hampered by a lack of good, open, and reproducible benchmarks for goal-oriented dialog. This paper directly and effectively fills this void. The inclusion of the OOV split is particularly insightful, as it forces the community to tackle the critical real-world problem of generalizing to new entities. By providing baselines and demonstrating that the synthetic tasks correlate with performance on real data, the authors have created a resource that is not only well-designed but also immediately useful.

**Supporting Arguments:**

This work is exactly the kind of foundational contribution that I believe is crucial for driving meaningful progress. Instead of presenting an incremental improvement on an existing model, the authors have taken a step back to address a more fundamental methodological problem in the field. The design of the tasks is thoughtful, isolating key challenges like state tracking (T1, T2), KB interaction (T3, T4), and their integration (T5). The results clearly illustrate the value of this approach: we can see that Memory Networks are capable of learning implicit state tracking but struggle with sorting and presenting options from a KB query. This is a far more useful insight than a single F1 score.

While the use of synthetic, template-based language is a simplification, it is a necessary one to create a controlled and interpretable testbed. The authors are transparent about this and wisely bolster their claims by correlating the results with performance on the much noisier DSTC2 and Concierge datasets. This shows that the skills tested in the controlled environment are indeed relevant to real-world challenges, making the benchmark a valid and effective proxy. The idea of building a testbed to gauge the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end systems in a domain where traditional methods excel is a powerful one, and it is executed very well here. The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the resource provided will undoubtedly spur new research.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

These points are intended to be constructive and are not critical to my decision to accept the paper.

*   **Discussion on Language Variation:** The template-based nature of the user simulator is a key design choice for control and reproducibility. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on the "sim-to-real" gap in terms of linguistic diversity. What specific challenges (e.g., paraphrasing, coreference, colloquialisms) are abstracted away in the current tasks, and how might these be incorporated in future versions of the benchmark?
*   **The Nature of "Match Type Features":** The match type features are an effective mechanism for handling OOV entities and highlight a key weakness of pure embedding-based approaches. This feels like a well-motivated, engineered feature. A short discussion on whether this points to a fundamental need for hybrid neuro-symbolic reasoning in such tasks could be very thought-provoking for the reader and could help frame future research directions.
*   **Beyond Ranking:** The evaluation is performed in a ranking setting over a fixed set of candidates. This is a standard and reasonable choice, but a comment on how these findings might translate to a fully generative setting would be beneficial. Does solving these tasks in a ranking paradigm provide strong evidence that a model could generate the correct response from scratch?

**Questions for Authors:**

*   The user simulator has deterministic elements (e.g., the order of questions in Task 1). Have you considered the impact of this determinism? How would introducing more stochasticity into the user or bot behaviors affect the difficulty of the tasks and the performance of the models?
*   Your central claim that the synthetic benchmark is a good proxy for real-world performance is supported by the consistent relative rankings of the models. Could you elaborate on what you believe are the primary sources of the large *absolute* performance drop on the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets? Is it primarily vocabulary size and language noise, or are there structural or pragmatic differences in the dialogs themselves that the synthetic tasks do not capture?
