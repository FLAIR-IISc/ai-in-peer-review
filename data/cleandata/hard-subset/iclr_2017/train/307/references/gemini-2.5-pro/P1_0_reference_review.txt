### Summary

This paper introduces a valuable new open-source benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while end-to-end models have shown promise, their effectiveness in goal-oriented settings is not well understood, partly due to a lack of suitable, reproducible evaluation frameworks. To address this, they propose a suite of tasks within the restaurant reservation domain. This includes five synthetically generated tasks designed to test specific capabilities in a controlled manner, such as issuing API calls, handling updates, interpreting KB results, and dealing with out-of-vocabulary (OOV) entities. The contribution is strengthened by also evaluating models on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary "Concierge" dataset) to demonstrate that findings on the synthetic tasks are a good proxy for real-world performance. The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation of several models, from classical IR baselines to supervised embeddings and Memory Networks, and introduce a "match type feature" to help models generalize to unseen entities. Their key finding is that while per-response accuracy for the best models is encouraging, per-dialog accuracy remains very low, highlighting the significant challenges that remain in building reliable end-to-end goal-oriented agents.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant Contribution to the Field:** The paper addresses a critical and well-motivated need for a standardized, reproducible, and analyzable benchmark for goal-oriented dialog. The public release of the datasets and tasks is a substantial contribution that will enable more rigorous and comparable research in this important area.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Design:** The methodology is scientifically rigorous. The breakdown of the problem into distinct sub-tasks, the inclusion of a challenging OOV setting, and the validation on real-world data provide a comprehensive and insightful analysis of the capabilities and limitations of current end-to-end models. This work not only provides a benchmark but also yields valuable insights into where these models succeed and fail.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper is the creation and validation of a much-needed public resource. The authors correctly identify the shortcomings of existing evaluation methods, which often lack reproducibility (e.g., live user studies) or are not tailored for end-to-end systems. The bAbI-style design, which decomposes the complex goal-oriented dialog problem into five targeted tasks, is an excellent choice. It allows researchers to perform fine-grained error analysis and focus on specific weaknesses, such as whether a model struggles with state tracking (Tasks 1-2) versus interpreting knowledge base results (Tasks 3-4).

The experimental setup is comprehensive and provides strong support for the paper's claims. The inclusion of diverse baselines (TF-IDF, Nearest Neighbor, Supervised Embeddings, a rule-based system) provides essential context for evaluating the performance of the end-to-end Memory Network. The creation of an OOV test set is a particularly strong feature, as it directly probes the ability of models to generalize to new entities—a crucial requirement for real-world deployment. The proposed "match type features" is a simple yet effective method for improving OOV performance, and its benefit is clearly demonstrated.

Crucially, the authors validate their synthetic benchmark against two real-world datasets. The finding that the relative performance patterns of the models are consistent across synthetic and real data (e.g., MemNN > Supervised Embeddings > IR) provides confidence that the proposed tasks are a meaningful proxy for real-world challenges. The analysis of the results is also insightful, drawing a clear distinction between per-response and per-dialog accuracy. This highlights the critical challenge of error propagation and long-term consistency, showing that even models with decent turn-level performance can consistently fail to complete the overall goal.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could be addressed to further improve it:

*   **Generation vs. Ranking:** The evaluation is conducted as a ranking task over a fixed candidate set. This is a practical and common methodology, but a brief discussion of its limitations would be beneficial. It would be useful to acknowledge the gap between this setup and a true text generation setting, and how the size and composition of the candidate set might influence results.
*   **Simulator Complexity:** The dialog simulator uses a limited set of linguistic patterns and deterministic logic (e.g., the order of questions in Task 1). This is valuable for control and reproducibility, but it is an oversimplification of real conversation. Acknowledging this limitation and suggesting future work on simulators with greater linguistic and behavioral variety would add valuable context.
*   **Concierge Data Anonymization:** The paper notes the use of NER and regex to anonymize the Concierge dataset. This process can sometimes introduce artifacts or errors. A brief mention of the potential impact of this preprocessing on model performance would enhance the transparency of the work.
*   **Clarity on "Match Type Features":** The description in Section 4.5 is good, but it could be made even clearer with a small, concrete example. For instance: "If the input contains 'london' and a candidate contains 'a cheap restaurant in london', the candidate's vector representation is augmented with a special `<LOCATION_MATCH>` feature." This would immediately clarify the mechanism for the reader.

### Questions for the Authors

*   **Candidate Set Details:** Could you specify the size of the candidate response set for the synthetic tasks, DSTC2, and the Concierge dataset? Understanding the scale of the ranking challenge is important for interpreting the accuracy scores.
*   **Impact of Training Data Scale:** The training sets were kept small (1,000 dialogs) to reflect "realistic learning conditions." This is a reasonable choice, but have you explored how performance, particularly the low per-dialog accuracy on Tasks 3 and 5, scales with larger training sets? It would be interesting to know if the performance gap between models changes or if the core challenges persist even with more data.
*   **Deeper Error Analysis:** The paper identifies Tasks 3 (Displaying options) and 5 (Full dialog) as unsolved challenges for the best model. Could you provide a more detailed qualitative error analysis for these tasks? For example, in Task 3, does the model fail to sort correctly, does it offer an option that was already rejected, or does it get stuck in a loop? Pinpointing these failure modes would be extremely valuable for guiding future research.
