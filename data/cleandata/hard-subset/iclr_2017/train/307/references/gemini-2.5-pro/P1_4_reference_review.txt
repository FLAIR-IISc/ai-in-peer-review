### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a new suite of tasks and datasets designed to test and analyze end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their capabilities in goal-oriented scenarios (which require skills like KB querying, state tracking, and presenting options) are not well understood due to a lack of suitable, reproducible benchmarks. To address this, they present a set of six tasks within the restaurant reservation domain. Five of these tasks are synthetically generated, inspired by the bAbI framework, to isolate and test specific abilities: issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing extra information, and conducting a full dialog. A key contribution is the creation of an Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set to evaluate generalization to unseen entities. The paper supplements these synthetic tasks with two datasets derived from real human interactions (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to validate the benchmark's relevance. The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation of several models, from rule-based and classical IR methods to supervised embeddings and Memory Networks, demonstrating the utility of their benchmark. Their key finding is that while modern end-to-end models like Memory Networks show promise and outperform baselines, their per-dialog success rate remains low, highlighting significant room for improvement.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Addresses a critical need in the community:** The paper provides a well-designed, open-source, and reproducible benchmark for goal-oriented dialog, a research area of high practical importance. The lack of such standardized testbeds has hindered progress and made it difficult to compare different end-to-end approaches rigorously. This work is a significant service to the field.
2.  **Strong methodology and experimental design:** The decomposition of a complex goal-oriented dialog into a series of sub-tasks is highly effective for systematic model analysis. The inclusion of an OOV test set is a crucial and well-executed addition that tests a vital aspect of generalization. Furthermore, validating the trends observed on synthetic data against two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) provides strong evidence for the benchmark's utility as a proxy for real-world performance.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's main strength is its contribution of a valuable research resource. The authors correctly identify a gap between the capabilities needed for chit-chat versus goal-oriented dialog and the lack of tools to measure this gap. Traditional dialog datasets are often noisy, proprietary, or not structured for end-to-end learning. This paper's bAbI-style approach provides a controlled environment where specific model capabilities can be isolated and evaluated. This "error analysis by design" is a powerful concept that will allow researchers to pinpoint specific weaknesses in their models (e.g., failing at KB interpretation in Task 3 vs. dialog state management in Task 2).

The experimental setup is thorough and provides a solid foundation for future work. The authors compare a sensible range of baselines, from simple IR methods to a strong end-to-end model (Memory Networks). The results are insightful: they confirm that goal-oriented dialog is a difficult task where simple lexical matching (TF-IDF) fails, learned representations (supervised embeddings) help, but structured reasoning (Memory Networks) is needed to make substantial progress. The consistently low per-dialog accuracy across all models powerfully supports the paper's central claim: current end-to-end models are not yet reliable for goal-oriented tasks. The introduction and evaluation of "match type features" is also a valuable practical insight, showing a simple way to help models handle OOV entities, a common failure point.

Finally, the paper's greatest validation comes from showing that the relative performance of the models is consistent across their synthetic tasks and the two real-world datasets. This suggests that improvements made on the controlled, reproducible tasks are likely to translate to more complex, noisy environments, making the proposed dataset an effective and efficient tool for research and development.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.

1.  **On Evaluation Metrics:** The per-dialog accuracy is a very strict metric, where a single mistake results in a score of zero for the entire dialog. While this is a clear indicator of reliability, it might be useful to also report a softer "task success" metric. For instance, did the system eventually identify a correct restaurant, even if it made a conversational misstep along the way? This could provide a more nuanced view of model performance, distinguishing between catastrophic failures and recoverable errors.
2.  **Model Selection:** The choice of Memory Networks as the primary end-to-end model is well-justified. However, given the prevalence of RNN/LSTM/Transformer-based architectures in dialog systems, a brief discussion or comparison against a standard sequence-to-sequence with attention model would have further strengthened the baseline results.
3.  **Analysis of "Match Type Features":** The match type features are an effective addition. It would be interesting if the authors could discuss this a bit more. Is this a necessary "crutch" for current models, or does it point to a fundamental need for hybrid neuro-symbolic reasoning? A brief discussion on how future models might learn this type-matching behavior implicitly would be a nice forward-looking addition.
4.  **Candidate Set Construction:** The paper states that the candidate set for ranking is composed of all bot utterances from the combined training, validation, and test sets. This is a standard practice in some research settings but could be seen as a minor form of information leakage from the test set. Could the authors clarify the size of this candidate set and comment on whether using only training/validation candidates would significantly alter the results or the task's difficulty?

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Following up on the point about metrics: Have you considered or experimented with any task-completion metrics beyond per-dialog accuracy? For example, in a full dialog (Task 5), what percentage of the time does the model successfully issue an API call with the correct constraints, even if it fails on a subsequent step? This could offer a more granular view of where models succeed and fail within a complete dialog.
2.  The deterministic order of asking for information in Task 1 is noted as making prediction possible. How much does this determinism help the models? Have you considered a variant where the bot asks for missing fields in a random order to test model robustness to conversational variations?
