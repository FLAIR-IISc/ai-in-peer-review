### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a new benchmark suite of tasks designed to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. Drawing inspiration from the bAbI tasks for question answering, the authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain. These tasks are carefully designed to break down the complex problem of goal-oriented conversation into distinct, testable capabilities: issuing API calls (T1), updating them (T2), presenting options from a knowledge base (T3), providing specific information (T4), and conducting a full dialog (T5). A key strength of the dataset design is the inclusion of an Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) test split, which specifically measures a model's ability to generalize to unseen entities (e.g., new restaurant names, cuisines).

To ground these synthetic tasks in reality, the authors also convert the DSTC2 dataset into their format (T6) and evaluate on a proprietary dataset of human-human concierge chats. They provide a thorough benchmarking of several models, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (MemNNs). Their experiments demonstrate that while MemNNs, particularly when augmented with "match type features," outperform other baselines, they still struggle with more complex reasoning, as evidenced by the significant gap between per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The paper's main claim is that this new resource provides a valuable, reproducible, and diagnostic tool for analyzing the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end dialog systems, and their results suggest that these models are not yet robust enough to reliably handle goal-oriented tasks.

### Decision: Accept

This is a clear accept. The paper makes a significant and timely contribution to the field.

1.  **High-Value Contribution:** The primary contribution is not a novel model but a well-designed and much-needed evaluation resource. The lack of standardized, reproducible, and diagnostic benchmarks has been a major impediment to progress in goal-oriented dialog. This work directly addresses this gap by providing a public dataset that allows for controlled experiments and fine-grained error analysis.
2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental setup is excellent. The progression from synthetic to real-world data (DSTC2, Concierge) convincingly validates the utility of the proposed tasks. The finding that relative model performance is consistent across synthetic and real datasets is a powerful argument for the benchmark's relevance. Furthermore, the analysis, particularly the distinction between per-response and per-dialog accuracy, provides crucial insights into the reliability limitations of current end-to-end models.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its thoughtful methodology and clear-sighted analysis.

**The Task Design is Excellent:** By breaking down the dialog flow into sub-tasks, the authors enable researchers to pinpoint specific model failures. For example, the results clearly show that current models are reasonably good at tracking state to issue an initial API call (T1) but fail at interpreting and presenting the results from that call (T3). This is a much more useful finding than a single accuracy number on a monolithic task. The inclusion of the OOV split is particularly forward-thinking, as generalization to new entities is a critical real-world requirement that is often overlooked in academic benchmarks.

**The Experimental Protocol is Sound:** The choice of baselines is appropriate, covering a spectrum from non-learning IR methods to a strong neural baseline in Memory Networks. The introduction of "match type features" is a simple but effective technique that addresses a known weakness of embedding-based models when dealing with entities, and the ablation demonstrates its value clearly. The honesty of the results is commendable; the authors do not oversell their models' performance and instead focus on what the low per-dialog accuracies reveal about the remaining challenges.

**Impact on the Field:** This work has the potential to become a standard benchmark, much like bAbI did for QA. It provides a common ground for comparing different architectures and ideas in a controlled setting, which is far preferable to evaluations on proprietary data or through non-reproducible crowdsourcing setups. By lowering the barrier to entry for research in this area and enabling more precise analysis, this contribution is likely to accelerate progress.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

1.  **Choice of End-to-End Model:** The paper uses Memory Networks as the representative end-to-end model. While MemNNs are a strong choice, Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) based sequence-to-sequence models with attention were also a dominant architecture for dialog at the time. Including an RNN baseline would have provided a more comprehensive picture of the end-to-end landscape and strengthened the claim that the observed weaknesses are general to the *class* of end-to-end models, rather than potentially specific to MemNNs. Could the authors comment on this choice?

2.  **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking task, where the model selects the best response from a large candidate set. This is a practical simplification but abstracts away the challenges of natural language generation. It would be valuable to add a brief discussion in the conclusion or future work section about the limitations of this paradigm and how the benchmark might be extended to evaluate generative models. For example, how would metrics need to change?

3.  **Task Simplifications:** In Task 1, the paper states the bot asks for information in a "deterministic order." This might make the task artificially easy, as the model can learn a simple, fixed sequence of questions. Could a more challenging version of the task involve a randomized order for requesting missing slots? This would better reflect real-world user-system interactions.

4.  **Deeper Error Analysis:** The paper successfully identifies which tasks are hard (T3, T5), but a more qualitative error analysis would be illuminating. For instance, in T3 (Displaying options), *why* does the model fail? Does it fail to sort the options by rating? Does it offer an option the user has already rejected? Does it hallucinate restaurant names? Including a small table with examples of common error types for the most difficult tasks would further enhance the paper's diagnostic value.
