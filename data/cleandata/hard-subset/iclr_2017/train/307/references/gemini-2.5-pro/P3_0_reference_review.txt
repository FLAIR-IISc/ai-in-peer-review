### **Review**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their capabilities in structured, goal-oriented tasks are not well understood, primarily due to the lack of suitable, open, and reproducible evaluation frameworks. To address this, they propose a set of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, inspired by the bAbI question-answering dataset. These tasks are designed to test specific capabilities in a controlled manner: issuing and updating API calls, interpreting and presenting KB results, and providing specific information. A key strength of the benchmark is its explicit Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set, which measures a model's ability to generalize to new entities not seen during training. To ground these synthetic tasks in reality, the authors also provide results on two datasets derived from real user interactions: a converted version of the DSTC2 dataset and a new dataset from a human-operated concierge service. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of several baselines, including IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (MemNNs), and introduces a simple but effective "match type feature" mechanism to help models handle OOV entities. The main findings are that while MemNNs outperform other learning-based baselines, they still struggle to achieve high per-dialog success rates, particularly on complex tasks requiring reasoning over KB results.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant Community Contribution:** The paper provides a much-needed, well-designed, and open-source benchmark for a critical area of research. By breaking down the complex problem of goal-oriented dialog into distinct, testable skills (à la bAbI), it enables systematic analysis and targeted model development, which will be invaluable for fostering progress in the field.
2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experimental Design:** The evaluation is comprehensive and compelling. The inclusion of an explicit OOV setting directly addresses the crucial real-world challenge of generalizing to unseen entities. Furthermore, validating the trends observed on the synthetic data against two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) strongly supports the claim that the proposed benchmark is a useful and relevant proxy for real-world performance.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its contribution as a research tool. The current landscape for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems is fragmented, often relying on noisy, proprietary, or non-reproducible setups. This paper introduces a clean, lightweight, and publicly available testbed that allows for direct and fair comparison of different models. The task design is particularly commendable. By isolating capabilities like dialog state tracking (Tasks 1 & 2), KB interaction and interpretation (Tasks 3 & 4), and their integration (Task 5), the authors provide a framework for detailed error analysis. This is far more insightful than a single performance metric on a monolithic task.

The experimental setup provides strong evidence for the paper's claims. The choice of baselines is appropriate, ranging from simple IR models to a strong end-to-end architecture (MemNNs). The results clearly demonstrate the limitations of current models. The high per-response accuracy coupled with very low per-dialog accuracy convincingly shows that while models can learn to handle individual turns, stringing together a sequence of correct actions to reliably complete a goal remains an open challenge. This is a crucial, if sobering, insight for the community.

Finally, the introduction of "match type features" is a simple but elegant contribution. It highlights a key deficiency of standard embedding methods—their inability to handle exact matches and OOV entities gracefully—and provides a practical solution that significantly boosts performance, especially in the OOV setting. This demonstrates that progress may not only come from more complex architectures but also from clever ways of integrating symbolic information into neural models. The fact that MemNNs with these features outperform a hand-crafted rule-based system on the noisy DSTC2 data further validates the end-to-end learning approach for real-world scenarios.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to increase its impact:

*   **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking task over a pre-defined set of candidate responses. This is a practical simplification but abstracts away the challenges of generation (e.g., fluency, correctness, avoiding repetition). A brief discussion in the conclusion or methodology section about the limitations of this setup and the potential challenges in extending this benchmark to a generation setting would be valuable.
*   **Analysis of Task 3 Failure:** The paper correctly identifies Task 3 (Displaying options) as a major failure point for the models. This task uniquely requires sorting based on an attribute (rating). It would be insightful to include a more detailed error analysis here. For instance, does the model simply offer restaurants in the order they appear in the KB context? Does it pick one randomly? Understanding the *nature* of the failure on this reasoning-heavy task could provide strong clues for future model development.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The appendix lists the best hyperparameters, but it would be interesting to know how sensitive the MemNN model is to them, particularly the number of hops. The paper notes that 3-4 hops are optimal for some tasks, which supports the need for iterative reasoning. A brief analysis of performance vs. number of hops for a key task (e.g., Task 5) would strengthen this point.
*   **Concierge Dataset Anonymization:** The anonymization process for the Concierge dataset seems aggressive (removing all NER-tagged entities). This could potentially remove information crucial for resolving the request (e.g., a user mentions a specific neighborhood, which is then tagged and removed). A comment on the potential impact of this data processing on task difficulty would be helpful.

#### **5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you provide more detail on the size and composition of the candidate set used for evaluation? Is it the union of all unique bot responses across all tasks and splits? The difficulty of the ranking task is highly dependent on the number of negative candidates, and clarifying this would help in interpreting the accuracy scores.
*   The "match type features" are shown to be very effective. Have you experimented with ablations to understand their impact better? For example, what is the performance if the feature is only added for OOV entities, or only when an entity is present in the latest user utterance (as opposed to the full memory)?
*   Regarding the failure on Task 3, does the attention mechanism of the MemNN provide any insight? For example, across the reasoning hops, does the model learn to attend to the "rating" values in the KB facts but still fail to produce the correctly sorted output, or does it fail to attend to the relevant information in the first place?
