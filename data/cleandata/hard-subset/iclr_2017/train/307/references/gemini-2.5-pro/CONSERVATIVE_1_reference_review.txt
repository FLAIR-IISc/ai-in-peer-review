### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper addresses the question of whether end-to-end neural dialog systems, successful in chit-chat settings, can be competitive with traditional slot-filling systems in goal-oriented tasks. The authors argue that a lack of suitable, reproducible benchmarks hinders progress in this area. Their primary contribution is a new open-source dataset and a set of five synthetically generated tasks for restaurant reservation, designed to probe specific capabilities like issuing API calls, handling knowledge base (KB) facts, and dealing with out-of-vocabulary (OOV) entities. The tasks are designed for systematic analysis and reproducibility. To ground these synthetic tasks in reality, the authors also adapt the DSTC2 dataset and introduce a proprietary "Concierge" dataset. They provide baseline results using several methods, including classical IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (MemNNs), concluding that while MemNNs show promise, their per-dialog performance remains low, indicating that end-to-end models are not yet ready to reliably handle complex, goal-oriented conversations.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**
**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Limited Novelty and Contribution:** The paper's main contribution is a dataset, not a new method. While benchmark creation is valuable, the core tasks are based on synthetic, template-generated language, which significantly oversimplifies the core challenges of natural language understanding in goal-oriented dialog. The findings largely confirm the existing consensus: end-to-end models struggle with symbolic reasoning and state tracking without explicit mechanisms.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper positions itself as a test of end-to-end models against "traditional" systems. However, it fails to include a rigorous baseline from the well-established paradigm of statistical dialog systems (e.g., POMDP-based). The hand-crafted rule-based system for the DSTC2 task is a weak substitute and does not represent the state of the art in traditional goal-oriented dialog, making the claim that MemNNs "outperform" it less impactful.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**On the Synthetic Nature of the Tasks:** The paper's primary evaluation relies on tasks generated from a simulator with a limited set of linguistic patterns (43 for the user, 20 for the bot). This controlled environment is useful for debugging but fails to capture the ambiguity, variability, and noise inherent in real user interactions. Crucial challenges in dialog systems, such as robust natural language understanding, coreference resolution, and handling user corrections in varied phrasing, are largely sidestepped. While the authors use DSTC2 and Concierge data to show that relative model performance trends are similar, this does not validate the synthetic tasks as a sufficient proxy for real-world performance. The gap between achieving high accuracy on template-based conversations and deploying a robust system is substantial, and this work does not provide enough evidence to suggest its findings will generalize.

**On the Weakness of Baselines:** The introduction explicitly mentions POMDP-based systems as the "most successful goal-oriented dialog systems." Given this, the absence of such a model as a baseline is a critical omission. For the DSTC2 task (Task 6), a comparison against a well-tuned statistical belief tracker and policy would have provided a much more meaningful benchmark. The authors' custom-built rule-based system, with 28 rules, is not representative of sophisticated, production-grade traditional systems. Showing that a MemNN can outperform this specific, limited rule-based system is not compelling evidence that end-to-end approaches are ready to supplant the established paradigm. Without a stronger baseline, the paper's central question remains only partially answered.

**On the End-to-End Paradigm:** The paper's best-performing model, MemNN with "match type features," highlights a weakness in the purely end-to-end approach it claims to evaluate. These features essentially re-introduce a form of symbolic, entity-aware knowledge into the model, moving away from a purely learned representation. This feels like a necessary concession to the fact that embedding-based models struggle with exact matching and grounding of symbolic information from a KB. This finding is not novel and reinforces the skepticism that a monolithic neural network can, without significant structural priors, effectively manage the distinct sub-problems of language understanding, state tracking, and symbolic API interaction.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The effort to create a reproducible benchmark is commendable, and the breakdown of the problem into subtasks is a methodologically sound approach for analysis. The paper is well-written and the experiments are clearly presented. However, the claims should be tempered to reflect the limitations of the synthetic data.

My questions for the authors are:
1.  Could you elaborate on the decision not to include a POMDP-based or other statistical dialog system baseline for the DSTC2 task? A comparison against a system from the original DSTC2 challenge, even if not perfectly aligned with the end-to-end evaluation format, would have provided valuable context.
2.  The evaluation is framed as a ranking task over a set of all possible candidate responses. This is a simplification compared to a generative setting. How do you believe the challenges and relative model performances would change if the models were required to generate responses from scratch?
3.  The "match type features" appear crucial for handling OOV entities and providing KB information. Does this not suggest that a hybrid approach, combining neural language modeling with more explicit, symbolic state and entity management, is more promising than the "pure" end-to-end models initially motivated by chit-chat systems? This seems to support the utility of established methods rather than argue for their replacement.
