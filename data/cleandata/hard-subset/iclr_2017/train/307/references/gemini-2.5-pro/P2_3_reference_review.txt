### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a new benchmark suite of tasks designed to evaluate the capabilities of end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their effectiveness in goal-oriented scenarios, which require skills like dialog state tracking, knowledge base (KB) querying, and interpreting results, is largely unassessed due to a lack of suitable, reproducible datasets. To address this, the paper presents a set of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, inspired by the bAbI QA benchmark. These tasks are designed to diagnostically test specific abilities: issuing API calls (T1), updating them (T2), presenting options from KB results (T3), providing extra information (T4), and conducting a full dialog (T5). A key contribution is the creation of an Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set to evaluate generalization to unseen entities. The authors benchmark several models, including IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (MemNNs), and validate their findings by showing that the relative model performance on the synthetic tasks is consistent with performance on two real-world datasets: a converted version of DSTC2 and a new, proprietary dataset from an online concierge service. The results demonstrate that while current end-to-end models like MemNNs outperform simpler baselines, they still struggle with key aspects of goal-oriented dialog, highlighting clear areas for future research.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to the community. My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:
1.  **Addresses a Critical Need:** The paper tackles a significant bottleneck in conversational AI research: the lack of a standardized, reproducible, and diagnostic benchmark for goal-oriented dialog. This work provides a well-designed tool that allows researchers to rigorously test and analyze the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end models.
2.  **Thoughtful and Rigorous Experimental Design:** The task design, particularly the breakdown into sub-tasks and the inclusion of an OOV test set, is excellent. The validation of the synthetic benchmark's utility by comparing performance patterns against two real-world datasets provides strong evidence for the paper's central claims.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated and executed. The introduction clearly frames the problem, distinguishing the challenges of goal-oriented dialog from open-domain chit-chat. The critique of existing datasets in the related work section is accurate and justifies the need for this new resource.

The primary strength lies in the design of the tasks. By breaking down a complex dialog into five constituent skills, the benchmark moves beyond a single performance metric and enables genuine error analysis. For example, the experiments clearly show that MemNNs are reasonably competent at tracking state to form a query (T1, T2) but fail when required to interpret and present the results of that query (T3, T5). This kind of diagnostic insight is precisely what the field needs to make targeted progress. The creation of an OOV test set is another major strength, as it directly tests a model's ability to generalize beyond memorizing entities seen in training, which is a crucial requirement for any practical system.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and provides a solid set of baselines for future work. The inclusion of classical IR models, supervised embeddings, and a state-of-the-art (at the time) end-to-end model like Memory Networks gives a clear picture of the landscape. The authors also introduce a simple but effective "match type feature" to help models handle OOV entities, demonstrating that they have thought carefully about the specific challenges of the tasks.

Crucially, the authors connect their synthetic benchmark back to reality. By converting the DSTC2 dataset and introducing the Concierge dataset, they demonstrate that the performance patterns observed on their tasks (i.e., MemNNs > Supervised Embeddings > IR) hold on noisy, human-generated data. This validation is essential, as it gives the community confidence that improvements on these synthetic tasks are likely to translate into better performance on real-world problems. The finding that the MemNN outperforms their hand-crafted rule-based system on the noisy DSTC2 task further underscores the value of end-to-end learning in complex environments.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Linguistic Diversity of the Simulator:** The paper states that user and bot utterances are generated from a limited set of patterns (43 for the user, 20 for the bot). While this is a reasonable starting point for a controlled environment, it raises the question of whether models might be learning syntactic shortcuts specific to these patterns rather than a more general understanding. Could you comment on the linguistic diversity of the generated utterances and the potential for models to overfit to the simulator's specific phraseology?
*   **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is performed in a ranking setting, where the model selects the correct response from a large candidate pool. This is a practical and common evaluation paradigm. However, the ultimate goal for many dialog systems is generation. Have you considered how these tasks might be adapted for a generative evaluation setting? What new challenges would that introduce, especially concerning evaluation metrics for API calls and factual correctness?
*   **Complexity of User Behavior:** The user simulator follows a relatively deterministic path (e.g., the bot asks for information in a fixed order in T1; users always eventually accept an option in T3). Real users can be far more unpredictable, changing their minds, providing irrelevant information, or asking out-of-scope questions. While this is beyond the scope of the current work, it would be interesting to hear your thoughts on how the task suite could be extended to incorporate more complex and stochastic user behaviors.
*   **Concierge Dataset Details:** The Concierge dataset is a valuable addition. The paper notes that it "does not contain results from API calls." This seems to limit its utility for evaluating tasks analogous to T3 and T4, which are centered on interpreting and using information returned from a KB. Could you clarify how this limitation affects the conclusions drawn from this dataset, particularly regarding a model's ability to ground its responses in retrieved knowledge?
