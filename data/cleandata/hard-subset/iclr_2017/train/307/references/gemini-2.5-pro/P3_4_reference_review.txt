### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a new benchmark suite for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while end-to-end models show promise, their capabilities in practical, goal-oriented settings are poorly understood due to a lack of suitable, reproducible evaluation frameworks. To address this, they propose a set of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, inspired by the diagnostic nature of the bAbI tasks. These tasks are designed to probe specific abilities required for successful goal-oriented conversation, such as managing dialog state, issuing API calls, and interpreting knowledge base (KB) results. A key contribution is the creation of an Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set to explicitly measure a model's ability to generalize to unseen entities. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of several baselines, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (MemNNs), demonstrating the utility of their benchmark. Finally, to ground their synthetic tasks in reality, they show that the relative performance of these models on the proposed tasks is consistent with their performance on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge dataset), suggesting the benchmark is a valid proxy for real-world performance.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Addresses a Critical Need:** The paper tackles a significant and well-recognized problem in the dialog systems community: the lack of standardized, diagnostic, and reproducible benchmarks for goal-oriented dialog. By providing an open-source dataset with a clear breakdown of sub-tasks, it enables rigorous analysis and comparison of end-to-end models, which is crucial for driving progress in the field.
2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experimental Design:** The methodology is exceptionally strong. The creation of five distinct tasks to isolate different dialog capabilities is well-conceived. The inclusion of an OOV test set is a particularly valuable contribution, as generalization to new entities is a core challenge for data-driven systems. Furthermore, validating the synthetic benchmark's findings against two real-world datasets (one public, one proprietary) significantly strengthens the paper's claims and the benchmark's utility.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its contribution of a well-designed evaluation methodology, not a novel model architecture. This is a service to the research community that has the potential for high impact. Traditional goal-oriented systems based on slot-filling are brittle and hard to scale, while end-to-end systems are often "black boxes" whose successes and failures are difficult to diagnose. This work provides a much-needed middle ground, offering a controlled environment to "unit test" the capabilities of end-to-end models.

The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the value of this approach. For example, the finding that MemNNs can successfully learn to track state and issue API calls (Tasks 1 & 2) but struggle to interpret and present options from a KB query (Task 3) is a clear, actionable insight. This would be difficult to disentangle from a single, monolithic task. The performance drop on the OOV tasks and the subsequent improvement from using "match type features" clearly isolates the challenge of handling unseen entities and provides a simple yet effective baseline for addressing it.

The validation on real-world data is crucial. The authors show that the relative ordering of model performance (MemNN > Supervised Embeddings > IR) is consistent across their synthetic tasks, DSTC2, and the Concierge dataset. This provides strong evidence that insights gained from their controlled benchmark are likely to translate to more complex, noisy, real-world scenarios, justifying its use as a primary development and evaluation tool.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could be discussed to further strengthen it:

*   **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking task over a candidate set, which is a common and practical simplification for ensuring reproducibility. However, the ultimate goal for many dialog systems is generation. It would be valuable for the authors to briefly discuss the limitations of the ranking setup and speculate on how the challenges might differ in a generative setting (e.g., hallucination of facts, maintaining fluency with OOV entities).
*   **Simulator Simplicity:** The user and bot simulators are based on a fixed set of templates. While this is necessary for a controlled environment, it limits linguistic diversity. A discussion of this limitation would be welcome. For instance, acknowledging that models performing well here might still struggle with more varied paraphrasing or colloquial language in the wild would add nuance.
*   **The Nature of "Match Type Features":** The introduction of match type features is an effective engineering solution that significantly boosts performance on OOV tasks. However, it feels like a small step back from a purely end-to-end philosophy, reintroducing a form of feature engineering. It would be beneficial to frame this not as a definitive solution, but as a strong baseline that highlights the need for more principled methods of integrating symbolic knowledge and handling OOV items, perhaps referencing concurrent or future work on copy mechanisms or pointer networks.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The evaluation metric for dialogs, per-dialog accuracy, is very strict (100% of turns must be correct). While this clearly measures goal completion, have you considered alternative, "softer" metrics like task success rate (i.e., the correct restaurant is eventually booked, even if there were minor errors along the way)? How might this change the interpretation of the results, particularly for tasks T3 and T5 where per-dialog accuracy is near zero?
*   The KB used in the simulation is of a modest size. How do you anticipate the tested models, particularly the MemNN, would scale as the number of entities (restaurants, locations, cuisines) and the complexity of the KB schema grow by orders of magnitude? Would the attention mechanism remain effective, or would more structured memory access be required?
*   Could you elaborate on the decision to have the bot ask for information in a deterministic order in Task 1? While this makes the task more tractable, real dialogs are often more flexible. How much harder do you think the task would be with a non-deterministic question order, and do you believe the relative model performances would hold?
