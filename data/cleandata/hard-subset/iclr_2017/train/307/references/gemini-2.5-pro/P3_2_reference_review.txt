### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a new public dataset and a set of tasks for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors correctly identify a major gap in the field: while end-to-end models have shown promise in open-domain chit-chat, their capabilities in structured, goal-oriented settings are poorly understood, and existing datasets are often proprietary, small-scale, or not suited for end-to-end training. To address this, the paper presents a suite of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, inspired by the diagnostic nature of the bAbI QA dataset. These tasks are designed to test specific capabilities in a controlled manner, such as issuing API calls, handling user updates, interpreting KB query results, and providing information. A crucial aspect of the benchmark is the inclusion of an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test set to evaluate generalization to unseen entities. To ground these synthetic tasks in reality, the authors also adapt and provide results on two real-world datasets: DSTC2 and a proprietary "Concierge" dataset. The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation of several baselines, from classical IR methods to supervised embeddings and Memory Networks, offering a clear analysis of their respective strengths and weaknesses on these tasks. The main finding is that while modern end-to-end models like Memory Networks outperform simpler baselines, they still struggle to achieve high per-dialog accuracy, particularly in tasks requiring reasoning over KB results.

**2. Recommendation: Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to the community. My decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **High-quality, well-motivated resource:** The paper provides a much-needed, open, and well-designed benchmark for a critical application area of dialog systems. The diagnostic breakdown of the problem into subtasks and the rigorous setup for testing OOV generalization are particularly well-conceived and will enable more systematic and interpretable research.
2.  **Thorough and insightful analysis:** The experimental section is not merely a leaderboard. It provides a careful analysis that validates the utility of the proposed tasks, demonstrating how they reveal specific failure modes of different model classes. The finding that performance patterns on the synthetic tasks correlate with those on real-world data strongly supports the benchmark's value as a proxy for more complex scenarios.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its clear focus on methodology and evaluation rather than claiming a novel model architecture. This is a service to the community that often has a more lasting impact than a minor state-of-the-art improvement.

*   **Problem Framing and Motivation:** The introduction and related work sections are excellent. They clearly articulate the limitations of traditional slot-filling systems and the challenges of applying chit-chat-oriented end-to-end models to goal-oriented tasks. The justification for a new dataset is compelling, correctly pointing out the inadequacy of existing resources for this specific purpose.
*   **Task Design:** The design of Tasks 1-5 is thoughtful. They build in complexity, moving from simple state tracking (T1, T2) to reasoning over returned knowledge (T3, T4) and finally to a full, combined dialog (T5). This allows researchers to isolate and address specific model weaknesses, which is far more useful than a single, monolithic task. The creation of the OOV split by partitioning the KB is a particularly clever and important design choice, as generalization to unseen entities is a core challenge for neural dialog systems.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The choice of baselines is appropriate, spanning from simple IR to strong embedding models and a representative end-to-end architecture (Memory Networks). The analysis of the results is insightful. For instance, the discussion of why TF-IDF fails in this setting (conversations move on quickly) compared to its success in chit-chat is a nuanced observation. The core conclusion—that per-response accuracy can be decent while per-dialog accuracy remains very low—is a critical finding that highlights the brittleness of current systems over longer conversations. The introduction of "match type features" is a simple but effective technique that directly addresses the OOV problem, further strengthening the paper's analysis.
*   **Grounding in Reality:** The inclusion of DSTC2 and the Concierge dataset is a vital component that prevents the work from being an exercise in a purely synthetic world. By showing that the relative performance of the models is consistent across synthetic and real data, the authors make a strong case for the utility and validity of their proposed benchmark.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking task over a set of all possible candidate responses. This is a common and practical simplification, but it is a significant departure from a true generative setting where a model must produce the response from scratch. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge this limitation in the discussion. A brief comment on the challenges of adapting these tasks for generative evaluation would be a welcome addition for future work.
*   **Linguistic Diversity of the Simulator:** The simulator uses a limited set of 43 user and 20 bot templates. While this ensures control, it also means the models are not being tested on their ability to handle significant linguistic variation (e.g., paraphrasing, complex sentence structures). This is a key difference from the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets, and it would be beneficial to state this trade-off (control vs. diversity) more directly.
*   **Memory Network Architecture Details:** The choice of Memory Networks is well-justified. However, it would be interesting to know if other contemporary end-to-end architectures (e.g., hierarchical RNNs) were considered or tried. A brief sentence motivating the specific choice of MemN2N over other variants would add context.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to formulate the task as next-utterance classification rather than generation? Was this primarily to ensure reproducible and straightforward evaluation, or do you believe this paradigm is sufficient for measuring progress on the core challenges you've identified?
2.  The rule-based system for Task 6 achieved 40.7% validation accuracy with 28 rules. Could you provide a little more color on what the primary failure modes of this system were? Understanding where the hand-crafted rules break down would provide even stronger context for the performance of the learning-based models.
3.  Regarding the Concierge dataset, the anonymization process removes named entities like locations. Given that location is a key slot in restaurant reservation, how do you think this pre-processing step might affect model performance and the realism of the task compared to DSTC2 or your synthetic data?
