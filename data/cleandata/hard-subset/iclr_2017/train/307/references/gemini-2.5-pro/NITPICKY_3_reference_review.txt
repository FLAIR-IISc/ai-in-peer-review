### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a new open-source dataset and a set of tasks designed to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. Drawing inspiration from the bAbI QA benchmark, the authors propose five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, which systematically test different capabilities such as dialog management, API call generation, and handling of KB information. A key contribution is the inclusion of an Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set to assess model generalization to unseen entities. The authors supplement these synthetic tasks with two datasets derived from real user interactions (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to validate the synthetic setup as a useful proxy. The paper provides extensive baseline results using IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, demonstrating that while current end-to-end models are promising, they still fall short of reliably completing complex, goal-oriented dialogs.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper addresses an important problem and the proposed resource is a valuable contribution to the community, the submission is marred by a significant number of flaws in its presentation, methodological clarity, and experimental design. The lack of rigor in these areas makes it difficult to fully trust the reported findings and undermines the paper's overall quality. The two primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Numerous presentation and formatting errors,** most critically in the reference section, which suggest a lack of care and professionalism unbecoming of an ICLR submission.
2.  **Several key methodological choices are either poorly explained or questionable,** which casts doubt on the scientific rigor of the evaluation and the validity of the conclusions drawn.

**Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on a meticulous examination of the paper, which revealed the following issues:

**1. Presentation and Formatting:**

*   **Reference Section:** The bibliography is severely malformed. Multiple entries contain BibTeX errors (e.g., "Bai et al\.,? 2009, Bai et al\.,", "Kim et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Kim et al\.,"). An entire spurious entry ("Dodge, Dodge,? \Q2016\E, Dodge,") appears at the end. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable and makes it difficult for a reader to consult the cited works.
*   **Typos and Inconsistencies:** The paper contains various minor but noticeable errors. For instance, "endto-end" is used instead of "end-to-end" (Introduction, para 3), and capitalization is inconsistent (e.g., "Memory networks" in a heading vs. "Memory Networks" in the text). While minor, these errors accumulate and detract from the paper's perceived quality.

**2. Methodological and Technical Concerns:**

*   **Unjustified Modeling Choice:** In Appendix A, a footnote reveals a crucial and highly non-intuitive implementation detail: "KB facts resulting from such calls [are stored] as user utterances". This conflates factual information retrieved from an external source with user-generated input. This is a significant modeling decision that could heavily influence the learning process, yet it is relegated to a footnote and is not justified in the main text. It feels like an ad-hoc hack to fit the data into the model's architecture.
*   **Data Leakage in Evaluation:** The evaluation protocol, as described in Section 3.1.2, states that the candidate set for ranking is composed of all bot utterances from the combined training, validation, and test sets. This means the model has access to the exact text of the ground-truth test responses during training and validation. While common in some retrieval-based dialogue work, this is a form of data leakage that limits the assessment of a model's true generative or retrieval capabilities on unseen data. This weakness should be more prominently discussed.
*   **Vague Mechanism for "Match Type Features":** The "match type features" are central to the best-performing model, yet their implementation is described vaguely in Section 4.5. The paper states a special word is "added to the candidate representation," but it is unclear *how* (e.g., appended, averaged) or what this representation is (e.g., bag-of-words vector). For a key component of the proposed model, this lack of precision is a significant flaw.
*   **Oversimplified Simulation:** The synthetic tasks are generated from a very small set of templates (43 for the user, 20 for the bot) and the bot's behavior is deterministic (e.g., fixed question order in T1). This creates a task environment with extremely low linguistic variance and predictable structure, which may not be a reliable indicator of model performance in more realistic, less constrained scenarios.
*   **Weak Justification for "Realistic Conditions":** The authors claim that small training sets of 1,000 examples create "realistic learning conditions." This justification is weak. While goal-oriented data can be scarce, 1,000 examples is exceptionally small for training models like Memory Networks. This choice seems arbitrary and its impact on the results and conclusions is not explored.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite the issues raised, the core idea of this paper is strong. A well-designed, bAbI-style benchmark for goal-oriented dialogue is a welcome contribution. Should the authors choose to revise this work, I would suggest the following:

*   Thoroughly proofread the entire manuscript, paying special attention to the reference list.
*   Move critical implementation details (like the treatment of KB facts) from appendices/footnotes into the main body of the paper and provide clear justifications for them.
*   Elaborate on the precise mechanism of the "match type features."
*   Acknowledge the limitations of the evaluation protocol (i.e., the candidate set construction) and the simulation's simplicity more explicitly.
*   Reconsider the phrasing around "realistic learning conditions" and perhaps provide a more nuanced discussion or experiments on how performance scales with data size.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a principled justification for modeling KB facts as "user utterances"? How does the model learn to distinguish these from actual user inputs, especially concerning the temporal features encoded in the memory?
2.  Can you clarify the exact implementation of the "match type features"? How are the special type words incorporated into the embedding-based representations of candidates for the MemNN, Supervised Embedding, and TF-IDF models?
3.  The paper notes that the rule-based system for Task 6 achieved 40.7% per-response accuracy on the validation set. How does this compare to its performance on the test set? It seems odd to only report validation accuracy for this baseline in the text.
