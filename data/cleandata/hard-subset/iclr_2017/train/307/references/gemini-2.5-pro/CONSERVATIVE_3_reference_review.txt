### Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces a new benchmark dataset and a series of tasks designed to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. Motivated by the lack of standardized and reproducible evaluation frameworks in this area, the authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, structured similarly to the bAbI QA tasks. These tasks are designed to test specific capabilities, such as issuing API calls, handling updates, and using information from a Knowledge Base (KB). The benchmark also includes two datasets derived from real user interactions (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to help validate the findings from the synthetic tasks. The authors provide baseline results for several models, including classic IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, demonstrating that while current end-to-end models show promise, they still fall short of reliably completing complex dialog goals.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Limited Significance of the Primary Contribution:** The paper's main contribution is a new, largely synthetic dataset. While the motivation is sound, the artificial nature of the tasks, which are based on a small number of linguistic templates, significantly limits the generalizability of any conclusions drawn. It is unclear if success on these tasks is indicative of true natural language understanding capabilities required for real-world dialog.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper frames its central question around whether end-to-end models can compete with traditional, handcrafted dialog systems in narrow domains. However, it fails to provide a rigorous comparison against a state-of-the-art traditional system (e.g., a POMDP-based slot-filling system). The provided rule-based baseline for the DSTC2 task is a new implementation and may not represent the true strength of established approaches, leaving the paper's core motivating question largely unanswered.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary concern with this work is the reliance on a synthetic dataset generated from a very limited set of templates (43 for the user, 20 for the bot). This approach creates a testbed that may reward models for learning to manipulate symbols within a constrained, artificial grammar rather than for robustly understanding and generating language. While the bAbI tasks provided a useful starting point for QA, the dialog domain is inherently more complex and less structured. An evaluation framework based on such rigid templates risks overstating the capabilities of the models tested and may not correlate well with performance on real, noisy, and varied human conversations. The paper attempts to mitigate this by including DSTC2 and Concierge data, but the core analysis and task breakdown revolve around the synthetic set.

Furthermore, the paper's experimental setup does not adequately support its claims about gauging the performance of end-to-end systems against traditional ones. The introduction rightly points out that slot-filling and POMDP-based systems are the "workhorse" of traditional goal-oriented dialog. A convincing study would benchmark the end-to-end models against a well-established, high-performing system from that paradigm on the same tasks. Instead, the paper offers a rule-based system for T1-T5 that achieves a perfect score (which is expected, as it is an oracle for the simulation) and a newly implemented rule-based system for T6. The latter's performance is surpassed by a Memory Network, but without knowing how this new rule-based system compares to established DSTC2 competitors, this result is difficult to interpret. The paper effectively compares a new method against a new, unproven baseline, rather than against the established state-of-the-art it claims to be challenging.

Finally, the results themselves demonstrate that the end-to-end models struggle significantly, with per-dialog accuracies remaining very low on the combined task (T5). The introduction of "match type features" seems to be a crucial but ad-hoc mechanism for handling OOV entities. This feels less like a learned capability of the end-to-end model and more like a form of feature engineering that moves away from the pure end-to-end philosophy. If such heuristics are required to achieve even modest performance on a synthetic task, it reinforces skepticism about the readiness of these models for real-world deployment.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While I cannot recommend acceptance at this time, the goal of creating reproducible benchmarks for goal-oriented dialog is important. The following points are offered as constructive feedback:
*   **Strengthen Baselines:** The most critical improvement would be to include a strong, established baseline from the traditional dialog systems literature (e.g., a publicly available POMDP or slot-filling toolkit) and evaluate it on the DSTC2 task (T6). This would provide a much more meaningful point of comparison.
*   **Analyze the Synthetic-to-Real Gap:** The paper claims that the "pattern of results" on synthetic data is a good proxy for real data. This claim could be substantiated with a more formal analysis. For instance, a correlation study between model performance on tasks 1-5 and performance on T6/Concierge across different model architectures and hyperparameters would be more convincing.
*   **Expand Linguistic Diversity:** For the dataset to have a longer-term impact, future versions should incorporate more linguistic diversity, perhaps by using paraphrasing models to generate variations of the templates or by incorporating more real-world utterances.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the development of the rule-based system for Task 6? What was the extent of the manual effort (e.g., person-hours), and how does its architecture and performance compare to the winning systems from the original DSTC2 challenge?
2.  The introduction frames the work as a direct comparison to traditional POMDP-based systems. Was there a reason a well-established system from this family was not included as a baseline? This seems like a critical omission given the paper's stated goals.
3.  The "match type features" are essential for OOV performance. How do you see this approach scaling to domains with much larger KBs, more ambiguous entity names, or a more complex ontology of types? Does this reliance on entity-type matching not undermine the goal of learning "with no assumption on the domain"?
