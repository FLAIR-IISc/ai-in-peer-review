**Summary**

The paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that existing resources are insufficient, as they are either designed for non-goal-oriented "chit-chat" or are unsuitable for training and reproducibly testing end-to-end models. Inspired by the bAbI QA tasks, they propose a suite of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, designed to probe specific capabilities such as issuing API calls, handling updates, and using knowledge base (KB) results. The tasks are grounded in a KB and come with both standard and out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test splits to assess generalization to unseen entities. To bridge the gap between synthetic and real-world performance, the authors also provide two additional tasks based on real dialogs: a converted version of the DSTC2 dataset and a new dataset from a human-operated concierge service. The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of several models, from classical IR baselines to supervised embeddings and Memory Networks, offering valuable insights into the current strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end approaches for goal-oriented dialog.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. The primary reasons are:
1.  **A Significant and Needed Resource:** The paper introduces a well-designed, open-source benchmark that fills a clear gap in the community's resources for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems. The "bAbI-style" decomposition into sub-tasks is a strong design choice that enables fine-grained analysis of model capabilities.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The paper provides a strong set of baseline results and a compelling analysis of current model limitations. The distinction between per-response and per-dialog accuracy highlights a crucial challenge, and the experiments on OOV entities and real-world datasets substantially strengthen the paper's conclusions.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the new dataset and task suite, which is extremely valuable. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing datasets for the purpose of developing and analyzing end-to-end models: reproducibility issues (e.g., crowdsourcing), lack of focus on goal-completion, and unsuitability for end-to-end training. By creating a controlled, reproducible, and analyzable environment, this work provides a much-needed tool for the research community. The task design is thoughtful, breaking down the complex problem of goal-oriented dialog into testable skills like dialog management (T1, T2), KB interaction (T3, T4), and the full pipeline (T5). This allows researchers to pinpoint model failures with much greater precision than a single, monolithic task would.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and serves as an excellent demonstration of the benchmark's utility. The choice of baselines is appropriate, covering traditional IR methods, strong supervised embedding models, and a representative end-to-end neural architecture (Memory Networks). The results are insightful: while end-to-end models show promise by outperforming other machine learning baselines, their low per-dialog accuracy reveals that they still struggle with maintaining consistency and achieving the overall goal. This is a critical finding for the field. Furthermore, the inclusion of the OOV split and the proposed "match type features" directly address the crucial real-world problem of handling unseen entities, showing that with the right architectural components, models can learn to generalize.

Finally, the paper validates its synthetic benchmark by showing that the relative performance of the tested models is consistent on real data from DSTC2 and the new Concierge dataset. This gives confidence that progress on the proposed synthetic tasks is likely to translate to progress on more realistic problems, making the resource an effective and efficient proxy for research and development.

**Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Generation vs. Ranking:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking task over a candidate set of responses. This is a reasonable and common simplification. However, it would be beneficial to explicitly discuss the limitations of this paradigm in the main body (perhaps in the conclusion). The ultimate goal is often generation, and acknowledging that the current setup does not test this capability would provide a more complete picture of the challenges that remain.
*   **Simplicity of Simulated Language:** The user and bot utterances in the synthetic tasks are generated from a relatively small number of patterns (43 for the user, 20 for the bot). While this is in the spirit of creating a controlled testbed, it is a significant simplification of real language. The paper could be strengthened by acknowledging this limitation more directly in Section 3.1 and framing it as a potential axis for future work (e.g., increasing linguistic diversity). The inclusion of the real-world datasets already does a great job of mitigating this concern, but a direct mention would be helpful.
*   **The "Match Type Features":** This is a simple but effective idea. The analysis of its impact is good, particularly on OOV performance. The small performance drop on Task 2 when using these features is interesting. Is this just statistical noise, or could it be that the features add a bias that is slightly unhelpful for that specific update-focused task? A brief comment on this would be welcome.

**Questions for the Authors**

*   The methodology states that the candidate set for ranking is created from all bot utterances and API calls appearing in the training, validation, and *test* sets combined. This seems to leak information from the test set into the model's decision space at test time. While the model hasn't seen the test *dialogs*, it has seen all possible correct responses. Could you clarify the reasoning for this design choice versus using a candidate set derived only from the training set, which would be a stricter test of generalization?
*   Could you elaborate briefly on the process and challenges of converting the DSTC2 data into your task format? Specifically, how were API calls and their results inferred from the provided dialog state annotations? Sharing this experience could be valuable for other researchers looking to adapt existing dialog state tracking corpora for end-to-end evaluation.
