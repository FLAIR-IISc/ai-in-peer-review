### Review

#### 1. Summary of the paper's contribution

This paper introduces a new benchmark suite of tasks designed to evaluate and analyze end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. Motivated by the lack of suitable open-source, reproducible datasets for this setting, the authors propose a set of six tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, inspired by the diagnostic nature of the bAbI QA tasks. The first five tasks are synthetically generated and designed to probe specific capabilities of a dialog agent, such as issuing API calls based on user constraints (Task 1), updating those calls (Task 2), presenting options from a knowledge base (Task 3), providing additional information (Task 4), and conducting a full dialog (Task 5). A key aspect of the dataset design is the inclusion of an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test set to assess generalization to unseen entities. The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of several models, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, establishing strong baselines. The authors find that while modern end-to-end models like Memory Networks are promising, they still struggle with multi-turn consistency, as evidenced by the gap between per-response and per-dialog accuracy. Critically, the paper validates the utility of its synthetic benchmark by showing that the relative performance of models on these tasks is consistent with their performance on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge dataset), suggesting the benchmark is an effective proxy for more realistic conditions.

#### 2. Decision and key reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This paper makes a significant and timely contribution to the field of conversational AI. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **A valuable and well-designed benchmark:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the community—the lack of a standardized, open, and diagnostic benchmark for goal-oriented dialog. The decomposition of the problem into distinct subtasks is an excellent design choice that will enable researchers to perform fine-grained analysis and better understand the specific strengths and weaknesses of their models.
2.  **Rigorous and insightful experimental validation:** The empirical study is comprehensive, establishing strong baselines across a range of model classes. The analysis correctly highlights the critical challenge of maintaining dialog-level correctness, and the validation on two real-world datasets provides strong evidence for the benchmark's utility as a research tool.

#### 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's primary strength lies in its core contribution: the dataset itself. The current landscape for training and evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems is fragmented, relying on proprietary data, non-reproducible human-in-the-loop setups, or datasets not suited for end-to-end training. This work provides a public resource that is not only accessible but thoughtfully designed. The bAbI-style breakdown into atomic skills (querying, updating, presenting results) allows for targeted model development and analysis, a much-needed departure from monolithic performance metrics on complex tasks. The inclusion of an OOV split is particularly laudable, as it directly addresses a known failure mode of many neural models and forces an evaluation of true generalization.

The experimental section is another major strength. The authors compare a sensible range of baselines, from simple IR methods to a strong end-to-end model (Memory Networks). The results presented in Table 2 are insightful, clearly demonstrating that while per-response accuracy can be encouraging, per-dialog accuracy remains very low for more complex tasks (T3, T5), underscoring the difficulty of multi-turn reasoning and consistency. This finding is an important one for the field. Furthermore, the introduction and evaluation of "match type features" is an elegant and effective way to handle OOV entities, providing both a performance boost and a valuable insight into model design.

Crucially, the authors take the extra step of validating their synthetic dataset against real-world data. By converting the DSTC2 dataset and using a proprietary concierge log, they show that the relative performance ranking of the tested models holds (MemNN > Supervised Embeddings > IR). This result lends significant credibility to the benchmark, suggesting that progress on these synthetic tasks is likely to translate to improved performance on real-world problems. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and the experimental setup is sound. This work will likely become a standard benchmark and spur further research in this important area.

#### 4. Additional feedback for improvement

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from my positive assessment of the paper.

*   **Linguistic Diversity of the Simulator:** The simulator uses a fixed set of patterns to generate utterances. While effective for creating controlled tasks, this results in limited linguistic variation. In a future version of the work or dataset, it would be beneficial to explore methods for increasing this diversity, perhaps by using paraphrasing models or incorporating a much larger set of crowdsourced templates. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential future directions would strengthen the paper.
*   **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking task over a set of candidate responses. This is a practical and widely used methodology, but it sidesteps the challenges of open-ended natural language generation. It would be valuable for the paper to include a short discussion in the conclusion about the challenges of moving to a full generation setting and how these tasks might need to be adapted for such an evaluation.
*   **Qualitative Error Analysis:** The appendix includes some excellent examples of model predictions and attentions. It would be beneficial to summarize some key qualitative error patterns in the main body of the paper. For instance, on Task 3 (Displaying options), where Memory Networks struggle, what are the typical mistakes? Do they fail to sort options by rating, repeat an option, or fail to stop after user acceptance? This level of analysis would further highlight the utility of the task decomposition.

#### 5. Questions for the authors

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to solidify my understanding:

1.  **Candidate Set Construction:** The paper states that the candidate set for ranking is composed of all bot utterances and API calls from the training, validation, and test sets combined. Could you please state the size of this candidate set? How sensitive do you believe the results (particularly the absolute accuracy scores) are to this construction? For instance, how would performance change if the candidate set was restricted to only utterances seen during training?
2.  **Match Type Features Nuances:** The "match type features" are shown to be very effective, especially for OOV tasks. Could you elaborate on why performance on Task 2 (Updating API calls) drops slightly when these features are used (from 99.4% to 95.8% per-response accuracy for MemNN)? Is it possible that the model becomes overly reliant on entity type matching and misses more subtle cues required for the update logic?
3.  **Impact of Determinism:** The simulator for Task 1 asks for missing information in a deterministic order. How critical is this assumption for model performance? Have you considered a variant where the clarifying questions are asked in a random order to better simulate real-world interactions where the flow is less predictable?
