### Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces a new suite of tasks and an open-source dataset for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that existing resources are inadequate for this purpose, being either focused on non-goal-oriented chit-chat or designed for specific sub-problems like state tracking rather than end-to-end evaluation. To address this, they propose a set of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain, grounded in a knowledge base (KB). These tasks are designed to be diagnostic, isolating specific system capabilities such as issuing API calls, handling KB results, and dealing with out-of-vocabulary (OOV) entities. The authors provide comprehensive baseline results using several models, including classical information retrieval methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks. To ground their synthetic benchmark in reality, they also evaluate these models on two datasets derived from real user interactions (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service), demonstrating that the relative performance of models on the synthetic tasks is a reasonable proxy for their performance on real data. The key takeaway is that while end-to-end models like Memory Networks are promising, they still fall short of reliably solving complex, multi-turn goal-oriented dialogs.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Addresses a Clear Need with a Methodologically Sound Resource:** The paper correctly identifies a significant gap in the field—the lack of a standardized, reproducible, and analyzable benchmark for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog. The proposed dataset and task suite provide a valuable resource that facilitates rigorous comparison and error analysis, much like the bAbI tasks did for question answering.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Baselines and Analysis:** The authors do not merely present a dataset; they conduct a thorough empirical study. The inclusion of strong, established baselines—from rule-based systems and TF-IDF to supervised embeddings—provides essential context for evaluating the more novel end-to-end models. The finding that a Memory Network can outperform a purpose-built rule-based system on the noisy, real-world DSTC2 data is a particularly compelling result that underscores the potential of learned systems.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its rigorous and systematic approach. The breakdown of a complex dialog into five discrete subtasks (Tasks 1-5) is a sound scientific methodology. It allows researchers to move beyond a single, monolithic performance metric and instead diagnose specific model failings. For example, the results clearly show that while models may learn to handle simple dialog state tracking (T1, T2), they struggle significantly with interpreting and presenting information from a KB (T3), a crucial capability for any real-world transactional agent.

The inclusion of an OOV test set is another methodologically strong choice. Generalization to unseen entities is a critical challenge for dialog systems, and the paper provides a controlled environment to measure it. The proposed "match type features" are a pragmatic solution, and the experiments clearly demonstrate their effectiveness in improving OOV performance for the Memory Network model. This provides a concrete direction for future work.

Furthermore, the effort to validate the synthetic benchmark against real-world data (DSTC2 and the Concierge dataset) is commendable. While synthetic data offers control, its relevance can be questionable. By showing that the relative performance patterns of the evaluated models are consistent across synthetic and real datasets, the authors provide convincing evidence that their benchmark is a useful and relevant tool for the research community. The use of both per-response and the stricter per-dialog accuracy is appropriate and effectively highlights the difficulty of maintaining a coherent and successful conversation, a nuance often missed by turn-level metrics.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong contribution, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen the paper:

*   **Simplicity of Simulated Language:** The use of a limited number of templates to generate user and bot utterances results in simplistic and repetitive language. This is a significant deviation from the variability of real user language. While the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets help mitigate this, the paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations imposed by this linguistic simplicity and how it might affect the generalization of models trained primarily on this data.
*   **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking problem over a predefined set of candidate responses. This is a practical simplification but avoids the more challenging and realistic task of natural language generation. The paper should more clearly acknowledge this limitation and briefly discuss how the findings might (or might not) translate to a generative setting.
*   **Heuristic Nature of "Match Type Features":** The "match type features" mechanism, while effective, is a form of feature engineering that moves away from a purely end-to-end approach. It requires prior knowledge of the KB schema and entity types. A discussion of this trade-off between tabula rasa learning and the integration of light domain knowledge would add valuable nuance.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  In Task 1, the bot asks for missing information in a deterministic order. This seems to significantly simplify the dialog management challenge. What was the rationale for this design choice over a randomized order, and how might performance be affected if the order were not fixed?
2.  The paper notes that the rule-based system for the DSTC2 task (T6) was optimized on the validation set. Could you provide more insight into the extent of this optimization and the manual effort involved? This context is crucial for fairly assessing its performance against the data-driven models.
3.  The results show that "match type features" are highly beneficial for OOV tasks and T4, but they slightly degrade performance on T2 and offer no benefit on T6. Do you have a hypothesis for this variance in effectiveness across different tasks?
