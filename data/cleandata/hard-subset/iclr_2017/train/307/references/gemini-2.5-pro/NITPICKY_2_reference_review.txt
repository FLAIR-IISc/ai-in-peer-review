### Review

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a new suite of benchmark tasks for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems, inspired by the bAbI question-answering dataset. The authors aim to provide a reproducible and lightweight testbed to probe the capabilities and limitations of current models. They propose five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, designed to test specific skills such as dialog management, API call generation, and use of knowledge base (KB) information. The tasks also include an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) setting to test generalization to unseen entities. To ground these synthetic tasks in reality, the authors also adapt the DSTC2 dataset and introduce a new proprietary "Concierge" dataset from a real-world service. They provide baseline results for several models, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, finding that Memory Networks with "match type features" perform best but that significant challenges remain, particularly in per-dialog accuracy.

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  A critical methodological flaw in the experimental setup concerning the construction of the candidate set appears to invalidate the reported results by leaking information from the test set into the training/evaluation procedure.
2.  The paper suffers from a significant number of formatting errors, inconsistencies, and a general lack of polish that undermines confidence in its scientific rigor and careful execution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary and most concerning issue is the evaluation protocol. In Section 3.1.2, the authors state: "Candidates are ranked from a set of all bot utterances and API calls appearing in training, validation and test sets (plain and OOV) for all tasks combined." This implies that during evaluation on the test set, the model is simply ranking a list of candidates that is guaranteed to contain the correct answer. More problematically, if this *same* candidate set is used during training, the model has access to the vocabulary and full responses of the test set. This is a form of data leakage that severely limits the conclusions that can be drawn from the experiments. The task becomes one of discriminating between known candidates rather than predicting a response in a more realistic setting. For a paper whose central contribution is a benchmark, such a fundamental flaw in the evaluation design is unacceptable.

Secondly, the manuscript is replete with errors that suggest a lack of careful preparation. The reference section is particularly problematic, with numerous formatting inconsistencies and errors. For example:
*   Several citations are malformed (e.g., `Bai et al\.,? 2009, Bai et al\.,`, `Kim et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Kim et al\.,`).
*   The `Dodge, 2016` reference at the end of the bibliography appears entirely broken.
*   The use of `et al\.,?` is inconsistent with standard formatting.

This lack of attention to detail extends to the main body of the paper.
*   A critical implementation detail—that KB facts are encoded as "user utterances" in the memory—is relegated to a footnote in the appendix (Footnote 2), obscuring a key part of the model's mechanism for handling KB information.
*   The use of a proprietary dataset ("Concierge") for evaluation, while illustrative, directly contradicts the paper's stated goal to "favor reproducibility and comparisons." Other researchers cannot use this data.
*   The OOV setup is not as challenging as it seems. The paper states that for training, "systems have access to... both KBs." This means the model has seen the OOV entities in the KB, even if not in a dialog context, which significantly aids the "match type features" and misrepresents the model's ability to generalize to truly unseen information.
*   The rule-based baseline for Task 6 is reported to have a validation accuracy of 40.7%, but its test accuracy is curiously absent from the main results in Table 2, making a full comparison with the learning-based models difficult.

While the idea of a diagnostic dataset for goal-oriented dialog is commendable, the execution presented here is not up to the standards of a top-tier publication. The combination of a flawed evaluation protocol and pervasive quality issues makes it impossible to trust the paper's conclusions or to build upon its results with confidence.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   The simulation is quite simplistic, relying on 43 user patterns and 20 bot patterns. This raises questions about how well performance on these tasks truly serves as a "proxy" for real-world performance. The authors should be more circumspect about this limitation.
*   The claim that Task 3 "requires to learn to sort" is strong. It is more likely that the model is learning to reproduce output sequences seen in training rather than learning a generalizable sorting algorithm. This should be rephrased more accurately.
*   The "match type features" are a form of feature engineering. While effective, their novelty is limited, and they rely heavily on having access to a structured KB, which may not always be available.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact procedure for constructing the candidate set for training, validation, and testing? If the test set responses are indeed included in the candidate set used during training, can you provide a justification for this choice and perform an experiment with a more standard protocol (e.g., candidates from the training set only)?
2.  What was the test set accuracy for the rule-based system on Task 6? Why was it not included in Table 2?
3.  Regarding the OOV task, could you clarify why the decision was made to provide the OOV KB to the model during training? Have you considered a more challenging setup where the OOV KB is also held out?
