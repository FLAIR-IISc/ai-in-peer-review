### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a valuable new resource for the community: a suite of tasks designed to test and analyze end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. Drawing inspiration from the bAbI tasks for question answering, the authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain. These tasks are carefully designed to isolate and evaluate specific capabilities essential for goal-oriented conversation, such as issuing API calls (Task 1), handling user updates (Task 2), presenting options from a knowledge base (Task 3), and providing specific information (Task 4). The fifth task combines these skills into a full dialog.

A key contribution is the inclusion of an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test set, which forces models to generalize to new entities not seen during training. To demonstrate the utility of their benchmark, the authors provide a thorough evaluation of several baseline models, from classical IR methods to supervised embeddings and Memory Networks (MemNNs). Finally, to validate that performance on their synthetic tasks is a reasonable proxy for real-world performance, they convert and evaluate the same models on the DSTC2 dataset and a proprietary "Concierge" dataset, showing that the relative model rankings are consistent. The paper concludes that while current end-to-end models like MemNNs show promise, they still struggle with key aspects of goal-oriented dialog, as revealed by low per-dialog success rates.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and useful contribution to the field. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Addresses a critical need:** The paper tackles the well-known problem of evaluating and diagnosing end-to-end dialog systems in a reproducible and systematic way. By providing an open, lightweight, and well-structured benchmark, it facilitates comparative research and helps pinpoint specific model weaknesses, which is crucial for making meaningful progress.
2.  **Rigorous and insightful experimental design:** The methodology is sound and compelling. Breaking the problem into subtasks provides excellent diagnostic value. The inclusion of an OOV setting is particularly important and forward-thinking, as generalization to unseen entities is a major real-world challenge. Crucially, the authors validate their synthetic benchmark against two real-world datasets, providing strong evidence that their tasks are a meaningful proxy for more complex, noisy interactions.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its contribution of a well-conceived public resource. The existing landscape of goal-oriented dialog datasets is fragmented, often proprietary, not designed for end-to-end learning (e.g., only annotated for state tracking), or based on non-reproducible crowdsourcing setups. This work provides a clean, controlled environment analogous to what bAbI provided for QA, allowing researchers to iterate quickly and compare methods on a level playing field.

The experimental analysis is thorough and provides clear takeaways. The choice of baselines is sensible, covering a spectrum from simple IR to a strong end-to-end model (MemNNs). The results presented in Table 2 are particularly illuminating. They clearly show a performance hierarchy (MemNN > Supervised Embeddings > IR) that holds across synthetic and real data. More importantly, the gap between per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy starkly illustrates the core challenge: stringing together a sequence of individually plausible turns does not guarantee task success. This finding, while perhaps intuitive, is quantified effectively here. For instance, on Task 3 (Displaying options), the best model achieves 74.7% per-response accuracy but 0% per-dialog accuracy, cleanly demonstrating a specific failure mode in using KB results.

The introduction of "match type features" is a simple but clever technique to help embedding-based models handle OOV entities, and the paper demonstrates its effectiveness, particularly in the OOV setting. This adds another layer of value, suggesting a practical method for improving such models. The validation on DSTC2 and the Concierge dataset is a critical step that grounds the entire work. By showing that the relative performance patterns are preserved, the authors make a convincing case that insights gained from their synthetic tasks can transfer to real-world challenges.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to make it even better. These are suggestions for improvement, not criticisms that undermine the core contribution.

*   **Linguistic Diversity of the Simulator:** The simulator uses a limited set of templates (43 for the user, 20 for the bot). This creates a controlled environment but also simplifies the natural language understanding (NLU) challenge significantly. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of this limitation and how the results might change with greater linguistic variance.
*   **Ranking vs. Generation:** The evaluation is framed as a ranking problem over a candidate set, not a free-form generation task. This is a practical and standard choice for ensuring automated and comparable evaluation. However, it would be helpful to explicitly state this choice and briefly comment on the different challenges a generative setting might introduce (e.g., hallucinating incorrect facts, maintaining fluency).
*   **Task Success Metric:** The per-dialog accuracy is a very strict, all-or-nothing metric. While valuable for highlighting brittleness, it might be overly harsh. A single incorrect turn fails the entire dialog, even if the system could have potentially recovered. It might be interesting to consider a softer "task success" metric in future work (e.g., was the correct restaurant eventually identified?), and a brief mention of this could be valuable in the discussion.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   Could you comment on the sensitivity of your models, particularly the MemNN, to the size of the candidate set? The paper notes it's composed of all bot utterances from all tasks—how does performance scale as this set grows?
*   The deterministic order of questioning in Task 1 is noted as making prediction possible. Did you experiment with a randomized order for asking for missing slots, and if so, how did it impact performance? This seems like a simple way to increase the task's difficulty and realism.
*   In the analysis of the Concierge dataset, you mention that API calls are predicted without arguments. Could you elaborate on how a model is expected to learn to handle this ambiguity (i.e., when to call Yelp vs. OpenTable) without explicit grounding, and how this differs from the structured API calls in Tasks 1-6?
