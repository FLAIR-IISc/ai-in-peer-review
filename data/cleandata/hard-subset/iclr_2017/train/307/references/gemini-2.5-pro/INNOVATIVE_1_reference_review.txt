### Review

**Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that existing datasets are inadequate for this purpose, being either proprietary, noisy, or not suited for end-to-end training. Drawing inspiration from the bAbI question-answering tasks, they propose a suite of five synthetic tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, designed to probe specific capabilities of a dialog agent, such as tracking state, issuing API calls, and reasoning over knowledge base (KB) results. A key feature is the inclusion of an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test set to evaluate generalization to unseen entities. To ground these synthetic tasks in reality, the authors also provide results on a converted version of the DSTC2 dataset and a new, proprietary dataset from a human-operated concierge service. They benchmark several models, including classic IR baselines and Memory Networks, demonstrating that while current end-to-end models show promise, they still struggle with core aspects of goal-oriented conversation, particularly when interacting with a KB. The paper's primary contribution is the open-source dataset itself, which provides a clean, reproducible, and diagnostic tool for analyzing and advancing goal-oriented dialog systems.

**Decision: Accept**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper's core contribution is not a new model, but a new evaluation paradigm and public resource for a critically important area of research. This work moves beyond simply reporting a new state-of-the-art on an existing, messy dataset. Instead, it provides a structured, diagnostic framework that enables the community to understand *why* models fail and to focus research on specific weaknesses. This is a bold and highly valuable contribution that has the potential to guide the field in a more principled direction.
2.  **Insightful Problem Decomposition:** The breakdown of a complex, goal-oriented dialog into five distinct subtasks is extremely insightful. It allows for a granular analysis of model capabilities, separating dialog management (T1, T2) from KB interaction and reasoning (T3, T4). The results clearly demonstrate that even a strong model like a Memory Network can succeed at the former while failing at the latter. This diagnostic power is precisely what is missing from existing benchmarks and is a major strength of this work.

**Supporting Arguments**
The main value of this paper lies in its conceptual contribution to evaluation methodology. While building a new dataset may not always be seen as a top-tier research contribution, this work is an exception because of its thoughtful design and the clear need it addresses. Traditional dialog systems have relied on hand-crafted features and slot-filling, while end-to-end systems have excelled at chit-chat. This paper tackles the crucial intersection of these two areas, asking how we can rigorously measure the ability of end-to-end models to perform concrete, goal-oriented tasks.

The design of the OOV test set is a particularly strong element. It forces models to move beyond simple memorization of training dialogs and test their ability to generalize by manipulating symbols and entities they have never seen in a conversational context. The introduction of "match type features" is a clever, simple mechanism to address this, and the experiments clearly show its effectiveness, highlighting a key challenge for pure embedding-based approaches.

A potential major criticism is the synthetic nature of the primary tasks (T1-T5). The language is generated from a small number of templates, which is a far cry from the complexity of real human conversation. However, the authors proactively address this weakness. First, they argue correctly that the purpose is not to solve natural language understanding in its entirety, but to create a controlled environment to test specific reasoning abilities. Second, and more importantly, they validate their approach by showing that the relative performance of the benchmarked models on the synthetic tasks mirrors their performance on the real-world DSTC2 and Concierge datasets. This provides compelling evidence that the synthetic tasks, despite their simplicity, are a good proxy for the challenges encountered in real dialogs, justifying their use as a research tool.

The experimental analysis is thorough for a resource paper. The choice of baselines, from simple IR to Memory Networks, provides a clear picture of the landscape. The results are not just a leaderboard; they tell a story about where current neural models succeed and fail. The finding that MemNNs struggle with T3 (displaying options, which requires sorting) and T4 (providing extra info) until augmented with match type features is a key insight. It suggests that architectural innovations are needed for these models to better integrate and reason over symbolic information retrieved from external knowledge sources.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**
While I am enthusiastic about this paper, the following points could further strengthen it:

*   **Linguistic Diversity:** The reliance on a small set of templates is the work's main limitation. While justified for this initial proposal, it would be valuable for the authors to comment on future plans to increase the linguistic diversity of the tasks, perhaps by incorporating paraphrasing models or crowd-sourcing to create more varied utterances for the same underlying semantic meaning.
*   **Model Selection:** The choice of Memory Networks is well-justified. However, at the time of this work, RNN/LSTM-based sequence-to-sequence models were also a dominant architecture for dialog. Including a baseline from this family would have made the comparison more comprehensive and strengthened the claim that MemNNs are a suitable end-to-end model for these tasks.
*   **The "Match Type" Feature:** This feature is an effective and pragmatic solution to the OOV problem. However, it feels somewhat like a carefully engineered feature that moves away from a purely "end-to-end" philosophy. Could the authors elaborate on their view of this? Is this kind of explicit feature engineering a necessary component for goal-oriented dialog with KBs, or do they envision future architectures that could learn to perform this type-based matching implicitly?
*   **Concierge Dataset:** The inclusion of the Concierge dataset is a great sanity check. Given that it is proprietary, its utility to the community is limited. Would it be possible to release a heavily anonymized and subsampled version of the data, or at least provide more detailed statistics and examples to give a better flavor of its complexity compared to DSTC2?
