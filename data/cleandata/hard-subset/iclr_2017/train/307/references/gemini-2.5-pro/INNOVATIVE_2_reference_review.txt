### Summary

This paper addresses a crucial gap in the development of goal-oriented dialog systems: the lack of a standardized, reproducible, and diagnostic benchmark for evaluating end-to-end models. The authors propose a new suite of tasks, inspired by the bAbI dataset for question answering, set in the domain of restaurant reservations. The contribution is twofold: first, a set of five synthetic, progressively difficult tasks that break down the goal-oriented dialog problem into core capabilities like issuing API calls, handling knowledge base (KB) results, and managing out-of-vocabulary (OOV) entities. Second, the paper provides a thorough evaluation of several baseline models, from classical IR to Memory Networks, demonstrating the utility of the benchmark in revealing specific strengths and weaknesses of current architectures. A key finding is that while models show promise, particularly with architectures like Memory Networks, they still fail at complex skills like interpreting and presenting KB results, highlighting clear directions for future research. The authors further support the validity of their synthetic tasks by showing that the relative performance of models on their benchmark correlates with performance on two real-world datasets.

### Decision: Accept

This paper is a clear accept. It makes a significant and timely contribution not by proposing yet another incremental model improvement, but by providing the community with a new tool and a new methodology for research. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper introduces a novel and much-needed evaluation framework. The idea of creating a "bAbI for dialog" is a bold and insightful step forward. It shifts the focus from monolithic, often uninterpretable performance on noisy datasets towards a more scientific, diagnostic approach where specific model capabilities can be isolated and tested. This kind of foundational work is essential for making systematic progress in a complex field like conversational AI.
2.  **Bold Hypothesis with Supporting Evidence:** The core hypothesis—that performance on these controlled, synthetic tasks can serve as a useful proxy for real-world capabilities—is a bold one. The paper does a commendable job of supporting this claim by evaluating models not only on the proposed tasks but also on the DSTC2 and a real-world Concierge dataset, demonstrating that the observed performance patterns hold. This provides confidence that improvements on the proposed benchmark will likely translate to more practical settings.

### Supporting Arguments

This work is exactly the kind of submission that ICLR should champion. It prioritizes a significant new idea over methodological perfection in a single model. The current landscape for goal-oriented dialog research is hampered by a lack of good benchmarks; datasets are often proprietary, noisy, small, or not designed for end-to-end learning. This paper provides a clean, open, and lightweight resource that directly addresses this problem.

The diagnostic power of the task breakdown is a major strength. By separating concerns like dialog state tracking (Task 1 & 2), KB interaction (Task 3 & 4), and their combination (Task 5), the authors enable researchers to pinpoint exactly where their models are failing. The results showing that Memory Networks struggle with Task 3 (Displaying options) is a far more useful insight than a single, lower accuracy score on a complex, monolithic task. It tells the community precisely where to focus its efforts: on integrating symbolic reasoning over KB results with neural language models.

Furthermore, the deliberate inclusion of an OOV test set is a brilliant and forward-thinking design choice. The ability to generalize to unseen entities is a cornerstone of any practical dialog system, and explicitly designing a test for this capability is a significant contribution. The analysis of "match type features" further illuminates the challenges that current embedding-based models face with OOV entities and suggests a path forward.

While one could criticize the use of templated language in the synthetic tasks, this is a necessary simplification to achieve the goals of reproducibility and interpretability. For a diagnostic tool, this controlled environment is a feature, not a bug. The authors wisely mitigate this potential weakness by validating their findings on real-world data.

### Additional Feedback

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Generation vs. Ranking:** The current evaluation is based on ranking a pre-defined set of candidates. This is a pragmatic choice, but it sidesteps the more challenging problem of natural language generation. It would be valuable for the authors to briefly discuss this limitation and the potential challenges of extending their benchmark to a generative setting.
*   **Linguistic Diversity:** The use of a small number of templates for utterances is a key simplification. While justified, a discussion on the potential gap between performance on this benchmark and performance on more linguistically diverse, real-world conversations would be welcome. Future work could explore augmenting the dataset with paraphrases to increase this diversity without sacrificing control.
*   **Proprietary Data:** The use of the proprietary Concierge dataset, while providing valuable validation, is a minor weakness as results on it are not fully reproducible by the community. Perhaps the authors could release more detailed statistics or heavily anonymized examples from this dataset to further strengthen their claims.

### Questions for Authors

1.  The "match type features" are shown to be very effective, especially for OOV entities. However, they feel somewhat like a hand-engineered feature being injected into an end-to-end system. Could you comment on this? Do you see a path towards learning such entity-typing capabilities in a more end-to-end fashion within the model itself?
2.  In Task 1, the bot asks for information in a deterministic order. How sensitive are the models to this assumption? Have you experimented with randomizing the order in which the bot needs to elicit information, and how does that affect performance?
3.  The paper argues that the relative ordering of model performance is consistent between the synthetic and real datasets. Is it possible to provide a more quantitative analysis of this correlation? For instance, does the magnitude of the performance gap between models remain proportional across the different datasets?
