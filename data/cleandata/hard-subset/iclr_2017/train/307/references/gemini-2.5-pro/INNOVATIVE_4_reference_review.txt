### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while end-to-end models have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their capabilities in structured, goal-driven conversations remain poorly understood, partly due to a lack of suitable, reproducible evaluation resources. To address this, they propose a set of six tasks centered around restaurant reservations, explicitly designed to be a diagnostic tool akin to the bAbI tasks for question answering.

The core contribution is a suite of five synthetically generated tasks that systematically test different dialog capabilities: issuing API calls (Task 1), updating them (Task 2), presenting options from a knowledge base (Task 3), providing extra information (Task 4), and conducting a full dialog (Task 5). Crucially, the authors include an Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set to evaluate generalization to new entities not seen during training. The benchmark is further grounded by including a sixth task derived from the real-world DSTC2 dataset and an additional evaluation on a proprietary "Concierge" dataset, demonstrating that findings on the synthetic tasks correlate with performance on real, noisy data. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of several baselines, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks, highlighting the strengths and, more importantly, the weaknesses of current end-to-end approaches.

**Decision: Strong Accept**

This paper is a clear and strong accept. Its primary contribution is not a novel model architecture or a state-of-the-art result, but something arguably more valuable: a novel and well-designed diagnostic benchmark that enables systematic analysis of goal-oriented dialog systems. This work takes a bold and necessary step away from monolithic, often-uninterpretable performance metrics on noisy datasets, and instead provides a framework for understanding precisely where our models succeed and fail. This kind of foundational contribution is exactly what the field needs to make principled progress.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The conception of a "bAbI for dialog" is a significant and novel idea. The current landscape of dialog evaluation is fraught with issues of reproducibility (e.g., live user studies) and questionable correlation with true task success (e.g., BLEU scores for dialog). By creating a lightweight, open, and controlled testbed, this paper provides a resource that can catalyze research. The breakdown into subtasks is particularly insightful, as it allows researchers to isolate and tackle specific challenges like KB interaction, state tracking, and entity generalization, rather than treating the dialog problem as an undifferentiated black box. This is a foundational piece of work that will likely be a standard benchmark for years to come.

2.  **Excellent Diagnostic Design:** The design of the tasks and the OOV split is a major strength. It moves beyond simply asking "how well does the model perform?" to asking "what can the model do?". The experiments clearly show that current models like Memory Networks can learn to handle basic dialog management and API calls (Tasks 1 & 2), but struggle significantly with interpreting and utilizing the symbolic information returned from a KB (Task 3). The stark difference between per-response accuracy and the near-zero per-dialog accuracy on harder tasks is a powerful illustration of the brittleness of current systems. This clear diagnosis of failure is immensely valuable and points directly to critical areas for future research.

3.  **Validation on Real-World Data:** A potential criticism of a synthetic benchmark is its questionable relevance to real-world scenarios. The authors wisely preempt this by evaluating their models on DSTC2 and the Concierge dataset. The finding that the relative performance patterns of the models hold across synthetic and real data is a crucial result that validates the entire approach. It provides confidence that improvements on the proposed synthetic tasks are likely to translate into meaningful gains on real, noisy, and complex human conversations.

While the models tested are not state-of-the-art by today's standards and the synthetic language is simplistic, these are minor points that do not detract from the core contribution. In fact, the failure of these models serves to underscore the utility and challenge of the benchmark. The goal was not to solve goal-oriented dialog, but to create a tool to help others do so, and in that, the paper succeeds unequivocally.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive and are not critical to my decision.
*   **Linguistic Diversity:** The synthetic user utterances are generated from a small set of patterns. While this ensures control, it also creates a risk that models learn to solve the tasks by pattern matching the template structure rather than by genuine understanding. It would be valuable to discuss this limitation and suggest potential future work on increasing the linguistic variety, perhaps using modern paraphrastic generation techniques.
*   **The "Match Type" Feature:** The introduction of match type features is a clever and pragmatic solution to the OOV problem. However, it feels like a well-engineered piece of domain knowledge rather than a learned, end-to-end capability. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on how this functionality might be learned more organically within a neural architecture, perhaps hinting at future research directions like entity-aware attention mechanisms or copy networks.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  The synthetic language is intentionally simple. Could you comment on how you expect the relative performance of the tested models to change if the linguistic diversity of the user utterances were significantly increased, approaching the complexity seen in the Concierge dataset? Do you believe the core reasoning challenges highlighted by the tasks would remain the primary bottleneck?
2.  The rule-based system for Task 6 was outperformed by the Memory Network. Could you provide a rough estimate of the human effort (e.g., in hours) required to create this rule-based system? This would provide valuable context for the trade-offs between engineered systems and learned end-to-end models.
