### **Summary**

This paper introduces a new benchmark dataset for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while end-to-end models are promising, their capabilities in goal-oriented settings are not well understood, partly due to a lack of suitable, reproducible, and analyzable evaluation resources. To address this, they propose a set of tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, inspired by the bAbI QA dataset. The benchmark consists of five synthetic tasks designed to test specific abilities like issuing API calls, handling updates, and interpreting knowledge base (KB) results. A key feature is an Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set designed to measure generalization to new entities. The authors supplement the synthetic data by converting the DSTC2 dataset into their format (Task 6) and also test on a real-world human-human concierge dataset. They provide a thorough evaluation of several baselines, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (MemNNs). Their experiments show that MemNNs, particularly when augmented with proposed "match type features," are the strongest learning-based models but still struggle to achieve high per-dialog accuracy on complex tasks, highlighting key areas for future research.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Addresses a Critical Need:** The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a much-needed, publicly available, and well-designed benchmark for goal-oriented dialog. The lack of standardized testbeds has hindered reproducible research and clear comparisons between end-to-end models in this practical and important application area.
2.  **Thoughtful Design and Rigorous Evaluation:** The design of the tasks is a major strength. By breaking down the complex problem of a full dialog into sub-tasks (T1-T5), the benchmark facilitates targeted analysis of model capabilities. The inclusion of an OOV setting is particularly crucial for assessing a model's ability to generalize beyond its training vocabulary, a known weakness of many neural approaches. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments with strong baselines and insightful analysis, and the validation on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) strengthens the relevance of the synthetic tasks.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The motivation for this work is exceptionally clear. The authors correctly identify the gap between the promise of end-to-end dialog systems and the reality of evaluating them robustly in goal-oriented scenarios. Traditional slot-filling systems work well but don't scale, while end-to-end systems are scalable but their performance on complex, multi-step tasks involving external knowledge is an open question. This benchmark provides the community with the tools to start answering this question systematically.

The design of the dataset itself is commendable. The progression from simpler components of a dialog (like issuing an API call in T1) to a full, combined dialog (T5) is logical and allows researchers to pinpoint model failures. For instance, the experimental results clearly show that while modern end-to-end models can learn to track state and issue API calls, they fail when required to interpret the results of those calls and present them coherently to the user (as seen in the low per-dialog accuracy on T3). This is a critical insight that would be difficult to extract from a single, monolithic task. The OOV evaluation setup is another major strength, directly addressing the challenge of handling new entities like restaurant names or locations that are common in real-world applications.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides a strong set of baselines for future work. The comparison between IR models, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks is comprehensive. The finding that MemNNs outperform a rule-based system on the noisy, real-world DSTC2 data is a powerful argument for the potential of learning-based approaches. Furthermore, the observation that the relative performance of the models is consistent across the synthetic tasks and the two real-world datasets provides strong evidence for the paper's central claim: that this benchmark can serve as a valuable and reliable proxy for real-world performance.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Generative vs. Ranking Evaluation:** The current evaluation is framed as a ranking task over a candidate set. While practical, this sidesteps the challenges of natural language generation. It would strengthen the paper to briefly acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion and perhaps discuss how the benchmark could be extended or adapted for generative models in the future.
*   **Clarity on Rule-Based Baseline in Results:** The paper states that the hand-coded rule-based system for DSTC2 (T6) was outperformed by the MemNN. This is a very important result. For clarity and impact, it would be beneficial to add the test accuracy of the rule-based system for T6 directly into the main results table (Table 2 is referenced but not provided) to allow for a direct comparison with the learning-based methods.
*   **Linguistic Diversity of Synthetic Data:** The simulation uses a fixed number of patterns (43 for the user, 20 for the bot) to generate utterances. This might limit the linguistic diversity and create a potential for models to overfit to the simulator's specific phrasing. A short discussion on this potential limitation and its implications would be valuable.
*   **Proprietary Data:** The use of the proprietary "Concierge" dataset is a double-edged sword. It strongly supports the paper's claims about the benchmark's relevance, but its private nature means these specific results are not fully reproducible by the community. This is a minor point, as the core contribution is the public dataset, but it is worth noting.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   The "match type features" are an elegant and effective way to incorporate knowledge about entities. Did you consider alternative approaches for handling OOV words, such as character-level embeddings or pointer/copy mechanisms, which are common in related sequence tasks? How do you think they would perform in this setting?
*   The gap between per-response accuracy and per-dialog accuracy is large, especially on T3 and T5. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis? For instance, in the full dialogs (T5), at which stage do models most frequently make the first, fatal error? Is it in understanding the initial request, interpreting the KB results, or something else? Such analysis would be highly valuable for guiding future research.
*   Your choice of Memory Networks is well-justified. Given the strong recent performance of Transformer-based architectures on many NLP tasks, have you considered them as a baseline? How might their self-attention mechanism compare to the MemNN's iterative memory-read mechanism for tracking state and reasoning over the dialog history in these tasks?
