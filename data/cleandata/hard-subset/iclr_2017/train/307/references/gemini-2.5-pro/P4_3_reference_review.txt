### 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces a new open-source benchmark for evaluating end-to-end, goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors argue that while traditional slot-filling systems perform well on narrow-domain tasks, they are difficult to scale, and the capabilities of scalable end-to-end systems in goal-oriented settings are not well understood due to a lack of suitable evaluation resources. To address this, the paper presents a suite of tasks in the restaurant reservation domain, inspired by the bAbI dataset for question answering.

The contribution is threefold:
1.  A set of five synthetically generated tasks that systematically test distinct capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog, such as issuing and updating API calls, interpreting KB results to present options, and providing additional information. The tasks are designed to be lightweight and reproducible. A crucial aspect is the inclusion of an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) test set to evaluate generalization to unseen entities.
2.  The integration of two datasets derived from real-world interactions (DSTC2 and an anonymized "Concierge" dataset) into the same format, allowing for comparison between performance on synthetic and real data.
3.  A comprehensive empirical evaluation of several baseline models on these tasks, including classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks. The results provide a detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of current end-to-end models, showing they can learn to handle dialog state and some API interactions but struggle with interpreting and using KB query results, especially when reasoning (e.g., sorting) is required. The paper demonstrates that the performance patterns observed on the synthetic tasks are a good proxy for performance on real data.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons
**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper introduces a well-designed, open, and reproducible benchmark for a critical application area (goal-oriented dialog) where such resources are lacking. The structured, multi-task framework enables systematic and fine-grained analysis of model capabilities, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2.  The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides valuable insights. By comparing multiple baselines and analyzing performance on both synthetic and real data, the authors convincingly demonstrate the utility of their benchmark and highlight specific, unsolved challenges for current end-to-end models.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision
The paper's primary strength lies in its contribution of a high-quality research resource. The motivation is clear and well-articulated: progress in end-to-end goal-oriented dialog is hampered by a lack of standardized, interpretable, and reproducible evaluation frameworks. This work directly addresses this gap.

The design of the synthetic tasks is a key strength. By breaking down the complex process of a goal-oriented conversation into five distinct sub-tasks (T1-T5), the authors provide a powerful tool for error analysis. This allows researchers to isolate specific model failings, such as the difficulty Memory Networks have with Task 3 (Displaying options, which requires sorting), which is obscured in a monolithic "full dialog" evaluation. The inclusion of an OOV split is also crucial, as generalization to new entities (e.g., new restaurants or locations) is a fundamental requirement for real-world dialog systems.

The experimental methodology is sound and supports the paper's claims. The authors provide a strong set of baselines, ranging from non-learning IR methods to rule-based systems and end-to-end neural networks. The results are presented clearly using both per-response and per-dialog accuracy, with the latter being a particularly important metric for goal completion. The finding that the relative performance of different models is consistent across the synthetic tasks and the two real-world datasets (DSTC2, Concierge) validates the benchmark's design, suggesting that improvements on the controlled, synthetic tasks are likely to translate to more complex, realistic scenarios. The analysis is insightful, correctly identifying that while models can learn to manage conversational flow and API calls, they struggle with reasoning over the symbolic information returned from a KB. This points to clear directions for future research.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem, proposed solution, and experimental results are all explained with clarity. By making the datasets and tasks publicly available, the authors provide a valuable service to the community that is likely to foster reproducible research and accelerate progress.

### 4. Additional Feedback
The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   The construction of the candidate set for the ranking evaluation could be clarified. The paper states that candidates are drawn from "all bot utterances and API calls appearing in training, validation and test sets (plain and OOV) for all tasks combined." This implies that at test time, the model chooses from a set that includes the ground truth response. While this is a common setup, it is worth discussing the potential for information leakage and whether a more stringent evaluation (e.g., using only candidates seen in the training set) was considered.
*   The paper notes that the rule-based system for Task 6 was constructed with "28 rules and extracted 21 patterns." While a full description is not necessary, providing a few examples of these rules in an appendix could offer more insight into the system's logic and the complexity of hand-crafting a solution for noisy, real-world data.
*   The evaluation is framed as a ranking task. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on the relationship between this setup and a generative one. For example, could a model that achieves high ranking accuracy be reasonably expected to perform well in a generation setting? This could help contextualize the results for researchers working on generative dialog models.

### 5. Questions for the Authors
*   Could you elaborate on the failure modes for Task 3 (Displaying options)? The task requires sorting restaurants by rating. Does the Memory Network fail by suggesting incorrect restaurants altogether, or does it correctly identify the restaurants but fail to present them in the specified sorted order? Understanding this could provide deeper insight into the model's reasoning limitations.
*   For the Concierge dataset, the anonymization process involved NER and regex filtering. Can you provide more detail on the verification process to ensure that this automated filtering did not inadvertently remove non-PII information that was nonetheless crucial for understanding the dialog context?
*   Regarding the candidate set used for evaluation: Have you experimented with a setup where the candidate set at test time is restricted to only those responses observed in the training data? This would represent a more challenging generalization task, and it would be interesting to see how the model performances are affected.
