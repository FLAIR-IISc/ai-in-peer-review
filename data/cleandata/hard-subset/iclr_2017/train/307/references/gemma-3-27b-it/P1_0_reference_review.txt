## ICLR 2024 Review

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents a novel benchmark for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. Recognizing the limitations of existing datasets and the difficulty of scaling traditional slot-filling approaches, the authors propose a set of five tasks grounded in a knowledge base, designed to isolate and test core dialog capabilities like API querying, knowledge interpretation, and dialog management. They demonstrate the utility of this benchmark by evaluating Memory Networks (MemNNs) and comparing their performance against rule-based and information retrieval baselines, both on the synthetic tasks and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge dataset). The key finding is that while end-to-end models show promise, they still lag behind traditional methods in achieving reliable goal completion (high per-dialog accuracy).

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper tackles a crucial open problem – evaluating goal-oriented end-to-end dialog systems – and provides a valuable contribution in the form of a well-defined, reproducible benchmark. The experiments are thorough, and the results clearly illustrate the current limitations of end-to-end approaches while offering a path forward for future research.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper articulates the challenges of evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems and convincingly argues for the need for a benchmark beyond chit-chat metrics. The motivation is strong and relevant.
* **Well-Designed Benchmark:** The proposed tasks are thoughtfully designed to break down the complex problem of goal-oriented dialog into manageable subtasks, allowing for targeted error analysis. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets strengthens the validity of the findings. The creation of an OOV test set is a particularly strong feature, pushing the boundaries of generalization.
* **Rigorous Experiments:** The comparison against multiple baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, MemNNs) provides a comprehensive assessment of the MemNN performance. The ablation studies (with and without match-type features) further contribute to the analysis.
* **Compelling Results:** The results are clearly presented and support the authors' claims that MemNNs can learn certain dialog skills but struggle with full goal completion. The consistency of the results across synthetic and real-world datasets is encouraging.
* **Reproducibility:** The public availability of the dataset (http://fb.ai/babi) is a significant strength, fostering further research in this area.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While hyperparameters are listed in the Appendix, a more detailed discussion of the tuning process and the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings would be beneficial.
* **Error Analysis:** The paper mentions the need for error analysis "by design," but could benefit from a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the MemNN's failure cases. Providing specific examples of incorrect predictions and identifying the underlying reasons would provide valuable insights.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** Expand the discussion of limitations. For example, the choice of Memory Networks as the primary end-to-end model is reasonable, but acknowledging the potential for other architectures (e.g., Transformers) to perform differently would enhance the paper's objectivity.
* **Concierge Dataset Details:** Provide more information on the concierge dataset – what specific service was it from, what were the characteristics of the users and operators, and how was the data anonymized?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Task Complexity:**  Were any attempts made to assess the inherent difficulty of each task?  Is it possible that some tasks are intrinsically harder for the models tested, beyond the stated goals of the benchmark?
* **Match-Type Features:** The introduction of match-type features significantly improves performance, especially on OOV examples. Could you elaborate on the design choices for these features and whether a more extensive feature engineering approach (e.g., incorporating entity types beyond the KB) could yield further improvements?
* **Scaling to Larger KBs:**  The current KB is relatively small. How do you anticipate the performance of the models will scale as the KB size and complexity increase?
* **Real-World Deployment:** While the benchmark provides valuable insights, what are the key challenges that would need to be addressed to deploy these models in a real-world restaurant booking system, beyond improving per-dialog accuracy?  



In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of dialog systems. The proposed benchmark is well-designed, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are informative. Addressing the minor feedback points would further strengthen the paper and solidify its impact.
