This paper presents a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors construct a set of five tasks, designed to isolate crucial capabilities like API querying and KB interpretation, alongside evaluations on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service). The motivation is strong: existing benchmarks lack reproducibility and focus too heavily on chit-chat, while goal-oriented tasks demand more than just language modeling. The work is well-written and the proposed tasks seem thoughtfully designed.

**Reject** with a recommendation for revision. While the paper identifies a clear problem and proposes a valuable resource, the results presented don’t convincingly demonstrate the utility of the proposed tasks as a *diagnostic* tool for end-to-end systems. The performance gap between methods is substantial, but the improvements observed with the introduced “match type features” are limited, and the per-dialog accuracy remains very low across all tasks, even in the synthetic setting. This casts doubt on the tasks' sensitivity to the nuances of end-to-end learning.

The primary weakness is the limited insight gained from the experiments. The fact that Memory Networks outperform simpler baselines is expected, but the analysis doesn't pinpoint *where* end-to-end models struggle specifically, beyond a general inability to maintain dialog coherence (low per-dialog accuracy). The tasks, while well-defined, appear too simplistic. For example, Task 1, issuing API calls, is solved reasonably well, but the deterministic nature of the task (bot asks questions in a predefined order) significantly reduces the complexity. This limits the ability to assess the model’s ability to handle ambiguity or user initiative. The OOV testing is a good idea, but the improvement with match-type features, while present, is not dramatic, suggesting the OOV problem may not be the primary bottleneck. The supplementary data analysis with DSTC2 and the concierge dataset only confirms the trend observed in the synthetic tasks – the Memory Networks are better, but still far from perfect – without revealing further insights. The analysis of the results lacks depth. For instance, the authors mention that MemNNs “fail to interpret the results of API calls to display options” (Task 3), but don’t delve into *why* this is happening. Are the representations of restaurant features inadequate? Is the attention mechanism failing to focus on relevant information?  More detailed error analysis is crucial.

Furthermore, while the authors explicitly state they are not aiming to achieve SOTA performance, a comparison to a more sophisticated dialogue state tracker would provide a useful point of reference. It would strengthen the argument that the tasks are indeed evaluating different capabilities than those addressed by traditional approaches. The ablation study of the hyperparameters in the appendix is helpful but lacks statistical significance tests.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Task Complexity:** Consider increasing the complexity of the tasks. For example, in Task 3, instead of simply listing restaurants by rating, require the bot to *justify* its recommendations based on user preferences. In Task 5, introduce user disfluencies, clarifications, and out-of-scope requests to simulate more realistic conversations.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide a more detailed qualitative error analysis. Include examples of dialogs where the model fails and explain the likely reasons for the failure. Visualizing attention weights could be particularly insightful.
*   **Metric Evaluation:** Explore alternative evaluation metrics beyond per-response and per-dialog accuracy. For instance, consider metrics that assess the naturalness and coherence of the generated responses.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Expand the ablation studies to include different variations of the Memory Network architecture, such as different hop sizes, attention mechanisms, or embedding dimensions. Include statistical significance to the results.
*   **Comparison with SOTA:** Include a comparison with a strong, contemporary dialogue state tracker (e.g., a transformer-based model) to better understand the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end models.
* **KB integration:** How is the KB incorporated into the model architecture? Are the restaurant features directly used as inputs, or are they retrieved dynamically during the conversation? Clarify the exact mechanism.
* **Dataset Scale**: While the synthetic datasets are designed for focused evaluation, are they large enough to train reasonably complex models (like the Memory Networks) effectively? A sensitivity analysis varying the training set size might be beneficial.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide examples of failure cases for each task, along with a detailed explanation of why the model failed?
*   Have you experimented with different ways of representing the KB features (e.g., using pre-trained embeddings or incorporating relational information)?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters? Did you perform a comprehensive hyperparameter search?
*   What are the limitations of the proposed tasks, and what types of dialog capabilities are not addressed by this benchmark?
*   How would you extend these tasks to cover more complex goal-oriented scenarios (e.g., multi-turn negotiations, complex constraints)?
