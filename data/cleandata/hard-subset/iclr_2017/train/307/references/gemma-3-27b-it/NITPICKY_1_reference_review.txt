This paper introduces a set of tasks designed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors argue that existing datasets are either unsuitable for end-to-end learning, lack reproducibility, or are noisy. They propose a simulated environment with a knowledge base and five tasks testing dialog management, KB querying, and entity handling. The work also includes experiments on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to validate the relevance of the synthetic tasks. The authors evaluate Memory Networks and demonstrate that while they show promise, per-dialog accuracy remains low, suggesting room for improvement in end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.

**Reject.** While the paper addresses a valid and important problem – evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented scenarios – the overall presentation and execution are marred by a multitude of minor flaws that, collectively, undermine confidence in the results and the conclusions drawn. The level of detail is excessive to the point of being distracting, and the relentless focus on minutiae overshadows the core contributions.

The primary issue lies in the overabundance of experimental detail coupled with a lack of clear, concise reporting of key findings. Tables are numerous and overwhelming. The extensive hyperparameter tuning details (Appendix C) feel unnecessary for a review intended to assess the core idea. Furthermore, the authors spend considerable effort detailing the implementation of Memory Networks, which isn't the central contribution. More focus should have been placed on analyzing *why* the models fail, not *how* they are implemented.

Several methodological concerns raise doubts. The choice of evaluating on both “plain” and “OOV” test sets seems arbitrary without a more robust justification of its relevance. The task definitions (Task 3, in particular) seem artificial and may not accurately reflect the challenges of real-world dialog. The rule-based baseline is poorly described and its limited scope feels like a missed opportunity for a more insightful comparison. Moreover, the authors mention a validation set being used for hyperparameter optimization, but the specifics of this validation split are not clearly outlined. 

The paper's claim of validating the synthetic tasks with real data is also weakened by the limited analysis provided. Simply stating that the “pattern of results observed…is a good proxy” isn't sufficient; a more rigorous comparison, potentially involving statistical significance tests, is needed.  The Concierge dataset description mentions anonymization but lacks detail on the specifics of the anonymization process and potential biases introduced.

Finally, numerous minor stylistic and formatting inconsistencies are present throughout the paper, including inconsistent use of italics, awkwardly phrased sentences, and redundancies. These detract from the paper's overall readability and professionalism.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Streamline the presentation:** Reduce the level of detail, focusing on the core contributions and key findings. Consolidate tables and remove unnecessary implementation details.
*   **Strengthen the validation:** Provide a more thorough analysis of the relationship between the synthetic tasks and real-world performance, including statistical tests.
*   **Improve the rule-based baseline:** Develop a more comprehensive rule-based system or provide a more detailed justification for its limited scope.
*   **Clarify the task definitions:** Re-evaluate the artificiality of the tasks and consider modifications to better reflect real-world dialog challenges.
*   **Address the OOV evaluation:** Provide a stronger rationale for the OOV test set and its relevance to generalizability.
*   **Refine the writing:** Proofread the paper carefully to eliminate stylistic inconsistencies and grammatical errors.
*   **Provide error analysis:** Instead of simply reporting accuracy scores, delve deeper into the types of errors made by the models and discuss potential reasons for these errors.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind splitting the KB into two disjoint sets for the OOV evaluation? What specific aspect of generalizability are you aiming to test with this setup?
2.  The rule-based baseline appears quite limited. Was it challenging to build a more comprehensive system, and if so, why?
3.  Can you provide more details on the validation set used for hyperparameter optimization, including its size and composition?
4.  What specific types of errors are most frequently observed in the low per-dialog accuracy results? Are there common patterns in the model's failures?
5.  Given the complexity of the Memory Network implementation details, did you investigate simpler architectures as baselines to establish a lower bound on performance?
