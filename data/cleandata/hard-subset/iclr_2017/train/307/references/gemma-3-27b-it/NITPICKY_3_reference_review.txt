This paper introduces a set of synthetic tasks, alongside two real-world datasets, for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems. The core motivation is to create a standardized, reproducible benchmark to assess the capabilities of end-to-end models compared to traditional slot-filling approaches. The authors propose five tasks centered around restaurant reservation, covering aspects like API querying, knowledge base interpretation, and dialogue management. They demonstrate the benchmark’s usage with Memory Networks and compare performance against rule-based and embedding-based baselines, finding that while end-to-end models show promise, they still lag behind in completing full dialogues.

Reject. While the idea of a dedicated benchmark is valuable, the paper’s relentless focus on minute implementation details and the identification of numerous minor flaws overshadows the potential contribution. The presentation feels overly critical and nitpicky, hindering a clear appreciation of the overall concept. The fact that so many supplementary results are relegated to appendices suggests a lack of confidence in their narrative impact.

The paper meticulously details the task definitions, datasets, and model configurations. However, this detail often devolves into excessive specification, such as exhaustively listing hyperparameter values and presenting numerous tables of results that don’t contribute substantially to the core argument. For example, the detailed breakdown of hyperparameters in Appendix C, while thorough, feels unnecessary for a high-level assessment of the proposed benchmark. The repeated emphasis on the shortcomings of the Memory Networks, while honest, becomes tiresome and detracts from a more balanced evaluation. 

Furthermore, the authors’ insistence on identifying limitations (e.g., the model's inability to perfectly handle KB interpretation) feels disproportionate to the potential benefits of the benchmark itself. A more constructive approach would be to frame these limitations as areas for future research, rather than dwelling on them in such detail. The excessive comparison to a hand-coded rule-based system, while intended to demonstrate the gap between current end-to-end models and traditional systems, comes across as unnecessarily harsh. 

The sheer volume of experimental results, presented across numerous tables (including those in the appendices), creates a sense of information overload. The paper would benefit from a more focused presentation of the key findings, emphasizing the broader trends rather than dwelling on specific numerical values. The concluding remarks feel somewhat dismissive, reiterating the limitations of the models without adequately acknowledging the potential value of the proposed benchmark.

Here are some points for improvement:

*   **Streamline the presentation:** Reduce the level of detail in certain sections, particularly those related to hyperparameter tuning and exhaustive result tables. Focus on the key findings and broader trends.
*   **Reframe limitations as opportunities:** Instead of dwelling on the shortcomings of the models, frame them as areas for future research and development.
*   **Provide a more balanced evaluation:** Acknowledge the potential benefits of the proposed benchmark and avoid overly critical comparisons to existing methods.
*   **Clarify the novelty:** While the authors mention the lack of existing benchmarks, they could more explicitly highlight what makes their approach unique and valuable.
*   **Address the potential limitations of the synthetic data:** Acknowledge the potential gap between performance on synthetic tasks and real-world scenarios.
*   **Explain the choice of Memory Networks:** Provide a stronger justification for selecting Memory Networks as the primary end-to-end model. Why are they well-suited for this task? What are their inherent limitations in this context?



I would like to ask the authors:

1.  Beyond reproducibility, what specific research questions are best answered *because* of this benchmark that wouldn’t be easily addressed otherwise?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the specific design choices of the synthetic tasks (e.g., the number of restaurant properties, the complexity of the user requests)?
3.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the match type features and how they specifically address the challenges of dealing with OOV entities?
4.  What steps have been taken to ensure that the synthetic data accurately reflects the characteristics of real-world dialogue?
