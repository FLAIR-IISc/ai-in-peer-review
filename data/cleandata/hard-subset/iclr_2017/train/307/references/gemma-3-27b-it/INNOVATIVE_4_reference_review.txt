This paper introduces a suite of tasks designed to evaluate goal-oriented dialogue systems, specifically aiming to bridge the gap between chit-chat performance and real-world applicability. The authors argue that existing datasets are insufficient for this purpose, lacking reproducibility and clear error analysis capabilities. They propose a simulated restaurant reservation environment, broken down into five core tasks (API calling, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and full dialogue), along with evaluations on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service). The core contribution lies in providing a controlled environment for systematically testing the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end dialogue models, particularly Memory Networks.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a valuable contribution in terms of creating a testing framework, the core findings – that end-to-end models still struggle with goal-oriented dialogue – are not particularly surprising, and the improvements achieved are incremental. The novelty lies primarily in the testbed itself, but the analysis doesn’t reveal substantially new insights beyond what’s already known in the field.

The motivation is strong. The authors correctly identify the limitations of current evaluation methods and the need for a more structured approach to assess the capabilities of end-to-end models in goal-oriented scenarios. The related work is well-covered, and the design of the tasks seems reasonable, breaking down the complex problem of restaurant reservation into manageable components. The use of both simulated and real-world data is commendable. 

However, the experimental results, while thorough, don’t demonstrate a significant breakthrough. While Memory Networks outperform the baselines, the per-dialog accuracy remains low, confirming existing limitations. The introduction of “match type features” offers some improvement, particularly in handling out-of-vocabulary entities, but doesn't fundamentally alter the overall performance. The paper feels more like a demonstration of the testbed than a presentation of novel research findings. The comparison to rule-based systems is somewhat superficial; a more sophisticated rule-based system might offer a stronger benchmark.  The lack of substantial improvement with the proposed framework, despite its thoughtful design, weakens the impact of the work. The authors acknowledge this in their conclusion, but this admission doesn't justify publication in its current form. The value primarily lies in providing a resource for the community, but that's not sufficient for ICLR acceptance.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Error Analysis:** The paper could benefit from a deeper dive into the types of errors made by the Memory Networks. What specific sub-tasks are the models struggling with most? Providing qualitative examples of failure cases would be highly insightful.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  More detailed ablation studies on the match type features would be helpful. How does performance vary with different feature combinations?
*   **Model Complexity:**  The authors focus solely on Memory Networks. Exploring other state-of-the-art architectures (e.g., Transformers) might reveal different strengths and weaknesses.
*   **KB Integration:** The paper briefly mentions the KB. Could the KB be integrated more directly into the model architecture (e.g., using knowledge graph embeddings)?
* **Reproducibility**: The authors state the data is available at http://fb.ai/babi. Is this still the correct location? Further, clearer instructions on reproducing the experiments would be welcome.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Given the limited improvement in per-dialog accuracy, do you believe the proposed tasks are sufficiently challenging, or do they need to be expanded or modified to better discriminate between different models?
*   What are your plans for further developing this testbed? Do you envision adding more tasks or incorporating more realistic scenarios?
*   Why were rule-based systems limited to DSTC2 and Concierge and not used for all tasks? Could a more robust rule-based system have offered more conclusive comparison?
*   Could you provide specific examples of failure cases for Memory Networks, illustrating the types of errors they commonly make?
