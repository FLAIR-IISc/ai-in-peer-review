This paper introduces a benchmark of five tasks, grounded in a restaurant reservation knowledge base, designed to evaluate the goal-oriented capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the limitations of existing datasets and evaluation metrics, arguing they lack reproducibility and clear error analysis. They present results using Memory Networks, comparing performance across synthetic tasks and two real-world datasets, demonstrating that while end-to-end models show promise, they still lag behind traditional slot-filling methods in achieving reliable goal completion.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a genuine need for better evaluation tools in goal-oriented dialog and proposes a potentially valuable benchmark, the core contribution feels more like engineering a testbed than presenting a novel algorithmic advance. The performance of Memory Networks, while reasonable, doesn't demonstrate a significant leap forward that would warrant acceptance given the current state of the field. The paper heavily relies on a known architecture (MemNN) and its performance is not particularly compelling, falling short of robust goal completion even within the controlled synthetic environment.

Here’s a breakdown of my reasoning:

* **Incremental Contribution:** The primary contribution is the dataset and task definition. While this is useful, it’s not a breakthrough. Many papers introduce datasets; the impact hinges on whether the dataset enables significant scientific progress. Here, the MemNN results on the new tasks don't dramatically shift our understanding of end-to-end dialog capabilities. The analysis mostly *confirms* existing observations (end-to-end models struggle with goal completion) rather than revealing something new.
* **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** Using Memory Networks is a reasonable choice for a baseline, but the extension with "match type features," while sensible, isn’t a fundamentally novel architectural idea. It feels more like a pragmatic adaptation than a conceptual innovation.
* **Weak Empirical Impact:** The fact that the rule-based system outperforms the Memory Networks on the real-world DSTC2 dataset is concerning. This highlights the limitations of the MemNN implementation and the dataset’s ability to truly capture the complexity of real-world interactions. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn’t offer a convincing path forward.
* **Evaluation Focus:** The per-dialog accuracy metric is excellent and rightly emphasized as the crucial measure, but the consistently low scores across all models are somewhat deflating. A more compelling paper would demonstrate a clear path toward improving this metric.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

* **Ablation Study:** A more thorough ablation study of the "match type features" is needed. How much of the improvement comes from each individual type feature? Is there redundancy?
* **Error Analysis:** While the paper aims for error analysis by design, the presented analysis feels superficial. Providing concrete examples of failure cases – showing *why* the MemNN fails in specific scenarios – would be extremely valuable.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Consider including comparison to more sophisticated slot-filling or state-tracking systems. A more competitive baseline would better highlight the challenges for end-to-end models.
* **Scalability of Tasks:** Discuss the scalability of these tasks. Can they be easily extended to more complex domains? Would the same limitations observed here persist in more intricate scenarios?
* **KB Interaction Details:** Provide more details about how the KB is accessed and queried. What API calls are available? Are there limitations to the API that could impact performance?

**Questions for the Authors:**

* What are your plans for expanding this benchmark? Are you envisioning a shared task or competition?
* Given the poor performance of the MemNN on the DSTC2 dataset, do you believe the synthetic tasks are truly representative of real-world dialog challenges?
* Could you provide more specific examples of why the MemNN fails in the T3 and T5 tasks, particularly related to interpreting KB results and maintaining dialog coherence?
* You mention that the current tasks are deterministic. Do you plan to introduce more stochasticity or ambiguity into the tasks to better reflect real-world conversations?
