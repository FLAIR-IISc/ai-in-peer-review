This paper introduces a set of synthetic tasks, along with two real-world datasets, designed to evaluate the goal-oriented dialogue capabilities of end-to-end models. The core idea is to decompose goal-oriented dialogue into smaller, testable sub-tasks – querying knowledge bases, updating requests, and interpreting results – to pinpoint where current systems struggle. The authors evaluate Memory Networks on these tasks, finding they show promise but still fall short of robust performance, particularly in completing full dialogues. They highlight the importance of handling out-of-vocabulary entities and propose match-type features to address this.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a valuable contribution in creating a controlled evaluation environment for goal-oriented dialogue, the experimental methodology and presentation are riddled with inconsistencies, lack sufficient detail, and the claims are not adequately supported by the results. The relentless focus on minor details, while demonstrating thoroughness, overshadows the broader significance of the proposed benchmark and makes it difficult to assess the validity of the conclusions.

The primary concern lies with the inconsistent application of evaluation metrics and the often superficial discussion of results. The constant reporting of both per-response and per-dialog accuracy (with the latter in parentheses) is cumbersome, and the authors rarely draw clear connections between these two metrics. The stark difference, consistently showing low per-dialog accuracy despite reasonable per-response accuracy, is acknowledged but not investigated with sufficient depth. Is this solely due to error propagation? Are specific sub-tasks particularly problematic? The paper doesn’t convincingly address these questions.

Furthermore, the description of the experimental setup is often vague. The precise hyperparameters used for each model on each task are relegated to an appendix, making it difficult to reproduce the results or understand the sensitivity of the findings. The rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters is not discussed. The mention of “optimizing” hyperparameters on the validation set is insufficient – what optimization procedure was used? How long was the optimization run? Were multiple runs performed with different random seeds?

The implementation details of the “match type features” are also unclear. The explanation in Section 4.5 and Appendix A feels contradictory and lacks precision. Specifically, how are these features integrated into the Memory Network architecture? Are they added to the input embeddings, the memory representations, or both? The assertion that the feature is “recorded” during API calls doesn’t provide enough information.

Finally, the level of detail is uneven. The simulation setup is exhaustively described, yet the preprocessing and adaptation of the DSTC2 dataset are glossed over. The description of the Concierge dataset is similarly superficial, relying heavily on qualitative statements about its characteristics without providing quantitative statistics beyond the dialog length. The claim that “the pattern of relative performances of methods is the same” on Concierge requires statistical justification.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper would benefit from a more streamlined presentation. Reduce redundancy, particularly in the introduction and related work sections.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests for the reported results. Are the observed differences between models statistically significant?
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct a more thorough error analysis. Identify the most common types of errors made by the models and propose potential solutions.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the Memory Network, including the match-type features.
*   **Code Release:** Publicly release the code and data (including the Concierge dataset, or a representative subset) to facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameters used for each model on each task, including the optimization procedure employed?
2.  How exactly are the “match type features” integrated into the Memory Network architecture? Please provide a clear and concise description of the feature engineering process.
3.  What statistical tests were used to determine the significance of the reported results?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the most common types of errors made by the models?
5.  Why was the decision made to report per-response and per-dialog accuracy simultaneously, given the limited insights gained from this presentation format? Could a different metric, or a more focused analysis, provide more meaningful results?
