## Review of "Goal-Oriented Dialog Tasks"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a set of five synthetic tasks, alongside two real-world datasets (DSTC2 & a proprietary concierge service dataset), designed to evaluate the goal-oriented capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. The motivation is the lack of standardized benchmarks for assessing these models in a structured, reproducible manner, particularly concerning skills beyond simple chit-chat like KB querying and dialog management. The authors demonstrate the utility of these tasks by evaluating Memory Networks, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses, and establishing a baseline for future research. The key contribution is a readily available, controllable environment for diagnosing end-to-end dialog systems' performance in goal-directed conversations.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a significant gap in the field – the lack of robust benchmarks for goal-oriented dialog – and offers a valuable resource for the community. The synthetic tasks are well-defined, and the experiments demonstrate the utility of the benchmark. While end-to-end models still lag behind traditional methods, the paper provides a clear framework for understanding *where* they fall short and guiding future research.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem: evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings is challenging due to the lack of appropriate benchmarks. The authors convincingly argue that existing datasets are often noisy, limited in scope, or lack reproducibility.
* **Well-Designed Tasks:** The five synthetic tasks progressively increase in complexity, testing crucial capabilities like API calls, KB integration, and multi-turn dialog management. The split into standard and out-of-vocabulary settings is a smart design choice to assess generalization.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct thorough experiments with Memory Networks and relevant baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings) on both the synthetic and real-world datasets. The results are well-presented and support their claims that current end-to-end models struggle with tasks requiring complex reasoning and KB interpretation. The consistency of performance trends across the different datasets strengthens the validity of the findings.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors explicitly prioritize reproducibility by making the dataset and tasks publicly available. This is a significant positive aspect.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of results, particularly the identification of specific failure modes (e.g., difficulty interpreting KB results), is valuable for guiding future research.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Task Complexity Justification:** While the task definitions are clear, a more detailed rationale for the chosen complexity levels (e.g., why the sequence of tasks 1-5 is optimal for assessing core capabilities) would be helpful.
* **Memory Network Hyperparameter Tuning:** While hyperparameters are reported, a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the MemNN performance to different hyperparameter settings would be valuable. Did some tasks require substantially different configurations?
* **Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis, perhaps with specific examples of incorrect predictions and their underlying causes, would strengthen the paper.  Beyond stating that the models struggle with KB interpretation, providing concrete examples would be highly informative.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  While the authors rightly focus on establishing a baseline, briefly discussing how the performance of MemNNs compares to the current state-of-the-art in slot-filling on the DSTC2 dataset, even if the comparison isn't direct, would be beneficial.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Regarding the choice of Memory Networks:** Why were Memory Networks selected as the primary end-to-end model for evaluation? Were other architectures (e.g., Transformers) considered, and if so, why were they not included in the experimental evaluation?
*   **Regarding the real-world datasets:** What specific anonymization techniques were employed to protect user privacy in the concierge service dataset?  Are there any potential biases introduced by the anonymization process?
* **Regarding Task Design:** How was the size of the training set determined for each task (1000 examples)? Was an ablation study conducted to assess the impact of training set size on performance?
*   **Regarding Failure Cases:** Could you provide a few specific examples of dialog turns where the Memory Network failed, highlighting the type of error (e.g., incorrect API call, misinterpretation of KB results) and potential reasons for the failure?
