This paper introduces a new benchmark, consisting of five tasks and two datasets, for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting. The core idea is to decompose a restaurant reservation task into subtasks, allowing for more granular analysis of model capabilities, and to provide a reproducible and lightweight evaluation environment. The authors also present experiments with Memory Networks, demonstrating their potential and limitations on these tasks.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a significant gap in evaluation methodologies for goal-oriented end-to-end dialog systems and proposes a reasonable approach to address it, the presented work doesn’t offer sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The proposed tasks, while well-defined, are relatively simple and feel like a direct adaptation of the bAbI tasks to a new domain. The experimental results, although informative, are not groundbreaking and largely confirm existing observations about the limitations of current end-to-end models. The contributions primarily lie in the *creation* of a benchmark, rather than a significant advancement in the core machine learning techniques applied.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The fundamental approach of creating synthetic tasks to evaluate specific capabilities of models is not new (e.g., bAbI). Adapting this framework to a new domain, while valuable, doesn’t constitute a substantial research contribution on its own. The task design, while logical, lacks innovative elements that would push the field forward. The extension to real-world datasets (DSTC2 and the concierge dataset) is a useful validation step, but doesn’t fundamentally alter the contribution.

2. **Incremental Results:** The experimental results with Memory Networks, while demonstrating performance on the tasks, are largely expected. The observed limitations of the model (low per-dialog accuracy) align with prior work and don't reveal surprising insights. The addition of match type features is a sensible improvement, but it’s a relatively straightforward extension of existing techniques. The performance improvements reported, while positive, are not of a magnitude that significantly alters the understanding of end-to-end dialog modeling. 

3. **Lack of Deep Technical Insight:** The paper primarily focuses on presenting the benchmark and applying a standard model (Memory Networks) to it. It lacks in-depth analysis of *why* the model struggles with certain tasks, or exploration of alternative architectures or training strategies that could potentially overcome the identified limitations. A deeper technical investigation into the shortcomings of end-to-end models, guided by the insights from this benchmark, would have significantly strengthened the paper.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Task Complexity:** Consider adding more challenging tasks that require more complex reasoning or world knowledge. For example, tasks involving ambiguous user requests, negotiation, or handling unexpected events.
* **Evaluation Metrics:** While per-response and per-dialog accuracy are useful, explore more nuanced evaluation metrics that capture the quality of the dialog beyond simple correctness.  For example, metrics that assess coherence, informativeness, or user satisfaction.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the Memory Network (e.g., the impact of different hop numbers, the effect of match type features on different tasks).
* **Error Analysis:** Provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the Memory Network makes and identifying the underlying causes. This would help guide future research.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:**  Compare the Memory Network to more recent and sophisticated end-to-end dialog models (e.g., Transformers-based models) to provide a more comprehensive assessment of its performance.
* **Dataset Release Details:** Provide clear and detailed instructions for accessing and using the dataset, including information about the data format, API calls, and evaluation scripts.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper states the goal is *not* to improve state-of-the-art performance. However, what design choices were made when creating the tasks to ensure they were appropriately challenging for current end-to-end systems? What level of performance would you consider "success" on these tasks, and why?
2. How sensitive are the results to the specific natural language patterns used to generate user and bot utterances? Have you experimented with different pattern sets or generation methods?
3. The OOV test sets are designed to evaluate generalization to new entities. What is the average overlap in vocabulary (excluding the entity names) between the training and OOV sets? This would help assess the difficulty of the OOV task beyond simply encountering new entities.
4. The concierge dataset is described as noisy. What specific types of noise were observed, and how were they addressed during the conversion to the task format?
5. Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind choosing Memory Networks as the primary model for evaluation? Were other models considered, and if so, why were they excluded?
