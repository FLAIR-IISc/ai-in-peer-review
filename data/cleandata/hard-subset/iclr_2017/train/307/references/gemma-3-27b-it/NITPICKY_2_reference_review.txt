This paper introduces a benchmark of five tasks, built around a restaurant reservation system and grounded in a knowledge base, designed to evaluate the goal-oriented capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the limitations of current evaluation metrics for dialog systems and the difficulty of scaling traditional, slot-filling based approaches to new domains. They demonstrate the use of these tasks with Memory Networks, comparing performance on synthetically generated data and two real-world datasets – DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service dataset – establishing a baseline and illustrating how the synthetic tasks correlate with real-world performance.

**Reject.** While the contribution of a dedicated benchmark for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems is valuable, the paper suffers from numerous, significant flaws in its presentation, methodology, and analysis, ultimately undermining its credibility. The level of detail is inconsistent – some aspects are overly verbose, while others, crucial for reproducibility, are glossed over.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of methodological rigor. The experimental setup, while presenting multiple datasets, lacks sufficient detail to ensure reproducibility. The hyperparameters used for each model and task are presented in an appendix but the justification for these specific choices is absent. Furthermore, the “rule-based system” is not described in sufficient detail to understand its capabilities or limitations beyond a general statement of its success on the simulated tasks. The claim of 100% accuracy for the rule-based system feels inflated without a detailed breakdown of how it handles the nuances of the simulated environment. The comparison to this system feels somewhat superficial given this lack of detail.

Secondly, the analysis of results is often superficial. While the paper notes performance discrepancies across tasks, it fails to provide a deep investigation into *why* certain models struggle with specific aspects of goal-oriented dialog. For example, the authors state the Memory Networks struggle with interpreting KB results but provide limited analysis beyond that observation. The impact of match type features, while highlighted, is presented without a clear explanation of how these features specifically contribute to improved performance on certain tasks. The paper would benefit significantly from an error analysis. The discussion of the Concierge dataset, while acknowledged as more realistic, feels somewhat tacked on and lacks a nuanced discussion of the challenges it presents.

Finally, the writing quality is inconsistent. The introduction, while providing context, is somewhat repetitive. The use of jargon is sometimes excessive, and the explanations of technical details (e.g., Memory Network architecture) could be more accessible. There are numerous instances of imprecise language and awkward phrasing throughout the paper. 

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision, though substantial work would be required):**

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide a detailed, step-by-step guide for reproducing the experiments, including specific library versions, hardware configurations, and random seeds. Release the code for the rule-based system.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct a thorough error analysis for each task and model. Identify common error patterns and discuss potential reasons for these errors.
*   **Hyperparameter Justification:** Provide a clear rationale for the hyperparameter settings used in the experiments. Consider including ablation studies to assess the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter values.
*   **Rule-Based System Details:** Elaborate on the implementation of the rule-based system, including the specific rules used and the decision-making process.
*   **KB Details:** Provide more information on the size and structure of the KB.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all comparisons.
*   **Clarify Evaluation Metrics:** Explain clearly how per-dialog accuracy is calculated, especially edge cases where partial completion exists.
*   **Address Inconsistencies:** Carefully review the paper for inconsistencies in terminology and notation.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide more details on the process of converting the DSTC2 data into the format of your tasks? What specific challenges did you encounter during this conversion?
*   How sensitive are your results to the size of the training datasets? Did you experiment with larger datasets?
*   You mention that the dialogs from the training and test sets are different, but based on different user requests. Does this fully prevent memorization effects? 
*   How was the validation set chosen? What criteria were used to ensure its representativeness?
*   What specific measures were taken to anonymize the data from the online concierge service? Can you provide more information on the types of errors present in this dataset?
*   Could you provide an example of a failed dialog where the MemNN model produced a plausible, but incorrect, response?
