This paper introduces a set of five tasks, along with two real-world datasets, designed to evaluate the goal-oriented capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. The core idea is to break down a restaurant reservation scenario into subtasks assessing dialog management, KB querying, and result interpretation, enabling a more granular analysis than traditional end-to-end evaluation metrics. The paper also presents baseline results using Memory Networks, comparing performance across the synthetic and real datasets.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a valid problem – the lack of robust evaluation benchmarks for goal-oriented end-to-end dialog systems – and proposes a potentially valuable resource, the technical contribution and the presented experimental results do not sufficiently justify acceptance at ICLR. The novelty lies primarily in the *creation* of the benchmark, rather than significant algorithmic advancements, which is a weaker contribution for this venue. The experimental results, while thorough, demonstrate that existing end-to-end models struggle with these tasks, but don’t offer substantial insights *why* beyond stating limitations of current approaches.

The primary supporting argument for rejection is the limited novelty and impact of the technical aspects. The use of Memory Networks, while a reasonable choice, doesn’t represent a significant advancement. The paper largely serves as an evaluation platform rather than a breakthrough in dialog modeling itself. The improvements gained by adding match-type features are incremental and expected. The core contribution is the benchmark itself, but a conference like ICLR generally prioritizes novel algorithms or theoretical insights.

Secondly, the paper's presentation of results feels descriptive rather than analytically insightful. While the authors show that performance on the synthetic tasks correlates with real-world datasets, the analysis of *where* the Memory Networks fail is superficial. A deeper dive into error patterns and the reasoning behind these failures would be more valuable.  The claim that the benchmark helps “gauge the strengths and weaknesses of current end-to-end systems” is not fully realized, as the analysis remains at a high level.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct a more detailed error analysis. What specific types of queries or reasoning steps are causing the Memory Networks to fail?  Provide examples of incorrect predictions and explain why the model might be making those mistakes.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  Expand the ablation studies. For example, what is the impact of varying the number of hops in the Memory Network? How does the performance change with different embedding sizes?
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Compare against more recent and competitive end-to-end models, such as transformers (e.g., DialoGPT, BlenderBot). This would provide a better benchmark for the Memory Network's performance.
*   **KB Integration:** Explore more sophisticated methods for integrating the KB information. Could graph neural networks be used to represent the KB and learn relationships between entities?
*   **Task Complexity:** The tasks, while well-defined, might be overly simplistic. Consider adding more complexity, such as incorporating user preferences, handling ambiguous requests, or dealing with multiple constraints.
*   **Generalization to other domains**: Discuss the possible generalization of the tasks to other goal-oriented scenarios.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper states that the tasks are designed to reflect skills crucial for dialog systems. Could you elaborate on the specific design choices made to ensure each task isolates and assesses a distinct skill (e.g., dialog management, KB querying)?
2.  The match-type features improve performance on OOV entities. Why does performance on Task 2 *decrease* with the inclusion of these features?
3.  The datasets are relatively small (1000 examples for training the simulated tasks). How sensitive is the performance to the size of the training data? Did you perform any experiments to assess the impact of larger datasets?
4.  You mention that "solving our tasks requires manipulating both natural language and symbols from a KB." Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how manipulating symbols (i.e., KB entities) differs from simply processing natural language, and why this is a challenging aspect for end-to-end models?
5.  How do you envision this benchmark being used by other researchers? What specific types of experiments or analyses would you recommend to those looking to build upon this work?
