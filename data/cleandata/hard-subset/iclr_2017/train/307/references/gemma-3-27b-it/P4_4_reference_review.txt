This paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The core contribution is a set of five tasks, built around a knowledge base, designed to test crucial dialog capabilities like API querying, KB interpretation, and dialog management. The authors also provide results on two additional datasets – DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service dataset – to demonstrate the correlation between performance on the synthetic tasks and real-world data.

**Decision:** Reject. While the presented work is a valuable contribution to the field, particularly in providing a controlled environment for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems, the novelty is incremental and the paper lacks sufficient justification for acceptance at a highly competitive venue like ICLR. Specifically, the experimental results, while thorough, do not demonstrate a significant breakthrough or novel insight.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The construction of a synthetic task-based benchmark, while useful, is not a fundamentally new approach. The bAbI tasks and other similar synthetic datasets (e.g., Dodge et al., 2016) already exist for evaluating specific aspects of language understanding. The main novelty lies in adapting this approach to goal-oriented dialog, but the tasks themselves are relatively straightforward extensions of existing concepts. The framing as a “testbed” does not elevate the work to a level commensurate with ICLR’s expectations.
2. **Limited Impact of Results:** The experimental results, while comprehensive, do not showcase a significant advancement in the field. The Memory Network model performs reasonably well on some tasks but struggles with full dialog completion, which is the ultimate goal. While this is a valuable observation, it doesn't represent a substantial contribution in terms of model development or a novel understanding of the limitations of end-to-end systems. The observation that per-response accuracy is higher than per-dialog accuracy is already well-established in the dialog community.
3. **Lack of Theoretical Contribution:** The paper is primarily empirical. While the authors provide a clear motivation for their work, there is no theoretical analysis or novel algorithmic development. ICLR typically favors papers with strong theoretical foundations or novel algorithmic approaches.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Task Design:** While the tasks are well-defined, the deterministic nature of some aspects (e.g., the bot asking questions in a fixed order in Task 1) might limit the generalizability of the findings. Consider introducing more variability and stochasticity into the task design.
* **Model Selection:** The choice of Memory Networks is reasonable, but a more diverse set of models, including more recent and powerful architectures (e.g., Transformers), would provide a stronger comparison.
* **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies on the match type features would be beneficial. Quantify the impact of each individual type feature.
* **Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis would be valuable. Provide specific examples of failures and discuss the underlying reasons. What types of errors are most frequent? How do they relate to the limitations of the Memory Network architecture?
* **KB Complexity:** The current KB is relatively simple. Explore more complex KBs with richer relationships and more diverse entities to assess the scalability of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper states the goal is “not to improve the state of the art.” However, this feels at odds with the spirit of ICLR, which aims to showcase cutting-edge research. Could you elaborate on why you chose this approach and how this work lays the foundation for future improvements?
2. The synthetic data is carefully constructed. How sensitive are the results to variations in the data generation process, such as the number of training examples or the distribution of user requests?
3. The results on DSTC2 and the concierge dataset are presented as validation of the synthetic tasks. Could you provide a quantitative measure of the correlation between performance on the synthetic tasks and these real-world datasets? (e.g., Spearman's rank correlation coefficient).
4. The match type features seem to significantly improve performance on the OOV tasks. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how these features are being utilized by the Memory Network? (e.g., attention weights)
