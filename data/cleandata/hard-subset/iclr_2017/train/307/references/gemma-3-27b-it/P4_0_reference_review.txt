This paper introduces a set of tasks, grounded in a restaurant reservation scenario and a knowledge base, designed for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems. The core motivation is to provide a controlled and reproducible environment to assess the capabilities of these systems, specifically addressing whether they can compete with traditional slot-filling methods in goal-oriented tasks. The paper also presents experiments using Memory Networks, comparing their performance against rule-based and information retrieval baselines on both the synthetic tasks and two real-world datasets.

**Reject**

The paper, while presenting a valuable contribution in creating a benchmark, ultimately falls short of convincingly demonstrating significant advancements or providing particularly insightful analyses. The core issue is the limited performance of the evaluated models, specifically the Memory Networks, and the subsequent weak conclusions drawn from the results. The tasks, while well-defined, do not appear to significantly challenge current end-to-end approaches beyond what has already been observed in the literature.

The paper clearly articulates the problem: evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented settings is difficult. The proposed solution—a set of tasks with varying levels of complexity—is a reasonable approach. The tasks are well-defined (Task 1-5) and the inclusion of real-world data (DSTC2 and the concierge dataset) strengthens the evaluation. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including several baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks). The use of “out-of-vocabulary” entities adds a level of complexity and realism. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the setup and results. The supplemental material provides helpful details on hyperparameters and example predictions.

However, the results are disappointing. While Memory Networks outperform the simpler baselines, their per-dialog accuracy remains low, indicating a significant gap in their ability to handle goal-oriented dialog. The authors acknowledge this, but the analysis lacks depth. Simply stating that “end-to-end models still need to improve” is not a strong conclusion, and the paper doesn’t pinpoint *where* they fall short beyond the limitations already known in the field (e.g., difficulty with KB reasoning and multi-turn consistency). The limited gains achieved with match-type features, while positive, are not substantial enough to warrant strong claims about the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The addition of match-type features mostly addresses the OOV problem rather than improving overall performance on the core tasks. Furthermore, the fact that a simple rule-based system can solve the synthetic tasks perfectly highlights the relative weakness of the learning-based approaches. Finally, the experiments and analysis feel somewhat descriptive rather than investigative. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Deeper Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis is crucial. What specific types of errors are the Memory Networks making? Are they failing to understand the user's intent, incorrectly querying the KB, or struggling with dialog management? Providing concrete examples of failure cases would be very helpful.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper could benefit from more ablation studies to understand the impact of different components of the Memory Network architecture. For example, what is the effect of the number of hops? How does the embedding dimension affect performance?
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to other state-of-the-art end-to-end dialog models (e.g., Transformers) would be beneficial. This would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the Memory Network's performance.
*   **Task Difficulty:** Evaluate the relative difficulty of each task. Some tasks may be significantly harder than others, and this should be acknowledged.
*   **KB Complexity:** The size and complexity of the knowledge base might be a limiting factor. Exploring larger and more complex KBs could reveal further challenges for the models.
*   **Justification of Task Design:**  Expand the discussion on *why* these specific five tasks were chosen to represent the crucial capabilities of a goal-oriented dialog system. Is there a theoretical justification for this selection?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific architectural modifications or training techniques do you believe are most promising for improving the performance of end-to-end dialog systems on these tasks?
2.  The paper mentions that the tasks were designed to isolate specific capabilities. However, the low per-dialog accuracy suggests that the models struggle with integrating these capabilities. Can you elaborate on how the tasks interact with each other and where the integration breaks down?
3.  Given the perfect performance of the rule-based system on the synthetic tasks, do you believe these tasks are sufficiently challenging for evaluating the capabilities of modern learning-based approaches? If not, what modifications could be made to increase the difficulty?
4.  The concierge dataset is described as "noisy". Can you provide more details about the nature of this noise and how it affects the evaluation? Did you consider any data cleaning or preprocessing steps to mitigate the noise?
5.  Why were the models tested with varying numbers of hops (up to 4)? Is there a principled way to determine the optimal number of hops for a given task?
