## Review of "Goal-Oriented Dialog Tasks"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel benchmark for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting – restaurant reservation. Recognizing the limitations of existing datasets (lack of scalability, noisy annotations, limited reproducibility), the authors propose a set of five tasks built around a simulated KB interaction. These tasks are designed to isolate and test crucial dialog capabilities like API querying, state tracking, and entity handling.  The authors also augment this synthetic benchmark with evaluations on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service) to demonstrate the correlation between performance on the synthetic tasks and real-world scenarios.  They demonstrate initial results using Memory Networks and show that while promising, end-to-end models still struggle with completing full dialogs reliably.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The work presents a valuable contribution in defining a targeted benchmark for goal-oriented dialog, but the empirical results, while insightful, are not strong enough to warrant acceptance in their current form. The gap between state-of-the-art and desired performance remains significant, and the paper doesn’t fully articulate *why* current models struggle beyond stating that they do.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Novelty in Approach:** While the *combination* of synthetic tasks and real-world validation is a good idea, the individual components are not particularly novel. Synthetic dialog task generation and KB interaction have been explored before. The core contribution lies in the *specific design* of these tasks, which the paper convincingly argues is well-motivated. However, the impact of this design is not fully realized in the results.
* **Disappointing Empirical Performance:** The key finding – that end-to-end models perform poorly on per-dialog accuracy – isn't surprising given the known challenges of goal-oriented dialog. However, the paper doesn’t offer much in the way of *analysis* as to *why* these models fail. Simply stating the per-dialog accuracy is low doesn’t provide much guidance for future research.  The improvement from match-type features is promising but feels incremental.
* **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** While the authors include several baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings), the comparison isn't comprehensive. The rule-based baseline is only used for the DSTC2 data, and the IR baselines seem simplistic. The experiments with Memory Networks are somewhat limited in scope. A more thorough comparison to other modern end-to-end architectures would strengthen the paper.
* **Overly Optimistic Conclusion:** The authors claim the results on the synthetic tasks correlate well with real-world performance. While a correlation *is* observed, it's not convincingly demonstrated to be strong enough to justify using the synthetic tasks as a primary evaluation tool *instead* of real-world data.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Deeper Error Analysis:** The most crucial improvement would be a detailed error analysis of the Memory Network's failures. *What specific sub-tasks are the models struggling with?* Is it KB querying, entity disambiguation, maintaining dialog state, or something else? Provide concrete examples of failure cases.
* **More Sophisticated Baselines:** Include comparisons to more recent and competitive end-to-end models, such as Transformers or more advanced memory networks. Exploring different architectural choices and hyperparameter settings would be valuable.
* **Ablation Studies:** Perform a more comprehensive ablation study of the match-type features.  What specific entity types benefit the most from this feature?  Are there negative side effects?
* **Expand Real-World Validation:**  The real-world validation is a good start, but a more extensive evaluation on more diverse datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Clarify KB Interaction:** Explain the API calls in more detail. How are they represented to the model? What are the limitations of the KB itself?
* **Discussion of Generalization:** The paper mentions the OOV test sets. Elaborate on how these sets are constructed and the challenges they present.  Are there strategies to improve generalization to unseen entities?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide more concrete examples of the types of errors the Memory Network makes during dialogs (especially in tasks 3 and 5)?
* How sensitive are the results to the size of the training set?  Would increasing the size of the training data improve per-dialog accuracy?
* You mention that the KB has limitations. Could these limitations be contributing to the poor per-dialog accuracy?
* Were any attempts made to incorporate reinforcement learning into the training process to directly optimize for per-dialog accuracy?
* What are the computational costs associated with training and evaluating the Memory Network models, especially on larger datasets?
