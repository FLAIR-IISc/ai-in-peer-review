This paper introduces a set of tasks and datasets designed to evaluate goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically focusing on assessing the capabilities of end-to-end models in a controlled environment. The authors argue that existing datasets are insufficient for rigorous evaluation and propose a simulation-based approach grounded in a knowledge base, alongside evaluations on existing datasets (DSTC2) and a proprietary concierge service dataset. They demonstrate the use of these tasks with Memory Networks, comparing performance to rule-based systems, information retrieval methods, and supervised embedding models. The core contribution is a reproducible benchmark for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems, intended to highlight strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

**Reject.** While the motivation for creating a controlled evaluation environment is sound, the paper ultimately demonstrates limited progress in bridging the gap between traditional, handcrafted systems and end-to-end neural models. The results, while informative, largely confirm existing understanding: end-to-end systems struggle with complex reasoning and knowledge integration required for truly goal-oriented dialog. The improvements observed with the introduction of match-type features are incremental and don't fundamentally resolve the core challenges. 

The paper's reliance on a simulation, while offering control, introduces concerns about realism. The observed performance on real data (DSTC2 and the concierge dataset) doesn't dramatically differ from the simulated tasks, but the fact that the simulation *needs* to be created to demonstrate the shortcomings of end-to-end models feels like a circular argument. The authors acknowledge that per-dialog accuracy remains low, essentially admitting the approach isn’t yet competitive with established methods. The introduction of match-type features is a valuable contribution, but its impact is relatively small and doesn't represent a significant breakthrough. The extensive comparison of various baselines is commendable but many of these, such as TF-IDF, are unsurprisingly outperformed by more sophisticated methods.  The analysis of the results isn’t particularly insightful beyond confirming that existing end-to-end models struggle with the complexities of goal-oriented dialog. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate the utility of the proposed tasks beyond what is already known in the field. A significant weakness is the lack of ablation studies evaluating the impact of different components within the Memory Network architecture itself, beyond the addition of match-type features.  

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Realism of the Simulation:** Provide a more thorough justification for the simulation’s design choices and discuss potential biases it might introduce. Compare the distribution of features (dialog length, API call complexity, etc.) in the simulated data to real-world datasets more comprehensively.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct a more detailed error analysis. What *specifically* are the end-to-end models struggling with? Are there patterns in the errors related to knowledge retrieval, reasoning, or dialog management?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform more detailed ablation studies on the Memory Network architecture. What is the impact of the number of hops, embedding dimensionality, and other hyperparameters?
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Consider comparing to more recent and sophisticated dialog state tracking systems, rather than solely relying on a rule-based system.
*   **Generalizability:** Address the limited scope of the restaurant reservation domain. How well would these tasks generalize to other goal-oriented domains?
*   **Quantitative Evaluation of Concierge Dataset:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the differences and difficulties associated with the Concierge dataset compared to the simulated data and DSTC2.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond identifying the shortcomings of end-to-end models, what specific design principles or architectural modifications do you believe are most promising for addressing the observed limitations?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the specific hyperparameters used in the Memory Network? Have you performed a thorough hyperparameter search?
3.  The simulation creates a controlled environment. But how confident are you that the performance on this simulation correlates strongly with real-world performance, especially in scenarios with more complex user behavior and noisy input?
4.   What steps have been taken to ensure that the tasks are truly evaluating dialog *capability* and not simply memorization of the training data, especially given the limited size of the datasets?
