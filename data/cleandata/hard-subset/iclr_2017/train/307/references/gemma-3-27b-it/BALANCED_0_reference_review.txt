This paper introduces a set of tasks designed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the difficulties in scaling traditional slot-filling systems and the limitations of evaluating end-to-end models on purely chit-chat datasets. They create a simulated environment with a knowledge base, defining five tasks that test crucial dialog capabilities like API querying and entity manipulation. The paper also includes experiments on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to validate the correlation between performance on the synthetic tasks and real-world scenarios.

**Reject** with a recommendation for revision. While the paper presents a valuable contribution in terms of a well-defined benchmark and a thorough evaluation, the conclusions drawn from the experiments, particularly regarding the limitations of Memory Networks, feel somewhat overstated given the relatively simple architecture employed and the limited hyperparameter search. The improvements over baselines aren’t substantial enough to confidently claim definitive shortcomings of end-to-end models, and the analysis could be significantly strengthened.

The primary reason for rejection is the limited exploration of more sophisticated end-to-end models. The authors test Memory Networks, which, while a reasonable starting point, are not state-of-the-art. Given the rapid advancements in transformer-based models (e.g., DialoGPT, BlenderBot) and their demonstrated success in dialog, it’s crucial to include such models as baselines to provide a more accurate assessment of the current capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. Without a comparison to these models, the conclusion that end-to-end models "still need to improve before being able to reliably handle goal-oriented dialog" feels premature.

Secondly, the analysis of the results lacks depth. While the per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics are useful, a more granular error analysis would be beneficial. For instance, identifying specific types of errors (e.g., incorrect API call generation, failure to interpret KB results, inability to handle out-of-vocabulary entities) would provide more actionable insights for future research. The attention visualizations (in Appendix B) are a good start, but further analysis is needed to understand *why* the model is failing in certain scenarios. The observation that the OOV tasks reveal weaknesses is important, but needs to be examined more closely with regards to the specific entity types that cause problems.

**Additional Feedback & Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** As mentioned, incorporating transformer-based dialog models is critical. Fine-tuning pre-trained models on the provided dataset would provide a more compelling comparison.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct a detailed error analysis to identify common failure modes. Categorize errors and provide specific examples.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the Memory Network (e.g., the number of hops, the use of match type features).
*   **Dataset Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the dataset itself. What are the key challenges? What types of queries are most difficult?
*   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** While hyperparameters were optimized on validation sets, further exploration might be warranted, especially for the Memory Networks. A more systematic search (e.g., using grid search or Bayesian optimization) could potentially yield better performance.
*   **KB Integration:** Explore more sophisticated ways to integrate the knowledge base into the model. For example, using graph neural networks to represent the KB and its relationships.
*   **Clarify “Task” vs “Dataset”:** The terminology is sometimes blurry. Is Task 6 a new task or a dataset? Clarifying this will improve readability.
*   **Concierge Dataset:** Elaborate on the anonymization process for the Concierge dataset. More details would increase confidence in its utility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why were Memory Networks chosen as the primary end-to-end model for evaluation, given the availability of more advanced architectures? What limitations of Memory Networks were anticipated and what specific aspects were they intended to highlight?
2.  What specific types of errors were observed during the error analysis? Can you provide some illustrative examples of failed dialogs and explain the model’s shortcomings in those cases?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly for the Memory Network? Did you observe significant variations in performance with different hyperparameter settings?
4.  Given the relatively small size of the training datasets, how confident are you that the observed performance differences are statistically significant? Were any statistical tests used to assess the significance of the results?
5.  Regarding the Concierge dataset, could you provide more details about the anonymization process and any potential biases that might be introduced as a result?
