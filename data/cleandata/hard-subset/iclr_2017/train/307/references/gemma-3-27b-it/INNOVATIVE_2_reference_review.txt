This paper introduces a suite of tasks, grounded in a restaurant reservation KB, designed to systematically evaluate the capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. Recognizing the limitations of existing datasets – often noisy, domain-specific, or lacking reproducibility – the authors propose a controlled environment for assessing crucial skills like dialog management, KB querying, and entity handling. The work also presents experimental results using Memory Networks, comparing their performance against baselines on both the synthetic tasks and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service). The core claim is that this task suite provides a valuable, interpretable benchmark for progress in goal-oriented dialog.

**Reject.** While the motivation is sound and the proposed task suite is a valuable contribution, the paper doesn't demonstrate a compelling enough advance in *modeling* to warrant acceptance. The results, while insightful, largely confirm existing intuitions: end-to-end models struggle with complex goal-oriented tasks and benefit from mechanisms to handle OOV entities. The performance gains achieved by Memory Networks with match type features are incremental and don't represent a significant breakthrough. 

The strength of this paper lies in the creation of the benchmark itself. The tasks are well-defined and thoughtfully designed to isolate specific dialog abilities. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets is a smart move, lending credibility to the findings. The analysis of per-response vs. per-dialog accuracy effectively highlights the challenges of maintaining coherence throughout a conversation. However, the experimental evaluation feels somewhat limited. The focus on Memory Networks, while justified, doesn't explore a wider range of contemporary architectures (e.g., Transformers) or training techniques. More importantly, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed tasks are *sufficient* to fully characterize the capabilities of a goal-oriented dialog system. The low per-dialog accuracy even with the proposed improvements suggests significant gaps remain. The rule-based system provides a good baseline for the synthetic tasks, but its performance on DSTC2 is less informative.

Here are additional points for improvement:

*   **Expand the model evaluation:** Explore more recent and powerful architectures beyond Memory Networks. Include comparisons with pre-trained language models fine-tuned for dialog.
*   **Ablation studies:** Provide more detailed ablation studies on the impact of different components of the Memory Network architecture, including the match type features.
*   **Error Analysis:** A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by the models would be helpful. Are they primarily related to KB querying, dialog state tracking, or response generation?
*   **Task Difficulty:** Analyze the inherent difficulty of each task. Are some tasks inherently easier than others? How does performance correlate across tasks?
*   **Generalization to other domains:** Discuss the potential for adapting the proposed task suite to other goal-oriented domains.

Questions for the authors:

*   What are the limitations of the current task suite? Are there specific aspects of goal-oriented dialog that are not adequately captured?
*   Do you have any hypotheses about why per-dialog accuracy remains so low despite improvements in per-response accuracy?
*   What future directions do you envision for this work? How can the task suite be extended or modified to further advance the field?
*   How sensitive are the results to the specific parameters of the simulation (e.g. the number of restaurants, the size of the vocabulary)?
