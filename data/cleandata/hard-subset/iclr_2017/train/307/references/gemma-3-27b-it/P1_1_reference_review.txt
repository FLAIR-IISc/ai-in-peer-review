## Review of "Goal-Oriented Dialog Tasks"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting – restaurant reservation. Recognizing the limitations of existing datasets and the difficulty in scaling traditional slot-filling methods, the authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks designed to test crucial capabilities like dialog management, KB querying, and entity handling. The benchmark is accompanied by experiments using Memory Networks, comparisons to rule-based and IR baselines, and validation against real-world dialog data from DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service. The core contribution lies in creating a controlled, reproducible environment for assessing the strengths and weaknesses of end-to-end models in a goal-directed context, specifically highlighting areas where they currently fall short.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a critical need in the field – a rigorous and reproducible benchmark for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting. The proposed tasks are well-defined, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the current limitations of end-to-end approaches while offering valuable insights for future research. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of evaluating end-to-end dialog systems for goal-oriented tasks, emphasizing the shortcomings of current benchmarks and the difficulty of scaling traditional methods.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The design of the tasks is logically motivated by the requirements of a restaurant reservation system and breaks down the complex goal into manageable sub-problems.  The analogy to bAbI tasks is apt and provides a familiar framework for understanding the paper’s contribution.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation of Memory Networks against several baselines across both synthetic and real-world datasets. The consistent pattern of results (strong per-response accuracy, weak per-dialog accuracy) strongly supports the claim that current end-to-end models struggle with complete task execution. The inclusion of OOV tests is a particularly valuable contribution.
* **Reproducibility:**  The open-source availability of the dataset and task definitions is a significant strength, facilitating future research and comparisons.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of the results is insightful, highlighting the need for improvements in areas like KB reasoning and maintaining dialog coherence.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Search Details:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter optimization, more detail on the search process (e.g., search space, optimization algorithm) would increase transparency and reproducibility.  A table summarizing the optimal hyperparameters for each model and task (as provided in the appendix) is excellent, but integrating this information into the main text would be beneficial.
* **Error Analysis:** Expand the error analysis. While the paper mentions the challenges in interpreting KB results, a more systematic investigation of common failure cases (e.g., specific types of API call errors, incorrect entity handling) would provide more actionable insights.
* **Discussion of Limitations:**  Explicitly discuss the limitations of the synthetic benchmark. While the validation on real-world data is a good step, it's important to acknowledge that the synthetic tasks may not fully capture the complexity of real user interactions.
* **Clarify Memory Network Architecture:** While a detailed explanation is provided in the appendix, briefly summarizing the key architectural choices of the Memory Network within the main text would improve readability.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Dataset Scale:**  The training sets are relatively small (1,000 examples).  Have you investigated the impact of increasing the training set size on the performance of the Memory Networks?  Is there a point of diminishing returns?
* **KB Complexity:** The current KB is relatively simple. How would the performance of the models change with a more complex KB, featuring more entities, attributes, and relationships?  Would the OOV performance degrade further?
* **Task Interdependence:** Are the tasks designed to be independent, or is there an inherent interdependence between them (e.g., successful completion of Task 1 necessary for Task 2)?  If interdependent, could this affect the evaluation metrics?
* **Real-World Data Analysis:**  Beyond confirming the observed patterns, what specific insights did you gain from analyzing the performance on the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets? Were there any discrepancies between the synthetic and real-world results?
* **Alternative Architectures:** Have you experimented with other end-to-end architectures besides Memory Networks? If so, how did their performance compare?



Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of dialog systems. The proposed benchmark offers a much-needed resource for evaluating and improving end-to-end models for goal-oriented tasks.  The minor suggestions above are intended to further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
