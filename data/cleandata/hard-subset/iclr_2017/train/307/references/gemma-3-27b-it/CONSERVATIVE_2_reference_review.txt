This paper introduces a benchmark of five tasks, built around a restaurant reservation system, designed to evaluate the goal-oriented capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. The authors argue that existing datasets are insufficient for this purpose, lacking reproducibility and interpretability. They present a synthetic environment grounded in a knowledge base, allowing for controlled experimentation and error analysis. The paper also evaluates Memory Networks on these tasks, comparing them to rule-based and information retrieval baselines, and validates findings on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge dataset). The core contribution lies in providing a structured and reproducible testbed for assessing progress in end-to-end dialog systems for goal-oriented tasks.

**Reject.** While the motivation for creating a more controlled evaluation environment is sound, and the tasks are thoughtfully designed, the paper ultimately fails to demonstrate a compelling case for the current state of end-to-end dialog systems. The limited performance, even with the proposed benchmark, highlights significant shortcomings and the improvements reported are incremental, not groundbreaking. The reliance on a synthetic environment, despite validation on real data, raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.

The primary argument for rejection stems from the underwhelming performance of the Memory Networks (and all tested methods) on the more complex tasks (Tasks 3 & 5). While the authors acknowledge low per-dialog accuracy, the extent of this limitation isn’t sufficiently addressed. The fact that even the rule-based system outperforms the neural models on the more realistic DSTC2 task (T6) is a significant drawback. The incremental improvement gained by incorporating match type features, while positive, doesn't bridge the gap to genuinely competent goal-oriented dialog. The paper focuses heavily on dissecting the tasks and demonstrating *where* the models fail, but provides little insight into *why* they fail, or how to overcome these limitations. The performance gap between the synthetic and real-world datasets also raises a red flag. Simply achieving good results on a synthetic task doesn’t equate to a robust system.

Furthermore, the comparison to baselines feels somewhat unbalanced. While the rule-based system is presented, it’s clear its scalability is limited. The information retrieval methods serve as weak baselines, and the lack of comparison to more recent and powerful dialog state trackers or hybrid approaches weakens the assessment of the end-to-end model’s true potential. The paper’s claim that the benchmark can “gauge the strengths and weaknesses” of end-to-end systems is only valid if those strengths and weaknesses are clearly demonstrated *relative* to established methods – and this is not sufficiently established here.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Error Analysis:** Deepen the error analysis. What *specific* types of errors are the Memory Networks making? Are they failing to correctly query the KB, misinterpreting the results, or struggling with dialog state management? Providing concrete examples of failure modes would be immensely valuable.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies on the Memory Network architecture. How does the number of hops affect performance on each task? Are there specific components of the model that are particularly problematic?
*   **Dataset Details:** Provide more detailed statistics for the concierge dataset. What is the average dialog length? What is the vocabulary size? How noisy is the data?
*   **Comparison to SOTA:** Include comparisons to more state-of-the-art dialog state trackers or hybrid systems on the DSTC2 task.
*   **Generalizability:** Discuss the limitations of the synthetic environment in more detail. How might the results differ if the KB were larger or more complex?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Given the low per-dialog accuracy, what specific architectural changes or training strategies do you believe would be most effective in improving the performance of end-to-end dialog systems on these tasks?
*   Why did the rule-based system outperform the Memory Networks on Task 6 (DSTC2)? What does this suggest about the strengths and weaknesses of each approach?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of natural language patterns used to generate the user and bot utterances in the simulation? Could this be a source of bias?
*   Are you planning to release the code and data for the concierge dataset to the research community?
