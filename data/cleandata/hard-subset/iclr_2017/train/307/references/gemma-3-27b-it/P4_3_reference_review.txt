This paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating goal-oriented dialogue systems, consisting of five tasks built around a restaurant reservation scenario, along with two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service). The core idea is to create a controlled environment where the capabilities of end-to-end dialogue models can be systematically assessed, focusing on skills like API querying, knowledge base interpretation, and dialogue management. The authors also present experiments using Memory Networks, demonstrating their performance on these tasks and providing a baseline for future research.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a valuable contribution in creating a benchmark for goal-oriented dialogue, the results obtained using Memory Networks, despite some improvements, remain relatively weak. The per-dialog accuracy remains low across almost all tasks, suggesting that current end-to-end models still struggle significantly with the core challenges of goal-oriented conversation. While demonstrating the limitations is important, the paper doesn’t offer sufficient novel insights beyond confirming existing understanding of the difficulties.

The primary reason for rejection stems from the limited impact of the proposed method. While the tasks are well-defined and the benchmark is a useful resource, the paper primarily showcases the *failure* of the tested model (Memory Networks) to solve these tasks convincingly. This doesn’t advance the state-of-the-art in end-to-end dialogue significantly and feels more like a diagnostic tool than a substantial research contribution. A stronger paper would have explored more advanced architectures or learning strategies to achieve higher performance on these tasks, thereby demonstrating the benchmark’s ability to differentiate between models and drive progress.

Secondly, while the authors claim the tasks can act as a proxy for real data, the results show that performance on the synthetic tasks do not translate directly to good performance on the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets. The gap between synthetic and real-world performance suggests the simulation might not fully capture the complexities of human-computer interaction. The relatively low performance on the DSTC2 data, even compared to the simulated tasks, raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Evaluation Metrics:** The use of per-response and per-dialog accuracy is appropriate. However, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. What specific types of errors are the models making? Are they failing to understand user requests, generating incorrect API calls, or struggling with knowledge base interpretation?
*   **Model Complexity:** The choice of Memory Networks is reasonable, but exploring more recent and powerful architectures (e.g., Transformers) could potentially yield more compelling results.
*   **KB Integration:** The interaction with the knowledge base is a key aspect of the tasks. Investigating different methods for representing and querying the KB could be valuable.
*   **Out-of-Vocabulary Handling:** The OOV evaluation is a good idea, but the paper could further investigate strategies for improving generalization to unseen entities.
*   **Data Details:** More details about the Concierge dataset would be helpful, including the size of the dataset, the distribution of user requests, and the characteristics of the operators.
*   **Ablation Study:** A more thorough ablation study of the match type features would clarify their impact on different tasks.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Given the low per-dialog accuracy, what specific modifications to the Memory Network architecture or training procedure do you believe would be most likely to improve performance?
2.  The results on DSTC2 are comparatively lower than those on the simulated tasks. Can you elaborate on the potential reasons for this discrepancy and how the simulation could be improved to better reflect the challenges of real-world dialogue?
3.  The paper focuses primarily on the limitations of current end-to-end models. Do you foresee any specific architectural innovations or learning strategies that could overcome these limitations and enable end-to-end models to achieve performance comparable to traditional slot-filling approaches in goal-oriented dialogue?
4.  Can you provide more detail on the process used to anonymize the concierge data and any potential biases that may have been introduced during anonymization?
5. How were the hyperparameters tuned, and what validation set was used? Details would be valuable for reproducibility.
