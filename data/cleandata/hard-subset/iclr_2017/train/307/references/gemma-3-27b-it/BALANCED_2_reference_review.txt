This paper presents a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors create a set of five tasks, grounded in a knowledge base, designed to test crucial dialog capabilities like API querying, entity manipulation, and dialog management. They also evaluate on two real-world datasets, DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service dataset, to demonstrate the relevance of their synthetic tasks. The core contribution lies in providing a reproducible and interpretable testbed, addressing the lack of such resources in the literature.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a significant problem – the lack of good evaluation benchmarks for goal-oriented end-to-end dialog systems – and proposes a reasonable approach, the experimental results, particularly the low per-dialog accuracy, do not yet convincingly demonstrate the utility of this benchmark as a strong indicator of real-world performance. Furthermore, the gains achieved by the proposed match-type features are limited, and the analysis feels somewhat incomplete.

The strengths of the paper are clear. The motivation is excellent – evaluating end-to-end systems in goal-oriented scenarios is crucial. The task design, breaking down a complex goal into smaller, manageable subtasks, is a sound approach for error analysis. The creation of both synthetic and real-world datasets, and the conversion of DSTC2 into the proposed format, are valuable contributions.  The inclusion of out-of-vocabulary tests is particularly insightful, highlighting a key challenge for these systems. The paper is well-written and the related work section provides a good overview of the existing landscape.

However, several concerns warrant rejection.  The most significant is the consistently low per-dialog accuracy across almost all methods and tasks. While the authors acknowledge this, the analysis of *why* this is the case feels superficial.  The memory networks, while outperforming baselines, still fall far short of solving the tasks reliably. The limited improvement from the match-type features, despite being a key contribution, is disappointing.  The paper lacks a thorough ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of the Memory Network, especially regarding the match-type features.  The discussion of hyperparameters feels somewhat cursory.  The analysis of the concierge dataset is brief, and it is not entirely clear what insights are gained from comparing performance on the synthetic and real datasets. Finally, the rule-based system, while acknowledging its limitations, doesn't feel sufficiently developed to provide a strong comparison point.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Deeper Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis is needed. What types of errors are the models consistently making? Are they struggling with KB querying, interpreting results, or maintaining dialog state? Providing specific examples of failure cases would be highly beneficial.
*   **Ablation Study:**  Conduct a thorough ablation study of the Memory Network architecture, including the impact of the number of hops, the embedding dimension, and the match-type features.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Provide a more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process and justify the chosen values.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:**  Consider including more recent and stronger baseline models, such as Transformers or more advanced neural dialog models, to provide a more challenging comparison.
*   **Concierge Dataset Analysis:** Expand the analysis of the concierge dataset. What are the key differences between this dataset and the synthetic data? Are there specific types of errors that are more prevalent in the real-world data?
*   **Rule-Based System Enhancement:** Invest more effort into developing a more robust rule-based system, perhaps leveraging more sophisticated NLP techniques. This will provide a more meaningful benchmark.
*   **Qualitative Examples:** Include more qualitative examples of dialogs generated by the models, showcasing both successes and failures.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   The paper states that the tasks are designed to be solvable by rule-based systems. What is the complexity of implementing a *fully* robust rule-based system for all five tasks (including handling OOV entities)? What are the main challenges in building such a system?
*   The per-dialog accuracy is consistently low. Is this inherent to the tasks themselves, or is it a limitation of the current models? Could the tasks be modified to make them more solvable while still retaining their diagnostic value?
*   What specific types of entities pose the biggest challenge for the Memory Networks, even with match-type features?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of vocabulary and the size of the embedding matrix?
*   What metrics beyond per-response and per-dialog accuracy could be used to evaluate the quality of the generated dialogs? (e.g., coherence, fluency, relevance)
