This paper introduces a set of synthetic tasks, alongside two real-world datasets, designed to evaluate goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically focusing on the capabilities of end-to-end models. The core motivation is to provide a controlled environment for assessing these systems, bridging the gap between successful performance on chit-chat and the more demanding requirements of goal-directed conversations. The authors argue existing datasets are lacking in reproducibility and interpretability and propose a benchmark based on a restaurant reservation scenario, broken down into subtasks like API querying and KB interpretation. They evaluate Memory Networks against several baselines, demonstrating some improvements but highlighting the ongoing challenges in achieving reliable goal completion.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a valid need for better evaluation benchmarks for goal-oriented dialog, the presented work feels underdeveloped and insufficiently validated to warrant acceptance. The synthetic tasks, while cleverly designed, appear *too* synthetic, with results not consistently translating to real-world data in a manner that justifies their use as a primary evaluation tool. The gains shown by Memory Networks, while positive, are incremental and don’t represent a significant advancement over established methods, particularly given the model’s complexity.

The primary issue is a lack of compelling evidence that the proposed tasks accurately reflect the challenges of real-world goal-oriented dialog. The authors acknowledge this, attempting to address it with the DSTC2 and Concierge datasets. However, the results on these datasets, while showing similar trends in relative performance, don't demonstrate that success on the synthetic tasks reliably predicts success in realistic settings. The rule-based baseline, while intentionally simple, outperforms the more complex Memory Networks on DSTC2, further questioning the relevance of the synthetic benchmark. The paper leans heavily on the observation that the *pattern* of results is consistent across datasets, but consistency in subpar performance doesn’t inspire confidence. The low per-dialog accuracy across all methods remains a serious concern and isn’t adequately explained or addressed. The addition of match-type features offers some improvement, but feels like a post-hoc fix rather than a fundamental advance. Furthermore, the reliance on a single model architecture (Memory Networks) limits the generalizability of the findings.

Several aspects could be improved. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art dialog systems, including more sophisticated slot-filling approaches and recent transformer-based models. A detailed error analysis of the Memory Network’s failures on both synthetic and real datasets is crucial. The authors should explore why the per-dialog accuracy remains so low and investigate potential solutions beyond simply adding match-type features. The impact of different KB sizes and complexities on performance should also be investigated. The ablation studies, while present, are not extensive enough to fully justify the design choices. Finally, the connection between the task breakdown and the skills required for successful goal-oriented dialog should be more clearly articulated.

Here are some specific questions for the authors:

*   Given the low per-dialog accuracy, what specific types of errors are most frequent? Is it primarily API call generation, KB interpretation, or dialog state management?
*   How sensitive are the results to the size and quality of the KB? Would performance improve with a larger, more comprehensive KB?
*   Why does the rule-based baseline outperform the Memory Network on the DSTC2 dataset?
*   Could you provide examples of dialogs where the Memory Network successfully completes subtasks (e.g., API calls) but fails to achieve the overall goal?
*   Have you explored other end-to-end architectures (e.g., Transformers) on these tasks? If so, how do their results compare to Memory Networks?
*   The paper states "solving our tasks requires manipulating both natural language and symbols from a KB." How much of the performance gain is attributable to the natural language processing component versus the KB interaction component? Could the tasks be simplified to isolate the contribution of each?
