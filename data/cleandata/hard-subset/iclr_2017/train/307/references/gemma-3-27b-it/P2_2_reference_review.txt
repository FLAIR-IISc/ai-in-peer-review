## ICLR 2024 Review

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper introduces a benchmark of five tasks, built around a restaurant reservation system with an underlying knowledge base, designed to evaluate the goal-oriented capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. The authors argue that existing datasets are insufficient for this purpose due to limitations in reproducibility, scalability, and interpretability. They demonstrate the utility of their benchmark by evaluating Memory Networks, comparing their performance against rule-based and information retrieval baselines, and validating the findings on real-world dialog datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge dataset). The core finding is that while end-to-end models show promise, they still lag behind traditional methods in reliably completing goal-oriented dialogs, highlighting the challenges in areas like KB integration and multi-turn reasoning.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper addresses a crucial gap in the evaluation of end-to-end dialog systems, providing a much-needed, controlled environment for systematic analysis. The benchmark itself is a valuable contribution, and the experimental results clearly demonstrate the limitations of current end-to-end approaches while offering directions for future research.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Well-defined Problem & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems and convincingly argues for the need for a dedicated benchmark. The shortcomings of existing datasets (noisy, state-focused, lack of reproducibility) are well-explained.
*   **Sound Approach:** The decomposition of goal-oriented dialog into specific subtasks (API calling, KB interpretation, etc.) is a clever approach that enables targeted error analysis. The use of a simulated environment with a knowledge base allows for precise control and reproducibility.
*   **Empirical Support:** The experiments are well-designed and executed. The comparison with strong baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings) provides a clear picture of the strengths and weaknesses of Memory Networks. The validation on real-world datasets strengthens the relevance of the findings.
*   **Clear Results & Analysis:** The results are presented clearly, and the authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the limitations of the models tested. The observation that per-response accuracy is encouraging, but per-dialog accuracy remains low, is particularly insightful. The impact of match type features is also well-demonstrated.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Task Complexity:** While the decomposition into tasks is helpful, some tasks (e.g., Task 5 - full dialogs) appear more complex than others. A more detailed analysis of the difficulty levels of each task and how they contribute to the overall challenge would be beneficial.
*   **Memory Network Details:** More details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the Memory Networks would be helpful, particularly regarding the choice of number of hops and embedding dimensions.
*   **Error Analysis:** Although the paper highlights areas where end-to-end models struggle, a more in-depth error analysis with specific examples of failure cases would strengthen the conclusions. What *specifically* causes the drop in per-dialog accuracy? Providing a categorization of common errors would be valuable.
*   **KB Size and Complexity:** Exploring the impact of larger and more complex knowledge bases on the performance of the models could provide further insights into the scalability of the approach.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge some limitations, a more comprehensive discussion of the potential biases in the simulated environment and the generalizability of the findings to other domains would be appreciated.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding the choice of Memory Networks as the primary end-to-end model, were other architectures (e.g., Transformers) explored? If so, why were Memory Networks selected for more extensive evaluation?
*   The "match type features" seem crucial for OOV performance. Could you elaborate on the design choices behind these features, and potentially explore other ways to incorporate type information into the model?
*   You mention the difficulty of building rule-based systems for real data (DSTC2). Could you quantify the effort required to create a competitive rule-based system for this dataset compared to the simulated tasks? This would highlight the practical challenges of hand-crafting systems.
*   The paper focuses on a restaurant reservation domain. Do you believe the proposed benchmark is generalizable to other goal-oriented tasks, or would significant modifications be needed?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of dialog systems. The proposed benchmark is a valuable resource for researchers, and the experimental results provide important insights into the challenges of building effective end-to-end conversational agents. The suggested improvements are minor and intended to further strengthen the impact of the work.
