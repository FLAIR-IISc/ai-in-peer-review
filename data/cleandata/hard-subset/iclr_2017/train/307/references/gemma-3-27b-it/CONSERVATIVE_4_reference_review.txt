This paper introduces a set of tasks and datasets designed to evaluate end-to-end dialog systems in a goal-oriented setting, specifically restaurant reservation. The authors argue that existing datasets are insufficient for rigorous evaluation and propose a synthetic environment grounded in a knowledge base, alongside conversions of existing datasets (DSTC2 and a proprietary concierge service dataset), to provide a more controlled and reproducible evaluation framework. They test Memory Networks on these tasks, comparing them to rule-based, information retrieval, and supervised embedding baselines, finding that while end-to-end models show promise, they still lag behind traditional methods in achieving full dialog success (per-dialog accuracy).

**Reject.** While the motivation for creating a more controlled evaluation environment is sound, and the tasks are well-defined, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating the utility of this framework or providing compelling evidence that end-to-end models are meaningfully progressing towards solving goal-oriented dialog. The performance gap between the proposed system and simple rule-based methods on the real-world datasets (DSTC2 and the concierge data) is concerning, and the improvements offered by the Memory Networks feel incremental rather than transformative. The reliance on a synthetic environment raises questions about the generalizability of the findings.

The primary issue is the lack of substantial evidence supporting the claim that these tasks accurately reflect the challenges of real-world goal-oriented dialog. While the authors acknowledge this and include real-world datasets, the performance on those datasets doesn’t inspire confidence. The rule-based baseline on DSTC2 achieving a non-trivial accuracy suggests the task, even when converted, may not be sufficiently complex to truly differentiate sophisticated models. The authors show improvements with Memory Networks compared to simpler baselines, but the overall per-dialog accuracy remains low, even with the addition of match-type features. This suggests fundamental limitations in the approach or a need for significantly more complex models. The justification for selecting Memory Networks specifically also feels somewhat arbitrary; a more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art goal-oriented dialog systems (even traditional POMDP-based methods) would be valuable. The paper’s focus on *creating* a testbed feels disproportionately large compared to the depth of analysis of the results obtained using that testbed.

Further, the claim that the synthetic environment provides good generalization to real data is not fully substantiated. The observation that the *relative* performance of methods is consistent across tasks doesn’t equate to the tasks being a valid proxy for real-world complexity. The OOV test sets are a good addition, but the results don't dramatically alter the overall conclusion. The addition of match type features provides some improvement, but the benefit is limited to specific tasks, and doesn't address the broader issue of low per-dialog accuracy. The paper lacks a thorough error analysis beyond simply stating that the models struggle with interpreting API results.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Stronger baselines:** Include comparisons with stronger, state-of-the-art goal-oriented dialog systems, even if they are traditional POMDP-based models. This would provide a more meaningful benchmark.
*   **More detailed error analysis:** Provide a detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the models on the real-world datasets. What specific aspects of the task are causing the most difficulty? Are the errors consistent across datasets?
*   **Ablation studies:** Conduct more detailed ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the Memory Network and the match-type features.
*   **Generalization argument:** Provide stronger justification for why the synthetic environment is a good proxy for real-world dialog. Perhaps demonstrate correlations between performance on the synthetic tasks and performance on other real-world datasets.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** Be more upfront about the limitations of the proposed framework and the challenges that remain in building effective end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   What is the computational cost of running the rule-based system compared to the Memory Networks, and how does this scale with task complexity?
*   Why were Memory Networks specifically chosen as the end-to-end model? Were other architectures considered, and if so, why were they excluded?
*   Can you provide specific examples of the types of errors made by the Memory Networks on the real-world datasets? What is causing these errors?
*   How sensitive are the results to the size and composition of the training data? Have you explored the effects of data augmentation or transfer learning?
*   Given the low per-dialog accuracy, what specific architectural or training modifications do you believe are most likely to improve performance?
