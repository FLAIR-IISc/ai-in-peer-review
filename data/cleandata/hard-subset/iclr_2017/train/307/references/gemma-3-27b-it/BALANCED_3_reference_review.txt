This paper introduces a benchmark of five tasks, built around a restaurant reservation domain, designed to evaluate the goal-oriented capabilities of end-to-end dialog systems. The motivation is clear: existing evaluation methods for dialog systems are often noisy, non-reproducible, or don’t effectively isolate specific skills needed for goal-oriented conversation. This work directly addresses these limitations by creating a controlled environment for testing dialog agents, providing both synthetic and real-world datasets. The tasks progressively increase in complexity, requiring dialog management, KB querying, result interpretation, and handling of unseen entities. The paper also presents experiments with Memory Networks, demonstrating their performance on the proposed tasks and comparisons to baselines.

**Reject**

While the proposed benchmark is a valuable contribution to the field, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating the *impact* of this benchmark and the *insights* gained from the experiments. The results, while thorough, do not reveal surprising or groundbreaking findings. The conclusions – that end-to-end models still struggle with goal-oriented dialog, particularly in complex reasoning – are well-known. The paper primarily *confirms* existing limitations rather than pushing the boundaries of understanding or offering novel solutions.  Specifically, the per-dialog accuracy remains low, and the paper doesn't deeply analyze *why* the models fail, beyond stating that interpreting KB results is a challenge.

The primary argument supporting the rejection lies in the incremental nature of the contribution and the lack of compelling findings. The paper convincingly establishes a useful benchmark, but the experiments performed don’t fully leverage its potential. The results are largely expected: Memory Networks perform better than simpler baselines, but still fall far short of human-level performance, and struggle with tasks requiring complex reasoning.  The addition of match-type features is a good idea, but the gains are modest and don’t fundamentally address the core limitations. The comparison to rule-based systems, while demonstrating the difficulty of the tasks, doesn’t offer much insight into the strengths and weaknesses of neural models. Finally, while the authors address concerns about reproducibility, the datasets are relatively small, and the experiments are somewhat limited in scope.

Here’s further feedback to improve the paper:

*   **Deeper Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit significantly from a more in-depth analysis of the errors made by the models. What specific types of KB queries are they struggling with? Are they failing to correctly track dialog state? Are there specific linguistic patterns that confuse them? Providing concrete examples of failure cases and analyzing the underlying reasons would significantly strengthen the contribution.
*   **More Advanced Models:** The experiments are primarily focused on Memory Networks. Exploring more recent and sophisticated end-to-end models, such as Transformers, or incorporating pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the current state-of-the-art.
*   **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies on the match-type features would be valuable. What is the contribution of each type of feature? Can they be combined more effectively?
*   **Expand the Dataset:** Consider expanding the size of the datasets, particularly the real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge). A larger dataset could provide more robust results and allow for more powerful models to be trained.
*   **Clarify Task Difficulty:** The paper states the tasks are designed to be progressively harder, but this isn't fully substantiated with clear metrics or analysis. Quantifying the difficulty of each task (e.g., by measuring the number of reasoning steps required) would be helpful.
*   **Addressing OOV:** While the OOV split is a good idea, the analysis of the OOV results could be expanded. What types of OOV entities are the models struggling with the most?
*   **Reproducibility Details:** While reproducibility is mentioned, the paper could benefit from more detailed information about the experimental setup, including the specific versions of the libraries used, the training hyperparameters, and the hardware configuration.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   What is the primary intended use case of this benchmark? Is it for developing new models, for evaluating existing models, or for identifying areas for future research?
*   What specific aspects of goal-oriented dialog are most challenging for current end-to-end models, based on your error analysis?
*   How do you see this benchmark evolving in the future? What types of tasks or datasets could be added to make it more comprehensive?
*   Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to train and evaluate the models on this benchmark?
*   Why was the choice made to only evaluate on Memory Networks and the baselines? Were there limitations that prevented evaluating other models?
