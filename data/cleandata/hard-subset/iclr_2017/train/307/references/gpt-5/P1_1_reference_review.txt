Summary and contributions
- Problem: The paper asks whether end-to-end neural dialog models can handle goal-oriented tasks that require state tracking, KB querying, and reasoning over retrieved facts, and proposes a reproducible testbed to evaluate these capabilities.
- Contributions:
  1) A suite of five synthetic goal-oriented dialog tasks (restaurant booking) that decompose key abilities: issuing/updating API calls, sorting and proposing options from KB results, extracting KB facts, and conducting full dialogs; with in-vocabulary and OOV test splits to probe entity generalization.
  2) A conversion of DSTC2 dialogs into the same framework (Task 6), and evaluation on a proprietary human-human “Concierge” dataset to assess realism and transferability of findings.
  3) Baselines spanning IR methods, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks (with an effective “match type” feature for entity handling), with per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics and ablations (hops, type features).
  4) Empirical insights: MemNNs learn to issue/update API calls and benefit greatly from type/match features and multiple hops, but still struggle with reasoning over KB results (e.g., sorted option display) and maintaining correctness over entire dialogs; patterns on synthetic tasks mirror performance on DSTC2 and Concierge.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Provides a well-motivated, reproducible benchmark that fills a clear gap for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog evaluation, with thoughtful task design (including OOV generalization) and metrics aligned with task success.
- Empirical study is careful and insightful, comparing multiple families of baselines and yielding actionable analyses (e.g., benefits of match types and hops, where models fail), with results consistent across synthetic and real data.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well placed in the literature, contrasting slot-filling/POMDP pipelines with neural end-to-end approaches, and highlighting the lack of suitable open, reproducible resources for goal-oriented evaluation. The connection to bAbI QA, DSTC, and recent end-to-end dialog work is appropriate and clear.
- Technical design: Tasks explicitly target core capabilities needed for transactional dialog (state elicitation and updates, KB interaction, sorting/ranking options, KB fact grounding), and the OOV split stresses realistic generalization to unseen entities. The ranking setup focuses the evaluation on decision-making rather than surface generation, which is reasonable for this benchmark’s goals.
- Empirical rigor: Includes multiple baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, MemNNs), ablations (with/without type features, number of hops), two metrics (per-response and per-dialog success), and cross-dataset validation (DSTC2, Concierge). The match type feature is a sensible and effective mechanism to handle entities and OOVs. The observation that synthetic-task trends transfer to DSTC2/Concierge supports claims that the benchmark is a useful proxy.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Task construction and evaluation are described clearly; the dataset is stated to be publicly available. Hyperparameters and additional results are provided in appendices.

Caveats and limitations (do not outweigh strengths)
- Synthetic language patterns are limited (43 user/20 bot templates), and the bot behavior/order is deterministic, which may reduce linguistic diversity and strategy variability. Nonetheless, the authors mitigate this with DSTC2 and Concierge experiments.
- The ranking evaluation uses a candidate set that includes utterances across train/val/test and tasks, which simplifies output space vs. true generation; generalization in a generative setting remains untested.
- The DSTC2 conversion (e.g., adding ratings, assuming deterministic option ordering) may introduce biases versus the original task.
- Comparisons are limited to MemNNs vs. IR/embedding baselines; more recent neural architectures with copy/pointer mechanisms or sequence-to-sequence models could provide a fuller picture. However, given the timeframe and the paper’s benchmark focus, this is acceptable.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify and justify the construction of the global candidate set and discuss potential leakage or simplification it introduces; consider reporting results with candidate sets restricted to plausible actions for the current state.
- Report robustness across random seeds and provide variance/confidence intervals; add significance tests for key comparisons.
- Expand analysis of failure cases on T3/T5 (e.g., diagnostic probes for sorting/option-selection errors, attention patterns) and consider minimal task variants to isolate sorting vs. grounding errors.
- Include or reference experiments with generative models or pointer/copy mechanisms, especially for OOV entities and KB grounding, to broaden baselines.
- For DSTC2 conversion: detail how added ratings were assigned and quantify how often bot behavior is non-deterministic; consider a setting that tolerates multiple acceptable orders when options have tied ratings.
- Consider releasing expanded language pattern sets and multi-domain variants to test cross-domain generalization, and, if possible, a sanitized subset of Concierge to enhance reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set: Does including all candidates from train/val/test (including OOV tasks) risk inflating per-response accuracy? Can you report results with a pruned candidate set constrained by dialog state or action type?
- OOV setup: Models have access to both KBs at training time. How realistic is this assumption, and how sensitive are results if the OOV KB is only available at test time? Do type features require full KB access or just schema?
- DSTC2 conversion: How were the ratings assigned and how often are multiple options tied? Did you test alternative tie-breaking policies and their impact on results?
- Task 3 sorting: Can you quantify whether failures are due more to sorting, mis-grounding, or stopping criteria (accept/reject)? Any evidence from attention weights?
- Training size sensitivity: How do results scale beyond 1k examples? Do MemNNs and supervised embeddings close the per-dialog gap with more data?
- Generalization to generation: Have you tried a generative MemNN or seq2seq with copy for these tasks, and if so, how do results compare?
- Reproducibility: Will code, data, and the specific Concierge evaluation splits be made available to ensure exact replication of results?
