- Introduces an open, lightweight benchmark for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog around restaurant booking, with five synthetic tasks plus two real datasets (DSTC2 re-cast, concierge).

- Decomposes goal achievement into API call issuance/updates, option ranking, factual lookup, and full dialog to probe distinct dialog skills.

- Benchmark probes implicit state tracking, KB querying/sorting, reading/using KB facts, and handling OOV entities; reports per-response and per-dialog metrics.

- Evaluates baselines (IR, supervised embeddings, MemN2N with/without type features) and a rule-based system, showing where end-to-end models succeed and fail.

- Finds MemN2N good at state tracking and issuing API calls but weak at interpreting KB results, option display, and end-to-end success; match-type features help OOV handling.

- Decision: Accept.

- Key reason: Fills a gap for reproducible, interpretable evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog with a well-motivated task decomposition.

- Key reason: Empirical study is thoughtful and reveals consistent, nontrivial insights about model strengths/limitations across synthetic and real data.

- Positioning: Well situated against slot-filling/POMDP and chit-chat work; appropriately connected to bAbI-style tasks and DSTC datasets.

- Methodology: Tasks are precisely defined with a structured KB, a well-designed OOV split, and a ranking evaluation that enables per-dialog accuracy measurement.

- Baselines & analysis: Useful spectrum of baselines; MemN2N outperforms simpler baselines and type features substantially improve OOV performance and Task 4 capability.

- Important finding: Large per-response vs per-dialog performance gap highlights limitations of current end-to-end approaches.

- Reproducibility/utility: Public release and lightweight design should foster adoption; task decomposition supports built-in error analysis.

- Weakness: Heavy reliance on synthetic, templated language and deterministic system behavior may bias models toward pattern matching.

- Weakness: Global candidate-set ranking (including test candidates) simplifies generation and may not reflect deployment constraints.

- Weakness: Match-type features add feature engineering and assume perfect KB entity recognition and access to all KBs at training time, possibly overstating OOV generalization.

- Weakness: Missing stronger neural baselines (copy/pointer seq2seq, key-value memory networks, Transformer retrieval/generation) that could change conclusions.

- Weakness: Limited analysis of potential leakage and robustness — impact of including all candidates, subword modeling for OOV, and sensitivity to noise not fully explored.

- Weakness: Evidence that synthetic tasks proxy real-world performance is suggestive but limited due to DSTC2 conversion heuristics and concierge lacking explicit KB outputs.

- Suggested improvement: Provide MemN2N ablations (hops, time/speaker features, memory size, attention temperature) and report variance across random seeds.

- Suggested improvement: Evaluate stronger baselines (copy-augmented seq2seq/pointer networks, key-value memories, Transformer retrieval) and include subword tokenization experiments.

- Suggested improvement: Clarify OOV setup — report results when the OOV KB is not exposed during training and when type lexicons or entity detection are noisy.

- Suggested improvement: Address candidate-set leakage by comparing to dynamic candidate sets and generative evaluations (slot/value/API accuracy).

- Suggested improvement: Expand error analysis for MemN2N on Tasks 3/5 (sort errors vs entity grounding vs dialog control) with qualitative traces and KB-attention visualizations.

- Suggested improvement: Discuss generalization to multi-domain, compositional slots, and portability beyond restaurants.

- Question: Does the candidate set include test-only responses, and how does performance change if candidates are restricted to training-only or evaluated generatively?

- Question: For OOV protocol, what are results when the OOV KB is only available at test time, and how sensitive are results to imperfect entity matching/type detection?

- Question: Do type features require exact string matching; how do they handle multi-token entities and typos, and could subword units reduce dependency on engineered types?

- Question: For Task 3, what fraction of errors stem from sorting vs selecting the right entity vs dialog flow, and have key-value memory variants been tried?

- Question: What is the standard deviation across runs, and how does performance degrade with increased lexical variation or ASR-like noise?

- Question: In DSTC2 conversion, how often does the deterministic "highest rating" policy contradict the original bot, and could that mismatch cap achievable accuracy?
