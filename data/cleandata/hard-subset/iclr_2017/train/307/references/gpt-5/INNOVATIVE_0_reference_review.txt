Summary
The paper introduces an open, lightweight, and reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, centered on restaurant reservations. It breaks the task into five synthetic subtasks (API call issuance, updating requests, option display with ranking, information retrieval from KB results, and full dialogs), adds an OOV split to stress entity generalization, and supplements with two more realistic datasets: a conversion of DSTC2 into the same format and an operator–user “Concierge” corpus. The authors benchmark rule-based systems, classical IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with a simple but effective “match type” mechanism for entities). Results show that end-to-end models can learn to issue/update API calls and handle OOV entities with type features, but still struggle with interpreting and presenting KB results and maintaining end-to-end dialog success. The resource is positioned as a controlled proxy to study capabilities that transfer to real settings.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, useful, and bold contribution: a unified, controlled benchmark for goal-oriented dialog with explicit stress tests (OOV entities, KB interaction, sorting) and per-dialog success metrics, plus bridging to real data.
- Clear empirical insights that identify where current end-to-end methods fail, likely to catalyze progress.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper convincingly argues the need for reproducible, interpretable evaluation of goal-oriented dialog beyond chit-chat metrics, and situates the work relative to POMDP systems, DSTC resources, and bAbI QA tasks. The decomposition of skills (state tracking, KB querying, KB result interpretation, entity handling) is well motivated.
- Contributions and novelty:
  - A compact suite of synthetic tasks that are diverse yet controlled, with a principled OOV split and per-dialog accuracy.
  - A standardized ranking setup and candidate pool across tasks (facilitating comparability).
  - Conversion of DSTC2 to the same format for realism, and evaluation on a proprietary Concierge dataset to test transfer.
  - A simple match-type feature that substantially improves OOV/entity handling in end-to-end models.
- Experimental support: Multiple families of baselines are compared under consistent settings. The paper provides detailed metric reporting (per-response and per-dialog), hyperparameters, ablations (type features, hops), and qualitative analyses. The observed pattern of results transfers from synthetic tasks to DSTC2 and Concierge, supporting the claim that the tasks are a useful proxy.
- Impact: A high-likelihood-of-use benchmark that targets a gap in the community (goal-oriented, end-to-end, reproducible), enabling finer-grained progress and clearer error analysis.

Caveats (not blocking)
- Synthetic data is templated and partially deterministic (e.g., fixed slot-question order, fixed bot templates), which may inflate performance for methods that exploit regularities and underrepresent real dialog variability.
- Evaluation is next-utterance ranking with a candidate set pooled from train/valid/test across tasks. While standard for classification-style evaluation, it is less faithful to generation and raises questions about including test-only candidates in the vocabulary.
- The OOV setup allows access to both KBs during training to enable type matches; this is practical but less stringent than withholding test-entity KB values entirely.
- Modeling baselines are somewhat dated and limited to one end-to-end architecture; pointer/copy, key–value memory, and retrieval-augmented architectures could change conclusions.
- No human evaluation of dialog success/naturalness; per-dialog accuracy is strict but still a proxy.

Suggestions to improve (for authors’ benefit; not required for acceptance)
- Increase linguistic and behavioral variability: diversify bot templates, randomize slot-question order, inject paraphrases/ASR-like noise to better stress robustness.
- Consider a generative track (e.g., copy/pointer, constrained decoding) alongside ranking, and report both success and calibration/uncertainty.
- Revisit the candidate-pool protocol; a setting with candidates derived only from train/valid (plus a standardized set of API formats) would remove any concern about test leakage.
- Provide more ablations: effect of time/speaker features; number of hops; contributions of each match-type to gains; learning curves vs. data size.
- Extend to additional domains or a multi-domain split to test transfer and schema generalization.
- Release code to convert DSTC2 and scripts to generate the synthetic tasks; clarify licensing and versioning for long-term reproducibility.
- Add error typology on T3/T5 failures (e.g., sorting mistakes vs. grounding vs. dialog flow) to guide modeling improvements.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you clarify the rationale for including test-set candidates in the ranking pool? Have you evaluated a variant where the candidate set is restricted to train/validation utterances/templates?
2. In the OOV setup, models can access the “test KB” during training for type matching. What happens if the test KB is withheld entirely (i.e., no exposure to test entities at training time)?
3. How sensitive are results to increasing the variability of bot templates and to relaxing the deterministic slot-question order?
4. On Task 3, do errors primarily stem from sorting, grounding to KB facts, or dialog control (e.g., turn-taking, knowing when to stop)? Any breakdown available?
5. Could you report human-judged success on a small subset (especially for T6/Concierge) to validate per-dialog accuracy as a proxy?
6. Are the synthetic generators and the DSTC2 conversion scripts planned for public release with fixed seeds to ensure exact reproducibility?
7. Did you test pointer/copy or key–value memory variants, and if so, how do they compare with match-type features?
8. How does performance scale with more training dialogs (e.g., 5k/10k), and does the gap on T3/T5 shrink meaningfully?

Overall, this is a timely, well-executed benchmark paper that opens a concrete path for progress on end-to-end goal-oriented dialog. Despite limitations inherent to synthetic data and the ranking setup, the contribution is novel, actionable, and likely to have significant impact. I recommend acceptance.
