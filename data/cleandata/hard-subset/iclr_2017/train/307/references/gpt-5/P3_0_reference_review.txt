Summary and contributions
- The paper introduces an open, lightweight benchmark for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog, centered on restaurant booking. It decomposes goal achievement into five synthetic tasks (API call issuance and updates, option ranking, factual lookup, and the full dialog), plus two datasets from real interactions (DSTC2 re-cast and a concierge service).
- The benchmark systematically probes capabilities that matter for task-oriented dialog: implicit state tracking, KB querying, sorting, reading and using KB facts, and handling OOV entities. It reports per-response and per-dialog metrics to reflect goal completion.
- The authors evaluate a broad set of baselines (IR, supervised embeddings, MemN2N with and without type-matching features) and a rule-based system, providing a clear picture of where end-to-end models succeed (state tracking and issuing API calls) and fail (interpreting KB results, option display, end-to-end success), including in OOV settings. The “match type” features are a simple and effective addition for OOV entity handling.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The dataset/benchmark fills a clear gap for reproducible, interpretable evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog. The decomposition into tasks with explicit skills is well-motivated and likely to be influential.
- The empirical study is thoughtful and reveals nontrivial insights about current end-to-end models’ strengths and limitations, including OOV handling and the challenges of KB reasoning, with consistent trends across synthetic and real data.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and positioning: The paper is well placed in the literature, contrasting traditional slot-filling/POMDP approaches with end-to-end systems, highlighting the lack of suitable goal-oriented benchmarks compared to chit-chat corpora. The connection to bAbI-style tasks and DSTC datasets is appropriate, and the motivation to create a reproducible, interpretable testbed is compelling.
- Methodology clarity and rigor: Tasks are precisely defined, with a controlled simulator grounded in a structured KB. The OOV split is well designed to test generalization to unseen entities. The evaluation protocol (ranking over a fixed candidate set) allows controlled comparisons and per-dialog accuracy captures goal completion.
- Baselines and analysis: A useful spectrum of baselines is covered. MemN2N outperforms IR and supervised embeddings on most tasks; type features substantially improve OOV performance and enable Task 4. The per-response vs per-dialog gap is an important and sobering finding. The inclusion of DSTC2- and concierge-based evaluations strengthens the claim that synthetic tasks reflect trends on real data.
- Reproducibility and utility: Public release and lightweight design will foster adoption. The built-in error analysis via task decomposition is a valuable design choice.

Main weaknesses/concerns
- Heavy reliance on synthetic, templated language with deterministic system behavior in Tasks 1–5 may bias models toward pattern matching; the rank-based evaluation with a global candidate set (including test candidates) simplifies the generation problem and may not reflect deployment constraints.
- The “match type” features, while effective, introduce feature engineering and an assumption of perfect KB entity recognition and access to all KBs (including the OOV half) at train time. This blurs the boundary of end-to-end learning and may overstate OOV generalization compared to settings without explicit KB lexicons.
- Missing stronger neural baselines: no sequence-to-sequence with attention/copy/pointer mechanisms, key-value memory networks, or Transformer-based retrieval/generation models; these are likely to materially change the conclusions about option display and KB reading.
- Limited analysis of potential leakage and robustness: the impact of including all candidate responses from train/val/test in the ranking set, the effect of subword modeling on OOV, and sensitivity to noise (ASR errors, typos) are not fully explored.
- The evidence that the synthetic tasks are a good proxy for real-world performance is suggestive but limited; DSTC2 conversion imposes heuristics (e.g., added ratings, deterministic option ordering), and concierge lacks explicit KB outputs.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide ablations on MemN2N: effect of hops, time and speaker features, memory size, and the attention temperature. Include variance across random seeds.
- Evaluate stronger baselines: copy-augmented seq2seq/pointer networks, key-value memories for KB facts, and a Transformer retrieval model. Include subword tokenization to assess OOV without type features.
- Clarify and tighten the OOV setup: report results when the second KB is not exposed at training time; report performance when type lexicons are unavailable, or when entity detection is noisy.
- Address potential candidate-set leakage: compare to a setting with a dynamic candidate set derived only from training and the task at hand, and to a generative evaluation (e.g., slot-value accuracy, API parameter accuracy).
- Expand error analysis: categorize MemN2N failures in Tasks 3/5 (e.g., sort errors vs entity grounding vs dialog control), and include qualitative traces with attention to KB facts.
- Discuss generalization: multi-domain extension, compositionality of slots, and portability beyond restaurants.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set: Does it include responses that appear only in test sets? How does performance change if candidates are restricted to training-only responses, or when evaluated with a generative model?
- OOV protocol: Models are given both KBs during training. Can you report results when the OOV KB is only available at test time? How sensitive are results to imperfect entity matching/type detection?
- Type features: Do they require string-exact matching only? How do they handle multi-token entities and typos? Could subword units reduce the dependency on engineered types?
- Sorting and KB reasoning: What proportion of Task 3 errors are due to sorting vs selecting the right entity vs dialog flow? Have you tried key-value memory variants to separate keys (attributes) from values?
- Robustness and variance: What is the standard deviation across runs? How does performance degrade with increased lexical variation or noise (e.g., paraphrase patterns, injected ASR-like errors)?
- DSTC2 conversion: How often does the deterministic “highest rating” policy contradict the original bot behavior? Could this mismatch cap achievable accuracy?
