Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a lightweight, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems. The main contributions are:
- A suite of five synthetic tasks in a restaurant-reservation domain that decompose goal-directed dialogue into subskills (API call issuance and updates, option display with sorting, KB grounding, and full dialogue).
- OOV test splits that stress handling unseen entities via KB grounding.
- A conversion of DSTC2 into the same task format (Task 6), and results on a proprietary Concierge dataset, to partially validate transfer from synthetic to real interactions.
- Baseline comparisons spanning IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with and without type-match features), with per-response and per-dialog metrics.

The goal is not to set SOTA but to provide a controlled, interpretable benchmark to analyze the strengths/weaknesses of end-to-end systems on goal-oriented dialogue.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The benchmark is well-motivated, addresses a recognized gap (reproducible evaluation for goal-oriented dialogue), and is carefully constructed with interpretable subtasks and OOV splits.
- The experimental analysis is thorough for a resource paper, situating end-to-end models relative to established baselines and highlighting persistent gaps (notably low per-dialog success on tasks requiring KB reasoning and option ranking).

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity and placement: The paper correctly identifies limitations of slot-filling/POMDP pipelines for domain scaling and the inadequacy of chit-chat datasets for goal-directed evaluation. Related work is covered, and the link to prior “bAbI”-style testbeds is explicit.
- Methodological soundness: The synthetic generator is transparent (templates, KB schema, deterministic bot behavior where appropriate), enabling reproducibility and targeted error analysis. The OOV split is principled for testing entity generalization via KB matching. The evaluation protocol (ranking over a fixed candidate set, per-response and per-dialog metrics) isolates system selection accuracy and end-to-end task completion.
- Empirical validation: Baselines are diverse and established. The MemN2N vs IR vs supervised embeddings comparison is fair and systematic. The analysis of match-type features is informative; showing gains on OOV and KB-grounding (T4) is useful. The observed performance pattern on synthetic tasks largely carries over to DSTC2 and Concierge, lending the synthetic tasks some external validity.
- Rigor: Hyperparameter selections and architectural details are given; ablations on hops and type features are discussed; examples and attention visualizations aid interpretability.

That said, there are limitations that temper enthusiasm:
- Synthetic language and deterministic policies reduce ecological validity; improvements may overfit to template regularities. The “display options” task embeds a fixed sorting heuristic and acceptance probability that may not reflect real decision-making.
- The ranking setup with a global candidate pool mixes utterance/API-call selection across tasks and can distort difficulty; it also sidesteps generation and grounding challenges present in deployed systems.
- The DSTC2 conversion introduces additional assumptions (e.g., injecting ratings to force determinism). This undermines comparability to prior DSTC2 results and potentially biases the task toward methods that exploit determinism.
- The OOV evaluation gives models access to both KBs during training; success there primarily reflects lookup/type matching rather than true generalization of representations to unseen entities.
- The Concierge evaluation is proprietary and stripped of API results, limiting transparency and reproducibility of those findings.

Suggestions for improvement (not required for acceptance)
- Strengthen baselines: include copy/pointer mechanisms, key–value memory networks, span-based retrieval for KB facts, and competitive seq2seq/Transformer with copy and entity-typing. Compare to a learned DST (e.g., RNN-based trackers) and simple supervised policy-learning baselines.
- Tighten OOV setting: evaluate a stricter variant where the OOV KB is not available during training, and/or measure performance when only entity types (but not lexicons) are known; control for reliance on exact matches by injecting surface form perturbations and paraphrases.
- Stress-test robustness: vary template sets, introduce noise/ASR-like errors, and report sensitivity to candidate set size, template perturbations, and random seeds.
- Expand evaluation: report task success rate in an interactive simulation (beyond per-dialog accuracy), latency/memory footprint, and calibration/confidence for action selection.
- Clarify DSTC2 conversion: document all modifications, error propagation from ASR, and non-deterministic bot behaviors; consider releasing the converted splits and scripts for full reproducibility.
- Isolate task effects: report results when candidates are restricted per task, and provide fine-grained error taxonomies (API formation vs option selection vs KB retrieval vs entity grounding).

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the choice/number of templates for users and bots? Do models trained on one template set transfer to held-out paraphrase templates?
2. Why is the OOV KB made available during training? Can you report results where the OOV KB is unseen until test time, to separate exact-lexicon matching from more principled generalization?
3. How does performance change if you remove deterministic ordering (e.g., the bot’s slot-asking order, or fixed rating-based sorting) or inject ambiguity (ties, missing attributes)?
4. For DSTC2, can you quantify how often the original bot’s non-determinism/ASR errors conflict with your injected determinism (ratings-based option choice) and how this impacts accuracy?
5. How robust are conclusions to the candidate set design? Do per-dialog accuracies improve if candidates are restricted to those admissible given the current state?
6. Can you release the generator, conversion scripts, and exact KBs/templates, and specify licensing for the synthetic tasks and the converted DSTC2 task?

Overall, this is a useful, well-motivated benchmark paper with careful baselines and actionable analysis. Despite limitations inherent to synthetic tasks and the ranking protocol, the resource fills a genuine gap and should be valuable to the community.
