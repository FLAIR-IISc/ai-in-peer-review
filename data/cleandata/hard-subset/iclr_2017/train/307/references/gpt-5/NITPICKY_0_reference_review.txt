Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a lightweight, reproducible testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog in the restaurant reservation domain: five synthetic tasks (bAbI-style), a conversion of DSTC2 into the same format, and results on a proprietary concierge dataset. The tasks probe capabilities such as implicit state tracking (Tasks 1–2), KB use and sorting (Tasks 3–4), and full dialogs (Task 5), with OOV splits to test generalization to unseen entities. The evaluation is next-utterance ranking with per-response and per-dialog accuracy.
- Baselines include TF-IDF, nearest neighbor IR, supervised embeddings, and MemN2N, with “match type” features for entities. MemN2N generally outperforms other baselines; match-type features substantially help OOV and Task 4; models still struggle on Tasks 3 and 5. The authors argue that trends carry over to DSTC2 and the concierge data, supporting the utility of the synthetic tasks as a proxy.

Decision
Reject. While the goal is worthy and the testbed is potentially useful, the work has significant methodological limitations and polish issues that, in aggregate, undermine its scientific rigor and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Evaluation protocol and leakage concerns:
  - Candidates are ranked from a set including all bot utterances across training, validation, and both test sets (including OOV). This substantially simplifies the task and may inflate scores by allowing models to exploit knowledge of exact test strings during evaluation. It is unclear how this setup translates to deployment or to generation tasks.
  - The OOV setup gives systems access to the KB containing unseen entities at training time. This weakens the OOV claim: models can rely on exact KB matches rather than learning to generalize from dialog context. The combination of test-wide candidate sets and KB access blurs what “OOV generalization” actually measures.
- Simulator realism and determinism:
  - Only 43 user and 20 bot templates; the bot behavior (slot order) is deterministic. This invites template memorization and does not reflect real dialog variability, uncertainty, or error. Task 3’s acceptance behavior is stochastic but with fixed probabilities; other behaviors are scripted and predictable, limiting ecological validity.
  - Task 3 requires sorting by rating based on injected KB facts; while interesting, the construction (and pruning to ≥3 options) is biased and does not test robust reasoning beyond a very specific pattern.
- Baselines and ablations:
  - End-to-end baselines are limited. Stronger contemporary baselines for goal-oriented dialog (pointer/copy mechanisms; key–value memory networks; pointer networks for sorting; sequence-to-sequence with copy; reinforcement learning policies; transformers) are absent. Given that sorting and entity copy are central, these omissions are consequential.
  - Little analysis of what “match type” features contribute beyond exact string/KB matches. No ablations per type, no comparison to simpler copy/lexical matching, and no analysis of errors beyond a few examples.
- Claims about proxy validity:
  - The claim that synthetic tasks are a good proxy for real settings rests largely on similar relative rankings across datasets. There is no statistical correlation analysis, no calibration of task difficulty to real data, and DSTC2 was altered (ratings injected, new split), making comparability to existing literature impossible.
  - The concierge dataset is proprietary and unavailable, limiting reproducibility and independent verification. Its preprocessing (NER+regex) may distort user intents; no quantitative assessment of anonymization impact is provided.
- Reproducibility gaps:
  - It is unclear if the DSTC2 conversion scripts and all task generators are publicly released. Hyperparameters are pushed to appendices; training details (e.g., memory size, stopping criteria, variance across seeds) are sparse. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported.
- Paper polish and technical presentation:
  - Numerous reference formatting errors (e.g., “Bai et al\.,? 2009”, “Kim et al\.,? \Q2016\E”), missing numbers (e.g., “th Annual Meeting”), stray LaTeX artifacts, dangling footnote markers (“1”, “2”) far from their content, and occasional grammatical issues. These reduce credibility and hinder readability.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Tighten the evaluation: restrict candidate sets to training-only or task-specific candidates; report a “closed-set” vs “open-set” sensitivity analysis; add a generation setting with delexicalization and exact-match evaluation; report statistical significance and variance over multiple seeds.
- Strengthen baselines: include sequence-to-sequence with copy/pointer mechanisms; key–value memory networks; pointer networks (sorting); transformers; a learned policy for Task 3 acceptance. Compare to hybrid systems (state tracker + NLG) on DSTC2 in the same evaluation format.
- Clarify OOV splits: evaluate stricter OOV where the KB with unseen entities is not accessible during training; probe generalization to unseen attribute values beyond cuisine/location; consider compositional generalization tests.
- Improve simulator realism: diversify templates and bot policies; add ASR noise, paraphrases, and conflicting user inputs; relax determinism in slot order; vary API outputs beyond synthetic ratings.
- Provide rigorous proxy validation: quantify correlation between synthetic metrics and real-world metrics; align DSTC2 evaluation with established tasks where possible; release conversion scripts.
- Release all resources (task generators, DSTC2 conversion, trained models, concierge subset if permissible) and document preprocessing/anonymization impacts.
- Fix references, formatting, and footnotes; ensure tables/figures are interpretable without appendices.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to restricting candidate sets to training-only utterances? What happens if candidates are task-specific rather than pooled across all tasks and test sets?
2. Why allow access to both KBs during training for OOV evaluation? Can you report results where the unseen-entity KB is entirely held out until test time?
3. Will you release the DSTC2 conversion scripts and the exact concierge preprocessing pipeline? If not, how can others reproduce Tasks 6 and Concierge?
4. Did you try copy/pointer mechanisms or key–value memory networks? If so, how do they fare on Tasks 3 and 5?
5. What is the variance across random seeds, and are differences between methods statistically significant?
6. In Task 3, does performance depend largely on learning to sort or on exploiting patterns in candidate options? Can you isolate sorting with controlled experiments?
7. How many distinct templates are effectively used at training vs test per task, and what is the out-of-template generalization rate?
