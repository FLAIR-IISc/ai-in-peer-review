- Proposes an open, reproducible evaluation suite for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog in restaurant reservations, decomposed into five synthetic tasks plus two real-data sets.
- Benchmark emphasizes KB interaction, fine-grained error analysis, and an explicit OOV split to stress entity handling.
- Evaluates baselines (Memory Networks with/without match-type), rule-based, TF-IDF, nearest neighbor, and supervised embeddings.
- Key empirical finding: MemNNs can learn API call issuance/update and benefit from match-type features for OOV entities.
- MemNNs struggle with ranking KB results and completing robust multi-turn dialogs; per-dialog accuracy remains low even if per-response accuracy is reasonable.
- Synthetic task trends reflect relative performance on real data (DSTC2 conversion and concierge), supporting external validity.
- Decision: Accept (borderline) because the resource fills a gap, is reproducible, and surfaces shortcomings of current end-to-end models.
- Strong problem motivation and clear positioning relative to prior work (POMDP, slot-filling, chit-chat, DSTC, bAbI).
- Good methodological clarity and reproducibility: detailed simulator, OOV split, evaluation metrics, hyperparameters, and code/data availability indicated.
- Empirical support is convincing with thorough experiments and consistent trends across datasets.
- High potential impact as a public, decomposed, KB-grounded benchmark that has influenced subsequent work.
- Caveat: Synthetic templates and deterministic dialog ordering reduce linguistic variability and may bias models toward superficial heuristics.
- Caveat: Ranking among a global candidate set simplifies the task versus generation and may inflate results by including test-side responses.
- Caveat: Match-type features inject hand-engineered signals tied to KB typing, partially masking whether neural mechanisms could learn entity handling.
- Caveat: Real-data evaluation limitations—DSTC2 conversion changes comparability and the concierge dataset is proprietary and not released.
- Suggestion: Broaden baselines to include pointer/copy mechanisms, key-value memories, and encoder-decoder models with copying.
- Suggestion: Make the simulator more robust (randomized question order, paraphrases, noise, ambiguous/missing KB entries) and run sensitivity analyses.
- Suggestion: Add generative evaluation and task-success metrics (slot accuracy, API-argument exact match) alongside ranking.
- Suggestion: Clarify candidate construction and provide ablations restricting candidates to training-only responses.
- Suggestion: Release concierge data or de-identified subset, or provide scripts to simulate similar variability.
- Questions for authors: Why include test-side candidates and how sensitive are results to restricting to training-only candidates?
- Questions for authors: What KB access is assumed for match-type typing, and how do results change with incomplete/noisy KBs or with copy mechanisms?
- Questions for authors: How do models handle randomized slot-question ordering or conflicting user updates?
- Questions for authors: Why do MemNNs fail at sorting KB results—attention capacity, differentiable sorting need, or multi-fact reading—and any ablations by number of options?
- Questions for authors: Did DSTC2 rating-based ordering introduce artifacts, and can results be compared to original DSTC metrics?
- Questions for authors: Can models generalize to unseen slot types, different KB schemas, or other domains (preliminary cross-domain results)?
- Questions for authors: Will the simulator, DSTC2 conversion scripts, and MemNN training code be released for full reproducibility?
