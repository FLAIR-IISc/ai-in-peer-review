- Introduces a lightweight, reproducible testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented restaurant dialogue with five synthetic tasks (T1–T5) plus DSTC2 conversion (T6) and a concierge dataset.
- Tasks decompose the goal into subskills: implicit state tracking (T1–T2), KB output use and sorting (T3), grounding in KB facts (T4), and full dialogues (T5).
- Benchmarks include rule-based systems, IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks (MemN2N) with match-type features for entities/OOVs.
- Evaluation uses per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics.
- Main findings: MemN2N outperforms IR and supervised embeddings; match-type features are critical for entity-heavy/OOV scenarios and T4.
- Main findings: Even best models struggle on KB interpretation and option ranking (T3/T5).
- Main findings: Performance patterns on synthetic tasks generalize to DSTC2 and concierge data, supporting external validity.
- Promises to release T1–T6 data and clear generation rules to promote comparability and error analysis.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason for acceptance: Fills a gap by providing a principled, reproducible benchmark for goal-oriented dialogue with KB and OOV evaluation, likely high community impact.
- Key reason for acceptance: Careful empirical study with multiple baselines, ablations (match-types, hops), per-dialog metrics, and cross-validation on real datasets.
- Problem positioning: Well-motivated contrast to brittle slot-filling POMDP and chit-chat-focused end-to-end work; complements DSTC and bAbI QA limitations.
- Methodological rigor: Tasks have controllable difficulty and OOV splits; per-dialog metric and match-type features are well-motivated; useful qualitative/attention analyses provided.
- Reproducibility and impact: Open data, modular tasks, and clear generation promote focused model improvement.
- Suggestion: Increase language variability—expand templates, paraphrases, and introduce nondeterminism to avoid overfitting to simulator artifacts.
- Suggestion: Clarify evaluation implications of ranking over a fixed candidate set and consider reporting generative or multi-valid-response success rates; analyze sensitivity to candidate set size/composition.
- Suggestion: Justify candidate pool construction (includes all bot utterances across splits) and provide an alternative restricted candidate setting.
- Suggestion: Clarify OOV/KB access design—report results when OOV KB is withheld during training to test true OOV handling versus typing via KB lookup.
- Suggestion: Add stronger baselines such as pointer/copy models, retrieval-augmented architectures, RNN/Transformer baselines, neural DST belief-tracking, or RL policies for option presentation.
- Suggestion: Provide code/scripts and analyze whether DSTC2 rating-based conversion introduces artifacts or biases.
- Suggestion: Release a subset or provide detailed schema/statistics for the proprietary concierge dataset to aid reproducibility.
- Question: Does including test utterances in the candidate set lead to optimistic estimates—can results be reported with candidates restricted to train/val only?
- Question: What changes when the OOV KB is not available during training—how much OOV gains come from type features vs. KB availability?
- Question: Do tasks admit multiple valid system utterances and how is label ambiguity handled in ranking?
- Question: Can you provide a finer-grained error analysis of T3/T5 (sorting vs. KB slot misunderstanding vs. dialogue control)?
- Question: Have you scaled cuisines/locations or increased linguistic diversity to test robustness and how do trends change?
- Question: How often does the injected rating-based DSTC2 policy disagree with the original system, and does this cause errors?
- Question: Beyond correlation, can you show causal evidence that additional hops enable correct multi-step reasoning (e.g., by ablating memories or perturbing KB facts)?
- Overall verdict: Timely, well-motivated benchmark with solid empirical analysis and impactful potential despite limitations (synthetic language, ranking setup).
