Summary and contributions
The paper introduces a lightweight, reproducible testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue in the restaurant reservation domain, with five synthetic tasks (T1–T5) built around a KB and API calls and two “real” settings: a conversion of DSTC2 (T6) and a proprietary concierge dataset. The tasks decompose the overall goal into subskills: implicit state tracking (T1–T2), using KB outputs and sorting (T3), grounding answers in KB facts (T4), and full dialogues (T5). The authors benchmark rule-based systems, IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks (MemN2N), including an important augmentation with “match-type” features to handle entities and OOVs. They evaluate with per-response and per-dialog accuracy and show: (i) MemNNs substantially outperform IR and supervised embeddings, (ii) typed matching is critical for entity-heavy/OOV scenarios and for T4, (iii) even the best models struggle on KB interpretation and option ranking (T3/T5), and (iv) the relative performance pattern on synthetic tasks carries over to DSTC2 and the concierge data, supporting the testbed’s external validity. The dataset (T1–T6) is promised to be open, aiming to catalyze comparable, interpretable progress on goal-oriented dialogue.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The paper fills a clear gap by providing a principled, reproducible benchmark for end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue with a KB, including OOV evaluation—likely to have high community impact. (2) The empirical study is careful and insightful: multiple baselines, ablations (match-type features, hops), per-dialog metrics, and cross-validation on real datasets convincingly support the claims about current model strengths/weaknesses.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: The motivation is strong—slot-filling POMDP systems are effective but brittle; end-to-end systems have mostly been tested in chit-chat with problematic evaluation. Prior testbeds (e.g., bAbI QA) do not measure multi-turn goal-directed behavior requiring questions, API calls, or KB reasoning. This work is well-placed in the literature and complementary to DSTC; the conversion of DSTC2 to the same format is valuable.
- Methodological rigor: The tasks are clearly specified, with controllable difficulty and OOV splits. The per-dialog metric appropriately captures task success beyond local accuracy. The use of match-type features is a well-motivated remedy for entity/OOV issues in embedding models. The comparisons cover rule-based, IR, and learned baselines, and ablations (hops; with/without types) are informative. Qualitative analyses and attention visualizations (appendix) aid interpretability.
- Findings: Results coherently support claims: MemNNs learn to issue/update API calls (T1–T2) and, with type features, can ground answers in KB (T4) and handle OOV entities. They remain weak on interpreting and ranking KB results (T3) and on end-to-end full dialogues (T5), especially in per-dialog accuracy. The consistent performance pattern on DSTC2/concierge bolsters the testbed’s proxy value.
- Reproducibility and impact: Open data for T1–T6 (with clear generation rules) promotes comparability and error analysis by design. The tasks’ modularity targets specific capabilities and should help drive focused model improvements.

Suggestions to improve (non-blocking)
- Language variability: The simulator’s limited surface forms and deterministic policies may overfit models to artifacts. Consider expanding templates, paraphrasing, and introducing controlled nondeterminism in system behavior.
- Evaluation protocol: Ranking over a fixed candidate set is practical but differs from generation; clarify implications and consider reporting generation results (or success rate with multiple valid responses) where possible. Analyze sensitivity to candidate set size and composition.
- Candidate set construction: The candidate pool includes all bot utterances across train/val/test and tasks. This can inflate performance and is unusual; justify this choice and provide an alternative setting where candidates are restricted to train/val.
- OOV split and KB access: Since models can access the full KB (including OOV entities) at train time, the OOV challenge is primarily “unseen in dialogs.” Clarify this design choice and report results when the OOV KB is withheld during training to stress true OOV handling.
- Baselines: Consider adding pointer/copy models and retrieval-augmented architectures that explicitly handle entities, and stronger RNN/Transformer baselines with attention. A belief-tracking baseline (neural DST) or simple RL policy for option presentation could be informative.
- DSTC2 conversion details: You add ratings to enable deterministic option ranking. Provide code/scripts and assess whether this introduces artifacts or biases compared to the original behavior.
- Concierge dataset: While proprietary, even a small released subset or detailed schema/statistics would help the community reproduce analyses.

Questions for the authors
1) Candidate set: Does including test utterances in the candidate set lead to optimistic estimates? Can you report results with candidates restricted to train/val only?
2) OOV/KB access: What changes when the OOV KB is not made available during training (i.e., models cannot type unseen entities via KB lookup)? How much of the OOV gains come from type features vs. KB availability?
3) Multiple valid responses: Do tasks admit multiple correct system utterances at a turn (e.g., varied phrasings)? If so, how do you handle label ambiguity in ranking?
4) Sorting and T3 errors: Can you provide a finer-grained error analysis of T3/T5 (e.g., failures due to sorting vs. misunderstanding KB slots vs. dialogue control)?
5) Generalization: Have you tried scaling the number of cuisines/locations or increasing linguistic diversity to test robustness? How do performance trends change?
6) DSTC2 conversion: How often does the injected rating-based policy disagree with the original system’s behavior, and does that account for some errors?
7) Hops and attention: Beyond correlation, can you show causal evidence that additional hops enable correct multi-step reasoning (e.g., ablate specific memories or perturb KB facts)?

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated benchmark paper with solid empirical analysis. Despite limitations (synthetic language, ranking setup), the contribution is impactful and should catalyze progress on end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue.
