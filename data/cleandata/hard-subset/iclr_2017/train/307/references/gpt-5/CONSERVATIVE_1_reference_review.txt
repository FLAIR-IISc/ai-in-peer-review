Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an open testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems focused on restaurant reservations. It decomposes the overall problem into five synthetic, controlled tasks (API issuing, API updating, displaying options with sorting, providing KB attributes, and full dialogs), plus a conversion of DSTC2 into this format and an additional evaluation on a proprietary human-human “Concierge” dataset. The authors benchmark a suite of baselines: rule-based systems, classical IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with and without “match type” features to handle entities). They report that MemNNs outperform other baselines and can handle some non-trivial behaviors (issuing API calls, some OOV handling with type features), but still struggle to achieve high per-dialog success, especially on tasks requiring interpreting KB results and ranking options.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The synthetic tasks and evaluation protocol are too simplified and deterministic to convincingly serve as a proxy for real goal-oriented dialog, and the empirical evidence connecting performance on these tasks to real-world outcomes is limited.
2) Comparisons to established baselines are incomplete, particularly with respect to strong slot-filling/POMDP approaches and modern neural architectures that address entities (e.g., copy/pointer mechanisms). The altered evaluation on DSTC2 prevents fair comparison to known baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Ecological validity of the synthetic tasks: The simulator relies on a small set of fixed templates (43 user patterns, 20 bot patterns), deterministic dialog flows (e.g., fixed slot ordering in T1), and ranking over a global candidate set. These choices reduce linguistic and policy variability and make the tasks highly predictable (indeed a rule-based system trivially achieves 100%). While control and interpretability are valuable, the resulting setting may not stress the capabilities required for robust real-world dialog.
- Evaluation protocol concerns:
  - Ranking setting with a candidate set comprising all utterances across train/valid/test (including test-only forms) simplifies the problem relative to generation and risks overestimating performance. This is especially problematic for claims about generalization and OOV handling.
  - Per-response accuracy is not a reliable proxy for task success; while the paper reports per-dialog metrics as well, the link to real user outcomes is not established.
  - On DSTC2, the authors modify the task and cannot compare to challenge results; the rule-based baseline for T6 is relatively weak and hand-tuned. Thus, conclusions about competitiveness with traditional methods are not well supported.
- OOV split and use of KBs: Models are allowed access to both KBs (including the OOV KB) during training. This grants the entity lexicon (types and strings) up front, making OOV generalization easier than in realistic deployment scenarios where new entities arrive at test time without prior KB exposure. It also conflates lexical exposure with true compositional generalization.
- Missing strong baselines:
  - Established goal-oriented dialog systems (e.g., DST state trackers, POMDP-based managers) or more modern neural slot-filling pipelines with trained NLU/NLG are not compared in their native evaluation regimes.
  - Neural architectures designed to handle entities (copy/pointer networks, pointer-generator, key-value memory networks, retrieval+copy hybrids) are absent; the “match type” features are a simplistic approximation of copying and do not establish competitiveness.
  - No comparisons to sequence-to-sequence models with attention and copy for the generation setting, even though the paper frames the contribution as broadly applicable to end-to-end dialog.
- Limited evidence for “proxy for real data” claim: While the paper shows a similar ranking of methods across synthetic tasks, DSTC2, and Concierge, this is qualitative and based on narrow metrics. The Concierge dataset is proprietary and not released, limiting reproducibility and undermining the claim that the benchmark fosters standardized comparison on realistic data.
- Analysis depth: The paper lacks statistical significance testing, sensitivity to training set size, and deeper error analyses (beyond example snippets) to substantiate claims about what capabilities are learned (e.g., sorting vs. memorized patterns). The match-type feature ablations are limited; more rigorous diagnostics would strengthen the conclusions.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines and evaluation:
  - Include comparisons to strong slot-filling/POMDP systems and published DSTC2 baselines within their established evaluation metrics, or provide a faithful reproduction to enable apples-to-apples comparisons.
  - Add modern neural baselines with explicit copy/pointer mechanisms and knowledge-grounded architectures (e.g., pointer networks, key-value memories, copy-augmented RNNs).
  - Provide significance testing and learning curves to assess robustness and data efficiency.
- Improve task realism and evaluation protocol:
  - Increase linguistic and policy variability (more templates, non-deterministic slot orders, paraphrases, disfluencies, ASR-like noise).
  - Avoid including test-only utterances in the candidate set; consider generation-based evaluation alongside retrieval to avoid leakage and more closely match deployment.
  - Revisit the OOV setup so that models do not see the OOV KB during training; evaluate true zero-shot entity generalization.
- Strengthen the bridge to real data:
  - Release the Concierge dataset, or otherwise substantiate the “proxy” claim with quantitative correlations between synthetic-task performance and real-task success (task success rate, user satisfaction, dialog length).
  - On DSTC2, keep the original evaluation wherever possible and report comparative results to SOTA systems.
- Clarify and expand analyses:
  - Provide detailed error taxonomies per task, calibration analyses, and attention/hop interpretability tied to concrete capabilities (e.g., sorting, entity resolution).
  - Explore robustness to template perturbations and larger candidate sets.

Questions for the authors
1) Do candidate responses include utterances that appear only in the test sets? If so, how do you mitigate potential leakage and over-optimism in the ranking setting?
2) Why not compare to established DSTC2 baselines within the original evaluation framework? Can you provide such comparisons or justify the deviation?
3) Is the Concierge dataset (or a suitably anonymized subset) planned for release? Without it, how should the community validate the proxy claim?
4) In the OOV setup, models have access to the OOV KB during training. How would performance change if the OOV entities/KB were unseen until test time?
5) Have you evaluated architectures with explicit copy/pointer mechanisms? If not, can you justify the choice of MemNNs as the principal end-to-end baseline?
6) How sensitive are the conclusions to increased linguistic variability (more templates, paraphrases) and to removing determinism in dialog policy (e.g., slot order)?
7) Do you observe true learned sorting behavior in T3 (e.g., generalizing to unseen rating distributions), or could results be explained by template-specific cues?
