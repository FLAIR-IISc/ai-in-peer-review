1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes an open, lightweight, and reproducible benchmark suite to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems in a controlled way. Inspired by bAbI QA tasks, it decomposes restaurant reservation dialogs into five synthetic subtasks (API issuance, API updates, option display with sorting, retrieval of attributes, and a full dialog) plus a conversion of DSTC2 into the same format, and reports results on an additional real human-human Concierge dataset. The benchmark explicitly tests skills like implicit state tracking, KB querying, sorting, handling OOV entities, and continuation after KB results. The authors provide comprehensive baselines (TF-IDF, nearest neighbor, supervised embeddings) and a strong end-to-end Memory Network (with and without match-type features). They show that while per-response accuracy is encouraging, per-dialog success remains low on nontrivial tasks, and that relative method rankings transfer from synthetic to real data, arguing the synthetic tasks are useful proxies. The dataset and code are made available to facilitate comparisons and error analysis.

2) Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (i) The benchmark is timely, well-motivated, and fills a clear gap for reproducible, capability-oriented evaluation of goal-oriented dialog; (ii) The study is thorough, connects synthetic and real settings, and provides solid baselines establishing what current end-to-end models can and cannot do.

3) Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses the lack of reproducible, interpretable, end-to-end evaluation for goal-oriented dialogs beyond chit-chat, focusing on capabilities needed to complete tasks: state tracking, KB interaction, sorting/options, and handling OOV entities.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It situates the work between slot-filling/POMDP systems and recent neural end-to-end dialog models, noting limitations of existing corpora (noise, state annotations not suited for end-to-end, lack of reproducibility). Positioning relative to bAbI QA tasks, DSTC, and non-goal datasets is clear.
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The experiments are carefully designed, hyperparameter selection is described, and multiple baselines are compared. The consistency of relative rankings across synthetic tasks, DSTC2-converted, and Concierge supports the claim that the tasks can proxy real performance. The OOV analyses and match-type feature ablations directly test the intended capability. The per-dialog results honestly demonstrate remaining gaps.
- Significance and impact: A standardized, decomposed benchmark that can elicit failure modes (e.g., interpreting KB results, sorting) is highly valuable to the community, likely to catalyze method development (copy/pointing, key-value memories, neural belief trackers).
- Clarity and reproducibility: The tasks are well specified, data advertised as open, and methods are described with sufficient detail. The ranking evaluation setting is consistent and pragmatic for controlled comparison.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect my decision)
- Scope and realism:
  - The synthetic tasks rely on deterministic templates (fixed bot wording, deterministic slot query order, last-option acceptance). Consider adding variability/noise (synonyms, paraphrases, different slot orders, optional-slot handling), and occasional inconsistencies to better mirror real users and systems.
  - Some KB assumptions (single party size availability) are artificial; broadening constraints (opening hours, availability conflicts, multiple attributes) would raise ecological validity.
  - The conversion of DSTC2 adds ratings and imposes deterministic choice rules; please discuss any biases introduced and provide a version with multiple acceptable outputs when ambiguity exists.

- Evaluation protocol:
  - Ranking over a fixed candidate set is convenient but can overestimate performance relative to generation and policy learning. Consider reporting generative results (e.g., seq2seq with copy/pointer, key-value MemNN, or retrieval-augmented generation) and/or success-rate under user simulation or human evaluation.
  - Per-dialog accuracy is very strict; adding task success metrics (booked with correct slots), time-to-success, and error decomposition would provide a more nuanced picture.

- Baselines and modeling:
  - Include stronger contemporary baselines: pointer-generator/copy networks, key-value memory networks, neural belief tracking with copy, and retrieval+generation hybrids. Reinforcement learning (policy optimization) under the simulator would be illuminating for Task 5.
  - The “match type” feature is an important engineering addition. Please analyze its scalability when entity schemas grow (more types, nested attributes), and compare against neural copy/slot-value linking approaches that don’t rely on manual type tokens.
  - Clarify whether models can exploit exact string matches without access to KB typing (e.g., via character/subword embeddings). A character-level baseline could be informative for OOV handling.

- Data and splits:
  - Provide statistics on pattern coverage and paraphrase diversity, and release scripts to generate additional linguistic variants to avoid overfitting to fixed templates.
  - The OOV split grants access to the OOV KB at training for feature typing; discuss a variant where the entity lexicon is only available at test time to better simulate deployment.
  - Consider multi-domain extensions (e.g., hotels, flights) and compositional generalization splits (unseen slot combinations and values simultaneously).

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set construction: Are candidate responses pooled across all tasks and splits? Could this leak task-specific patterns? Would per-task candidate sets change conclusions?
- OOV setup: Do models have access to exact KB entity lexicons during training for the OOV domain (for match typing)? How would performance change if the lexicon were only available at test, or partially missing?
- Sorting in Task 3: How is tie-breaking by rating handled, and how is supervision provided when multiple orderings are plausible? Did you explore stochastic policies for presenting options?
- Joint vs per-task training: Did you try multi-task training across Tasks 1–5 (or curriculum learning)? Does joint training improve Task 5 success?
- Variance and sample efficiency: With only 1k training dialogs per task, what is the variance across runs/seeds? How does performance scale with data size?
- DSTC2 conversion: Since you injected ratings and synthesized API outputs, can you share the conversion scripts and any quality checks to ensure fidelity to the original dialogs?

Overall, this is a valuable and well-executed benchmark contribution with careful analysis and clear avenues for future research.
