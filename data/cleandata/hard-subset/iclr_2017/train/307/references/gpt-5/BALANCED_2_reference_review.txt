1) Summary of contributions
The paper introduces an open, lightweight, and reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems grounded in a restaurant-booking domain. The benchmark decomposes the task into five simulated subtasks that probe core capabilities (implicit state tracking, issuing/updating API calls, using KB results including sorting, and providing entity attributes), plus two evaluations on real dialog data: a reformatting of DSTC2 into the same task structure and a more challenging human-human concierge dataset. The authors systematically compare several baselines—rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with/without match-type features)—under per-response and per-dialog accuracy. Key empirical findings include: (i) end-to-end MemNNs outperform IR and supervised embeddings, (ii) match-type features substantially improve OOV handling and KB fact usage, and (iii) despite promising per-response scores, per-dialog success remains low on the more complex tasks, highlighting gaps in KB interpretation and option presentation. The paper’s strengths are the clear task factorization, explicit OOV evaluation, careful baselines, and an effort to connect synthetic performance to real data via relative ranking consistency.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The evaluation setting is overly synthetic and constrained (template generation, deterministic policies, candidate-ranking with access to all test candidates), making it hard to conclude about effectiveness on real-world goal-oriented dialogue.
- Methodological novelty is limited; beyond a known MemNN variant with type features, the paper mainly reports a dataset and baseline comparisons without stronger evidence that success on the proposed tasks transfers to realistic deployments.

3) Supporting arguments
On the three guiding questions:
- Problem clarity: Strong. The paper targets an important gap—reproducible evaluation of end-to-end, KB-grounded, goal-oriented dialogue. The task breakdown is well thought out and emphasizes crucial capabilities (state tracking, KB use, OOV entities).
- Motivation and placement: Generally solid. The work is positioned against slot-filling/POMDP pipelines, existing non-goal-oriented datasets, and bAbI QA tasks. The contribution of OOV splits and KB grounding is well argued.
- Support for claims: Mixed. The experiments convincingly show relative performance trends across models and that type features help OOV handling. However:
  • The synthetic data relies on fixed templates and deterministic bot behavior, and the small training sets limit realism.  
  • The candidate-ranking setup uses a global candidate pool that includes all test utterances and API strings, which may inflate results versus generative settings and blur OOV difficulty (especially since both KBs are accessible during training for matching).  
  • The validation of “proxy value” is suggestive but limited: while relative rankings align across DSTC2 and Concierge, absolute per-dialog scores remain low, and the real-data coverage is comparatively narrow.  
  • Baselines omit strong contemporary alternatives specifically suited to KB-grounded dialogue, such as sequence models with copy/pointer mechanisms or neural retrieval augmented with exact-match/canonicalization; thus the case that MemNNs are a strong end-to-end baseline is incomplete.  

Further concerns:
- Task design choices (e.g., deterministic question order, ranking by ratings, templated language) reduce ecological validity and risk overfitting to benchmark idiosyncrasies.  
- The OOV protocol allows access to the OOV KB at training (for typing/matching), which weakens the generalization test and may create leakage via candidates.  
- The switch from simulated to real dialogs involves modifying DSTC2 (adding ratings) and a proprietary Concierge dataset lacking released KB outputs, complicating reproducibility and interpretability of the “real-world” validation.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen real-world validation:  
  • Include more diverse, real multi-domain datasets and/or user studies measuring task success and user satisfaction.  
  • Report results in a generative setting (with BLEU/slot-F1 and end-task success), not only ranking.  
  • Evaluate with and without access to test-time candidate sets and report sensitivity to candidate set size.
- Tighten the OOV protocol:  
  • Ensure no access to OOV KB entries during training; separate “lexical” OOV (surface forms) from “schema” OOV (new slot types) and report both.  
  • Clarify whether candidate strings contain OOV entities and how that affects scoring.
- Expand model comparisons and ablations:  
  • Add strong baselines: pointer-generator/copy networks, retrieval-augmented transformers, span-based KB grounding, and structured policy networks.  
  • Ablate match-type features (exact match, type-only, KB-only, no-KB) and analyze failure modes (e.g., sorting errors vs. entity grounding vs. state tracking).  
  • Report hop-depth sensitivity and attention visualizations aligned with specific reasoning steps (especially for sorting in Task 3).
- Improve task realism:  
  • Introduce non-deterministic policies, paraphrastic variability beyond templates, and noise (ASR-like, typos).  
  • Include richer KB operations (multi-constraint filtering, soft preferences, tie-breaking, availability).  
  • Consider multi-turn revision, confirmations, and error recovery beyond fixed update counts.
- Reproducibility details:  
  • Release data generators, candidate lists, KBs, and all conversion scripts for DSTC2 and Concierge.  
  • Provide compute costs, negative sampling details, and candidate pool statistics for each task.  
  • Report confidence intervals and significance on key metrics.

Questions for the authors
- OOV protocol: Are models allowed to see the OOV KB during training to build type-match features? Do candidate responses include surface forms from the OOV KB, and if so, how do you control for leakage in ranking?  
- Candidate ranking: How sensitive are results to the size/composition of the candidate pool? Can you report performance when the candidate pool excludes test-only strings, or under a purely generative decoding regime?  
- Sorting and KB reasoning: What concrete evidence shows that the model learns sorting (Task 3) rather than exploiting dataset artifacts? Can you provide targeted tests and attention analyses?  
- Real-data conversion: For DSTC2, how do the added ratings interact with the original dialog flows? Did you validate that the induced “highest rating” policy mirrors the original system’s choices?  
- Error analysis: Where do most per-dialog failures occur (e.g., state tracking vs. KB lookup vs. presenting options)? Can you provide a taxonomy of error types and their frequencies?  
- Generality: How would the approach scale to multi-domain, multi-API scenarios with schema variation? Can the dataset/generator be extended in that direction?

Overall, the paper makes a useful step toward standardized evaluation of goal-oriented, KB-grounded dialogue, but the current setup is too constrained to support strong conclusions about real-world readiness, and the methodological advances are modest. Strengthening the realism, baselines, and OOV protocol would substantially increase impact.
