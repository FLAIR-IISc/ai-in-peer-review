1) Summary of contributions
The paper introduces an open, lightweight, and reproducible testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog in the restaurant reservation domain. It decomposes the overall task into five synthetic subtasks that probe key capabilities: implicit dialog state tracking (Tasks 1–2), KB querying and interpretation including sorting (Tasks 3–4), and full dialogs (Task 5). It further adds a converted DSTC2 dataset (Task 6) and reports on an additional, more realistic Concierge dataset. The evaluation uses both per-response and per-dialog accuracies and includes in-vocabulary and OOV settings by splitting KB entities to test generalization to unseen restaurants, cuisines, and locations. The paper benchmarks rule-based, classical IR, supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks with and without “match type” features for entity handling. Empirically, MemN2N performs best among learning methods, match-type features help with OOV and entity-centric responses, but per-dialog success remains low in the more complex tasks, highlighting current limitations. The dataset and task design aim to facilitate reproducibility, error analysis, and progress on goal-oriented dialog.

2) Decision
Accept (weak accept), primarily because:
- The paper provides a timely, well-motivated, and openly available benchmark that fills a clear gap for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog with controlled KB interaction and OOV evaluation.
- The experimental analysis is careful, establishes informative baselines, and offers actionable insights into the strengths and failure modes of contemporary end-to-end models.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: The work targets an important question—how to systematically evaluate and compare end-to-end dialog systems on goal-oriented tasks that require state tracking, KB integration, and entity handling. Existing resources either focus on chit-chat, are not end-to-end, or lack reproducibility/coverage.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well contextualized relative to slot-filling/POMDP systems and end-to-end chit-chat efforts. It builds on bAbI-style synthetic tasks but extends to true dialog (including asking questions, issuing API calls) and links to real data (DSTC2, Concierge). The emphasis on OOV entities and KB interaction is overdue in this space.
- Do the results support the claims? Yes. Experiments cover multiple baselines and settings, with clear, interpretable metrics. The finding that MemN2N benefits from multiple hops and match-type features, yet struggles on sorting/option presentation (Task 3) and full dialogs (Task 5), is consistent with the goals and provides a realistic assessment of current limitations. The alignment between trends on synthetic and real data strengthens the claim that the tasks are a useful proxy. While statistical significance testing is not reported, the result gaps are large and consistent across tasks.

4) Suggestions to improve (helpful; not all part of my accept decision)
- Expand baselines: Include sequence-to-sequence models with attention and explicit copy/pointer mechanisms, key-value memory networks, and retrieval-augmented transformers to better probe entity/OOV handling, sorting, and KB grounding. These comparisons would make the benchmark more directly useful.
- Generation vs ranking: The current setting ranks from a candidate set. Reporting parallel results for generative models (e.g., constrained decoding) would strengthen ecological validity and broaden applicability.
- Error analysis: Provide a finer-grained breakdown of failure modes (e.g., sorting errors vs acceptance-handling vs mis-grounding) and per-turn confusion analysis to guide model development.
- Less determinism: Consider variants with non-deterministic bot strategies (ordering of questions, paraphrastic diversity), noisy user language, and multi-criterion selection (e.g., tie-breaking by distance) to better mirror real dialogs and reduce template biases.
- Robustness: Add controlled noise (ASR-like errors, paraphrases, typos) to the synthetic tasks to more closely match DSTC2/Concierge conditions and assess model brittleness.
- OOV protocol: Clarify and potentially harden OOV splits (e.g., hold out entity surface forms completely, limit KB access at training time, or test cross-domain transfer) to better measure generalization.
- Release details: Ensure that the simulator/templates and the DSTC2 conversion scripts are released; if the Concierge data cannot be released, provide a pseudo-realistic public variant (e.g., crowdsourced paraphrases over the same KB) so improvements are measurable end-to-end.
- Statistical reporting: Add confidence intervals and/or significance tests, especially where differences are modest (e.g., with vs without match types), and include learning curves to show data requirements.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate ranking: How large is the candidate set per task in practice, and how sensitive are results to its size/composition? Could you provide ablations on candidate set scaling?
- OOV setup: Since models can access both KBs during training, OOV is only at the dialog level. How would performance change if the KB containing test entities were hidden during training (i.e., no prior exposure for type-matching), or if surface-form collisions are introduced?
- Sorting (Task 3): Do models fail mainly at ordering by rating or at tracking acceptance/next-option logic? Could you share detailed error breakdowns or attention patterns?
- Match-type features: Are these features derived solely from KB gazetteers? How do they handle ambiguity (e.g., words that can be both common tokens and entity names)? Any failure cases where type-features mislead?
- DSTC2 conversion: To what extent do artifacts (e.g., adding ratings for determinism) bias the task? Can you quantify how much noise/determinism remains post-conversion, and whether that affects per-dialog accuracy?
- Generalization: Have you tried models with copy/pointer mechanisms or key-value memories? If so, how do they compare, especially on OOV and Task 3/5?
- Beyond restaurants: How easily can the simulator/KB be reparameterized to other domains, and do you plan to release additional domains to test cross-domain generalization?

Overall, this paper provides a valuable benchmark and a well-executed initial analysis. With broadened baselines, richer error analysis, and some robustness extensions, it could become a standard resource for goal-oriented dialog research.
