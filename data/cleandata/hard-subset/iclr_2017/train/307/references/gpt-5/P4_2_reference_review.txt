1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes an open, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. It decomposes restaurant-reservation dialogs into five synthetic tasks probing: (1) dialog state tracking for issuing API calls, (2) updating API calls, (3) ranking and presenting KB options, (4) extracting KB attributes, and (5) conducting full dialogs. It further adds two real-world datasets: a conversion of DSTC2 to the same task format and a proprietary concierge dataset, to assess transfer from synthetic to realistic settings. The authors benchmark several methods (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks with/without match-type features) across in-vocabulary and OOV splits, with per-response and per-dialog metrics. They report that MemN2N models can learn nontrivial capabilities (implicit state tracking, issuing calls, limited OOV handling with type features), but struggle with options presentation and full dialogs; patterns observed on synthetic tasks largely carry over to real data. The main contribution is the dataset and protocol enabling fine-grained, reproducible evaluation of goal-directed dialog, plus an analysis clarifying strengths and limitations of current end-to-end approaches.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper provides a well-motivated, carefully designed, and much-needed benchmark for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog with KB interaction and OOV evaluation, filling a recognized gap in the literature.
- The empirical study is systematic and rigorous, demonstrating clear, interpretable strengths and weaknesses of multiple baselines and an end-to-end model, with results that plausibly transfer to real settings.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: The work addresses the lack of standardized, reproducible evaluation for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog, especially involving KB interaction, sorting, and OOV entities. This is distinct from chit-chat datasets and from state-tracking-only benchmarks.
- Motivation and placement: The design is well-situated relative to bAbI (QA), POMDP/slot-filling systems, and DSTC datasets. Prior resources either lack dialog dynamics with KB use, are not open/reproducible, or focus on state tracking rather than end-to-end behavior. The synthetic tasks are intentionally controllable for diagnostics, and the addition of DSTC2/Concierge helps bridge to real-world noise and variability.
- Claims supported by evidence:
  - Task definitions and data generation are clearly specified, including KB schema, OOV splits by entity types, and evaluation metrics. Access to both KBs during training for typing OOV entities is explicitly stated.
  - Baselines span non-learning IR to supervised embeddings to MemN2N, with and without type features. Hyperparameters and ablations (number of hops, match types) are documented. Both per-response and per-dialog accuracies are reported, appropriately highlighting goal completion.
  - Findings are consistent and interpretable: MemN2N solves T1–T2, type features enable T4 and OOV handling, but T3 (sorting/option presentation) and T5 remain hard. Similar relative performance trends hold on DSTC2 and Concierge, supporting the claim that the synthetic suite is a useful proxy.
- Technical soundness:
  - Experimental protocols are coherent; the candidate-ranking formulation avoids generation confounds and allows controlled comparison. OOV splits are principled. The match-type feature mechanism is clearly described and addresses entity exact-match and OOV issues.
  - Limitations are acknowledged: fully hand-coded systems can solve synthetic tasks; per-dialog performance remains low; real-world bots introduce noise and non-determinism.
- Novelty: The primary novelty lies in the benchmark and evaluation framework (task design, OOV split, KB-grounded, sorting), plus a simple but effective augmentation (match-type features) for MemN2N. While the modeling is incremental, the resource and analysis constitute a valuable contribution.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not strictly part of the accept decision)
- Clarify the implications of using a candidate set that includes responses drawn from train/valid/test across tasks. Discuss potential leakage concerns and how this setup maps to real deployments with larger or open-ended candidate spaces.
- Provide learning curves versus training set size on key tasks to quantify data efficiency and to guide future work on scaling.
- Deepen diagnostics for Task 3 to disentangle failures due to sorting, long-context reasoning, and handling multiple KB returns (e.g., add controlled variants that remove sorting or vary list length).
- For DSTC2 conversion, detail the impact of adding synthetic ratings and enforcing highest-rating recommendation; report a variant that follows the original bot’s (possibly suboptimal) behavior to bound the effect of this intervention.
- Discuss portability of match-type features: what is required to build the entity lexicon in a new domain, and how does performance degrade with incomplete KB coverage?
- Consider reporting a few generation-based results (even if weak) to contextualize the ranking setup, or justify more explicitly why ranking is the preferred initial step for this benchmark.
- Release code for data generation and evaluation scripts (if not already), and specify exact versions/splits to maximize reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set: Does including test-set utterances in the candidate pool risk inflating per-response accuracy? How does performance change when the candidate set is restricted to training utterances only, or expanded substantially?
- OOV setup: During training, models can access both KBs for typing. What happens if the OOV KB is only available at test time (i.e., no exposure to its entity surface forms during training for typing)? Can the model still exploit type features reliably?
- Task 3 analysis: Can you provide ablations that (i) remove sorting, (ii) reduce the number of returned options, or (iii) add explicit supervision for the ordering, to pinpoint the bottleneck?
- Data efficiency: How sensitive are results to the 1k training examples per task? Are there regimes where supervised embeddings outperform MemN2N (e.g., very low data)?
- DSTC2 conversion: Adding ratings imposes a deterministic policy for option selection. Could you report results without this addition or provide statistics on how often the original bot followed that policy?
- Realistic noise: Have you tested robustness to ASR-like noise or paraphrasing beyond the defined templates, and do trends hold?
- Generalization: Beyond cuisine/location/restaurant OOVs, have you considered OOV slot types (new entity categories) and how match-type features would extend to that setting?
