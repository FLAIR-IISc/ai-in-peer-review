- Problem & goal: Assess whether end-to-end models can replace slot-filling systems in narrow, KB-grounded goal-oriented dialog and identify where they fail.
- Main contribution: A lightweight, reproducible benchmark suite for restaurant booking with five synthetic subtasks plus two realistic datasets (DSTC2 conversion and Concierge).
- Evaluation design: Per-response and per-dialog accuracy, an OOV split forcing unseen-entity generalization, unified candidate ranking, and task-decomposed error analysis.
- Modeling & baselines: IR and supervised embedding baselines, MemN2N with multi-hop attention, a “match type” feature for OOV handling, and a rule-based DSTC2 system.
- Empirical findings: MemN2N with match types outperforms simpler baselines, can handle dialog state and some OOVs, but struggles with KB interpretation, sorting, and multi-step behaviors, yielding low per-dialog accuracy; trends hold on real data.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: The benchmark and OOV design fill an important, reproducible gap for evaluating KB-grounded goal-oriented dialog.
- Key reason 2: Careful empirical analysis identifies concrete strengths and failure modes, and match types offer a simple, effective OOV solution validated on real data.
- Significance & timeliness: Provides a standardized, decomposed benchmark addressing noisy and non-comparable prior evaluations; already influential in practice.
- Motivation & placement: Well-motivated within POMDP/slot-filling, chit-chat, DSTC, and bAbI/QA literature; clearly explains why goal-directed evaluation needs different treatment.
- Soundness & rigor: Transparent data generation and OOV construction, appropriate metrics, documented hyperparameters, attention visualizations, ablations, and real-data validation.
- Clarity & reproducibility: Clear paper with detailed models and appendices; public release of Tasks 1–6 enables reproducibility (Concierge release unclear).
- Caveat — language & policy simplicity: Synthetic dialogs use limited templates and deterministic bot behavior, which may understate real-world complexity.
- Caveat — ranking evaluation: Selecting from a known candidate set differs from free-form generation and can leak knowledge via test candidates.
- Caveat — baseline breadth: Modern generative/copy/pointer and structured KB models are not compared, leaving open potential gains from stronger architectures.
- Caveat — real-data generality: DSTC2 and Concierge help, but Concierge is proprietary, limiting public generality.
- Feedback 1: Add an evaluation variant where candidate sets are training-only to reduce leakage and test sensitivity to candidate size.
- Feedback 2: Compare or discuss generative baselines with copy/pointer and constrained decoding for API calls.
- Feedback 3: Increase language variability or add noise (paraphrases, ASR-like errors) to bridge synthetic–real gap and test robustness.
- Feedback 4: Include a data-scaling study and an ablation on KB size/density to assess data efficiency and generalization.
- Feedback 5: Clarify Concierge release plans or provide a synthetic variant mimicking its variability for public benchmarking.
- Feedback 6: Separate action selection (policy) from surface realization in candidate sets to disentangle policy competence from templating.
- Feedback 7: Report sensitivity analyses (e.g., candidate set size) and possibly auxiliary supervision for complex subtasks like sorting.
- Question 1: Does including all train/val/test bot utterances and API calls in the candidate set introduce test-time information leakage, and what changes with training-only candidates?
- Question 2: How often do DSTC2 bots deviate from deterministic behavior and how does that affect per-dialog failures—can you breakdown failure modes?
- Question 3: How much of the OOV improvement is due to type matching versus exact lexical matching; would pointer/copy mechanisms match or exceed these gains?
- Question 4: For Task 3 (sorting/options), did you try explicit supervision or architectural biases (differentiable sorting, attention over ranked KB facts)?
- Question 5: Will Tasks 1–6 and code be fully released, and can any portion of Concierge be shared or synthesized for public use?
- Question 6: Have you tried generative decoding on these tasks, and how does its per-response/per-dialog performance compare to ranking?
