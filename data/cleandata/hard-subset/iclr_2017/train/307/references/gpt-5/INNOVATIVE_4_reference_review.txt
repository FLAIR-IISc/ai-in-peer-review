1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a new open, lightweight, and reproducible testbed for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, centered on the restaurant reservation domain. It decomposes the goal into five synthetic tasks (API call issuance, updates, option display with sorting, extra info retrieval, and full dialogs), adds an OOV split to stress generalization to unseen entities, and supplements with two “real” datasets: a conversion of DSTC2 to the proposed task format and a proprietary human-human “Concierge” dataset. The paper evaluates a spectrum of baselines (IR, supervised embeddings) and an end-to-end Memory Network with a simple but effective “match type” feature for entity handling. Results show that while per-response accuracy can be decent, per-dialog success remains low on harder tasks (especially option display and full dialogs), and that the relative ranking of methods on synthetic tasks carries over to DSTC2 and Concierge, supporting the testbed’s validity as a proxy.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- The paper introduces a timely, ambitious, and well-scoped benchmark suite that isolates core capabilities for goal-oriented dialog with an OOV stress test, plus bridging to real data. This resource direction is novel, useful, and likely to catalyze progress and comparability in the community.
- The empirical study—covering multiple baselines, MemNN variants, and both synthetic and real datasets—provides clear insights and establishes hard failure modes, seeding future research.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem framing and motivation: Clear articulation of the gap between slot-filling POMDP pipelines and end-to-end methods, and the lack of reproducible, capability-oriented evaluation for goal-directed dialogs. The decomposition into tasks mirrors actual dialog sub-skills (state tracking, API issuance, KB grounding, ranking/sorting) and is well motivated.
- Placement in literature: Solid contextualization with bAbI QA tasks, DSTC, chit-chat corpora, and recent end-to-end dialog work. The positioning as a dialog counterpart to bAbI is convincing.
- Methodology and claims support:
  - Synthetic tasks are controlled yet non-trivial (sorting, KB usage, OOV entities).
  - OOV split is thoughtfully designed (entities unseen in training dialogs), and match-type features directly address realistic entity handling issues.
  - The cross-dataset pattern of results (T1–T5 vs. DSTC2 vs. Concierge) supports the claim that the synthetic suite is an informative proxy.
  - Results are comprehensive: IR baselines, supervised embeddings, MemNN with/without types, and a rule-based baseline for DSTC2. Reporting both per-response and per-dialog accuracy is appropriate for goal completion.
- Novelty/value: Although not introducing a new model, the benchmark and the “match type” augmentation are impactful contributions. The resource and experimental framing offer a bold, field-shaping direction.

4) Suggestions for improvement (not critical to accept)
- Evaluation protocol:
  - The ranking setup uses a candidate pool containing utterances from train/val/test across tasks, including OOV test. Discuss potential biases this introduces and consider a stricter candidate set or a generation setting to better reflect deployment.
  - Add success-rate metrics that reflect user-level goals beyond exact-match ranking (e.g., entity-slot F1, API correctness, KB grounding accuracy), and error taxonomies for deeper diagnosis.
- Realism:
  - The simulator enforces deterministic bot behavior and templated language. Consider variants with stochastic policies, paraphrase augmentation, ASR-like noise, or entity coreference to stress robustness.
  - Clarify how much of the observed performance gap on T3/T5 is due to sorting vs. multi-step grounding vs. dialog control; small controlled ablations would be helpful.
- OOV setup:
  - Currently, models may access both KBs at train time to define type features. Discuss a stricter OOV condition (e.g., KB entities introduced only at test) and the implications for match-type features.
- Models/ablations:
  - Provide more ablations on MemNN (hops, attention behavior when sorting, impact of time and speaker features) and compare to a strong sequence model (e.g., seq2seq with attention) to broaden baselines.
  - Consider adding retrieval-augmented or copy/pointing mechanisms; these are natural fits for entity-heavy, OOV-prone tasks.
- DSTC2 conversion:
  - Expand details and limitations of the conversion process, including any noise introduced and how it might affect comparability.

5) Questions for the authors
- Candidate set construction: Since candidates include all utterances from train/val/test (including OOV sets), how sensitive are results to reducing candidates to only training utterances or to a dynamic pool? Have you measured how performance drops as the candidate pool expands?
- Sorting and reasoning: For Task 3, can you quantify where MemNNs fail (incorrect sort order vs. failing to stop proposing vs. misreading KB facts)? Any attention visualizations that demonstrate systematic issues?
- OOV protocol: If the KB with OOV entities were withheld until test time, would match-type features still function (e.g., via lexicon-free typing)? How would performance change?
- DSTC2 conversion: Are there cases where the “highest rating” heuristic you added for option selection conflicts with the original dialog flow? Any analysis of errors attributable to conversion artifacts?
- Generalization: Did you attempt domain transfer (e.g., train on the synthetic tasks and test zero-shot on DSTC2/Concierge), and what happens to per-dialog success?
- Scalability: How does performance change with more training dialogs (e.g., 10k) and more linguistic variation (more templates or paraphrases)?
- Reproducibility: Will you release the simulator/code to regenerate datasets with new random seeds, additional domains, and noise configurations?

Overall, this paper offers a strong and much-needed benchmark and analysis framework for goal-oriented, end-to-end dialogue. Despite limitations of synthetic language and ranking-based evaluation, the contribution is substantial and likely to have meaningful impact.
