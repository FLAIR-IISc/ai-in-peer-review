Summary
The paper introduces an open evaluation suite for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog in the restaurant reservation domain, consisting of five simulated tasks (issuing/updating API calls, presenting sorted options, providing KB facts, and full dialogs), plus two datasets derived from real interactions (DSTC2 converted into your format and an online concierge dataset). The authors argue this benchmark enables reproducible, interpretable assessment of key capabilities (dialog management, KB use, sorting, OOV handling). They report results for rule-based, IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks (with/without “match-type” features). MemNNs do best among learning methods; they handle issuing/updating API calls and specific KB lookups (with types), while still failing on displaying options and full-dialog success. The authors claim the observed patterns transfer to DSTC2 and Concierge, suggesting the synthetic tasks can serve as a proxy for real data.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited ecological validity and overly templated simulation and evaluation (ranking over fixed candidates) with weak evidence it correlates with real-world task success.
- Insufficient baselines and comparisons to established goal-oriented dialog methods; methodology around DSTC2 conversion and type features raises concerns about fairness and interpretability.

Supporting arguments
- Realism and generality: The simulated dialogs are generated from 43 user and 20 bot patterns with a deterministic questioning order and templated API usage. Rule-based systems trivially achieve 100% on T1–T5, which underscores how far the tasks are from the variability and ambiguity of real goal-oriented dialog (non-deterministic behavior, ASR/intent noise, user digressions). The ranking setting with a fixed candidate set (including test and OOV candidates across tasks) further reduces ecological validity: it bypasses generation and grounding challenges and introduces confounds related to candidate set construction.
- Validation on real data is limited: For DSTC2, the dataset is converted post hoc into your task format, with ratings injected and a re-split, making it impossible to compare against established DSTC results. The claim that performance patterns “carry over” is based on a single model family comparison to an ad hoc rule-based baseline (40.7% per-response), not to strong state-tracking/slot-filling baselines (word-level RNN trackers, delexicalized models, or modern copy/pointer methods). For Concierge, while the Next-Utterance-Classification framing is reasonable, the absence of structured KB grounding and the reliance solely on operator utterance candidates leaves unclear how success maps to goal completion.
- Baseline coverage: The chosen end-to-end baseline is MemN2N with match-type features. Missing are competitive established approaches for goal-oriented dialog: (i) supervised state-tracking + NLG pipelines; (ii) seq2seq/transformer-based models with attention and copy/pointer mechanisms or delexicalization; (iii) retrieval models using contextual encoders (bi-encoders with hard negatives) that are now standard for NUC. Without these, it’s hard to assess whether the tasks truly stress “end-to-end” systems or simply reflect design choices favorable to MemNNs.
- Methodological concerns: The “match-type” features leverage exact KB membership during evaluation (including OOV KB), which is a strong oracle. That design choice conflates representation learning with external gazetteer matching; improvements may largely stem from feature engineering rather than model capability. Additionally, the OOV split relies on disjoint cuisines/locations but still gives access to the OOV KB at training, which weakens the OOV challenge if type features can directly fire. Finally, artificially adding ratings to DSTC2 to make options “consistently predictable” alters the task and may bias evaluation.
- Claims vs. evidence: The key claim is that the synthetic tasks are a useful proxy for real-world goal-oriented dialog. The presented evidence (similar performance ordering across methods) is suggestive but thin, given the absence of strong baselines and the substantial reformatting of DSTC2. The paper stops short of demonstrating that progress on T1–T5 materially predicts improvements in real goal completion rates.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decisive)
- Strengthen baselines: include delexicalized RNN/LSTM/transformer state trackers, pointer/copy networks, span-based extractive responders, and modern retrieval bi-encoders with in-batch hard negatives. Compare to strong DSTC baselines in a comparable metric when possible.
- Evaluation: complement ranking with generation and end-to-end task success, including exact API correctness, KB consistency, and dialog-level success against a simulated user; report calibration/ablation on candidate set size and composition.
- Realism: expand user/bot linguistic variety, non-deterministic policies, noise (ASR-like paraphrases), and goal changes; validate via human annotation whether solving T1–T5 predicts success on held-out realistic logs.
- Fairness and clarity: disentangle gains from match-type features vs. model; ablate KB access and type lexicons; justify rating injection into DSTC2 and quantify its impact.
- Broaden scope: add at least one additional domain to assess cross-domain generalization; report out-of-domain transfer.
- Reporting: include statistical significance, error typology, and learning curves (given small training sets).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are your conclusions to the candidate set? What happens when candidates are restricted to utterances seen only in-training or only in-domain? Do results change materially with larger candidate sets or different negative sampling?
- Can you provide results for strong contemporary baselines (e.g., delexicalized state trackers, pointer-generator seq2seq, transformer bi-encoders) on T1–T6?
- How much of the OOV improvement comes from match-type features vs. the MemNN architecture? What are the results when the KB lexicon for types is not available (or partially available)?
- For DSTC2, how does injecting ratings and your re-splitting affect difficulty? Can you provide a setting directly comparable to standard DSTC metrics and baselines?
- Do per-dialog gains on T1–T5 correlate with human-judged task success on Concierge? Any evidence beyond method ordering?
- Can you release the simulator and pattern generator to facilitate community variation (e.g., non-deterministic policies, noise) and avoid overfitting to fixed templates?

Overall, the idea of a controlled testbed is valuable, but the current setup is too templated and the validation against real systems is too limited to support the central claim that these tasks are a reliable proxy for goal-oriented dialog performance.
