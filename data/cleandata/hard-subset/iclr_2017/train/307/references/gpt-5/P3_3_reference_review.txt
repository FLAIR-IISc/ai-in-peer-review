Summary and contributions
The paper introduces a publicly available testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue systems, centered on restaurant booking. It decomposes the overall objective into five synthetic tasks (issuing/updating API calls, presenting sorted options, retrieving KB attributes, and combining all), plus a conversion of DSTC2 into the same framework and a proprietary “Concierge” human-human dataset for external validation. The evaluation is retrieval-based (next-utterance selection) with per-response and per-dialog accuracy. The paper benchmarks IR baselines, supervised embeddings, and End-to-End Memory Networks (MemN2N), and proposes “match type” features to handle entities and OOV generalization. Results show MemN2N outperforming baselines, with strong gains from match types, but still low per-dialog success on the harder tasks (e.g., option presentation and full dialogue).

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated, much-needed standardized testbed for goal-oriented dialogue with careful task design that isolates key capabilities (state tracking, KB operations, sorting, OOV handling).
- Solid baseline study that surfaces concrete failure modes of contemporary end-to-end models and provides simple yet useful engineering (match types) that improves OOV and KB usage.

Supporting arguments
- Problem framing and motivation: The paper clearly articulates the gap between slot-filling systems (robust but domain-constrained) and end-to-end neural approaches (scalable but brittle), and proposes a reproducible, interpretable path to analyze progress. The task decomposition is a strong design choice for diagnosis and error analysis by construction.

- Relation to prior work: It situates the testbed with respect to bAbI QA tasks, DSTC datasets, IR-style dialogue work, and MemNN-based chit-chat benchmarks. The novelty lies in a goal-oriented, KB-grounded, multi-skill test suite with OOV stress tests, which was missing at the time. The conversion of DSTC2 to the same task format is helpful for connecting synthetic and real settings.

- Methodology and baselines: The evaluation suite covers a reasonable spectrum: rules, IR (TF-IDF and nearest neighbor), supervised embeddings, and MemN2N, with ablations on hops and entity handling. The “match type” feature is a pragmatic, interpretable addition that addresses two known weaknesses of low-dimensional embeddings (exact matching and OOVs). Reporting per-response and per-dialog accuracy is appropriate, and the degradation from encouraging per-response to low per-dialog success is an important empirical finding.

- Empirical support: Results consistently show: (i) task decomposition matters—models succeed on state-tracking/API issuance but fail on interpreting/sorting KB outputs in multi-turn settings; (ii) multi-hop improves MemNN; (iii) match types materially help OOV and KB attribute retrieval; (iv) relative rankings transfer from synthetic to DSTC2 and Concierge, supporting the claim that the synthetic tasks are a useful proxy. The rule-based ceiling confirms the tasks are solvable by design.

- Significance: Releasing such a testbed can steer the community toward more rigorous, comparable evaluation of goal-oriented dialogue, stimulating work on KB interfaces, copying/pointer mechanisms, and planning. Even negative results (low per-dialog accuracy) are informative.

Concerns and limitations (not decision-critical)
- Language and policy realism: Synthetic tasks rely on 43 user and 20 bot templates with deterministic system policy ordering. This creates risks of pattern overfitting and limits generalization to the diversity and non-determinism observed in real interactions. The authors partly mitigate this by including DSTC2 and Concierge, but the main testbed remains templatic.

- Evaluation protocol: Retrieval over a candidate set that includes all responses from train+val+test (including OOV test) raises questions. It ensures a closed response space but also means models score among canonical strings they never saw in training. This highlights OOV issues but also partially conflates modeling of language with response-space design. Clarification of whether embeddings for test-only tokens are randomly initialized or masked, and the exact impact of match types versus lexical memorization, would help.

- KB and OOV setup: Systems can access both KB halves during training, while OOV entities do not appear in training dialogs. This setup tests dialog-level OOV generalization but presumes a known KB. It would be useful to also test stricter OOV regimes where entities are unseen even in the KB at training time (true zero-shot), or where KB schemas change.

- Baselines: Given the centrality of entities and KB grounding, comparisons to architectures with explicit copy/pointer mechanisms, key-value memories, or sequence-to-sequence with attention and pointer-generator would strengthen the story. While MemNN is a reasonable choice for the time, the conclusions might shift with stronger entity-aware models.

- Metrics: Per-dialog accuracy is strict; complementary task-success metrics (e.g., correctness of API call arguments or successful booking regardless of minor non-critical utterance errors) would provide a more nuanced picture.

Suggestions for improvement
- Broaden linguistic and policy variability in the simulator (paraphrases, disfluencies, non-deterministic slot order, optional slots), and release multiple “difficulty modes.”
- Include stronger baselines tailored to entity grounding (copy/pointer, key-value memory networks) and report ablations isolating sorting vs. retrieval errors on Task 3.
- Refine evaluation: add task-success rates, slot-level F1, and calibration analyses; avoid including test-only responses in the candidate set if possible, or justify this choice and its effects.
- Provide more detailed error analysis (e.g., confusion matrices for wrong slot updates, mis-sorted options, failure to terminate).
- Release the DSTC2 conversion scripts and the rule-based baseline for transparency and reproducibility; if possible, release an anonymized subset of Concierge.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set construction: Do models see embeddings for tokens that only appear in OOV test candidates? If not, how is the softmax over C candidates implemented for those tokens; if yes, how are these embeddings initialized/trained without leakage?
- KB accessibility: Why allow access to both KB halves at training time for OOV evaluation? Have you tried a stricter condition where the OOV entities are absent from the KB during training, to test zero-shot transfer via typing alone?
- Sorting and KB interpretation: On Task 3, can you quantify whether failures stem from ranking the wrong restaurant among candidates, failure to read ratings from memory, or dialogue control (e.g., failing to stop when accepted)?
- DSTC2 conversion: Adding ratings “so that options can be consistently predicted” may alter the original interaction semantics. How sensitive are results to this design choice, and can you provide a variant without added ratings?
- Scalability: How does performance vary with larger training sets and with increased candidate set sizes? Any signs of overfitting to the small synthetic training sets?
- Training details: For MemNN, do you train with full softmax over all candidates or negative sampling? How many hops are optimal per task, and how stable are results across random seeds?

Overall, this is a useful, well-motivated benchmark contribution with thoughtful empirical analysis. Despite limitations, it is likely to have positive impact on research in end-to-end goal-oriented dialogue.
