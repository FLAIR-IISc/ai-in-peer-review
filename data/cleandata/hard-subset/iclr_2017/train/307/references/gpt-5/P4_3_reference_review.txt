1. Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces a lightweight, reproducible benchmark suite for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog in the restaurant-reservation domain. It decomposes the overall objective into five synthetic tasks (T1–T5) that test specific capabilities: dialog state tracking via questioning (T1), updating requests (T2), interpreting KB results and ranking/sorting options (T3), retrieving factual attributes (T4), and full dialogs (T5). It additionally provides an adapted real-world task from DSTC2 (T6) and evaluates on a proprietary human-human “Concierge” dataset to assess external validity.
- The setup includes an OOV split by holding out half of entity types (cuisines, locations) in the dialog training set while exposing the full KB, allowing precise testing of entity generalization.
- Evaluation is cast as next-utterance selection (per-response and per-dialog accuracy), enabling controlled, comparable experiments across methods.
- A set of baselines is provided: IR (TF–IDF, Nearest Neighbor), supervised embeddings, and end-to-end Memory Networks (MemN2N), with a simple but effective extension via match-type features to handle entities and OOVs. Results highlight that MemN2N can learn to issue and update API calls and, with type features, retrieve factual attributes; however, it struggles with option ranking and full dialogs, and overall per-dialog success remains low.
- The paper argues that performance patterns on synthetic tasks transfer to real data (DSTC2 and Concierge), supporting the benchmark’s utility as a proxy for real-world progress.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated benchmark contribution that enables reproducible, fine-grained evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog models, including principled OOV testing.
- Sound and thorough baseline study that exposes concrete strengths and weaknesses of current end-to-end approaches, providing actionable insight for the community.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- Assessing whether end-to-end dialog systems can replace traditional slot-filling/POMDP methods in goal-oriented, transactional settings, and diagnosing where they fall short (state tracking, KB access, entity handling, sorting, and multi-turn control).

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- The motivation is strong: prior end-to-end work focused on chit-chat with weak evaluation; goal-oriented systems have relied on hand-engineering. The proposed tasks extend the spirit of bAbI QA to dialog, targeting controllable capability testing. Related datasets (DSTC, ATIS, SFCore) are either not end-to-end, noisy, or not open, and prior “goal-ish” tasks are closer to QA than dialog. The paper situates contributions relative to MemN2N and IR/embedding baselines, and discusses user simulators in POMDPs.

Do the results support the claims and are they rigorous?
- Claims are supported by systematic experiments across T1–T6 and OOV splits with clear metrics. MemN2N correctly handles issuing/updating API calls and improves with multiple hops; type features yield substantial gains for OOV entities and factual retrieval (T4). The persistent failures on option display/sorting and full dialogs are documented via per-response vs per-dialog gaps. Transfer of relative method ranking on DSTC2 and Concierge strengthens the proxy claim. Hyperparameters and implementation details (time features, hops, candidate set) are described; appendices provide examples and settings. While not exhaustive, the baselines are reasonable and the analysis is careful about the limits.

Caveats:
- Synthetic dialogs use templates and deterministic policies for the bot, which reduces linguistic and strategic variability; the ranking setup abstracts away generation. The candidate set includes all candidates across tasks/splits, and a discussion of potential selection bias or leakage could be expanded. The real-world Concierge set is proprietary (not released), limiting external reproducibility of that portion. Statistical significance and variance across runs are not reported.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not strictly part of the decision)
- Clarify candidate construction: confirm that the candidate pool is fixed and identical at train/val/test and across tasks; discuss how this affects difficulty and whether results change with task-specific candidate sets.
- Report variance across random seeds and confidence intervals for key results, especially per-dialog accuracy, which can be sensitive to small per-response differences.
- Expand analysis of failure modes on T3/T5 (e.g., attention patterns vs required sorting logic; whether failures arise from reading KB facts, maintaining shortlist state, or rank-ordering).
- Consider including additional baselines more tailored to entity manipulation (pointer/copy networks, key-value memory networks, or seq2seq with copy), and a strong slot-filling baseline on T1–T5 to quantify the gap to hand-engineered systems.
- Provide more detail on the simulator: distribution of missing/updated fields, language pattern variability, and how option acceptance is sampled. A small set of human paraphrases in training could test robustness to lexical variability.
- For DSTC2 conversion, give examples of inconsistencies/noise and quantify how often bot behavior deviates from deterministic policies. If possible, release conversion scripts.
- For Concierge, describe the labeling for API-use turns, inter-annotator checks (if any), and provide a small public sample (even if further anonymized) to aid comparability.
- Discuss the generalization beyond a single domain (multi-domain KBs) and to ASR noise.

Questions for the authors
- Candidate set: Does the candidate pool include all utterances and API calls from all tasks and both test splits simultaneously? Have you evaluated with task-specific candidate pools, and if so, how do results differ?
- OOV handling: Are type features derived purely from the KB (i.e., an exact lexical match triggers a type), and do they require any manual lexicons beyond the KB? How do you handle ambiguous tokens or multiword entities?
- Sorting in T3: Is there any auxiliary supervision for sorting/ranking, or must the model infer ordering solely from dialog success signals? Do attention hops correlate with correctly identifying the highest-rated option?
- Robustness and variance: How many random seeds were used? Please provide mean and standard deviation for key metrics, especially on T3–T5 and OOV splits.
- DSTC2 conversion: Are ratings you added deterministic mappings or randomly assigned? Could this introduce label noise in option ordering?
- Concierge dataset: Can you release a de-identified subset and/or detailed guidelines to reconstruct similar datasets? How were “API use” labels determined?
- Scalability: How do results change with larger training sets (beyond 1k dialogs) or increased linguistic variability (more user patterns)? Do more hops continue to help, or do gains saturate?

Overall, this is a clear, technically sound benchmark paper whose main value is the dataset/task design and diagnostic evaluation rather than algorithmic novelty. The work is likely to be useful to the community and to stimulate progress on goal-oriented dialog.
