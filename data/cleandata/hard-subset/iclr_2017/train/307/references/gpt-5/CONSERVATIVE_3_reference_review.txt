Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a lightweight, open testbed to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, focused on a restaurant reservation domain. It decomposes the goal into five synthetic subtasks (API call issuance, updates, sorted option display, retrieval of facts, and full dialogs), with an OOV split to probe entity generalization. It further includes a converted version of DSTC2 (Task 6) and reports results on a proprietary “Concierge” human-human dataset to assess realism. The authors benchmark several baselines (TF-IDF, nearest neighbor, supervised embeddings) and a Memory Network variant with “match type” features for entity handling. They report per-response and per-dialog accuracies and conclude that, while MemN2N variants outperform simple baselines and handle some nontrivial behaviors (API calls, some OOV handling), reliable goal completion remains challenging, especially for option display and full dialogs.

Decision: Reject
Main reasons:
- Heavy reliance on synthetic, deterministic tasks and a ranking-based evaluation makes it difficult to conclude about real-world goal-oriented dialog performance. The link to established, strong goal-oriented pipelines (e.g., slot-filling/POMDP trackers) is not convincingly demonstrated.
- Baselines against established methods are weak and uneven. The DSTC2 comparison excludes strong state trackers and uses a custom rule-based baseline; the Concierge dataset is proprietary and not reproducible. Claims that the synthetic tasks proxy real performance are insufficiently validated.

Supporting arguments
- Problem framing: The paper aims to provide a testbed for end-to-end models in goal-oriented settings. This is worthwhile; however, the synthetic tasks are fully predictable by design and solvable by rules. While this enables ablative diagnosis, it also risks overfitting to artifacts and limits ecological validity. The deterministic ordering of questions and option display diminishes the diversity of dialog management strategies.
- Evaluation methodology: The closed-set ranking setup (selecting from a global pool of candidate responses collected across splits and tasks) greatly simplifies response prediction and deviates from generation and action grounding used in practical systems. This may inflate per-response accuracy and obscures whether models actually learn dialog control or exploit lexical overlap with a known candidate space. The authors do not report sensitivity to candidate set size/composition nor do they conduct interactive or human-in-the-loop evaluations.
- OOV setup: Models are given access to both KBs during training (including entities reserved for OOV test dialogs). With match-type features relying on exact KB matches, this mitigates the OOV challenge and blurs whether the model truly generalizes to unseen entities versus exploiting KB lookups. A stricter OOV regime (no access to test KB entities at training) would be more convincing.
- Baselines and comparisons: The paper omits comparisons to strong, established slot-filling and POMDP-based systems on DSTC2, which is the de facto benchmark for task-oriented dialog. The custom rule-based baseline on DSTC2 achieves low accuracy and is not representative of the best traditional methods; hence claims that end-to-end methods “outperform a dedicated slot-filling rule-based baseline” are not compelling. Likewise, neural baselines are limited: no comparison to strong RNN/seq2seq architectures with attention/copy mechanisms or pointer networks that directly address entity handling and sorting. The “match type” feature is a straightforward exact-match heuristic rather than a substantive modeling contribution.
- Realism: The DSTC2 conversion modifies the dataset (e.g., artificial rating insertion) and changes the task definition (ranking next utterance, not state tracking). This prevents comparison to published DSTC2 results. The Concierge dataset is not open, which undermines reproducibility. The claim that the synthetic tasks are a “good proxy” is based on similar relative ordering of baselines but without robust statistical analysis or evidence of correlation with goal success in real deployments.
- Results: Even the best models achieve near-zero per-dialog accuracy on key tasks (T3/T5), highlighting limited practical utility in goal completion. While the paper is careful to acknowledge this, it undercuts the central claim that the benchmark advances end-to-end goal-oriented dialog. More error analysis and ablations (e.g., removal of deterministic ordering, candidate set changes, harder OOV) would strengthen conclusions.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen baselines: Include established slot-filling/POMDP trackers and state-of-the-art RNN/Transformer architectures with copy/pointer mechanisms and KB attention, and evaluate under the same ranking/generation regimes.
- Tighten OOV evaluation: Disallow access to test KB entities during training; test truly unseen entities and attributes; report performance under varying degrees of KB mismatch.
- Revisit evaluation: Complement ranking with generation and interactive rollouts measuring actual task success (e.g., simulated users, success rates, dialog length, and error recovery). Add statistical significance tests and calibration analyses across candidate set sizes.
- Reduce determinism: Introduce variability in dialog policy (order of questions, paraphrases), noise and ASR-like imperfections, and more complex KB operations.
- Bridge to real data: For DSTC2, either match the original task (state tracking) to compare to published baselines or provide a rigorously justified conversion. Consider releasing a de-identified subset of Concierge to foster reproducibility.
- Clarify contributions: The “match type” feature is a useful engineering addition but not novel; present it as a practical heuristic and provide broader ablations (e.g., copy mechanisms) addressing entities and OOVs.

Questions for the authors
- How large is the candidate set at test time per task, and how sensitive are results to its size and composition? Does excluding test-only candidates significantly change outcomes?
- In the OOV setting, do models have access to embeddings or type lists for test KB entities during training? Can you report a stricter OOV variant where test KB entities are entirely hidden until test?
- Why not compare to strong DSTC2 baselines (e.g., RNN-based trackers, POMDP) under a common metric? Can you provide state-tracking or goal-success evaluations to align with established literature?
- How robust are the conclusions to removing deterministic ordering (e.g., randomize the order of slot requests, break the assumption that bots always use the same patterns)?
- Can you release the Concierge data (or a subset) to ensure the community can reproduce those findings? If not, can you provide a detailed protocol to emulate it from public sources?
