Summary and contributions
- Problem addressed: The paper targets evaluation of end-to-end models for goal-oriented dialogue, where success depends on tracking constraints, interacting with a knowledge base (KB), and completing tasks. It argues that existing chit-chat benchmarks don’t test these capabilities and that reproducible, interpretable evaluation is lacking.
- Contributions: (i) A suite of five synthetic, controlled tasks in a restaurant-booking domain that probe specific sub-competencies (slot elicitation, updating, KB querying, sorting/interpreting KB return, providing attributes), plus a “full-dialog” task; (ii) an OOV test split to assess generalization to unseen entities; (iii) reformatting DSTC2 into the same task format (Task 6); (iv) evaluation on a proprietary Concierge dataset to test ecological validity; (v) baselines spanning rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, and MemN2N, and a simple but effective “match type” feature to handle entities. The findings provide a clear, interpretable picture: end-to-end models can learn to issue/update API calls and handle OOVs with type features, but struggle with interpreting KB returns and multi-step reasoning, yielding low per-dialog success on the harder tasks.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The benchmark is well-motivated, fills a recognized gap (goal-oriented, reproducible, diagnostic evaluation), and is likely to have strong community impact.
- The empirical study is careful and revealing: it uses diverse baselines, OOV testing, and both synthetic and real data to support claims about capabilities and limitations of end-to-end systems.

Supporting arguments
On criterion 1 (what problem is tackled?): The paper precisely targets the need for reproducible, decomposed evaluation of goal-oriented dialogue—a distinct setting from open-domain chit-chat—requiring implicit state tracking, KB interaction, and entity handling. This is a meaningful and timely problem.

On criterion 2 (motivation and positioning): The motivation is clear and well-placed in the literature. The authors contrast with slot-filling/POMDP approaches (handcrafted states), prior chit-chat corpora and evaluation issues, and other synthetic testbeds (bAbI QA) that don’t require the bot to ask questions or manage task progress. The choice to include an OOV split and to compare to DSTC2/Concierge data directly addresses external validity concerns.

On criterion 3 (do results support claims, are they rigorous?): Yes. The study uses:
- Multiple baselines (rule-based, TF-IDF, nearest neighbor, supervised embeddings) and a competitive end-to-end model (MemN2N) with careful hyperparameter tuning.
- Two complementary metrics (per-response and per-dialog); the latter is essential for goal completion. The observed gap is itself an important finding.
- An OOV evaluation with a principled split (entities disjoint across KB halves) to probe generalization.
- Real data (DSTC2 reformatted; Concierge) to check whether patterns from synthetic tasks carry over; relative performance trends are consistent.
- Ablations that matter: number of hops, and match-type features; these directly test the claims about reasoning and entity handling. The analysis that T3/T5 remain difficult, and that type features dramatically help T4 and OOV cases, is credible and actionable.

Limitations worth noting (do not outweigh strengths):
- The synthetic tasks use fixed bot templates and deterministic orders (e.g., the question order in Task 1), which can make some behaviors easier to learn and may under-represent real dialog variability.
- The ranking setup simplifies generation; while appropriate for controlled evaluation, it leaves open how systems would perform in fully generative settings or under human evaluation.
- Type matching features inject light domain knowledge (entity types) and assume KB access for OOV entity detection; this is practical but less “purely end-to-end.”
- The DSTC2 conversion involves adding ratings and changing splits; direct comparison to DSTC2 leaderboard is not possible. Still, it serves the paper’s purpose of pattern validation.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Increase simulator variability: randomize the order of slot requests, diversify bot templates, and vary dialogue flows to reduce determinism and better approximate real usage.
- Add a generative baseline (e.g., seq2seq with attention and copy/pointer mechanism) adapted to ranking or generation to contextualize MemN2N results, given the centrality of copying entities.
- Provide finer-grained error analysis: categorize failure modes in T3/T5 (e.g., sorting errors vs. grounding errors vs. stopping criterion mistakes) and relate them to hops/attention patterns.
- Report per-dialog accuracy for DSTC2/Concierge where feasible to complement per-response accuracy.
- Clarify the impact of candidate set size/overlap across tasks on ranking difficulty; consider task-specific candidate subsets to test robustness to candidate scaling.
- Discuss how the match-type mechanism relates to copy/pointer networks and whether similar benefits could be obtained in a more model-agnostic way.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to removing deterministic aspects of the simulator (e.g., randomizing the order of slot queries in Task 1/2)? Do MemN2N gains hold?
- How are entities identified for type features in practice on real data (DSTC2/Concierge)? Pure lexicon lookup from the KB, or any NER/normalization? What is the error rate of entity detection and its effect?
- In OOV experiments, models “have access to both KBs” during training. Does that imply the type lexicon includes OOV entities at training time? If not, how would performance change?
- Could you report per-dialog accuracy on DSTC2/Concierge (even if low), to better quantify goal completion under noise?
- For Task 3 (sorting options), did you analyze hop attention trajectories to understand whether the model attempts to rank by rating or defaults to memorized patterns? Any interventions (e.g., auxiliary loss for ordering) tried?
- Will you release code for data generation, splits (including OOV), and the MemN2N+type implementation to ensure full reproducibility?

Overall, the paper is clear, well-structured, and its contributions are likely to shape and accelerate research on goal-oriented end-to-end dialogue.
