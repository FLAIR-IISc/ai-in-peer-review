

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper introduces a novel benchmark dataset and set of tasks designed to systematically evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems in a reproducible and interpretable manner. The authors identify a critical gap in the literature: while end-to-end dialog systems have shown promise in chit-chat settings, their performance in goal-oriented domains (like restaurant reservation) remains unclear due to the lack of standardized evaluation resources. They propose a set of five tasks grounded in a restaurant reservation domain with an underlying knowledge base, designed to test specific capabilities of dialog systems (e.g., issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, and providing extra information). The paper also converts real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset) into this task format to validate that the synthetic benchmark serves as a good proxy for real-world performance. The authors demonstrate that their benchmark can effectively highlight both the strengths and limitations of current end-to-end models, particularly Memory Networks, which show promising but incomplete performance on these tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a well-designed, reproducible benchmark for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems, which addresses a clear and important gap in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in addressing three key criteria:

**First, the problem is well-motivated and clearly articulated.** The authors provide a strong rationale for their work by highlighting the limitations of both traditional slot-filling approaches (hard to scale) and end-to-end models (unclear performance in goal-oriented settings). The related work section thoroughly covers existing datasets and explains why they are inadequate for evaluating end-to-end systems (e.g., they're annotated at the state level, noisy, or not open source). The paper's positioning as an extension of the bAbI tasks to the dialog domain is particularly strong and well-placed in the literature.

**Second, the methodology is scientifically rigorous.** The authors carefully designed five tasks that test specific capabilities of dialog systems, with clear specifications and evaluation metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy). They created a synthetic dataset with controlled variables (including OOV testing) to isolate specific challenges, and they supplemented this with real-world datasets (DSTC2 and concierge data) to validate that their benchmark serves as a good proxy for real performance. The experimental setup is thorough, with multiple baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) and careful attention to hyperparameter tuning.

**Third, the results convincingly support the paper's claims.** The results show that while end-to-end models (specifically Memory Networks) can handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (issuing API calls, updating requests), they struggle with others (interpreting API results to display options). Crucially, the pattern of performance across their synthetic tasks matches performance on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and concierge), validating that their benchmark is a useful proxy. The authors also demonstrate that their proposed "match type features" significantly improve performance on OOV tasks, providing valuable insights into model limitations.

The paper's contribution is particularly valuable because it provides a standard evaluation framework that addresses the reproducibility issues plaguing current evaluation methods (e.g., Mechanical Turk-based evaluations). This will enable more consistent comparisons across future work and help focus research on specific capabilities that need improvement.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few minor improvements would enhance its impact:

- **Clarify the practical utility of the benchmark:** While the paper demonstrates the benchmark's validity, it could more explicitly state how this will accelerate research. For example, how many researchers have already used the benchmark, or how it might be integrated into future dialog system competitions.

- **Expand on the limitations of the benchmark:** The authors mention that the synthetic data is "partially artificial" and that the concierge dataset has additional challenges. It would be helpful to more thoroughly discuss the specific limitations of their benchmark in relation to other real-world dialog domains beyond restaurant reservation.

- **Address potential scalability concerns:** The current benchmark focuses on a single domain (restaurant reservation). The authors could briefly discuss how easily this framework could be extended to other domains, which is crucial for assessing the benchmark's broader utility.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper notes that Memory Networks with match type features outperform other methods, but the performance on Task 3 (Displaying options) remains low. Could the authors speculate on why this particular task is so challenging for end-to-end models, and what specific architectural modifications might help overcome this limitation?

2. The authors demonstrate that their synthetic benchmark correlates with real-world performance. Could they provide more details about how they validated this correlation (e.g., statistical significance tests), and whether they observed similar correlations on other real-world datasets beyond DSTC2 and the concierge service?

3. The paper mentions that the concierge dataset has "much wider" user requests than their synthetic tasks. Could the authors elaborate on how they handled these additional complexities in their evaluation, and whether they plan to expand their benchmark to include such broader request types in future work?

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of dialog systems. The benchmark it proposes has the potential to significantly improve how we evaluate and compare end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, and the results presented convincingly demonstrate the utility of this approach. I recommend acceptance with the minor revisions outlined above.
