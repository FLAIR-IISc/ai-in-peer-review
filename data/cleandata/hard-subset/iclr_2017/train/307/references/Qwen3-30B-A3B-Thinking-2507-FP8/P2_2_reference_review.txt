

# Review for ICLR Submission

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of dialog systems by introducing a well-designed, open-source testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors identify a critical gap in the literature: the lack of standardized, reproducible evaluation methods for end-to-end models in goal-oriented settings. Current approaches either rely on noisy, domain-specific datasets (like DSTC) or focus on non-goal-oriented chit-chat tasks where performance doesn't necessarily transfer to goal-directed dialog. 

The authors propose a set of five carefully designed tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that systematically test key capabilities required for successful goal-oriented dialog: issuing API calls, updating API calls, displaying options, providing extra information, and conducting full dialogs. The tasks are grounded in a structured knowledge base and include an Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set to evaluate generalization to unseen entities. Crucially, the authors validate that their synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance by demonstrating consistent model performance patterns on two additional real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset).

The paper provides a thorough baseline comparison across multiple model types (rule-based, classical IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks), with the Memory Network approach emerging as the most effective baseline. The authors clearly demonstrate that while end-to-end models can handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (like tracking implicit state and issuing API calls), they still struggle with key capabilities like interpreting KB results to present options to users.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a clear, important gap in the field with a well-motivated and carefully designed solution.
2. The proposed testbed is scientifically rigorous, well-documented, and validated against real-world data, providing a reproducible standard for future work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper excels in several critical areas that make it a strong candidate for ICLR:

**Strong motivation and literature placement:** The paper clearly identifies the limitations of current evaluation methods for goal-oriented dialog systems (Section 1). It properly situates its work within the broader context of both traditional slot-filling systems and end-to-end neural approaches, and draws a compelling parallel to the influential bAbI question-answering tasks. The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the related work, particularly in Section 2, where they critically analyze existing datasets and their limitations.

**Scientific rigor and validation:** The authors don't just propose a testbed; they rigorously validate it. The synthetic tasks are designed with specific capabilities in mind, and the OOV test sets provide an important challenge for generalization. Most importantly, they demonstrate that performance patterns on their synthetic tasks correlate with performance on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and concierge service), which is a powerful validation of the testbed's usefulness. This validation is crucial and significantly strengthens the paper's contribution.

**Clear methodology and reproducibility:** The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including dataset statistics, task definitions, and implementation details (Appendix A). The authors make the dataset publicly available, which is essential for the community to build upon their work. The evaluation metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy) are well-justified and appropriate for goal-oriented dialog.

**Comprehensive baseline evaluation:** The paper doesn't just present the testbed; it demonstrates how to use it effectively by evaluating multiple model types. The results clearly show the relative strengths and weaknesses of different approaches, with the Memory Network approach emerging as the most promising baseline. The analysis of why certain models fail (particularly on Task 3, displaying options) provides valuable insights for future work.

**Well-written and organized:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the motivation, methodology, and results.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, here are some suggestions to further enhance it:

- **Limitations discussion:** While the paper acknowledges that end-to-end models still have limitations, a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the testbed itself would be valuable. For example, what aspects of real-world goal-oriented dialog might not be captured by these synthetic tasks? How might these limitations affect the generalizability of the testbed to other domains?

- **Domain transfer discussion:** The paper mentions that the testbed is designed for restaurant reservation, but could provide more concrete discussion about how it might be extended to other domains (e.g., hotel booking, travel planning). What aspects would need modification, and what would remain the same?

- **Implementation details:** While the paper provides good implementation details in Appendix A, a brief example of how to use the testbed with a simple machine learning framework would be helpful for readers looking to adopt the testbed quickly.

- **Broader impact:** A brief discussion of the broader impact of this testbed on the field (e.g., how it might accelerate progress in end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems) would strengthen the conclusion.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in my assessment:

1. The paper states that the concierge dataset is more challenging than the synthetic tasks. Could you elaborate on which specific aspects of the concierge dataset make it more challenging (beyond just being "noisier"), and how these aspects might be incorporated into future versions of the testbed?

2. The Memory Networks with match type features show significant improvement on OOV tasks. Could you provide more detail about how these match type features interact with the Memory Network architecture to handle OOV entities, and why they're particularly effective for this task?

3. The paper mentions that the synthetic tasks are "lightweight and easy to use." Could you provide a brief example of how a researcher might integrate this testbed into their existing dialog system evaluation pipeline?

4. The authors state they're not trying to improve the state-of-the-art in restaurant booking, but rather to provide a testbed. How do you envision this testbed being used by researchers who are trying to develop new end-to-end goal-oriented dialog models? What specific insights would this testbed provide that current evaluation methods don't?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The testbed is well-designed, rigorously validated, and will likely become a standard evaluation resource for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. I recommend acceptance.
