

# Review of "A Benchmark for Goal-Oriented Dialog Systems"

## 1. Summary
This paper proposes a valuable contribution: a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors design a set of five tasks within a restaurant reservation domain, grounded in a knowledge base, to systematically test crucial dialog capabilities such as dialog management, KB querying, and handling unseen entities. They provide a reproducible, lightweight testbed with both synthetic and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service), and evaluate multiple baseline models. The key insight is that while end-to-end models show promise in chit-chat settings, their performance in goal-oriented dialog remains unclear, and current evaluation methods lack reproducibility. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks with match type features perform best among their baselines, but still struggle with interpreting KB results to present options to users.

## 2. Decision
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a useful benchmark, it fails to sufficiently justify the significance of their specific task design and overstates the broader implications of their work. The evaluation methodology and claims require stronger justification to meet ICLR's high standards.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main weakness is its limited scope and insufficient justification for why their specific restaurant reservation tasks represent broader challenges in goal-oriented dialog. While the authors correctly identify the gap in existing evaluation methods, they fail to convincingly argue that their narrow domain (restaurant reservation) with five specific tasks captures the essential challenges of goal-oriented dialog systems. The benchmark's domain-specificity limits its generalizability to more complex, multi-domain dialog systems.

The evaluation methodology also has significant limitations. The paper uses a ranking approach (selecting from candidate responses) rather than a generation approach, which is common in modern end-to-end dialog systems. This may not reflect the true capabilities of these models. Additionally, the extremely small training sets (1,000 examples per task) create unrealistic learning conditions that don't reflect how these models would actually be deployed in practice.

The paper overstates the significance of their contribution, claiming it will "help foster progress of end-to-end conversational agents" and "help focus research and development." However, the tasks they design are quite narrow and specific to restaurant reservation, with no clear path to generalization to more complex dialog scenarios. The authors also fail to adequately address the limitations of their benchmark, such as how it would handle ambiguity in user requests or scale to multi-domain conversations.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Broaden the scope of justification**: The authors should better explain why their specific restaurant reservation tasks are representative of broader goal-oriented dialog challenges, rather than just being domain-specific. They should discuss how their benchmark could be adapted for other domains or more complex tasks.

- **Include more recent baselines**: The paper evaluates Memory Networks but doesn't compare with more recent end-to-end dialog models (e.g., transformer-based architectures). Including these would strengthen the benchmark's relevance to current research.

- **Clarify evaluation methodology**: The authors should explicitly discuss the implications of using a ranking approach rather than generation for evaluation, and how their benchmark would need to be adapted for generation-based models.

- **Provide deeper analysis of failure modes**: The paper identifies that models struggle with Task 3 (Displaying options), but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of *why* these failures occur. A more detailed error analysis would be valuable for the community.

- **Address scalability concerns**: The authors should discuss how their benchmark would scale to more complex domains, multi-turn dialogues with multiple goals, and handling of ambiguous user requests.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The rule-based system achieves 100% accuracy on the synthetic tasks, while end-to-end models achieve much lower performance. How does this affect the interpretation of the results, especially when the authors claim their benchmark will help "focus research and development"?

- How would you expect this benchmark to scale to more complex, multi-domain dialog systems (e.g., booking both restaurant reservations and hotel rooms), and what modifications would be necessary?

- The paper states that "the goal here is explicitly not to improve the state of the art in the narrow domain of restaurant booking." However, the rule-based baseline achieves perfect performance on the synthetic tasks. How does this impact the paper's claim that their benchmark will help identify where end-to-end models fall short?

- The authors show that Memory Networks with match type features improve OOV performance, but the improvement is limited. What are the specific limitations of the match type features, and how might they be overcome in future work?

- The paper validates the benchmark on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge), but the performance on these datasets is not as strong as on synthetic tasks. How would you characterize the relationship between synthetic and real-world performance, and what does this say about the generalizability of the benchmark?
