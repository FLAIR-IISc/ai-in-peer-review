

# Review for ICLR

## Summary
This paper proposes a new benchmark dataset and set of tasks for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, specifically designed to test crucial capabilities like dialog management, KB querying, and handling novel entities. The authors create a simulation-based restaurant reservation domain with five specific tasks and validate their approach on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service). The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a reproducible framework for evaluating end-to-end dialog systems in goal-oriented scenarios, which is lacking in the literature. The authors demonstrate that Memory Networks with match type features outperform baselines on their tasks, though they still struggle with certain dialog management aspects.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. The paper contains multiple formatting and presentation inconsistencies that undermine its credibility and reproducibility, including missing tables (Tables 1, 2, 10), missing figures (Figure 1), and inconsistent terminology throughout the text.

2. The methodology lacks sufficient justification for key design choices, particularly the ranking-based evaluation approach and the OOV test setup, which are critical to the paper's claims.

## Supporting Arguments

**Formatting and Presentation Inconsistencies (Major Issue)**
The paper suffers from numerous formatting errors that make it difficult to assess the work. The text repeatedly references tables (Tables 1, 2, 10) and Figure 1 that are not included in the provided text. This is a critical issue for reproducibility, which the authors claim is a primary goal of their work. The paper also uses inconsistent terminology: "dialog" vs. "dialogue," "bot" vs. "bot," "KB" vs. "knowledge base," and inconsistent capitalization of "Dialog State Tracking Challenge." These inconsistencies make the paper difficult to follow and undermine its scholarly rigor.

**Methodological Justification Gaps (Major Issue)**
The paper makes a strong claim that their tasks are a good proxy for real-world performance, but provides insufficient evidence. They state "the pattern of results observed in our tasks is indeed a good proxy for what would be observed on real data," but the justification is limited to showing similar performance patterns across datasets without demonstrating that the specific errors made by models on the synthetic tasks map to the same types of errors on real data.

The paper's evaluation methodology has significant gaps. They claim that "Dialog systems are evaluated in a ranking, not a generation, setting," but never justify why ranking is preferable to generation for this specific evaluation. This is a major methodological choice that requires substantial justification, particularly since the field has largely moved toward generative evaluation.

The OOV analysis is problematic. The paper states "on the OOV tasks again performance is improved, but this is all due to better performance on known words, as unknown words are simply not used without the match type features," but provides no evidence to support this claim (e.g., no breakdown of performance on known vs. unknown words). This is a critical claim that lacks empirical support.

**Technical Inconsistencies (Minor but Cumulative Issue)**
The paper has multiple minor but consistent technical inconsistencies that collectively undermine confidence in the work. For example:
- In Task 1, the paper states "the bot asks for information in a deterministic order, making prediction possible," but if the order is deterministic, why is it a challenge for the model?
- The paper uses "bot" and "bot" interchangeably without standardization.
- The paper claims to "convert DSTC2 into our task format" but doesn't explain how they handled the difference in required fields (DSTC2 uses 3 fields while their tasks use 4).
- The paper states "We only keep examples with API calls retrieving at least 3 options" for Task 3 but "at least 1 option" for Task 5 without explaining this inconsistency.

## Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Include all referenced tables and figures**: The paper cannot be properly evaluated without Tables 1, 2, and 10 and Figure 1. These must be included to allow assessment of the claims.

2. **Justify the ranking-based evaluation**: Provide a clear rationale for why ranking is preferred over generation for this specific evaluation of goal-oriented dialog systems. Compare with previous work that has used similar evaluation approaches.

3. **Clarify the OOV analysis**: Provide evidence supporting the claim about performance on known vs. unknown words. A breakdown of performance on these categories would strengthen the analysis significantly.

4. **Standardize terminology**: Choose one form of "dialog" vs. "dialogue," "bot" vs. "bot," and "KB" vs. "knowledge base" and use it consistently throughout the paper.

5. **Explain Task 5's different threshold**: Justify why Task 3 requires at least 3 options while Task 5 requires at least 1 option, as this inconsistency affects the comparability of results.

## Questions for Authors

1. How exactly did you handle the difference in required fields between DSTC2 (3 fields) and your tasks (4 fields) when converting DSTC2 into your task format? Did you make any assumptions about missing fields?

2. Can you provide evidence supporting the claim that "on the OOV tasks performance is improved, but this is all due to better performance on known words"? Specifically, what percentage of the improvement comes from known words versus unknown words?

3. Why did you choose to use a ranking-based evaluation rather than a generative one for this specific task? What evidence do you have that ranking-based evaluation correlates better with goal achievement in this domain?

4. In Task 1, if the bot asks for information in a deterministic order, why is this considered a challenge for end-to-end models? How does this differ from traditional rule-based approaches that would also follow a deterministic order?
