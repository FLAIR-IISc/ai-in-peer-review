

# Review

## Summary
The paper proposes a new open resource for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, consisting of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain. The authors argue that current evaluation resources for goal-oriented dialog systems are limited, noisy, or not suitable for end-to-end learning. They create a controlled simulation with a knowledge base to generate dialog data that tests specific capabilities (issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs). They evaluate multiple baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) on these tasks and validate on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset). The authors claim their synthetic data is a good proxy for real-world performance, as evidenced by similar relative performance patterns across datasets.

## Decision
Reject

## Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a significant claim that the synthetic data is a "good proxy for what would be observed on real data," but provides insufficient evidence to support this claim. The authors show that the relative performance of models is similar across synthetic and real datasets (Table 2), but this is not sufficient evidence that the synthetic data is a good proxy for real-world performance. The synthetic data is highly constrained with fixed patterns, deterministic behavior, and no noise, while real-world data contains noise, variability, and complex interactions. The authors do not provide evidence that the synthetic data captures the key challenges of real-world dialog systems.

The paper also makes an unproven claim about the practical utility of end-to-end models for goal-oriented dialog. The authors state that "Memory Networks can outperform a dedicated slot-filling rule-based baseline" on DSTC2, but their rule-based system is quite simple (40.7% per-response accuracy), while the Memory Networks achieve only 56.1% per-response accuracy on the same dataset. This is not a strong claim about the competitiveness of end-to-end models with traditional methods. In fact, the paper acknowledges that "the per-dialog accuracy remains low, indicating that end-to-end models still need to improve before being able to reliably handle goal-oriented dialog."

The paper's main contribution is a new evaluation resource, which is valuable, but the authors overstate its significance by claiming it solves the problem of evaluating end-to-end systems. They fail to compare against state-of-the-art goal-oriented systems (like POMDP-based approaches), which would be necessary to properly assess whether end-to-end models are competitive with traditional methods. The paper's focus on synthetic data rather than real-world evaluation is problematic given their main claim.

## Additional Feedback
1. The authors should temper their claims about the synthetic data being a "good proxy" for real-world data. The evidence provided (similar relative performance patterns) is not sufficient to support this claim. They should acknowledge the limitations of their synthetic data.

2. The per-dialog accuracy metric is overly strict (requiring all responses to be correct to count as a successful dialog). This doesn't reflect real-world usability, where a single error might not prevent successful completion of the goal. The authors should consider alternative metrics that better capture practical performance.

3. The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing against state-of-the-art goal-oriented systems (e.g., POMDP-based approaches) rather than just rule-based baselines. This would provide a more meaningful assessment of whether end-to-end models are competitive with traditional methods.

4. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of why Memory Networks fail on certain tasks (particularly T3 and T5), rather than simply stating that "they are still unable to perfectly handle interpreting knowledge about entities."

## Questions for Authors
1. How did you determine that the synthetic data's structure (fixed patterns, deterministic behavior, no noise) is representative of real-world goal-oriented dialog systems, given that these characteristics are known to be atypical of real user interactions?

2. The authors claim that "Memory Networks can outperform a dedicated slot-filling rule-based baseline" on DSTC2, but their rule-based system achieves only 40.7% per-response accuracy. How does this compare to more sophisticated rule-based or traditional slot-filling systems that have been previously reported in the literature?

3. The paper states that "the per-dialog accuracy is close to 0 on T3 and T4" for all models. How do the authors interpret this result? Is it because the tasks are too difficult for current end-to-end models, or is the per-dialog accuracy metric fundamentally flawed for evaluating these tasks?
