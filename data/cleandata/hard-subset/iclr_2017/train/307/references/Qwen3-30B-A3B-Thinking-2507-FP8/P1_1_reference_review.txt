

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing a comprehensive, open-source benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors identify a critical gap in the literature: while end-to-end dialog systems have shown promise in non-goal-oriented settings, there is a lack of reproducible, controlled benchmarks to evaluate their performance in goal-oriented scenarios like restaurant reservation. 

The authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks (Tasks 1-5) grounded in a restaurant reservation scenario with a structured knowledge base, designed to test specific capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog (dialog management, KB querying, interpreting results, handling new entities). These tasks are carefully designed to break down the goal-directed objective into testable subtasks, enabling error analysis by design. The authors also supplement their synthetic data with two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to validate that their benchmark serves as a good proxy for real-world performance.

The paper provides a thorough evaluation of multiple model architectures (rule-based systems, classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks) on this benchmark. The key finding is that while end-to-end models can learn to handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (like issuing API calls), they still struggle with interpreting and presenting results from knowledge bases (e.g., displaying restaurant options sorted by rating), which is crucial for achieving the overall goal.

This work is well-motivated and addresses a significant problem in the dialog systems community. The benchmark is designed to be lightweight, reproducible, and focused on the specific capabilities needed for goal-oriented dialog, making it a valuable resource for future research.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper identifies and addresses a genuine, important gap in the dialog systems literature: the lack of reproducible benchmarks for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.
2. The proposed benchmark is well-designed, with clear tasks that test specific capabilities, and the authors provide strong evidence that it serves as a good proxy for real-world performance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a strong case for the need for this benchmark. The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of current approaches (both traditional slot-filling systems and end-to-end neural models) in goal-oriented settings, and explains why existing datasets are unsuitable for end-to-end evaluation (e.g., they're annotated at the state level, noisy, or require specific licenses).

The benchmark design is particularly strong. The five tasks systematically test different capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog, with Tasks 1-2 testing dialog management, Task 3 testing KB query interpretation, Task 4 testing information retrieval, and Task 5 combining all capabilities. The OOV test sets are a particularly clever design choice that forces models to handle new entities, which is a critical challenge for real-world deployment.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and scientifically rigorous. The authors test multiple model architectures on both the synthetic tasks and real-world datasets, with proper train/validation/test splits and hyperparameter tuning. The ablation studies (with and without match type features) provide valuable insights into what makes the Memory Networks perform well. The results are compelling: while end-to-end models can handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialog, they still struggle with the core challenge of interpreting and presenting KB results, which is exactly where traditional rule-based systems excel.

The paper also provides a strong validation of the benchmark's utility by showing that the relative performance of models on the synthetic tasks matches their performance on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge), suggesting that the synthetic data is indeed a good proxy for real-world performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions to further improve it:

1. **Clarify the practical implications**: The paper mentions that "the per-dialog performance remains low" but doesn't fully explore why this is a critical issue. A brief discussion of how this impacts real-world deployment (e.g., what percentage of dialogs would fail to complete the reservation) would strengthen the argument for why this benchmark is needed.

2. **Expand the discussion of limitations**: The paper mentions that Memory Networks with match type features still struggle with Task 3 (displaying options), but doesn't speculate on why this is particularly challenging. A short discussion of potential reasons (e.g., the need for sorting, the difficulty of learning to present options in a natural way) would provide valuable insights for future work.

3. **Improve the presentation of results**: The paper includes tables with results, but it would be helpful to have a more detailed breakdown of where the models fail (e.g., for Task 3, which specific responses are most likely to be incorrect). This would help readers understand the specific challenges of the task.

4. **Clarify the relationship to previous work**: The paper mentions that the closest resource to theirs is "the set of tasks described in (Dodge et al., 2016)," but doesn't explain why those tasks aren't sufficient for evaluating goal-oriented dialog. A brief comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "the per-dialog accuracy is still close to 0 on T3 and T4" despite improved per-response accuracy. Can you elaborate on what specific aspects of the dialog are failing (e.g., is it the sorting of restaurants, the presentation of options, or the ability to handle user acceptance)? This would help clarify the specific challenges.

2. The authors mention that "the pattern of relative performances of methods is the same on Concierge and on our series of tasks." What specific metrics or aspects of the performance are most consistent between the synthetic and real datasets? This would help readers understand the strength of the benchmark as a proxy.

3. The paper states that "the set of user requests is much wider" in the Concierge dataset. Could you provide a brief example of the types of requests that are outside the scope of your synthetic tasks (e.g., requests for recommendations, specific information, or managing reservations)? This would help contextualize the real-world applicability of your benchmark.

4. The authors mention that "Memory Networks with match type features give two performance gains," but the paper doesn't explicitly state whether these features are practical for real-world deployment (e.g., would they require access to the KB at inference time?). Clarifying this would be valuable for practitioners.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The benchmark is well-designed, the evaluation is thorough, and the results provide valuable insights into the current capabilities and limitations of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. I believe this work will be a valuable resource for the community and help drive progress in this important area.
