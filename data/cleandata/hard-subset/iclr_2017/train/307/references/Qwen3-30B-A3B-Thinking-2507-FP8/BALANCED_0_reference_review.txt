

# Review of "A Dataset for Evaluating End-to-End Goal-Oriented Dialog Systems"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contribution

The paper proposes a benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, consisting of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain. The authors create a controlled environment to test specific dialog capabilities (dialog management, KB querying, interpreting results, etc.) and demonstrate how this benchmark can be used to evaluate models like Memory Networks. They validate their approach by showing that patterns observed on synthetic data correlate with performance on real datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset). The contribution is positioned as a reproducible, lightweight resource to help researchers systematically evaluate end-to-end dialog models in goal-directed settings.

## 2. Decision

Reject. While the paper presents a well-motivated and potentially useful benchmark, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and impact for ICLR. The benchmark is a straightforward extension of the bAbI tasks to a dialog context, and the experimental results don't provide compelling evidence that this benchmark will significantly advance the field.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's main contribution is a benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. However, this contribution doesn't meet ICLR's standard for novelty. The benchmark is essentially a direct adaptation of the bAbI question answering tasks to a dialog setting (as noted in the related work), and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this adaptation is significantly more valuable than existing benchmarks like DSTC.

The experimental validation is limited in scope. The authors validate their benchmark using only two real datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset), both of which are relatively small (DSTC2 is from 2014, and the concierge dataset contains only ~4k chats). The paper doesn't demonstrate the benchmark's value across multiple domains or with larger-scale datasets. Additionally, the paper doesn't compare their benchmark with existing benchmarks (like DSTC or ATIS) to show why it's superior or complementary.

The analysis of model failures is superficial. The paper identifies that Memory Networks fail on Tasks 3 and 5 (displaying options and full dialogs) but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why these failures occur. Understanding model limitations is crucial for advancing the field, but the paper lacks error analysis, attention visualization, or other techniques to explain these failures.

## 4. Additional feedback to improve the paper

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. **Expand the benchmark validation**: Demonstrate the benchmark's utility across multiple domains (e.g., hotel booking, flight booking) and with larger, more recent datasets. This would better establish the benchmark's generalizability and value to the community.

2. **Provide deeper failure analysis**: Conduct a detailed error analysis of why models fail on specific tasks. For example, why do Memory Networks fail to correctly display restaurant options in Task 3? This would provide valuable insights for the community and strengthen the paper's contribution.

3. **Conduct a more comprehensive comparison with existing benchmarks**: Provide a detailed comparison of the proposed benchmark with existing benchmarks (DSTC, ATIS, etc.) to clearly articulate its advantages and limitations. This would better position the contribution within the literature.

4. **Address the synthetic-to-real gap more thoroughly**: The paper acknowledges that synthetic data may not fully capture real-world complexity. The authors should discuss how to bridge this gap, perhaps by proposing methods to incorporate more diverse synthetic data or creating a hybrid benchmark.

5. **Clarify the evaluation metrics**: The paper uses per-response and per-dialog accuracy but doesn't fully justify why these metrics are appropriate for goal-oriented dialog. A more thorough discussion of evaluation metrics in this context would strengthen the paper.

## Questions for the authors

1. How does your benchmark specifically improve upon the Dialog State Tracking Challenge (DSTC) benchmark in terms of capturing the full range of goal-oriented dialog capabilities? What specific advantages does your benchmark offer that DSTC doesn't?

2. The paper states that the synthetic data may not fully capture real-world complexity. How would you address this limitation in future work, and what specific changes to the benchmark would you propose to better simulate real-world dialog?

3. The paper identifies that Memory Networks fail on Task 3 (displaying options) but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why. Could you share any insights from your error analysis that might help explain this failure mode?

4. The paper uses a small training set (1,000 examples per task) to create realistic learning conditions. How would the results change if the training set were larger (e.g., 10,000 examples), and what implications would this have for the benchmark's validity?

While the paper presents a useful resource for the community, the contribution lacks the novelty and depth required for ICLR. With significant expansion of the validation, deeper failure analysis, and clearer articulation of the benchmark's advantages over existing work, this could become a valuable contribution. As it stands, the paper doesn't demonstrate sufficient novelty or impact to warrant acceptance.
