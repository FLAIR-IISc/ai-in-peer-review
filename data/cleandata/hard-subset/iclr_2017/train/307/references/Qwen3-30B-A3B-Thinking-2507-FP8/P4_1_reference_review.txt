

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a systematic testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, addressing a clear gap in the literature where existing evaluation methods lack reproducibility or fail to correlate with real-world performance. The authors design five specific tasks within a restaurant reservation domain that test crucial capabilities such as issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs. These tasks are grounded in a simulated restaurant knowledge base and include Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) evaluation to test generalization to unseen entities. The paper also validates the testbed's relevance by evaluating on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset), demonstrating that the pattern of results on synthetic tasks correlates with real-world performance. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of multiple baseline models (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks) using both per-response and per-dialog accuracy metrics.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a reproducible, well-designed testbed for evaluating goal-oriented dialog systems, and the results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a clear and important problem: the lack of systematic, reproducible evaluation methods for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors effectively motivate their approach by contrasting traditional slot-filling systems with end-to-end neural approaches, noting the gap in understanding how well the latter perform in goal-directed settings. Their evaluation framework is well-placed in the literature, building on the bAbI task concept while addressing the specific challenges of goal-oriented dialog.

The paper provides strong evidence for its claims. The synthetic tasks are carefully designed to test specific capabilities, with clear definitions of each task and detailed descriptions of the underlying knowledge base. The evaluation framework is scientifically rigorous, with proper ablation studies (e.g., with/without match type features), multiple datasets (including OOV testing), and comparison across multiple model types. Crucially, the authors validate their synthetic testbed against real-world data (DSTC2 and concierge service), showing that the performance patterns observed on synthetic tasks correlate with real-world performance. This validation strengthens the paper's claim that their testbed is a useful proxy for real-world evaluation.

The results are well-presented and support the paper's key claim: while end-to-end models (particularly Memory Networks) can learn some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (like issuing API calls), they still struggle with others (particularly interpreting KB results to display options), which aligns with the authors' hypothesis about the limitations of current end-to-end approaches in goal-oriented settings.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper could more explicitly discuss the limitations of the synthetic testbed. For instance, how might the fixed patterns for user and bot utterances (43 patterns for users, 20 for bots) affect the generalizability of the findings to more diverse language patterns?
- The paper mentions that "the set of user requests is much wider" in the concierge dataset, but it would be helpful to quantify this difference (e.g., number of distinct cuisine types, locations, etc.) to better understand the challenge it poses.
- The paper could provide more details about the concierge dataset's preprocessing steps, particularly regarding how named entities were anonymized, to better assess the potential impact on model performance.
- While the paper demonstrates that the synthetic tasks correlate with real-world performance, it would be valuable to include a brief discussion of how the testbed might be extended to other domains beyond restaurant reservations.

## 5. Clarifying Questions
- How were the 43 user patterns and 20 bot patterns selected? Were they based on existing dialog data, or designed to cover all possible semantic variations within the task constraints?
- In Task 3 (Displaying options), the paper states that "users have a 25% chance of accepting" each option. What was the rationale for choosing this specific probability, and how might different acceptance rates affect the difficulty of the task?
- For the concierge dataset, the paper mentions that "API calls are not always used" and "facts about restaurants are not structured nor constrained as in a KB." How did the authors handle these variations in the evaluation framework compared to the synthetic tasks?
