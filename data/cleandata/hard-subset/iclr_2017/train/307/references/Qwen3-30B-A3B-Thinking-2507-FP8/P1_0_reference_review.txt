

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of dialog systems by introducing a new, open resource for systematically evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors address a critical gap in the literature: the lack of reproducible, well-defined evaluation methods for goal-oriented dialog systems that can accurately measure their capabilities beyond simple chit-chat performance. 

The authors propose a set of five tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that systematically test crucial capabilities of dialog systems, including dialog management (Task 1-2), KB querying and interpretation (Task 3-4), and full dialog completion (Task 5). The tasks are designed to be lightweight, reproducible, and to enable error analysis by breaking down the goal-oriented dialogue into specific subtasks. The authors also validate their findings on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset), demonstrating that their synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance.

The paper thoroughly evaluates multiple baseline models (rule-based systems, classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and memory networks) on these tasks, with a particular focus on Memory Networks as an end-to-end model. Their results show that while end-to-end models can learn to track implicit dialog states and handle OOV entities (Tasks 1-2), they still struggle with key capabilities like interpreting KB results to present options to users (Task 3), which is essential for goal completion.

The authors' resource is made publicly available at http://fb.ai/babi, which will significantly benefit the community by enabling consistent, reproducible comparisons of future end-to-end dialog models.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a much-needed reproducible evaluation framework for goal-oriented dialog systems. The motivation is strong, the approach is well-justified, and the experimental results are scientifically rigorous.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in several key areas:

1. **Strong motivation and problem statement**: The authors clearly articulate the gap in current evaluation methods for goal-oriented dialog systems. They correctly identify that evaluating end-to-end models on chit-chat data does not necessarily translate to goal-oriented performance, and that existing goal-oriented datasets lack reproducibility and clear task decomposition.

2. **Well-designed task set**: The five tasks systematically test specific capabilities of dialog systems, with Task 5 combining all capabilities into a full dialog. The inclusion of an OOV test set to evaluate generalization to new entities is particularly valuable and addresses a critical challenge for embedding-based methods.

3. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The authors evaluate multiple baseline models across both synthetic and real-world datasets, using appropriate metrics (per-response and per-dialog accuracy). The ablation studies (e.g., with/without match type features) provide valuable insights into model capabilities.

4. **Validation on real-world data**: By demonstrating that the pattern of relative performance across methods is consistent between the synthetic tasks and real-world datasets (DSTC2 and concierge), the authors convincingly show that their testbed serves as a good proxy for real-world performance.

5. **Clear contribution to the field**: The paper provides a resource that will directly benefit the community by enabling more consistent and reproducible research on end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.

The paper's contribution is timely and addresses a significant need in the field. The results provide important insights into the current limitations of end-to-end models for goal-oriented dialog, which will guide future research.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are several areas where it could be improved:

1. **More detail on synthetic data generation**: The paper mentions that they use "natural language patterns" to create user and bot utterances, but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how these patterns were created or validated. It would be helpful to know if these patterns were designed to mimic natural language or if they were simplified for the sake of the simulation. A brief analysis of how the synthetic data compares to natural language would strengthen the paper.

2. **Clarification on Task 3**: The paper states that "the bot must propose options to users by listing the restaurant names sorted by their corresponding rating (from higher to lower) until users accept." However, it's not clear how the bot knows the ratings of restaurants before the API call. The paper mentions that "we query the KB using the corresponding API call and add the facts resulting from the call to the dialog history," but it's unclear how the bot would have the ratings to sort before the API call is made. This is a critical point that needs clarification.

3. **More detailed discussion of limitations**: While the paper identifies limitations of end-to-end models, it could provide more specific guidance on how to address these limitations. For example, what specific model architectures or training techniques might help with the interpretation of KB results (Task 3)?

4. **Statistical significance**: The paper claims "the pattern of relative performances of methods is the same on Concierge and on our series of tasks," but doesn't provide statistical evidence for this claim. A simple statistical test (e.g., paired t-test) would strengthen this conclusion.

5. **Comparison to more recent work**: The paper is well-placed in the literature, but could benefit from a brief comparison to more recent work on end-to-end dialog systems (e.g., Transformer-based models) to better position the contribution.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. For Task 3 (Displaying options), how does the bot know the ratings of restaurants before the API call is made? The paper states that the bot must sort by rating, but it's not clear how the bot would have this information before querying the KB.

2. The paper mentions that "the set of user requests is much wider" in the Concierge dataset. Could you provide specific examples of the types of requests that fall outside the restaurant reservation domain (e.g., "managing restaurant reservations to asking for recommendations or specific information")?

3. How did you choose the specific patterns for generating user and bot utterances? Were these patterns designed to be representative of natural language, or were they simplified for the sake of the simulation?

4. The paper states that "the per-dialog accuracy is still low" for end-to-end models. Could you provide more specific examples of dialog failures, particularly for Task 3 (Displaying options), to help readers understand where the models are struggling?

5. The paper mentions that "Memory Networks with match type features give two performance gains" on Tasks 3 and 4. Could you elaborate on how exactly the match type features helped with these specific tasks, and whether you see these features being applicable to other domains beyond restaurant reservation?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed testbed will be an important resource for the community, and the results provide important insights into the current capabilities and limitations of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.
