

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of conversational AI by introducing a standardized, reproducible benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks grounded in a restaurant reservation scenario, designed to test specific capabilities like dialog management, KB querying, and entity handling. They also provide two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) to validate that their synthetic tasks serve as a useful proxy for real-world performance. The benchmark includes careful attention to OOV generalization and provides error analysis by design through task decomposition. The evaluation of multiple baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) demonstrates that while end-to-end models can handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (e.g., issuing API calls), they still struggle with key capabilities like interpreting KB results to display options.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper addresses a critical gap in the field with a well-designed benchmark that enables reproducible comparisons and targeted error analysis. While the evaluation methodology is sound, the paper could strengthen its claims about the synthetic-to-real-world transferability with additional analysis.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution - a standardized benchmark for goal-oriented dialog evaluation - is timely and necessary. The authors correctly identify that existing datasets either lack standardization (e.g., Mechanical Turk-based data), are domain-specific (e.g., DSTC datasets), or are designed for specific components rather than full dialog systems. Their task decomposition (T1-T5) is particularly well-conceived, as it isolates specific capabilities that are crucial for goal-oriented dialog but often conflated in previous work.

The methodology is scientifically rigorous: the synthetic data generation is transparent, the OOV test sets are well-designed to test generalization, and the inclusion of real-world datasets (T6 and Concierge) provides strong validation. The authors correctly note that their goal is not to improve state-of-the-art in restaurant booking, but to provide a controlled environment for evaluating end-to-end methods. The results showing that Memory Networks (with match type features) outperform rule-based systems on real data (T6) while still struggling with key aspects of the synthetic tasks (T3) is compelling evidence for the value of their benchmark.

However, the paper could strengthen its key claim that "the pattern of results observed in our tasks is indeed a good proxy for what would be observed on real data" with more specific analysis. While the relative performance patterns correlate between synthetic and real data, the authors don't quantify how much performance on T3 (displaying options) predicts performance on real-world dialog completion. A more detailed analysis of which specific failures in synthetic tasks map to real-world errors would strengthen this claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- **Clarify the "end-to-end" nature of the evaluation**: The paper uses a ranking approach (selecting from candidate responses) rather than generation. This is not truly end-to-end in the way some might expect (e.g., generating novel responses). The authors should clarify whether they consider this a limitation of their benchmark or a practical approach that aligns with how such systems might be deployed.

- **Expand the analysis of task difficulty**: The paper identifies that T3 (displaying options) is particularly challenging, but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of *why*. Is it the sorting requirement, the need to interpret KB results in context, or something else? A more detailed error analysis would help the community understand where to focus improvements.

- **Consider broader domain applicability**: The benchmark is focused on restaurant reservation. While this is a good starting point, the authors should discuss how easily this benchmark could be adapted to other goal-oriented domains (e.g., hotel booking, customer service). A brief discussion of the transferability of their task design to other domains would strengthen the paper's broader impact.

- **Compare with more recent models**: The paper uses Memory Networks (2015) as the end-to-end baseline. Given the field's rapid evolution, a brief comparison to more recent architectures (e.g., Transformer-based models) would help situate the work within the current state of the art.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that the synthetic tasks are a good proxy for real-world performance. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of which specific failure modes in the synthetic tasks (e.g., in T3) correspond to errors observed in the real-world datasets (T6 and Concierge)?

2. The match type features are introduced as a solution for handling entities, but they require access to the underlying KB structure. How would this approach scale to domains where the KB structure is not explicitly known or is more complex than the restaurant example? 

3. The paper notes that Memory Networks with match type features improve performance on T4 but not on T3. Could you elaborate on why the match type features help for T4 (providing information) but not for T3 (displaying options)? Is there something about the nature of the tasks that makes T3 more challenging to solve with this approach?

4. The paper states that "the per-dialog accuracy remains low" for end-to-end models. Could you provide more context about what constitutes "low" in this domain? For example, how does this compare to the performance of traditional slot-filling systems on the same tasks? 

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a much-needed standardized benchmark for goal-oriented dialog evaluation. With the suggested clarifications, it will be an even more valuable resource for the community.
