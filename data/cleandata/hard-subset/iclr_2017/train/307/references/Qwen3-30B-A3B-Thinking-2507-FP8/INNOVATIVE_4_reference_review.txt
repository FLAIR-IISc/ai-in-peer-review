

# Review of "A New Benchmark for End-to-End Goal-Oriented Dialog Systems"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel benchmark dataset and task set for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, addressing a critical gap in the literature. The authors propose five synthetic tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that systematically test specific capabilities required for goal-oriented dialog: issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs. These tasks are designed to be reproducible, lightweight, and to enable error analysis by design. Crucially, the authors validate their synthetic tasks against real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), demonstrating that the patterns observed on synthetic data correlate with performance on real data. The paper does not claim to improve state-of-the-art in restaurant booking but instead creates a testbed to understand the strengths and weaknesses of current end-to-end models in goal-directed settings.

## Decision

**Accept**

## Supporting Arguments

This paper makes a significant contribution by creating a new benchmark that directly addresses a major gap in the dialog systems literature. The authors' approach is bold and novel in its focus on creating a systematic, reproducible evaluation framework rather than proposing a new model or technique. The motivation is exceptionally well-grounded in the literature, clearly articulating why existing datasets are unsuitable for end-to-end training and evaluation of goal-oriented dialog systems.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While previous work has created datasets for specific components of dialog systems (like state tracking), this is the first to provide a comprehensive, goal-oriented benchmark designed specifically for end-to-end training and evaluation. The inspiration from bAbI tasks (Weston et al., 2015b) is apt and well-executed, and the authors demonstrate how their tasks can be used to diagnose model failures (e.g., the inability to interpret KB results to display options).

The experimental validation is strong, particularly the use of both synthetic tasks and real-world data to show that the synthetic tasks are a good proxy for real performance. The authors' findings that Memory Networks (with match type features) can solve some tasks but not others (particularly interpreting KB results) provide valuable insights into the current limitations of end-to-end models. This is exactly the kind of diagnostic tool that the field needs.

The paper's focus on reproducibility and interpretability is highly valuable. By creating a standardized testbed with clear task definitions and evaluation metrics, the authors enable fair comparisons across different models and future work. This is a significant step forward from the current situation where datasets are often noisy, domain-specific, or require participation in challenges.

While the paper acknowledges that the synthetic nature of their data might not perfectly match real-world complexity, they provide strong evidence that their tasks correlate with real performance, making the benchmark genuinely useful for the field.

## Additional Feedback

1. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of how this benchmark could be extended to other domains beyond restaurant reservation. While the authors mention this as a future direction, a more concrete proposal would strengthen the contribution.

2. The authors could provide more specific guidance on how to interpret the per-dialog accuracy metric in practice. Since the paper notes that per-response accuracy is higher than per-dialog accuracy (often 0%), it would be helpful to explain the practical implications of this gap for real-world deployment.

3. The paper could include a more detailed analysis of which specific failure modes are most common across tasks. For example, what are the most frequent types of errors in Task 3 (displaying options) compared to Task 4 (providing information)?

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you envision this benchmark being used by the community to drive future research? Are there specific research questions that this testbed could help answer that other benchmarks cannot?

2. The paper shows that Memory Networks with match type features improve OOV performance. How might these match type features be integrated into other end-to-end architectures (e.g., transformers) that are increasingly popular in dialog systems?

3. The paper mentions that the Concierge dataset has "more diverse vocabulary" and "more variable dialog structure." How do you think this increased complexity affects the relative performance of different model architectures compared to the synthetic tasks?
