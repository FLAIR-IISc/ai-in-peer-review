

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of dialog systems by introducing a well-designed benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog models. The authors address a critical gap in the literature: the lack of systematic, reproducible benchmarks for evaluating end-to-end models in goal-oriented settings, as existing resources either focus on chit-chat (which doesn't test goal-directed capabilities) or are designed for traditional slot-filling systems.

The authors propose a set of 5 synthetic tasks within the restaurant reservation domain that test specific capabilities required for successful goal-oriented dialog: issuing API calls (Task 1), updating API calls (Task 2), displaying options (Task 3), providing extra information (Task 4), and conducting full dialogs (Task 5). Crucially, they also introduce an Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) test set to evaluate generalization to unseen entities. The benchmark is designed to be lightweight, reproducible, and to provide clear error analysis by breaking down goal-oriented dialog into subtasks.

The authors evaluate multiple baselines (rule-based systems, classical IR methods, supervised embeddings, and Memory Networks) on these tasks, and validate their benchmark on two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service). Their key finding is that while end-to-end models (particularly Memory Networks) can handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (e.g., issuing API calls), they still struggle with interpreting KB results to display options to users (Task 3), which is a critical capability for successful goal-oriented dialog.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a genuinely important and timely gap in the literature for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.
2. The benchmark is well-designed, with clear tasks that test specific capabilities, and the validation on real-world datasets confirms its utility as a proxy for real-world performance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of both traditional slot-filling systems (hard to scale) and end-to-end models (not well-evaluated in goal-oriented settings). The authors effectively position their work as a "bAbI-like" testbed for goal-oriented dialog, which is a strong analogy to a well-known benchmark in the literature. The related work section thoroughly reviews the existing landscape and identifies the specific gap their work fills.

The experimental design is scientifically rigorous. The authors evaluate multiple baselines on both synthetic and real-world data, providing a comprehensive comparison. The OOV test set is particularly valuable for evaluating generalization capabilities, and the ablation study with match type features provides important insights into handling entities in dialog systems. The validation on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge) is crucial - it demonstrates that the synthetic tasks are indeed a good proxy for real-world performance, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper's contribution is both practical and foundational. By providing a reproducible benchmark that tests specific capabilities rather than just overall performance, the authors enable more focused research and development in end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. This will help the community better understand where current models succeed and fail, rather than just measuring overall accuracy.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their benchmark. For example, how well do the tasks generalize to other domains beyond restaurant reservation? A brief discussion of the challenges in extending this benchmark to other domains would strengthen the paper.

- While the paper mentions that they're not trying to improve the state of the art for restaurant booking, they could clarify why they chose this specific domain (it's a common benchmark domain, but could be worth explicitly stating why it's suitable for their evaluation purpose).

- The paper could provide more guidance on how to use the benchmark for future research. For example, what are the next steps after using this benchmark to evaluate models? How might researchers build upon this work to address the specific limitations identified?

- The paper mentions that Memory Networks with match type features still fail on Task 3 (displaying options), but doesn't provide much analysis of why this is the case. A more detailed error analysis would strengthen the paper and provide clearer direction for future work.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "the goal here is explicitly not to improve the state of the art in the narrow domain of restaurant booking." Could you elaborate on why you chose to focus on evaluation rather than improvement, and how this approach helps the broader research community?

2. The OOV test set is a strong contribution, but it only tests generalization to new entities within the same domain. How would you extend this benchmark to test generalization to completely new domains (e.g., from restaurant booking to hotel booking)?

3. The paper mentions that "the pattern of results observed in our tasks is indeed a good proxy for what would be observed on real data." Could you provide more details about the statistical significance of these results, and how confident you are that the synthetic tasks are a good proxy?

4. In the Memory Networks implementation, the authors use 3-4 hops for best performance. Could you explain why more hops don't provide better results, and if there's a theoretical limit to the number of hops needed for these tasks?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The benchmark is well-designed, the experiments are rigorous, and the findings provide important insights into the current capabilities and limitations of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. With minor revisions addressing the feedback above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
