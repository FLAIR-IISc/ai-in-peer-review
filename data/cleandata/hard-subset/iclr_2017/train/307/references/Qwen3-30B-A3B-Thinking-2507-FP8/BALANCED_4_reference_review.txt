

# Review of "A BAbI-like Testbed for Goal-Oriented Dialog Systems"

## Summary
This paper introduces a new benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, consisting of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain. The authors design tasks that test specific capabilities like dialog management, knowledge base querying, and handling new entities, with the goal of creating a reproducible and interpretable evaluation framework. They validate their benchmark using real-world datasets (DSTC2 and a concierge service dataset), demonstrating that the pattern of results observed on synthetic tasks correlates well with performance on real data. The paper provides a thorough comparison of multiple baselines (rule-based systems, IR methods, supervised embeddings, and memory networks), showing that while memory networks outperform other methods on many aspects, they still struggle with key capabilities like interpreting KB results to present options to users.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a critical gap in the evaluation of end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The benchmark is well-designed, reproducible, and provides valuable insights into the capabilities and limitations of current approaches.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper excels in identifying a genuine problem: the lack of standardized, reproducible benchmarks for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. Current datasets are often designed for specific components (like dialog state tracking), lack reproducibility, or are too noisy for systematic analysis. The authors correctly position their work as an extension of the bAbI tasks to the dialog domain, which provides a strong conceptual foundation.

The benchmark design is particularly strong. The five tasks are well-motivated and cover different aspects of goal-oriented dialog: Task 1-2 test dialog management, Task 3-4 test KB utilization, and Task 5 combines all capabilities. The inclusion of OOV test sets to evaluate generalization to new entities is a thoughtful touch that addresses a critical challenge for embedding-based methods. The validation against real-world datasets (DSTC2 and concierge) is compelling evidence that the synthetic testbed is a good proxy for real-world performance.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed. The authors compare multiple baselines (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, memory networks) across all tasks and datasets. The results clearly demonstrate that while memory networks are the best-performing method overall, they still struggle with key capabilities like displaying options (Task 3), which is a significant insight for the field. The paper also makes an important contribution by showing that the pattern of relative performance across methods is consistent between synthetic and real data, suggesting that the benchmark can effectively guide future research.

## Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further improve it:

1. **Domain scope**: The restaurant reservation domain is narrow and may not generalize to other domains. Consider discussing how the benchmark could be extended to other domains or what specific challenges might arise when adapting it to different application areas.

2. **Error analysis**: The paper mentions error analysis by design but doesn't provide detailed error analysis. A deeper analysis of specific failure cases (e.g., why memory networks fail on Task 3) would provide more actionable insights for the community.

3. **Metrics**: Per-response and per-dialog accuracy are appropriate for the tasks, but the paper could discuss how these metrics relate to human evaluation of dialog quality (e.g., naturalness, coherence). This would strengthen the argument for the benchmark's validity.

4. **Data generation**: More details on the data generation process would improve reproducibility. For instance, how were the natural language patterns for user and bot utterances created? What criteria were used to ensure diversity in the synthetic dialogs?

## Questions for Authors
1. How would you expect the benchmark to scale to more complex domains (e.g., hotel booking with multiple constraints, multi-domain dialog) and what modifications would be necessary?

2. The paper shows that memory networks with match type features improve performance on OOV tasks. How would you expect this approach to scale to domains with more complex entity types or more diverse KB structures?

3. The authors state that "the per-dialog one remains low" indicating that end-to-end models still need to improve. Could you provide more specific guidance on what kind of improvements would be most impactful for achieving higher per-dialog accuracy?

4. The paper mentions that TF-IDF Match performs worse than Nearest Neighbor on goal-oriented dialog, which is contrary to results on non-goal-oriented conversations. Could you elaborate on why this difference might occur and whether it suggests fundamental differences in the nature of goal-oriented versus chit-chat dialog?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a standardized, reproducible testbed for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The benchmark is well-designed, the experiments are thorough, and the insights provided will be useful for researchers working on this important problem. I recommend acceptance to ICLR.
