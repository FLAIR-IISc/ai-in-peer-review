

# Review

## 1. Summary of the paper's contribution

The paper proposes a novel open resource for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, consisting of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain. The authors design these tasks to systematically test critical capabilities of dialog systems (issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs), with careful attention to reproducibility, interpretability, and OOV generalization. They validate that their synthetic tasks serve as a good proxy for real-world performance by evaluating on two additional datasets: DSTC2 (from real human-bot interactions) and a concierge service dataset. The authors demonstrate that their framework can effectively highlight both strengths and weaknesses of current end-to-end models, with Memory Networks showing promising but incomplete performance on these tasks.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a standardized, reproducible, and well-designed evaluation framework for goal-oriented dialog systems, which addresses a clear gap in the literature.

## 3. Supporting arguments

The paper is well-motivated and clearly addresses an important problem in dialog systems research: the lack of standardized, reproducible, and controlled evaluation methods for end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. The authors provide a thorough literature review that positions their work well within the existing landscape, acknowledging the limitations of current datasets (e.g., noise in DSTC datasets, lack of reproducibility in crowdsourced settings, and lack of open-source availability).

The evaluation framework is scientifically rigorous, with careful attention to task design, dataset generation, and validation against real-world data. The authors demonstrate that their synthetic tasks correlate well with performance on real datasets (DSTC2 and Concierge), providing strong evidence that their framework is a valid proxy for real-world performance. The ablation studies on match type features and the comparison across multiple model types (rule-based, IR, supervised embeddings, Memory Networks) provide valuable insights into the specific challenges facing current end-to-end approaches.

The contribution is significant because it provides the community with a standardized benchmark that enables fair comparisons across methods, which has been a major challenge in dialog systems research. The open-source nature of the dataset further enhances its value to the research community.

## 4. Additional feedback

While the paper is strong, I offer these suggestions to improve it further:

1. The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the specific parameters of the synthetic tasks (e.g., the 25% acceptance rate in Task 3) were chosen and whether alternative parameterizations would change the conclusions. Some justification for these design choices would strengthen the paper.

2. The authors should consider expanding the evaluation to include more recent end-to-end dialog models (e.g., transformer-based architectures) to demonstrate that their framework can capture the performance of state-of-the-art methods.

3. The paper could provide more concrete examples of how this framework could be used to guide future research. For instance, how might the task breakdown help identify specific areas for model improvement?

4. The paper mentions "a set of five tasks" but the actual tasks are described in detail. It would be helpful to provide a more concise summary of each task's specific contribution to the overall evaluation framework.

## 5. Questions for authors

1. The paper states that "the goal here is explicitly not to improve the state of the art in the narrow domain of restaurant booking." How do you reconcile this with the fact that your framework is designed to be used for future improvements to end-to-end models? What specific improvements would you expect to see using your framework?

2. In Task 3, users have a 25% chance of accepting an option. Why was this specific probability chosen, and how sensitive are your results to this parameter? Could you provide results with different acceptance probabilities to demonstrate the robustness of your framework?

3. The authors mention that "the pattern of relative performances of methods is the same on Concierge and on our series of tasks." Could you elaborate on how this pattern is defined and whether there are any specific tasks where the pattern breaks down? This would strengthen the claim that the synthetic tasks are a good proxy for real-world performance.
