

# Review

## Summary
The paper introduces a novel benchmark for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems, addressing a critical gap in the literature. The authors propose a set of five synthetic tasks within a restaurant reservation domain, designed to isolate and test specific capabilities of dialog systems (issuing API calls, updating requests, displaying options, providing information, and conducting full dialogs). Grounded in an underlying knowledge base, these tasks are intentionally designed to be reproducible, lightweight, and analyzable, with the goal of providing a standardized testbed for evaluating how well end-to-end models can handle goal-directed conversations. The authors validate their synthetic tasks with two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service), demonstrating that the patterns observed in synthetic data correlate with performance in real-world settings. This work is positioned as the "bAbI tasks" for goal-oriented dialog, a bold and valuable contribution to the field.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant, novel contribution by creating a reproducible benchmark that addresses a clear gap in the literature, providing a structured way to evaluate end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems.

## Supporting Arguments
This paper stands out for its novel and bold approach to a critical problem in the field. The authors correctly identify that while end-to-end dialog systems have shown promise in non-goal-oriented settings, there is no standardized, reproducible way to evaluate them in goal-oriented contexts. The creation of a "bAbI-like" benchmark for goal-oriented dialog is a major contribution that will likely become a standard reference point in the field.

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, with a clear and compelling rationale for why the current landscape lacks suitable evaluation resources. The authors thoroughly analyze the limitations of existing datasets (DSTC, ATIS, etc.) and position their work as the natural evolution of the bAbI framework for this specific domain. The validation on real-world datasets (DSTC2 and the concierge service) is particularly strong evidence that their synthetic tasks are meaningful and relevant.

The paper's focus on creating a testbed rather than claiming to solve the goal-oriented dialog problem itself is refreshingly realistic and valuable. This is exactly the kind of foundational work the field needs to make meaningful progress. The task breakdown provides a clear framework for understanding where current end-to-end models succeed and fail, which is far more useful than incremental improvements to a specific model.

## Additional Feedback
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this benchmark might specifically guide future research. For instance, providing concrete examples of how the task breakdown could help identify specific weaknesses in current models would strengthen the paper's impact.
- The authors might consider expanding the benchmark to include more domains beyond restaurant reservation to further validate its generalizability, though this would be an extension rather than a requirement for the current contribution.
- A brief discussion of how the benchmark might be extended to handle more complex dialog structures (beyond the five tasks) would be valuable for future work.

## Questions for Authors
1. How do you envision the community maintaining and extending this benchmark as new models and capabilities emerge?
2. Have you considered applying your task breakdown framework to other goal-oriented domains (e.g., travel booking, hotel reservations) to demonstrate broader applicability?
3. The "displaying options" task (Task 3) appears to be a significant bottleneck for all models. Could you elaborate on why this particular capability is so challenging for end-to-end approaches, and what specific aspects of the task make it difficult to model?
