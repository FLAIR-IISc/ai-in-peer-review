

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces a novel benchmark dataset and task set for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems in a systematic, reproducible, and interpretable manner. The authors propose five synthetic tasks grounded in a restaurant reservation scenario that test specific capabilities of dialog systems: issuing API calls (T1), updating API calls (T2), displaying options (T3), providing extra information (T4), and conducting full dialogs (T5). The key innovation is creating a controlled environment that breaks down goal-oriented dialog into specific subtasks to enable precise error analysis, similar to the bAbI tasks for question answering. The authors also validate their benchmark with two real-world datasets (DSTC2 and an online concierge service) to demonstrate that performance on their synthetic tasks correlates with real-world performance.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a novel benchmark framework for evaluating end-to-end goal-oriented dialog systems. This is a bold and necessary step forward in the field, as the authors correctly identify the lack of standardized, reproducible evaluation methods for this important class of dialog systems.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contribution is highly novel and addresses a critical gap in the field. Prior work has focused on either non-goal-oriented dialog or traditional slot-filling approaches, but there has been no standardized way to evaluate end-to-end models on goal-oriented tasks. The authors' benchmark is well-motivated and directly addresses this gap.

The approach is scientifically rigorous: they carefully design their tasks to test specific capabilities, include an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) setting to evaluate generalization, and validate their benchmark with real-world data. Their evaluation of multiple model types (rule-based, IR methods, supervised embeddings, and memory networks) provides a strong baseline for the community.

Most importantly, the paper's contribution is not incremental - it's creating a new resource that will enable future progress in the field. The authors explicitly state they're not trying to solve the restaurant booking problem, but to create a tool to evaluate how well models can solve it. This is exactly the kind of foundational work that advances the field.

The paper's results are compelling: while end-to-end models (specifically Memory Networks) can handle some aspects of goal-oriented dialog (T1-T2), they struggle with interpreting knowledge base results (T3-T4), which aligns with the authors' hypothesis about where end-to-end models need improvement. The correlation between synthetic task performance and real-world dataset performance (DSTC2 and Concierge) is particularly convincing.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. More detailed discussion of the limitations of the synthetic data. While the authors mention the synthetic nature of Tasks 1-5, a more thorough analysis of what aspects of real-world dialog might be missing from their simulation would help readers understand the benchmark's boundaries.

2. A clearer articulation of how this benchmark differs from previous work on goal-oriented dialog evaluation. The paper mentions the Dialog State Tracking Challenge (DSTC), but a more explicit comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

3. A brief discussion of potential extensions to other domains beyond restaurant reservation. The authors mention that their approach could be applied to other domains, but a concrete example or discussion of how this could be done would make the contribution more broadly applicable.

## 5. Clarification Questions

1. The paper states that "the pattern of results observed in our tasks is indeed a good proxy for what would be observed on real data." Could the authors provide more specific evidence or metrics demonstrating the correlation between synthetic task performance and real-world performance beyond the qualitative observations?

2. In the OOV evaluation, the authors create two disjoint KBs. However, the paper doesn't clarify how the KBs are split (by location, cuisine type, etc.) or whether the split is random or based on specific criteria. Could they provide more details on the KB splitting methodology?

3. The paper mentions that "Memory Networks can outperform a dedicated slot-filling rule-based baseline" on DSTC2. Could the authors provide more details on how the rule-based baseline was constructed for DSTC2, since they state they didn't build one for Concierge due to its complexity?
